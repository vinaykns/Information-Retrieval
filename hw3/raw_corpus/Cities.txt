			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">City</h1>

<p>A <b>city</b> is a large and permanent <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">human settlement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodall_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kuper_and_Kuper_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuper_and_Kuper-2">[2]</a></sup> Although there is no agreement on how a city is distinguished from a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in general English language meanings, many cities have a particular administrative, legal, or historical status based on local law.</p>
<p><b>Cities</b> generally have complex systems for <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>, utilities, land usage, housing, and transportation. The concentration of development greatly facilitates interaction between people and businesses, sometimes benefiting both parties in the process, but it also presents challenges to managing <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban growth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A big city or <a href="/wiki/Metropolis" title="Metropolis">metropolis</a> usually has associated <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commuter_town" title="Commuter town">exurbs</a>. Such cities are usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan areas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a>, creating numerous business commuters traveling to urban centers for employment. Once a city expands far enough to reach another city, this region can be deemed a <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Megalopolis_(city_type)" class="mw-redirect" title="Megalopolis (city type)">megalopolis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a> is arguably the oldest city in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In terms of population, the largest city proper is <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, while the fastest-growing is <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg/220px-Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg/330px-Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg/440px-Ur-Nassiriyah.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>

<p>There is not enough <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> to assert what conditions gave rise to the first cities. Some theorists have speculated on what they consider suitable pre-conditions and basic mechanisms that might have been important driving forces.</p>
<p>The conventional view holds that cities first formed after the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Neolithic revolution">Neolithic revolution</a>. The Neolithic revolution brought agriculture, which made denser human populations possible, thereby supporting city development.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The advent of farming encouraged hunter-gatherers to abandon nomadic lifestyles and to settle near others who lived by agricultural production. The increased population density encouraged by farming and the increased output of food per unit of land created conditions that seem more suitable for city-like activities. In his book, <i>Cities and Economic Development</i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bairoch" title="Paul Bairoch">Paul Bairoch</a> takes up this position in his argument that agricultural activity appears necessary before true cities can form.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Vere_Gordon_Childe" class="mw-redirect" title="Vere Gordon Childe">Vere Gordon Childe</a>, for a settlement to qualify as a city, it must have enough surplus of raw materials to support trade and a relatively large population.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Bairoch points out that, due to sparse population densities that would have persisted in pre-Neolithic, hunter-gatherer societies, the amount of land that would be required to produce enough food for subsistence and trade for a large population would make it impossible to control the flow of trade. To illustrate this point, Bairoch offers an example: "Western Europe during the pre-Neolithic, [where] the density must have been less than 0.1 person per square kilometre".<sup id="cite_ref-Bairoch_1988_13_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bairoch_1988_13-9">[9]</a></sup> Using this population density as a base for calculation, and allotting 10% of food towards surplus for trade and assuming that city dwellers do no farming, he calculates that "...to maintain a city with a population of 1,000, and without taking the cost of transport into account, an area of 100,000 square kilometres would have been required. When the cost of transport is taken into account, the figure rises to 200,000 square kilometres&#160;...".<sup id="cite_ref-Bairoch_1988_13_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bairoch_1988_13-9">[9]</a></sup> Bairoch noted that this is roughly the size of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The urban theorist <a href="/wiki/Jane_Jacobs" title="Jane Jacobs">Jane Jacobs</a> suggests that city formation preceded the birth of agriculture, but this view is not widely accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobs_1969_23_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs_1969_23-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book <i>City Economics</i>, Brendan O'Flaherty asserts "Cities could persist—as they have for thousands of years—only if their advantages offset the disadvantages" (<a href="#CITEREFO.27Flaherty2005">O'Flaherty 2005</a>, p.&#160;12). O'Flaherty illustrates two similar attracting advantages known as <a href="/wiki/Increasing_returns_to_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Increasing returns to scale">increasing returns to scale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, which are concepts usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a>. Their applications are seen in more basic economic systems as well. Increasing returns to scale occurs when "doubling all inputs more than doubles the output [and] an activity has economies of scale if doubling output less than doubles cost" (<a href="#CITEREFO.27Flaherty2005">O'Flaherty 2005</a>, pp.&#160;572–573). To offer an example of these concepts, O'Flaherty makes use of "one of the oldest reasons why cities were built: military protection" (<a href="#CITEREFO.27Flaherty2005">O'Flaherty 2005</a>, p.&#160;13). In this example, the inputs are anything that would be used for protection (e.g., a wall) and the output is the area protected and everything of value contained in it. O'Flaherty then asks that we suppose the protected area is square, and each hectare inside it has the same value of protection. <p>Similarly, "Are Cities Dying?", a paper by Harvard economist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Glaeser" title="Edward Glaeser">Edward L. Glaeser</a>, delves into similar reasons for city formation: reduced transport costs for goods, people and ideas. Discussing the benefits of proximity, Glaeser claims that if a city is doubled in size, workers get a ten percent increase in earnings. Glaeser furthers his argument by stating that bigger cities do not pay more for equal productivity than in a smaller city, so it is reasonable to assume that workers become more productive if they move to a city twice the size as they initially worked in. The workers do not benefit much from the ten percent wage increase, because it is recycled back into the higher cost of living in a larger city. <p>City planning has seen many different schemes for how a city should look. The most commonly seen pattern is the <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">grid</a>, used for thousands of years in China, independently invented by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>'s city planner <a href="/wiki/Dinocrates_of_Rhodes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinocrates of Rhodes">Dinocrates of Rhodes</a> and favoured by the Romans, while almost a rule in parts of <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Columbian America">pre-Columbian America</a>. <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, begun in 1613, was the first <a href="/wiki/Planned_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Planned city">planned city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, with the walls being completed five years later. The central diamond within a walled city with four gates was considered a good design for defence. The grid pattern was widely copied in the colonies of <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greeks">ancient Greeks</a> often gave their colonies around the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> a grid plan. One of the best examples is the city of <a href="/wiki/Priene" title="Priene">Priene</a>. This city had different specialised districts, much as is seen in modern city planning today. Fifteen centuries earlier, the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation" title="Indus Valley Civilisation">Indus Valley Civilisation</a> was using grids in such cities as <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-Daro" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohenjo-Daro">Mohenjo-Daro</a>. In medieval times there was evidence of a preference for linear planning. Good examples are the cities established by various rulers in the south of France and city expansions in old Dutch and Flemish cities.</p>
<p>Grid plans were popular among planners in the 19th century, particularly after the redesign of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. They cut through the meandering, organic streets that followed old paths. The United States imposed grid plans in new territories and towns, as the <a href="/wiki/American_West" class="mw-redirect" title="American West">American West</a> was rapidly established, in places such as <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.</p>
<p>Other forms may include a radial structure, in which main roads converge on a central point. This was often a historic form, the effect of successive growth over long time with concentric traces of <a href="/wiki/Town_wall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town wall">town walls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citadel" title="Citadel">citadels</a>. In more recent history, such forms were supplemented by ring-roads that take traffic around the outskirts of a town. Many Dutch cities are structured this way: a central square surrounded by concentric canals. Every city expansion would imply a new circle (canals together with town walls). In cities such as <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> and also <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, this pattern is still clearly visible.</p>

<p>Towns and cities have a long history, although opinions vary on whether any particular <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient</a> settlement can be considered a city. Cities formed as a result of geographically centralized trade, benefiting the members living in close proximity to others and facilitating interactions including, but not limited to, economics. These interactions generate both positive and negative externalities between others' actions. Benefits include reduced transport costs, exchange of ideas, sharing of natural resources, large local markets, and later in their development, amenities such as <a href="/wiki/Running_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Running water">running water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> disposal. Possible costs would include higher rate of crime, higher mortality rates, higher cost of living, worse pollution, traffic and high commuting times. Cities grow when the benefits of proximity between people and firms are higher than the cost.</p>
<p>The first true towns are sometimes considered large settlements where the inhabitants were no longer simply farmers of the surrounding area, but began to take on specialized occupations, and where trade, food storage and power were centralized. In 1950 <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Childe" class="mw-redirect" title="Gordon Childe">Gordon Childe</a> attempted to define a historic city with 10 general metrics.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Size and density of the population should be above normal.</li>
<li>Differentiation of the population. Not all residents grow their own food, leading to specialists.</li>
<li>Payment of taxes to a deity or king.</li>
<li>Monumental public buildings.</li>
<li>Those not producing their own food are supported by the king.</li>
<li>Systems of recording and practical science.</li>
<li>A system of writing.</li>
<li>Development of symbolic art.</li>
<li>Trade and import of raw materials.</li>
<li>Specialist craftsmen from outside the kin-group.</li>
</ol>
<p>This categorisation is descriptive, and it is used as a general touchstone when considering ancient cities, although not all have each of its characteristics.</p>
<p>One characteristic that can be used to distinguish a small city from a large town is organized government. A town accomplishes common goals through informal agreements between neighbors or the leadership of a chief. A city has professional administrators, regulations, and some form of taxation (food and other necessities or means to trade for them) to feed the government workers. The governments may be based on heredity, religion, military power, work projects (such as canal building), food distribution, land ownership, agriculture, commerce, manufacturing, finance, or a combination of those. Societies that live in cities are often called <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a>.</p>

<p>The more complex human societies, called the first <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a> emerged around 3000 BC in the river valleys of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. An increase in <a href="/wiki/Food_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Food production">food production</a> led to the significant growth in human population and the rise of cities. The peoples of <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwest Asia">Southwest Asia</a> and Egypt laid the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Western_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Western civilization">Western civilization</a>, they developed cities and struggled with the problems of organised states as they moved from individual communities to larger territorial units and eventually to <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empires</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Indus Valley Civilization and ancient China are two other areas with major indigenous urban traditions. Among the early Old World cities, <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a> of the Indus Valley Civilization in present-day <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, existing from about 2600 BC, was one of the largest, with a population of 50,000 or more.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>In ancient Greece, beginning in the early 1st millennium BC, there emerged independent <a href="/wiki/City-states" class="mw-redirect" title="City-states">city-states</a> that evolved for the first time the notion of <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>, becoming in the process the archetype of the <a href="/wiki/Free_city_(antiquity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Free city (antiquity)">free city</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">polis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tws2Y21_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tws2Y21-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">Agora</a>, meaning "gathering place" or "assembly", was the center of athletic, artistic, spiritual and political life of the polis.<sup id="cite_ref-InternationalDictionary_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InternationalDictionary-15">[15]</a></sup> These Greek city-states reached great levels of prosperity that resulted in an unprecedented cultural boom, that of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">classical Greece</a>, expressed in architecture, drama, science, mathematics and philosophy, and nurtured in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> under a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic government</a>. The Greek <a href="/wiki/Hippodamus_of_Miletus" title="Hippodamus of Miletus">Hippodamus of Miletus</a> (c. 407 BC) has been dubbed the "Father of City Planning" for his design of <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a>; the Hippodamian, or grid plan, was the basis for subsequent Greek and Roman cities.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-16">[16]</a></sup> In the 4th century BC, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> commissioned <a href="/wiki/Dinocrates_of_Rhodes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinocrates of Rhodes">Dinocrates of Rhodes</a> to lay out his new city of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, the grandest example of idealized urban planning of the ancient Mediterranean world, where the city's regularity was facilitated by its level site near a mouth of the Nile.</p>
<p>This roster of early urban traditions is notable for its diversity. Excavations at early urban sites show that some cities were sparsely populated political capitals, others were trade centers, and still other cities had a primarily religious focus. Some cities had large dense populations, whereas others carried out urban activities in the realms of politics or religion without having large associated populations. Theories that attempt to explain ancient urbanism by a single factor, such as economic benefit, fail to capture the range of variation documented by archaeologists.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The growth of the population of ancient civilizations, the formation of ancient empires concentrating political power, and the growth in commerce and manufacturing led to ever greater capital cities and centres of commerce and industry, with <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seleucia_on_the_Tigris" class="mw-redirect" title="Seleucia on the Tigris">Seleucia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic civilization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pataliputra" title="Pataliputra">Pataliputra</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Patna" title="Patna">Patna</a>) in India, <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang'an">Chang'an</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi'an">Xi'an</a>) in China, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, ancient Rome, its eastern successor Constantinople (later <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>).</p>
<p>Keith Hopkins estimates that ancient Rome had a population of about a million people by the end of the 1st century BC,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> after growing continually during the 3rd, 2nd, and 1st centuries BC, making it the largest city in the world at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-cities_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cities-19">[19]</a></sup> Alexandria's population was also close to Rome's population at around the same time, the historian Rostovtzeff estimates a total population close to a million based on a census dated from 32 AD that counted 180,000 adult male citizens in Alexandria.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity#Cities" title="Late Antiquity">Cities of Late Antiquity</a> underwent transformations as the urban power base shrank and was transferred to the local bishop (see <a href="/wiki/Late_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Roman Empire">Late Roman Empire</a>). Cities essentially disappeared, earliest in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germania" title="Germania">Germania</a> and latest in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigothic Spain">Visigothic Spain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="for the entire paragraph (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the ancient Americas, early urban traditions developed in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>. In the Andes, the first urban centers developed in the <a href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization" title="Norte Chico civilization">Norte Chico civilization</a> (also <a href="/wiki/Caral" title="Caral">Caral</a> or Caral-Supe civilization), <a href="/wiki/Chav%C3%ADn_culture" title="Chavín culture">Chavin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moche_(culture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moche (culture)">Moche</a> cultures, followed by major cities in the <a href="/wiki/Huari_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Huari culture">Huari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chimu" class="mw-redirect" title="Chimu">Chimu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inca" class="mw-redirect" title="Inca">Inca</a> cultures. The Norte Chico civilization included as many as 30 major population centers in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_(Peruvian_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norte Chico (Peruvian region)">Norte Chico region</a> of north-central coastal <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. It is the oldest known civilization in the Americas, flourishing between the 30th century BC and the 18th century BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady1997_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady1997-21">[21]</a></sup> Mesoamerica saw the rise of early urbanism in several cultural regions, including the <a href="/wiki/Maya_city" title="Maya city">Preclassic Maya</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Zapotec_civilization" title="Zapotec civilization">Zapotec</a> of Oaxaca, and <a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a> in central Mexico. Later cultures such as the <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec</a> drew on these earlier urban traditions.</p>
<p>In the first millennium AD, an urban tradition developed in the Khmer region of Cambodia, where Angkor grew into one of the largest cities (in area) of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Evans_PNAS_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evans_PNAS-22">[22]</a></sup> The closest rival to Angkor, the Mayan city of <a href="/wiki/Tikal" title="Tikal">Tikal</a> in Guatemala, was between 100 and 150 square kilometres (39 and 58&#160;sq&#160;mi) in total size.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_2007_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_2007-23">[23]</a></sup> Although its population remains a topic of research and debate, newly identified agricultural systems in the Angkor area may have supported up to one million people.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agriculture was practiced in sub-Saharan Africa since the third millennium BC. Because of this, cities could develop as centers of non-agricultural activity. Exactly when this first happened is still a topic of archeological and historical investigation. Western scholarship has tended to focus on cities in Europe and Mesopotamia, but emerging archeological evidence indicates that urbanization occurred south of the Sahara well before the influence of Arab <a href="/wiki/Urban_culture" title="Urban culture">urban culture</a>. One of the oldest sites documented thus far, <a href="/wiki/Jenn%C3%A9-Jeno" class="mw-redirect" title="Jenné-Jeno">Jenné-Jeno</a> in what is today Mali, has in fact been dated back to the third century BC. According to Roderick and Susan McIntosh, Jenné-Jeno did not fit into traditional Western conceptions of urbanity as it lacked monumental architecture and a distinctive elite social class, but it should indeed be considered a city based on more a more functional redefinition of urban development. In particular, Jenné-Jeno featured settlement mounds arranged according to a horizontal, rather than vertical, power hierarchy, and served as a center of specialized production and exhibited functional interdependence with the surrounding hinterland.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Archaeological evidence from Jenné-Jeno, specifically the presence of non-West African glass beads dated from the third century BC to the fourth century AD, indicates that pre-Arabic trade contacts probably existed between Jenné-Jeno and North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Magnavita_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Magnavita-26">[26]</a></sup> Additionally, other early urban centers in sub-Saharan Africa, dated to around 500 AD, include Awdaghust, Kumbi-Saleh the ancient capital of Ghana, and Maranda a center located on a trade rout between Egypt and Gao.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>While David Kessler and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Temin" title="Peter Temin">Peter Temin</a> consider ancient Rome the largest city before the 19th century, London was the first to exceed a population of 1 million.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> George Modelski considers medieval Baghdad, with an estimated population of 1.2 million at its peak, the largest city before 19th century London and the first with a population of over one million.<sup id="cite_ref-Modelski_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modelski-29">[29]</a></sup> Others estimate that Baghdad's population may have been as large as 2 million in the 9th century.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 9th through the end of the 12th century, the city of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, capital of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>, was the largest and wealthiest city in Europe, with a population approaching 1 million.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/European_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="European Middle Ages">European Middle Ages</a>, a town was as much a political entity as a collection of <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">houses</a>. City residence brought freedom from customary rural obligations to lord and community: <i>"<a href="/wiki/Stadtluft_macht_frei" title="Stadtluft macht frei">Stadtluft macht frei</a>"</i> ("City air makes you free") was a saying in Germany. In <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a> cities with a legislature of their own were not unheard of, the laws for towns as a rule other than for the countryside, the lord of a town often being another than for surrounding land. In the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, some cities had no other lord than the emperor. In Italy <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">medieval communes</a> had quite a statelike power. In exceptional cases like <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> or <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a>, cities themselves became powerful states, sometimes taking surrounding areas under their control or establishing extensive maritime empires. Similar phenomena existed elsewhere, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Sakai,_Osaka" title="Sakai, Osaka">Sakai</a>, which enjoyed a considerable autonomy in late medieval Japan.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern">Early modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>While the <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">poleis</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> languished from the 16th century, Europe's larger capitals benefited from the growth of commerce following the emergence of an <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> trade. By the early 19th century, London had become the largest city in the world with a population of over a million, while Paris rivaled the well-developed regionally traditional capital cities of Baghdad, Beijing, Istanbul and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>. During the Spanish colonization of the Americas the old Roman city concept was extensively used. Cities were founded in the middle of the newly conquered territories, and were bound to several laws about administration, finances and urbanism.</p>
<p>Most towns remained far smaller, so that in 1500 only some two dozen places in the world contained more than 100,000 inhabitants. As late as 1700, there were fewer than forty, a figure that rose to 300 in 1900.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_age">Industrial age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Industrial age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The growth of modern industry from the late 18th century onward led to massive urbanization and the rise of new great cities, first in Europe and then in other regions, as new opportunities brought huge numbers of migrants from rural communities into urban areas. In the United States from 1860 to 1910, the introduction of railroads reduced transportation costs, and large manufacturing centers began to emerge, thus allowing migration from rural to city areas. Cities during this period were deadly places to live in, due to health problems resulting from contaminated water and air, and communicable diseases. In the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of the 1930s <a href="/wiki/Cities_in_the_great_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Cities in the great depression">cities were hard hit by unemployment</a>, especially those with a base in heavy industry. In the U.S. urbanization rate increased forty to eighty percent during 1900–1990. Today the world's population is slightly over half urban,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and continues to urbanize, with roughly a million people moving into cities every 24 hours worldwide.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="External_effects">External effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: External effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern cities are known for creating their own <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimates</a>. This is due to the large clustering of heat absorbent surfaces that heat up in <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> and that channel rainwater into underground ducts.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">Waste</a> and sewage are two major problems for cities, as is air pollution from various forms of combustion,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> including fireplaces, wood or coal-burning stoves, other heating systems,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>. The impact of cities on places elsewhere, be it hinterlands or places far away, is considered in the notion of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_Footprint" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological Footprint">city footprinting</a> (<i>ecological footprint</i>). Other negative external effects include health consequences such as communicable diseases, crime, and high traffic and commuting times. Cities cause more interaction with more people than rural areas, leading to a higher probability of contracting contagious diseases. However, many inventions such as inoculations, vaccines, and water filtration systems have also lowered health concerns. Crime is also a concern in the cities. Studies have shown that crime rates in cities are higher and the chance of punishment after getting caught is lower. In cases such as burglary, the higher concentration of people in cities create more items of higher value worth the risk of crime. The high concentration of people also makes using auto mobiles inconvenient and pedestrian traffic is more prominent in metropolitan areas than a rural or suburban one.</p>
<p>Cities also generate positive external effects. The close physical proximity facilitates <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_spillover" title="Knowledge spillover">knowledge spillovers</a>, helping people and firms exchange information and generate new ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A thicker labor market allows for better skill matching between firms and individuals. Population density enables also sharing of common infrastructure and production facilities, however in very dense cities, increased crowding and waiting times may lead to some negative effects.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cities may have a positive influence on the environment. <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Settlements_Programme" title="United Nations Human Settlements Programme">UN-HABITAT</a> stated in its reports that city living can be the best solution for dealing with the rising population numbers (and thus still be a good approach on dealing with overpopulation)<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This is because cities concentrate human activity into one place, making the environmental damage on other places smaller.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> However, this can be achieved only if <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> is improved<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and if city services are properly maintained.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_cities_and_towns">Distinction between cities and towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Distinction between cities and towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>There are probably as many different ways of conceiving what a city is as there are cities. A simple definition therefore has its attractions. The simplest is that a city is a human settlement where strangers are likely to meet.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Richard_Sennett" title="Richard Sennett">Richard Sennett</a>, <i>The Fall of Public Man</i>, 1977, p. 39.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The difference between <i>towns</i> and <i>cities</i> is differently understood in different parts of the world.</p>
<p>Even within the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> there is no one standard definition of a city: the term may be used either for a town possessing city status; for an urban locality exceeding an arbitrary population size; for a town dominating other towns with particular regional economic or administrative significance. <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">British city status</a> was historically conferred on settlements with a diocesan cathedral; in more recent times towns apply to receive city status at times of national celebration. Larger settlements which are not designated as cities are towns, smaller settlements are villages and very small settlements are called hamlets. In the United States <i>city</i> can be used for much smaller settlements.</p>
<p>Historically, city status was a privilege granted by royal letters of patent. The status would allow markets and/or foreign trade, in contrast to towns. Sovereigns could establish cities by decree, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, regardless of what was in the location beforehand. Also, with the establishment of federal governments, the new capital could be established from scratch, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Brasília">Brasília</a>, without going through organic growth from a village to a town.</p>
<p>Although <i>city</i> can refer to an agglomeration including suburban and satellite areas, the term is not usually applied to a conurbation (cluster) of distinct urban places, nor for a wider <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> including more than one city, each acting as a focus for parts of the area. And the word "town" (also "downtown") may mean the center of the city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_cities">Global cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_city" title="Global city">Global city</a></div>
<p>A global city, also known as a world city, is a prominent centre of trade, banking, finance, innovation, and markets. The term "global city", as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Megacity" title="Megacity">megacity</a>, was coined by <a href="/wiki/Saskia_Sassen" title="Saskia Sassen">Saskia Sassen</a> in a seminal 1991 work.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Whereas "megacity" refers to any city of enormous size, a global city is one of enormous <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> or influence. Global cities, according to Sassen, have more in common with each other than with other cities in their host nations.</p>
<p>The notion of global cities is rooted in the concentration of power and capabilities within all cities. The city is seen as a container where skills and resources are concentrated: the better able a city is to concentrate its skills and resources, the more successful and powerful the city. This makes the city itself more powerful in the sense that it can influence what is happening around the world. Following this view of cities, it is possible to <a href="/wiki/Global_city#GaWC_Inventory_of_World_Cities" title="Global city">rank the world's cities hierarchically</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ranking_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ranking-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critics of the notion point to the different realms of power and interchange. The term "global city" is heavily influenced by economic factors and, thus, may not account for places that are otherwise significant. One writer, for example argues that the term is 'reductive and skewed':</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Against those writers who, by emphasizing the importance of financial exchange systems, distinguish a few special cities as 'global cities'—commonly London, Paris, New York and Tokyo—we recognize the uneven global dimensions of all the cities that we study. Los Angeles, the home of Hollywood, is a globalizing city, though perhaps more significantly in cultural than economic terms. And so is Dili globalizing, the small and 'insignificant' capital of Timor Leste—except this time it is predominantly in political terms...<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1995, Kanter argued that successful cities can be identified by three elements: good thinkers (concepts), good makers (competence) or good traders (<a href="/wiki/City_network" title="City network">connections</a>). The interplay of these three elements, Kanter argued, means that good cities are not planned but managed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inner_city">Inner city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Inner city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inner_city" title="Inner city">Inner city</a></div>
<p>In Paris, the inner city is the richest part of the metropolitan area, where housing is the most expensive, and where elites and high-income individuals dwell. In the developing world, economic modernization brings poor newcomers from the countryside to build haphazardly at the edge of current settlement (see <a href="/wiki/Favelas" class="mw-redirect" title="Favelas">favelas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shack" title="Shack">shacks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shanty_towns" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanty towns">shanty towns</a>).</p>
<p>The United States, in particular, has a culture of anti-urbanism that dates back to colonial times. The American <a href="/wiki/City_Beautiful" class="mw-redirect" title="City Beautiful">City Beautiful</a> architecture movement of the late 19th century was a reaction to perceived <a href="/wiki/Urban_decay" title="Urban decay">urban decay</a> and sought to provide stately civic buildings and boulevards to inspire civic pride in the motley residents of the urban core. Modern anti-urban attitudes are found in the United States in the form of a planning profession that continues to develop land on a low-density suburban basis, where access to amenities, work and shopping is provided almost exclusively by car rather than by foot or transit.</p>
<p>There is a growing movement in North America called "<a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>" that calls for a return to traditional city planning methods where mixed-use zoning allows people to walk from one type of land-use to another. The idea is that housing, shopping, office space, and leisure facilities are all provided within walking distance of each other, thus reducing the demand for road-space and also improving the efficiency and effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Mass_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass transit">mass transit</a>.</p>

<p>There is a debate about whether <a href="/wiki/High_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="High technology">technology</a> and instantaneous communications are making cities obsolete, or reinforcing the importance of big cities as centres of the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_economy" title="Knowledge economy">knowledge economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Knowledge-based development of cities, globalization of innovation networks, and broadband services are driving forces of a new city planning paradigm towards <a href="/wiki/Smart_city" title="Smart city">smart cities</a> that use technology and communication to create more efficient agglomerations in terms of competitiveness, innovation, environment, energy, utilities, governance, and delivery of services to the citizen. Some companies are building brand new <a href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" class="mw-redirect" title="Land use planning">masterplanned</a> cities from scratch on <a href="/wiki/Greenfield_land" title="Greenfield land">greenfield</a> sites.</p>
