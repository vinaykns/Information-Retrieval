<p><b>Smart growth</b> is an urban <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transportation_planning" title="Transportation planning">transportation</a> theory that concentrates growth in compact walkable urban centers to avoid <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">sprawl</a>. It also advocates compact, <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">transit-oriented</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian-friendly" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedestrian-friendly">walkable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicycle-friendly" title="Bicycle-friendly">bicycle-friendly</a> land use, including neighborhood schools, <a href="/wiki/Complete_streets" title="Complete streets">complete streets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mixed-use_development" title="Mixed-use development">mixed-use development</a> with a range of housing choices.<sup id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup> The term 'smart growth' is particularly used in North America. In Europe and particularly the UK, the terms '<a href="/wiki/Compact_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact City">Compact City</a>' or 'urban intensification' have often been used to describe similar concepts, which have influenced government planning policies in the UK, the Netherlands and several other European countries.</p>
<p>Smart growth values long-range, regional considerations of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> over a short-term focus. Its <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> goals are to achieve a unique sense of community and place; expand the range of transportation, employment, and housing choices; equitably distribute the costs and benefits of development; preserve and enhance natural and cultural resources; and promote public health.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_concept">Basic concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smart Growth is a theory of <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land development</a> that accepts that growth and development will continue to occur, and so seeks to direct that growth in an intentional, comprehensive way. Its proponents include urban planners, architects, developers, community activists, and historic preservationists. The term "Smart Growth" is an attempt to reframe the conversation from "growth" versus "no growth" (or <a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">NIMBY</a>) to good/smart growth versus bad/dumb growth. Proponents seek to distinguish Smart Growth from <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> which they claim causes most of the problems that fuel opposition to urban growth, such as traffic congestion and environmental degradation. Smart growth principles are directed at developing sustainable communities that provide a greater range of transportation and housing choices and prioritize infill and redevelopment in existing communities rather than development of "greenfield" farmland or natural lands. Some of the fundamental aims for the benefits of residents and the communities are increasing family income and wealth, providing safe walking routes to schools, fostering livable, safe and healthy places, stimulating economic activity (both locally and regionally), and developing, preserving and investing in built and natural resources. Smart growth "principles" describe the elements of community that are envisioned and smart growth "regulations" describe the various approaches to implementation, that is, how federal, state, and municipal governments choose to fulfill smart growth principles. Some of these regulatory approaches such as <a href="/wiki/Urban_Growth_Boundary" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban Growth Boundary">Urban Growth Boundaries</a> predate the use of the term Smart Growth. One of the earliest efforts to establish smart growth forward as an explicit regulatory framework were put forth by the <a href="/wiki/American_Planning_Association" title="American Planning Association">American Planning Association</a>. In 1997, the APA introduced a project called Growing Smart and published "Growing Smart Legislative Guidebook: Model Statutes for Planning and the Management of Change." <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency defines smart growth as “development that serves the economy, the community, and the environment. It changes the terms of the development debate away from the traditional growth/no growth question to how and where should new development be accommodated” <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smart growth is related to, or may be used in combination with the following concepts:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Growth_management" title="Growth management">Growth management</a></li>
<li>New Community Design</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable Development">Sustainable Development</a></li>
<li>Resource Stewardship</li>
<li>Land Preservation</li>
<li>Preventing <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a></li>
<li>Creating <a href="/wiki/Sense_of_place" title="Sense of place">sense of place</a></li>
<li>Development <a href="/wiki/Best_Practices" class="mw-redirect" title="Best Practices">Best Practices</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Preservation_development" title="Preservation development">Preservation Development</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">Sustainable transport</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Triple_Bottom_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Triple Bottom Line">Triple Bottom Line</a> (TBL) Accounting - People, Planet, Profit</li>
<li>The Three Pillars - Human, Natural, and Created Capital</li>
</ul>
<p>The smart growth approach to development is multifaceted and can encompass a variety of techniques. For example, in the state of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> smart growth is enacted by a combination of techniques including increasing housing density along transit nodes, conserving farm land, and mixing residential and commercial use areas.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Perhaps the most descriptive term to characterize this concept is <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Neighborhood_Development" title="Traditional Neighborhood Development">Traditional Neighborhood Development</a>, which recognizes that smart growth and related concepts are not necessarily new, but are a response to car culture and sprawl. Many favor the term <a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>, which invokes a new, but traditional way of looking at urban planning.</p>
<p>There are a range of best practices associated with smart growth, these include: supporting existing communities, redeveloping underutilized sites, enhancing economic competitiveness, providing more transportation choices, developing livability measures and tools, promoting equitable and affordable housing, providing a vision for sustainable growth, enhancing integrated planning and investment, aligning, coordinating, and leveraging government polices, redefining housing affordability and making the development process transparent.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Related, but somewhat different, are the overarching goals of smart growth, and they include: making the community more competitive for new businesses, providing alternative places to shop, work, and play, creating a better "Sense of Place," providing jobs for residents, increasing property values, improving quality of life, expanding the tax base, preserving open space, controlling growth, and improving safety.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_principles">Basic principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basic principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are 10 accepted principles that define smart growth:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Mix land uses</li>
<li>Take advantage of compact building design</li>
<li>Create a range of housing opportunities and choices</li>
<li>Create walkable neighborhoods</li>
<li>Foster distinctive, attractive communities with a strong sense of place</li>
<li>Preserve open space, farmland, natural beauty, and critical environmental areas</li>
<li>Strengthen and direct development towards existing communities</li>
<li>Provide a variety of transportation choices</li>
<li>Make development decisions predictable, fair, and cost effective</li>
<li>Encourage community and stakeholder collaboration in development decisions</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transportation and community planners began to promote the idea of compact cities and communities and adopt many of the regulatory approaches associated with Smart Growth in the early 1970s. The cost and difficulty of acquiring land (particularly in historic and/or areas designated as conservancies) to build and widen highways caused some politicians to reconsider basing transportation planning on motor vehicles.</p>
<p>The Congress for the <a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>, with architect <a href="/wiki/Peter_Calthorpe" title="Peter Calthorpe">Peter Calthorpe</a> promoted and popularized the idea of <a href="/wiki/Urban_village" title="Urban village">urban villages</a> that relied on public transportation, bicycling, and walking instead of automobile use. Architect <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Duany" title="Andrés Duany">Andrés Duany</a> promoted changing design codes to promote a sense of community, and to discourage driving. Colin Buchanan and Stephen Plowden helped to lead the debate in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</p>
<p>The Local Government Commission which presents the annual New Partners for Smart Growth conference adopted the original Ahwahnee Principles in 1991<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which articulates many of the major principles now generally accepted as part of smart growth movement such as <a href="/wiki/Transit_oriented_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Transit oriented development">Transit oriented development</a>, a focus on walking distance, greenbelts and wildlife corridors, and infill and redevelopment. The document was co-authored by several of the founders of the New Urbanist movement. The Local Government Commission has been co-sponsoring Smart Growth related conferences since 1997. The New Partners for Smart Growth Conference started under that name circa 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-newpartners.org_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newpartners.org-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smart Growth America, an organization devoted to the promoting Smart Growth in the United States, was founded in 2002. This organization leads an evolving coalition of national and regional organizations most of which predated its founding such as <a href="/wiki/1000_Friends_of_Oregon" title="1000 Friends of Oregon">1000 Friends of Oregon</a> founded in 1975 and the <a href="/wiki/Congress_for_the_New_Urbanism" class="mw-redirect" title="Congress for the New Urbanism">Congress for the New Urbanism</a> founded in 1993. The <a href="/wiki/EPA" class="mw-redirect" title="EPA">EPA</a> launched its smart growth program in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-newpartners.org_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newpartners.org-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale_for_smart_growth">Rationale for smart growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rationale for smart growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smart growth is an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a>, disconnected neighborhoods, and <a href="/wiki/Urban_decay" title="Urban decay">urban decay</a>. Its principles challenge old assumptions in urban planning, such as the value of detached houses and automobile use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><br /></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_protection">Environmental protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmentalists promote smart growth by advocating <a href="/wiki/Urban-growth_boundaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban-growth boundaries">urban-growth boundaries</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">Green belts</a>, as they have been termed in England since the 1930s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health">Public health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transit-oriented development can improve the quality of life and encourage a healthier, pedestrian-based lifestyle with less pollution. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> suggests Smart growth to reduce <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_to_existing_subsidies">Reaction to existing subsidies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reaction to existing subsidies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Smart Growth advocates claim that much of the <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> of the 19th Century was due to government subsidies for infrastructure that redistribute the true costs of sprawl. Examples include subsidies for highway building, fossil fuels, and electricity.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_subsidies">Electrical subsidies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electrical subsidies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With electricity, there is a cost associated with extending and maintaining the service delivery system, as with water and sewage, but there also is a loss in the commodity being delivered. The farther from the generator, the more power is lost in distribution. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a>'s (DOE) Energy Information Administration (EIA), 9 percent of energy is lost in transmission. <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Current average cost pricing, where customers pay the same price per unit of power regardless of the true cost of their service, subsidizes sprawl development. With electricity deregulation, some states now charge customers/developers fees for extending distribution to new locations rather than rolling such costs into utility rates.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>New Jersey, for example, has implemented a plan that divides the state into five planning areas, some of which are designated for growth, while others are protected. The state is developing a series of incentives to coax local governments into changing <a href="/wiki/Zoning_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoning laws">zoning laws</a> that will be compatible with the state plan. The New Jersey Board of Public Utilities recently proposed a revised rule that presents a tiered approach to utility financing. In areas not designated for growth, utilities and their ratepayers are forbidden to cover the costs of extending utility lines to new developments—and developers will be required to pay the full cost of public utility infrastructure. In designated growth areas that have local smart plans endorsed by the State Planning Commission, developers will be refunded the cost of extending utility lines to new developments at two times the rate of the revenue received by developers in smart growth areas that do not have approved plans.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements">Elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Growth is "smart growth", to the extent that it includes the elements listed below.<sup id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compact_neighborhoods">Compact neighborhoods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Compact neighborhoods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Compact, livable urban neighborhoods attract more people and business. Creating such neighborhoods is a critical element of reducing urban sprawl and protecting the climate. Such a tactic includes adopting redevelopment strategies and zoning policies that channel housing and job growth into urban centers and neighborhood business districts, to create compact, walkable, and bike- and transit-friendly hubs. This sometimes requires local governmental bodies to implement code changes that allow increased height and density downtown and regulations that not only eliminate minimum parking requirements for new development but establish a maximum number of allowed spaces. Other topics fall under this concept:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mixed-use_development" title="Mixed-use development">mixed-use development</a></li>
<li>inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordable housing</a></li>
<li>restrictions or limitations on suburban design forms (e.g., <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">detached houses</a> on individual lots, <a href="/wiki/Strip_malls" class="mw-redirect" title="Strip malls">strip malls</a> and surface <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">parking</a> lots)</li>
<li>inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Parks" class="mw-redirect" title="Parks">parks</a> and recreation areas</li>
</ul>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a> the recent movements of <a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Classical_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="New Classical Architecture">New Classical Architecture</a> promote a sustainable approach towards construction, that appreciates and develops smart growth, <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">architectural tradition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">classical design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">modernist</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">globally uniform</a> architecture, as well as leaning against solitary <a href="/wiki/Housing_estate" title="Housing estate">housing estates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">suburban sprawl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Both trends started in the 1980s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transit-oriented_development">Transit-oriented development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transit-oriented development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">Transit-oriented development</a> (TOD) is a residential or commercial area designed to maximize access to public transport, and mixed-use/compact neighborhoods tend to use transit at all times of the day.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<pre>
Many cities striving to implement better TOD strategies seek to secure funding to create new public transportation infrastructure and improve existing services. Other measures might include regional cooperation to increase efficiency and expand services, and moving buses and trains more frequently through high-use areas. Other topics fall under this concept:
</pre>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transportation_Demand_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation Demand Management">Transportation Demand Management</a> measures</li>
<li>road pricing system (tolling)</li>
<li>commercial parking taxes</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pedestrian-_and_bicycle-friendly_design">Pedestrian- and bicycle-friendly design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pedestrian- and bicycle-friendly design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biking and walking instead of driving can reduce emissions, save money on fuel and maintenance, and foster a healthier population. Pedestrian- and bicycle-friendly improvements include bike lanes on main streets, an urban bike-trail system, bike parking, pedestrian crossings, and associated master plans. The most pedestrian- and bike-friendly variant of smart growth and New Urbanism is <a href="/wiki/New_Pedestrianism" class="mw-redirect" title="New Pedestrianism">New Pedestrianism</a> because motor vehicles are on a separate grid.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>preserving open space and critical habitat, reusing land, and protecting water supplies and air quality</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transparency_(humanities)" class="mw-redirect" title="Transparency (humanities)">transparent</a>, predictable, fair and cost-effective rules for development</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Historic_preservation" title="Historic preservation">historic preservation</a></li>
<li>Setting aside large areas where development is prohibited, nature is able to run its course, providing fresh air and clean water.</li>
<li>Expansion around already existing areas allows public services to be located where people are living without taking away from the core city neighborhoods in large urban areas.</li>
<li>Developing around preexisting areas decreases the socioeconomic segregation allowing society to function more equitably, generating a tax base for housing, educational and employment programs.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_tools">Policy tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Policy tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zoning_ordinances">Zoning ordinances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Zoning ordinances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most widely used tool for achieving smart growth is modification of local <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> laws. Zoning laws are applicable to most cities and counties in the United States. Smart Growth advocates often seek to modify zoning ordinances to increase the density of development and redevelopment allowed in or near existing towns and neighborhoods and/or restrict new development in outlying or environmentally sensitive areas. Additional density incentives can be offered for development of <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_land" title="Brownfield land">brownfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greyfield_land" title="Greyfield land">greyfield land</a> or for providing amenities such as parks and open space. Zoning ordinances typically include minimum parking requirements. Reductions in or elimination of parking minimums or imposition of parking maximums can also reduce the amount of parking built with new development increasing land available for parks and other community amenities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_growth_boundaries">Urban growth boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Urban growth boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Related to zoning ordinances, an <a href="/wiki/Urban_Growth_Boundary" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban Growth Boundary">Urban Growth Boundary</a> (UGB) is a tool that used in some U.S. cities to contain high density development to certain areas. The first urban growth boundary in the United States was established in 1958 in Kentucky. Subsequently urban growth boundaries were established in Oregon in the 1970s and Florida in 1980s. Some believe that UGBs contributed to the escalation of housing prices from 2000 to 2006, as they limited the supply of developable land.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, this is not completely substantiated because prices continued to rise even after municipalities expanded their growth boundaries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_of_development_rights">Transfer of development rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Transfer of development rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_development_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Transfer of development rights">Transfer of development rights</a> systems are intended to allow property owners in areas deemed desirable for growth (such as infill and brownfield sites) to purchase the right to build at higher densities from owners of properties in areas deemed undesirable for growth such as environmental lands, farmlands or lands outside of an <a href="/wiki/Urban_growth_boundary" title="Urban growth boundary">urban growth boundary</a>. Areas that have implemented transfer of development rights include several counties in Maryland (as early as 1978)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and St. Lucie County.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provision_of_social_infrastructure">Provision of social infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Provision of social infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systematic provision of infrastructure such as schools, libraries, sporting facilities and community facilities is an integral component of smart growth communities. This is commonly known as 'social infrastructure' or 'community infrastructure'. In Australia, for example, most new suburban developments are master planned, and key social infrastructure is planned at the outset.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact_assessments">Environmental impact assessments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental impact assessments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One popular approach to assist in smart growth in democratic countries is for law-makers to require prospective developers to prepare <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">environmental impact assessments</a> of their plans as a condition for state and/or local governments to give them <a href="/wiki/Building_permit" class="mw-redirect" title="Building permit">permission</a> to build their buildings. These reports often indicate how significant impacts generated by the development will be <a href="/wiki/Environmental_mitigation" title="Environmental mitigation">mitigated</a>, the cost of which is usually paid by the developer. These assessments are frequently controversial. Conservationists, neighborhood advocacy groups and <a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">NIMBYs</a> are often skeptical about such impact reports, even when they are prepared by independent agencies and subsequently approved by the decision makers rather than the promoters. Conversely, developers will sometimes strongly resist being required to implement the mitigation measures required by the local government as they may be quite costly.</p>
<p>In communities practicing these smart growth policies, developers comply with local codes and requirements. Consequently, developer compliance builds communal trust because it demonstrates a genuine interest in the environmental quality of the community.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smart_growth.2C_urban_sprawl_and_automobile_dependency">Smart growth, urban sprawl and automobile dependency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Smart growth, urban sprawl and automobile dependency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whether smart growth (or the 'Compact City') does or can reduce problems of <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependency</a> associated with urban sprawl have been fiercely contested issues over several decades. A 2007 meta-study by Keith Barthomomew of the University of Utah found that reductions in driving associated with compact development scenarios averaged 8 percent ranging up to 31.7 percent with the variation being explained by degree of land use mixing and density.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> An influential study in 1989 by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)" title="Peter Newman (environmental scientist)">Peter Newman</a> and Jeff Kenworthy compared 32 cities across North America, Australia, Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The study has been criticised for its methodology <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> but the main finding that denser cities, particularly in Asia, have lower car use than sprawling cities, particularly in North America, has been largely accepted — although the relationship is clearer at the extremes across continents than it is within countries where conditions are more similar.</p>
<p>Within cities studies from across many countries (mainly in the developed world) have shown that denser urban areas with greater mixture of land use and better public transport tend to have lower car use than less dense suburban and ex-urban residential areas. This usually holds true even after controlling for socio-economic factors such as differences in household composition and income.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This does not necessarily imply that suburban sprawl causes high car use, however. One confounding factor, which has been the subject of many studies, is residential self-selection:<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> people who prefer to drive tend to move towards low density suburbs, whereas people who prefer to walk, cycle or use transit tend to move towards higher density urban areas, better served by public transport. Some studies have found that, when self-selection is controlled for, the built environment has no significant effect on travel behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> More recent studies using more sophisticated methodologies have generally refuted these findings: density, land use and public transport accessibility can influence travel behaviour, although social and economic factors, particularly household income, usually exert a stronger influence.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paradox_of_intensification">Paradox of intensification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Paradox of intensification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reviewing the evidence on urban intensification, smart growth and their effects on travel behaviour Melia <i>et al.</i> (2011)<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> found support for the arguments of both supporters and opponents of smart growth. Planning policies which increase population densities in urban areas do tend to reduce car use, but the effect is a weak one, so doubling the population density of a particular area will not halve the frequency or distance of car use.</p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> a U.S. city which has pursued smart growth policies, substantially increased its population density between 1990 and 2000 when other US cities of a similar size were reducing in density. As predicted by the paradox, traffic volumes and congestion both increased more rapidly than in the other cities, despite a substantial increase in transit use.</p>
<p>These findings led them to propose the paradox of intensification, which states "<a href="/wiki/Ceteris_paribus" title="Ceteris paribus">Ceteris paribus</a>, urban intensification which increases population density will reduce per capita car use, with benefits to the global environment, but will also increase concentrations of motor traffic, worsening the local environment in those locations where it occurs".</p>
<p>At the city-wide level it may be possible, through a range of positive measures to counteract the increases in traffic and congestion which would otherwise result from increasing population densities: <a href="/wiki/Freiburg_im_Breisgau" title="Freiburg im Breisgau">Freiburg im Breisgau</a> in Germany is one example of a city which has been more successful in this respect.</p>
<p>This study also reviewed evidence on the local effects of building at higher densities. At the level of the neighbourhood or individual development positive measures (e.g. improvements to public transport) will usually be insufficient to counteract the traffic effect of increasing population density. This leaves policy-makers with four choices: intensify and accept the local consequences, sprawl and accept the wider consequences, a compromise with some element of both, or intensify accompanied by more radical measures such as parking restrictions, closing roads to traffic and <a href="/wiki/Carfree_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Carfree zone">carfree zones</a>. Where possible, this is the authors' preferred option.</p>
<p>In contrast the City of Cambridge, Massachusetts reported that its Kendall Square Neighborhood saw a 40% increase in commercial space attended by a traffic decrease of 14%.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>A report by CEOs for Cities report, "Driven Apart," showed that while denser cities in the United States may have more congested commutes they are also shorter on average in both time and distance. This is in contrast to cities where commuters face less congestion but drive longer distances resulting in commutes that take as long or longer.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smart_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Bruegmann" title="Robert Bruegmann">Robert Bruegmann</a>, professor of art history, architecture, and urban planning at the University of Illinois at Chicago and author of <i>Sprawl: A Compact History</i>, stated that historical attempts to combat urban sprawl have failed, and that the high population density of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, currently the most dense urban area in the United States, "lies at the root of many of the woes experienced by L.A. today."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wendell_Cox" title="Wendell Cox">Wendell Cox</a> is a vocal opponent of smart growth policies. He argued before the United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works that, "smart growth strategies tend to intensify the very problems they are purported to solve."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Cox and Joshua Utt analyzed smart growth and sprawl, and argued that:<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Our analysis indicates that the Current Urban Planning Assumptions are of virtually no value in predicting local government expenditures per capita. The lowest local government expenditures per capita are not in the higher density, slower growing, and older municipalities.</p>
<p>On the contrary, the actual data indicate that the lowest expenditures per capita tend to be in medium- and lower-density municipalities (though not the lowest density); medium- and faster-growing municipalities; and newer municipalities. This is after 50 years of unprecedented urban decentralization, which seems to be more than enough time to have developed the purported urban sprawl-related higher local government expenditures. It seems unlikely that the higher expenditures that did not develop due to sprawl in the last 50 years will evolve in the next 20 year, despite predictions to the contrary in The Costs of Sprawl 2000 research.</p>
It seems much more likely that the differences in municipal expenditures per capita are the result of political, rather than economic factors, especially the influence of special interests.</blockquote>
<p>The phrase "smart growth" implies that other growth and development theories are not "smart". There is debate about whether <a href="/wiki/Transit-proximate_development" title="Transit-proximate development">transit-proximate development</a> constitutes smart growth when it is not transit-oriented. The <a href="/wiki/National_Motorists_Association" title="National Motorists Association">National Motorists Association</a> does not object to smart growth as a whole, but strongly objects to <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a>, which is intended to reduce automobile accidents and fatalities,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> but may also reduce automobile usage and increase alternate forms of public transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Public_Policy_Research" title="National Center for Public Policy Research">National Center for Public Policy Research</a>, a self-described <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a>, published an economic study entitled "Smart Growth and Its Effects on Housing Markets: The New Segregation" which termed smart growth "restricted growth" and suggested that smart growth policies disfavor minorities and the poor by driving up housing prices.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a>, criticize smart growth on the grounds that it leads to greatly increased land values, and people with average incomes can no longer afford to buy <a href="/wiki/Houses" class="mw-redirect" title="Houses">detached houses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economists</a> claim that industrial civilization has already "overshot" the carrying capacity of the Earth, and "smart growth" is mostly an illusion. Instead, a <a href="/wiki/Steady_state_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Steady state economy">steady state economy</a> would be needed to bring human societies back into a necessary balance with the ability of the ecosystem to sustain humans (and other species).<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study released in November 2009 characterized the smart-growth policies in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> as a failure, concluding that "[t]here is no evidence after ten years that [smart-growth laws] have had any effect on development patterns."<sup id="cite_ref-Rein_11-2-2009_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rein_11-2-2009-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Factors include a lack of incentives for builders to redevelop older neighborhoods and limits on the ability of state planners to force local jurisdictions to approve high-density developments in "smart-growth" areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Rein_11-2-2009_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rein_11-2-2009-48">[48]</a></sup> Buyers demand low-density development and because voters tend to oppose high density developments near them.<sup id="cite_ref-Rein_11-2-2009_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rein_11-2-2009-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 2010, groups generally associated with the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Party_movement" title="Tea Party movement">Tea Party movement</a> began to identify Smart Growth as an outgrowth of the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> which they viewed as an attempt by international interests to force a "sustainable" lifestyle on the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However planning groups and even some smart growth opponents counter that Smart Growth concepts and groups predate the 1992 Agenda 21 conference.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In addition the word "sustainable development" as used in the <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> report is often misread to mean real estate development when it typically refers to the much broader concept of <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" title="Human development (humanity)">human development</a> in the United Nations and foreign aid context which addresses a broader slate of economic, health, poverty, and education issues.</p>
