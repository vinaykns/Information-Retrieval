<p>The <b>United States Forest Service</b> (<b>USFS</b>) is an agency of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a> that administers the nation's 154 <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" title="United States National Forest">national forests</a> and 20 <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Grassland" title="United States National Grassland">national grasslands</a>, which encompass 193&#160;million acres (780,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Major divisions of the agency include the National Forest System, State and Private Forestry, Business Operations, and the Research and Development branch.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Managing approximately 25% of <a href="/wiki/Federal_lands" title="Federal lands">federal lands</a>, it is the only major national land agency that is outside the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_the_Interior" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of the Interior">U.S. Department of the Interior</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gao.gov_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gao.gov-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_Forest_Service" title="History of the United States Forest Service">History of the United States Forest Service</a></div>
<p>The concept of the National Forests was born from <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>’s conservation group, <a href="/wiki/Boone_and_Crockett_Club" title="Boone and Crockett Club">Boone and Crockett Club</a>, due to concerns regarding <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a> beginning as early as 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1876, Congress created the office of Special Agent in the Department of Agriculture to assess the quality and conditions of forests in the United States. <a href="/wiki/Franklin_B._Hough" title="Franklin B. Hough">Franklin B. Hough</a> was appointed the head of the office. In 1881, the office was expanded into the newly formed <b>Division of Forestry</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Forest_Reserve_Act_of_1891" title="Forest Reserve Act of 1891">Forest Reserve Act of 1891</a> authorized withdrawing land from the public domain as "forest reserves," managed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a>. In 1901, the Division of Forestry was renamed the <b>Bureau of Forestry</b>. The Transfer Act of 1905 transferred the management of forest reserves from the <a href="/wiki/General_Land_Office" title="General Land Office">General Land Office</a> of the Interior Department to the Bureau of Forestry, henceforth known as the <b>United States Forest Service</b>. <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Chief_Foresters" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Chief Foresters">United States Chief Forester</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Presidency of Theodore Roosevelt">Presidency of Theodore Roosevelt</a>.</p>
<p>Significant federal legislation affecting the Forest Service includes the <a href="/wiki/Weeks_Act" title="Weeks Act">Weeks Act</a> of 1911, the <a href="/wiki/Multiple_Use_%E2%80%93_Sustained_Yield_Act_of_1960" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiple Use – Sustained Yield Act of 1960">Multiple Use – Sustained Yield Act of 1960</a>, P.L. 86-517; the <a href="/wiki/Wilderness_Act" title="Wilderness Act">Wilderness Act</a>, P.L. 88-577; the <a href="/wiki/National_Forest_Management_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Forest Management Act">National Forest Management Act</a>, P.L. 94-588; the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a>, P.L. 91-190; the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cooperative_Forestry_Assistance_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cooperative Forestry Assistance Act (page does not exist)">Cooperative Forestry Assistance Act</a>, P.L. 95-313; and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_and_Rangelands_Renewable_Resources_Planning_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Forest and Rangelands Renewable Resources Planning Act">Forest and Rangelands Renewable Resources Planning Act</a>, P.L. 95-307.</p>
<p>In February 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> evaluated whether the Forest Service should be moved from the Department of Agriculture to the Department of the Interior, which already includes the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="United States Fish and Wildlife Service">Fish and Wildlife Service</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a>, managing some 438,000,000 acres (1,770,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of public land.<sup id="cite_ref-gao.gov_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gao.gov-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2009, the Forest Service has a total budget authority of $5.5 billion, of which 42% is spent fighting fires. The Forest Service employs 34,250 employees in 750 locations, including 10,050 firefighters, 737 law enforcement personnel, and 500 scientists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The mission of the Forest Service is "To sustain the health, diversity, and productivity of the Nation's forests and grasslands to meet the needs of present and future generations." Its motto is "Caring for the land and serving people." As the lead federal agency in natural resource conservation, the US Forest Service provides leadership in the protection, management, and use of the nation's forest, rangeland, and aquatic ecosystems. The agency's ecosystem approach to management integrates ecological, economic, and social factors to maintain and enhance the quality of the environment to meet current and future needs. Through implementation of land and resource management plans, the agency ensures sustainable ecosystems by restoring and maintaining species diversity and ecological productivity that helps provide recreation, water, timber, minerals, fish, wildlife, wilderness, and aesthetic values for current and future generations of people.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The everyday work of the Forest Service balances resource extraction, resource protection, and providing recreation. The work includes managing 193,000,000 acres (780,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of national forest and grasslands, including 59,000,000 acres (240,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roadless_area&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roadless area (page does not exist)">roadless areas</a>; 14,077 recreation sites; 143,346 miles (230,693&#160;km) of trails; 374,883 miles (603,316&#160;km) of roads; and the harvesting of 1.5 billion trees per year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Further, the Forest Service fought fires on 2,996,000 acres (12,120&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of land.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2010)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Forest Service organization includes ranger districts, national forests, regions, research stations and research work units and the Northeastern Area Office for State and Private Forestry. Each level has responsibility for a variety of functions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National">National</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Chief of the Forest Service is a career federal employee who oversees the entire agency. The Chief reports to the Under Secretary for Natural Resources and Environment in the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), an appointee of the President confirmed by the Senate. The Chief's staff provides broad policy and direction for the agency, works with the Administration to develop a budget to submit to Congress, provides information to Congress on accomplishments, and monitors activities of the agency. There are five deputy chiefs for the following areas: National Forest System, State and Private Forestry, Research and Development, Business Operations, and Finance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_Stations_and_Research_Work_Units">Research Stations and Research Work Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Research Stations and Research Work Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_Service_Research_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Forest Service Research and Development (page does not exist)">Forest Service Research and Development</a> deputy area includes five research stations, the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Products_Laboratory" title="Forest Products Laboratory">Forest Products Laboratory</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_of_Tropical_Forestry" title="International Institute of Tropical Forestry">International Institute of Tropical Forestry</a>, in Puerto Rico. Station directors, like regional foresters, report to the Chief. Research stations include Northern, Pacific Northwest, Pacific Southwest, Rocky Mountain, and Southern. There are 92 research work units located at 67 sites throughout the United States. there are 80 Experimental Forests and Ranges that have been established progressively since 1908; many sites are more than 50 years old. The system provides places for long-term science and management studies in major vegetation types of the 195&#160;million acres (790,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of public land administered by the Forest Service. Individual sites range from 47 to 22,500&#160;ha in size.</p>
<p>Operations of Experimental Forests and Ranges are directed by local research teams for the individual sites, by Research Stations for the regions in which they are located, and at the level of the Forest Service.</p>
<p>Major themes in research at the Experimental Forests and Ranges includes: develop of systems for managing and restoring forests, range lands, and watersheds; investigate the workings of forest and stream ecosystems; characterize plant and animal communities; observe and interpret long-term environmental change and many other themes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USFS_regions_map.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/USFS_regions_map.jpg/300px-USFS_regions_map.jpg" width="300" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/USFS_regions_map.jpg/450px-USFS_regions_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/USFS_regions_map.jpg 2x" data-file-width="522" data-file-height="363" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USFS_regions_map.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of the nine regions</div>

<p>There are nine regions in the USDA Forest Service; numbered 1 through 10 (Region 7 was eliminated in 1965 when the current Eastern Region was created from the former Eastern and North Central regions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> ). Each encompasses a broad geographic area and is headed by a regional forester who reports directly to the Chief. The regional forester has broad responsibility for coordinating activities among the various forests within the region, for providing overall leadership for regional natural resource and social programs, and for coordinated regional land use planning.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Northern Region</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Missoula,_Montana" title="Missoula, Montana">Missoula, Montana</a>, the Northern Region (R1) covers six states (Montana, Northern Idaho, North Dakota, Northwestern South Dakota, Northeast Washington, and Northwest Wyoming), twelve National Forests and one National Grassland.</li>
<li><b>Rocky Mountain</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Golden,_Colorado" title="Golden, Colorado">Golden, Colorado</a>, the Rocky Mountain Region (R2) covers five states (Colorado, Nebraska, Kansas and most of Wyoming and South Dakota), sixteen National Forests and seven National Grasslands.</li>
<li><b>Southwestern</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>, the Southwestern Region (R3) covers two states (New Mexico and Arizona) and eleven National Forests.</li>
<li><b>Intermountain</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Ogden,_Utah" title="Ogden, Utah">Ogden, Utah</a>, the Intermountain Region (R4) covers four states (Southern Idaho, Nevada, Utah and Western Wyoming), twelve national forests.</li>
<li><b>Pacific Southwest</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Vallejo,_California" title="Vallejo, California">Vallejo, California</a>, The Pacific Southwest Region (R5) covers two states (California and Hawaii), eighteen National Forests and one Management Unit.</li>
<li><b>Pacific Northwest</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> the Pacific Northwest Region (R6) covers two states (Washington and Oregon), twenty-one National Forests and one National Scenic Area.</li>
<li><b>Southern</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, the Southern Region (R8) covers thirteen states (Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, North and South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Oklahoma and Virginia; and Puerto Rico and the US Virgin Islands), and thirty-four National Forests.</li>
<li><b>Eastern</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" class="mw-redirect" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a>, the Eastern Region (R9) covers twenty states (Maine, Illinois, Ohio, Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, Missouri, Indiana, Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Maryland, New York, Connecticut, Rhode Island, Massachusetts, Vermont, New Hampshire, Delaware, and New Jersey), seventeen National Forests, one Grassland and America's Outdoors Center for Conservation, Recreation, and Resources.</li>
<li><b>Alaska</b>: based in <a href="/wiki/Juneau,_Alaska" title="Juneau, Alaska">Juneau, Alaska</a>, the Alaska Region (R10) covers one state (Alaska), and two National Forests.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Forest_or_Grassland">National Forest or Grassland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: National Forest or Grassland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._National_Forests" title="List of U.S. National Forests">List of U.S. National Forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Grassland" title="United States National Grassland">United States National Grassland</a></div>
<p>The Forest Service oversees 155 national forests and 20 grasslands. Each administrative unit typically comprises several ranger districts, under the overall direction of a forest supervisor. Within the supervisor's office, the staff coordinates activities among districts, allocates the budget, and provides technical support to each district. Forest supervisors are line officers and report to regional foresters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranger_District">Ranger District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ranger District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Forest Service has over 600 ranger districts. Each district has a staff of 10 to 100 people under the direction of a district ranger, a line officer who reports to a forest supervisor. The districts vary in size from 50,000 acres (200&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) to more than 1&#160;million acres (4,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Most on-the-ground activities occur on ranger districts, including trail construction and maintenance, operation of campgrounds, and management of vegetation and wildlife habitat.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_divisions">Major divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Major divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The U.S. Forest Service Law Enforcement &amp; Investigations unit (LEI), headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_law_enforcement_agencies" title="List of law enforcement agencies">federal law enforcement agency</a> of the U.S. government. It is responsible for enforcement of federal laws and regulations governing national forest lands and resources. All Law Enforcement Officers and Special Agents Receive their training through <a href="/wiki/Federal_Law_Enforcement_Training_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Law Enforcement Training Center">Federal Law Enforcement Training Center</a> (FLETC).</p>
<p>Operations are divided into two major functional areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Law enforcement: uniformed, high-visibility enforcement of laws</li>
<li>Investigations: <a href="/wiki/Special_agent" title="Special agent">special agents</a> who investigate crimes against property, visitors, and employees</li>
</ul>
<p>Uniformed Law Enforcement Officers (LEOs) enforce federal laws and regulations governing national forest lands and resources. LEOs also enforce some or all state laws on National Forest Lands. As part of that mission, LEOs carry firearms, defensive equipment, make arrests, execute <a href="/wiki/Search_warrant" title="Search warrant">search warrants</a>, complete reports, and testify in court. They establish a regular and recurring presence on a vast amount of public lands, roads, and recreation sites. The primary focus of their jobs is the protection of natural resources, protection of Forest Service employees and the protection of visitors. To cover the vast and varied terrain under their jurisdiction, they use <a href="/wiki/Ford_Crown_Victoria_Police_Interceptor" title="Ford Crown Victoria Police Interceptor">Ford Crown Victoria Police Interceptors</a>, special service <a href="/wiki/SUV" class="mw-redirect" title="SUV">SUVs</a>, horses, <a href="/wiki/Police_dog" title="Police dog">K-9 units</a>, helicopters, <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile" title="Snowmobile">snowmobiles</a>, dirt bikes, and boats.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Special_Agents" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Agents">Special Agents</a> are criminal investigators who plan and conduct investigations concerning possible violations of criminal and administrative provisions of the Forest Service and other statues under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">United States Code</a>. Special agents are normally plainclothes officers who carry concealed firearms, and other defensive equipment, make arrests, carry out complex criminal investigations, present cases for prosecution to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Attorneys" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Attorneys">U.S. Attorneys</a>, and prepare investigative reports. All field agents are required to travel a great deal and usually maintain a case load of ten to fifteen ongoing criminal investigations at one time. Criminal investigators occasionally conduct internal and civil claim investigations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Forest_System">National Forest System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: National Forest System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The 193&#160;million acres (780,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of public land that are managed as national forests and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> are collectively known as the National Forest System. These lands are located in 44 states, Puerto Rico, and the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> and comprise about 9% of the total land area in the United States. The lands are organized into 155 national forests and 20 national grasslands. The mission of the National Forest System is to protect and manage the forest lands so they best demonstrate the sustainable multiple-use management concept, using an ecological approach, to meet the diverse needs of people.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_and_Private_Forestry">State and Private Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: State and Private Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mission of the State and Private Forestry program is to provide technical and financial assistance to private landowners, state agencies, tribes, and community resource managers to help sustain the United States' urban and rural forests and to protect communities and the environment from wildland fires, insects, disease, and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> plants. The program employs approximately 537 staff located at 17 sites throughout the country. The delivery of the State and Private Forestry program is carried out by eight National Forest System regions and the Northeastern Area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_Development">Research and Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Research and Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The U.S. Forest Service <a href="/wiki/R%26D" class="mw-redirect" title="R&amp;D">R&amp;D</a> lab in <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Washington" title="Olympia, Washington">Olympia</a>, Washington</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> (R&amp;D) arm of the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Forest Service works to improve the health and use of the United States' forests and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>. Research has been part of the Forest Service mission since the agency's inception in 1905. Today, Forest Service researchers work in a range of biological, physical, and social science fields to promote sustainable management of United States' diverse forests and rangelands. Research employs about 550 scientists and several hundred technical and support staff, located at 67 sites throughout the United States and in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. Discovery and technology development and transfer is carried out through seven research stations.</p>
<p>Research focuses on informing policy and land management decisions and includes addressing <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive</a> insects, degraded <a href="/wiki/River_ecosystem" title="River ecosystem">river ecosystems</a>, or sustainable ways to harvest forest products. The researchers work independently and with a range of partners, including other agencies, academia, nonprofit groups, and industry. The information and technology produced through basic and applied science programs is available to the public for its benefit and use.</p>
<p>In addition to the Research Stations, the USFS R&amp;D branch also leads several National Centers such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Genomics_Center_for_Wildlife_and_Fish_Conservation" title="National Genomics Center for Wildlife and Fish Conservation">National Genomics Center for Wildlife and Fish Conservation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Programs">International Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: International Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Forest Service plays a key role in formulating policy and coordinating U.S. support for the protection and sound management of the world's forest resources. It works closely with other agencies such as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">USAID</a>, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of State">State Department</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>, as well as with nonprofit development organizations, wildlife organizations, universities, and international assistance organizations. The Forest Service's international work serves to link people and communities striving to protect and manage forests throughout the world. The program also promotes <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_land_management" title="Sustainable land management">sustainable land management</a> overseas and brings important technologies and innovations back to the United States. The program focuses on conserving key natural resource in cooperation with countries across the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although a large volume of <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">timber</a> is <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logged</a> every year, not all <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" title="United States National Forest">National Forests</a> are entirely forested. There are tidewater glaciers in the <a href="/wiki/Tongass_National_Forest" title="Tongass National Forest">Tongass National Forest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and ski areas such as <a href="/wiki/Alta,_Utah" title="Alta, Utah">Alta, Utah</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wasatch-Cache_National_Forest" title="Wasatch-Cache National Forest">Wasatch-Cache National Forest</a>. In addition, the Forest Service is responsible for managing <a href="/wiki/National_Grassland" class="mw-redirect" title="National Grassland">National Grasslands</a> in the midwest. Furthermore, areas designated as wilderness by acts of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Congress">Congress</a>, prohibit logging, mining, road and building construction and land leases for purposes of farming and or livestock grazing.</p>
<p>Since 1978, several <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">Presidents</a> have directed the USFS to administer <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. National Monument">National Monuments</a> inside of preexisting National Forests.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Admiralty_Island_National_Monument" title="Admiralty Island National Monument">Admiralty Island National Monument</a> – Alaska</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Giant_Sequoia_National_Monument" title="Giant Sequoia National Monument">Giant Sequoia National Monument</a> – California</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Misty_Fjords_National_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Misty Fjords National Monument">Misty Fjords National Monument</a> – Alaska</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens_National_Volcanic_Monument" title="Mount St. Helens National Volcanic Monument">Mount St. Helens National Volcanic Monument</a> – Washington</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Newberry_National_Volcanic_Monument" title="Newberry National Volcanic Monument">Newberry National Volcanic Monument</a> – Oregon</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_and_San_Jacinto_Mountains_National_Monument" title="Santa Rosa and San Jacinto Mountains National Monument">Santa Rosa and San Jacinto Mountains National Monument</a> – California (jointly with the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Forest Service also manages <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers National Historic Site</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Pennsylvania" title="Milford, Pennsylvania">Milford, Pennsylvania</a>, the home and estate of its first Chief, <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fighting_fires">Fighting fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Fighting fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_wildfire_suppression_in_the_United_States" title="History of wildfire suppression in the United States">History of wildfire suppression in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Smokey3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Smokey3.jpg/170px-Smokey3.jpg" width="170" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Smokey3.jpg/255px-Smokey3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Smokey3.jpg/340px-Smokey3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="660" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Smokey3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bear" title="Smokey Bear">Smokey Bear</a> poster</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In August 1944, to reduce the number of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">forest fires</a>, the Forest Service and the <a href="/wiki/Wartime_Advertising_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Wartime Advertising Council">Wartime Advertising Council</a> began distributing fire education posters featuring a <a href="/wiki/American_black_bear" title="American black bear">black bear</a>. The poster campaign was a success; the black bear would later be named "<a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bear" title="Smokey Bear">Smokey Bear</a>", and would, for decades, be the "spokesbear" for the Forest Service. Smokey Bear has appeared in innumerable TV commercials; his popular catch phrase, "Only YOU can prevent forest fires", is one of the most widely recognized slogans in the United States. A recent study found that 95% of the people surveyed could complete the phrase when given the first few words.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2000, the Departments of Agriculture and the Interior developed a plan to respond to the fires of 2000, to reduce the impacts of these wildland fires on rural communities, and to ensure sufficient firefighting resources in the future. The report is entitled "Managing the Impacts of Wildfire on Communities and the Environment: A Report to the President In Response to the Wildfires of 2000"—The National Fire Plan for short. The National Fire Plan continues to be an integral part of the Forest Service today. The following are important operational features of the National Fire Plan:</p>
<ul>
<li>Federal Wildland Fire Management Policy: The 1995 Federal Wildland Fire Management Policy and the subsequent 2001 Federal Wildland Fire Management Policy act as the foundation of the National Fire Plan.</li>
<li>Basic Premise of the National Fire Plan: Investing now in an optimal firefighting force, hazardous fuels reduction, and overall community protection will provide for immediate protection and future cost savings.</li>
<li>Funding: Initially (2001), the National Fire Plan provided for an additional $1,100,994,000 for the Forest Service for a total wildland fire management budget of $1,910,193,000. In 2008, the total amount for the Forest Service in wildland fire management (not including emergency fire suppression funding) is $1,974,276,000.</li>
</ul>
<p>In August 2014, Tom Vilsack, the Secretary of Agriculture, announced that the agency will have to put $400 to $500 million in wildfire prevention projects on hold because funding for firefighting is running low as the fiscal year ends. The decision is meant to preserve resources for fighting active fires burning in California, Oregon, Washington and Idaho. Politicians of both parties have indicated that they believe the current funding structure is broken, but they have not agreed on steps to fix the funding allocation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG/170px-Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG" width="170" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG/255px-Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG/340px-Robert_Bray_Lassie_1967.JPG 2x" data-file-width="718" data-file-height="942" /></a>

<p>The U.S. Forest Service achieved widespread awareness during the 1960s, as it became the setting for the long running classic TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Lassie_(1954_TV_series)" title="Lassie (1954 TV series)">Lassie</a></i>, with storylines focusing on Lassie's adventures with various forest rangers.<sup id="cite_ref-jcs_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jcs-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The iconic collie's association with the Forest Service led to Lassie receiving numerous awards and citations from the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> and the Department of Agriculture, and was partly responsible for a bill regarding soil and water pollution that was signed into law in early 1968 by President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a>, which was dubbed by some as "The Lassie Program".<sup id="cite_ref-jcs_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jcs-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Forest_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The history of the Forest Service has been fraught with controversy, as various interests and national values have grappled with the appropriate management of the many resources within the forests. These values and resources include <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">timber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, wildlife habitat, and <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>. Because of continuing development elsewhere, the large size of National Forests have made them de facto wildlife reserves for a number of rare and common species. In recent decades, the importance of mature forest for the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_owl" title="Spotted owl">spotted owl</a> and a number of other species led to great changes in timber harvest levels.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, the agency was involved in scandal when it illegally provided surplus military aircraft to private contractors for use as <a href="/wiki/Airtanker" class="mw-redirect" title="Airtanker">airtankers</a>. (See <a href="/wiki/U.S._Forest_Service_airtanker_scandal" title="U.S. Forest Service airtanker scandal">U.S. Forest Service airtanker scandal</a>.)</p>
<p>Another controversial issue is the policy on road building within the National Forests. In 1999, President Clinton ordered a temporary moratorium on new road construction in the National Forests to "assess their ecological, economic, and social values and to evaluate long-term options for their management."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Five and half years later, the Bush administration replaced this with a system where each state could petition the Forest Service to open forests in their territory to road building.</p>
<p>Some years the agency actually loses money on its timber sales.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
