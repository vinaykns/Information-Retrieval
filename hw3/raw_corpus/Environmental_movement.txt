<p>The <b>environmental movement</b> (sometimes referred to as the <b>ecology movement</b>), also including <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>, is a diverse scientific, social, and <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movement</a> for addressing <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental issues">environmental issues</a>. <a href="/wiki/Environmentalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmentalists">Environmentalists</a> advocate the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> management of resources and <a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a> through changes in public policy and individual behavior. In its recognition of humanity as a participant in (not enemy of) ecosystems, the movement is centered on <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>.</p>
<p>The environmental movement is an international movement, represented by a range of organizations, from the large to <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> and varies from country to country. Due to its large membership, varying and strong beliefs, and occasionally speculative nature, the environmental movement is not always united in its goals. The movement also encompasses some other movements with a more specific focus, such as the <a href="/wiki/Climate_movement" title="Climate movement">climate movement</a>. At its broadest, the movement includes private citizens, professionals, religious devotees, politicians, scientists, <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonprofit organizations">nonprofit organizations</a> and individual advocates.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_awareness">Early awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg/220px-StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg/330px-StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg/440px-StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="653" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StRolloxChemical_1831.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Levels of air pollution rose during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, sparking the first modern environmental laws to be passed in the mid-19th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early interest in the environment was a feature of the <a href="/wiki/Romantic_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic movement">Romantic movement</a> in the early 19th century. The poet <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a> had travelled extensively in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and wrote that it is a "sort of national property in which every man has a right and interest who has an eye to perceive and a heart to enjoy".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The origins of the environmental movement lay in the response to increasing levels of <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. The emergence of great factories and the concomitant immense growth in <a href="/wiki/History_of_coal_mining" title="History of coal mining">coal consumption</a> gave rise to an unprecedented level of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> in industrial centers; after 1900 the large volume of industrial <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemical</a> discharges added to the growing load of untreated human waste.<sup id="cite_ref-Donora_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donora-2">[2]</a></sup> Under increasing political pressure from the urban middle-class, the first large-scale, modern environmental laws came in the form of Britain's <a href="/wiki/Alkali_Acts" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkali Acts">Alkali Acts</a>, passed in 1863, to regulate the deleterious air pollution (<a href="/wiki/Gaseous" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaseous">gaseous</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>) given off by the <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">Leblanc process</a>, used to produce <a href="/wiki/Soda_ash" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda ash">soda ash</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_movement">Conservation movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservation movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">Conservation movement</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg/300px-Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg" width="300" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg/450px-Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg/600px-Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="790" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_William_Schlich07.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Students from the forestry school at <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, on a visit to the forests of Saxony in the year 1892</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation movement</a> was first manifested in the forests of India, with the practical application of scientific conservation principles. The conservation ethic that began to evolve included three core principles: that the human activity damaged the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, that there was a <a href="/wiki/Civic_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Civic duty">civic duty</a> to maintain the environment for future generations, and that scientific, empirically based methods should be applied to ensure this duty was carried out. Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Ranald_Martin" title="James Ranald Martin">James Ranald Martin</a> was prominent in promoting this ideology, publishing many medico-topographical reports that demonstrated the scale of damage wrought through large-scale deforestation and desiccation, and lobbying extensively for the institutionalization of forest conservation activities in <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a> through the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Forest_Service" title="Indian Forest Service">Forest Departments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Madras" class="mw-redirect" title="Madras">Madras</a> <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Revenue" title="Board of Revenue">Board of Revenue</a> started local conservation efforts in 1842, headed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gibson_(botanist)" title="Alexander Gibson (botanist)">Alexander Gibson</a>, a professional <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanist</a> who systematically adopted a forest conservation program based on scientific principles. This was the first case of state management of forests in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Eventually, the government under <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_India" title="Governor-General of India">Governor-General</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Broun-Ramsay,_1st_Marquess_of_Dalhousie" title="James Broun-Ramsay, 1st Marquess of Dalhousie">Lord Dalhousie</a> introduced the first permanent and large-scale forest conservation program in the world in 1855, a model that soon spread to <a href="/wiki/British_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="British empire">other colonies</a>, as well the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In 1860, the Department banned the use <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Francis_Cleghorn" class="mw-redirect" title="Hugh Francis Cleghorn">Dr. Hugh Cleghorn</a>'s 1861 manual, <i>The forests and gardens of South India</i>, became the definitive work on the subject and was widely used by forest assistants in the subcontinent.<sup id="cite_ref-Cleghorn_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cleghorn-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Brandis" title="Dietrich Brandis">Dietrich Brandis</a> joined the British service in 1856 as superintendent of the teak forests of Pegu division in eastern <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Burma</a>. During that time Burma's <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> forests were controlled by militant <a href="/wiki/Karen_people" title="Karen people">Karen</a> tribals. He introduced the "taungya" system,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in which Karen villagers provided labour for clearing, planting and weeding teak plantations. He formulated new forest legislation and helped establish research and training institutions. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Forestry_School_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Imperial Forestry School (India) (page does not exist)">Imperial Forest School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dehradun" title="Dehradun">Dehradun</a> was founded by him.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_environmental_protection_societies">Formation of environmental protection societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Formation of environmental protection societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The late 19th century saw the formation of the first wildlife conservation societies. The zoologist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newton" title="Alfred Newton">Alfred Newton</a> published a series of investigations into the <i>Desirability of establishing a 'Close-time' for the preservation of indigenous animals</i> between 1872 and 1903. His advocacy for legislation to protect animals from hunting during the mating season led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Plumage League</a> (later the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds) in 1889.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-11">[11]</a></sup> The society acted as a <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">protest group</a> campaigning against the use of <a href="/wiki/Great_crested_grebe" title="Great crested grebe">great crested grebe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-legged_kittiwake" title="Black-legged kittiwake">kittiwake</a> skins and feathers in <a href="/wiki/Fur_clothing" title="Fur clothing">fur clothing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup> The Society attracted growing support from the suburban middle-classes,<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones-11">[11]</a></sup> and influenced the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Birds_Preservation_Act_1869" title="Sea Birds Preservation Act 1869">Sea Birds Preservation Act</a> in 1869 as the first nature protection law in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>For most of the century from 1850 to 1950, however, the primary environmental cause was the mitigation of air pollution. The <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_UK" title="Environmental Protection UK">Coal Smoke Abatement Society</a> was formed in 1898 making it one of the oldest environmental NGOs. It was founded by artist Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Blake_Richmond" title="William Blake Richmond">William Blake Richmond</a>, frustrated with the pall cast by coal smoke. Although there were earlier pieces of legislation, the <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_Act_1875" title="Public Health Act 1875">Public Health Act 1875</a> required all furnaces and fireplaces to consume their own smoke.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg/220px-John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg" width="220" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg/330px-John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg 2x" data-file-width="370" data-file-height="437" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Ruskin_-_Portrait_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_17774.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> an influential thinker who articulated the Romantic ideal of environmental protection and conservation</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Systematic and general efforts on behalf of the environment only began in the late 19th century; it grew out of the amenity movement in Britain in the 1870s, which was a reaction to <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, the growth of cities, and worsening air and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>. Starting with the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Commons_Preservation_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Commons Preservation Society">Commons Preservation Society</a> in 1865, the movement championed rural preservation against the encroachments of industrialisation. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hunter_(National_Trust)" title="Robert Hunter (National Trust)">Robert Hunter</a>, solicitor for the society, worked with <a href="/wiki/Hardwicke_Rawnsley" title="Hardwicke Rawnsley">Hardwicke Rawnsley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octavia_Hill" title="Octavia Hill">Octavia Hill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> to lead a successful campaign to prevent the construction of railways to carry slate from the quarries, which would have ruined the unspoilt valleys of <a href="/wiki/Newlands_Valley" title="Newlands Valley">Newlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ennerdale_Water" title="Ennerdale Water">Ennerdale</a>. This success led to the formation of the Lake District Defence Society (later to become The Friends of the Lake District).<sup id="cite_ref-vc_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1893 Hill, Hunter and Rawnsley agreed to set up a national body to coordinate environmental conservation efforts across the country; the "<a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty</a>" was formally inaugurated in 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-t94_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t94-16">[16]</a></sup> The organisation obtained secure footing through the 1907 National Trust Bill, which gave the trust the status of a statutory corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the bill was passed in August 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-act_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-act-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>An early "Back-to-Nature" movement, which anticipated the romantic ideal of modern environmentalism, was advocated by intellectuals such as <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carpenter" title="Edward Carpenter">Edward Carpenter</a>, who were all against <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and other activities that were harmful to the natural world.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The movement was a reaction to the urban conditions of the industrial towns, where sanitation was awful, pollution levels intolerable and housing terribly cramped. Idealists championed the rural life as a mythical <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">Utopia</a> and advocated a return to it. John Ruskin argued that people should return to a <i>small piece of English ground, beautiful, peaceful, and fruitful. We will have no steam engines upon it . . . we will have plenty of flowers and vegetables . . . we will have some music and poetry; the children will learn to dance to it and sing it.</i><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Practical ventures in the establishment of small cooperative farms were even attempted and old rural traditions, without the "taint of manufacture or the canker of artificiality", were enthusiastically revived, including the <a href="/wiki/Morris_dance" title="Morris dance">Morris dance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maypole" title="Maypole">maypole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Walden_Thoreau.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Walden_Thoreau.jpg/220px-Walden_Thoreau.jpg" width="220" height="371" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Walden_Thoreau.jpg/330px-Walden_Thoreau.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Walden_Thoreau.jpg/440px-Walden_Thoreau.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1360" data-file-height="2292" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Walden_Thoreau.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Original title page of <i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The movement in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> began in the late 19th century, out of concerns for protecting the natural resources of the West, with individuals such as <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a> making key philosophical contributions. Thoreau was interested in peoples' relationship with nature and studied this by living close to nature in a simple life. He published his experiences in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i>, which argues that people should become intimately close with nature. Muir came to believe in nature's inherent right, especially after spending time hiking in <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">Yosemite Valley</a> and studying both the ecology and geology. He successfully lobbied congress to form <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a> and went on to set up the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> in 1892. The conservationist principles as well as the belief in an inherent right of nature were to become the bedrock of modern environmentalism. However, the early movement in the U.S. developed with a contradiction; preservationists like <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> wanted land and nature set aside for its own sake, and conservationists, such as <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a> (appointed as the first Chief of the <a href="/wiki/US_Forest_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="US Forest Service">US Forest Service</a> from 1905-1910), wanted to manage natural resources for human use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 20th century, environmental ideas continued to grow in popularity and recognition. Efforts were starting to be made to save some wildlife, particularly the <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">American bison</a>. The death of the last <a href="/wiki/Passenger_pigeon" title="Passenger pigeon">passenger pigeon</a> as well as the endangerment of the American bison helped to focus the minds of conservationists and popularize their concerns. In 1916 the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> was founded by US President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>. Pioneers of the movement called for more efficient and professional management of natural resources. They fought for reform because they believed the destruction of forests, fertile soil, minerals, wildlife and water resources would lead to the downfall of society.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Wars_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Wars-22">[22]</a></sup> The group that has been the most active in recent years is the <a href="/wiki/Climate_movement" title="Climate movement">climate movement</a>.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p><i>The conservation of natural resources is the fundamental problem. Unless we solve that problem, it will avail us little to solve all others</i>.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> (4 October 1907)<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The U.S movement did not really take off until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as people began to recognize the costs of environmental negligence, disease, and widespread air and water pollution through the occurrence of several environmental disasters that occurred post-World War II. <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> wrote "<a href="/wiki/A_Sand_County_Almanac" title="A Sand County Almanac">A Sand County Almanac</a>" in the 1940s. He believed in a land ethic that recognized that maintaining the "beauty, integrity, and health of natural systems" as a moral and ethical imperative.</p>
<p>Another important book in the promotion of the environmental movement was <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a>" about declining bird populations due to <a href="/wiki/DDT" class="mw-redirect" title="DDT">DDT</a>, an insecticide, pollution and man's attempts to control nature through use of synthetic substances. Her core message for her readers, was to identify the complex and fragile ecosystem and the threats facing the people. In 1958 Carson started to work on her last book, with an idea that nature needs human protection. Her influence was radioactive fallout, smog, food additives, and pesticide use. Carson’s main focus was on pesticides, which led her to identify nature ad fragile and the use of technology dangerous to humans and other species.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both of these books helped bring the issues into the public eye <sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Wars_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Wars-22">[22]</a></sup> Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" sold over two million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_silent_spring_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_silent_spring-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG/220px-Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG/330px-Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG/440px-Earth_Day_2007_at_City_College_San_Diego.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>

<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a> was celebrated on 22 April 1970. Its founder, former Wisconsin Senator, <a href="/wiki/Gaylord_Nelson" title="Gaylord Nelson">Gaylord Nelson</a> was inspired to create this day of environmental education and awareness after seeing the oil spill off the coast of Santa Barbara in 1969. <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> was created in 1971 as an organization that believed that political advocacy and legislation were ineffective or inefficient solutions and supported non-violent action. 1980 saw the creation of <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a>, a group with an <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">ecocentric</a> view of the world – believing in equality between the rights of humans to flourish, the rights of all other species to flourish and the rights of life-sustaining systems to flourish.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Wars_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Wars-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1950s, 1960s, and 1970s, several events illustrated the magnitude of environmental damage caused by humans. In 1954, a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bomb</a> test at <a href="/wiki/Bikini_Atoll" title="Bikini Atoll">Bikini Atoll</a> exposed the 23 man crew of the Japanese fishing vessel <i><a href="/wiki/Daigo_Fukury%C5%AB_Maru" title="Daigo Fukuryū Maru">Lucky Dragon 5</a></i> to radioactive fallout. In 1967 the oil tanker <a href="/wiki/SS_Torrey_Canyon" title="SS Torrey Canyon"><i>Torrey Canyon</i></a> ran aground off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, and in 1969 oil spilled from an offshore well in California's <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara_Channel" title="Santa Barbara Channel">Santa Barbara Channel</a>. In 1971, the conclusion of a lawsuit in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> drew international attention to the effects of decades of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a> on the people of <a href="/wiki/Minamata" class="mw-redirect" title="Minamata">Minamata</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the same time, emerging scientific research drew new attention to existing and hypothetical threats to the environment and humanity. Among them were <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>, whose book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i> (1968) revived <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Malthusian</a> concerns about the impact of exponential population growth. Biologist <a href="/wiki/Barry_Commoner" title="Barry Commoner">Barry Commoner</a> generated a debate about growth, affluence and "flawed technology." Additionally, an association of scientists and political leaders known as the <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> published their report <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i> in 1972, and drew attention to the growing pressure on natural resources from human activities.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, technological accomplishments such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear proliferation</a> and photos of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> from <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> provided both new insights and new reasons for concern over Earth's seemingly small and unique place in the universe.</p>
<p>In 1972, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a> was held in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, and for the first time united the representatives of multiple governments in discussion relating to the state of the global environment. This conference led directly to the creation of government environmental agencies and the <a href="/wiki/UN_Environment_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Environment Program">UN Environment Program</a>.</p>
<p>By the mid-1970s anti-nuclear activism had moved beyond local protests and politics to gain a wider appeal and influence. Although it lacked a single co-ordinating organization the <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear movement</a>'s efforts gained a great deal of attention, especially in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and United States.<sup id="cite_ref-eleven_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-27">[27]</a></sup> In the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">Three Mile Island accident</a> in 1979, many mass demonstrations took place. The largest one was held in New York City in September 1979 and involved 200,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-diablo_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diablo-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1970s, public awareness, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, and technology have advanced to include modern focus points like <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> depletion, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, and the potential of <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically modified organisms</a> (GMOs). Some argue that genetically modified plants and animals are inherently bad because they are unnatural. Others point out the possible benefits of GM crops such as water conservation through corn modified to be less "thirsty" and decreased pesticide use through insect - resistant crops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement_in_the_United_States" title="Environmental movement in the United States">Environmental movement in the United States</a></div>
<p>Beginning in the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation movement</a> at the beginning of the 20th century, the contemporary environmental movement's roots can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Synthetic_Environment" title="Our Synthetic Environment">Our Synthetic Environment</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i>. American environmentalists have campaigned against <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> in 1960s and 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> in the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in the 1990s, and most recently <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</p>
<p>The United States passed many pieces of environmental legislation in the 1970s, such as the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a>. These remain as the foundations for current environmental standards.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/International_Environmental_Conference_in_Stockholm" class="mw-redirect" title="International Environmental Conference in Stockholm">International Environmental Conference in Stockholm</a> in 1972 Latin American officials returned with a high hope of growth and protection of the fairly untouched natural resources. Governments spent millions of dollars, and created departments and pollution standards. However, the outcomes have not always been what officials had initially hoped. Activists blame this on growing urban populations and industrial growth. Many Latin American countries have had a large inflow of immigrants that are living in substandard housing. Enforcement of the pollution standards is lax and penalties are minimal; in Venezuela, the largest penalty for violating an environmental law is 50,000 <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_bol%C3%ADvar" title="Venezuelan bolívar">bolivar</a> fine ($3,400) and 3 days in jail. In the 1970s or 1980s many Latin American countries were transitioning from military dictatorships to democratic governments.<sup id="cite_ref-Latin_America_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Latin_America-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_Brazil#Solutions_and_policies" title="Environmental issues in Brazil">Environmental issues in Brazil §&#160;Solutions and policies</a></div>
<p>In 1992, Brazil came under scrutiny with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Environment_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Conference on Environment and Development">United Nations Conference on Environment and Development</a> in Rio de Janeiro. Brazil has a history of little environmental awareness. It has the highest <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> in the world and also the highest amount of habitat destruction. One-third of the world's forests lie in Brazil, and they have the largest river, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">The Amazon</a>, and the largest rainforest, the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>. The people have raised funds to create state parks and increase the consciousness of people who have destroyed forests and polluted waterways. They have several organizations that have fronted the environmental movement. The Blue Wave Foundation was created in 1989 and has partnered with advertising companies to promote national education campaigns to keep Brazil's beaches clean. Funatura was created in 1986 and is a wildlife sanctuary program. <a href="/wiki/Pro-Natura_International" title="Pro-Natura International">Pro-Natura International</a> is a private environmental organization created in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1952 the <a href="/wiki/Great_London_Smog" class="mw-redirect" title="Great London Smog">Great London Smog</a> episode killed thousands of people and led the UK to create the first Clean Air Act in 1956. In 1957 the first major nuclear accident occurred in Windscale in northern England. The <a href="/wiki/Torrey_Canyon_oil_spill" title="Torrey Canyon oil spill">supertanker <i>Torrey Canyon</i> ran aground</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in 1967 causing the first major oil leak that killed marine life along the coast. In 1972, in Stockholm, the United Nations Conference on the Human Environment created the <a href="/wiki/UN_Environment_Programme" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Environment Programme">UN Environment Programme</a>. The EU's environmental policy was formally founded by a <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a> declaration and the first five-year environment programme was adopted. The main idea of the declaration was that prevention is better than the cure and the polluter should pay. 1979 saw the partial meltdown of <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Mile Island">Three Mile Island</a> in the USA.</p>
<p>In the 1980s the green parties that were created a decade before began to have some political success.. In 1986, there was a nuclear accident in <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl" title="Chernobyl">Chernobyl</a>, Ukraine. The end of the 1980s and start of the 1990s saw the fall of communism across central and Eastern Europe, the fall of the [Berlin Wall], and the Union of East and West Germany. In 1992 there was a UN summit held in Rio de Janeiro where <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> was adopted. The <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> was created in 1997 which set specific targets and deadlines to reduce global greenhouse gas emissions. In the early 2000s activists believed that environmental policy concerns were overshadowed by energy security, globalism, and terrorism.<sup id="cite_ref-Europe_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Europe-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The environmental movement is reaching the less developed world with different degrees of success. The <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, has different adaptations of the environmental movement. Countries on the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> have high incomes and rely heavily on the large amount of energy resources in the area. Each country in the Arab world has varying combinations of low or high amounts of natural resources and low or high amounts of labor.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/League_of_Arab_States" class="mw-redirect" title="League of Arab States">League of Arab States</a> has one specialized sub-committee, of 12 standing specialized subcommittee in the Foreign Affairs Ministerial Committees, which deals with Environmental Issues. Countries in the League of Arab States have demonstrated an interest in environmental issue, on paper some environmental activists have doubts about the level of commitment to environmental issues;; being a part of the world community may have obliged these countries to portray concern for the environment. Initial level of environmental awareness may be the creation of a ministry of the environment. The year of establishment of a ministry is also indicative of level of engagement. Saudi Arabia was the first to establish environmental law in 1992 followed by Egypt in 1994. Somalia is the only country without environmental law. In 2010 the Environmental Performance Index listed Algeria as the top Arab country at 42 of 163; Morocco was at 52 and Syria at 56. The <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a> measures the ability of a country to actively manage and protect their environment and the health of their citizens. A weighted index is created by giving 50% weight for environmental health objective (health) and 50% for ecosystem vitality (ecosystem); values range from 0-100. No Arab countries were in the top quartile, and 7 countries were in the lowest quartile.<sup id="cite_ref-Arab_world_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arab_world-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Korea_and_Taiwan">South Korea and Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: South Korea and Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Korea and Taiwan experienced similar growth in industrialization from 1965-1990 with few environmental controls.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> South Korea's <a href="/wiki/Han_River_(Korea)" title="Han River (Korea)">Han River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nakdong_River" title="Nakdong River">Nakdong River</a> were so polluted by unchecked dumping of industrial waste that they were close to being classified as biologically dead. Taiwan's formula for balanced growth was to prevent industrial concentration and encourage manufacturers to set up in the countryside. This led to 20% of the farmland being polluted by industrial waste and 30% of the rice grown on the island was contaminated with heavy metals. Both countries had spontaneous environmental movements drawing participants from different classes. Their demands were linked with issues of employment, occupational health, and agricultural crisis. They were also quite militant; the people learned that protesting can bring results. The polluting factories were forced to make immediate improvements of the conditions or pay compensation to victims. Some were even forced to shut down or move locations. The people were able to force the government to come out with new restrictive rules on toxins, industrial waste, and air pollution. All of these new regulations caused the migration of those polluting industries from Taiwan and South Korea to China and other countries in Southeast Asia with more relaxed environmental laws.<sup id="cite_ref-Asia_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Asia-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China's environmental movement is characterized by spontaneous alliances that often only occur at the local level. The Chinese have realized the ability of riots and protests to have success and had led to an increase in disputes in China by 30% since 2005 to more than 50,000 events. Protests cover topics such as environmental issues, <a href="/wiki/Land_consumption" title="Land consumption">land loss</a>, income, and political issues. They have also grown in size from about 10 people or fewer in the mid-1990s to 52 people per incident in 2004. China has more relaxed environmental laws than other countries in Asia, so many polluting factories have relocated to China causing pollution in China. Water pollution, water scarcity, soil pollution, soil degradation, and desertification are issues currently in discussion in China. The groundwater table of the <a href="/wiki/North_China_Plain" title="North China Plain">North China Plain</a> is dropping by 1.5&#160;m (5&#160;ft) per year. This groundwater table occurs in the region of China that produces 40% of the country's grain.<sup id="cite_ref-Asia_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Asia-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental and public health is an ongoing struggle within India. The first seed of an environmental movement in India was the foundation in 1964 of <i>Dasholi Gram Swarajya Sangh</i>, a labour coperative started by <a href="/wiki/Chandi_Prasad_Bhatt" title="Chandi Prasad Bhatt">Chandi Prasad Bhatt</a>. It was inaugurated by <a href="/wiki/Sucheta_Kriplani" title="Sucheta Kriplani">Sucheta Kriplani</a> and founded on a land donated by Shyma Devi. This initiative was eventually followed up with the <a href="/wiki/Chipko_movement" title="Chipko movement">Chipko movement</a> starting in 1974.</p>
<p>The most severe single event underpinning the movement was the <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_disaster" title="Bhopal disaster">Bhopal gas leakage</a> on 3 December 1984. 40 tons of <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isocyanate" title="Methyl isocyanate">methyl isocyanate</a> was released, immediately killing 2,259 people and ultimately affecting 700,000 citizens.</p>
<p>India has a national campaign against <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepsi_Cola" class="mw-redirect" title="Pepsi Cola">Pepsi Cola</a> plants due to their practices of drawing ground water and contaminating fields with sludge. The movement is characterized by local struggles against intensive aquaculture farms. The most influential part of the environmental movement in India is the anti-dam movement. Dam creation has been thought of as a way for India to catch up with the West by connecting to the <a href="/wiki/Power_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Power grid">power grid</a> with giant dams, coal or oil-powered plants, or nuclear plants. Jhola Aandolan a mass <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">movement</a> is conducting as fighting against <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> carry bags uses and promoting cloth/jute/paper carry bags to protect environment &amp; <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>. Activists in the Indian environmental movement consider global warming, sea levels rising, and glaciers retreating decreasing the amount of water flowing into streams to be the biggest challenges for them to face in the early twenty-first century.<sup id="cite_ref-Asia_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Asia-37">[37]</a></sup> Eco Revolution movement has been started by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eco_Needs_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eco Needs Foundation (page does not exist)">Eco Needs Foundation</a> in 2008 from Aurangabad Maharashtra state .The pioneer of Eco Revolution movement is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Priyanand_Agale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Priyanand Agale (page does not exist)">Priyanand Agale</a> more than 20,000 youths associated with this movement to strengthen this movement Eco Needs Foundation organized mass environmental awareness programmes. To sought participation of children,Youths, researchers, spiritual and political leaders.Foundation had conducted International conferences at India <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eco_Revolution_2011&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eco Revolution 2011 (page does not exist)">Eco Revolution 2011</a> which concluded with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurangabad_Declaration_for_River_Conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aurangabad Declaration for River Conservation (page does not exist)">Aurangabad Declaration for River Conservation</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eco_Revolution_2012&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eco Revolution 2012 (page does not exist)">Eco Revolution 2012</a> conference was conducted in Sri Lanka which concluded with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombo_Declaration_on_spirituality_for_environmental_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Colombo Declaration on spirituality for environmental conservation (page does not exist)">Colombo Declaration on spirituality for environmental conservation</a> in collaboration with Sri Lanka government. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eco_Revolution_2013&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eco Revolution 2013 (page does not exist)">Eco Revolution 2013</a> was conducted at Nepal which concluded with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phokhara_Declaration_for_effect_of_climate_change_at_high_altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phokhara Declaration for effect of climate change at high altitude (page does not exist)">Phokhara Declaration for effect of climate change at high altitude</a> .Foundation launched the worlds first environmental social networking site <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecoface.in&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ecoface.in (page does not exist)">ecoface.in</a>. Foundation developed model of sustainable development at Dhanora village of Dholpur, Rajasthan as a <a href="/w/index.php?title=India%27s_First_smart_village&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="India's First smart village (page does not exist)">India's First smart village</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bangladesh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

He said Tipaimukh Dam project will be another "death trap for Bangladesh like the <a href="/wiki/Farakka_Barrage" title="Farakka Barrage">Farakka Barrage</a>," that would lead to an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_disaster" title="Environmental disaster">environmental disaster</a> for 50 million people in the <a href="/wiki/Meghna_River" title="Meghna River">Meghna River</a> basin. He said that this project will start <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> in Bangladesh.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">Environmental science</a> is the study of the interactions among the physical, chemical and biological components of the environment.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a>, or ecological science, is the scientific study of the distribution and abundance of living organisms and how these properties are affected by interactions between the organisms and their environment.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_focus_points">Primary focus points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Primary focus points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The environmental movement is broad in scope and can include any topic related to the environment, conservation, and biology, as well as preservation of landscapes, flora, and fauna for a variety of purposes and uses. See <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_issues" title="List of environmental issues">List of environmental issues</a>. When an act of violence is committed against someone or some institution in the name of environmental defense it is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Eco_terrorism" class="mw-redirect" title="Eco terrorism">eco terrorism</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation movement</a> seeks to protect natural areas for sustainable consumption, as well as traditional (hunting, fishing, trapping) and spiritual use.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environmental_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental conservation">Environmental conservation</a> is the process in which one is involved in conserving the natural aspects of the environment. Whether through <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, or pollution control, environmental conservation sustains the natural quality of life.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environmental_health" title="Environmental health">Environmental health</a> movement dates at least to <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Era" title="Progressive Era">Progressive Era</a>, and focuses on urban standards like clean water, efficient sewage handling, and stable population growth. Environmental health could also deal with <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preventive_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Preventive medicine">preventive medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aging" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging">aging</a>, and other concerns specific to human well-being. Environmental health is also seen as an indicator for the state of the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>, or an early warning system for what may happen to humans</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">Environmental justice</a> is a movement that began in the U.S. in the 1980s and seeks an end to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_racism" title="Environmental racism">environmental racism</a> and prevent low-income and minority communities from an unbalanced exposure to highways, garbage dumps, and factories. The Environmental Justice movement seeks to link "social" and "ecological" environmental concerns, while at the same time preventing <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a> racism, and classism. This makes it particularly adequate for the construction of labor-environmental alliances.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecology movement">Ecology movement</a> could involve the <a href="/wiki/Gaia_theory_(science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaia theory (science)">Gaia Theory</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Value_of_Earth" title="Value of Earth">Value of Earth</a> and other interactions between humans, science, and responsibility.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bright_green_environmentalism" title="Bright green environmentalism">Bright green environmentalism</a> is a currently popular sub-movement, which emphasizes the idea that through technology, good design and more thoughtful use of energy and resources, people can live responsible, sustainable lives while enjoying prosperity.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_green_environmentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Light green environmentalism (page does not exist)">Light green</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dark_green_environmentalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Dark green environmentalism">dark green environmentalism</a><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> are yet other sub-movements, respectively distinguished by seeing environmentalism as a <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyle</a> choice (light greens), and promoting reduction in human numbers and/or a <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">relinquishment of technology</a> (dark greens)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Deep_Ecology" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep Ecology">Deep Ecology</a> is an ideological spinoff of the ecology movement that views the diversity and integrity of the planetary ecosystem, in and for itself, as its primary value.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear movement</a> opposes the use of various <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_technology" title="Nuclear technology">nuclear technologies</a>. The initial anti-nuclear objective was <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">nuclear disarmament</a> and later the focus began to shift to other issues, mainly opposition to the use of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>. There have been many large anti-nuclear <a href="/wiki/Demonstration_(people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demonstration (people)">demonstrations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protest" title="Protest">protests</a>. Major <a href="/wiki/List_of_anti-nuclear_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="List of anti-nuclear groups">anti-nuclear groups</a> include <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Physicians_for_the_Prevention_of_Nuclear_War" title="International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War">International Physicians for the Prevention of Nuclear War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Information_and_Resource_Service" title="Nuclear Information and Resource Service">Nuclear Information and Resource Service</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_law_and_theory">Environmental law and theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Environmental law and theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Property_rights">Property rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Property rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many environmental lawsuits question the legal rights of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> owners, and whether the general public has a right to intervene with detrimental practices occurring on someone else's land. Environmental law organizations exist all across the world, such as the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Law_and_Policy_Center" title="Environmental Law and Policy Center">Environmental Law and Policy Center</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern" class="mw-redirect" title="Midwestern">midwestern</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citizens.27_rights">Citizens' rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Citizens' rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the earliest lawsuits to establish that citizens may sue for environmental and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> harms was Scenic Hudson Preservation Conference v. Federal Power Commission, decided in 1965 by the Second Circuit Court of Appeals. The case helped halt the construction of a power plant on <a href="/wiki/Storm_King_Mountain_(New_York)" title="Storm King Mountain (New York)">Storm King Mountain</a> in New York State. See also <a href="/wiki/United_States_environmental_law" title="United States environmental law">United States environmental law</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Sive" title="David Sive">David Sive</a>, an attorney who was involved in the case.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature.27s_rights">Nature's rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Nature's rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christopher D. Stone's 1972 essay, "Should trees have standing?" addressed the question of whether natural objects themselves should have legal rights. In the essay, Stone suggests that his argument is valid because many current rightsholders (women, children) were once seen as objects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_reactivism">Environmental reactivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Environmental reactivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Numerous criticisms and ethical ambiguities have led to growing concerns about technology, including the use of potentially harmful <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, water additives like <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, and the extremely dangerous <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>-processing plants.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">NIMBY syndrome</a> refers to public outcry caused by knee-jerk reaction to an unwillingness to be exposed to even necessary developments. Some serious <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologists</a> created the scientific <a href="/wiki/Ecology_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecology movement">ecology movement</a> which would not confuse empirical data with visions of a desirable future world.</p>

<p>Today, the sciences of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a>, in addition to any aesthetic goals, provide the basis of unity to some of the serious environmentalists. As more information is gathered in scientific fields, more scientific issues like <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, as opposed to mere aesthetics, are a concern to environmentalists. <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">Conservation biology</a> is a rapidly developing field.</p>
<p>In recent years, the environmental movement has increasingly focused on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> as one of the top issues. As concerns about <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> moved more into the mainstream, from the connections drawn between global warming and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> to <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>'s film <a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth" title="An Inconvenient Truth">An Inconvenient Truth</a>, more and more environmental groups refocused their efforts. In the United States, 2007 witnessed the largest grassroots environmental demonstration in years, <a href="/wiki/Step_It_Up_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="Step It Up 2007">Step It Up 2007</a>, with rallies in over 1,400 communities and all 50 states for real global warming solutions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many religious organizations and individual churches now have programs and activities dedicated to environmental issues.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The religious movement is often supported by interpretation of scriptures.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Major_religious_groups" title="Major religious groups">major religious groups</a> are represented including Jewish, Islamic, Anglican, Orthodox, Evangelical, Christian and Catholic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radical_environmentalism">Radical environmentalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Radical environmentalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radical_environmentalism" title="Radical environmentalism">Radical environmentalism</a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Radical environmentalism emerged from an <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">ecocentrism</a>-based frustration with the co-option of mainstream environmentalism. The radical environmental movement aspires to what scolar Christopher Manes calls "a new kind of environmental activism: iconoclastic, uncompromising, discontented with traditional conservation policy, at times illegal ..." Radical environmentalism presupposes a need to reconsider <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western</a> ideas of religion and philosophy (including <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a><sup id="cite_ref-ern_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ern-48">[48]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-jeff1_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jeff1-49">[49]</a></sup> sometimes through "resacralising" and reconnecting with nature.<sup id="cite_ref-ern_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ern-48">[48]</a></sup> Greenpeace represents an organisation with a radical approach, but has contributed in serious ways towards understanding of critical issues, and has a science-oriented core with radicalism as a means to media exposure. Groups like <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a> take a much more radical posture. Some radical environmentalist groups, like Earth First! and the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Liberation_Front" title="Earth Liberation Front">Earth Liberation Front</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eco-terrorism" title="Eco-terrorism">illegally sabotage or destroy</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrastructural capital">infrastructural capital</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_skepticism" title="Environmental skepticism">Environmental skepticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-environmentalism" title="Anti-environmentalism">Anti-environmentalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_the_environmental_movement_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Criticism of the environmental movement in the United States">Criticism of the environmental movement in the United States</a></div>
<p>Conservative critics of the movement characterize it as radical and misguided. Especially critics of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Endangered_Species_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Endangered Species Act">United States Endangered Species Act</a>, which has come under scrutiny lately, and the Clean Air Act, which they said conflict with private property rights, corporate profits and the nation's overall economic growth. Critics also <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">challenge the scientific evidence for global warming</a>. They argue that the environmental movement has diverted attention from more pressing issues.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Wars_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Wars-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
