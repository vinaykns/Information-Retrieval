			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Generation IV reactor</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:482px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GenIVRoadmap-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/GenIVRoadmap-en.svg/480px-GenIVRoadmap-en.svg.png" width="480" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/GenIVRoadmap-en.svg/720px-GenIVRoadmap-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/GenIVRoadmap-en.svg/960px-GenIVRoadmap-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="971" data-file-height="531" /></a>

Nuclear Energy Systems Deployable no later than 2030 and offering significant advances in sustainability, safety and reliability, and economics</div>
</div>

<p><b>Generation IV reactors</b> (<b>Gen IV</b>) are a set of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> designs currently being researched for commercial applications by the Generation IV International Forum, with <a href="/wiki/Technology_readiness_level" title="Technology readiness level">Technology readiness levels</a> varying between the level requiring a demonstration, to economical competitive implementation. They are motivated by a variety of goals including improved safety, sustainability, efficiency, and cost.</p>
<p>Most of these designs are generally not expected to be available for commercial construction until 2020–30.<sup id="cite_ref-Generation_IV_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generation_IV-1">[1]</a></sup> Presently the majority of reactors in operation around the world are considered <a href="/wiki/Generation_II_reactor" title="Generation II reactor">second generation reactor</a> systems, as the vast majority of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor#Classification_by_generation" title="Nuclear reactor">first generation</a> systems were retired some time ago, and there are only a dozen or so <a href="/wiki/Generation_III_reactor" title="Generation III reactor">Generation III reactors</a> in operation (2014). <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor#Generation_V.2B_reactors" title="Nuclear reactor">Generation V reactors</a> refer to reactors that are purely theoretical and are therefore not yet considered feasible in the short term, resulting in limited <a href="/wiki/R%26D" class="mw-redirect" title="R&amp;D">R&amp;D</a> funding.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Generation IV International Forum (GIF) is "a co-operative international endeavour which was set up to carry out the research and development needed to establish the feasibility and performance capabilities of the next generation nuclear energy systems."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was founded in 2001. There are currently ten active members of the Generation IV International Forum (GIF): <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Atomic_Energy_Community" title="European Atomic Energy Community">European Atomic Energy Community</a> (Euratom), <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The non-active members are <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gen-4_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen-4-3">[3]</a></sup> Switzerland joined in 2002, Euratom in 2003, and China and Russia in 2006. The remaining countries (excluding Australia) were founding members.<sup id="cite_ref-gen-4_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen-4-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 36th GIF meeting in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> was held in November 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <i>Technology Roadmap Update for Generation IV Nuclear Energy Systems</i> was published in January 2014 which details R&amp;D objectives for the next decade.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A breakdown of the reactor designs being researched by each forum member has been made available.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Australia" title="Nuclear power in Australia">Australia</a> joined the forum in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactor_types">Reactor types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactor types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many reactor types were considered initially; however, the list was downsized to focus on the most promising technologies and those that could most likely meet the goals of the Gen IV initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-Generation_IV_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Generation_IV-1">[1]</a></sup> Three systems are nominally <a href="/wiki/Thermal_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal reactor">thermal reactors</a> and three are <a href="/wiki/Fast_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast reactor">fast reactors</a>. The Very High Temperature Reactor (VHTR) is also being researched for potentially providing high quality process heat for <a href="/wiki/Thermochemical_cycle" title="Thermochemical cycle">hydrogen production</a>. The fast reactors offer the possibility of burning <a href="/wiki/Actinides" class="mw-redirect" title="Actinides">actinides</a> to further reduce waste and of being able to "<a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breed more fuel</a>" than they consume. These systems offer significant advances in sustainability, safety and reliability, economics, proliferation resistance (depending on perspective) and physical protection.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_reactors">Thermal reactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermal reactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Thermal-neutron_reactor" title="Thermal-neutron reactor">thermal reactor</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> that uses slow or <a href="/wiki/Thermal_neutrons" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal neutrons">thermal neutrons</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderator</a> is used to slow the <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> emitted by fission to make them more likely to be captured by the fuel.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Very-high-temperature_reactor_.28VHTR.29">Very-high-temperature reactor (VHTR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Very-high-temperature reactor (VHTR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg/220px-Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg.png" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg/330px-Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg/440px-Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="380" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Very_High_Temperature_Reactor.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Very-High-Temperature Reactor (VHTR)</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Very_high_temperature_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Very high temperature reactor">Very high temperature reactor</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fort_St._Vrain_Generating_Station" title="Fort St. Vrain Generating Station">Fort St. Vrain Generating Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/HTTR" title="HTTR">HTTR</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HTR-10" title="HTR-10">HTR-10</a></div>
<p>The <b>very high temperature reactor</b> concept uses a <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>-moderated core with a once-through <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> fuel cycle, using helium or molten salt as the <a href="/wiki/Very_high_temperature_reactor#Coolant" class="mw-redirect" title="Very high temperature reactor">coolant</a>. This reactor design envisions an outlet temperature of 1,000&#160;°C. The reactor core can be either a prismatic-block or a <a href="/wiki/Pebble_bed_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Pebble bed reactor">pebble bed reactor</a> design. The high temperatures enable applications such as process heat or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> production via the thermochemical <a href="/wiki/Sulfur-iodine_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulfur-iodine cycle">iodine-sulfur</a> process. It would also be <a href="/wiki/Passive_nuclear_safety" title="Passive nuclear safety">passively safe</a>.</p>
<p>The planned construction of the first VHTR, the South African PBMR (<a href="/wiki/Pebble_bed_modular_reactor" title="Pebble bed modular reactor">pebble bed modular reactor</a>), lost government funding in February, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A pronounced increase of costs and concerns about possible unexpected technical problems had discouraged potential investors and customers.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peoples_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Peoples Republic of China">Peoples Republic of China</a> began construction of a 200-MW High Temperature Pebble bed reactor in 2012 as a successor to its <a href="/wiki/HTR-10" title="HTR-10">HTR-10</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 2012, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Next_Generation_Nuclear_Plant" title="Next Generation Nuclear Plant">Next Generation Nuclear Plant</a> competition, <a href="/wiki/Idaho_National_Laboratory" title="Idaho National Laboratory">Idaho National Laboratory</a> approved a design similar to <a href="/wiki/Areva" title="Areva">Areva</a>'s prismatic block <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://us.areva.com/EN/home-3225/areva-inc-areva-htgr.html">Antares reactor</a> as the chosen <a href="/wiki/HTGR" class="mw-redirect" title="HTGR">HTGR</a> to be deployed as a prototype by 2021. It was in competition with <a href="/wiki/General_Atomics" title="General Atomics">General Atomics</a>' <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine_modular_helium_reactor" title="Gas turbine modular helium reactor">Gas turbine modular helium reactor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pebble_Bed_Modular_Reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Pebble Bed Modular Reactor">Pebble Bed Modular Reactor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-World_nuclear_news_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-World_nuclear_news-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Molten-salt_reactor_.28MSR.29">Molten-salt reactor (MSR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Molten-salt reactor (MSR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg/220px-Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg.png" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg/330px-Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg/440px-Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Molten_Salt_Reactor.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Molten Salt Reactor (MSR)</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">Molten salt reactor</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fluoride_thorium_reactor" title="Liquid fluoride thorium reactor">Liquid fluoride thorium reactor</a></div>
<p>A <b>molten salt reactor</b><sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-12">[12]</a></sup> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> where the primary <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a>, or even the fuel itself is a molten salt mixture. There have been many designs put forward for this type of reactor and a few prototypes built. The early concepts and many current ones rely on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> dissolved in the molten <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> salt as <a href="/wiki/Uranium_tetrafluoride" title="Uranium tetrafluoride">uranium tetrafluoride</a> (UF<sub>4</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Thorium_tetrafluoride" title="Thorium tetrafluoride">thorium tetrafluoride</a> (ThF<sub>4</sub>). The fluid would reach <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor_physics#Criticality" title="Nuclear reactor physics">criticality</a> by flowing into a <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> core which would also serve as the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">moderator</a>. Many current concepts rely on fuel that is dispersed in a graphite matrix with the molten salt providing low pressure, high temperature cooling.</p>
<p>The Gen IV MSR is more accurately termed an <a href="/wiki/Epithermal_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Epithermal neutron">epithermal reactor</a> than a thermal reactor due to the average speed of the neutrons that would cause the fission events within its fuel being faster than <a href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutrons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The principle of a MSR can be used for thermal, epithermal and fast reactors. Since 2005 the focus has moved towards a fast spectrum MSR (MSFR).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>While most MSR designs being pursued are largely derived from the 1960s <a href="/wiki/Molten-Salt_Reactor_Experiment" title="Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment">Molten-Salt Reactor Experiment</a>(MSRE), variants of molten salt technology include the conceptual <i><a href="/wiki/Dual_fluid_reactor" title="Dual fluid reactor">Dual fluid reactor</a></i> which is being designed with lead as a cooling medium but molten salt fuel, commonly as the metal chloride e.g <a href="/wiki/Plutonium(III)_chloride" title="Plutonium(III) chloride">Plutonium(III) chloride</a>, to aid in greater "nuclear waste" closed-fuel cycle capabilities. Other notable approaches differing substantially from those with MSRE <a href="/wiki/Pedigree" class="mw-disambig" title="Pedigree">pedigree</a> include the <i><a href="/wiki/Stable_Salt_Reactor" title="Stable Salt Reactor">Stable Salt Reactor</a></i>(SSR) concept promoted by MOLTEX, which encases the molten salt in hundreds of the common solid <a href="/wiki/Fuel_rod" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel rod">fuel rods</a> that are already well established in the nuclear industry. This latter British design was found to be the most competitive for <a href="/wiki/Small_modular_reactor" title="Small modular reactor">Small modular reactor</a> development by a British based consultancy firm <i>Energy Process Development</i> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical-water-cooled_reactor_.28SCWR.29">Supercritical-water-cooled reactor (SCWR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Supercritical-water-cooled reactor (SCWR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg/220px-Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg.png" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg/330px-Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg/440px-Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="482" data-file-height="491" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Supercritical-Water-Cooled_Reactor.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Supercritical-Water-Cooled Reactor (SCWR)</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_water_reactor" title="Supercritical water reactor">Supercritical water reactor</a></div>
<p>The <b>supercritical water reactor</b> (SCWR)<sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-12">[12]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Reduced_moderation_water_reactor" title="Reduced moderation water reactor">reduced moderation water reactor</a> concept that, due to the average speed of the neutrons that would cause the fission events within the fuel being faster than <a href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutrons</a>, it is more accurately termed an <a href="/wiki/Epithermal_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Epithermal neutron">epithermal reactor</a> than a thermal reactor. It uses <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical water</a> as the working fluid. SCWRs are basically <a href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a> (LWR) operating at higher pressure and temperatures with a direct, once-through heat exchange cycle. As most commonly envisioned, it would operate on a direct cycle, much like a boiling water reactor (<a href="/wiki/BWR" class="mw-redirect" title="BWR">BWR</a>), but since it uses supercritical water (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Critical_mass_(nuclear)" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical mass (nuclear)">critical mass</a>) as the working fluid, it would have only one water phase present, which makes the supercritical heat exchange method more similar to a pressurized water reactor (<a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">PWR</a>). It could operate at much higher temperatures than both current PWRs and BWRs.</p>
<p>Supercritical water-cooled reactors (SCWRs) are promising advanced nuclear systems because of their high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> (i.e., about 45% vs. about 33% efficiency for current LWRs) and considerable plant simplification.</p>
<p>The main mission of the SCWR is generation of low-cost <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. It is built upon two proven technologies, LWRs, which are the most commonly deployed power generating reactors in the world, and superheated <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> fired <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>, a large number of which are also in use around the world. The SCWR concept is being investigated by 32 organizations in 13 countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A SCWR Design under development is the <a href="/wiki/VVER" title="VVER">VVER</a>-1700/393 (VVER-SCWR or VVER-SKD) — a Russian Supercritical-water-cooled reactor with double-inlet-core and a <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeding ratio</a> of 0.95.<sup id="cite_ref-powertecrussia.com_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powertecrussia.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fast_reactors">Fast reactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fast reactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fast-neutron_reactor" title="Fast-neutron reactor">fast reactor</a> directly uses the fast neutrons emitted by fission, without moderation. Unlike thermal neutron reactors, fast neutron reactors can be configured to "<b>burn</b>", or fission, all <a href="/wiki/Actinides" class="mw-redirect" title="Actinides">actinides</a>, and given enough time, therefore drastically reduce the actinides fraction in <a href="/wiki/Spent_nuclear_fuel" title="Spent nuclear fuel">spent nuclear fuel</a> produced by the present world fleet of thermal neutron <a href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a>, thus closing the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">nuclear fuel cycle</a>. Alternatively, if configured differently, they can also <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor"><b>breed</b></a> more actinide fuel than they consume.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas-cooled_fast_reactor_.28GFR.29">Gas-cooled fast reactor (GFR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gas-cooled fast reactor (GFR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/220px-Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/330px-Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/440px-Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1030" data-file-height="1023" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gas-Cooled Fast Reactor (GFR)</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gas-cooled_fast_reactor" title="Gas-cooled fast reactor">Gas-cooled fast reactor</a></div>
<p>The <b>gas-cooled fast reactor</b> (GFR)<sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-12">[12]</a></sup> system features a fast-neutron spectrum and closed fuel cycle for efficient conversion of <a href="/wiki/Fertile_material" title="Fertile material">fertile uranium</a> and management of actinides. The reactor is <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>-cooled and with an outlet temperature of 850&#160;°C it is an evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Very-high-temperature_reactor" title="Very-high-temperature reactor">very-high-temperature reactor</a> (VHTR) to a more sustainable fuel cycle. It will use a direct <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> <a href="/wiki/Closed-cycle_gas_turbine" title="Closed-cycle gas turbine">gas turbine</a> for high thermal efficiency. Several fuel forms are being considered for their potential to operate at very high temperatures and to ensure an excellent retention of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">fission</a> products: composite <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> fuel, advanced fuel particles, or ceramic clad elements of actinide compounds. Core configurations are being considered based on pin- or plate-based fuel assemblies or prismatic blocks.</p>
<p>The European Sustainable Nuclear Industrial Initiative is funding three Generation IV reactor systems, one of which is a gas-cooled fast reactor, called <i>Allegro</i>, 100 MW(t), which will be built in a central or eastern European country with construction expected to begin in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-euronuclear.org_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euronuclear.org-18">[18]</a></sup> The central European <a href="/wiki/Visegr%C3%A1d_Group" title="Visegrád Group">Visegrád Group</a> are committed to pursuing the technology.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2013 German, British, and French institutes finished a 3-year collaboration study on the follow on industrial scale design, known as <i>GoFastR</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> They were funded by the EU's 7th FWP <a href="/wiki/Framework_programme" class="mw-redirect" title="Framework programme">framework programme</a>, with the goal of making a sustainable VHTR.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sodium-cooled_fast_reactor_.28SFR.29">Sodium-cooled fast reactor (SFR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sodium-cooled fast reactor (SFR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/220px-Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/330px-Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/440px-Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1030" data-file-height="738" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sodium-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pool design Sodium-Cooled Fast Reactor (SFR)</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sodium-cooled_fast_reactor" title="Sodium-cooled fast reactor">Sodium-cooled fast reactor</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Prototype_Fast_Breeder_Reactor" title="Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor">Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Experimental_Breeder_Reactor_II" title="Experimental Breeder Reactor II">Experimental Breeder Reactor II</a>, <a href="/wiki/S-PRISM" class="mw-redirect" title="S-PRISM">S-PRISM</a>, <a href="/wiki/BN-800_reactor" title="BN-800 reactor">BN-800 reactor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toshiba_4S" title="Toshiba 4S">Toshiba 4S</a></div>
<p>In India, the <a href="/wiki/Fast_Breeder_Test_Reactor" title="Fast Breeder Test Reactor">Fast Breeder Test Reactor</a> reached criticality in October 1985. In September 2002, fuel burn-up in the FBTR for the first time reached the 100,000 megawatt-days per metric ton uranium (MWd/MTU) mark. This is considered an important milestone in breeder reactor technology. Using the experience gained from the operation of the FBTR, the <a href="/wiki/Prototype_Fast_Breeder_Reactor" title="Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor">Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor</a>, a 500 MWe Sodium cooled fast reactor is being built at a cost of INR 5,677 crores (~US$900 million) and is expected to be critical by June 2016. The PFBR will be followed by six more Commercial Fast Breeder Reactors (CFBRs) of 600 MWe each.</p>
<p>The SFR<sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-12">[12]</a></sup> is a project that builds on two closely related existing projects, the liquid metal <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor#Fast_breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">fast breeder reactor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Integral_fast_reactor" title="Integral fast reactor">integral fast reactor</a>.</p>
<p>The goals are to increase the efficiency of uranium usage by <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeding</a> plutonium and eliminating the need for <a href="/wiki/Transuranic" class="mw-redirect" title="Transuranic">transuranic</a> isotopes ever to leave the site. The reactor design uses an unmoderated core running on <a href="/wiki/Fast_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast neutron">fast neutrons</a>, designed to allow any transuranic isotope to be consumed (and in some cases used as fuel). In addition to the benefits of removing the long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> transuranics from the waste cycle, the SFR fuel expands when the reactor overheats, and the chain reaction automatically slows down. In this manner, it is passively safe.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SFR reactor concept is cooled by liquid <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and fueled by a metallic alloy of uranium and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spent_nuclear_fuel" title="Spent nuclear fuel">spent nuclear fuel</a>, the "nuclear waste" of <a href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a>. The SFR fuel is contained in steel cladding with liquid sodium filling in the space between the clad elements which make up the fuel assembly. One of the design challenges of an SFR is the risks of handling sodium, which reacts explosively if it comes into contact with water. However, the use of liquid metal instead of water as coolant allows the system to work at atmospheric pressure, reducing the risk of leakage.</p>
<p>The European Sustainable Nuclear Industrial Initiative is funding three Generation IV reactor systems, one of which is a sodium-cooled fast reactor, called <i><a href="/wiki/ASTRID_(reactor)" title="ASTRID (reactor)">ASTRID</a></i>, Advanced Sodium Technical Reactor for Industrial Demonstration, <a href="/wiki/Areva" title="Areva">Areva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commissariat_%C3%A0_l%27%C3%A9nergie_atomique_et_aux_%C3%A9nergies_alternatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissariat à l'énergie atomique et aux énergies alternatives">CEA</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lectricit%C3%A9_de_France" title="Électricité de France">EDF</a> are leading the design with British collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Astrid will be rated about 600 MWe and is proposed to be built in France, near to the <a href="/wiki/Ph%C3%A9nix" title="Phénix">Phénix</a> reactor. A final decision in construction is to be made in 2019<sup id="cite_ref-euronuclear.org_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euronuclear.org-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">PRC</a>'s first commercial-scale, 800 MWe, fast neutron reactor, to be situated near <a href="/wiki/Sanming" title="Sanming">Sanming</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fujian_province" class="mw-redirect" title="Fujian province">Fujian province</a> will be a SFR. In 2009 an agreement was signed that would entail the Russian <a href="/wiki/BN-800_reactor" title="BN-800 reactor">BN-800 reactor</a> design to be sold to the PRC once it is completed, this would be the first time commercial-scale fast neutron reactors have ever been exported.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The BN-800 reactor became operational in 2014.</p>
<p>The 400 MWe <a href="/wiki/Fast_Flux_Test_Facility" title="Fast Flux Test Facility">Fast Flux Test Facility</a> operated successfully for ten years at the Hanford site in Washington State.</p>
<p>The 20 MWe <a href="/wiki/EBR_II" class="mw-redirect" title="EBR II">EBR II</a> operated successfully for over thirty years at the Idaho National Laboratory, until it was shut down in 1994.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead-cooled_fast_reactor_.28LFR.29">Lead-cooled fast reactor (LFR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lead-cooled fast reactor (LFR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/220px-Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/330px-Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg/440px-Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="930" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lead-Cooled_Fast_Reactor_Schemata.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lead-Cooled Fast Reactor (LFR)</div>
</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lead-cooled_fast_reactor" title="Lead-cooled fast reactor">Lead-cooled fast reactor</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/MYRRHA" title="MYRRHA">MYRRHA</a></div>
<p>The <b>lead-cooled fast reactor</b><sup id="cite_ref-Roadmap_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roadmap-12">[12]</a></sup> features a fast-neutron-spectrum <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> <a href="/wiki/Eutectic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lead-bismuth_eutectic" title="Lead-bismuth eutectic">LBE</a>) liquid-metal-cooled reactor with a closed <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">fuel cycle</a>. Options include a range of plant ratings, including a "battery" of 50 to 150&#160;MW of electricity that features a very long refueling interval, a modular system rated at 300 to 400&#160;MW, and a large monolithic plant option at 1,200&#160;MW. (The term <i>battery</i> refers to the long-life, factory-fabricated core, not to any provision for electrochemical energy conversion.) The fuel is metal or nitride-based containing <a href="/wiki/Fertile_material" title="Fertile material">fertile uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transuranic" class="mw-redirect" title="Transuranic">transuranics</a>. The LFR is cooled by natural <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> with a reactor outlet coolant temperature of 550&#160;°C, possibly ranging up to 800&#160;°C with advanced materials. The higher temperature enables the production of <a href="/wiki/Thermochemical_cycle" title="Thermochemical cycle">hydrogen by thermochemical processes</a>.</p>
<p>The European Sustainable Nuclear Industrial Initiative is funding three Generation IV reactor systems, one of which is a lead-cooled fast reactor that is also an <a href="/wiki/Subcritical_reactor" title="Subcritical reactor">accelerator-driven sub-critical</a> reactor, called <i><a href="/wiki/MYRRHA" title="MYRRHA">MYRRHA</a></i>, 100 MW(t), which will be built in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> with construction expected to begin after 2014 and the industrial scale version, known as <i>Alfred</i>, slated to be constructed sometime after 2017. A reduced-power model of Myrrha called <i>Guinevere</i> was started up at <a href="/wiki/Mol,_Belgium" title="Mol, Belgium">Mol</a> in March 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-euronuclear.org_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euronuclear.org-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2012 the research team reported that Guinevere was operational.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two other lead-cooled fast reactors under development are the SVBR-100, a modular 100 MWe lead-bismuth cooled fast neutron reactor concept designed by OKB <a href="/wiki/Gidropress" class="mw-redirect" title="Gidropress">Gidropress</a> in Russia and the <a href="/wiki/BREST_(Reactor)" class="mw-redirect" title="BREST (Reactor)">BREST-OD-300</a> (Lead-cooled fast reactor) 300 MWe, to be developed after the SVBR-100, and built over 2016-20, it will dispense with the <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">fertile blanket</a> around the core and will supersede the sodium cooled <a href="/wiki/BN-600_reactor" title="BN-600 reactor">BN-600 reactor</a> design, to purportedly give enhanced proliferation resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-powertecrussia.com_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powertecrussia.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Generation_IV_reactor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Relative to current nuclear power plant technology, the claimed benefits for 4th generation reactors include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Nuclear waste that remains radioactive for a few centuries instead of millennia<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>100–300 times more energy yield from the same amount of nuclear fuel<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>Broader range of fuels, and even unencapsulated raw fuels (non-pebble <a href="/wiki/Molten_Salt_Reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Molten Salt Reactor">MSR</a>, <a href="/wiki/LFTR" class="mw-redirect" title="LFTR">LFTR</a>).</li>
<li>In some reactors, the ability to consume existing nuclear waste in the production of electricity, that is, a Closed <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">nuclear fuel cycle</a>. This strengthens the argument to deem <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_as_renewable_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear power as renewable energy">nuclear power as renewable energy</a>.</li>
<li>Improved operating safety features, such as (depending on design) avoidance of pressurized operation, automatic passive (unpowered, uncommanded) reactor shutdown, avoidance of water cooling and the associated risks of loss of water (leaks or boiling) and hydrogen generation/explosion and contamination of coolant water.</li>
</ul>
<p>Nuclear reactors do not emit CO<sub>2</sub> during operation, although like all <a href="/wiki/Low_carbon_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Low carbon power">low carbon power</a> sources, the mining and construction phase can result in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, if energy sources which are not carbon neutral (such as fossil fuels), or CO<sub>2</sub> emitting cements are used during the construction process. A 2012 <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> review published in the Journal of Industrial Ecology analyzing CO<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle assessment">life cycle assessment</a> (LCA) emissions from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> determined that:<sup id="cite_ref-Warner_.2B_Heath.2C_JoIE_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Warner_.2B_Heath.2C_JoIE-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The collective LCA literature indicates that life cycle <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">GHG</a> [greenhouse gas] emissions from nuclear power are only a fraction of traditional fossil sources and comparable to renewable technologies.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Although the paper primarily dealt with data from <a href="/wiki/Generation_II_reactor" title="Generation II reactor">Generation II reactors</a>, and did not analyze the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by 2050 of the presently under construction <a href="/wiki/Generation_III_reactor" title="Generation III reactor">Generation III reactors</a>, it did summarize the Life Cycle Assessment findings of in development reactor technologies.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>FBRs ['<a href="/wiki/Fast_Breeder_Reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast Breeder Reactor">Fast Breeder Reactors</a>'] have been evaluated in the LCA literature. The limited literature that evaluates this potential future technology reports <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> life cycle GHG emissions... similar to or lower than LWRs [Gen II <a href="/wiki/Light_water_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Light water reactor">light water reactors</a>] and purports to consume little or no <a href="/wiki/Uranium_market" title="Uranium market">uranium ore</a>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>A specific risk of the sodium-cooled fast reactor is related to using metallic sodium as a coolant. In case of a breach, sodium explosively reacts with water. Fixing breaches may also prove dangerous, as the cheapest noble gas <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> is also used to prevent sodium oxidation. Argon, like helium, can displace oxygen in the air and can pose <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxia</a> concerns, so workers may be exposed to this additional risk. This is a pertinent problem as can be testified by the events at the <a href="/wiki/LMFBR" class="mw-redirect" title="LMFBR">loop type</a> <a href="/wiki/Monju_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Monju Nuclear Power Plant">Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor Monju</a> at Tsuruga, Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Using lead or molten salts mitigates this problem by making the coolant less reactive and allowing a high freezing temperature and low pressure in case of a leak. Disadvantages of lead compared to sodium are much higher viscosity, much higher density, lower heat capacity, and more radioactive neutron activation products.</p>
<p>In many cases, there is already a large amount of experience built up with numerous proof of concept Gen IV designs. For example, the reactors at <a href="/wiki/Fort_St._Vrain_Generating_Station" title="Fort St. Vrain Generating Station">Fort St. Vrain Generating Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/HTR-10" title="HTR-10">HTR-10</a> are similar to the proposed Gen IV <a href="/wiki/VHTR" class="mw-redirect" title="VHTR">VHTR</a> designs, and the <a href="/wiki/LMFBR" class="mw-redirect" title="LMFBR">pool type</a> <a href="/wiki/EBR-II" class="mw-redirect" title="EBR-II">EBR-II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ph%C3%A9nix" title="Phénix">Phénix</a> and <a href="/wiki/BN-600" class="mw-redirect" title="BN-600">BN-600</a> reactor are similar to the proposed pool type Gen IV Sodium Cooled Fast reactors being designed.</p>
