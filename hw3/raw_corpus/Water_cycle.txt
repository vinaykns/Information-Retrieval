<p>The <b>water cycle</b>, also known as the <b>hydrological cycle</b> or the <b>hydrologic cycle</b>, describes the continuous movement of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> on, above and below the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. The mass of water on Earth remains fairly constant over time but the partitioning of the water into the major reservoirs of ice, <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, saline water and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric water</a> is variable depending on a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climatic variables</a>. The water moves from one reservoir to another, such as from river to <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, or from the ocean to the atmosphere, by the physical processes of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, and subsurface flow. In doing so, the water goes through different forms: liquid, solid (<a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">vapor</a>.</p>
<p>The water cycle involves the exchange of energy, which leads to <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> changes. For instance, when water evaporates, it takes up energy from its surroundings and cools the environment. When it condenses, it releases energy and warms the environment. These heat exchanges influence <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.</p>
<p>The evaporative phase of the cycle purifies water which then replenishes the land with freshwater. The flow of liquid water and ice transports minerals across the globe. It is also involved in reshaping the geological features of the Earth, through processes including <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>. The water cycle is also essential for the maintenance of most life and ecosystems on the planet.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sun, which drives the water cycle, heats water in oceans and seas. Water evaporates as water vapor into the <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a>. Ice and snow can <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimate</a> directly into water vapour. <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">Evapotranspiration</a> is water <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpired</a> from plants and evaporated from the soil. The water vapour molecule <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>O</span> has less density compared to the major components of the atmosphere, nitrogen and oxygen, <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> and<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>. Due to the significant difference in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a>, water vapor in gas form gains height in open air as a result of <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. However, as altitude increases, air pressure decreases and the temperature drops (see <a href="/wiki/Gas_laws" title="Gas laws">Gas laws</a>). The lowered temperature causes water vapour to condense into a tiny liquid water droplet which is heavier than the air, such that it falls unless supported by an updraft. A huge concentration of these droplets over a large space up in the atmosphere become visible as cloud. Fog is formed if the water vapour condenses near ground level, as a result of moist air and cool air collision or an abrupt reduction in air pressure. Air currents move water vapour around the globe, cloud particles collide, grow, and fall out of the upper atmospheric layers as <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. Some precipitation falls as snow or hail, sleet, and can accumulate as ice caps and glaciers, which can store frozen water for thousands of years. Most water falls back into the oceans or onto land as rain, where the water flows over the ground as <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>. A portion of runoff enters rivers in valleys in the landscape, with streamflow moving water towards the oceans. Runoff and water emerging from the ground (<a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>) may be stored as freshwater in lakes. Not all runoff flows into rivers, much of it soaks into the ground as <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltration</a>. Some water infiltrates deep into the ground and replenishes <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, which can store freshwater for long periods of time. Some infiltration stays close to the land surface and can seep back into surface-water bodies (and the ocean) as groundwater discharge. Some groundwater finds openings in the land surface and comes out as freshwater springs. In river valleys and floodplains, there is often continuous water exchange between surface water and ground water in the <a href="/wiki/Hyporheic_zone" title="Hyporheic zone">hyporheic zone</a>. Over time, the water returns to the ocean, to continue the water cycle.</p>

Many different processes lead to movements and phase changes in water</div>

<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation</a></dt>
<dd>Condensed water vapor that falls to the Earth's surface. Most precipitation occurs as <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, but also includes <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fog_drip" title="Fog drip">fog drip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">graupel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">sleet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Approximately 505,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (121,000&#160;cu&#160;mi) of water falls as precipitation each year, 398,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (95,000&#160;cu&#160;mi) of it over the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Water_Cycle_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Water_Cycle-2">[2]</a></sup> The rain on land contains 107,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (26,000&#160;cu&#160;mi) of water per year and a snowing only 1,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (240&#160;cu&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 78% of global precipitation occurs over the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-science.nasa.gov_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-science.nasa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Interception_(water)" title="Interception (water)">Canopy interception</a></dt>
<dd>The precipitation that is intercepted by plant foliage eventually evaporates back to the atmosphere rather than falling to the ground.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">Snowmelt</a></dt>
<dd>The runoff produced by melting snow.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Runoff_(hydrology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Runoff (hydrology)">Runoff</a></dt>
<dd>The variety of ways by which water moves across the land. This includes both surface runoff and <a href="/wiki/Channel_runoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Channel runoff">channel runoff</a>. As it flows, the water may seep into the ground, evaporate into the air, become stored in lakes or reservoirs, or be extracted for agricultural or other human uses.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">Infiltration</a></dt>
<dd>The flow of water from the ground surface into the ground. Once infiltrated, the water becomes <a href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil moisture">soil moisture</a> or groundwater.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A recent global study using water stable isotopes, however, shows that not all soil moisture is equally available for groundwater recharge or for plant transpiration.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Subsurface_flow" title="Subsurface flow">Subsurface flow</a></dt>
<dd>The flow of water underground, in the <a href="/wiki/Vadose_zone" title="Vadose zone">vadose zone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a>. Subsurface water may return to the surface (e.g. as a spring or by being pumped) or eventually seep into the oceans. Water returns to the land surface at lower elevation than where it infiltrated, under the force of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> or gravity induced pressures. Groundwater tends to move slowly and is replenished slowly, so it can remain in aquifers for thousands of years.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">Evaporation</a></dt>
<dd>The transformation of water from liquid to gas phases as it moves from the ground or bodies of water into the overlying atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The source of energy for evaporation is primarily <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>. Evaporation often implicitly includes <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> from <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, though together they are specifically referred to as <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>. Total annual evapotranspiration amounts to approximately 505,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (121,000&#160;cu&#160;mi) of water, 434,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (104,000&#160;cu&#160;mi) of which evaporates from the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Water_Cycle_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Water_Cycle-2">[2]</a></sup> 86% of global evaporation occurs over the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-science.nasa.gov_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-science.nasa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">Sublimation</a></dt>
<dd>The state change directly from solid water (snow or ice) to water vapor.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Deposition_(phase_transition)" title="Deposition (phase transition)">Deposition</a></dt>
<dd>This refers to changing of water vapor directly to ice.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">Advection</a></dt>
<dd>The movement of water&#160;— in solid, liquid, or vapor states&#160;— through the atmosphere. Without advection, water that evaporated over the oceans could not precipitate over land.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">Condensation</a></dt>
<dd>The transformation of water vapor to liquid water droplets in the air, creating <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and fog.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">Transpiration</a></dt>
<dd>The release of water vapor from plants and soil into the air. Water vapor is a gas that cannot be seen.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">Percolation</a></dt>
<dd>Water flows vertically through the soil and rocks under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a></dt>
<dd>Water enters the mantle via subduction of oceanic crust. Water returns to the surface via volcanism.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Water cycle thus involves many of the intermediate processes.</p>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Residence_time_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Residence time (fluid dynamics)">residence time</a></i> of a reservoir within the hydrologic cycle is the average time a water molecule will spend in that reservoir (<i>see adjacent table</i>). It is a measure of the average age of the water in that reservoir.</p>
<p>Groundwater can spend over 10,000 years beneath Earth's surface before leaving. Particularly old groundwater is called <a href="/wiki/Fossil_water" title="Fossil water">fossil water</a>. Water stored in the soil remains there very briefly, because it is spread thinly across the Earth, and is readily lost by evaporation, transpiration, stream flow, or groundwater recharge. After evaporating, the residence time in the atmosphere is about 9 days before condensing and falling to the Earth as precipitation.</p>
<p>The major ice sheets - <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> - store ice for very long periods. Ice from Antarctica has been reliably dated to 800,000 years before present, though the average residence time is shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In hydrology, residence times can be estimated in two ways. The more common method relies on the principle of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">conservation of mass</a> and assumes the amount of water in a given reservoir is roughly constant. With this method, residence times are estimated by dividing the volume of the reservoir by the rate by which water either enters or exits the reservoir. Conceptually, this is equivalent to timing how long it would take the reservoir to become filled from empty if no water were to leave (or how long it would take the reservoir to empty from full if no water were to enter).</p>
<p>An alternative method to estimate residence times, which is gaining in popularity for dating groundwater, is the use of <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> techniques. This is done in the subfield of <a href="/wiki/Isotope_hydrology" title="Isotope hydrology">isotope hydrology</a>.</p>

<p>The water cycle describes the processes that drive the movement of water throughout the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>. However, much more water is "in storage" for long periods of time than is actually moving through the cycle. The storehouses for the vast majority of all water on Earth are the oceans. It is estimated that of the 332,500,000&#160;mi<sup>3</sup> (1,386,000,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of the world's water supply, about 321,000,000&#160;mi<sup>3</sup> (1,338,000,000&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) is stored in oceans, or about 97%. It is also estimated that the oceans supply about 90% of the evaporated water that goes into the water cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>During colder climatic periods more ice caps and glaciers form, and enough of the global water supply accumulates as ice to lessen the amounts in other parts of the water cycle. The reverse is true during warm periods. During the last ice age glaciers covered almost one-third of Earth's land mass, with the result being that the oceans were about 400&#160;ft (122 m) lower than today. During the last global "warm spell," about 125,000 years ago, the seas were about 18&#160;ft (5.5&#160;m) higher than they are now. About three million years ago the oceans could have been up to 165&#160;ft (50 m) higher.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scientific consensus expressed in the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) Summary for Policymakers<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> is for the water cycle to continue to intensify throughout the 21st century, though this does not mean that precipitation will increase in all regions. In subtropical land areas&#160;— places that are already relatively dry&#160;— precipitation is projected to decrease during the 21st century, increasing the probability of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>. The drying is projected to be strongest near the poleward margins of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a> (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a>). Annual precipitation amounts are expected to increase in near-equatorial regions that tend to be wet in the present climate, and also at high latitudes. These large-scale patterns are present in nearly all of the <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate model</a> simulations conducted at several international research centers as part of the 4th Assessment of the IPCC. There is now ample evidence that increased hydrologic variability and change in climate has and will continue to have a profound impact on the water sector through the hydrologic cycle, water availability, water demand, and water allocation at the global, regional, basin, and local levels.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Research published in 2012 in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> based on surface ocean salinity over the period 1950 to 2000 confirm this projection of an intensified global water cycle with salty areas becoming more saline and fresher areas becoming more fresh over the period:<sup id="cite_ref-NYTIWC_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTIWC-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Fundamental thermodynamics and climate models suggest that dry regions will become drier and wet regions will become wetter in response to warming. Efforts to detect this long-term response in sparse surface observations of rainfall and evaporation remain ambiguous. We show that ocean salinity patterns express an identifiable fingerprint of an intensifying water cycle. Our 50-year observed global surface salinity changes, combined with changes from global climate models, present robust evidence of an intensified global water cycle at a rate of 8 ± 5% per degree of surface warming. This rate is double the response projected by current-generation climate models and suggests that a substantial (16 to 24%) intensification of the global water cycle will occur in a future 2° to 3° warmer world.<sup id="cite_ref-ScienceIWC_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceIWC-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Aquarius_(SAC-D_instrument)" title="Aquarius (SAC-D instrument)">instrument</a> carried by the <a href="/wiki/SAC-D" title="SAC-D">SAC-D</a> satellite launched in June, 2011 measures global sea surface <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> but data collection began only in June, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTIWC_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTIWC-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-overview_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glacial_retreat" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial retreat">Glacial retreat</a> is also an example of a changing water cycle, where the supply of water to glaciers from precipitation cannot keep up with the loss of water from melting and sublimation. <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Glacial retreat since 1850</a> has been extensive.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_climate">Effects on climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects on climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The water cycle is powered from solar energy. 86% of the global evaporation occurs from the oceans, reducing their temperature by <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Without the cooling, the effect of evaporation on the greenhouse effect would lead to a much higher surface temperature of 67&#160;°C (153&#160;°F), and a warmer planet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Previous citation makes no mention of a theoretical surface temperature. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Aquifer <a href="/wiki/Drawdown_(hydrology)" title="Drawdown (hydrology)">drawdown</a> or <a href="/wiki/Overdrafting" title="Overdrafting">overdrafting</a> and the pumping of fossil water increases the total amount of water in the hydrosphere, and has been postulated to be a contributor to sea-level rise.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_biogeochemical_cycling">Effects on biogeochemical cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effects on biogeochemical cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the water cycle is itself a <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> flow of water over and beneath the Earth is a key component of the cycling of other biogeochemicals. Runoff is responsible for almost all of the transport of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> from land to <a href="/wiki/Waterbody" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterbody">waterbodies</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> of the oceans is derived from erosion and transport of dissolved salts from the land. Cultural <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of lakes is primarily due to phosphorus, applied in excess to <a href="/wiki/Field_(agriculture)" title="Field (agriculture)">agricultural fields</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, and then transported overland and down rivers. Both runoff and groundwater flow play significant roles in transporting nitrogen from the land to waterbodies.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">dead zone</a> at the outlet of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a> from fertilizer being carried off agricultural fields and funnelled down the <a href="/wiki/River_system" class="mw-redirect" title="River system">river system</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Runoff also plays a part in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, again through the transport of eroded rock and soil.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slow_loss_over_geologic_time">Slow loss over geologic time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slow loss over geologic time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_escape" title="Atmospheric escape">Atmospheric escape</a></div>
<p>The hydrodynamic wind within the upper portion of a planet's atmosphere allows light chemical elements such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> to move up to the <a href="/wiki/Exobase" class="mw-redirect" title="Exobase">exobase</a>, the lower limit of the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a>, where the gases can then reach <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a>, entering <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> without impacting other particles of gas. This type of gas loss from a planet into space is known as <a href="/wiki/Planetary_wind" class="mw-redirect" title="Planetary wind">planetary wind</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Planets with hot lower atmospheres could result in humid upper atmospheres that accelerate the loss of hydrogen.<sup id="cite_ref-Dvorak_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dvorak-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_hydrologic_cycle_theory">History of hydrologic cycle theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History of hydrologic cycle theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Floating_land_mass">Floating land mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Floating land mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In ancient times, it was thought that the land mass floated on a body of water, and that most of the water in rivers has its origin under the earth. Examples of this belief can be found in the works of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> (circa 800 BCE).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_of_rain">Source of rain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Source of rain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the ancient near east, Hebrew scholars observed that even though the rivers ran into the sea, the sea never became full (Ecclesiastes 1:7). Some scholars conclude that the water cycle was described completely during this time in this passage: "The wind goeth toward the south, and turneth about unto the north; it whirleth about continually, and the wind returneth again according to its circuits. All the rivers run into the sea, yet the sea is not full; unto the place from whence the rivers come, thither they return again" (Ecclesiastes 1:6-7, KJV).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup> Scholars are not in agreement as to the date of Ecclesiastes, though most scholars point to a date during the time of Solomon, the son of David and Bathsheba, "three thousand years ago,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup> there is some agreement that the time period is 962-922 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">[29]</a></sup> Furthermore, it was also observed that when the clouds were full, they emptied rain on the earth (Ecclesiastes 11:3). In addition, during 793-740 BC<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> a Hebrew prophet, Amos, stated that water comes from the sea and is poured out on the earth (Amos 5:8, 9:6).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation_and_percolation">Precipitation and percolation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Precipitation and percolation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Adityahridayam" title="Adityahridayam">Adityahridayam</a> (a devotional hymn to the Sun God) of <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a>, a Hindu epic dated to the 4th century BC, it is mentioned in the 22nd verse that the Sun heats up water and sends it down as rain. By roughly 500 BCE, Greek scholars were speculating that much of the water in rivers can be attributed to rain. The origin of rain was also known by then. These scholars maintained the belief, however, that water rising up through the earth contributed a great deal to rivers. Examples of this thinking included <a href="/wiki/Anaximander" title="Anaximander">Anaximander</a> (570 BCE) (who also speculated about the <a href="/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought" title="History of evolutionary thought">evolution of land animals from fish</a><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Xenophanes" title="Xenophanes">Xenophanes of Colophon</a> (530 BCE).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Chinese scholars such as Chi Ni Tzu (320 BC) and Lu Shih Ch'un Ch'iu (239 BCE) had similar thoughts.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The idea that the water cycle is a closed cycle can be found in the works of <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras of Clazomenae</a> (460 BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Diogenes_of_Apollonia" title="Diogenes of Apollonia">Diogenes of Apollonia</a> (460 BCE). Both <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> (390 BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (350 BCE) speculated about percolation as part of the water cycle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation_alone">Precipitation alone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Precipitation alone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Biblical <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Book of Job</a>, dated between 7th and 2nd centuries BCE,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">[29]</a></sup> there is a description of precipitation in the hydrologic cycle,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup> "For he maketh small the drops of water: they pour down rain according to the vapour thereof; Which the clouds do drop and distil upon man abundantly" (Job 36:27-28, KJV). Also found in the book of <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastes" title="Ecclesiastes">Ecclesiastes</a> "All the rivers flow into the sea, Yet the sea is not full. To the place where the rivers flow, There they flow again." (Ecclesiastes 1:7)</p>
<p>Up to the time of the Renaissance, it was thought that precipitation alone was insufficient to feed rivers, for a complete water cycle, and that underground water pushing upwards from the oceans were the main contributors to river water. <a href="/wiki/Bartholomeus_Anglicus" title="Bartholomeus Anglicus">Bartholomew of England</a> held this view (1240 CE), as did Leonardo da Vinci (1500 CE) and <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">Athanasius Kircher</a> (1644 CE).</p>
<p>The first published thinker to assert that rainfall alone was sufficient for the maintenance of rivers was <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Palissy" title="Bernard Palissy">Bernard Palissy</a> (1580 CE), who is often credited as the "discoverer" of the modern theory of the water cycle. Palissy's theories were not tested scientifically until 1674, in a study commonly attributed to <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Perrault_(scientist)" title="Pierre Perrault (scientist)">Pierre Perrault</a>. Even then, these beliefs were not accepted in mainstream science until the early nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
