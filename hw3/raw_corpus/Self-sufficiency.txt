			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Self-sufficiency</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Self-sufficiency</b> (also called <b>self-containment</b>) is the state of not requiring any aid, support, or interaction for survival; it is a type of personal or collective <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kains_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kains-1">[1]</a></sup> On a national scale, a totally self-sufficient <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> that does not trade with the outside world is called an <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a>.</p>
<p>The term self-sufficiency is usually applied to varieties of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">sustainable living</a> in which nothing is consumed other than what is produced by the self-sufficient individuals. Examples of attempts at self-sufficiency in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> include <a href="/wiki/Simple_living" title="Simple living">simple living</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homesteading" title="Homesteading">homesteading</a>, <a href="/wiki/Off-the-grid" title="Off-the-grid">off-the-grid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Survivalism" title="Survivalism">survivalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/DIY_ethic" title="DIY ethic">DIY ethic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Back-to-the-land_movement" title="Back-to-the-land movement">back-to-the-land movement</a>.</p>
<p>Practices that enable or aid self-sufficiency include <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_building" title="Autonomous building">autonomous building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. The term is also applied to limited forms of self-sufficiency, for example growing one's own food or becoming economically independent of state <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidies</a>. The self-sufficiency of an electrical installation measures its degree of grid independence and is defined as the ratio between the amount of locally produced energy that is locally consumed, either directly or after storage, and the total consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Silva-Hendrick_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silva-Hendrick-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
