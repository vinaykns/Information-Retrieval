			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Nitrogen oxide</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_oxides&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">Nitrogen oxides</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Nitrogen oxide</b> may refer to a <a href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, or a mixture of such compounds:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">Nitric oxide</a>, also known as nitrogen monoxide (NO), nitrogen(II) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">Nitrogen dioxide</a> (NO<sub>2</sub>), nitrogen(IV) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O), nitrogen(−I,III) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrosylazide" title="Nitrosylazide">Nitrosylazide</a> (N<sub>4</sub>O), nitrogen(−I,0,I,II) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxatetrazole" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxatetrazole">Oxatetrazole</a> (N<sub>4</sub>O)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_trioxide" title="Dinitrogen trioxide">Dinitrogen trioxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>), nitrogen(II,IV) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">Dinitrogen tetroxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), nitrogen(IV) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_pentoxide" title="Dinitrogen pentoxide">Dinitrogen pentoxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>), nitrogen(V) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trinitramide" title="Trinitramide">Trinitramide</a> (N(NO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub> or N<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>), nitrogen(0,IV) oxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_dinitramide" title="Ammonium dinitramide">Dinitramide</a> (N(NO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">Nitrite</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br />
2</span></span>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">Nitrate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br />
3</span></span>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitronium" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitronium">Nitronium</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br />
2</span></span>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">Nitrosonium</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br /></span></span>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peroxynitrite" title="Peroxynitrite">peroxonitrite</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">ONO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br />
2</span></span>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The compounds NO and NO<sub>2</sub> are <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radicals</a>. In addition, there are several anions of nitrogen oxides. The most stable of these is the nitrate anion:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">Nitrate</a> (NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>), trioxonitrate(V) ion</li>
</ul>
<p>but other nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Oxoanions" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxoanions">oxoanions</a> include <a href="/wiki/Hyponitrite" title="Hyponitrite">hyponitrite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peroxynitrite" title="Peroxynitrite">peroxonitrite</a>, trioxodinitrate (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyponitrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hyponitrate (page does not exist)">hyponitrate</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Nitroxylate" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitroxylate">nitroxylate</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and related fields, <b>nitrogen oxides</b> refers specifically to <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a> (NO and NO<sub>2</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only the first three of these compounds can be isolated at room temperature. N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> all decompose rapidly at room temperature. NO<sub>3</sub>, N<sub>4</sub>O, and N(NO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub> are very reactive.</p>
<p>N<sub>2</sub>O is stable and rather unreactive at room temperature, while NO and NO<sub>2</sub> are quite reactive but nevertheless quite stable when isolated.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NOx">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: NOx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a></div>
<p>NO<sub><i>x</i></sub> (often written NOx) refers to <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">NO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">NO<sub>2</sub></a>. They are produced during <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, especially at high temperature. These two chemicals are important trace species in Earth's atmosphere. In the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, during daylight, NO reacts with partly oxidised organic species (or the <a href="/wiki/Hydroperoxyl" title="Hydroperoxyl">peroxy radical</a>) to form NO<sub>2</sub>, which is then <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation" title="Photodissociation">photolysed</a> by sunlight to reform NO:</p>
<dl>
<dd>NO + CH<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> → NO<sub>2</sub> + CH<sub>3</sub>O</dd>
<dd>NO<sub>2</sub> + sunlight → NO + O</dd>
</dl>
<p>The oxygen <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> formed in the second reaction then goes on to form <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>; this series of reactions is the main source of <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">tropospheric ozone</a>. CH<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is just one example of many partly oxidised organic molecules that can react with NO to form NO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>These reactions are rather fast, so NO and NO<sub>2</sub> tend to cycle, but the sum of their concentration ([NO] + [NO<sub>2</sub>]) tends to remain fairly constant. Because of this cycling, it is convenient to think of the two chemicals as a group; hence the term NO<sub><i>x</i></sub>.</p>
<p>In addition to acting as a main precursor for <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">tropospheric ozone</a>, NO<sub><i>x</i></sub> is also harmful to human health in its own right.</p>
<p>NO<sub><i>x</i></sub> may react with water to make <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, which may end up in the soil, where it makes <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, which is of use to growing plants.</p>
