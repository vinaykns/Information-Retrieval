			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Overfishing</h1>

<p><b>Overfishing</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> where <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> are reduced to below acceptable levels. Overfishing can occur in water bodies of any sizes, such as ponds, rivers, lakes or oceans, and can result in <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>, reduced biological growth rates and low <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> levels. Sustained overfishing can lead to <a href="/wiki/Critical_depensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical depensation">critical depensation</a>, where the fish population is no longer able to sustain itself. Some forms of overfishing, for example the <a href="/wiki/Threatened_sharks" class="mw-redirect" title="Threatened sharks">overfishing of sharks</a>, has led to the upset of entire marine ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ability of a <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> to recover from overfishing depends on whether the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>'s conditions are suitable for the recovery. Dramatic changes in species composition can result in an ecosystem shift, where other equilibrium energy flows involve species compositions different from those that had been present before the depletion of the original fish stock. For example, once trout have been overfished, carp might take over in a way that makes it impossible for the trout to re-establish a breeding population.</p>
<p>Overfishing occurs when more fish are caught than the population can replace through natural reproduction. Gathering as many fish as possible may seem like a profitable practice, but overfishing has serious consequences. The results not only affect the balance of life in the oceans, but also the social and economic well-being of the coastal communities who depend on fish for their way of life.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_overfishing">Global overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Global overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/350px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" width="350" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/525px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/700px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5433" data-file-height="3675" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Fishing_down_the_food_web" title="Fishing down the food web">Fishing down the food web</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Overfishing has significantly affected many fisheries around the world. As much as 85% of the world's fisheries may be <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">over-exploited</a>, depleted, fully exploited or in recovery from exploitation.<sup id="cite_ref-gaiavince_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gaiavince-2">[2]</a></sup> Significant overfishing has been observed in pre-industrial times. In particular, the overfishing of the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> from the earliest days of <a href="/wiki/European_colonisation_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="European colonisation of the Americas">European colonisation of the Americas</a> has been well documented.<sup id="cite_ref-Bolster_2012_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bolster_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> Following World War Two, industrial fishing rapidly expanded with rapid increases in worldwide fishing catches. However, many fisheries have either collapsed or degraded to a point where increased catches are no longer possible.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries scientist">fisheries scientist</a> known for pioneering work on the human impacts on global fisheries, has commented:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is almost as though we use our military to fight the animals in the ocean. We are gradually winning this war to exterminate them. And to see this destruction happen, for nothing really – for no reason – that is a bit frustrating. Strangely enough, these effects are all reversible, all the animals that have disappeared would reappear, all the animals that were small would grow, all the relationships that you can't see any more would re-establish themselves, and the system would re-emerge.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_overfishing">Examples of overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples of overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of overfishing exist in areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_the_North_Sea" title="Fishing in the North Sea">North Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland" title="Grand Banks of Newfoundland">Grand Banks of Newfoundland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_China_Sea" title="East China Sea">East China Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In these locations, overfishing has not only proved disastrous to fish stocks but also to the fishing communities relying on the harvest. Like other <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary sector of industry">extractive industries</a> such as forestry and hunting, fisheries are susceptible to economic interaction between ownership or stewardship and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, otherwise known as the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li>The Peruvian coastal <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_anchoveta" title="Peruvian anchoveta">anchovy</a> fisheries crashed in the 1970s after overfishing and an <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> season<sup id="cite_ref-peru_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peru-6">[6]</a></sup> largely depleted anchovies from its waters.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Anchovies were a major natural resource in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>; indeed, 1971 alone yielded 10.2 million metric tons of anchovies. However, the following five years saw the Peruvian fleet's catch amount to only about 4 million tons.<sup id="cite_ref-peru_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peru-6">[6]</a></sup> This was a major loss to Peru's economy.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> fishery off <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the 1992 decision by <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to impose an indefinite <a href="/wiki/Moratorium_(law)" title="Moratorium (law)">moratorium</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a>, is a dramatic example of the consequences of overfishing.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Solea_solea" class="mw-redirect" title="Solea solea">sole</a> fisheries in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>, the west <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>, and other locations have become overfished to the point of virtual collapse, according to the UK government's official <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a>. The United Kingdom has created elements within this plan to attempt to restore this fishery, but <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">the expanding global human population</a> and the expanding demand for fish has reached a point where demand for food threatens the stability of these fisheries, if not the species' survival.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Many <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea_fish" title="Deep sea fish">deep sea fish</a> are at risk, such as <a href="/wiki/Orange_roughy" title="Orange roughy">orange roughy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patagonian_toothfish" title="Patagonian toothfish">Patagonian toothfish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sablefish" title="Sablefish">sablefish</a>. The deep sea is almost completely dark, near freezing and has little food. Deep sea fish grow slowly because of limited food, have slow metabolisms, low reproductive rates, and many don't reach breeding maturity for 30 to 40 years. A fillet of orange roughy at the store is probably at least 50 years old. Most deep sea fish are in international waters, where there are no legal protections. Most of these fish are caught by <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">deep trawlers</a> near <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a>, where they congregate because of food. <a href="/wiki/Flash_freezing" title="Flash freezing">Flash freezing</a> allows the trawlers to work for days at a time, and modern <a href="/wiki/Fishfinder" title="Fishfinder">fishfinders</a> target the fish with ease.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blue_walleye" title="Blue walleye">Blue walleye</a> became extinct in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> in the 1980s. Until the middle of the 20th century, it was a commercially valuable fish, with about a half million tonnes being landed during the period from about 1880 to the late 1950s, when the populations collapsed, apparently through a combination of overfishing, <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Competition#Competition_in_biology_and_ecology" title="Competition">competition</a> with the introduced <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_smelt" title="Rainbow smelt">rainbow smelt</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a> jointly issued their "Living Blue Planet Report" on 16 September 2015 which states that there was a dramatic fall of 74% in worldwide stocks of the important scombridae fish such as <a href="/wiki/Mackerel" title="Mackerel">mackerel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bonito" title="Bonito">bonitos</a> between 1970 and 2010, and the global overall "population sizes of mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians and fish fell by half on average in just 40 years."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_good_fisheries_management">Examples of good fisheries management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples of good fisheries management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several countries are now effectively managing their fisheries. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gaiavince_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gaiavince-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> has turned many of its fisheries around from being in a highly depleted state.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/300px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" width="300" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/450px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/600px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="942" data-file-height="738" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> stocks were severely overfished in the 1970s and 1980s, leading to their abrupt collapse in 1992</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to a 2008 UN report, the world's fishing fleets are losing US$50 billion each year through depleted stocks and poor <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a>. The report, produced jointly by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and the UN <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), asserts that half the world's <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleet</a> could be scrapped with no change in catch. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> of global <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> have been allowed to run down to the point where it is no longer possible to catch the amount of fish that could be caught.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Increased incidence of <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a> in Africa has been linked to declines of fish species that eat the snails carrying the disease-causing parasites.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Massive growth of <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> populations threaten fish stocks, as they compete with fish for food, eat fish eggs, and poison or swarm fish, and can survive in oxygen depleted environments where fish cannot; they wreak massive havoc on commercial fisheries. Overfishing eliminates a major jellyfish competitor and predator exacerbating the jellyfish population explosion.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Both climate change and a restructuring of the ecosystem have been found to be major roles in an increase in jellyfish population in the Irish Sea in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three recognized types of biological overfishing: growth overfishing, recruit overfishing and ecosystem overfishing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_overfishing">Growth overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Growth overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Growth overfishing occurs when fish are harvested at an average size that is smaller than the size that would produce the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">maximum yield</a> per recruit. A <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries#Recruitment" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">recruit</a> is an individual that makes it to maturity, or into the limits specified by a fishery, which are usually size or age.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This makes the total yield less than it would be if the fish were allowed to grow to an appropriate size. It can be countered by reducing fishing mortality to lower levels and increasing the average size of harvested fish to a size that will allow maximum yield per recruit.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauly_1983_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauly_1983-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recruitment_overfishing">Recruitment overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recruitment overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recruitment overfishing occurs when the mature adult population (spawning <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>) is depleted to a level where it no longer has the reproductive capacity to replenish itself—there are not enough adults to produce offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauly_1983_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauly_1983-20">[20]</a></sup> Increasing the spawning stock biomass to a target level is the approach taken by managers to restore an overfished population to sustainable levels. This is generally accomplished by placing moratoriums, <a href="/wiki/Individual_fishing_quota" title="Individual fishing quota">quotas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minimum_landing_size" title="Minimum landing size">minimum size</a> limits on a fish population.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_overfishing">Ecosystem overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ecosystem overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecosystem overfishing occurs when the balance of the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> is altered by overfishing. With declines in the abundance of large predatory species, the abundance of small <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage type</a> increases causing a shift in the balance of the ecosystem towards smaller fish species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acceptable_levels">Acceptable levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Acceptable levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The notion of overfishing hinges on what is meant by an <b>acceptable level</b> of fishing. More precise <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioeconomics_(fisheries)" title="Bioeconomics (fisheries)">bioeconomic</a> terms define acceptable level as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Biological overfishing</b> occurs when fishing <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality</a> has reached a level where the stock <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> has negative <a href="/wiki/Marginalism" title="Marginalism">marginal growth</a> (reduced rate of biomass growth), as indicated by the red area in the figure. (Fish are being taken out of the water so quickly that the replenishment of stock by breeding slows down. If the replenishment continues to diminish for long enough, replenishment will go into reverse and the population will decrease.)<sup id="cite_ref-Tropical_Fisheries_Management_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tropical_Fisheries_Management-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Economic</b> or <b>bioeconomic overfishing</b> additionally considers the cost of fishing when determining acceptable catches. Under this framework, a fishery is considered to be overfished when catches exceed <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Glossary of fisheries">maximum economic yield</a> where <a href="/wiki/Resource_rent" title="Resource rent">resource rent</a> is at its maximum. Fish are being removed from the fishery so quickly that the profitability of the fishery is sub-optimal. A more dynamic definition of <b>economic overfishing</b> also considers the <a href="/wiki/Present_value" title="Present value">present value</a> of the fishery using a relevant <a href="/wiki/Discount_window" title="Discount window">discount rate</a> to maximise the flow of resource rent over all future catches.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HCR.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/HCR.gif/300px-HCR.gif" width="300" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/HCR.gif/450px-HCR.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/06/HCR.gif 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="425" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvest_control_rule">Harvest control rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Harvest control rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A model proposed in 2010 for predicting acceptable levels of fishing is the Harvest Control Rule (HCR),<sup id="cite_ref-HCR_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCR-23">[23]</a></sup> which is a set of tools and protocols with which management has some direct control of harvest rates and strategies in relation to predicting stock status, and long-term maximum sustainable yields. Constant catch and constant fishing mortality are two types of simple harvest control rules.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_and_output_orientations">Input and output orientations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Input and output orientations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fishing_capacity" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing capacity">Fishing capacity</a> can also be defined using an input or output orientation.</p>
<ul>
<li>An input-oriented fishing capacity is defined as the maximum available <a href="/wiki/Capital_stock" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital stock">capital stock</a> in a fishery that is fully utilized at the maximum technical efficiency in a given time period, given resource and market conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>An output-oriented fishing capacity is defined as the maximum catch a vessel (fleet) can produce if inputs are fully utilized given the biomass, the fixed inputs, the age structure of the fish stock, and the present stage of technology.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Technical efficiency of each vessel of the fleet is assumed necessary to attain this maximum catch. The degree of <a href="/wiki/Capacity_utilization" title="Capacity utilization">capacity utilization</a> results from the comparison of the actual level of output (input) and the capacity output (input) of a vessel or a fleet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="this paragraph is not understandable by those not versed in the esoterica of scientific literature; (May 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With present and forecast world population levels it is not possible to solve the over fishing issue;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> however, there are mitigation measures that can save selected fisheries and forestall the collapse of others.</p>
<p>In order to meet the problems of overfishing, a precautionary approach and Harvest Control Rule (HCR) management principles have been introduced in the main fisheries around the world. The Traffic Light color convention introduces sets of rules based on predefined critical values, which could be adjusted as more information is gained.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a> treaty deals with aspects of over fishing in articles 61, 62, and 65.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Article 61 requires all coastal states to ensure that the maintenance of living resources in their <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a> is not endangered by <a href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-exploitation">over-exploitation</a>. The same article addresses the maintenance or restoration of populations of species above levels at which their reproduction may become seriously threatened.</li>
<li>Article 62 provides that coastal states: "shall promote the objective of optimum utilization of the living resources in the exclusive economic zone without prejudice to Article 61"</li>
<li>Article 65 provides generally for the rights of, inter alia, coastal states to prohibit, limit, or regulate the exploitation of marine mammals.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to some observers, overfishing can be viewed as an example of the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>; appropriate solutions would therefore promote <a href="/wiki/Property_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Property right">property rights</a> through, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farming</a>. Daniel K. Benjamin, in <i>Fisheries are Classic Example of the "Tragedy of the Commons"</i>, cites research by Grafton, Squires and Fox to support the idea that privatization can solve the overfishing problem:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>According to recent research on the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> <a href="/wiki/Halibut" title="Halibut">halibut</a> fishery, where the commons has been at least partly privatized, substantial ecological and economic benefits have resulted. There is less damage to fish stocks, the fishing is safer, and fewer resources are needed to achieve a given harvest.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Another possible solution, at least for some areas, is <a href="/wiki/Fishing_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing quota">quotas</a>, so fishermen can only legally take a certain amount of fish. A more radical possibility is declaring certain areas of the sea "<a href="/wiki/Exclusion_zone" title="Exclusion zone">no-go zones</a>" and make fishing there strictly illegal, so the fish in that area have time to recover and repopulate.</p>
<p>Controlling consumer behavior and demand is a key in mitigating action. Worldwide, a number of initiatives emerged to provide consumers with information regarding the conservation status of the seafood available to them. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://overfishing.org/pages/guide_to_good_fish.php">Guide to Good Fish Guides</a> lists a number of these.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_regulation">Government regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Government regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_law" title="Fisheries law">Fisheries law</a></div>
<p>Many regulatory measures are available for controlling overfishing. These measures include <a href="/wiki/Fishing_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing quota">fishing quotas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bag_limits" title="Bag limits">bag limits</a>, licensing, <a href="/wiki/Closed_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Closed season">closed seasons</a>, size limits and the creation of <a href="/wiki/Marine_reserve" title="Marine reserve">marine reserves</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Marine_protected_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine protected areas">marine protected areas</a>.</p>
<p>A model of the interaction between fish and fishers showed that when an area is closed to fishers, but there are no catch regulations such as <a href="/wiki/Individual_transferable_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Individual transferable quota">individual transferable quotas</a>, fish catches are temporarily increased but overall fish <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> is reduced, resulting in the opposite outcome from the one desired for fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Thus, a displacement of the fleet from one locality to another will generally have little effect if the same quota is taken. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">management measures</a> such as temporary closures or establishing a <a href="/wiki/Marine_protected_area" title="Marine protected area">marine protected area</a> of fishing areas are ineffective when not combined with individual fishing quotas. An inherent problem with quotas is that fish populations vary from year to year. A study has found that fish populations rise dramatically after stormy years due to more nutrients reaching the surface and therefore greater primary production.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> To fish sustainably, quotas need to be changed each year to account for fish population.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Individual_fishing_quota" title="Individual fishing quota">Individual transferable quotas</a> (ITQs) are fishery rationalization instruments defined under the <a href="/wiki/Magnuson-Stevens_Fishery_Conservation_and_Management_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act">Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act</a> as limited access permits to harvest quantities of fish. <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">Fisheries scientists</a> decide the optimal amount of fish (<a href="/wiki/Total_Allowable_Catch" class="mw-redirect" title="Total Allowable Catch">total allowable catch</a>) to be harvested in a certain fishery. The decision considers carrying capacity, regeneration rates and future values. Under ITQs, members of a <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> are granted rights to a percentage of the total allowable catch that can be harvested each year. These quotas can be fished, bought, sold, or leased allowing for the least cost vessels to be used. ITQs are used in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Only three ITQ programs have been implemented in the United States due to a <a href="/wiki/Moratorium_(law)" title="Moratorium (law)">moratorium</a> supported by <a href="/wiki/Ted_Stevens" title="Ted Stevens">Ted Stevens</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, a large-scale study of fisheries that used ITQs compared to ones that didn't provided strong evidence that ITQs can help to prevent collapses and restore fisheries that appear to be in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>China bans fishing in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> for a period each year.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Removal_of_subsidies">Removal of subsidies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Removal of subsidies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several scientists have called for an end to subsidies paid to deep sea fisheries. In international waters beyond the 200 nautical mile <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zones" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusive economic zones">exclusive economic zones</a> of coastal countries, many fisheries are unregulated, and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleets</a> plunder the depths with state-of-the-art technology. In a few hours, massive nets weighing up to 15 tons, dragged along the bottom by <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">deep-water trawlers</a>, can destroy deep-sea corals and sponge beds that have taken centuries or millennia to grow. The trawlers can target <a href="/wiki/Orange_roughy" title="Orange roughy">orange roughy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rattail" class="mw-redirect" title="Rattail">grenadiers</a>, or sharks. These fish are usually long-lived and late maturing, and their populations take decades, even centuries to recover.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_2007_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS_2007-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fisheries scientist <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a> and economist <a href="/wiki/Ussif_Rashid_Sumaila" title="Ussif Rashid Sumaila">Ussif Rashid Sumaila</a> have examined subsidies paid to <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawl" class="mw-redirect" title="Bottom trawl">bottom trawl</a> fleets around the world. They found that US$152 million per year are paid to deep-sea fisheries. Without these subsidies, global deep-sea fisheries would operate at a loss of $50 million a year. A great deal of the subsidies paid to deep-sea trawlers is to subsidize the large amount of fuel required to travel beyond the 200-mile limit and drag weighted nets.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_2007_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS_2007-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"There is surely a better way for governments to spend money than by paying subsidies to a fleet that burns 1.1 billion litres of fuel annually to maintain paltry catches of old growth fish from highly vulnerable stocks, while destroying their habitat in the process" – <i>Pauly</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_2007_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS_2007-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Eliminating global subsidies would render these fleets economically unviable and would relieve tremendous pressure on over-fishing and vulnerable deep-sea ecosystems" – <i>Sumaila</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_2007_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS_2007-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minimizing_fishing_impact">Minimizing fishing impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Minimizing fishing impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fishing techniques may be altered to minimize bycatch and reduce impacts on marine habitats. These techniques include using varied gear types depending on target species and habitat type. For example, a net with larger holes will allow undersized fish to avoid capture. A <a href="/wiki/Turtle_excluder_device" title="Turtle excluder device">turtle excluder device</a> (TED) allows sea turtles and other megafauna to escape from shrimp trawls. Avoiding fishing in spawning grounds may allow fish stocks to rebuild by giving adults a chance to reproduce.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Aquaculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/w/index.php?title=File:Global_total_fish_harvest.svg&amp;lang=en" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Global_total_fish_harvest.svg/220px-Global_total_fish_harvest.svg.png" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Global_total_fish_harvest.svg/330px-Global_total_fish_harvest.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Global_total_fish_harvest.svg/440px-Global_total_fish_harvest.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="490" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_total_fish_harvest.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Global harvest of aquatic organisms in million tonnes, 1950–2010, as reported by the FAO.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Aquaculture involves the farming of fish in captivity. This approach effectively privatizes fish stocks and creates incentives for farmers to conserve their stocks. It also reduces environmental impact. However, farming <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnivorous fish">carnivorous fish</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, does not always reduce pressure on wild fisheries, since carnivorous farmed fish are usually fed <a href="/wiki/Fishmeal" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishmeal">fishmeal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_oil" title="Fish oil">fish oil</a> extracted from wild <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a>.</p>
<p>Aquaculture played a minor role in the harvesting of marine organisms until the 1970s. Growth in aquaculture increased rapidly in 1990s when the rate of wild capture plateaued. Aquaculture now provides approximately half of all harvested aquatic organisms. Aquaculture production rates continue to grow while wild harvest remains steady.</p>
<p>Fish farming can enclose the entire <a href="/wiki/Breeding_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding cycle">breeding cycle</a> of the fish, with fish being bred in captivity. Some fish prove difficult to breed in captivity and can be caught in the wild as juveniles and brought into captivity to increase their weight. With scientific progress more species are being made to breed in captivity. This was the case with <a href="/wiki/Southern_bluefin_tuna" title="Southern bluefin tuna">southern bluefin tuna</a>, which were first bred in captivity in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumer_awareness">Consumer awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Consumer awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_seafood" title="Sustainable seafood">Sustainable seafood</a></div>
<p>Sustainable seafood is a movement that has gained momentum as more people become aware of overfishing and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_fishing" title="Environmental impact of fishing">environmentally destructive fishing methods</a>. Sustainable seafood is <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> from either fished or farmed sources that can maintain or increase production in the future without jeopardizing the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> from which it was acquired. In general, slow-growing fish that reproduce late in life, such as orange roughy, are vulnerable to overfishing. Seafood species that grow quickly and breed young, such as anchovies and sardines, are much more resistant to overfishing. Several organizations, including the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Stewardship_Council" title="Marine Stewardship Council">Marine Stewardship Council</a> (MSC), and <a href="/wiki/Friend_of_the_Sea" title="Friend of the Sea">Friend of the Sea</a>, certify seafood fisheries as sustainable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Marine Stewardship Council has developed an environmental standard for sustainable and well-managed fisheries. Environmentally responsible fisheries management and practices are rewarded with the use of its blue product <a href="/wiki/Ecolabel" title="Ecolabel">ecolabel</a>. Consumers concerned about overfishing and its consequences are increasingly able to choose seafood products that have been independently assessed against the MSC's environmental standard. This enables consumers to play a part in reversing the decline of fish stocks. As of February 2012, over 100 fisheries around the world have been independently assessed and certified as meeting the MSC standard. Their <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.msc.org/where-to-buy/">where-to-buy</a> page lists the currently available certified seafood. As of February 2012 over 13,000 MSC-labelled products are available in 74 countries around the world. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fishandkids.org/">Fish &amp; Kids</a> is an MSC project to teach schoolchildren about marine environmental issues, including overfishing.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium">Monterey Bay Aquarium</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Seafood_Watch" title="Seafood Watch">Seafood Watch</a> Program, although not an official certifying body like the MSC, also provides guidance on the sustainability of certain fish species.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Some seafood restaurants have begun to offer more sustainable seafood options. The <a href="/wiki/Seafood_Choices_Alliance" title="Seafood Choices Alliance">Seafood Choices Alliance</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> is an organization whose members include chefs that serve sustainable seafood at their establishments. In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishery" title="Sustainable fishery">Sustainable Fisheries</a> Act defines sustainable practices through national standards. Although there is no official certifying body like the MSC, the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> has created <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fishwatch.gov/">FishWatch</a> to help guide concerned consumers to sustainable seafood choices. See also a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://overfishing.org/pages/guide_to_good_fish.php">guide to good fish guides</a>.</p>
<p>In September 2016, a partnership of Google and <a href="/wiki/Oceana_(non-profit_group)" title="Oceana (non-profit group)">Oceana</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Skytruth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Skytruth (page does not exist)">Skytruth</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Global_Fishing_Watch" title="Global Fishing Watch">Global Fishing Watch</a>, a website designed to assist citizens of the globe in monitoring fishing activities.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awareness_in_the_fishing_community">Awareness in the fishing community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Awareness in the fishing community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Creating awareness of overfishing and effective measures can be effective in fisheries management. Improving awareness of regulations can improve compliance. Also, creating public awareness of environmental impacts of fishing can lead to fishermen voluntarily engaging in practices such as catch and release.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Barriers_to_effective_fishery_management">Barriers to effective fishery management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Barriers to effective fishery management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a> has a strong financial incentive to oppose some measures aimed at improving the sustainability of fish stocks.<sup id="cite_ref-Bolster_2012_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bolster_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> Recreational fisherman also have an interest in maintaining access to fish stocks. This leads to extensive <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> that can block or water down government policies intended to prevent overfishing.</p>
<p>Outside of countries' exclusive economic zones, fishing is difficult to control. Large oceangoing fishing boats are free to exploit fish stocks at will.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In waters that are the subject of territorial disputes, countries may actively encourage overfishing. A notable example is the <a href="/wiki/Cod_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cod wars">cod wars</a> where Britain used its navy to protect its trawlers fishing in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>'s exclusive economic zone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fish are highly transitory. Many species will freely move through different jurisdictions. The conservation efforts of one country can then be exploited by another.</p>
<p>While governments can create regulations to control people's behaviours this can be undermined by <a href="/wiki/Illegal,_unreported_and_unregulated_fishing" title="Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing">illegal fishing activity</a>. Estimates of the size of the illegal catch range from 11 to 26 million tonnes, which represents 14-33% of the world's reported catch.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Illegal fishing can take many forms. In some developing countries, large numbers of poor people are dependent on fishing. It can prove difficult to regulate this kind of overfishing, especially for weak governments. Even in regulated environments, illegal fishing may occur. While industrial fishing is often effectively controlled, smaller scale and recreational fishermen can often break regulations such as <a href="/wiki/Bag_limit" class="mw-redirect" title="Bag limit">bag limits</a> and seasonal closures. Fisherman can also easily fish illegally by doing things such as underreporting the amount of fish they caught or reporting that they caught one type of fish while actually catching another.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> There is also a large problem with surveillance of illegal fishing activity. In 2001, the UN <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), passed the International Plan of Action to Prevent, Deter and Eliminate Illegal, Unreported and Unregulated Fishing (IPOA-IUU). This is an agreement with the intention to stop port states from allowing boats to dock that participated in illegal, unreported or unregulated fishing. It also gives details for port states on effective measures of inspecting and reporting illegal fishing.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Some illegal fishing takes place on an industrial scale with financed commercial operations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The fishing capacity problem is not only related to the conservation of <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> but also to the sustainability of fishing activity. Causes of the fishing problem can be found in the property rights regime of fishing resources. Overexploitation and rent dissipation of fishermen arise in open-access <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> as was shown in Gordon.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In open-access resources like fish stocks, in the absence of a system like <a href="/wiki/Individual_transferable_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Individual transferable quota">individual transferable quotas</a>, the impossibility of excluding others provokes the fishermen who want to increase catch to do so effectively by taking someone else' share, intensifying competition. This <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a> provokes a capitalization process that leads them to increase their costs until they are equal to their revenue, dissipating their rent completely.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_from_fishermen">Resistance from fishermen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overfishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Resistance from fishermen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote>
<p>There is always disagreement between fishermen and government scientists... Imagine an overfished area of the sea in the shape of a hockey field with nets at either end. The few fish left therein would gather around the goals because fish <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_fish#Floating_objects" title="Pelagic fish">like structured habitats</a>. Scientists would survey the entire field, make lots of unsuccessful hauls, and conclude that it contains few fish. The fishermen would make a beeline to the goals, catch the fish around them, and say the scientists do not know what they are talking about. The subjective impression the fishermen get is always that there's lots of fish - because they only go to places that still have them... fisheries scientists survey and compare entire areas, not only the productive fishing spots.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> – <i>Fisheries scientist <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a></i></p>
</blockquote>
