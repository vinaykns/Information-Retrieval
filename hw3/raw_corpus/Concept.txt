			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Concept</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Concept_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Concept (disambiguation)">Concept (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>concept</b> is an abstract <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">idea</a> representing the fundamental characteristics of what it represents. Concepts arise as <a href="/wiki/Abstractions" class="mw-redirect" title="Abstractions">abstractions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Generalisations" class="mw-redirect" title="Generalisations">generalisations</a> from experience or the result of a transformation of existing ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The concept is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/instantiate#Verb" class="extiw" title="wikt:instantiate">instantiated (reified)</a> by all of its actual or potential instances, whether these are things in the real world or other <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">ideas</a>. Concepts are treated in many if not most disciplines both explicitly, such as in <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, etc., and implicitly, such as in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, etc. In <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/informal#Adjective" class="extiw" title="wikt:informal">informal</a> use the word <i>concept</i> often just means any <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">idea</a>, but formally it involves the abstraction component. These concepts are then stored in long term memory<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Generalization_process_using_trees_PNG_version.png" class="image"><img alt="Diagram" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Generalization_process_using_trees_PNG_version.png/220px-Generalization_process_using_trees_PNG_version.png" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/Generalization_process_using_trees_PNG_version.png 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Generalization_process_using_trees_PNG_version.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
When the mind makes a generalization such as the concept of <i>tree</i>, it extracts similarities from numerous examples; the simplification enables <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_(thinking)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hierarchy (thinking)">higher-level thinking</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, and especially <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>, a concept is a fundamental <a href="/wiki/Category_of_being" title="Category of being">category of existence</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy" title="Contemporary philosophy">contemporary philosophy</a>, there are at least three prevailing ways to understand what a concept is:<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Talk:Concept#Redacting_lede_per_the_archived_threads" title="Talk:Concept"><span title="Talk:Concept#Redacting lede per the archived threads">See talk page</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are hierarchical organizations of concepts and they are at the top, superordinate, the middle basic level categories, and at the bottom, subordinate categories.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It would go furniture, chair, and easy chair.</p>
<ul>
<li>Concepts as <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">mental representations</a>, where concepts are entities that exist in the brain (mental objects)</li>
<li>Concepts as <a href="/wiki/Skill" title="Skill">abilities</a>, where concepts are abilities peculiar to cognitive agents (mental states)</li>
<li>Concepts as <a href="/wiki/Fregean" class="mw-redirect" title="Fregean">Fregean</a> senses (see <a href="/wiki/Sense_and_reference" title="Sense and reference">sense and reference</a>), where concepts are <a href="/wiki/Abstract_objects" class="mw-redirect" title="Abstract objects">abstract objects</a>, as opposed to mental objects and mental states</li>
</ul>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "concept" is traced back to 1554–60 (Latin <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conceptus" class="extiw" title="wikt:conceptus">conceptum</a></i> – "something conceived"),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but what is today termed "the classical theory of concepts" is the theory of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> on the definition of terms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The meaning of "concept" is explored in mainstream <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">philosophy of mind</a>. In computer and information science contexts, especially, the term 'concept' is often used in unclear or inconsistent ways.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstract_objects">Abstract objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abstract objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Abstract_object" class="mw-redirect" title="Abstract object">Abstract object</a></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">platonist</a> theory of mind, concepts are construed as abstract objects.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> This debate concerns the <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontological</a> status of concepts – what they are really like.</p>
<p>There is debate as to the relationship between concepts and <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> However, it is necessary at least to begin by understanding that the concept "dog" is philosophically distinct from the things in the world grouped by this concept – or the reference class or <a href="/wiki/Extension_(semantics)" title="Extension (semantics)">extension</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Origin_of_Concepts_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_Concepts-10">[10]</a></sup> Concepts that can be equated to a single word are called "lexical concepts".<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Study of concepts and conceptual structure falls into the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the simplest terms, a concept is a name or label that regards or treats an abstraction as if it had concrete or material existence, such as a person, a place, or a thing. It may represent a natural object that exists in the real world like a tree, an animal, a stone, etc. It may also name an artificial (man-made) object like a chair, computer, house, etc. Abstract ideas and knowledge domains such as freedom, equality, science, happiness, etc., are also symbolized by concepts. It is important to realize that a concept is merely a symbol, a representation of the abstraction. The word is not to be mistaken for the thing. For example, the word "moon" (a concept) is not the large, bright, shape-changing object up in the sky, but only <i>represents</i> that celestial object. Concepts are created (named) to describe, explain and capture reality as it is known and understood.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_in_concept_theory">Issues in concept theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Issues in concept theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_priori_concepts">A priori concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: A priori concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">A priori and a posteriori</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category_(Kant)" title="Category (Kant)">Category (Kant)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Kant</a> declared that human minds possess pure or <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> concepts. Instead of being abstracted from individual perceptions, like empirical concepts, they originate in the mind itself. He called these concepts <a href="/wiki/Category_(Kant)" title="Category (Kant)">categories</a>, in the sense of the word that means <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">predicate</a>, attribute, characteristic, or <a href="/wiki/Quality_(philosophy)" title="Quality (philosophy)">quality</a>. But these pure categories are predicates of things <i>in general</i>, not of a particular thing. According to Kant, there are 12 categories that constitute the understanding of phenomenal objects. Each category is that one predicate which is common to multiple empirical concepts. In order to explain how an <i>a priori</i> concept can relate to individual phenomena, in a manner analogous to an <i><a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">a posteriori</a></i> concept, Kant employed the technical concept of the <a href="/wiki/Schema_(Kant)" title="Schema (Kant)">schema</a>. He held that the account of the concept as an abstraction of experience is only partly correct. He called those concepts that result from abstraction "a posteriori concepts" (meaning concepts that arise out of experience). An empirical or an <i>a posteriori</i> concept is a general representation (<i>Vorstellung</i>) or non-specific thought of that which is common to several specific perceived objects (<a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">Logic</a>, I, 1., §1, Note 1)</p>
<p>A concept is a common feature or characteristic. Kant investigated the way that empirical <i>a posteriori</i> concepts are created.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The logical acts of the understanding by which concepts are generated as to their form are:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>comparison</i>, i.e., the likening of mental images to one another in relation to the unity of consciousness;</li>
<li><i>reflection</i>, i.e., the going back over different mental images, how they can be comprehended in one consciousness; and finally</li>
<li><i>abstraction</i> or the segregation of everything else by which the mental images differ ...</li>
</ol>
In order to make our mental images into concepts, one must thus be able to compare, reflect, and abstract, for these three logical operations of the understanding are essential and general conditions of generating any concept whatever. For example, I see a fir, a willow, and a linden. In firstly comparing these objects, I notice that they are different from one another in respect of trunk, branches, leaves, and the like; further, however, I reflect only on what they have in common, the trunk, the branches, the leaves themselves, and abstract from their size, shape, and so forth; thus I gain a concept of a tree.
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Logic, §6</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embodied_content">Embodied content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Embodied content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Embodied_cognition" title="Embodied cognition">Embodied cognition</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_linguistics" title="Cognitive linguistics">cognitive linguistics</a>, abstract concepts are transformations of concrete concepts derived from embodied experience. The mechanism of transformation is structural mapping, in which properties of two or more source domains are selectively mapped onto a blended space (Fauconnier &amp; Turner, 1995; see <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_blending" title="Conceptual blending">conceptual blending</a>). A common class of blends are <a href="/wiki/Metaphors" class="mw-redirect" title="Metaphors">metaphors</a>. This theory contrasts with the rationalist view that concepts are perceptions (or <i>recollections</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s term) of an independently existing world of ideas, in that it denies the existence of any such realm. It also contrasts with the empiricist view that concepts are abstract generalizations of individual experiences, because the contingent and bodily experience is preserved in a concept, and not abstracted away. While the perspective is compatible with Jamesian pragmatism, the notion of the transformation of embodied concepts through structural mapping makes a distinct contribution to the problem of concept formation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontology">Ontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">Ontology</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> was the starkest proponent of the realist thesis of universal concepts. By his view, concepts (and ideas in general) are innate ideas that were instantiations of a transcendental world of pure forms that lay behind the veil of the physical world. In this way, universals were explained as transcendent objects. Needless to say this form of realism was tied deeply with Plato's ontological projects. This remark on Plato is not of merely historical interest. For example, the view that numbers are Platonic objects was revived by <a href="/wiki/Kurt_G%C3%B6del" title="Kurt Gödel">Kurt Gödel</a> as a result of certain puzzles that he took to arise from the phenomenological accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gottlob_Frege" title="Gottlob Frege">Gottlob Frege</a>, founder of the analytic tradition in philosophy, famously argued for the analysis of language in terms of sense and reference. For him, the sense of an expression in language describes a certain state of affairs in the world, namely, the way that some object is presented. Since many commentators view the notion of sense as identical to the notion of concept, and Frege regards senses as the linguistic representations of states of affairs in the world, it seems to follow that we may understand concepts as the manner in which we grasp the world. Accordingly, concepts (as senses) have an ontological status (Morgolis:7).</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Carl_Benjamin_Boyer" title="Carl Benjamin Boyer">Carl Benjamin Boyer</a>, in the introduction to his <i>The History of the Calculus and its Conceptual Development</i>, concepts in calculus do not refer to perceptions. As long as the concepts are useful and mutually compatible, they are accepted on their own. For example, the concepts of the <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">derivative</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integral</a> are not considered to refer to spatial or temporal perceptions of the external world of experience. Neither are they related in any way to mysterious <a href="/wiki/Limit_(mathematics)" title="Limit (mathematics)">limits</a> in which quantities are on the verge of nascence or evanescence, that is, coming into or going out of existence. The abstract concepts are now considered to be totally autonomous, even though they originated from the process of abstracting or taking away qualities from perceptions until only the common, essential attributes remained.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_representations">Mental representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mental representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">Mental representation</a></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Physicalism" title="Physicalism">physicalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">theory of mind</a>, a concept is a mental representation, which the brain uses to denote a class of things in the world. This is to say that it is literally, a symbol or group of symbols together made from the physical material of the brain.<sup id="cite_ref-Origin_of_Concepts_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_Concepts-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> Concepts are mental representations that allow us to draw appropriate inferences about the type of entities we encounter in our everyday lives.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> Concepts do not encompass all mental representations, but are merely a subset of them.<sup id="cite_ref-Origin_of_Concepts_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_Concepts-10">[10]</a></sup> The use of concepts is necessary to cognitive processes such as <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">categorization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision_making" class="mw-redirect" title="Decision making">decision making</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">inference</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_theories_on_the_structure_of_concepts">Notable theories on the structure of concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable theories on the structure of concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_theory">Classical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Definitionism" title="Definitionism">Definitionism</a></div>
<p>The classical theory of concepts, also referred to as the empiricist theory of concepts,<sup id="cite_ref-Origin_of_Concepts_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_Concepts-10">[10]</a></sup> is the oldest theory about the structure of concepts (it can be traced back to Aristotle<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup>), and was prominently held until the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> The classical theory of concepts says that concepts have a definitional structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> Adequate definitions of the kind required by this theory usually take the form of a list of features. These features must have two important qualities to provide a comprehensive definition.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> Features entailed by the definition of a concept must be both <i><a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency" title="Necessity and sufficiency">necessary</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency" title="Necessity and sufficiency">sufficient</a></i> for membership in the class of things covered by a particular concept.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> A feature is considered necessary if every member of the denoted class has that feature. A feature is considered sufficient if something has all the parts required by the definition.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> For example, the classic example <i><a href="/wiki/Bachelor" title="Bachelor">bachelor</a></i> is said to be defined by <i>unmarried</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">man</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> An entity is a bachelor (by this definition) if and only if it is both unmarried and a man. To check whether something is a member of the class, you compare its qualities to the features in the definition.<sup id="cite_ref-Origin_of_Concepts_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origin_of_Concepts-10">[10]</a></sup> Another key part of this theory is that it obeys the <i><a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_excluded_middle" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of the excluded middle">law of the excluded middle</a></i>, which means that there are no partial members of a class, you are either in or out.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The classical theory persisted for so long unquestioned because it seemed intuitively correct and has great explanatory power. It can explain how concepts would be acquired, how we use them to categorize and how we use the structure of a concept to determine its referent class.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> In fact, for many years it was one of the major activities in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> – <a href="/wiki/Formal_concept_analysis" title="Formal concept analysis">concept analysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> Concept analysis is the act of trying to articulate the necessary and sufficient conditions for the membership in the referent class of a concept.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_the_classical_theory">Arguments against the classical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arguments against the classical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Given that most later theories of concepts were born out of the rejection of some or all of the classical theory,<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> it seems appropriate to give an account of what might be wrong with this theory. In the 20th century, philosophers such as Wittgenstein and Rosch argued against the classical theory. There are six primary arguments<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> summarized as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>It seems that there simply are no definitions – especially those based in sensory primitive concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>It seems as though there can be cases where our ignorance or error about a class means that we either don't know the definition of a concept, or have incorrect notions about what a definition of a particular concept might entail.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine" title="Willard Van Orman Quine">Quine</a>'s argument against analyticity in <a href="/wiki/Two_Dogmas_of_Empiricism" title="Two Dogmas of Empiricism">Two Dogmas of Empiricism</a> also holds as an argument against definitions.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Some concepts have fuzzy membership. There are items for which it is vague whether or not they fall into (or out of) a particular referent class. This is not possible in the classical theory as everything has equal and full membership.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Rosch" title="Eleanor Rosch">Rosch</a> found typicality effects which cannot be explained by the classical theory of concepts, these sparked the prototype theory.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> See below.</li>
<li>Psychological experiments show no evidence for our using concepts as strict definitions.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototype_theory">Prototype theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prototype theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prototype_theory" title="Prototype theory">Prototype theory</a></div>
<p>Prototype theory came out of problems with the classical view of conceptual structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> Prototype theory says that concepts specify properties that members of a class tend to possess, rather than must possess.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Rosch" title="Eleanor Rosch">Rosch</a>, Mervis, <a href="/wiki/Brent_Berlin" title="Brent Berlin">Berlin</a>, Anglin, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Posner_(psychologist)" title="Michael Posner (psychologist)">Posner</a> are a few of the key proponents and creators of this theory.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-new_paradigm_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_paradigm-13">[13]</a></sup> Wittgenstein describes the relationship between members of a class as <i>family resemblances</i>. There are not necessarily any necessary conditions for membership, a dog can still be a dog with only three legs.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> This view is particularly supported by psychological experimental evidence for prototypicality effects.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> Participants willingly and consistently rate objects in categories like 'vegetable' or 'furniture' as more or less typical of that class.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-new_paradigm_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_paradigm-13">[13]</a></sup> It seems that our categories are fuzzy psychologically, and so this structure has explanatory power.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> We can judge an item's membership to the referent class of a concept by comparing it to the typical member – the most central member of the concept. If it is similar enough in the relevant ways, it will be cognitively admitted as a member of the relevant class of entities.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> Rosch suggests that every category is represented by a central exemplar which embodies all or the maximum possible number of features of a given category.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> According to Lech, Gunturkun, and Suchan explain that categorization involves many areas of the brain, some of these are; visual association areas, prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia, and temporal lobe -</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory-theory">Theory-theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theory-theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Theory-theory is a reaction to the previous two theories and develops them further.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> This theory postulates that categorization by concepts is something like scientific theorizing.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_Encycl_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Encycl-3">[3]</a></sup> Concepts are not learned in isolation, but rather are learned as a part of our experiences with the world around us.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Book_11-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Book-11">[11]</a></sup> In this sense, concepts' structure relies on their relationships to other concepts as mandated by a particular mental theory about the state of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> How this is supposed to work is a little less clear than in the previous two theories, but is still a prominent and notable theory.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> This is supposed to explain some of the issues of ignorance and error that come up in prototype and classical theories as concepts that are structured around each other seem to account for errors such as whale as a fish (this misconception came from an incorrect theory about what a whale is like, combining with our theory of what a fish is).<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup> When we learn that a whale is not a fish, we are recognizing that whales don't in fact fit the theory we had about what makes something a fish. In this sense, the Theory-Theory of concepts is responding to some of the issues of prototype theory and classic theory.<sup id="cite_ref-concepts_core_readings_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concepts_core_readings-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideasthesia">Ideasthesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concept&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ideasthesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Ideasthesia" title="Ideasthesia">ideasthesia</a> (or "sensing concepts"), activation of a concept may be the main mechanism responsible for creation of phenomenal experiences. Therefore, understanding how the brain processes concepts may be central to solving the mystery of how conscious experiences (or <a href="/wiki/Qualia" title="Qualia">qualia</a>) emerge within a physical system e.g., the sourness of the sour taste of lemon.<sup id="cite_ref-MroczkoNikolic2014_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MroczkoNikolic2014-14">[14]</a></sup> This question is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Hard_problem_of_consciousness" title="Hard problem of consciousness">hard problem of consciousness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Research on ideasthesia emerged from research on <a href="/wiki/Synesthesia" title="Synesthesia">synesthesia</a> where it was noted that a synesthetic experience requires first an activation of a concept of the inducer.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Later research expanded these results into everyday perception.<sup id="cite_ref-Gomez2013_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gomez2013-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a lot of discussion on the most effective theory in concepts. Another theory is semantic pointers, these use perceptual and motor representations and these representations are like symbols.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
