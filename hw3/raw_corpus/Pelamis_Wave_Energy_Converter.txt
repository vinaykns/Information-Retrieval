<p>The <b>Pelamis Wave Energy Converter</b> was a technology that used the motion of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface waves</a> to create electricity. The machine was made up of connected sections which flex and bend as waves pass; it is this motion which is used to generate electricity.</p>
<p>Developed by the now defunct <sup id="cite_ref-admin2014_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-admin2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Scottish company <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Power" title="Pelamis Wave Power">Pelamis Wave Power</a> (formerly Ocean Power Delivery), the Pelamis became the first offshore wave machine to generate electricity into the grid, when it was first connected to the UK grid in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Pelamis Wave Power then went on to build and test five additional Pelamis machines: three first-generation P1 machines, which were tested in a farm off the coast of Portugal in 2009, and two second-generation machines, the Pelamis P2, were tested off Orkney between 2010 and 2014. The company went into administration in November 2014, with the <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> body <a href="/wiki/Wave_Energy_Scotland" title="Wave Energy Scotland">Wave Energy Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Pelamis machine is an offshore wave energy converter, operating in water depths greater than 50m. The machine consists of a series of semi-submerged cylindrical sections linked by hinged joints. As waves pass along the length of the machine, the sections move relative to one another. The wave-induced <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a> of the sections is resisted by <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_cylinder" title="Hydraulic cylinder">hydraulic cylinders</a> which pump high pressure oil through <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_machinery" title="Hydraulic machinery">hydraulic motors</a> via smoothing <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_accumulators" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydraulic accumulators">hydraulic accumulators</a>. The hydraulic motors drive <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generators</a> to produce electricity. Electricity from all the joints is fed down a single <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical cable</a> to a junction on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_bed" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea bed">sea bed</a>. Several devices can be connected and linked to shore through a single seabed cable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle">Principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Pelamis is an attenuating wave energy converter. The machine responds to the curvature of the waves (their shape) rather than the wave height. As waves can only reach a certain curvature before naturally breaking, this limits the range of motion through which the machine must move but maintains large motion at the joints in small waves.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projects">Projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="P2_Pelamis_Testing_at_EMEC">P2 Pelamis Testing at EMEC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: P2 Pelamis Testing at EMEC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The P2 Pelamis design was Pelamis Wave Power's second generation Pelamis machine. The Pelamis P2 is 180m long, 4m diameter and approximately 1350 tonnes in weight. Consisting of five tube sections and four flexible joints, the design is longer and fatter than the previous P1 design.</p>
<p>In 2010, Pelamis Wave Power began tests of the first Pelamis P2 machine at the <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a>, Orkney, Scotland. The machine was owned by the German utility company, <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a>, and was the UK's first commercial supply contract in the <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a> sector.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In March 2010 Pelamis Wave Power announced a second order for a P2 machine, from <a href="/wiki/ScottishPower" class="mw-redirect" title="ScottishPower">ScottishPower</a> Renewables, part of <a href="/wiki/Iberdrola_Renovables" title="Iberdrola Renovables">Iberdrola Renovables</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This second machine was first installed at EMEC in May 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The two utility companies announced that they will work together to share and collaborate in testing of the P2 Pelamis technology.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the demise of the company, the P2-001 device was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Wave_Energy_Scotland" title="Wave Energy Scotland">Wave Energy Scotland</a>, having completed over 15,000 hours of operation. The device was decommissioned in April 2016. The other device, P2-002 was sold to the <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a> for use as a test rig.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projects_Formerly_in_Development">Projects Formerly in Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Projects Formerly in Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>E.ON and ScottishPower Renewables announced plans to build larger projects using Pelamis machines in the waters off Orkney's west coast.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Both companies won leases in 2010 from <a href="/wiki/The_Crown_Estate" class="mw-redirect" title="The Crown Estate">The Crown Estate</a>, who own the seabed around the UK, for projects of up to 50MW. The "Pentland Firth and Orkney Waters Leasing Round" was the world's first commercial scale wave and tidal energy leasing opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_History">Development History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Development History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototype_Pelamis_Machine">Prototype Pelamis Machine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prototype Pelamis Machine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Pelamis Wave Power tested their first full-scale prototype at the <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">European Marine Energy Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, Scotland between 2004 and 2007. The machine, which was rated at 750&#160;kW, was the world's first offshore <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> machine to generate electricity into the grid system.</p>
<p>The prototype was 120m long and 3.5m in diameter. It consisted of four tube sections linked by three, shorter, and power conversion modules<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agu.C3.A7adoura_Wave_Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aguçadoura Wave Farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a></div>
<p>In 2008 Pelamis tested three first generation, P1 Pelamis waves at the <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a>. Located off the northwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> near <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">Póvoa de Varzim</a>, the farm had an installed capacity of 2.25MW and was the world's first multiple machine wave power project.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The project was part funded by Portuguese utility <a href="/wiki/Enersis" title="Enersis">Enersis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> at the time owned by Australian global investment company <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Brown" title="Babcock &amp; Brown">Babcock &amp; Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The farm first generated electricity in July 2008 but was taken offline in November 2008 at the same time as Babcock &amp; Brown encountered financial difficulties.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Pelamis_platurus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelamis platurus">Pelamis platurus</a></i> is a yellow-bellied <a href="/wiki/Sea_snake" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea snake">sea snake</a> that lives in tropical and subtropical waters. It prefers shallow inshore waters.</p>
