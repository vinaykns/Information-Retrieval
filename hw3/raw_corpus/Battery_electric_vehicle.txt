			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Battery electric vehicle</h1>

The <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a> is world's top selling highway-capable <a href="/wiki/All-electric_car" class="mw-redirect" title="All-electric car">all-electric car</a>, with more than 250,000 units delivered globally through December 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-LeafBest250K_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeafBest250K-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Top10PEVs2016_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top10PEVs2016-2">[2]</a></sup></div>

<p>A <b>battery electric vehicle</b> (<b>BEV</b>), <b>battery-only electric vehicle</b> (<b>BOEV</b>) or <b>all-electric vehicle</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> (EV) that uses <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> stored in <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable battery</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_pack" title="Battery pack">packs</a>. BEVs use <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">motor controllers</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> (ICEs) for propulsion. They derive all power from battery packs and thus have no internal combustion engine, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tank</a>. BEVs include bicycles, scooters, skateboards, rail cars, watercraft, forklifts, buses, trucks and cars.</p>
<p>Cumulative global sales of highway-capable light-duty pure electric vehicles passed the one million unit milestone in September 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-1miBEVs_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1miBEVs-3">[3]</a></sup> As of December 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the world's top selling highway legal all-electric car in history is the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a> with global sales of over 250,000 units, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">Tesla Model S</a> with more than 158,000 units delivered worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-LeafBest250K_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeafBest250K-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Top10PEVs2016_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top10PEVs2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">Hybrid electric vehicle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">Plug-in hybrid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">Plug-in electric vehicle</a></div>
<p>Vehicles using both electric motors and internal combustion engines are examples of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid electric vehicles</a>, and are not considered pure or all-electric vehicles because they cannot be externally charged (operate in charge-sustaining mode) and instead they are continually recharged with power from the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_braking" class="mw-redirect" title="Regenerative braking">regenerative braking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PEVs_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PEVs-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">Hybrid vehicles</a> with batteries that can be charged externally to displace some or all of their internal combustion engine power and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> fuel are called <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> electric vehicles (PHEV), and run as BEVs during their <a href="/wiki/Charge-depleting" title="Charge-depleting">charge-depleting</a> mode. PHEVs with a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle_drivetrain#Series_hybrid" title="Hybrid vehicle drivetrain">series</a> <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">powertrain</a> are also called range-extended electric vehicles (REEVs), such as the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Volt" title="Chevrolet Volt">Chevrolet Volt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fisker_Karma" title="Fisker Karma">Fisker Karma</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">Plug-in electric vehicles</a> (PEVs) are a subcategory of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> that includes battery electric vehicles (BEVs), plug-in hybrid vehicles, (PHEVs), and <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_conversion" title="Electric vehicle conversion">electric vehicle conversions</a> of hybrid electric vehicles and conventional <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-PEVs_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PEVs-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CSE_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSE-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In China, plug-in electric vehicles, together with hybrid electric vehicles are called new energy vehicles (NEVs).<sup id="cite_ref-NEVs_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEVs-6">[6]</a></sup> However, in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a> (NEVs) are battery electric vehicles that are legally limited to roads with posted speed limits no higher than 45 miles per hour (72&#160;km/h), are usually built to have a top speed of 30 miles per hour (48&#160;km/h), and have a maximum loaded weight of 3,000&#160;lbs.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles_by_type">Vehicles by type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vehicles by type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of battery electric vehicles is to use charged <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_battery" title="Electric vehicle battery">batteries</a> on board vehicles for propulsion. Battery electric cars are becoming more and more attractive with the advancement of new battery technology (<a href="/wiki/Lithium_Ion" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium Ion">Lithium Ion</a>) that have higher power and <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> (i.e., greater possible acceleration and more range with fewer batteries) and higher oil prices.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>BEVs include <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truck#Light_trucks" title="Truck">light trucks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a> operates nine <a href="/wiki/Zero-fare" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-fare">zero-fare</a> electric buses, which have been in operation since 1992 and have carried 11.3 million passengers and covered a distance of 3,100,000 kilometres (1,900,000&#160;mi), They were made locally by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Advanced_Vehicle_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Advanced Vehicle Systems (page does not exist)">Advanced Vehicle Systems</a>. Two of these buses were used for the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996 Summer Olympics</a> in Atlanta.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the summer of 2000, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Hong Kong Airport">Hong Kong Airport</a> began operating a 16-passenger <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Rosa" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitsubishi Rosa">Mitsubishi Rosa</a> electric shuttle bus, and in the fall of 2000, New York City began testing a 66-passenger battery-powered <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school bus</a>, an all-electric version of the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Bird_TC/2000" title="Blue Bird TC/2000">Blue Bird TC/2000</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A similar bus was operated in <a href="/wiki/Napa_County,_California" title="Napa County, California">Napa Valley, California</a> for 14 months ending in April, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Beijing Olympics</a> used a fleet of 50 electric buses, which have a range of 130&#160;km (81&#160;mi) with the air conditioning on. They use <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium-ion">Lithium-ion</a> batteries, and consume about 1&#160;kW·h/mi (0.62&#160;kW·h/km; 2.2&#160;MJ/km). The buses were designed by the Beijing Institute of Technology and built by the Jinghua Coach Co. Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The batteries are replaced with fully charged ones at the recharging station to allow 24-hour operation of the buses.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the bus electric phenomenon is in development, but some buses are already operating in numerous cities.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> PVI, a medium company located in the Paris region, is one of the leader of the market with its brand <a href="/wiki/Gepebus" title="Gepebus">Gepebus</a> (offering <a href="/wiki/Oreos_2X" class="mw-redirect" title="Oreos 2X">Oreos 2X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oreos_4X" class="mw-redirect" title="Oreos 4X">Oreos 4X</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the first battery-electric, fast-charge bus has been in operation in <a href="/wiki/Pomona,_California" title="Pomona, California">Pomona, California</a> since September 2010 at <a href="/wiki/Foothill_Transit" title="Foothill Transit">Foothill Transit</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Proterra,_Inc." title="Proterra, Inc.">Proterra</a> EcoRide BE35 uses <a href="/wiki/Lithium-titanate_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium-titanate batteries">lithium-titanate batteries</a> and is able to fast-charge in less than 10 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, the first production model all-electric school bus was delivered to the Kings Canyon Unified School District in California’s <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a>. The bus was one of four the district ordered. This battery electric school bus, which has 4 sodium nickel batteries, is the first modern electric school bus approved for student transportation by any state.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunder_Sky">Thunder Sky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thunder Sky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Thunder Sky (based in Hong Kong) builds <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium-ion batteries">lithium-ion batteries</a> used in submarines and has three models of electric buses, the 10/21 passenger EV-6700 with a range of 280&#160;km (170&#160;mi) under 20 mins quick-charge, the EV-2009 city buses, and the 43 passenger EV-2008 highway bus, which has a range of 300&#160;km (190&#160;mi) under quick-charge (20 mins to 80 percent), and 350&#160;km (220&#160;mi) under full charge (25 mins). <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_Tindo">Free Tindo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Free Tindo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Tindo is an all-electric bus from <a href="/wiki/Adelaide,_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Adelaide, Australia">Adelaide, Australia</a>. The Tindo (aboriginal word for sun) is made by <a href="/wiki/Designline" class="mw-redirect" title="Designline">Designline International</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and gets its electricity from a <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">solar PV</a> system on Adelaide's central <a href="/wiki/Bus_station" title="Bus station">bus station</a>. Rides are <a href="/wiki/Zero-fare" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-fare">zero-fare</a> as part of Adelaide's public transport system.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Fast-Charge.2C_Battery-Electric_Transit_Bus">First Fast-Charge, Battery-Electric Transit Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First Fast-Charge, Battery-Electric Transit Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Proterra,_Inc." title="Proterra, Inc.">Proterra</a>'s EcoRide BE35 transit bus, called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecoliner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ecoliner (page does not exist)">Ecoliner</a> by Foothill Transit in West Covina, California, is a heavy duty, fast charge, battery-electric bus. Proterra's ProDrive drive-system uses a <a href="/wiki/UQM" class="mw-redirect" title="UQM">UQM</a> motor and regenerative braking that captures 90 percent of the available energy and returns it to the TerraVolt energy storage system, which in turn increases the total distance the bus can drive by 31–35 percent. It can travel 30–40 miles on a single charge, is up to 600 percent more fuel-efficient than a typical diesel or CNG bus, and produces 44 percent less carbon than CNG.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_cars">Electric cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electric cars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">Electric car</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_production_battery_electric_vehicles" title="List of production battery electric vehicles">List of production battery electric vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_car_use_by_country" title="Electric car use by country">electric car use by country</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a> is a plug-in battery powered <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> which is <a href="/wiki/Ground_propulsion" title="Ground propulsion">propelled</a> by <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a>(s). Although electric cars often give good acceleration and have generally acceptable top speed, the lower <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> of production batteries available in 2015 compared with <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_fuel" title="Carbon-based fuel">carbon-based fuels</a> means that electric cars need batteries that are fairly large fraction of the vehicle mass but still often give relatively low range between charges. Recharging can also take significant lengths of time. For journeys within a single battery charge, rather than long journeys, electric cars are practical forms of transportation and can be recharged overnight.</p>

<p>Electric cars have the potential of significantly reducing <a href="/wiki/City_pollution" class="mw-redirect" title="City pollution">city pollution</a> by having <a in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint <p>Electric cars are expected to have a major impact in the auto industry<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> given advantages in <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor vehicle emissions">city pollution</a>, less dependence on oil, and expected rise in gasoline prices.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> World governments are pledging billions to fund development of electric vehicles and their components. <p>Cumulative global sales of highway-capable battery electric cars and vans passed the 1&#160;million unit milestone in September 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-1miBEVs_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1miBEVs-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Renault-Nissan_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault-Nissan Alliance">Renault-Nissan Alliance</a> is the leading all-electric vehicle manufacturer. <p><a href="/wiki/Formula_E" title="Formula E">Formula E</a> is a fully electric international single seater championship. The series was conceived in 2012, and the inaugural championship started in Beijing on 13 September 2014. The series is sanctioned by the FIA. Alejandro Agag is the current CEO of Formula E.</p>
<p>The Formula E championship is currently contested by ten teams with two drivers each (after the withdrawal of Team Trulli, there are temporarily only nine teams competing). Racing generally takes place on temporary city-center street circuits which are approximately 2 to 3.4&#160;km (1.2 to 2.1&#160;mi) long. Currently, only the Mexico City ePrix takes place on a road course, a modified version of the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Special-purpose_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Special-purpose vehicle">Special-purpose vehicles</a> come in a wide range of types, ranging from relatively common ones such as <a href="/wiki/Golf_cart" title="Golf cart">golf carts</a>, things like <a href="/wiki/Electric_golf_trolley" title="Electric golf trolley">electric golf trolleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk_float" title="Milk float">milk floats</a>, <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">all-terrain vehicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicles</a>, and a wide range of other devices. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-_and_three-wheeled_vehicles">Two- and three-wheeled vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Two- and three-wheeled vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An increasing variety of two-wheeled electric vehicles is being made for transportation and entertainment. These include devices for sitting on such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_motorcycles_and_scooters" title="Electric motorcycles and scooters">electric motorcycles and scooters</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_bicycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric bicycles">electric bicycles</a>, as well as devices for standing on such as the <a href="/wiki/Segway_PT" title="Segway PT">Segway PT</a> or <a href="/wiki/Self-balancing_two-wheeled_board" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-balancing two-wheeled board">self-balancing two-wheeled boards</a>.</p>
<p>Three-wheeled vehicles include <a href="/wiki/Electric_rickshaw" title="Electric rickshaw">electric rickshaws</a>, a powered variant of the <a href="/wiki/Cycle_rickshaw" title="Cycle rickshaw">cycle rickshaw</a>.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_boat" title="Electric boat">Electric boat</a></div>
<p>Several battery electric ships operate throughout the world, some for business. Electric <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferries</a> are being operated and constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motors">Motors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Motors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Traction_motor" title="Traction motor">Traction motor</a></div>
<p>Electric cars have traditionally used series wound DC motors, a form of <a href="/wiki/Brushed_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushed DC electric motor">brushed DC electric motor</a>. Separately excited and permanent magnet are just two of the types of DC motors available. More recent electric vehicles have made use of a variety of <a href="/wiki/AC_motor" title="AC motor">AC motor</a> types, as these are simpler to build and have no brushes that can wear out. These are usually <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">induction motors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brushless_AC_electric_motor" class="mw-redirect" title="Brushless AC electric motor">brushless AC electric motors</a> which use permanent magnets. There are several variations of the permanent magnet motor which offer simpler drive schemes and/or lower cost including the <a href="/wiki/Brushless_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushless DC electric motor">brushless DC electric motor</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_controllers">Motor controllers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_electric_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Motor controllers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Motor_controller" title="Motor controller">Motor controller</a></div>
<p>The motor controller regulates the power to the motor, supplying either variable pulse width DC or variable frequency variable amplitude AC, depending on the motor type, DC or AC.</p>
