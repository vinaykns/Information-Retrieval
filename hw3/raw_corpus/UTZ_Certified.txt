<p><b>UTZ Certified</b> is a program and a label for sustainable farming. The UTZ Certified label is featured on more than 10,000 different product packages in over 116 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> As of 2014, UTZ Certified is the largest program for sustainable farming of coffee and cocoa in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The UTZ Certified program covers good <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> practices, farm management and the environment.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UTZ was launched in 2002 as Utz Kapeh, meaning ‘Good Coffee’ in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mayan_languages" title="List of Mayan languages">Mayan</a> language <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_language" title="K'iche' language">Quiché</a>. It was founded by Nick Bocklandt, a Belgian-Guatemalan coffee grower, and Ward de Groote, a Dutch coffee roaster, with the goal of implementing sustainability on a large scale in the worldwide market. Solidaridad (www.solidaridadnetwork.org) was another co-initiator of UTZ Certified and assisted UTZ to become a global standard through financial support and field implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 7 March 2007, the Utz Kapeh Foundation officially changed its name and logo to UTZ Certified.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Coffee</dt>
</dl>
<p>UTZ Certified is the largest sustainability program in the world for <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>. In 2012, 9% of the coffee produced in the world was UTZ certified (716,000 MT(Metric Ton = 1000&#160;kg), out of global production of 8,200,000 MT<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>).</p>
<dl>
<dt>Cocoa</dt>
</dl>
<p>On 10 October 2007, the Cocoa Program was launched. UTZ Certified cooperated with <a href="/wiki/Ahold" title="Ahold">Ahold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cargill" title="Cargill">Cargill</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._J._Heinz_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="H. J. Heinz Company">Heinz</a> <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars,_Incorporated" title="Mars, Incorporated">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=ECOM&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ECOM (page does not exist)">ECOM</a> to set up a new certification and traceability system for sustainable cocoa. <a href="/wiki/Solidaridad" title="Solidaridad">Solidaridad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxfam_Novib" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxfam Novib">Oxfam Novib</a> also supported the initiative from the beginning. In 2009 the first UTZ Certified cocoa products reached the market. As of 2014, UTZ Certified is the largest sustainability program for cocoa in the world. By now, over 336,300 cocoa farmers from 16 countries are UTZ Certified. In 2012, 13% of the cocoa produced in the world was UTZ Certified (535,000 MT, out of global production of 2,889,000MT<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>).</p>
<dl>
<dt>Tea</dt>
</dl>
<p>The UTZ Certified program for <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rooibos" title="Rooibos">rooibos</a> was launched in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Hazelnuts</dt>
</dl>
<p>UTZ Certified is currently running a pilot project in Turkey to explore the possibilities for sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Code_of_Conduct">Code of Conduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Code of Conduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_the_Code_of_Conduct">Summary of the Code of Conduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Summary of the Code of Conduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The UTZ certification program is based on the UTZ Certified Code of Conduct:<sup id="cite_ref-code_of_conduct_2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-code_of_conduct_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> a set of social and environmental criteria for responsible growing practices and efficient farm management. Coffee, cocoa and tea producers who are UTZ certified comply with this code. The Code of Conduct version 2014<sup id="cite_ref-code_of_conduct_2014_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-code_of_conduct_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> is based on the international <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Labour_Organization_Conventions" title="List of International Labour Organization Conventions">ILO Conventions</a> and the expertise of many different stakeholders, including the farmers who use it. The Code has been developed in a broad <a href="/wiki/Project_stakeholder" title="Project stakeholder">stakeholder</a> process and therefore widely accepted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A core code is applicable to all farmers, and there are also additional requirements in product specific modules for coffee, cocoa and tea.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The certification system is based on a model of <a href="/wiki/Continual_improvement_process" title="Continual improvement process">continuous improvement</a>. Producers have to comply with core safety and quality standards from year one. Additional control points are added in the following years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_Categories_in_the_Code_of_Conduct">Common Categories in the Code of Conduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Common Categories in the Code of Conduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The criteria of the UTZ Certified Code of Conduct fall into four categories:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Farm Management</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Measures to optimize the yield</li>
<li>Internal Management System for groups, with responsibilities including:
<ul>
<li>Arranging annual internal inspections</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Record keeping</li>
<li>Risk assessments</li>
<li>Training and awareness raising</li>
<li>Recording of volumes in the UTZ Certified traceability system</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Farming Practices</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Choice of suitable planting variety</li>
<li>Farm maintenance</li>
<li>Soil fertility management</li>
<li>Diversification of production, to support ecological diversity and economic resilience</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Integrated_pest_management" title="Integrated pest management">Integrated pest management</a></li>
<li>Responsible and appropriate choice and use of <a href="/wiki/Agro-chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Agro-chemicals">agro-chemicals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a>, and records of application</li>
<li>Irrigation</li>
<li>Product handling during and after the harvest</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Social and Living Conditions</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Application of national laws and <a href="/wiki/ILO" class="mw-redirect" title="ILO">ILO</a> conventions regarding wages and working hours, including the living wage concept for individual farms</li>
<li>No forced labor or child labor</li>
<li>Freedom of association and collective bargaining</li>
<li>Safe and healthy working conditions, including:
<ul>
<li>Protective clothing for work with chemicals</li>
<li>Safety training of workers in their own language</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Gender equality</li>
<li>No discrimination</li>
<li>Freedom of cultural expression</li>
<li>Access to education for children</li>
<li>Access to decent housing, clean drinking water and <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> for workers and their families</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Environment</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Efficient use of water and energy</li>
<li>Waste management</li>
<li>Promotion of ecological diversity</li>
<li>Protection of nature</li>
<li>No <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Primary_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary forest">primary forests</a></li>
<li>Respect for protected areas</li>
<li>Protection of endangered species</li>
<li>Reduction and prevention of <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a></li>
<li>Measures to adapt to climate change</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product-Specific_Modules_in_the_Code_of_Conduct">Product-Specific Modules in the Code of Conduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Product-Specific Modules in the Code of Conduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dt>Coffee</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Correct fermentation and handling of coffee after harvest</li>
<li>Treatment of waste water from processing</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Cocoa</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Use of <a href="/wiki/Shade-grown_coffee" title="Shade-grown coffee">shade trees</a></li>
<li>Correct drying and fermentation</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Tea</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Harvest carried out at the appropriate time</li>
<li>Correct handling of harvested leaves</li>
<li>Quality control</li>
<li>Energy and waste water management</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Rooibos</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Crop rotation</li>
<li>Harvest carried out at the appropriate time</li>
<li>Correct post-harvest handling</li>
<li>Bush fire prevention</li>
<li>Identification and protection of wild rooibos areas</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chain_of_Custody">Chain of Custody</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chain of Custody">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To enhance the guarantee that a consumer product with an UTZ Certified logo does indeed credibly link to an UTZ certified producer, the UTZ Certified program contains Chain of Custody requirements. This is a set of chain-wide administrative, logistical and technical requirements for traceability. These requirements include criteria for separation of UTZ Certified products and conventional/non-UTZ Certified products, and keeping records of direct suppliers and buyers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traceability">Traceability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Traceability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UTZ certified coffee, cocoa and tea is traceable through the supply chain, as UTZ uses a web-based traceability system.</p>
<p>When an UTZ certified producer sells his products (e.g. coffee, cocoa, tea) to a registered UTZ Certified buyer, the product is announced in the UTZ Certified web-based system. By doing so the seller announces when he is shipping what amount to whom. The buyer then gets notified and needs to confirm this in the traceability system. UTZ Certified assigns a unique tracking number to this lot. At the end of the supply chain, the end product manufacturer uses the unique tracking number to know his product credibly links to a certified source. Some brands use this unique tracking system to make the product traceable for their consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Traceability_Services">Traceability Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Traceability Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Traceability services UTZ Certified has developed, implemented and is currently managing the traceability system for the <a href="/wiki/Roundtable_on_Sustainable_Palm_Oil" title="Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil">Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil</a>. This system was launched in December 2008.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification">Certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Independent, third party auditors make annual inspections to ensure coffee producers comply with the Code of Conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-code_of_conduct_2014_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-code_of_conduct_2014-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Certification Body</dt>
</dl>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Certification_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Certification body">Certification body</a> (CB) is an independent, third-party certifier. When approved by UTZ Certified, these organizations conduct annual certification inspections of coffee, cocoa and tea producers to determine whether they comply with the UTZ Certified Code of Conduct<sup id="cite_ref-code_of_conduct_2014_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-code_of_conduct_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> and Chain of Custody requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Trained Agronomists</dt>
</dl>
<p>An UTZ Certified trained <a href="/wiki/Agronomist" class="mw-redirect" title="Agronomist">agronomist</a> is a technical consultant specifically trained in assisting producers to comply with the UTZ Certified Code of Conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-code_of_conduct_2014_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-code_of_conduct_2014-9">[9]</a></sup> Trained agronomists can advise on practical implementation of elements of the Code and give directions on improvement of efficiency in farm management.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014, UTZ Certified published its first impact report.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This brought together 24 external studies and data collected by UTZ Certified. It demonstrated that UTZ Certified cocoa, coffee and tea farms generate higher yields and better quality crops than conventional farms, and being trained in the UTZ Code of Conduct helps farmers to improve their knowledge and adopt sustainable farming practices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pricing">Pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coffee, cocoa and tea with an UTZ certification has added value in the sense that it assures buyers that it has been produced according to an internationally recognized standard for responsible production, i.e. according to the UTZ Certified Code of Conduct. The price for UTZ certified coffee is determined in the negotiation process between buyer and seller. UTZ Certified does not interfere in price negotiations. In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Fair_Trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Fair Trade">Fair Trade</a>, no minimum purchase price is set. Instead, the focus on good agricultural practices in the UTZ program is intended to enable farmers to increase the quantity and quality of their yield, and thus to increase their income.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UTZ_Certified&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UTZ Certified certification, like the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Alliance" title="Rainforest Alliance">Rainforest Alliance</a> coffee certification program, has been criticized because it offers producers no minimum or guaranteed price for their crop. Some consider UTZ certified producer organizations to be vulnerable to the volatility of the coffee market.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Observer_2006_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Observer_2006-15">[15]</a></sup> The price difference and the fact companies do not pay any marketing costs for logo use, makes the UTZ Certified label considerably cheaper than <a href="/wiki/Fairtrade" class="mw-redirect" title="Fairtrade">Fairtrade</a> for companies and farmers interested in tapping the ethical market.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Michael Conroy, an independent consultant on certification for sustainable development, criticized UTZ Certified in his 2007 book <i>Branded!</i>: "the environmental standards of UTZ Certified are far weaker than those of either Fairtrade or Rainforest Alliance".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> UTZ Certified's standards for example, explicitly announces that <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically modified</a> coffee plants, though not at present available, would be allowable so long as farmers obey local regulations on their use. Any kind of chemical fertilizer may be used as long as an external, technically qualified advisor has determined the quantity of fertilizer to be used. No chemical <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> or fungicides banned in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the U.S. or <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> may be used, but any that are acceptable in those three markets are acceptable on coffee farms if they are applied "according to the label".<sup id="cite_ref-Conroy.2C_Michael_2007_p252_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conroy.2C_Michael_2007_p252-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2012, German magazine "Ökotest" published an article labeling UTZ Certified, among others, as unfair, due to a lack of pre-financing and guaranteed minimum purchase prices.<sup id="cite_ref-.C3.96kotest_2012_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-.C3.96kotest_2012-18">[18]</a></sup> In response UTZ Certified stated that the foundation does not consider its standards fair trade, nor that it claims that they are, while maintaining that its standards contribute positively to sustainable development of tropical farming communities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
