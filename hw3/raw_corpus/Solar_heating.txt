<p>A <b>solar thermal collector</b> collects <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> by <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(optics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Absorption (optics)">absorbing</a> <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>. A collector is a device for capturing solar radiation. Solar radiation is energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> (long) to the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (short) wavelengths. The quantity of solar energy striking the Earth's surface (<a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>) averages about 1,000 watts per square meter under clear skies, depending upon weather conditions, location and orientation.</p>
<p>The term "solar collector" commonly refers to <a href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water_panel" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar hot water panel">solar hot water panels</a>, but may refer to installations such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_parabolic_trough" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar parabolic trough">solar parabolic troughs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar towers</a>; or basic installations such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_heat" title="Solar air heat">solar air heaters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">Concentrated solar power</a> plants usually use the more complex collectors to generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> by heating a fluid to drive a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> connected to an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Norton_2013_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> Simple collectors are typically used in residential and commercial buildings for space heating. The first solar thermal collector designed for building roofs was patented by William H. Goettl and called the "<a href="/wiki/Solar_heat_collector_and_radiator_for_building_roof" title="Solar heat collector and radiator for building roof">Solar heat collector and radiator for building roof</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar-thermal_collectors_heating_liquid">Solar-thermal collectors heating liquid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solar-thermal collectors heating liquid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solar collectors are either non-concentrating or concentrating. In the non-concentrating type, the collector area (i.e., the area that intercepts the solar radiation) is the same as the absorber area (i.e., the area absorbing the radiation). In these types the whole solar panel absorbs light. Concentrating collectors have a bigger interceptor than absorber.<sup id="cite_ref-Norton_2013_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norton_2013-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flat-plate and evacuated-tube solar collectors are used to collect heat for space heating, domestic hot water or cooling with an absorption chiller.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flat_plate_collectors">Flat plate collectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flat plate collectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels,_Santorini2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg/220px-Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg/330px-Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg/440px-Solar_panels%2C_Santorini2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>

<p>Flat-plate collectors, developed by Hottel and Whillier in the 1950s, are the most common type. They consist of (1) a dark flat-plate absorber, (2) a transparent cover that reduces heat losses, (3) a heat-transport fluid (air, antifreeze or water) to remove heat from the absorber, and (4) a heat insulating backing. The absorber consists of a thin absorber sheet (of thermally stable polymers, aluminum, steel or <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, to which a matte black or selective coating is applied) often backed by a grid or coil of fluid tubing placed in an insulated casing with a glass or polycarbonate cover. In water heat panels, fluid is usually circulated through tubing to transfer heat from the absorber to an insulated water tank. This may be achieved directly or through a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a>.</p>
<p>Most air heat fabricators and some water heat manufacturers have a completely flooded absorber consisting of two sheets of metal which the fluid passes between. Because the heat exchange area is greater they may be marginally more efficient than traditional absorbers.<sup id="cite_ref-Domestic_Solar_Hot_Water_Systems_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Domestic_Solar_Hot_Water_Systems-3">[3]</a></sup> Sunlight passes through the glazing and strikes the absorber plate, which heats up, changing solar energy into heat energy. The heat is transferred to liquid passing through pipes attached to the absorber plate. Absorber plates are commonly painted with "selective coatings", which absorb and retain heat better than ordinary black paint. Absorber plates are usually made of metal—typically copper or aluminum—because the metal is a good heat conductor. Copper is more expensive, but is a better conductor and less prone to corrosion than aluminum. <i>(See: <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_renewable_energy#Solar_water_heaters" title="Copper in renewable energy">Copper in solar water heaters</a>).</i> In locations with average available solar energy, flat plate collectors are sized approximately one-half to one square foot per gallon of one day's hot water use. Absorber piping configurations include:</p>
<ul>
<li>harp&#160;– traditional design with bottom pipe risers and top collection pipe, used in low pressure thermosyphon and pumped systems;</li>
<li>serpentine&#160;– one continuous S that maximizes temperature but not total energy yield in variable flow systems, used in compact solar domestic hot water only systems (no space heating role);</li>
<li>flooded absorber consisting of two sheets of metal stamped to produce a circulation zone;</li>
<li>boundary layer absorber collectors consisting of several layers of transparent and opaque sheets that enable absorption in a boundary layer. Because the energy is absorbed in the boundary layer, heat conversion may be more efficient than for collectors where absorbed heat is conducted through a material before the heat is accumulated in a circulating liquid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Polymer flat plate collectors are an alternative to metal collectors and are now being produced in Europe. These may be wholly <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>, or they may include metal plates in front of freeze-tolerant water channels made of <a href="/wiki/Silicone_rubber" title="Silicone rubber">silicone rubber</a>. Polymers are flexible and therefore freeze-tolerant and can employ plain water instead of antifreeze, so that they may be plumbed directly into existing water tanks instead of needing <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchangers" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat exchangers">heat exchangers</a> that lower efficiency. By dispensing with a heat exchanger, temperatures need not be quite so high for the circulation system to be switched on, so such direct circulation panels, whether polymer or otherwise, can be more efficient, particularly at low light levels. Some early selectively coated polymer collectors suffered from overheating when insulated, as stagnation temperatures can exceed the polymer's melting point.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, the melting point of <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> is 160&#160;°C (320&#160;°F), while the stagnation temperature of insulated thermal collectors can exceed 180&#160;°C (356&#160;°F) if control strategies are not used. For this reason polypropylene is not often used in glazed selectively coated solar collectors. Increasingly polymers such as high temperate silicones (which melt at over 250&#160;°C (482&#160;°F)) are being used. Some non polypropylene polymer based glazed solar collectors are matte black coated rather than selectively coated to reduce the stagnation temperature to 150&#160;°C (302&#160;°F) or less.</p>
<p>In areas where freezing is a possibility, freeze-tolerance (the capability to freeze repeatedly without cracking) can be achieved by the use of flexible polymers. Silicone rubber pipes have been used for this purpose in UK since 1999. Conventional metal collectors are vulnerable to damage from freezing, so if they are water filled they must be carefully plumbed so they completely drain using gravity before freezing is expected, so that they do not crack. Many metal collectors are installed as part of a sealed heat exchanger system. Rather than having potable water flow directly through the collectors, a mixture of water and antifreeze such as propylene glycol is used. A heat exchange fluid protects against freeze damage down to a locally determined risk temperature that depends on the proportion of propylene glycol in the mixture. The use of glycol lowers the water's heat carrying capacity marginally, while the addition of an extra heat exchanger may lower system performance at low light levels.</p>
<p>A pool or unglazed collector is a simple form of flat-plate collector without a transparent cover. Typically polypropylene or EPDM rubber or silicone rubber is used as an absorber. Used for pool heating it can work quite well when the desired output temperature is near the ambient temperature (that is, when it is warm outside). As the ambient temperature gets cooler, these collectors become less effective. Most flat plate collectors have a life expectancy of over 25 years.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The main use of this technology is in residential buildings where the demand for hot water has a large impact on energy bills. This generally means a situation with a large family, or a situation in which the hot water demand is excessive due to frequent laundry washing. Commercial applications include laundromats, car washes, military laundry facilities and eating establishments. The technology can also be used for space heating if the building is located off-grid or if utility power is subject to frequent outages. <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">Solar water heating</a> systems are most likely to be cost effective for facilities with water heating systems that are expensive to operate, or with operations such as laundries or kitchens that require large quantities of hot water. Unglazed liquid collectors are commonly used to heat water for swimming pools but can also be applied to large scale water pre-heating. When loads are large relative to available collector area the bulk of the water heating can be done at low temperature, lower than at swimming pool temperatures where unglazed collectors are well established in the marketplace as the right choice. Because these collectors need not withstand high temperatures, they can use less expensive materials such as plastic or rubber. Many unglazed collectors are made of polypropylene and must be drained fully to avoid freeze damage when air temperatures drop below 44F on clear nights.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A smaller but growing percentage of unglazed collectors are flexible meaning they can withstand water freezing solid inside their absorber. The freeze concern only need be the water filled piping and collector manifolds in a hard freeze condition. Unglazed solar hot water systems should be installed to "drainback" to a storage tank whenever solar radiation is insufficient. There are no thermal shock concerns with unglazed systems. Commonly used in swimming pool heating since solar energy's early beginnings, unglazed solar collectors heat swimming pool water directly without the need for antifreeze or heat exchangers. Hot water solar systems require heat exchangers due to contamination possibilities and in the case of unglazed collectors, the pressure difference between the solar working fluid (water) and the load (pressurized cold city water). Large scale unglazed solar hot water heaters like the one at the Minoru Aquatic Center in Richmond BC<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> operate at lower temperatures than evacuated tube or boxed and glazed collector systems so they require larger more expensive heat exchangers but all other components including vented storage tanks and uninsulated plastic PVC piping reduce costs of this alternative dramatically compared to the higher temperature collector types. When heating hot water we are actually heating cold to warm and warm to hot. We can heat cold to warm as efficiently with unglazed collectors as we can heat warm to hot with high temperature collectors</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evacuated_tube_collectors">Evacuated tube collectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evacuated tube collectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Most vacuum tube collectors in use in middle Europe use heat pipes for their core instead of passing liquid directly through them. Direct flow is more popular in China. Evacuated heat pipe tubes (EHPTs) are composed of multiple <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">evacuated</a> <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> tubes each containing an absorber plate fused to a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pipe" title="Heat pipe">heat pipe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The heat is transferred to the transfer fluid (water or an <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a> mix—typically <a href="/wiki/Propylene_glycol" title="Propylene glycol">propylene glycol</a>) of a domestic hot water or <a href="/wiki/Hydronic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydronic">hydronic</a> space heating system in a heat exchanger called a "manifold". The manifold is wrapped in insulation and covered by a protective sheet metal or plastic case. The vacuum inside of the evacuated tube collectors have been proven to last more than 25 years, the reflective coating for the design is encapsulated in the vacuum inside of the tube, which will not degrade until the vacuum is lost.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The vacuum that surrounds the outside of the tube greatly reduces <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conduction_(heat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conduction (heat)">conduction</a> heat loss, therefore achieving greater efficiency than flat-plate collectors, especially in colder conditions. This advantage is largely lost in warmer climates, except in those cases where very hot water is desirable, e.g., for commercial processes. The high temperatures that can occur may require special design to prevent overheating.</p>

<p>Some evacuated tubes (glass-metal) are made with one layer of glass that fuses to the heat pipe at the upper end and encloses the heat pipe and absorber in the vacuum. Others (glass-glass) are made with a double layer of glass fused together at one or both ends with a vacuum between the layers (like a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_bottle" class="mw-redirect" title="Vacuum bottle">vacuum bottle</a> or flask), with the absorber and heat pipe contained at normal atmospheric pressure. Glass-glass tubes have a highly reliable vacuum seal, but the two layers of glass reduce the light that reaches the absorber. Moisture may enter the non-evacuated area of the tube and cause absorber corrosion. Glass-metal tubes allow more light to reach the absorber, and protect the absorber and heat pipe from corrosion even if they are made from dissimilar materials (see <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">galvanic corrosion</a>).</p>
<p>The gaps between the tubes may allow for snow to fall through the collector, minimizing the loss of production in some snowy conditions, though the lack of radiated heat from the tubes can also prevent effective shedding of accumulated snow.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>A longstanding argument exists between proponents of these two technologies. Some of this can be related to the physical structure of evacuated tube collectors which have a discontinuous absorbance area. An array of evacuated tubes on a roof has open space between the collector tubes, and vacuum between the two concentric glass tubes of each collector. Collector tubes cover only a fraction of a unit area of a roof. If evacuated tubes are compared with flat-plate collectors on the basis of area of roof occupied, a different conclusion might be reached than if the areas of absorber were compared. In addition, the ISO 9806 standard<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> is ambiguous in describing the way in which the efficiency of solar thermal collectors should be measured, since these could be measured either in terms of gross area or in terms of absorber area. Unfortunately, power output is not given for thermal collectors as it is for <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">PV panels</a>. This makes it difficult for purchasers and engineers to make informed decisions.</p>

<p>Flat-plate collectors usually lose more heat to the environment than evacuated tubes, as an increasing function of temperature. They are inappropriate for high temperature applications such as process steam production. Evacuated tube collectors have a lower absorber plate area to gross area ratio (typically 60–80% of gross area) compared to flat plates. Based on absorber plate area, most evacuated tube systems are more efficient per square meter than equivalent flat plate systems. This makes them suitable where roof space is limiting, for example where the number of occupants of a building is higher than the number of square metres of suitable and available roof space. In general, per installed square metre, evacuated tubes deliver marginally more energy when the ambient temperature is low (e.g. during winter) or when the sky is overcast. However even in areas without much sunshine and solar heat, some low cost flat plate collectors can be more cost efficient than evacuated tube collectors. Although several European companies manufacture evacuated tube collectors, the evacuated tube market is dominated by manufacturers in the East. Several Chinese companies have track records of 15–30 years. There is no unambiguous evidence that the two designs differ in long term reliability. However, evacuated tube technology is younger and (especially for newer variants with sealed heat pipes) still need to demonstrate competitive lifetimes. The modularity of evacuated tubes can be advantageous in terms of extensibility and maintenance, for example if the vacuum in one tube diminishes.</p>

<p>For a given absorber area, evacuated tubes can therefore maintain their efficiency over a wide range of ambient temperatures and heating requirements. In most climates, flat-plate collectors will generally be more cost-effective than evacuated tubes. When employed in arrays and considered instead on a per square metre basis, the efficient but costly evacuated tube collectors can have a net benefit in winter and summer. They are well-suited to cold ambient temperatures and work well in situations of consistently low sunshine, providing heat more consistently than flat plate collectors per square metre. Heating of water by a medium to low amount (i.e. Tm-Ta) is much more efficiently performed by flat plate collectors. Domestic hot water frequently falls into this medium category. Glazed or unglazed flat collectors are the preferred devices for heating swimming pool water.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Unglazed collectors may be suitable in tropical or subtropical environments if domestic hot water needs to be heated by less than 20&#160;°C. A contour map can show which type is more effective (both thermal efficiency and energy/cost) for any geographic region.</p>
<p>EHPT's work as a thermal one-way valve due to their heat pipes. This gives them an inherent maximum <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a> that acts as a safety feature. They have less aerodynamic drag, which may allow them to be placed onto the roof without being tied down. They can collect thermal radiation from the bottom in addition to the top. Tubes can be replaced individually without stopping the entire system. There is no condensation or corrosion within the tubes. One hurdle to wider adoption of evacuated tube collectors in some markets is their inability to pass internal thermal shock tests where ISO 9806-2 section 9 class b is a requirement for durability certification.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This means that if unprotected evacuated tube collectors are exposed to full sun for too long prior to being filled with cold water the tubes may shatter due to the rapid temperature shift. There is also the question of vacuum leakage. Flat panels have been around much longer and are less expensive. They may be easier to clean. Other properties, such as appearance and ease of installation are more subjective.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowl">Bowl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bowl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>solar bowl</i> is a type of solar thermal collector that operates similarly to a <a href="#Parabolic_dish">parabolic dish</a>, but instead of using a tracking parabolic mirror with a fixed receiver, it has a fixed spherical mirror with a tracking receiver. This reduces efficiency, but makes it cheaper to build and operate. Designers call it a <i>fixed mirror distributed focus solar power system</i>. The main reason for its development was to eliminate the cost of moving a large mirror to track the sun as with parabolic dish systems.<sup id="cite_ref-solarbowl_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solarbowl-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fixed parabolic mirror creates a variously shaped image of the sun as it moves across the sky. Only when the mirror is pointed directly at the sun does the light focus on one point. That is why parabolic dish systems track the sun. A fixed <a href="/wiki/Curved_mirror" title="Curved mirror">spherical mirror</a> focuses the light in the same place independent of the sun's position. The light, however, is not directed to one point but is distributed on a line from the surface of the mirror to one half radius (along a line that runs through the sphere center and the sun).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg/220px-Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg/330px-Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg/440px-Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="401" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sphericalmirrorimage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical energy density along the 1/2 radius length focal line of a spherical reflector</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As the sun moves across the sky, the aperture of any fixed collector changes. This causes changes in the amount of captured sunlight, producing what is called the <i>sinus effect</i> of power output. Proponents of the solar bowl design claim the reduction in overall power output compared with tracking parabolic mirrors is offset by lower system costs.<sup id="cite_ref-solarbowl_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solarbowl-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sunlight concentrated at the focal line of a spherical reflector is collected using a tracking receiver. This receiver is pivoted around the focal line and is usually counterbalanced. The receiver may consist of pipes carrying fluid for thermal transfer or <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">photovoltaic cells</a> for direct conversion of light to electricity.</p>
<p>The solar bowl design resulted from a project of the Electrical Engineering Department of the Texas Technical University, headed by Edwin O'Hair, to develop a 5 MWe power plant. A solar bowl was built for the town of <a href="/wiki/Crosbyton,_Texas" title="Crosbyton, Texas">Crosbyton, Texas</a> as a pilot facility.<sup id="cite_ref-solarbowl_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solarbowl-17">[17]</a></sup> The bowl had a diameter of 65&#160;ft (20&#160;m), tilted at a 15° angle to optimize the cost/yield relation (33° would have maximized yield). The rim of the hemisphere was "trimmed" to 60°, creating a maximum aperture of 3,318 square feet (308.3&#160;m<sup>2</sup>). This pilot bowl produced electricity at a rate of 10&#160;kW peak.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A 15-meter diameter Auroville solar bowl was developed from an earlier test of a 3.5-meter bowl in 1979–1982 by the <a href="/wiki/Tata_Energy_Research_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Tata Energy Research Institute">Tata Energy Research Institute</a>. That test showed the use of the solar bowl in the production of steam for cooking. The full-scale project to build a solar bowl and kitchen ran from 1996, and was fully operational by 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar-thermal_collectors_heating_air">Solar-thermal collectors heating air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solar-thermal collectors heating air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A simple solar air collector consists of an absorber material, sometimes having a selective surface, to capture radiation from the sun and transfers this thermal energy to air via conduction heat transfer. This heated air is then ducted to the building space or to the process area where the heated air is used for space heating or process heating needs. Functioning in a similar manner as a conventional forced air furnace, solar-thermal-air systems provide heat by circulating air over an energy collecting surface, absorbing the sun’s thermal energy, and ducting air coming in contact with it. Simple and effective collectors can be made for a variety of air conditioning and process applications.</p>
<p>A variety of applications can utilize solar air heat technologies to reduce the carbon footprint from use of conventional heat sources, such as fossil fuels, to create a sustainable means to produce thermal energy. Applications such as space heating, greenhouse season extension, pre-heating ventilation makeup air, or process heat can be addressed by solar air heat devices. In the field of ‘solar co-generation’ solar thermal technologies are paired with photovoltaics (PV) to increase the efficiency of the system by taking heat away from the PV collectors, cooling the PV panels to improve their electrical performance while simultaneously warming air for space heating.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_heating_and_ventilating">Space heating and ventilating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Space heating and ventilating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Space heating for residential and commercial applications can be done through the use of solar air heating panels. This configuration operates by drawing air from the building envelope or from the outdoor environment and passing it through the collector where the air warms via conduction from the absorber and is then supplied to the living or working space by either passive means or with the assistance of a fan. A pioneering figure of this type of system was George Löf, who built solar heated air system for a house in Boulder, Colorado, in 1945. He later included a gravel bed for heat storage.</p>
<p>Ventilation, fresh air or makeup air is required in most commercial, industrial and institutional buildings to meet code requirements. By drawing air through a properly designed unglazed transpired air collector or an air heater the solar heated fresh air can reduce the heating load during daytime operation. Many applications are now being installed where the transpired collector preheats the fresh air entering a heat recovery ventilator to reduce the defrost time of HRV's. The higher your ventilation and temperature the better your payback time will be.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_heating">Process heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Process heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solar air heat is also used in process applications such as drying laundry, crops (i.e. tea, corn, coffee) and other drying applications. Air heated through a solar collector and then passed over a medium to be dried can provide an efficient means by which to reduce the moisture content of the material.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_air_heating_collector_types">Solar air heating collector types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Solar air heating collector types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Collectors are commonly classified by their air-ducting methods as one of three types:</p>
<ul>
<li>through-pass collectors</li>
<li>front-pass</li>
<li>back pass</li>
<li>combination front and back pass collectors</li>
</ul>
<p>Collectors can also be classified by their outer surface:</p>
<ul>
<li>glazed</li>
<li>unglazed</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Through-pass_air_collector">Through-pass air collector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Through-pass air collector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Offering the highest efficiency of any solar technology the through-pass configuration, air ducted onto one side of the absorber passes through a perforated material and is heated from the conductive properties of the material and the convective properties of the moving air. Through-pass absorbers have the most surface area which enables relatively high conductive heat transfer rates, but significant pressure drop can require greater fan power, and deterioration of certain absorber material after many years of solar radiation exposure can additionally create problems with air quality and performance.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Back.2C_front.2C_and_combination_passage_air_collector">Back, front, and combination passage air collector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Back, front, and combination passage air collector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In back-pass, front-pass, and combination type configurations the air is directed on either the back, the front, or on both sides of the absorber to be heated from the return to the supply ducting headers. Although passing the air on both sides of the absorber will provide a greater surface area for conductive heat transfer, issues with dust (fouling) can arise from passing air on the front side of the absorber which reduces absorber efficiency by limiting the amount of sunlight received. In cold climates, air passing next to the glazing will additionally cause greater heat loss, resulting in lower overall performance of the collector.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Glazed_systems">Glazed systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Glazed systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Glazed systems usually have a transparent top sheet and insulated side and back panels to minimize heat loss to ambient air. The absorber plates in modern panels can have <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorptivity</a> of more than 93%. Glazed Solar Collectors (recirculating types that are usually used for space heating). Air typically passes along the front or back of the absorber plate while scrubbing heat directly from it. Heated air can then be distributed directly for applications such as space heating and drying or may be stored for later use. Payback for glazed solar air heating panels can be less than 9–15 years depending on the fuel being replaced.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unglazed_systems">Unglazed systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Unglazed systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unglazed systems, or transpired air systems have been used to heat make-up or ventilation air in commercial, industrial, agriculture and process applications. They consist of an absorber plate which air passes across or through as it scrubs heat from the absorber. Non-tranparent glazing materials are less expensive, and decrease expected payback periods. Transpired collectors are considered "unglazed" because their collector surfaces are exposed to the elements, are often not transparent and not hermetically sealed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unglazed_transpired_solar_collectors">Unglazed transpired solar collectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Unglazed transpired solar collectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The term "unglazed air collector" refers to a solar air heating system that consists of a metal absorber without any glass or glazing over top. The most common type of unglazed collector on the market is the transpired solar collector. The technology has been extensively monitored by these government agencies, and Natural Resources Canada developed the feasibility tool RETScreen™ to model the energy savings from transpired solar collectors. Since that time, several thousand transpired solar collector systems have been installed in a variety of commercial, industrial, institutional, agricultural, and process applications in countries around the world. The technology was originally used primarily in industrial applications such as manufacturing and assembly plants where there were high ventilation requirements, stratified ceiling heat, and often negative pressure in the building. With the increasing drive to install renewable energy systems on buildings, transpired solar collectors are now used across the entire building stock because of high energy production (up to 750 peak thermal Watts/square metre), high solar conversion (up to 90%) and lower capital costs when compared against solar photovoltaic and solar water heating.</p>

<p>Solar air heating is a renewable energy heating technology used to heat or condition air for buildings or process heat applications. It is typically the most cost-effective of all the solar technologies, especially in large scale applications, and it addresses the largest usage of building energy in heating climates, which is space heating and industrial process heating. They are either glazed or unglazed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_operation">Method of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Method of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unglazed air collectors heat ambient (outside) air instead of recirculated building air. Transpired solar collectors are usually wall-mounted to capture the lower sun angle in the winter heating months as well as sun reflection off the snow and achieve their optimum performance and return on investment when operating at flow rates of between 4 and 8 CFM per square foot (72 to 144 m3/h.m2) of collector area.</p>
<p>The exterior surface of a transpired solar collector consists of thousands of tiny micro-perforations that allow the boundary layer of heat to be captured and uniformly drawn into an air cavity behind the exterior panels. This heated ventilation air is drawn under negative pressure into the building’s ventilation system where it is then distributed via conventional means or using a solar ducting system.</p>
<p>Hot air that may enter an HVAC system connected to a transpired collector that has air outlets positioned along the top of the collector, particularly if the collector is west facing. To counter this problem, Matrix Energy has patented a transpired collector with a lower air outlet position and perforated cavity framing to perpetrate increased air turbulence behind the perforated absorber for increased performance.</p>
<p>This cutaway view shows the MatrixAir transpired solar collector components and air flow. The lower air inlet mitigates the intake of heated air to the HVAC system during summer operation.</p>
<p>The extensive monitoring by Natural Resources Canada and NREL has shown that transpired solar collector systems reduce between 10-50% of the conventional heating load and that RETScreen is an accurate predictor of system performance. Transpired solar collectors act as a rainscreen and they also capture heat loss escaping from the building envelope which is collected in the collector air cavity and drawn back into the ventilation system. There is no maintenance required with solar air heating systems and the expected lifespan is over 30 years.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_of_transpired_solar_collectors">Variations of transpired solar collectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Variations of transpired solar collectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unglazed transpired collectors can also be roof-mounted for applications in which there is not a suitable south facing wall or for other architectural considerations. Matrix Energy Inc. has patented a roof mounted product called the “Delta” a modular, roof-mounted solar air heating system where southerly, east or west facing facades are simply not available.</p>
<p>Each ten foot (3.05 m) module will deliver 250 CFM (425 m3/h)of preheated fresh air typically providing annual energy savings of 1100 kWh (4 GJ) annually. This unique two stage, modular roof mounted transpired collector operating a nearly 90% efficiency each module delivering over 118 l/s of preheated air per two square meter collector. Up to seven collectors may be connected in series in one row, with no limit to the number of rows connected in parallel along one central duct typically yielding 4 CFM of preheated air per square foot of available roof area. +</p>
<p>Transpired collectors can be configured to heat the air twice to increase the delivered air temperature making it suitable for space heating applications as well as ventilation air heating. In a 2-stage system, the first stage is the typical unglazed transpired collector and the second stage has glazing covering the transpired collector. The glazing allows all of that heated air from the first stage to be directed through a second set of transpired collectors for a second stage of solar heating.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar-thermal_collectors_generating_electricity">Solar-thermal collectors generating electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Solar-thermal collectors generating electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">Parabolic troughs</a>, dishes and towers described in this section are used almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_generating_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar power generating station">solar power generating stations</a> or for research purposes. Although simple, these solar concentrators are quite far from the theoretical maximum concentration.<sup id="cite_ref-IntroNio2e_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IntroNio2e-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> For example, the parabolic trough concentration is about 1/3 of the theoretical maximum for the same <a href="/wiki/Acceptance_angle_(solar_concentrator)" title="Acceptance angle (solar concentrator)">acceptance angle</a>, that is, for the same overall tolerances for the system. Approaching the theoretical maximum may be achieved by using more elaborate concentrators based on <a href="/wiki/Nonimaging_optics" title="Nonimaging optics">nonimaging optics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IntroNio2e_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IntroNio2e-18">[18]</a></sup> Solar thermal collectors may also be used in conjunction with photovoltaic collectors to obtain combined heat and power.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parabolic_trough">Parabolic trough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Parabolic trough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>This type of collector is generally used in <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> plants. A trough-shaped <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflector</a> is used to concentrate sunlight on an insulated tube (<a href="/wiki/Dewar_tube" class="mw-redirect" title="Dewar tube">Dewar tube</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Heat_pipe" title="Heat pipe">heat pipe</a>, placed at the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focal point</a>, containing <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> which transfers heat from the collectors to the <a href="/wiki/Boilers" class="mw-redirect" title="Boilers">boilers</a> in the power station.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parabolic_dish">Parabolic dish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Parabolic dish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parabolic-dish.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parabolic-dish.jpg/220px-Parabolic-dish.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parabolic-dish.jpg/330px-Parabolic-dish.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Parabolic-dish.jpg/440px-Parabolic-dish.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parabolic-dish.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Solar Parabolic dish</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With a parabolic dish collector, one or more <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabolic</a> dishes concentrate solar energy at a single focal point, similar to the way a <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a> focuses starlight, or a <a href="/wiki/Dish_antenna" class="mw-redirect" title="Dish antenna">dish antenna</a> focuses radio waves. This geometry may be used in <a href="/wiki/Solar_furnace" title="Solar furnace">solar furnaces</a> and solar power plants.</p>
<p>The shape of a parabola means that incoming light rays which are parallel to the dish's axis will be reflected toward the focus, no matter where on the dish they arrive. Light from the sun arrives at the Earth's surface almost completely parallel. So the dish is aligned with its axis pointing at the sun, allowing almost all incoming radiation to be reflected towards the focal point of the dish. Most losses in such collectors are due to imperfections in the parabolic shape and imperfect reflection.</p>
<p>Losses due to atmospheric scattering are generally minimal. However, on a hazy or foggy day, light is diffused in all directions through the atmosphere, which reduces the efficiency of a parabolic dish significantly.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power#Dish_Stirling" title="Concentrated solar power">dish stirling</a> power plant designs, a <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">stirling engine</a> coupled to a dynamo, is placed at the focus of the dish. This absorbs the energy focused onto it and converts it into electricity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_tower">Power tower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_thermal_collector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Power tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">Solar power tower</a></div>
<p>A power tower is a large tower surrounded by tracking mirrors called <a href="/wiki/Heliostat" title="Heliostat">heliostats</a>. These mirrors align themselves and focus sunlight on the receiver at the top of tower, collected heat is transferred to a power station below. This design reaches very high temperatures. High temperatures are suitable for electricity generation using conventional methods like <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> or a direct high temperature chemical reaction such as liquid salt.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By concentrating sunlight current systems can get better efficiency than simple solar cells. A larger area can be covered by using relatively inexpensive mirrors rather than using expensive <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>. Concentrated light can be redirected to a suitable location via <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber_cable" title="Optical fiber cable">optical fiber cable</a> for such uses as illuminating buildings. Heat storage for power production during cloudy and overnight conditions can be accomplished, often by underground tank storage of heated fluids. <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">Molten salts</a> have been used to good effect. Other working fluids, such as liquid metals, have also been proposed due to their superior thermal properties.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, concentrating systems require <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">sun tracking</a> to maintain sunlight focus at the collector. They are unable to provide significant power in <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffused light</a> conditions. Solar cells are able to provide some output even if the sky becomes cloudy, but power output from concentrating systems drops drastically in cloudy conditions as diffused light cannot be concentrated.</p>
