			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">E. F. Schumacher</h1>

<p><b>Ernst Friedrich "Fritz" Schumacher</b> (19 August 1911 – 4 September 1977) was an internationally influential economic thinker, statistician and economist in Britain, serving as Chief Economic Advisor to the UK <a href="/wiki/National_Coal_Board" title="National Coal Board">National Coal Board</a> for two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-SiBbiog_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiBbiog-1">[1]</a></sup> His ideas became popularised in much of the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> during the 1970s. He is best known for his critique of Western economies and his proposals for human-scale, decentralised and <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technologies</a>.</p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_Literary_Supplement" title="The Times Literary Supplement">The Times Literary Supplement</a></i> in 1995, his 1973 book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a>: a study of economics as if people mattered</i> was among the 100 most influential books published since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TLS100_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLS100-2">[2]</a></sup> It was soon translated into many languages, bringing him international fame. Schumacher's basic <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">development theories</a> have been summed up in the catch-phrases <i><a href="/wiki/Intermediate_Size" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermediate Size">Intermediate Size</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Intermediate_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermediate Technology">Intermediate Technology</a></i>. In 1977 he published <i><a href="/wiki/A_Guide_for_the_Perplexed" title="A Guide for the Perplexed">A Guide for the Perplexed</a></i> as a critique of materialist <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a> and as an exploration of the nature and organisation of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>.</p>
<p>Together with long-time friends and associates like <a href="/wiki/Mansur_Hoda" title="Mansur Hoda">Professor Mansur Hoda</a>, Schumacher founded the <i>Intermediate Technology Development Group</i> (now <a href="/wiki/Practical_Action" title="Practical Action">Practical Action</a>) in 1966.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schumacher was born in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, Germany in 1911. His father was a professor of <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>. The younger Schumacher studied in Bonn and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, then from 1930 in England as a <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship" title="Rhodes Scholarship">Rhodes Scholar</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_College,_Oxford" title="New College, Oxford">New College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SiBbiog_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiBbiog-1">[1]</a></sup> and later at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, earning a diploma in economics. He then worked in business, farming and journalism.<sup id="cite_ref-SiBbiog_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiBbiog-1">[1]</a></sup> His sister, Elizabeth, was the wife of the physicist <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economist">Economist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prot.C3.A9g.C3.A9_of_Keynes">Protégé of Keynes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protégé of Keynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Schumacher moved back to England before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, as he had no intention of living under <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>. For a period during the War, he was <a href="/wiki/Interned" class="mw-redirect" title="Interned">interned</a> on an isolated English farm as an "enemy alien". In these years, Schumacher captured the attention of <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> with a paper entitled "Multilateral Clearing"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> that he had written between sessions working in the fields of the internment camp. Keynes recognised the young German's understanding and abilities, and he was able to have Schumacher released from internment. Schumacher helped the <a href="/wiki/British_government" class="mw-redirect" title="British government">British government</a> mobilise economically and financially during World War II, and Keynes found a position for him at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Kohr" title="Leopold Kohr">Leopold Kohr</a>'s obituary for Schumacher, when "Multilateral Clearing" "was published in the spring of 1943 in <i>Economica</i>, it caused some embarrassment to Keynes who, instead of arranging for its separate publication, had incorporated the text almost verbatim in his famous "<a href="/wiki/Plan_for_an_International_Clearing_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Plan for an International Clearing Union">Plan for an International Clearing Union</a>", which the British government issued as a <a href="/wiki/White_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="White Paper">White Paper</a> a few weeks later."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adviser_to_the_Coal_Board">Adviser to the Coal Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adviser to the Coal Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the War, Schumacher worked as an economic advisor to, and later Chief Statistician for, the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Commission" title="Allied Commission">British Control Commission</a>, which was charged with rebuilding the <a href="/wiki/German_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="German economy">German economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SiBbiog_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiBbiog-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1950 to 1970 he was Chief Economic Adviser to the <a href="/wiki/National_Coal_Board" title="National Coal Board">National Coal Board</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SiBbiog_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiBbiog-1">[1]</a></sup> one of the world's largest organisations, with 800,000 employees. In this position, he argued that coal, not petroleum, should be used to supply the energy needs of the world's population. He saw oil as a <a href="/wiki/Finite_resource" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite resource">finite resource</a>, fearing its depletion and eventually prohibitive <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a>, and viewed with alarm the reality that "the richest and cheapest reserves are located in some of the world's most unstable countries"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His position on the Coal Board was often mentioned later by those introducing Schumacher or his ideas. It is generally thought that his farsighted planning contributed to Britain's post-war economic recovery. Schumacher predicted the rise of <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a> and many of the problems of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thinking_outside_the_box">Thinking outside the box</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thinking outside the box">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1955 Schumacher travelled to <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> as an economic consultant. While there, he developed the set of principles he called "<a href="/wiki/Buddhist_economics" title="Buddhist economics">Buddhist economics</a>", based on the belief that individuals need good work for proper <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" title="Human development (humanity)">human development</a>. He also proclaimed that "production from local resources for local needs is the most rational way of economic life." He travelled throughout many <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> countries, encouraging local governments to create <a href="/wiki/Self-reliant_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-reliant economy">self-reliant economies</a>. Schumacher's experience led him to become a pioneer of what is now called <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a>: <a href="/wiki/User-friendly" class="mw-redirect" title="User-friendly">user-friendly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecologically" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecologically">ecologically</a> suitable technology applicable to the scale of the community; a concept very close to <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Illich" title="Ivan Illich">Ivan Illich</a>'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conviviality&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Conviviality (page does not exist)">conviviality</a>. He founded the Intermediate Technology Development Group (now <a href="/wiki/Practical_Action" title="Practical Action">Practical Action</a>) in 1966. His theories of development have been summed up for many in catch phrases such as "<a href="/wiki/Intermediate_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermediate size">intermediate size</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Intermediate_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermediate technology">intermediate technology</a>". He was a <a href="/wiki/Trustee" title="Trustee">trustee</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scott_Bader_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Scott Bader Commonwealth">Scott Bader Commonwealth</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and in 1970 the president of the <a href="/wiki/Soil_Association" title="Soil Association">Soil Association</a>.</p>
<p>E F Schumacher was greatly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._C._Kumarappa" title="J. C. Kumarappa">J. C. Kumarappa</a> and Gandhi's concepts of "Economy of Permanence" and appropriate technology. While delivering the Gandhi Memorial Lecture at the Gandhian Institute of Studies at <a href="/wiki/Varanasi" title="Varanasi">Varanasi</a> (India) in 1973, Schumacher described Gandhi as the greatest '<b>People's Economist'</b> whose economic thinking was compatible with spirituality as opposed to materialism.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schumacher_as_writer">Schumacher as writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Schumacher as writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schumacher wrote on economics for London's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and became one of the paper's chief <a href="/wiki/Editorial_board" title="Editorial board">editorial writers</a>. At this post he was assigned the somewhat uncomfortable task of compiling information for the obituary of John Maynard Keynes many years before the event of his death. He also wrote for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Resurgence_%26_Ecologist" title="Resurgence &amp; Ecologist">Resurgence</a></i>. He served as adviser to the <a href="/wiki/India_Planning_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="India Planning Commission">India Planning Commission</a>, as well as to the governments of Zambia and Burma – an experience that led to his much-read essay "Buddhist Economics".</p>
<p>The 1973 publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Small_is_Beautiful" class="mw-redirect" title="Small is Beautiful">Small is Beautiful: a study of economics as if people mattered</a></i>, a collection of essays, finished in the house of his friend Leopold Kohr, brought his ideas to a wider audience. One of his main arguments in <i>Small is Beautiful</i> is that we cannot consider the problem of technological production solved if it requires that we recklessly erode our finite natural capital and deprive future generations of its benefits. Schumacher's work coincided with the growth of ecological concerns and with the birth of environmentalism, and he became a hero to many in the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Community movement (page does not exist)">community movement</a>.</p>
<p>In 1976, he received the prestigious award <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prix_Europ%C3%A9en_de_l%27Essai_Charles_Veillon" class="extiw" title="fr:Prix Européen de l'Essai Charles Veillon">Prix Européen de l'Essai Charles Veillon</a> for <i>Small is Beautiful</i>.</p>
<p>His 1977 work <i><a href="/wiki/A_Guide_for_the_Perplexed" title="A Guide for the Perplexed">A Guide for the Perplexed</a></i> is both a critique of <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a> and an exploration of the nature and organisation of knowledge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a young man, Schumacher was a dedicated atheist, but his later rejection of materialist, capitalist, agnostic modernity was paralleled by a growing fascination with religion.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He developed an interest in Buddhism, but beginning in the late 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Catholicism</a> heavily influenced his thinking. He noted the similarities between his own economic views and the teaching of papal encyclicals on socio-economic issues, from <a href="/wiki/Leo_XIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Leo XIII">Leo XIII</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Rerum_novarum" title="Rerum novarum">Rerum novarum</a>" to <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Mater_et_magistra" class="mw-redirect" title="Mater et magistra">Mater et magistra</a></i>, as well as with the <a href="/wiki/Distributism" title="Distributism">distributism</a> supported by the Catholic thinkers <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Belloc" title="Hilaire Belloc">Hilaire Belloc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vincent_McNabb" title="Vincent McNabb">Vincent McNabb</a>. Philosophically, he absorbed much of <a href="/wiki/Thomism" title="Thomism">Thomism</a>, which provided an objective system in contrast to what he saw as the self-centered <a href="/wiki/Subjectivism" title="Subjectivism">subjectivism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relativism" title="Relativism">relativism</a> of modern philosophy and society.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He also was greatly interested in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Christian_mysticism" title="Christian mysticism">Christian mysticism</a> and read deeply such writers as St. <a href="/wiki/Teresa_of_Avila" class="mw-redirect" title="Teresa of Avila">Teresa of Avila</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Merton" title="Thomas Merton">Thomas Merton</a>. These were all interests that he shared with his friend, the Catholic writer <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Derrick" title="Christopher Derrick">Christopher Derrick</a>. In 1971, he converted to Catholicism.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schumacher gave interviews and published articles for a wide readership in his later years. He also pursued one of the loves of his life: gardening. He died of a heart attack on 4 September 1977, in Switzerland, during a <a href="/wiki/Lecture_tour" class="mw-redirect" title="Lecture tour">lecture tour</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schumacher's personal collection of books and archives are held by the <a href="/wiki/Schumacher_Center_for_New_Economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Schumacher Center for New Economics">Schumacher Center for New Economics</a> library in <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrington,_Massachusetts" title="Great Barrington, Massachusetts">Great Barrington, Massachusetts</a>. The Center continues the work of E. F. Schumacher by maintaining a research library, organising lectures and seminars, publishing papers, developing model economic programs, and providing technical assistance to groups all for the purpose of linking people, land, and community to build strong, diverse local economies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schumacher_Circle">Schumacher Circle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E._F._Schumacher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Schumacher Circle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Schumacher Circle</b> is a family of organisations which were founded in E.F. Schumacher's memory or were inspired by his work, and which co-operate to support each other. The circle includes<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Schumacher_College" title="Schumacher College">Schumacher College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Totnes" title="Totnes">Totnes</a>, Devon, <i>Resurgence Magazine</i> (now <a href="/wiki/Resurgence_%26_Ecologist" title="Resurgence &amp; Ecologist">Resurgence &amp; Ecologist</a>), publishing company <i>Green Books</i>, international non-governmental organisation <a href="/wiki/Practical_Action" title="Practical Action">Practical Action</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Economics_Foundation" title="New Economics Foundation">New Economics Foundation</a> in the UK, the <a href="/wiki/Schumacher_Center_for_a_New_Economics" title="Schumacher Center for a New Economics">Schumacher Center for a New Economics</a> (heir to the legacy programs of the former <i>E. F. Schumacher Society</i>) founded in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Soil_Association" title="Soil Association">Soil Association</a>, the educational <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Alternative_Technology" title="Centre for Alternative Technology">Centre for Alternative Technology</a> (CAT) North Wales, the <i>Jeevika Trust</i>, and the research organisation <i>Schumacher Institute</i> in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>.</p>
