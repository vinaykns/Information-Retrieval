			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Geothermal power in Iceland</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Borehole.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/120px-Borehole.jpg" width="120" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/180px-Borehole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Borehole.jpg/240px-Borehole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="450" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Borehole.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Geothermal borehole outside the <a href="/wiki/Reykjanes_Power_Station" title="Reykjanes Power Station">Reykjanes Power Station</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Due to the geological location of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> (over a rift in continental plates), the high concentration of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> in the area is often an advantage in the generation of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>, the heating and making of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, pavements near these areas (such as <a href="/wiki/Reykjavik" class="mw-redirect" title="Reykjavik">Reykjavik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akureyri" title="Akureyri">Akureyri</a>) are heated up.</p>
<p>Five major <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a> power plants exist in Iceland, which produce approximately 26.2% (2010)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> of the nation's electricity. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a> meets the heating and hot water requirements of approximately 87% of all buildings in Iceland. Apart from geothermal energy, 73.8% of the nation’s electricity is generated by <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydro power</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and 0.1% from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Consumption of primary geothermal energy in 2004 was 79.7 <a href="/wiki/Petajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Petajoule">petajoules (PJ)</a>, approximately 53.4% of the total national consumption of <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">primary energy</a>, 149.1 PJ. The corresponding share for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydro power</a> was 17.2%, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> was 26.3%, and coal was 3%.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Plans are underway to turn Iceland into a 100% fossil-fuel-free nation in the near future.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, Iceland's abundant geothermal energy has enabled renewable energy initiatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a>'s carbon dioxide to methanol fuel process.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The following are the five largest power stations in Iceland.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hellishei%C3%B0i_Power_Station" title="Hellisheiði Power Station">Hellisheiði Power Station</a> (303 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nesjavellir_Geothermal_Power_Station" title="Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station">Nesjavellir Geothermal Power Station</a> (120 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reykjanes_Power_Station" title="Reykjanes Power Station">Reykjanes Power Station</a> (100 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Svartsengi_Power_Station" title="Svartsengi Power Station">Svartsengi Power Station</a> (76.5 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Krafla_Power_Station" title="Krafla Power Station">Krafla Power Station</a> (60 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a>)</li>
</ol>
