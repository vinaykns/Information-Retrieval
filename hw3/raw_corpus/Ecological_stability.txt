			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecological stability</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Ecological stability</b> can refer to types of stability in a continuum ranging from <a href="/wiki/Regeneration_(ecology)" title="Regeneration (ecology)">regeneration</a> via <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a> (returning quickly to a previous state), to constancy to persistence. The precise definition depends on the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> in question, the variable or variables of interest, and the overall context. In the context of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation ecology</a>, stable <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">populations</a> are often defined as ones that do not go extinct. Researchers applying <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical models</a> from system <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a> usually use <a href="/wiki/Lyapunov_stability" title="Lyapunov stability">Lyapunov stability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_ecological_stability">Types of ecological stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_stability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of ecological stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Local_stability" class="mw-redirect" title="Local stability">Local stability</a> indicates that a system is stable over small short-lived disturbances, while global stability indicates a system highly resistant to change in <a href="/wiki/Species_composition" class="mw-redirect" title="Species composition">species composition</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Food_web_dynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Food web dynamics">food web dynamics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constancy">Constancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_stability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Constancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Observational studies of ecosystems use <b>constancy</b> to describe living systems that can remain unchanged.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_and_inertia_.28persistence.29">Resistance and inertia (persistence)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_stability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resistance and inertia (persistence)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Resistance</i> and <i>inertia</i> deal with a system's inherent response to some perturbation.</p>
<p>A perturbation is any externally imposed change in conditions, usually happening in a short time period. <b>Resistance</b> is a measure of how little the variable of interest changes in response to external pressures. <b>Inertia</b> (or persistence) implies that the living system is able to resist external fluctuations. In the context of changing <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> in post-glacial North America, <a href="/wiki/E.C._Pielou" class="mw-redirect" title="E.C. Pielou">E.C. Pielou</a> remarked at the outset of her overview,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"It obviously takes considerable time for mature vegetation to become established on newly exposed ice scoured rocks or glacial till...it also takes considerable time for whole ecosystems to change, with their numerous interdependent plant species, the habitats these create, and the animals that live in the habitats. Therefore, climatically caused fluctuations in ecological communities are a damped, smoothed-out version of the climatic fluctuations that cause them."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resilience.2C_elasticity_and_amplitude">Resilience, elasticity and amplitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_stability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Resilience, elasticity and amplitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">Resilience</a></i> is the tendency of a system to retain its functional and organisational structure after a perturbation. <i>Elasticity</i> and <i>amplitude</i> are measures of resilience. Elasticity is the speed with which a system returns. Amplitude is a measure of how far a system can be moved from the previous state and still return. Ecology borrows the idea of neighborhood stability and a domain of attraction from <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a> theory.</p>
