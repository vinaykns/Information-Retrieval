			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Precautionary principle</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>precautionary principle</b> (or <b>precautionary approach</b>) to <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> states that if an action or policy has a suspected risk of causing harm to the <a href="/wiki/General_public" class="mw-redirect" title="General public">public,</a> or to the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> (that the action or policy is not harmful), the <a href="/wiki/Legal_burden_of_proof" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal burden of proof">burden of proof</a> that it is <i>not</i> harmful falls on those taking that action.</p>
<p>The principle is used by policy makers to justify discretionary decisions in situations where there is the possibility of harm from making a certain decision (e.g. taking a particular course of action) when extensive scientific knowledge on the matter is lacking. The principle implies that there is a <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a> to protect the public from exposure to harm, when scientific investigation has found a plausible risk. These protections can be relaxed only if further scientific findings emerge that provide sound evidence that no harm will result.</p>
<p>In some legal systems, as in <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of the European Union">law of the European Union</a>, the application of the precautionary principle has been made a <a href="/wiki/Statutory" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory">statutory</a> requirement in some areas of law.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding international conduct, the first endorsement of the principle was in 1982 when the <a href="/wiki/World_Charter_for_Nature" title="World Charter for Nature">World Charter for Nature</a> was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>, while its first international implementation was in 1987 through the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>. Soon after, the principle integrated with many other legally binding international treaties such as the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_theory">Origins and theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept "precautionary principle" is generally considered to have arisen in English from a translation of the German term <i>Vorsorgeprinzip</i> in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Christiansen_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christiansen-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31</sup></p>
<p>The concepts underpinning the precautionary principle pre-date the term's inception. For example, the essence of the principle is captured in a number of cautionary <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">aphorisms</a> such as "an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure", "better safe than sorry", and "look before you leap". The precautionary principle may also be interpreted as the evolution of the "ancient-medical principle" of "<a href="/wiki/Primum_non_nocere" title="Primum non nocere">first, do no harm</a>" to apply to institutions and institutional decision-making processes rather than individuals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole sentence (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In economics, the Precautionary Principle has been analysed in terms of "the effect on rational decision-making", of "the interaction of <a href="/wiki/Irreversibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Irreversibility">irreversibility</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a>". Authors such as Epstein (1980)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and Arrow and Fischer (1974)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> show that "irreversibility of possible future consequences" creates a "quasi-<a href="/wiki/Option_(finance)" title="Option (finance)">option</a> effect" which should induce a "<a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a>-neutral" society to favour current decisions that allow for more flexibility in the future. Gollier et al. (2000)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> conclude that "more scientific uncertainty as to the distribution of a future risk – that is, a larger variability of beliefs – should induce society to take stronger prevention measures today."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="citation with page number required (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formulations">Formulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many definitions of the precautionary principle exist: Precaution may be defined as "caution in advance," "caution practised in the context of uncertainty," or informed <a href="/wiki/Prudence" title="Prudence">prudence</a>. Two ideas lie at the core of the principle:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34</sup></p>
<ol>
<li>an expression of a need by decision-makers to anticipate harm before it occurs. Within this element lies an implicit reversal of the onus of proof: under the precautionary principle it is the responsibility of an activity-proponent to establish that the proposed activity will not (or is very unlikely to) result in significant harm.</li>
<li>the concept of proportionality of the risk and the cost and feasibility of a proposed action</li>
</ol>
<p>One of the primary foundations of the precautionary principle, and globally accepted definitions, results from the work of the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Conference">Rio Conference</a>, or "<a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a>" in 1992. The principle 15 of the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a> notes: "In order to protect the environment, the precautionary approach shall be widely applied by States according to their capabilities. Where there are threats of serious or irreversible damage, lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent environmental degradation."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1998 <a href="/wiki/Wingspread_Conference_on_the_Precautionary_Principle" title="Wingspread Conference on the Precautionary Principle">Wingspread Statement on the Precautionary Principle</a> summarises the principle this way: "When an activity raises threats of harm to human health or the environment, precautionary measures should be taken even if some cause and effect relationships are not fully established scientifically."<sup id="cite_ref-SEHN-Wingspread_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEHN-Wingspread-8">[8]</a></sup> The Wingspread Conference on the Precautionary Principle was convened by the Science and Environmental Health Network.<sup id="cite_ref-SEHN-Wingspread_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEHN-Wingspread-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2 February 2000, Commission of the European Communities, <i>Communication from the Commission on the Precautionary Principle</i>, noted that, "The precautionary principle is not defined in the <a href="/wiki/Treaties_of_the_European_Union" title="Treaties of the European Union">Treaty</a>, which prescribes it [the Precautionary Principle] only once – to protect the environment. But in practice, its scope is much wider, and specifically where preliminary-objective-scientific-evaluation indicates that there are reasonable grounds for concern that potentially dangerous effects on the environment, human, animal or [and] plant health may be inconsistent with the high level of protection [for what] chosen for the Community."<sup id="cite_ref-EU-Comm-PP_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU-Comm-PP-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:10</sup></p>
<p>The 29 January 2000 <a href="/wiki/Cartagena_Protocol_on_Biosafety" title="Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety">Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety</a> says, in regard to controversies over <a href="/wiki/GMO" class="mw-redirect" title="GMO">GMO</a>s: "Lack of scientific certainty due to insufficient relevant scientific information ... shall not prevent the Party of [I]mport, in order to avoid or minimize such potential adverse effects, from taking a decision, as appropriate, with regard to the import of the living modified organism in question."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:6</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The application of the precautionary principle is hampered by both lack of political will,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as well as the wide range of interpretations placed on it. One study identified 14 different formulations of the principle in treaties and nontreaty declarations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> R.B. Stewart (2002)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> reduced the precautionary principle to four basic versions:</p>
<ol>
<li>Scientific uncertainty should not automatically preclude regulation of activities that pose a potential risk of significant harm (Non-Preclusion PP).</li>
<li>Regulatory controls should incorporate a margin of safety; activities should be limited below the level at which no adverse effect has been observed or predicted (Margin of Safety PP).</li>
<li>Activities that present an uncertain potential for significant harm should be subject to best technology available requirements to minimise the risk of harm unless the proponent of the activity shows that they present no appreciable risk of harm (BAT PP).</li>
<li>Activities that present an uncertain potential for significant harm should be prohibited unless the proponent of the activity shows that it presents no appreciable risk of harm (Prohibitory PP).</li>
</ol>
<p>In deciding how to apply the principle, analysis may use a <a href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a> that factors in both the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a> of not acting, and the <a href="/wiki/Option_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Option value">option value</a> of waiting for further information before acting. One of the difficulties of the application of the principle in modern policy-making is that there is often an irreducible conflict between different interests, so that the debate necessarily involves politics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong_vs._weak">Strong vs. weak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Strong vs. weak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Strong precaution</i> holds that regulation is required whenever there is a possible risk to health, safety, or the environment, even if the supporting evidence is speculative and even if the economic costs of regulation are high.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1295–96</sup> In 1982, the United Nations <a href="/wiki/World_Charter_for_Nature" title="World Charter for Nature">World Charter for Nature</a> gave the first international recognition to the strong version of the principle, suggesting that when "potential adverse effects are not fully understood, the activities should not proceed." The widely publicised <a href="/wiki/Wingspread_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Wingspread Declaration">Wingspread Declaration</a>, from a meeting of environmentalists in 1998, is another example of the strong version.<sup id="cite_ref-goliath.ecnext.com_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goliath.ecnext.com-14">[14]</a></sup> Strong precaution can also be termed as a "no-regrets" principle, where costs are not considered in preventative action.</p>
<p><i>Weak precaution</i> holds that lack of scientific evidence does not preclude action if damage would otherwise be serious and irreversible.<sup id="cite_ref-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1039</sup> Humans practice weak precaution every day, and often incur costs, to avoid hazards that are far from certain: we do not walk in moderately dangerous areas at night, we exercise, we buy smoke detectors, we buckle our seatbelts.<sup id="cite_ref-goliath.ecnext.com_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goliath.ecnext.com-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a publication by the New Zealand Treasury Department,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The weak version [of the Precautionary Principle] is the least restrictive and allows preventive measures to be taken in the face of uncertainty, but does not require them (eg, Rio Declaration 1992; United Nations Framework Convention of Climate Change 1992). To satisfy the threshold of harm, there must be some evidence relating to both the likelihood of occurrence and the severity of consequences. Some, but not all, require consideration of the costs of precautionary measures. Weak formulations do not preclude weighing benefits against the costs. Factors other than scientific uncertainty, including economic considerations, may provide legitimate grounds for postponing action. Under weak formulations, the requirement to justify the need for action (the burden of proof) generally falls on those advocating precautionary action. No mention is made of assignment of liability for environmental harm.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Strong versions justify or require precautionary measures and some also establish liability for environmental harm, which is effectively a strong form of "polluter pays". For example, the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Charter" title="Earth Charter">Earth Charter</a> states: "When knowledge is limited apply a precautionary approach ... Place the burden of proof on those who argue that a proposed activity will not cause significant harm, and make the responsible parties liable for environmental harm." Reversal of proof requires those proposing an activity to prove that the product, process or technology is sufficiently "safe" before approval is granted. Requiring proof of "no environmental harm" before any action proceeds implies the public is not prepared to accept any environmental risk, no matter what economic or social benefits may arise (Peterson, 2006). At the extreme, such a requirement could involve bans and prohibitions on entire classes of potentially threatening activities or substances (Cooney, 2005). Over time, there has been a gradual transformation of the precautionary principle from what appears in the Rio Declaration to a stronger form that arguably [by whom] acts as restraint on development in the absence of firm evidence that it will do no harm.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_agreements_and_declarations">International agreements and declarations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International agreements and declarations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The World Charter for Nature, which was adopted by the UN General Assembly in 1982, was the first international endorsement of the precautionary principle. The principle was implemented in an international treaty as early as the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>, and among other international treaties and declarations is reflected in the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration_on_Environment_and_Development" title="Rio Declaration on Environment and Development">Rio Declaration on Environment and Development</a> (signed at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Environment_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Conference on Environment and Development">United Nations Conference on Environment and Development</a>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Principle.22_vs._.22approach.22">"Principle" vs. "approach"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: &quot;Principle&quot; vs. &quot;approach&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<blockquote>
<p>No introduction to the precautionary principle would be complete without brief reference to the difference between the precautionary <b>principle</b> and the precautionary <b>approach</b>. Principle 15 of the Rio Declaration 1992 states that: "in order to protect the environment, the precautionary approach shall be widely applied by States according to their capabilities. Where there are threats of serious or irreversible damage, lack of full scientific certainty shall be not used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent environmental degradation." As Garcia (1995) pointed out, "the wording, largely similar to that of the principle, is subtly different in that: (1) it recognizes that there may be differences in local capabilities to apply the approach, and (2) it calls for cost-effectiveness in applying the approach, e.g., taking economic and social costs into account." The 'approach' is generally considered a softening of the 'principle'.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>"As Recuerda has noted, the distinction between the 'precautionary principle' and a 'precautionary approach' is diffuse and, in some contexts, controversial. In the negotiations of international declarations, the United States has opposed the use of the term 'principle' because this term has special connotations in legal language, due to the fact that a 'principle of law` is a source of law. This means that it is compulsory, so a court can quash or confirm a decision through the application of the precautionary principle. In this sense, the precautionary principle is not a simple idea or a desideratum but a source of law. This is the legal status of the precautionary principle in the European Union. On the other hand, an 'approach' usually does not have the same meaning, although in some particular cases an approach could be binding. A precautionary approach is a particular 'lens' used to identify risk that every prudent person possesses (Recuerda, 2008)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Commission">European Commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: European Commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 2 February 2000, the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> issued a Communication on the precautionary principle,<sup id="cite_ref-EU-Comm-PP_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU-Comm-PP-9">[9]</a></sup> in which it adopted a procedure for the application of this concept, but without giving a detailed definition of it. Paragraph 2 of article 191 of the <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisbon Treaty">Lisbon Treaty</a> states that</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>"Union policy on the environment shall aim at a high level of protection taking into account the diversity of situations in the various regions of the Union. It shall be based on the precautionary principle and on the principles that preventive action should be taken, that environmental damage should as a priority be rectified at source and that the polluter should pay."</i><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>After the adoption of the European Commission's Communication on the precautionary principle, the principle has come to inform much EU policy, including areas beyond environmental policy. As of 2006 it had been integrated into EU laws "in matters such as general product safety, the use of additives for use in animal nutrition, the incineration of waste, and the regulation of genetically modified organisms."<sup id="cite_ref-Recuerda_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Recuerda-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:282–83</sup> Through its application in <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a>, it has become a "general principle of EU law."<sup id="cite_ref-Recuerda_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Recuerda-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:283</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In France, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_for_the_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charter for the Environment (page does not exist)">Charter for the Environment</a> contains a formulation of the precautionary principle (article 5):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When the occurrence of any damage, albeit unpredictable in the current state of scientific knowledge, may seriously and irreversibly harm the environment, public authorities shall, with due respect for the principle of precaution and the areas within their jurisdiction, ensure the implementation of procedures for risk assessment and the adoption of temporary measures commensurate with the risk involved in order to preclude the occurrence of such damage.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 18 July 2005, the City of San Francisco passed a Precautionary Principle Purchasing ordinance,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> which requires the city to weigh the environmental and health costs of its $600&#160;million in annual purchases – for everything from cleaning supplies to computers. Members of the Bay Area Working Group on the Precautionary Principle including the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breast_Cancer_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Breast Cancer Fund (page does not exist)">Breast Cancer Fund</a>, helped bring this to fruition.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1997, Japan tried to use the consideration of the precautionary principle in a WTO SPS <a href="/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Application_of_Sanitary_and_Phytosanitary_Measures" title="Agreement on the Application of Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures">Agreement on the Application of Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures</a> case, as Japan's requirement to test each variety of agricultural products (apples, cherries, peaches, walnuts, apricots, pears, plums and quinces) for the efficacy of treatment against codling moths was challenged.</p>
<p>This moth is a pest that does not occur in Japan, and whose introduction has the potential to cause serious damage. The United States claimed that it was not necessary to test each variety of a fruit for the efficacy of the treatment, and that this varietal testing requirement was unnecessarily burdensome.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most important Australian court case so far, due to its exceptionally detailed consideration of the precautionary principle, is Telstra Corporation Limited v Hornsby Shire Council.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Principle was summarised by reference to the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">NSW</a> <i>Protection of the Environment Administration Act 1991</i>, which itself provides a good definition of the principle:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>"If there are threats of serious or irreversible environmental damage, lack of full scientific certainty should not be used as a reasoning for postponing measures to prevent environmental degradation. In the application of the principle… decisions should be guided by: (i) careful evaluation to avoid, wherever practicable, serious or irreversible damage to the environment; and (ii) an assessment of risk-weighted consequence of various options".</p>
<p>The most significant points of Justice Preston's decision are the following findings:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The principle and accompanying need to take precautionary measures is "triggered" when two prior conditions exist: a threat of serious or irreversible damage, and scientific uncertainty as to the extent of possible damage.</li>
<li>Once both are satisfied, "a proportionate precautionary measure may be taken to avert the anticipated threat of environmental damage, but it should be proportionate."</li>
<li>The threat of serious or irreversible damage should invoke consideration of five factors: the scale of threat (local, regional etc.); the perceived value of the threatened environment; whether the possible impacts are manageable; the level of public concern, and whether there is a rational or scientific basis for the concern.</li>
<li>The consideration of the level of scientific uncertainty should involve factors which may include: what would constitute sufficient evidence; the level and kind of uncertainty; and the potential to reduce uncertainty.</li>
<li>The principle shifts the burden of proof. If the principle applies, the burden shifts: "a decision maker must assume the threat of serious or irreversible environmental damage is… a reality [and] the burden of showing this threat… is negligible reverts to the proponent…"</li>
<li>The precautionary principle invokes preventative action: "the principle permits the taking of preventative measures without having to wait until the reality and seriousness of the threat become fully known".</li>
<li>"The precautionary principle should not be used to try to avoid all risks."</li>
<li>The precautionary measures appropriate will depend on the combined effect of "the degree of seriousness and irreversibility of the threat and the degree of uncertainty… the more significant and uncertain the threat, the greater…the precaution required". "…measures should be adopted… proportionate to the potential threats".</li>
</ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A petition filed 17 May 2013 by environmental group Greenpeace Southeast Asia and farmer-scientist coalition Masipag (<i>Magsasaka at Siyentipiko sa Pagpapaunlad ng Agrikultura</i>) asked the Appellate court to stop the planting of Bt eggplant in test fields, saying the impacts of such an undertaking to the environment, native crops and human health are still unknown. The Court of Appeals granted the petition, citing the precautionary principle stating "when human activities may lead to threats of serious and irreversible damage to the environment that is scientifically plausible but uncertain, actions shall be taken to avoid or diminish the threat."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Respondents filed a motion for reconsideration in June 2013 and on 20 September 2013 the Court of Appeals chose to uphold their May decision saying the <i>bt talong</i> field trials violate the people's constitutional right to a "balanced and healthful ecology."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Supreme Court on 8 December 2015 permanently stopped the field testing for Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) talong (eggplant), upholding the decision of the Court of Appeals which stopped the field trials for the genetically modified eggplant.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The court is the first in the world to adopt the precautionary principle regarding GMO products in its decision.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate">Corporate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Corporate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Body_Shop" class="mw-redirect" title="Body Shop">Body Shop International</a>, a UK-based cosmetics company, included the precautionary principle in their 2006 Chemicals Strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment_and_health">Environment and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environment and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fields typically concerned by the precautionary principle are the possibility of:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a> in general</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">Extinction</a> of species</li>
<li>Introduction of new and potentially harmful products into the environment, threatening <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically modified organisms</a>)</li>
<li>Threats to <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, due to new diseases and techniques (e.g., AIDS transmitted through blood transfusion)</li>
<li>Long-term effects of new technologies (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone_radiation_and_health" title="Mobile phone radiation and health">health concerns regarding radiation from cell phones</a> and other electronics communications devices)</li>
<li>Persistent or acute pollution (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptors" class="mw-redirect" title="Endocrine disruptors">endocrine disruptors</a>)</li>
<li>Food safety (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Creutzfeldt%E2%80%93Jakob_disease" title="Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease">Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease</a>)</li>
<li>Other new <a href="/wiki/Biosafety" title="Biosafety">biosafety</a> issues (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">artificial life</a>, new <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The precautionary principle is often applied to <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> fields because changes cannot be easily <a href="/wiki/Containment" title="Containment">contained</a> and have the potential of being global. The principle has less relevance to contained fields such as <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>, where the few people undergoing <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> have given <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>). In the case of technological innovation, containment of impact tends to be more difficult if that technology can self-replicate. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Joy" title="Bill Joy">Bill Joy</a> emphasised the dangers of replicating genetic technology, nanotechnology, and robotic technology in his article in <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/Why_the_future_doesn%27t_need_us" class="mw-redirect" title="Why the future doesn't need us">Why the future doesn't need us</a>", though he does not specifically cite the precautionary principle. The application of the principle can be seen in the public policy of requiring <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical companies</a> to carry out <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a> to show that new <a href="/wiki/Medications" class="mw-redirect" title="Medications">medications</a> are safe.</p>
<p>Oxford based philosopher <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bostrom" title="Nick Bostrom">Nick Bostrom</a> discusses the idea of a future powerful <a href="/wiki/Superintelligence" title="Superintelligence">superintelligence</a>, and the risks that we/it face should it attempt to gain atomic level control of matter.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Application of the principle modifies the status of innovation and <a href="/wiki/Risk_assessment" title="Risk assessment">risk assessment</a>: it is not the risk that must be avoided or amended, but a potential risk that must be prevented. Thus, in the case of regulation of scientific research, there is a third party beyond the scientist and the regulator: the consumer.</p>
<p>In an analysis concerning application of the precautionary principle to <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>, Chris Phoenix and Mike Treder posit that there are <i>two forms</i> of the principle, which they call the "strict form" and the "active form".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The former "requires inaction when action might pose a risk", while the latter means "choosing less risky alternatives when they are available, and [...] taking responsibility for potential risks." <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Alured_Faunce&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Alured Faunce (page does not exist)">Thomas Alured Faunce</a> has argued for stronger application of the precautionary principle by chemical and health technology regulators particularly in relation to Ti0<sub>2</sub> and ZnO nanoparticles in <a href="/wiki/Sunscreens" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunscreens">sunscreens</a>, biocidal nanosilver in waterways and products whose manufacture, handling or recycling exposes humans to the risk of inhaling multi-walled carbon nanotubes.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_management">Resource management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Resource management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HCR.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/HCR.gif/350px-HCR.gif" width="350" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/06/HCR.gif/525px-HCR.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/06/HCR.gif 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="425" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HCR.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Traffic Light colour convention, showing the concept of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvest_Control_Rule&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harvest Control Rule (page does not exist)">Harvest Control Rule</a> (HCR), specifying when a rebuilding plan is mandatory in terms of <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_approach" class="mw-redirect" title="Precautionary approach">precautionary</a> and limit reference points for <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing mortality">fishing mortality rate</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several natural resources like fish stocks are now managed by precautionary approach, through <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvest_Control_Rules&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harvest Control Rules (page does not exist)">Harvest Control Rules</a> (HCR) based upon the precautionary principle. The figure indicates how the principle is implemented in the <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> fisheries management proposed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_the_Exploration_of_the_Sea" title="International Council for the Exploration of the Sea">International Council for the Exploration of the Sea</a>.</p>
<p>In classifying <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>, the precautionary principle means that if there is doubt about an animal's or plant's exact <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Conservation_status" title="Wikipedia:Conservation status">conservation status</a>, the one that would cause the strongest protective measures to be realised should be chosen. Thus, a species like the <a href="/wiki/Silvery_pigeon" title="Silvery pigeon">silvery pigeon</a> that might exist in considerable numbers and simply be under-recorded or might just as probably be long extinct is not classified as "data deficient" or "extinct" (which both do not require any protective action to be taken), but as "critically endangered" (the conservation status that confers the need for the strongest protection), whereas the increasingly rare, but probably not yet endangered <a href="/wiki/Emerald_starling" title="Emerald starling">emerald starling</a> is classified as "data deficient", because there is urgent need for research to clarify its status rather than for conservation action to save it from extinction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>If, for example, a large ground-water body that many people use for drinking water is contaminated by bacteria (e-coli 0157 H7, campylobacter or leptospirosis) and the source of contamination is strongly suspected to be dairy cows but the exact science is not yet able to provide absolute proof, the cows should be removed from the environment until they are proved, by the dairy industry, not to be the source or until that industry ensures that such contamination will not recur.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critics of the principle use arguments similar to those against other formulations of technological conservatism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_inconsistency_.E2.80.93_applying_strong_PP_risks_causing_harm">Internal inconsistency – applying strong PP risks causing harm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Internal inconsistency – applying strong PP risks causing harm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strong formulations of the precautionary principle, without regard to its most basic provisions that it is to be applied only where risks are potentially high AND not easily calculable, applied to the principle itself as a policy decision, may rule out its own use.<sup id="cite_ref-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26ff</sup> The reason suggested is that preventing innovation from coming to market means that only current technology may be used, and current technology itself may cause harm or leave needs unmet; there is a risk of causing harm by blocking innovation.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> wrote in his novel <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_Fear" title="State of Fear">State of Fear</a></i>: "The 'precautionary principle', properly applied, forbids the precautionary principle."<sup id="cite_ref-CAST52_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAST52-33">[33]</a></sup> For example, forbidding nuclear power plants based on concerns about risk means continuing to rely on power plants that burn fossil fuels, which continue to release <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beyond_Laws_of_Fear-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:27</sup> In another example, the Hazardous Air Pollutant provisions in the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(1990)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean Air Act (1990)">1990 amendments to the US Clean Air Act</a> are an example of the Precautionary Principle where the onus is now on showing a listed compound is harmless. Under this rule no distinction is made between those air Pollutants that provide a higher or lower risk, so operators tend to choose less-examined agents that are not on the existing list.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blocking_innovation_and_progress_generally">Blocking innovation and progress generally</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Blocking innovation and progress generally">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fear,_uncertainty_and_doubt" title="Fear, uncertainty and doubt">Fear, uncertainty and doubt</a></div>
<p>Because applications of strong formulations of the precautionary principle can be used to block innovation, a technology which brings advantages may be banned by precautionary principle because of its potential for negative impacts, leaving the positive benefits unrealised.<sup id="cite_ref-Sunstein_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sunstein-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:201</sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The precautionary principle has been ethically questioned on the basis that its application could block progress in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vagueness_and_plausibility">Vagueness and plausibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precautionary_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Vagueness and plausibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The precautionary principle calls for inaction in the face of scientific uncertainty, but some formulations do not specify the minimal threshold of plausibility of risk that acts as a "triggering" condition, so that any indication that a proposed product or activity might harm health or the environment is sufficient to invoke the principle.<sup id="cite_ref-vandenBelt_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vandenBelt-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In <i>Sancho vs. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">DOE</a></i>, Helen Gillmor, Senior District Judge, wrote in a dismissal of Wagner's lawsuit which included a popular<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> worry that the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">LHC</a> could cause "destruction of the earth" by a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Injury in fact requires some "credible threat of harm." <i>Cent. Delta Water Agency v. United States</i>, 306 F.3d 938, 950 (9th Cir. 2002). At most, Wagner has alleged that experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (the "Collider") have "potential adverse consequences." Speculative fear of future harm does not constitute an injury in fact sufficient to confer <a href="/wiki/Standing_(law)#United_States" title="Standing (law)">standing</a>. <i>Mayfield</i>, 599 F.3d at 970.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
