<p><b>Jet fuel</b>, <b>aviation turbine fuel</b> (<b>ATF</b>), or <b>avtur</b>, is a type of <a href="/wiki/Aviation_fuel" title="Aviation fuel">aviation fuel</a> designed for use in <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> powered by <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas-turbine engines</a>. It is colorless to straw-colored in appearance. The most commonly used fuels for commercial aviation are Jet A and Jet A-1, which are produced to a standardized international specification. The only other jet fuel commonly used in civilian turbine-engine powered aviation is Jet B, which is used for its enhanced cold-weather performance.</p>
<p>Jet fuel is a mixture of a large number of different <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>. The range of their sizes (molecular weights or carbon numbers) is defined by the requirements for the product, such as the freezing or smoke point. <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">Kerosene</a>-type jet fuel (including Jet A and Jet A-1) has a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_number" title="Carbon number">carbon number</a> distribution between about 8 and 16 (carbon atoms per molecule); wide-cut or <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>-type jet fuel (including Jet B), between about 5 and 15.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fuel for <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">piston-engine</a> powered aircraft (usually a high-<a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane</a> <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Avgas" title="Avgas">avgas</a>) has a low <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> to improve its ignition characteristics. Turbine engines can operate with a wide range of fuels, and jet-aircraft engines typically use fuels with higher <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash points</a>, which are less flammable and therefore safer to transport and handle.</p>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Axial_compressor" title="Axial compressor">axial compressor</a> jet engine in widespread production and combat service, the <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Jumo_004" title="Junkers Jumo 004">Junkers Jumo 004</a> used on the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_262" title="Messerschmitt Me 262">Messerschmitt Me 262A</a> fighter and the <a href="/wiki/Arado_Ar_234" title="Arado Ar 234">Arado Ar 234B</a> jet recon-bomber, burned either a special synthetic "J2" fuel or diesel fuel. Gasoline was a third option but unattractive due to high fuel consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Other fuels used were kerosene or kerosene and gasoline mixtures. Most jet fuels in use since the end of World War II are kerosene-based. Both British and American standards for jet fuels were first established at the end of World War II. British standards derived from standards for kerosene use for lamps—known as paraffin in the UK—whereas American standards derived from aviation gasoline practices. Over the subsequent years, details of specifications were adjusted, such as minimum freezing point, to balance performance requirements and availability of fuels. Very low temperature <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">freezing points</a> reduce the availability of fuel. Higher <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> products required for use on aircraft carriers are more expensive to produce.<sup id="cite_ref-Rand2010_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand2010-3">[3]</a></sup> In the United States, <a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM International</a> produces standards for civilian fuel types, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> produces standards for military use. The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">British Ministry of Defence</a> establishes standards for both civil and military jet fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-Rand2010_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand2010-3">[3]</a></sup> For reasons of inter-operational ability, British and United States military standards are harmonized to a degree. In Russia and former Soviet Union countries, grades of jet fuels are covered by the State Standard (GOST) number, or a Technical Condition number, with the principal grade available in Russia and members of the CIS being TS-1.</p>

<p>Jet A specification fuel has been used in the United States since the 1950s and is usually not available outside the United States<sup id="cite_ref-shelljet_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shelljet-4">[4]</a></sup> and a few Canadian airports such as <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">Toronto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_International_Airport" title="Vancouver International Airport">Vancouver</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> whereas Jet A-1 is the standard specification fuel used in the rest of the world other than the former Soviet states where TS-1 is the most common standard. Both Jet A and Jet A-1 have a <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> higher than 38&#160;°C (100&#160;°F), with an <a href="/wiki/Autoignition_temperature" title="Autoignition temperature">autoignition temperature</a> of 210&#160;°C (410&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-exxonmobil.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-exxonmobil.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>The primary difference is the lower freezing point of A-1:<sup id="cite_ref-shelljet_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shelljet-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Jet A's is −40&#160;°C (−40&#160;°F)</li>
<li>Jet A-1's is −47&#160;°C (−53&#160;°F)</li>
</ul>
<p>The other difference is the mandatory addition of an anti-static additive to Jet A-1.</p>
<p>Jet A trucks, storage tanks, and plumbing that carry Jet A are marked with a black sticker with "Jet A" in white printed on it, adjacent to another black stripe.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_B">Jet B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jet B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jet B is a fuel in the naphtha-kerosene region that is used for its enhanced cold-weather performance. However, Jet B's lighter composition makes it more dangerous to handle.<sup id="cite_ref-Csgnetwork.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Csgnetwork.com-7">[7]</a></sup> For this reason, it is rarely used, except in very cold climates. A blend of approximately 30% kerosene and 70% gasoline, it is known as wide-cut fuel. It has a very low freezing point of −60&#160;°C (−76&#160;°F), and a low <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> as well. It is primarily used in some military aircraft. It is also used in Northern Canada, Alaska, and sometimes Russia, because of its low freezing point.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additives">Additives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Additives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The DEF STAN 91-91 (UK) and ASTM D1655 (international) specifications allow for certain additives to be added to jet fuel, including:<sup id="cite_ref-DEF_STAN_91-91_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DEF_STAN_91-91-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ASTM_D1655_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM_D1655-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">Antioxidants</a> to prevent <a href="/wiki/Gumming" class="mw-redirect" title="Gumming">gumming</a>, usually based on <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylated</a> <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenols</a>, e.g., AO-30, AO-31, or AO-37;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antistatic_agent" title="Antistatic agent">Antistatic agents</a>, to dissipate <a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">static electricity</a> and prevent sparking; <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadis_450&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadis 450 (page does not exist)">Stadis 450</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Dinonylnaphthylsulfonic_acid" title="Dinonylnaphthylsulfonic acid">dinonylnaphthylsulfonic acid</a> (DINNSA) as a component, is an example</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Corrosion_inhibitor" title="Corrosion inhibitor">Corrosion inhibitors</a>, e.g., <a href="/w/index.php?title=DCI-4A&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="DCI-4A (page does not exist)">DCI-4A</a> used for civilian and military fuels, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=DCI-6A&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="DCI-6A (page does not exist)">DCI-6A</a> used for military fuels;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fuel_system_icing_inhibitor" title="Fuel system icing inhibitor">Fuel system icing inhibitor</a> (FSII) agents, e.g., <a href="/wiki/2-(2-Methoxyethoxy)ethanol" title="2-(2-Methoxyethoxy)ethanol">Di-EGME</a>; FSII is often mixed at the point-of-sale so that users with heated fuel lines do not have to pay the extra expense.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biocide" title="Biocide">Biocides</a> are to remediate microbial (i.e., bacterial and fungal) growth present in aircraft fuel systems. Currently, two biocides are approved for use by most aircraft and turbine engine <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">original equipment manufacturers</a> (OEMs); Kathon FP1.5 Microbiocide and Biobor JF.<sup id="cite_ref-Lombardo_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lombardo-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metal_deactivator" title="Metal deactivator">Metal deactivator</a> can be added to remediate the deleterious effects of trace metals on the thermal stability of the fuel. The one allowable additive is <a href="/wiki/1,2-Diaminopropane#metaldeactivating" title="1,2-Diaminopropane">N,N’-disalicylidene 1,2-propanediamine</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>As the aviation industry’s jet kerosene demands have increased to more than 5% of all refined products derived from crude, it has been necessary for the refiner to optimize the yield of jet kerosene, a high value product, by varying process techniques. New processes have allowed flexibility in the choice of crudes, the use of coal tar sands as a source of molecules and the manufacture of synthetic blend stocks. Due to the number and severity of the processes used, it is often necessary and sometimes mandatory to use additives. These additives may, for example, prevent the formation of harmful chemical species or improve a property of a fuel to prevent further engine wear.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_in_jet_fuel">Water in jet fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water in jet fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is very important that jet fuel be free from water <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a>. During flight, the temperature of the fuel in the tanks decreases, due to the low temperatures in the upper <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">atmosphere</a>. This causes precipitation of the dissolved water from the fuel. The separated water then drops to the bottom of the tank, because it is denser than the fuel. Since the water is no longer in solution, it can form droplets which can supercool to below 0&#160;°C. If these supercooled droplets collide with a surface they can freeze and may result in blocked fuel inlet pipes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This was the cause of the <a href="/wiki/British_Airways_Flight_38" title="British Airways Flight 38">British Airways Flight 38</a> accident. Removing all water from fuel is impractical; therefore, fuel heaters are usually used on commercial aircraft to prevent water in fuel from freezing.</p>
<p>There are several methods for detecting water in jet fuel. A visual check may detect high concentrations of suspended water, as this will cause the fuel to become hazy in appearance. An industry standard chemical test for the detection of free water in jet fuel uses a water-sensitive filter pad that turns green if the fuel exceeds the specification limit of 30&#160;ppm (parts per million) free water.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A critical test to rate the ability of jet fuel to release emulsified water when passed through coalescing filters is ASTM standard D3948 Standard Test Method for Determining Water Separation Characteristics of Aviation Turbine Fuels by Portable Separometer.</p>

<p>Military organizations around the world use a different classification system of JP (for "Jet Propellant") numbers. Some are almost identical to their civilian counterparts and differ only by the amounts of a few additives; Jet A-1 is similar to <a href="/wiki/JP-8" title="JP-8">JP-8</a>, Jet B is similar to <a href="/wiki/JP-4_(fuel)" title="JP-4 (fuel)">JP-4</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shell_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shell-18">[18]</a></sup> Other military fuels are highly specialized products and are developed for very specific applications.</p>
<p>Jet fuels are sometimes classified as kerosene or naphtha-type.<sup id="cite_ref-Rand2010_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand2010-3">[3]</a></sup> Kerosene-type fuels include Jet A, Jet A-1, JP-5 and JP-8. Naphtha-type jet fuels, sometimes referred to as "wide-cut" jet fuel, include Jet B and JP-4.<sup id="cite_ref-Rand2010_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rand2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>JP-1</dt>
<dd>was an early jet fuel<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> specified in 1944 by the United States government (AN-F-32). It was a pure kerosene fuel with high <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> (relative to aviation gasoline) and a freezing point of −60&#160;°C (−76&#160;°F). The low freezing point requirement limited availability of the fuel and it was soon superseded by other "wide cut" jet fuels which were kerosene-naphtha or kerosene-gasoline blends. It was also known as <b>avtur</b>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>JP-2 and JP-3</dt>
<dd>are obsolete types developed during World War II. JP-2 was intended to be easier to produce than JP-1 since it had a higher freezing point, but was never widely used. JP-3 was even more volatile than JP-2 and intended to improve production, but its volatility led to high evaporation loss in service.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/JP-4" class="mw-redirect" title="JP-4">JP-4</a></dt>
<dd>was a 50-50 kerosene-gasoline blend. It had lower <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> than JP-1, but was preferred because of its greater availability. It was the primary <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> jet fuel between 1951 and 1995. Its <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> code is <b>F-40</b>. It is also known as <b>avtag</b>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>JP-5</dt>
<dd>is a yellow kerosene-based jet fuel developed in 1952 for use in aircraft stationed aboard <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a>, where the risk from fire is particularly great. JP-5 is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons, containing <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">naphthenes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic hydrocarbons</a> that weighs 6.8 pounds per U.S. gallon (0.81&#160;kg/l) and has a high <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> (min. 60&#160;°C or 140&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Because some US <a href="/wiki/Naval_air_station" title="Naval air station">naval air stations</a>, Marine Corps air stations and Coast Guard air stations host both sea-based and shore-based (e.g., "land based") naval aircraft, these installations will also typically fuel their shore-based aircraft with JP-5, thus precluding the need to maintain separate fuel facilities for JP-5 and non-JP-5 fuel. In addition, JP-5 may well have been used by other countries for their military aircraft. Its freezing point is −46&#160;°C (−51&#160;°F). It does not contain antistatic agents. JP5 is also known as NCI-C54784. JP-5's NATO code is <b>F-44</b>. It is also called <b>AVCAT</b> fuel for <b>Av</b>iation <b>Ca</b>rrier <b>T</b>urbine fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The JP-4 and JP-5 fuels, covered by the MIL-DTL-5624 and meeting the British Specification DEF STAN 91-86 AVCAT/<a href="/wiki/Fuel_system_icing_inhibitor" title="Fuel system icing inhibitor">FSII</a> (formerly DERD 2452),<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> are intended for use in aircraft <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">turbine engines</a>. These fuels require military-unique additives that are necessary in military <a href="/wiki/Weapon_system" title="Weapon system">weapon systems</a>, engines, and missions.</p>

<dt>JP-6</dt>
<dd>This is a type of jet fuel developed for the <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_YJ93" title="General Electric YJ93">General Electric YJ93</a> jet engine of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">XB-70 Valkyrie</a> <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_speed" title="Supersonic speed">supersonic</a> aircraft. <dt><a href="/wiki/Syntroleum" title="Syntroleum">Syntroleum</a></dt>
<dd>has been working with the USAF to develop a synthetic jet fuel blend that will help them reduce their dependence on imported petroleum. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Piston_engine_use">Piston engine use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Piston engine use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-style ambox-confusing" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/20px-Edit-clear.svg.png" width="20" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/30px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>may be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">confusing or unclear</a> to readers</b>. <small><i>(July 2014)</i></small> <small class="hide-when-compact"><i>(<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></span></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Jet fuel is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, and in some cases, may be burned in <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>. The possibility of environmental legislation banning the use of <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyllead" title="Tetraethyllead">leaded</a> avgas, and the lack of a replacement fuel with similar performance, has left aircraft designers and pilot's organizations searching for alternative engines for use in small aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As a result, a few aircraft engine manufacturers, most notably <a href="/wiki/Thielert" title="Thielert">Thielert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austro_Engine" title="Austro Engine">Austro Engine</a>, have begun offering <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_diesel_engine" title="Aircraft diesel engine">aircraft diesel engines</a> which run on jet fuel. This technology has potential to simplify airport logistics by reducing the number of fuel types required. Jet fuel is available in most places in the world, whereas avgas is only widely available in a few countries which have a large number of <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> aircraft. A diesel engine may also potentially be more environmentally friendly and fuel-efficient than an avgas engine. However, very few diesel aircraft engines have been certified by aviation authorities. Diesel aircraft engines are uncommon today, even though opposed-piston aviation diesel powerplants such as the <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Jumo_205" title="Junkers Jumo 205">Junkers Jumo 205</a> family had been used during the Second World War.</p>
<p>Jet fuel is often used in ground support vehicles at airports, instead of diesel. The United States military makes heavy use of JP-8, for instance. However, jet fuel tends to have poor lubricating ability in comparison to diesel, thereby increasing wear on fuel pumps and other related engine parts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Civilian vehicles tend to disallow its use, or require that an additive be mixed with the jet fuel to restore its <a href="/wiki/Lubricity" title="Lubricity">lubricity</a>. Jet fuel is more expensive than diesel fuel but the logistical advantages of using one fuel can offset the extra expense of its use in certain circumstances.</p>
<p>Jet fuel contains more sulfur, up to 1,000&#160;ppm, which therefore means it is more lubricative and does not currently require a lubricity additive as all pipeline diesel fuels require. The introduction of Ultra Low Sulfur Diesel or ULSD brought with it the need for lubricity modifiers. Pipeline diesels before ULSD were able to contain up to 500&#160;ppm of sulfur and was called Low Sulfur Diesel or LSD. LSD is now only available to the off-road construction, locomotive and marine markets. As more EPA regulations are introduced, more refineries are hydrotreating their jet fuel production, thus limiting the lubricating abilities of jet fuel, as determined by ASTM Standard D445.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_jet_fuel">Synthetic jet fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Synthetic jet fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">Synthetic fuel</a></div>
<p>A significant effort is under way to certify <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch</a> (FT) Synthesized Paraffinic Kerosene (SPK) synthetic fuels for use in United States and international aviation fleets. In this effort is being led by an industry coalition known as the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Aviation_Alternative_Fuels_Initiative" title="Commercial Aviation Alternative Fuels Initiative">Commercial Aviation Alternative Fuels Initiative</a> (CAAFI),<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> also supported by a parallel initiative under way in the USAF,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> to certify FT fuel for use in all aviation platforms. The USAF has a stated goal of certifying its entire fleet for use with FT synthetic fuel blends by 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The CAAFI initiative aims to certify the civilian aviation fleet for FT synthetic fuels blends by 2010, and has programs under way to certify Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids (HEFA) (aka Hydrogenated Renewable Jet (HRJ)) SPK biofuels as early as 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> "Hydroprocessed" and "hydrotreated" have also been used in lieu of "hydrogenated". Both FT and HEFA based SPKs blended with JP-8 are specified in MIL-DTL-83133H.</p>
<p>Some synthetic jet fuels show a reduction in pollutants such as SOx, NOx, particulate matter, and sometimes carbon emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It is envisaged that usage of synthetic jet fuels will increase air quality around airports which will be particularly advantageous at inner city airports.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Qatar_Airways" title="Qatar Airways">Qatar Airways</a> became the first airline to operate a commercial flight on a 50:50 blend of synthetic Gas to Liquid (GTL) jet fuel and conventional jet fuel. The natural gas derived synthetic kerosene for the six-hour flight from London to Doha came from Shell's GTL plant in Bintulu, Malaysia.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>The world's first passenger aircraft flight to use only synthetic jet fuel was from <a href="/wiki/Lanseria_International_Airport" title="Lanseria International Airport">Lanseria International Airport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town_International_Airport" title="Cape Town International Airport">Cape Town International Airport</a> on September 22, 2010. The fuel was developed by <a href="/wiki/Sasol" title="Sasol">Sasol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Chemist <a href="/wiki/Heather_Willauer" title="Heather Willauer">Heather Willauer</a> is leading a team of researchers at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="United States Naval Research Laboratory">U.S. Naval Research Laboratory</a> who are developing a process to make jet fuel from seawater. The technology requires an input of electrical energy to separate <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) gas from seawater using an iron-based catalyst, followed by an <a href="/wiki/Oligomer" title="Oligomer">oligomerization</a> step wherein carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen are recombined into long-chain hydrocarbons, using <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> as the catalyst. The technology is expected to be deployed in the 2020s by U.S. Navy warships, especially nuclear-powered aircraft carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tozer2014_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tozer2014-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="USAF_synthetic_fuel_trials">USAF synthetic fuel trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: USAF synthetic fuel trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 8, 2007, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Air_Force" title="United States Secretary of the Air Force">Air Force Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wynne" title="Michael Wynne">Michael Wynne</a> certified the B-52H as fully approved to use the FT blend, marking the formal conclusion of the test program.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:C-17_on_an_airport_in_Iraq.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The USAF C-17 Globemaster III was built to perform development testing.</div>

<p>This program is part of the Department of Defense Assured Fuel Initiative, an effort to develop secure domestic sources for the military energy needs. The Pentagon hopes to reduce its use of crude oil from foreign producers and obtain about half of its aviation fuel from alternative sources by 2016. With the B-52 now approved to use the FT blend, the USAF will use the test protocols developed during the program to certify the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_C-17_Globemaster_III" title="Boeing C-17 Globemaster III">C-17 Globemaster III</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_B-1_Lancer" title="Rockwell B-1 Lancer">B-1B</a> to use the fuel. To test these two aircraft, the USAF has ordered 281,000&#160;US&#160;gal (1,060,000&#160;l) of FT fuel. The USAF intends to test and certify every airframe in its inventory to use the fuel by 2011. They will also supply over 9,000 US gallons (34,000&#160;l; 7,500&#160;imp&#160;gal) to <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> for testing in various aircraft and engines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (October 2014)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The USAF has certified the B-1B, B-52H, C-17, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_C-130J_Super_Hercules" title="Lockheed Martin C-130J Super Hercules">C-130J</a>, <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II" title="McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II">F-4</a> (as QF-4 <a href="/wiki/Target_drone" title="Target drone">target drones</a>), <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle">F-15</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">F-22</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">T-38</a> to use the synthetic fuel blend.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Air Force's C-17 Globemaster III, <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16</a> and F-15 are certified for use of hydrotreated renewable jet fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-one_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-44">[44]</a></sup> The USAF plans to certify over 40 models for fuels derived from waste oils and plants by 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-one_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> is considered one of the few customers of <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> large enough to potentially bring biofuels up to the volume production needed to reduce costs.<sup id="cite_ref-one_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> has also flown a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_F/A-18E/F_Super_Hornet" title="Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet">Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet</a> dubbed the "Green Hornet" at 1.7 times the speed of sound using a biofuel blend.<sup id="cite_ref-one_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA) funded a $6.7 million project with <a href="/wiki/UOP_LLC" title="UOP LLC">Honeywell UOP</a> to develop technologies to create jet fuels from biofeedstocks for use by the United States and NATO militaries.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_biofuels">Jet biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Jet biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_biofuel" title="Aviation biofuel">Aviation biofuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Aviation_Fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable Aviation Fuel">Sustainable Aviation Fuel</a></div>
<p>The air transport industry is responsible for 2-3 percent of man-made <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emitted.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> estimates that biofuels could reduce flight-related <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse-gas</a> emissions by 60 to 80 percent. One possible solution which has received more media coverage than others would be blending synthetic fuel derived from <a href="/wiki/Algae_fuel" title="Algae fuel">algae</a> with existing jet fuel:<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Green Flight International became the first airline to fly jet aircraft on 100% biofuel. The flight from <a href="/wiki/Reno_Stead_Airport" title="Reno Stead Airport">Reno Stead Airport</a> in Stead, Nevada was in an <a href="/wiki/Aero_L-29_Delf%C3%ADn" title="Aero L-29 Delfín">Aero L-29 Delfín</a> piloted by Carol Sugars and Douglas Rodante.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>Boeing and <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">Air New Zealand</a> are collaborating with Tecbio<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Aquaflow Bionomic and other jet biofuel developers around the world.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic" title="Virgin Atlantic">Virgin Atlantic</a> successfully tested a biofuel blend consisting of 20 percent <a href="/wiki/Babassu_oil" title="Babassu oil">babassu nuts</a> and coconut and 80 percent conventional jet fuel, which was fed to a single engine on a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">747</a> flight from <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow</a> to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">Amsterdam Schiphol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>A consortium consisting of Boeing, NASA's <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Research_Center" title="Glenn Research Center">Glenn Research Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/MTU_Aero_Engines" title="MTU Aero Engines">MTU Aero Engines</a> (Germany), and the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Research_Laboratory" title="Air Force Research Laboratory">Air Force Research Laboratory</a> is working on development of jet fuel blends containing a substantial percentage of biofuel.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> and Solena Group are establishing a sustainable jet fuel plant in <a href="/wiki/East_London" title="East London">East London</a>, UK as BA plans to use the biofuel to power part of its fleet from 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>24 commercial and military biofuel flights have taken place using <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">Honeywell</a> “Green Jet Fuel,” including a Navy F/A-18 Hornet.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/United_Continental_Holdings" title="United Continental Holdings">United Continental Holdings</a> was the first United States airline to fly passengers on a commercial flight using a blend of sustainable, advanced biofuels and traditional petroleum-derived jet fuel. <a href="/wiki/Solazyme" class="mw-redirect" title="Solazyme">Solazyme</a> developed the algae oil, which was refined utilizing Honeywell's UOP process technology, into jet fuel to power the commercial flight.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solazyme" class="mw-redirect" title="Solazyme">Solazyme</a> produced the world's first 100 percent algae-derived jet fuel, Solajet, for both commercial and military applications.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/2000s_energy_crisis" title="2000s energy crisis">Oil prices increased about fivefold</a> from 2003 to 2008, raising fears that world petroleum production is becoming <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">unable to keep up with demand</a>. The fact that there are few alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> for aviation fuel adds urgency to the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation#Alternative_fuels" title="Environmental impact of aviation">search for alternatives</a>. Twenty-five airlines were bankrupted or stopped operations in the first six months of 2008, largely due to fuel costs.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 ASTM approved a modification to Specification D1655 Standard Specification for Aviation Turbine Fuels to permit up to 50 ppm (50&#160;mg/kg) of FAME (<a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid_methyl_ester" title="Fatty acid methyl ester">fatty acid methyl ester</a>) in jet fuel to allow higher cross-contamination from biofuel production.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worldwide_consumption_of_jet_fuel">Worldwide consumption of jet fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jet_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Worldwide consumption of jet fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Worldwide demand of jet fuel has been steadily increasing since 1980. Consumption more than tripled in 30 years from 1,837,000 barrels/day in 1980, to 5,220,000 in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Around 30% of the worldwide consumption of jet fuel is in the US (1,398,130 barrels/day in 2012).</p>
