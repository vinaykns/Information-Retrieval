<p><b>Sustainable agriculture</b> is <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">farming</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a> ways based on an understanding of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, the study of relationships between organisms and their environment. It has been defined as "an integrated system of plant and animal production practices having a site-specific application that will last over the long term", for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Satisfy human food and fiber needs</li>
<li>Enhance <a href="/wiki/Environmental_quality" title="Environmental quality">environmental quality</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> base upon which the agricultural economy depends</li>
<li>Make the most efficient use of <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resources</a> and on-farm resources and integrate, where appropriate, natural biological cycles and controls</li>
<li>Sustain the economic viability of farm operations</li>
<li>Enhance the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> for farmers and society as a whole<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>Sustainable agriculture can be understood as an ecosystem approach to agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Practices that can cause long-term damage to <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> include excessive <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tilling</a> of the soil (leading to <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> without adequate drainage (leading to <a href="/wiki/Salinization" class="mw-redirect" title="Salinization">salinization</a>). <a href="/wiki/Long-term_experiments" class="mw-redirect" title="Long-term experiments">Long-term experiments</a> have provided some of the best data on how various practices affect soil properties essential to sustainability. In the United States a federal agency, USDA-Natural Resources Conservation Service, specializes in providing technical and financial assistance for those interested in pursuing natural resource conservation and production agriculture as compatible goals.</p>
<p>The most important factors for an individual site are sun, air, soil, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water</a>. Of the five, water and <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil quality</a> and quantity are most amenable to human intervention through time and labor.</p>
<p>Although air and sunlight are available everywhere on Earth, crops also depend on soil nutrients and the availability of water. When farmers grow and harvest crops, they remove some of these nutrients from the soil. Without replenishment, land suffers from nutrient depletion and becomes either unusable or suffers from reduced <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yields</a>. Sustainable agriculture depends on replenishing the soil while minimizing the use or need of non-renewable resources, such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (used in converting atmospheric nitrogen into synthetic fertilizer), or mineral ores (e.g., phosphate). Possible sources of nitrogen that would, in principle, be available indefinitely, include:</p>
<ol>
<li>recycling crop waste and <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> or treated <a href="/wiki/Humanure" class="mw-redirect" title="Humanure">human manure</a></li>
<li>growing <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> crops and <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a>s such as <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> that form symbioses with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> called <a href="/wiki/Rhizobia" title="Rhizobia">rhizobia</a></li>
<li>industrial production of nitrogen by the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a> uses hydrogen, which is currently derived from natural gas (but this hydrogen could instead be made by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water using electricity (perhaps from solar cells or windmills)) or</li>
<li>genetically engineering (non-legume) crops to form nitrogen-fixing symbioses or fix nitrogen without microbial symbionts.</li>
</ol>
<p>The last option was proposed in the 1970s, but is only recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> becoming feasible.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Sustainable options for replacing other nutrient inputs (phosphorus, potassium, etc.) are more limited.</p>
<p>More realistic, and often overlooked, options include <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">long-term crop rotations</a>, returning to natural cycles that annually flood cultivated lands (returning lost nutrients indefinitely) such as the <a href="/wiki/Flooding_of_the_Nile" title="Flooding of the Nile">flooding of the Nile</a>, the long-term use of <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, and use of crop and livestock <a href="/wiki/Landrace" title="Landrace">landraces</a> that are adapted to less than ideal conditions such as pests, drought, or lack of nutrients.</p>
<p>Crops that require high levels of soil nutrients can be cultivated in a more sustainable manner if certain fertilizer management practices are adhered to.</p>
<p>Nationwide food producers require vast amounts of land and soil to produce food at an accelerated rate. This diminishes the nutrients in the soil and decimates the idea of sustainable agriculture, which is best built through local, regional agricultural methods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some areas sufficient <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> is available for crop growth, but many other areas require <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>. For irrigation systems to be sustainable, they require proper management (to avoid <a href="/wiki/Salinization" class="mw-redirect" title="Salinization">salinization</a>) and must not use more water from their source than is naturally replenishable. Otherwise, the water source effectively becomes a <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resource</a>. Improvements in water <a href="/wiki/Well_drilling" title="Well drilling">well drilling</a> technology and <a href="/wiki/Submersible_pump" title="Submersible pump">submersible pumps</a>, combined with the development of <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">drip irrigation</a> and low-pressure pivots, have made it possible to regularly achieve high crop yields in areas where reliance on rainfall alone had previously made successful agriculture unpredictable. However, this progress has come at a price. In many areas, such as the <a href="/wiki/Ogallala_Aquifer" title="Ogallala Aquifer">Ogallala Aquifer</a>, the water is being used faster than it can be replenished.</p>
<p>Several steps must be taken to develop drought-resistant farming systems even in "normal" years with average rainfall. These measures include both policy and management actions:</p>
<ol>
<li>improving <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a> and storage measures,</li>
<li>providing incentives for selection of <a href="/wiki/Drought-tolerant" class="mw-redirect" title="Drought-tolerant">drought-tolerant</a> crop species,</li>
<li>using reduced-volume irrigation systems,</li>
<li>managing crops to reduce water loss, and</li>
<li>not planting crops at all.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Indicators for sustainable water resource development are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Internal renewable water resources. This is the average annual flow of rivers and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> generated from endogenous precipitation, after ensuring that there is no double counting. It represents the maximum amount of water resource produced within the boundaries of a country. This value, which is expressed as an average on a yearly basis, is invariant in time (except in the case of proved <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>). The indicator can be expressed in three different units: in absolute terms (km³/yr), in mm/yr (it is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> of the country), and as a function of population (m³/person per year).</li>
<li>Global renewable water resources. This is the sum of internal renewable water resources and incoming flow originating outside the country. Unlike internal resources, this value can vary with time if upstream development reduces <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water availability</a> at the border. Treaties ensuring a specific flow to be reserved from upstream to downstream countries may be taken into account in the computation of global water resources in both countries.</li>
<li>Dependency ratio. This is the proportion of the global renewable water resources originating outside the country, expressed in percentage. It is an expression of the level to which the water resources of a country depend on neighbouring countries.</li>
<li>Water withdrawal. In view of the limitations described above, only gross water withdrawal can be computed systematically on a country basis as a measure of water use. Absolute or per-person value of yearly water withdrawal gives a measure of the importance of water in the country's economy. When expressed in percentage of water resources, it shows the degree of pressure on water resources. A rough estimate shows that if water withdrawal exceeds a quarter of global renewable water resources of a country, water can be considered a limiting factor to development and, reciprocally, the pressure on water resources can affect all sectors, from agriculture to environment and fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">Soil erosion</a> is fast becoming one of the world's severe problems. It is estimated that "more than a thousand million tonnes of southern Africa's soil are eroded every year. Experts predict that crop yields will be halved within thirty to fifty years if erosion continues at present rates."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Soil erosion is not unique to Africa but is occurring worldwide. The phenomenon is being called <i>peak soil</i> as present large-scale factory farming techniques are jeopardizing humanity's ability to grow food in the present and in the future. Without efforts to improve soil management practices, the availability of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable soil</a> will become increasingly problematic.</p>
<p>Some soil management techniques</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">No-till farming</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Keyline_design" title="Keyline design">Keyline design</a></li>
<li>Growing <a href="/wiki/Windbreaks" class="mw-redirect" title="Windbreaks">windbreaks</a> to hold the soil</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">Incorporating organic matter</a> back into fields</li>
<li>Stop using <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">chemical fertilizers</a> (which contain salt)</li>
<li>Protecting soil from water run-off (soil erosion)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phosphate">Phosphate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phosphate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Phosphate is a primary component in the chemical fertilizer which is applied in modern agricultural production. However, scientists estimate that rock phosphate reserves will be depleted in 50–100 years and that <a href="/wiki/Peak_phosphorus" title="Peak phosphorus">peak phosphorus</a> will occur in about 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The phenomenon of peak phosphorus is expected to increase food prices as fertilizer costs increase as rock phosphate reserves become more difficult to extract. In the long term, phosphate will therefore have to be recovered and recycled from human and animal waste in order to maintain food production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land">Land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the global population increases and demand for food increases, there is pressure on land resources. Land can also be considered a finite resource on Earth. Expansion of agricultural land decreases <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and contributes to <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a> of the United Nations estimates that in coming decades, cropland will continue to be lost to industrial and urban development, along with reclamation of wetlands, and conversion of forest to cultivation, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a> and increased soil erosion.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_for_agriculture">Energy for agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy for agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a></div>
<p>Energy is used all the way down the food chain from farm to fork. In industrial agriculture, energy is used in on-farm mechanisation, food processing, storage, and transportation processes.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It has therefore been found that energy prices are closely linked to food prices.<sup id="cite_ref-fao_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-14">[14]</a></sup> Oil is also used as an input in agricultural chemicals. Higher prices of non-renewable energy resources are projected by the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>. Increased energy prices as a result of fossil fuel resources being depleted may therefore decrease global food security unless action is taken to 'decouple' fossil fuel energy from food production, with a move towards 'energy-smart' agricultural systems.<sup id="cite_ref-fao_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-14">[14]</a></sup> The use of solar powered irrigation in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> has come to be recognized as a leading example of energy use in creating a closed system for water irrigation in agricultural activity.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Socioeconomic aspects of sustainability are also partly understood. Regarding less concentrated farming, the best known analysis is Netting's study on smallholder systems through history.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Sustainable_Group" title="Oxford Sustainable Group">Oxford Sustainable Group</a> defines sustainability in this context in a much broader form, considering effect on all stakeholders in a 360 degree approach</p>
<p>Given the finite supply of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> at any specific cost and location, agriculture that is inefficient or damaging to needed resources may eventually exhaust the available resources or the ability to afford and acquire them. It may also generate negative <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a>, such as pollution as well as financial and production costs. There are several studies incooperating these negative externalities in an economic analysis concerning ecosystem services, biodiversity, land degradation and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_land_management" title="Sustainable land management">sustainable land management</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/The_Economics_of_Ecosystems_and_Biodiversity" title="The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity">The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity (TEEB)</a> study led by Pavan Sukhdev and the <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_Land_Degradation_Initiative" title="Economics of Land Degradation Initiative">Economics of Land Degradation Initiative</a> which seeks to establish an economic cost benefit analysis on the practice of sustainable land management and sustainable agriculture.</p>
<p>The way that crops are <a href="/wiki/Selling" class="mw-redirect" title="Selling">sold</a> must be accounted for in the sustainability <a href="/wiki/Equation" title="Equation">equation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">Food</a> sold locally does not require additional energy for transportation (including consumers). Food sold at a remote location, whether at a <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_market" title="Farmers' market">farmers' market</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">supermarket</a>, incurs a different set of energy cost for materials, <a href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour (economics)">labour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>.</p>
<p>Pursuing sustainable agriculture results in many localized benefits. Having the opportunities to sell products directly to consumers, rather than at wholesale or commodity prices, allows farmers to bring in optimal profit.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polyculture.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Polyculture.JPG/220px-Polyculture.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Polyculture.JPG/330px-Polyculture.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Polyculture.JPG/440px-Polyculture.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2496" data-file-height="1664" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polyculture.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Polyculture practices in <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>What grows where and how it is grown are a matter of choice. Two of the many possible practices of sustainable agriculture are <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_amendments" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil amendments">soil amendment</a>, both designed to ensure that crops being cultivated can obtain the necessary <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> for healthy growth. Soil amendments would include using locally available compost from community recycling centers. These community recycling centers help produce the compost needed by the local organic farms.</p>
<p>Using community recycling from yard and kitchen waste utilizes a local area's commonly available resources. These resources in the past were thrown away into large waste disposal sites, are now used to produce low cost organic compost for organic farming. Other practices includes growing a diverse number of perennial crops in a single field, each of which would grow in separate season so as not to compete with each other for natural resources.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This system would result in increased resistance to diseases and decreased effects of erosion and loss of nutrients in soil. <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">Nitrogen fixation</a> from legumes, for example, used in conjunction with plants that rely on nitrate from soil for growth, helps to allow the land to be reused annually. Legumes will grow for a season and replenish the soil with ammonium and nitrate, and the next season other plants can be seeded and grown in the field in preparation for harvest.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_(4753)(NRCS_Photo_Gallery).tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif/lossless-page1-220px-NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif.png" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif/lossless-page1-330px-NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif/lossless-page1-440px-NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_%284753%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.tif.png 2x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NRCSMO02014_-_Missouri_(4753)(NRCS_Photo_Gallery).tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rotational grazing practices in use with paddocks</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">Monoculture</a>, a method of growing only one crop at a time in a given field, is a very widespread practice, but there are questions about its sustainability, especially if the same crop is grown every year. Today it is realized to get around this problem local cities and farms can work together to produce the needed compost for the farmers around them. This combined with growing a mixture of crops (<a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a>) sometimes reduces disease or pest problems<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> but polyculture has rarely, if ever, been compared to the more widespread practice of growing different crops in successive years (<a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>) with the same overall <a href="/wiki/Crop_diversity" title="Crop diversity">crop diversity</a>. Cropping systems that include a variety of crops (polyculture and/or rotation) may also replenish nitrogen (if <a href="/wiki/Legumes" class="mw-redirect" title="Legumes">legumes</a> are included) and may also use resources such as sunlight, water, or nutrients more efficiently (Field Crops Res. 34:239).</p>
<p>Replacing a natural ecosystem with a few specifically chosen plant varieties reduces the genetic diversity found in wildlife and makes the organisms susceptible to widespread <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Irish Famine">Great Irish Famine (1845–1849)</a> is a well-known example of the dangers of <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a>. In practice, there is no single approach to sustainable agriculture, as the precise goals and methods must be adapted to each individual case. There may be some techniques of farming that are inherently in conflict with the concept of sustainability, but there is widespread misunderstanding on effects of some practices. Today the growth of local farmers' markets offer small farms the ability to sell the products that they have grown back to the cities that they got the recycled compost from. By using local recycling this will help move people away from the <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> techniques that are the characteristic feature of <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivators</a> are often cited as inherently destructive, yet slash-and-burn cultivation has been practiced in the Amazon for at least 6000 years;<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> serious deforestation did not begin until the 1970s, largely as the result of Brazilian government programs and policies.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> To note that it may not have been slash-and-burn so much as <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-char" title="Slash-and-char">slash-and-char</a>, which with the addition of organic matter produces <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta</a>, one of the richest soils on Earth and the only one that regenerates itself.</p>
<p>There are also many ways to practice sustainable animal husbandry. Some of the key tools to grazing management include fencing off the grazing area into smaller areas called <a href="/wiki/Field_(agriculture)" title="Field (agriculture)">paddocks</a>, lowering stock density, and moving the stock between paddocks frequently.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_intensification">Sustainable intensification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sustainable intensification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming#Sustainable_intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">Intensive farming §&#160;Sustainable intensive farming</a></div>
<p>In light of concerns about <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_population" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population">human population</a> growth and dwindling land suitable for agriculture, sustainable intensive farming practises are needed to maintain high <a href="/wiki/Crop_yields" class="mw-redirect" title="Crop yields">crop yields</a>, while maintaining <a href="/wiki/Soil_health" title="Soil health">soil health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>. The capacity for ecosystem services to be strong enough to allow a reduction in use of synthetic, non renewable inputs whilst maintaining or even boosting yields has been the subject of much debate. Recent work in the globally important irrigated rice production system of east Asia has suggested that - in relation to pest management at least - promoting the ecosystem service of biological control using nectar plants can reduce the need for insecticides by 70% whilst delivering a 5% yield advantage compared with standard practice (<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.nature.com/articles/nplants201614">http://www.nature.com/articles/nplants201614</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_treatment">Soil treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Soil treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Daempfen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Daempfen.jpg/220px-Daempfen.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Daempfen.jpg/330px-Daempfen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Daempfen.jpg/440px-Daempfen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daempfen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Soil_steam_sterilization#Sheet_steaming" title="Soil steam sterilization">Sheet steaming</a> with a MSD/moeschle steam boiler (left side)</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Soil_steam_sterilization" title="Soil steam sterilization">Soil steaming</a> can be used as an ecological alternative to chemicals for soil sterilization. Different methods are available to induce steam into the soil in order to kill pests and increase soil health.</p>
<p>Solarizing is based on the same principle, used to increase the temperature of the soil to kill pathogens and pests.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Certain crops act as natural biofumigants, releasing pest suppressing compounds. Mustard, radishes, and other plants in the brassica family are best known for this effect.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> There exist varieties of mustard shown to be almost as effective as synthetic fumigants at a similar or lesser cost.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Off-farm_impacts">Off-farm impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Off-farm impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A farm that is able to "produce perpetually", yet has negative effects on environmental quality elsewhere is not sustainable agriculture. An example of a case in which a global view may be warranted is over-application of synthetic <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> or animal <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manures</a>, which can improve productivity of a farm but can pollute nearby rivers and coastal waters (<a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>). The other extreme can also be undesirable, as the problem of low crop yields due to exhaustion of nutrients in the soil has been related to <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> destruction, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" class="mw-redirect" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> farming for livestock feed.In Asia, specific land for sustainable farming is about 12.5 acres which includes land for animal fodder, cereals productions lands for some cash crops and even recycling of related food crops.In some cases even a small unit of aquaculture is also included in this number (AARI-1996)</p>
<p>Sustainability affects overall production, which must increase to meet the increasing food and fiber requirements as the world's human population expands to a projected 9.3 billion people by <a href="/wiki/2050" title="2050">2050</a>. Increased production may come from creating new farmland, which may <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/amelioration" class="extiw" title="wikt:amelioration">ameliorate</a> carbon dioxide emissions if done through reclamation of desert as in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a>, or may worsen emissions if done through <a href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" class="mw-redirect" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> farming, as in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social">Social</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Social">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> reported on "<a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">Organic Agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">Food Security</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> stating that using organic and sustainable agriculture could be used as a tool to reach global food security without expanding land usage and reducing <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impacts" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impacts">environmental impacts</a>. Another way to define sustainable agriculture is to give attention to the "human and environmental aspects,"<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> because of the turn to a more unsustainable way of farming in U.S. agriculture. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_States" title="Great Depression in the United States">Great Depression</a> in the United States many farming families were living in subhuman and hungry conditions and treated "sustainability as a resource-input and food-output equation." Though conditions have improved, the farming has not as much done so. There has been evidence provided by developing nations from the early 2000's stating that when people in their communities are not factoreed into the agricultural process that serious harm is done.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> Although global <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> would most likely not drastically fall, these practices would impact, first hand, <a href="/wiki/Local" class="mw-disambig" title="Local">local</a>, rural farming communities, making them unable to feed themselves and their families. The <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social scientist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kellogg" class="mw-disambig" title="Charles Kellogg">Charles Kellogg</a> has stated that, "In a final effort, exploited people pass their suffering to the land."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> This turn to more unsustainable farming has seen suffering for many people. For if something is sustainable, it should be that way in all aspects of it, not just the crop yield or soil health. It has been seen in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing country</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, the starving of <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural</a> farming communities due to their unsustainable farming methods. Sustainable agriculture mean the ability to permanently and continuously "feed its constituent populations."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the past 30 years (1978-2007) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> the number of women farm operators has tripled.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-25">[25]</a></sup> Today, women operate 14 percent of farms, compared to five percent in 1978. Much of the growth is due to women farming outside the "male dominated field of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">conventional agriculture</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-:1_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-25">[25]</a></sup> In community supported agriculture women represent 40 percent of <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> operators, and 21 percent of <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farmers</a>. With the change of laws in <a href="/wiki/Land_ownership" class="mw-redirect" title="Land ownership">land ownership</a> over the past century, <a href="/wiki/Woman" title="Woman">women</a> are now allowed all the same freedom of land ownership that <a href="/wiki/Man" title="Man">men</a> have.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_policy">International policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: International policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sustainable agriculture has become a topic of interest in the international policy arena, especially with regards to its potential to reduce the risks associated with a changing climate and growing human population.</p>
<p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://cgspace.cgiar.org/handle/10568/10701">Commission on Sustainable Agriculture and Climate Change</a>, as part of its recommendations for policy makers on achieving food security in the face of climate change, urged that sustainable agriculture must be integrated into national and international policy. The Commission stressed that increasing weather variability and climate shocks will negatively affect agricultural yields, necessitating early action to drive change in agricultural production systems towards increasing resilience. It also called for dramatically increased investments in sustainable agriculture in the next decade, including in national research and development budgets, land rehabilitation, economic incentives, and infrastructure improvement.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_Ethics">Policy Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Policy Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most agricultural professionals agree that there is a "moral obligation to pursue [the] goal [of] sustainability."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> The major debate comes from what system will provide a path to that goal. Because if an unsustainable method is used on a large scale it will have a massive negative effect on the environment and human population. The best way to create policy for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> is to be free of any bias. A good review would be done with "practical wisdom,"<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> a virtue identified by <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, distinguishing practical wisdom from scientific knowledge, this coming from <a href="/wiki/Nicomachean_Ethics" title="Nicomachean Ethics">Nichomachean Ethics</a>. The science of agriculture is called "<a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomy</a>," the root of this word relating to scientific law<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup>. Although agriculture may not fit well under scientific law, and may not be designed to be treated as an <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian" class="mw-disambig" title="Aristotelian">Aristotelian</a> scientific knowledge, but more practical wisdom. Practical wisdom requires recognition of past failures in agriculture to better attain a more sustainable <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural systems">agricultural system</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_planning">Urban planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Urban planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There has been considerable debate about which form of human residential habitat may be a better social form for sustainable agriculture.</p>
<p>Many environmentalists advocate urban developments with high population density as a way of preserving agricultural land and maximizing energy efficiency. However, others have theorized that sustainable <a href="/wiki/Ecocities" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecocities">ecocities</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">ecovillages</a> which combine habitation and farming with close proximity between producers and consumers, may provide greater sustainability<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>The use of available city space (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Rooftop_gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Rooftop gardens">rooftop gardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Community gardens">community gardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garden_sharing" title="Garden sharing">garden sharing</a>, and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Urban_agriculture" title="Urban agriculture">urban agriculture</a>) for cooperative food production is another way to achieve greater sustainability<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>One of the latest ideas in achieving sustainable agriculture involves shifting the production of food plants from major factory farming operations to large, urban, technical facilities called vertical farms. The advantages of <a href="/wiki/Vertical_farming" title="Vertical farming">vertical farming</a> include year-round production, isolation from pests and diseases, controllable resource recycling, and on-site production that reduces transportation costs<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. While a vertical farm has yet to become a reality, the idea is gaining momentum among those who believe that current sustainable farming methods will be insufficient to provide for a growing global population.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Barriers">Barriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Barriers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since World War II, agriculture in the United States and the entire national food system in its entirety has been characterized by models that focus on monetary <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profitability</a> at the expense of social and environmental integrity.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visible">Visible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Visible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In sustainable agriculture, changes in lower rates of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> loss, improved <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">soil structure</a>, and higher levels of beneficial <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> are not quick.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-28">[28]</a></sup> The changes are not immediately evident to the operate when using sustainable agriculture. In conventional agriculture the befits are easily visible with no weeds, pests, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-28">[28]</a></sup> The "process of externalization" <sup id="cite_ref-:2_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-28">[28]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Conventional_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Conventional agriculture">conventional agriculture</a> also hides the costs to soil and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> around it. A major barrier to sustainable agriculture is the lack of knowledge of all the benefits of it. Much of the benefits are not <a href="/wiki/Visibility_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Visibility (disambiguation)">visible</a>, so they are often unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Efforts toward more sustainable agriculture are supported in the sustainability community, however, these are often viewed only as incremental steps and not as an end. Some foresee a true sustainable <a href="/wiki/Steady_state_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Steady state economy">steady state economy</a> that may be very different from today's: greatly reduced energy usage, minimal <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>, fewer <a href="/wiki/Consumer_packaged_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumer packaged goods">consumer packaged goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_purchasing" title="Local purchasing">local purchasing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Short_food_supply_chains" title="Short food supply chains">short food supply chains</a>, little <a href="/wiki/Processed_foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Processed foods">processed foods</a>, more home and <a href="/wiki/Community_garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Community garden">community gardens</a>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Agriculture would be very different in this type of sustainable economy.</p>
