<p>A <b>reservoir</b> (<a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>: from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>réservoir</i> a "storehouse" <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a storage space for fluids. These fluids may be water, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> or gas. A reservoir usually means an enlarged <b>natural</b> or <b>artificial lake</b>, <b>storage pond</b> or <b>impoundment</b> created using a <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dam</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">lock</a> to store water. Reservoirs can be created by controlling a stream that drains an existing body of water. They can also be constructed in river valleys using a dam. Alternately, a reservoir can be built by excavating flat ground and/or constructing <a href="/wiki/Retaining_wall" title="Retaining wall">retaining walls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>.</p>
<p><i>Tank reservoirs</i> store liquids or gases in <a href="/wiki/Storage_tanks" class="mw-redirect" title="Storage tanks">storage tanks</a> that may be elevated, at grade level, or buried. Tank reservoirs for water are also called <a href="/wiki/Cistern" title="Cistern">cisterns</a>.</p>
<p>Underground reservoirs are used to store liquids, principally either water or <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, below ground.</p>

<p>A dam constructed in a valley relies on the natural <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> to provide most of the basin of the reservoir. Dams are typically located at a narrow part of a valley downstream of a natural basin. The valley sides act as natural walls, with the dam located at the narrowest practical point to provide strength and the lowest cost of construction. In many reservoir construction projects, people have to be moved and re-housed, historical artifacts moved or rare environments relocated. Examples are the temples of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Simbel" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Simbel">Abu Simbel</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (which were moved before the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">Aswan Dam</a> to create <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nasser" title="Lake Nasser">Lake Nasser</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>), the relocation of the village of <a href="/wiki/Capel_Celyn" title="Capel Celyn">Capel Celyn</a> during the construction of <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Celyn" title="Llyn Celyn">Llyn Celyn</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the relocation of <a href="/wiki/Petrella_Salto" title="Petrella Salto">Borgo San Pietro of Petrella Salto</a> during the construction of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Salto" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Salto">Lake Salto</a>.</p>
<p>Construction of a reservoir in a valley will usually need the river to be diverted during part of the build, often through a temporary <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnel</a> or by-pass channel.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In hilly regions, reservoirs are often constructed by enlarging existing lakes. Sometimes in such reservoirs the new top water level exceeds the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> height on one or more of the feeder streams such as at <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Clywedog" class="mw-redirect" title="Llyn Clywedog">Llyn Clywedog</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">Mid Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In such cases additional side dams are required to contain the reservoir.</p>
<p>Where the topography is poorly suited to a single large reservoir, a number of smaller reservoirs may be constructed in a chain, as in the <a href="/wiki/River_Taff" title="River Taff">River Taff</a> valley where the <a href="/wiki/Llwyn-on_Reservoir" title="Llwyn-on Reservoir">Llwyn-on</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantref_Reservoir" title="Cantref Reservoir">Cantref</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beacons_Reservoir" title="Beacons Reservoir">Beacons Reservoirs</a> form a chain up the valley.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bank-side_reservoir">Bank-side reservoir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bank-side reservoir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Where water is pumped or <a href="/wiki/Siphon" title="Siphon">siphoned</a> from a river of variable quality or quantity<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, bank-side reservoirs may be built to store the water. Such reservoirs are usually formed partly by excavation and partly by building a complete encircling bund or <a href="/wiki/Bank_(geography)" title="Bank (geography)">embankment</a>, which may exceed 6&#160;km (4 miles) in circumference.<sup id="cite_ref-ICEQueenMary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICEQueenMary-7">[7]</a></sup> Both the floor of the reservoir and the bund must have an impermeable lining or core: initially these were often made of <a href="/wiki/Puddling_(engineering)" title="Puddling (engineering)">puddled clay</a>, but this has generally been superseded by the modern use of <a href="/wiki/Road_roller" title="Road roller">rolled</a> clay. The water stored in such reservoirs may stay there for several months, during which time normal biological processes may substantially reduce many contaminants and almost eliminate any <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>. The use of bank-side reservoirs also allows water abstraction to be stopped for some time, when the river is unacceptably polluted or when flow conditions are very low due to drought. The London water supply system is one example of the use of bank-side storage: the water is taken from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Lee_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Lee (England)">River Lee</a>; several large Thames-side reservoirs such as <a href="/wiki/Queen_Mary_Reservoir" title="Queen Mary Reservoir">Queen Mary Reservoir</a> can be seen along the approach to <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ICEQueenMary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICEQueenMary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_reservoir">Service reservoir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Service reservoir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Service reservoirs<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> store fully treated potable water close to the point of distribution. Many service reservoirs are constructed as <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">water towers</a>, often as elevated structures on concrete pillars where the landscape is relatively flat. Other service reservoirs can be almost entirely underground, especially in more hilly or mountainous country. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thames_Water" title="Thames Water">Thames Water</a> has many underground reservoirs, sometimes also called <a href="/wiki/Cisterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Cisterns">cisterns</a>, built in the 1800s, most of which are lined with brick. A good example is the <a href="/wiki/Honor_Oak" title="Honor Oak">Honor Oak</a> Reservoir in London, constructed between 1901 and 1909. When it was completed it was said to be the largest brick built underground reservoir in the world<sup id="cite_ref-Honor_Oak_Reservoir_1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honor_Oak_Reservoir_1-9">[9]</a></sup> and it is still one of the largest in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Honor_Oak_Reservoir_2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honor_Oak_Reservoir_2-10">[10]</a></sup> This reservoir now forms part of the southern extension of the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Water_Ring_Main" title="Thames Water Ring Main">Thames Water Ring Main</a>. The top of the reservoir has been grassed over and is now used by the Aquarius Golf Club.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Service reservoirs perform several functions, including ensuring sufficient head of water in the <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water distribution system</a> and providing water capacity to even out peak demand from consumers, enabling the treatment plant to run at optimum efficiency. Large service reservoirs can also be managed to reduce the cost of pumping, by refilling the reservoir at times of day when energy costs are low.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Circa 3 000 BC, the craters of extinct <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> were used as reservoirs by farmers for their <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> water.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dry climate and <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity_in_India" title="Water scarcity in India">water scarcity in India</a> led to early development of <a href="/wiki/Water_resource_management" title="Water resource management">water resource management</a> techniques, including the building of a reservoir at <a href="/wiki/Girnar" title="Girnar">Girnar</a> in 3000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Rodda_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rodda-13">[13]</a></sup> Artificial lakes dating to the 5th century BC have been found in ancient <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> An artificial lake in present-day <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a> state of India, constructed in the 11th century, covered 650 square metres (7,000&#160;sq&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Rodda_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rodda-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> large reservoirs were created by ancient <a href="/wiki/Sinhala_Kingdom" title="Sinhala Kingdom">Sinhalese</a> kings in order to save the water for irrigation. The famous Sri Lankan king <a href="/wiki/Par%C4%81kramab%C4%81hu_I_of_Sri_Lanka" class="mw-redirect" title="Parākramabāhu I of Sri Lanka">Parākramabāhu I of Sri Lanka</a> said "Do not let a drop of water seep into the ocean without benefiting mankind". He created the reservoir named <a href="/wiki/Parakrama_Samudra" title="Parakrama Samudra">Parakrama Samudra</a> (sea of King Parakrama).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Vast artificial reservoirs were also built by various ancient kingdoms in Bengal, Assam and Cambodia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_water_supply">Direct water supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Direct water supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Many dammed river reservoirs and most bank-side reservoirs are used to provide the <a href="/wiki/Raw_water" title="Raw water">raw water</a> feed to a <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a> plant which delivers <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> through water mains. The reservoir does not merely hold water until it is needed: it can also be the first part of the <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a> process. The time the water is held before it is released is known as the <i>retention time</i>. This is a design feature that allows particles and <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silts</a> to settle out, as well as time for natural biological treatment using <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> that naturally live in the water. However natural <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnological</a> processes in temperate climate lakes produce temperature <a href="/wiki/Lake_stratification" title="Lake stratification">stratification</a> in the water, which tends to partition some elements such as <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> into deep, cold anoxic water during the summer months. In the autumn and winter the lake becomes fully mixed again. During drought conditions, it is sometimes necessary to draw down the cold bottom water, and the elevated levels of manganese in particular can cause problems in water treatment plants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hydroelectric_dam.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Hydroelectric_dam.svg/220px-Hydroelectric_dam.svg.png" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Hydroelectric_dam.svg/330px-Hydroelectric_dam.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Hydroelectric_dam.svg/440px-Hydroelectric_dam.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="575" data-file-height="390" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hydroelectric_dam.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hydroelectric dam in cross section.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2005 about 25% of the world's 33,105 large dams (over 15  metres in height) were used for hydroelectricity.<sup id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-16">[16]</a></sup> However of 80,000 dams of all sizes in the U.S., only 3% produce electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A reservoir generating <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">turbines</a> connected to the retained water body by large-diameter pipes. These generating sets may be at the base of the dam or some distance away. In a flat river valley a reservoir needs to be deep enough to create a <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">head</a> of water at the turbines; and if there are periods of drought the reservoir needs to hold enough water to average out the river's flow throughout the year(s). <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">Run-of-the-river hydro</a> in a steep valley with constant flow needs no reservoir.</p>
<p>Some reservoirs generating hydroelectricity use pumped recharge: a high-level reservoir is filled with water using high-performance electric pumps at times when electricity demand is low, and then uses this stored water to generate electricity by releasing the stored water into a low-level reservoir when electricity demand is high. Such systems are called <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pump-storage</a> schemes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>Reservoirs can be used in a number of ways to control how water flows through downstream waterways:</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Downstream water supply</b> – water may be released from an upland reservoir so that it can be abstracted for drinking water lower down the system, sometimes hundred of miles further downstream.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>Irrigation</b> – water in an <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> reservoir may be released into networks of <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> for use in <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farmlands</a> or secondary water systems. Irrigation may also be supported by reservoirs which maintain river flows, allowing water to be abstracted for irrigation lower down the river.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>Flood control</b> – also known as an "attenuation" or "balancing" reservoirs, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> control reservoirs collect water at times of very high rainfall, then release it slowly during the following weeks or months. Some of these reservoirs are constructed across the river line, with the onward flow controlled by an <a href="/wiki/Orifice_plate" title="Orifice plate">orifice plate</a>. When river flow exceeds the capacity of the orifice plate, water builds up behind the dam; but as soon as the flow rate reduces, the water behind the dam is slowly released until the reservoir is empty again. In some cases such reservoirs only function a few times in a decade, and the land behind the reservoir may be developed as community or recreational land. A new generation of balancing dams are being developed to combat the possible consequences of climate change. They are called "Flood Detention Reservoirs". Because these reservoirs will remain dry for long periods, there may be a risk of the clay core drying out, reducing its structural stability. Recent developments include the use of composite core fill made from recycled materials as an alternative to clay.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>Canals</b> – Where a natural watercourse's water is not available to be diverted into a <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a>, a reservoir may be built to guarantee the water level in the canal: for example, where a canal climbs through <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">locks</a> to cross a range of hills.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<dl>
<dd><b>Recreation</b> – water may be released from a reservoir to create or supplement <a href="/wiki/White_water" class="mw-redirect" title="White water">white water</a> conditions for <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayaking</a> and other white-water sports.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmonid</a> rivers special releases (in Britain called <i><a href="/wiki/Freshet" title="Freshet">freshets</a></i>) are made to encourage natural migration behaviours in fish and to provide a variety of fishing conditions for anglers.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_balancing">Flow balancing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flow balancing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reservoirs can be used to balance the flow in highly managed systems, taking in water during high flows and releasing it again during low flows. In order for this to work without pumping requires careful control of water levels using spillways. When a major storm approaches, the dam operators calculate the volume of water that the storm will add to the reservoir. If forecast storm water will overfill the reservoir, water is slowly let out of the reservoir prior to, and during, the storm. If done with sufficient lead time, the major storm will not fill the reservoir and areas downstream will not experience damaging flows. Accurate weather forecasts are essential so that dam operators can correctly plan drawdowns prior to a high rainfall event. Dam operators blamed a faulty weather forecast on the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%932011_Queensland_floods" class="mw-redirect" title="2010–2011 Queensland floods">2010–2011 Queensland floods</a>. Examples of highly managed reservoirs are <a href="/wiki/Burrendong_Dam" title="Burrendong Dam">Burrendong Dam</a> in Australia and <a href="/wiki/Bala_Lake" title="Bala Lake">Bala Lake</a> (<i>Llyn Tegid</i>) in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a>. Bala Lake is a natural lake whose level was raised by a low dam and into which the <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Wales" title="River Dee, Wales">River Dee</a> flows or discharges depending upon flow conditions, as part of the <a href="/wiki/River_Dee_regulation_system" class="mw-redirect" title="River Dee regulation system">River Dee regulation system</a>. This mode of operation is a form of hydraulic <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a> in the river system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many reservoirs often allow some <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreational</a> uses, such as <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boating" title="Boating">boating</a>. Special rules may apply for the safety of the public and to protect the quality of the water and the ecology of the surrounding area. Many reservoirs now support and encourage less formal and less structured recreation such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird_watching" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird watching">bird watching</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_painting" title="Landscape painting">landscape painting</a>, walking and <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, and often provide information boards and interpretation material to encourage responsible use.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water falling as rain upstream of the reservoir, together with any <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> emerging as springs, is stored in the reservoir. Any excess water can be spilled via a specifically designed spillway. Stored water may be piped by <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> for use as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, to generate <a href="/wiki/Hydro-electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydro-electricity">hydro-electricity</a> or to maintain river flows to support downstream uses. Occasionally reservoirs can be managed to retain water during high rainfall events to prevent or reduce downstream flooding. Some reservoirs support several uses, and the operating rules may be complex.</p>

<p>Most modern reservoirs have a specially designed <a href="/wiki/Draw-off_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Draw-off tower">draw-off tower</a> that can discharge water from the reservoir at different levels, both to access water as the water level falls, and to allow water of a specific quality to be discharged into the downstream river as "compensation water": the operators of many upland or in-river reservoirs have obligations to release water into the downstream river to maintain river quality, support fisheries, to maintain downstream industrial and recreational uses or for a range of other purposes. Such releases are known as <i>compensation water</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The terminology for reservoirs varies from country to country. In most of the world, reservoir areas are expressed in square kilometres; in the United States acres are commonly used. For volume either cubic metres or cubic kilometres are widely used, with acre-feet used in the US.</p>
<p>The capacity, volume or storage of a reservoir is usually divided into distinguishable areas. <i>Dead</i> or <i>inactive</i> storage refers to water in a reservoir that cannot be drained by gravity through a dam's <a href="/wiki/Outlet_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Outlet works">outlet works</a>, spillway or power plant intake and can only be pumped out. Dead storage allows sediments to settle, which improves water quality and also creates an area for fish during low levels. <i>Active</i> or <i>live</i> storage is the portion of the reservoir that can be used for flood control, power production, <a href="/wiki/Navigability" title="Navigability">navigation</a> and downstream releases. In addition, a reservoir's "flood control capacity" is the amount of water it can regulate during flooding. The "surcharge capacity" is the capacity of the reservoir above the spillway crest that cannot be regulated.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States the water below the normal maximum level of a reservoir is called the "conservation pool".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, "top water level" describes the reservoir full state, whilst "fully drawn down" describes the minimum retained volume.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling_reservoir_management">Modelling reservoir management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modelling reservoir management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a wide variety of software for modelling reservoirs, from the specialist Dam Safety Program Management Tools (DSPMT) to the relatively simple <a href="/wiki/WAFLEX" title="WAFLEX">WAFLEX</a>, to integrated models like the <a href="/wiki/WEAP" title="WEAP">Water Evaluation And Planning system (WEAP)</a> that place reservoir operations in the context of system-wide demands and supplies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many countries large reservoirs are closely regulated to try to prevent or minimise failures of containment.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>While much of the effort is directed at the dam and its associated structures as the weakest part of the overall structure, the aim of such controls is to prevent an uncontrolled release of water from the reservoir. Reservoir failures can generate huge increases in flow down a river valley, with the potential to wash away towns and villages and cause considerable loss of life such as the devastation following the failure of containment at <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Eigiau" title="Llyn Eigiau">Llyn Eigiau</a> which killed 17 people.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>(see also <a href="/wiki/List_of_dam_failures" class="mw-redirect" title="List of dam failures">List of dam failures</a>)</p>
<p>A notable case of reservoirs being used as an instrument of war involved the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/No._617_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 617 Squadron RAF">Dambusters</a> raid on <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (codenamed "<a href="/wiki/Operation_Chastise" title="Operation Chastise">Operation Chastise</a>" <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>), in which three German reservoir dams were selected to be breached in order to damage German infrastructure and manufacturing and power capabilities deriving from the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eder" title="Eder">Eder</a> rivers. The economic and social impact was derived from the enormous volumes of previously stored water that swept down the valleys, wreaking destruction. This raid later became the basis for several films.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Whole_life_environmental_impact">Whole life environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Whole life environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All reservoirs will have a monetary cost/benefit assessment made before construction to see if the project is worth proceeding with.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, such analysis can often omit the environmental impacts of dams and the reservoirs that they contain. Some impacts such as the greenhouse gas production associated with concrete manufacture are relatively easy to estimate. Other impact on the natural environment and social and cultural effects can be more difficult to assess and to weigh in the balance but identification and quantification of these issues are now commonly required in major construction projects in the developed world <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Naturally occurring lakes receive organic sediments which decay in an <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> environment releasing (<a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>). The methane released is approximately 8 times more potent as a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> than carbon dioxide <sup id="cite_ref-Houghton_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houghton-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a man made reservoir fills, existing plants are submerged and during the years it takes for this matter to decay, will give off considerably more greenhouse gases than lakes do. A reservoir in a narrow valley or canyon may cover relatively little vegetation, one situated on a plain may flood a great deal of vegetation. The site may be cleared of vegetation first or simply flooded. Tropical flooding can produce far more greenhouse gases than in temperate regions.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity_and_climate_change">Hydroelectricity and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Depending upon the area flooded vs power produced, a reservoir built for hydro-electricity generation can either reduce or increase the net production of greenhouse gases when compared to other sources of power.</p>
<p>A study for the National Institute for Research in the Amazon found that Hydroelectric reservoirs release a large pulse of carbon dioxide from decay of trees left standing in the reservoirs, especially during the first decade after flooding.<sup id="cite_ref-envcon_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-envcon-32">[32]</a></sup> This elevates the global warming impact of the dams to levels much higher than would occur by generating the same power from fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-envcon_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-envcon-32">[32]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Dams" title="World Commission on Dams">World Commission on Dams</a> report (Dams And Development), when the reservoir is relatively large and no prior clearing of forest in the flooded area was undertaken, greenhouse gas emissions from the reservoir could be higher than those of a conventional oil-fired thermal generation plant.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> For instance, In 1990, the impoundment behind the <a href="/wiki/Balbina_Dam" title="Balbina Dam">Balbina Dam</a> in Brazil (inaugurated in 1987) had over 20 times the impact on global warming than would generating the same power from fossil fuels, due to the large area flooded per unit of electricity generated.<sup id="cite_ref-envcon_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-envcon-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tucuru%C3%AD_Dam" title="Tucuruí Dam">Tucuruí Dam</a> in Brazil (completed in 1984) had only 0.4 times the impact on global warming than would generating the same power from fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-envcon_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-envcon-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>A two-year study of carbon dioxide and methane releases in Canada concluded that while the hydroelectric reservoirs there do emit greenhouse gases, it is on a much smaller scale than thermal power plants of similar capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Hydropower typically emits 35 to 70 times less greenhouse gases per TWh of electricity than thermal power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>A decrease in air pollution occurs when a dam is used in place of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal power">thermal power</a> generation, since electricity produced from hydroelectric generation does not give rise to any <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas_emissions_from_fossil_fuel_combustion" class="mw-redirect" title="Flue gas emissions from fossil fuel combustion">flue gas emissions from fossil fuel combustion</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dams can produce a block for migrating fish, trapping them in one area, producing food and a habitat for various water-birds. They can also flood various <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> on land and may cause extinctions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_impact">Human impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Human impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dams can severely reduce the amount of water reaching countries downstream of them, causing water stress between the countries, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, which damages farming businesses in the downstream countries, and reduces drinking water.</p>
<p>Farms and villages, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ashopton" title="Ashopton">Ashopton</a> can be flooded by the creation of reservoirs, ruining many livelihoods. For this very reason, worldwide 80 million people (figure is as of 2009, from the Edexcel GCSE Geography textbook) have had to be forcibly relocated due to dam construction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limnology">Limnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Limnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a> of reservoirs has many similarities to that of lakes of equivalent size. There are however significant differences.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Many reservoirs experience considerable variations in level producing significant areas that are intermittently underwater or dried out. This greatly limits the productivity or the water margins and limits the number of species able to survive in these conditions.</p>
<p>Upland reservoirs tend to have a much shorter residence time than natural lakes and this can lead to more rapid cycling of <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> through the water body so that they are more quickly lost to the system. This may be seen as a mismatch between water chemistry and water biology with a tendency for the biological component to be more <a href="/wiki/Oligotroph" title="Oligotroph">oligotrophic</a> than the chemistry would suggest.</p>
<p>Conversely, lowland reservoirs drawing water from nutrient rich rivers, may show exaggerated <a href="/wiki/Eutrophic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutrophic">eutrophic</a> characteristics because the residence time in the reservoir is much greater than in the river and the biological systems have a much greater opportunity to utilise the available nutrients.</p>
<p>Deep reservoirs with multiple level draw off towers can discharge deep cold water into the downstream river greatly reducing the size of any <a href="/wiki/Hypolimnion" title="Hypolimnion">hypolimnion</a>. This in turn can reduce the concentrations of phosphorus released during any annual mixing event and may therefore reduce <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>.</p>
<p>The dams in front of reservoirs act as <a href="/wiki/Knickpoint" title="Knickpoint">knickpoints</a>-the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravitational potential energy">energy</a> of the water falling from them reduces and deposition is a result below the dams.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seismicity">Seismicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Seismicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The filling (impounding) of reservoirs has often been attributed to <a href="/wiki/Induced_seismicity#Reservoirs" title="Induced seismicity">reservoir-triggered seismicity</a> (RTS) as seismic events have occurred near large dams or within their reservoirs in the past. These events may have been triggered by the filling or operation of the reservoir and are on a small scale when compared to the amount of reservoirs worldwide. Of over 100 recorded events, early examples include the 60&#160;m (197&#160;ft) tall <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Dam" title="Marathon Dam">Marathon Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> (1929), the 221&#160;m (725&#160;ft) tall <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">Hoover Dam</a> in the U.S. (1935). Most events involve large dams and small amounts of seismicity. The only four recorded events above a 6.0-<a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">magnitude (M<sub>w</sub>)</a> are the 103&#160;m (338&#160;ft) tall <a href="/wiki/Koyna_Dam" title="Koyna Dam">Koyna Dam</a> in India and the 120&#160;m (394&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Kremasta_Dam" title="Kremasta Dam">Kremasta Dam</a> in Greece which both registered 6.3-M<sub>w</sub>, the 122&#160;m (400&#160;ft) high <a href="/wiki/Kariba_Dam" title="Kariba Dam">Kariba Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> at 6.25-M<sub>w</sub> and the 105&#160;m (344&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Xinfengjiang_Dam" title="Xinfengjiang Dam">Xinfengjiang Dam</a> in China at 6.1-M<sub>w</sub>. Disputes occur over when RTS has occurred due to a lack of hydrogeological knowledge at the time of the event. It is accepted, though, that the infiltration of water into pores and the weight of the reservoir do contribute to RTS patterns. For RTS to occur, there must be a seismic structure near the dam or its reservoir and the seismic structure must be close to failure. Additionally, water must be able to infiltrate the deep rock <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">stratum</a> as the weight of a 100&#160;m (328&#160;ft) deep reservoir will have little impact when compared the deadweight of rock on a <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology#Stress_Fields" title="Structural geology">crustal stress field</a> which may be located at a depth of 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) or more.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microclimate">Microclimate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Microclimate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reservoirs may change the local micro-climate increasing humidity and reducing extremes of temperature, especially in dry areas. Such effects are claimed also by some <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Winery" title="Winery">wineries</a> as increasing the quality of the wine production.</p>
