			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Oxyhydrogen</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Knallgas" redirects here. For bacteria which oxidize hydrogen, see <a href="/wiki/Knallgas-bacteria" class="mw-redirect" title="Knallgas-bacteria">Knallgas-bacteria</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Electrolyser_1884.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Electrolyser_1884.png/190px-Electrolyser_1884.png" width="190" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Electrolyser_1884.png/285px-Electrolyser_1884.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Electrolyser_1884.png/380px-Electrolyser_1884.png 2x" data-file-width="566" data-file-height="650" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Electrolyser_1884.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Nineteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Electrolytic_cell" title="Electrolytic cell">electrolytic cell</a> for producing oxyhydrogen.</div>

<p><b>Oxyhydrogen</b> is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) gases. This gaseous mixture is used for torches to process <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> materials and was the first<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> gaseous mixture used for <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>. Theoretically, a ratio of 2:1 hydrogen:oxygen is enough to achieve maximum efficiency; in practice a ratio 4:1 or 5:1 is needed to avoid an <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_flame" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidizing flame">oxidizing flame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>This mixture may also be referred to as <i><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Knallgas</span></b></i> (Scandinavian and German <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Knallgas" class="extiw" title="wikt:Knallgas">Knallgas</a>: "bang-gas"), although some authors define knallgas to be a generic term for the mixture of fuel with the precise amount of oxygen required for complete combustion, thus 2:1 oxyhydrogen would be called "hydrogen-knallgas".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Brown's gas</b><sup id="cite_ref-Ball2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2007-4">[4]</a></sup> and <b>HHO</b> are <a href="/wiki/Fringe_science" title="Fringe science">fringe science</a> terms for a 2:1 mixture of oxyhydrogen obtained under certain special conditions; its proponents claim that it has special properties.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxyhydrogen will <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combust</a> when brought to its <a href="/wiki/Autoignition_temperature" title="Autoignition temperature">autoignition temperature</a>. For the <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> mixture, 2:1 hydrogen:oxygen, at normal <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, autoignition occurs at about 570&#160;°C (1065&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since October 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> The minimum energy required to ignite such a mixture with a spark is about 20 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">microjoules</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since October 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, oxyhydrogen can burn when it is between about 4% and 95% hydrogen by volume.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since October 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<p>When ignited, the gas mixture converts to <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">releases energy</a>, which sustains the reaction: 241.8 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a> of energy (<a href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower heating value">LHV</a>) for every <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sub>2</sub> burned. The amount of heat energy released is independent of the mode of combustion, but the <a href="/wiki/Flame_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Flame temperature">temperature of the flame</a> varies.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> The maximum temperature of about 2,800&#160;°C (5,100&#160;°F) is achieved with an exact stoichiometric mixture, about 700&#160;°C (1,300&#160;°F) hotter than a hydrogen flame in air.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When either of the gases are mixed in excess of this ratio, or when mixed with an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert gas</a> like nitrogen, the heat must spread throughout a greater quantity of matter and the temperature will be lower.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A pure stoichiometric mixture may be obtained by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">water electrolysis</a>, which uses an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> to dissociate the water molecules:</p>
<dl>
<dd>electrolysis: 2 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>combustion: 2 H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(chemist)" title="William Nicholson (chemist)">William Nicholson</a> was the first to decompose water in this manner in 1800. In theory, the input energy of a closed system will always equal the output energy, as the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a> states. However, in practice no systems are perfectly closed, and the energy required to generate the oxyhydrogen will always exceed the energy released by combusting it, even at maximum practical efficiency, as the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> implies. (See <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water#Efficiency" title="Electrolysis of water">Electrolysis of water#Efficiency</a>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">Lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many forms of oxyhydrogen <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">lamps</a> have been described, such as the <a href="/wiki/Limelight" title="Limelight">limelight</a>, which used an oxyhydrogen flame to heat a piece of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">lime</a> to <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">white hot</a> <a href="/wiki/Incandescence" title="Incandescence">incandescence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WT_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WT-10">[10]</a></sup> Because of the explosiveness of the oxyhydrogen, limelights have been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Electric_lighting" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric lighting">electric lighting</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxyhydrogen_blowpipe">Oxyhydrogen blowpipe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oxyhydrogen blowpipe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg/220px-Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg/330px-Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg/440px-Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg 2x" data-file-width="518" data-file-height="374" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blowpipe_-_circa_1827.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Nineteenth-century bellows-operated oxy-hydrogen blowpipe, including two different types of <a href="/wiki/Flashback_arrestor" title="Flashback arrestor">flashback arrestor</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The oxy-hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Blowpipe_(tool)" title="Blowpipe (tool)">blowpipe</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogist</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Daniel_Clarke" title="Edward Daniel Clarke">Edward Daniel Clarke</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hare_(chemist)" title="Robert Hare (chemist)">Robert Hare</a> in the early nineteenth century. It produced a flame hot enough to melt such <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> materials as <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire_brick" title="Fire brick">fire brick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>, and was a valuable tool in several fields of science.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is used in the <a href="/wiki/Verneuil_process" title="Verneuil process">Verneuil process</a> to produce synthetic corundum.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxyhydrogen_torch">Oxyhydrogen torch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oxyhydrogen torch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <i>oxyhydrogen torch</i> is an <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting#Hydrogen" title="Oxy-fuel welding and cutting">oxy-gas torch</a>, which burns hydrogen (the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>) with oxygen (the <a href="/wiki/Oxidizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a>). It is used for cutting and <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WT_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WT-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to competition from the acetylene-fueled cutting torch and from arc welding, the oxyhydrogen torch is seldom used today, but it remains the preferred cutting tool in some niche applications—see <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting" title="Oxy-fuel welding and cutting">oxy-fuel welding and cutting</a>.</p>
<p>Oxyhydrogen was once used in working <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> because at the time such a torch was the only device that could attain the temperature required to melt the metal 1,768.3&#160;°C (3,214.9&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-6">[6]</a></sup> These techniques have been superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brown.27s_Gas">Brown's Gas <span id="HHO_gas"></span> <span id="Brown.27s_gas"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhydrogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brown's Gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Brown's Gas" is oxyhydrogen with a 2:1 <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar ratio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a> gases, the same proportion as in <a href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>. It is named after Yull Brown, who claimed that it could be used as a fuel for the internal combustion engine.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball2007_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ball2006_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2006-13">[13]</a></sup> It's also called "HHO gas" after the claims of fringe physicist<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ruggero_Santilli#HHO_gas" title="Ruggero Santilli">Ruggero Santilli</a>, who claims that his HHO gas, produced by a special apparatus, is "a new form of water", with new properties, based on his <a href="/wiki/Fringe_theory" title="Fringe theory">fringe theory</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Magnecule" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnecule">magnecules</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Ball2006_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2006-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many other pseudoscientific claims have been made about Brown's Gas's pretended ability to neutralize radioactive waste, help plants to germinate, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball2006_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2006-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oxyhydrogen is often mentioned in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Water-fuelled_car" title="Water-fuelled car">vehicles that claim to use water as a fuel</a>. The most common and decisive counter-argument against producing this gas on board to use as a fuel or fuel additive is that more energy is needed to split water molecules than is recouped by burning the resulting gas.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball2007_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball2007-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Additionally, the volume of gas that can be produced for on-demand consumption through electrolysis is very small in comparison to the volume consumed by an internal combustion engine.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>An article in <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">Popular Mechanics</a></i> reported that Brown's Gas does not increase the <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel economy in automobiles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Water-fueled" cars should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen-fueled_car" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen-fueled car">hydrogen-fueled cars</a>, where the hydrogen is produced elsewhere and used as fuel or where it is used <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel_enhancement" title="Hydrogen fuel enhancement">as fuel enhancement</a>.</p>
