			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Evidence-based medicine</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Evidence-based">Evidence-based</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Science-based medicine" redirects here. For the website, see <a href="/wiki/Science-Based_Medicine" title="Science-Based Medicine">Science-Based Medicine</a>.</div>
<p><b>Evidence-based medicine</b> (<b>EBM</b>) is an approach to medical practice intended to optimize <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a> by emphasizing the use of <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> from well-designed and -conducted <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>. Although all medicine based on <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> has some degree of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical</a> support, EBM goes further, classifying evidence by its <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemologic</a> strength and requiring that only the strongest types (coming from <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">meta-analyses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systematic_review" title="Systematic review">systematic reviews</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trials</a>) can yield strong recommendations; weaker types (such as from <a href="/wiki/Case-control_study" title="Case-control study">case-control studies</a>) can yield only weak recommendations. The term was originally used to describe an approach to teaching the practice of medicine and improving decisions by individual physicians about individual patients.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyatt_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyatt-1">[1]</a></sup> Use of the term rapidly expanded to include a previously described approach that emphasized the use of evidence in the design of guidelines and policies that apply to groups of patients and populations ("evidence-based practice policies").<sup id="cite_ref-Eddy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eddy-2">[2]</a></sup> It has subsequently spread to describe an approach to decision-making that is used at virtually every level of <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> as well as other fields (<a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_practice" title="Evidence-based practice">evidence-based practice</a>).</p>
<p>Whether applied to medical education, decisions about individuals, guidelines and policies applied to populations, or administration of health services in general, evidence-based medicine advocates that to the greatest extent possible, decisions and policies should be based on evidence, not just the beliefs of practitioners, experts, or administrators. It thus tries to assure that a <a href="/wiki/Clinician" title="Clinician">clinician</a>'s opinion, which may be limited by knowledge gaps or biases, is supplemented with all available knowledge from the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a> so that <a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">best practice</a> can be determined and applied. It promotes the use of formal, explicit methods to analyze evidence and makes it available to decision makers. It promotes programs to teach the methods to medical students, practitioners, and policy makers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background.2C_history_and_definition">Background, history and definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background, history and definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In its broadest form, evidence-based medicine is the application of the scientific method into healthcare decision-making. Medicine has a long tradition of both basic and clinical research that dates back at least to <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid11058989_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11058989-4">[4]</a></sup> An early critique of statistical methods in medicine was published in 1835.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, until recently, the process by which research results were incorporated in medical decisions was highly subjective. Called "clinical judgment" and "the art of medicine", the traditional approach to making decisions about individual patients depended on having each individual physician determine what research evidence, if any, to consider, and how to merge that evidence with personal beliefs and other factors. In the case of decisions that applied to groups of patients or populations, the guidelines and policies would usually be developed by committees of experts, but there was no formal process for determining the extent to which research evidence should be considered or how it should be merged with the beliefs of the committee members. There was an implicit assumption that decision makers and policy makers would incorporate evidence in their thinking appropriately, based on their education, experience, and ongoing study of the applicable literature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_decision_making">Clinical decision making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinical decision making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in the late 1960s, several flaws became apparent in the traditional approach to medical decision-making. <a href="/wiki/Alvan_Feinstein" title="Alvan Feinstein">Alvan Feinstein</a>'s publication of <i>Clinical Judgment</i> in 1967 focused attention on the role of clinical reasoning and identified biases that can affect it.<sup id="cite_ref-feinstein_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feinstein-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1972, <a href="/wiki/Archie_Cochrane" title="Archie Cochrane">Archie Cochrane</a> published <i>Effectiveness and Efficiency</i>, which described the lack of controlled trials supporting many practices that had previously been assumed to be effective.<sup id="cite_ref-cochraneal_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cochraneal-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1973, <a href="/wiki/John_Wennberg" class="mw-redirect" title="John Wennberg">John Wennberg</a> began to document wide variations in how physicians practiced.<sup id="cite_ref-wennberg_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wennberg-8">[8]</a></sup> Through the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/David_M._Eddy" title="David M. Eddy">David M. Eddy</a> described errors in clinical reasoning and gaps in evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eddy3_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy3-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eddy4_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy4-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eddy5_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy5-12">[12]</a></sup> In the mid 1980s, Alvin Feinstein, <a href="/wiki/David_Sackett" title="David Sackett">David Sackett</a> and others published textbooks on clinical epidemiology, which translated epidemiological methods to physician decision making.<sup id="cite_ref-feinstein2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feinstein2-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sackett_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett-14">[14]</a></sup> Toward the end of the 1980s, a group at <a href="/wiki/RAND" class="mw-redirect" title="RAND">RAND</a> showed that large proportions of procedures performed by physicians were considered inappropriate even by the standards of their own experts.<sup id="cite_ref-chassin_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chassin-15">[15]</a></sup> These areas of research increased awareness of the weaknesses in medical decision making at the level of both individual patients and populations, and paved the way for the introduction of evidence-based methods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence-based">Evidence-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evidence-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "evidence-based medicine", as it is currently used, has two main tributaries. Chronologically, the first is the insistence on explicit evaluation of evidence of effectiveness when issuing clinical practice guidelines and other population-level policies. The second is the introduction of epidemiological methods into medical education and individual patient-level decision-making.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence-based_guidelines_and_policies">Evidence-based guidelines and policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evidence-based guidelines and policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "evidence-based" was first used by David M. Eddy in the course of his work on population-level policies such as clinical practice guidelines and insurance coverage of new technologies. He first began to use the term "evidence-based" in 1987 in workshops and a manual commissioned by the Council of Medical Specialty Societies to teach formal methods for designing clinical practice guidelines. The manual was widely available in unpublished form in the late 1980s and eventually published by the American College of Medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy1992_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy1992-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-field_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-field-17">[17]</a></sup> Eddy first published the term "evidence-based" in March, 1990 in an article in the <i>Journal of the American Medical Association</i> that laid out the principles of evidence-based guidelines and population-level policies, which Eddy described as "explicitly describing the available evidence that pertains to a policy and tying the policy to evidence. Consciously anchoring a policy, not to current practices or the beliefs of experts, but to experimental evidence. The policy must be consistent with and supported by evidence. The pertinent evidence must be identified, described, and analyzed. The policymakers must determine whether the policy is justified by the evidence. A rationale must be written."<sup id="cite_ref-eddy6_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy6-18">[18]</a></sup> He discussed "evidence-based" policies in several other papers published in <i>JAMA</i> in the spring of 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy6_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy6-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eddy7_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy7-19">[19]</a></sup> Those papers were part of a series of 28 published in <i>JAMA</i> between 1990 and 1997 on formal methods for designing population-level guidelines and policies.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy8_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy8-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_education">Medical education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medical education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "evidence-based medicine" was introduced slightly later, in the context of medical education. This branch of evidence-based medicine has its roots in clinical epidemiology. In the autumn of 1990, Gordon Guyatt used it in an unpublished description of a program at <a href="/wiki/McMaster_University" title="McMaster University">McMaster University</a> for prospective or new medical students.<sup id="cite_ref-howick_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howick-21">[21]</a></sup> Guyatt and others first published the term two years later (1992) to describe a new approach to teaching the practice of medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyatt_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyatt-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1996, David Sackett and colleagues clarified the definition of this tributary of evidence-based medicine as "the conscientious, explicit and judicious use of current best evidence in making decisions about the care of individual patients. ... [It] means integrating individual clinical expertise with the best available external clinical evidence from systematic research."<sup id="cite_ref-sackett2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett2-22">[22]</a></sup> This branch of evidence-based medicine aims to make individual decision making more structured and objective by better reflecting the evidence from research.<sup id="cite_ref-katz_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-katz-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-grobbee_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grobbee-24">[24]</a></sup> It requires the application of population-based data to the care of an individual patient,<sup id="cite_ref-doi_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi-25">[25]</a></sup> while respecting the fact that practitioners have clinical expertise reflected in effective and efficient diagnosis and thoughtful identification and compassionate use of individual patients' predicaments, rights, and preferences.<sup id="cite_ref-sackett2_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett2-22">[22]</a></sup> This tributary of evidence-based medicine had its foundations in clinical epidemiology, a discipline that teaches medical students and physicians how to apply clinical and epidemiological research studies to their practices. The methods were published to a broad physician audience in a series of 25 "Users’ Guides to the Medical Literature" published in <i>JAMA</i> between 1993 and 2000 by the Evidence-based Medicine Working Group at McMaster University. Other definitions for individual level evidence-based medicine have been put forth. For example, in 1995 Rosenberg and Donald defined it as "the process of finding, appraising, and using contemporaneous research findings as the basis for medical decisions."<sup id="cite_ref-rosenberg2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rosenberg2-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2010, Greenhalgh used a definition that emphasized quantitative methods: "the use of mathematical estimates of the risk of benefit and harm, derived from high-quality research on population samples, to inform clinical decision-making in the diagnosis, investigation or management of individual patients."<sup id="cite_ref-greenhalgh1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenhalgh1-27">[27]</a></sup> Many other definitions have been offered for individual level evidence-based medicine, but the one by Sackett and colleagues is the most commonly cited.<sup id="cite_ref-sackett2_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two original definitions highlight important differences in how evidence-based medicine is applied to populations versus individuals. When designing policies such as guidelines that will be applied to large groups of people in settings where there is relatively little opportunity for modification by individual physicians, evidence-based policymaking stresses that there be good evidence documenting that the effectiveness of the test or treatment under consideration.<sup id="cite_ref-Eddy1990_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eddy1990-28">[28]</a></sup> In the setting of individual decision-making there is additional information about the individual patients. Practitioners can be given greater latitude in how they interpret research and combine it with their clinical judgment.<sup id="cite_ref-sackett2_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett2-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-greenhalgh2_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenhalgh2-29">[29]</a></sup> Recognizing the two branches of EBM, in 2005 Eddy offered an umbrella definition: "Evidence-based medicine is a set of principles and methods intended to ensure that to the greatest extent possible, medical decisions, guidelines, and other types of policies are based on and consistent with good evidence of effectiveness and benefit."<sup id="cite_ref-eddybmapproach_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddybmapproach-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Progress">Progress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Progress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both branches of evidence-based medicine spread rapidly. On the evidence-based guidelines and policies side, explicit insistence on evidence of effectiveness was introduced by the American Cancer Society in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-guidelines_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guidelines-31">[31]</a></sup> The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) began issuing guidelines for preventive interventions based on evidence-based principles in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-taskforce_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taskforce-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1985, the Blue Cross Blue Shield Association applied strict evidence-based criteria for covering new technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-rettig_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rettig-33">[33]</a></sup> Beginning in 1987, specialty societies such as the American College of Physicians, and voluntary health organizations such as the American Heart Association, wrote many evidence-based guidelines. In 1991, Kaiser Permanente, a managed care organization in the US, began an evidence-based guidelines program.<sup id="cite_ref-kaiserpermanente_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaiserpermanente-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1991, Richard Smith wrote an editorial in the <i>British Medical Journal</i> and introduced the ideas of evidence-based policies in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-smith_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1993, the Cochrane Collaboration created a network of 13 countries to produce of systematic reviews and guidelines.<sup id="cite_ref-cochrane_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cochrane-36">[36]</a></sup> In 1997, the US Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (then known as the Agency for Health Care Policy and Research, or AHCPR) established Evidence-based Practice Centers (EPCs) to produce evidence reports and technology assessments to support the development of guidelines.<sup id="cite_ref-ahrq_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ahrq-37">[37]</a></sup> In the same year, a National Guideline Clearinghouse that followed the principles of evidence-based policies was created by AHRQ, the AMA, and the American Association of Health Plans (now America's Health Insurance Plans).<sup id="cite_ref-guideline_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guideline-38">[38]</a></sup> In 1999, the National Institute for Clinical Excellence (NICE) was created in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-nice_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nice-39">[39]</a></sup> A central idea of this branch of evidence-based medicine is that evidence should be classified according to the rigor of its experimental design, and the strength of a recommendation should depend on the strength of the evidence.</p>
<p>On the medical education side, programs to teach evidence-based medicine have been created in medical schools in Canada, the US, the UK, Australia, and other countries. A 2009 study of UK programs found the more than half of UK medical schools offered some training in evidence-based medicine, although there was considerable variation in the methods and content, and EBM teaching was restricted by lack of curriculum time, trained tutors and teaching materials.<sup id="cite_ref-medteach_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medteach-40">[40]</a></sup> Many programs have been developed to help individual physicians gain better access to evidence. For example, Up-to-date was created in the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-uptodate_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uptodate-41">[41]</a></sup> The Cochrane Center began publishing evidence reviews in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-kaiserpermanente_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaiserpermanente-34">[34]</a></sup> BMJ Publishing Group launched a 6-monthly periodical in 1995 called Clinical Evidence that provided brief summaries of the current state of evidence about important clinical questions for clinicians.<sup id="cite_ref-clinicalevidence_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clinicalevidence-42">[42]</a></sup> Since then many other programs have been developed to make evidence more accessible to practitioners.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_practice">Current practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term evidence-based medicine is now applied to both the programs that are designing evidence-based guidelines and the programs that teach evidence-based medicine to practitioners. By 2000, "evidence-based medicine" had become an umbrella term for the emphasis on evidence in both population-level and individual-level decisions. In subsequent years, use of the term "evidence-based" had extended to other levels of the health care system. An example is "evidence-based health services", which seek to increase the competence of health service decision makers and the practice of evidence-based medicine at the organizational or institutional level.<sup id="cite_ref-gray_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gray-43">[43]</a></sup> The concept has also spread outside of healthcare; for example, in his 1996 inaugural speech as President of the Royal Statistical Society, Adrian Smith proposed that "<a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_policy" title="Evidence-based policy">evidence-based policy</a>" should be established for education, prisons and policing policy and all areas of government work.</p>
<p>The multiple tributaries of evidence-based medicine share an emphasis on the importance of incorporating evidence from formal research in medical policies and decisions. However they differ on the extent to which they require good evidence of effectiveness before promulgating a guideline or payment policy, and they differ on the extent to which it is feasible to incorporate individual-level information in decisions. Thus, evidence-based guidelines and policies may not readily 'hybridise' with experience-based practices orientated towards ethical clinical judgement, and can lead to contradictions, contest, and unintended crises.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy5_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy5-12">[12]</a></sup> The most effective 'knowledge leaders' (managers and clinical leaders) use a broad range of management knowledge in their decision making, rather than just formal evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-feinstein2_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feinstein2-13">[13]</a></sup> Evidence-based guidelines may provide the basis for <a href="/wiki/Governmentality" title="Governmentality">governmentality</a> in health care and consequently play a central role in the distant governance of contemporary health care systems.<sup id="cite_ref-sackett_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sackett-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps">Steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The steps for designing explicit, evidence-based guidelines were described in the late 1980s: Formulate the question (population, intervention, comparison intervention, outcomes, time horizon, setting); search the literature to identify studies that inform the question; interpret each study to determine precisely what it says about the question; if several studies address the question, synthesize their results (meta-analysis); summarize the evidence in "evidence tables"; compare the benefits, harms and costs in a "balance sheet"; draw a conclusion about the preferred practice; write the guideline; write the rationale for the guideline; have others review each of the previous steps; implement the guideline.<sup id="cite_ref-eddy4_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eddy4-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the purposes of medical education and individual-level decision making, five steps of EBM in practice were described in 1992<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and the experience of delegates attending the 2003 Conference of Evidence-Based Health Care Teachers and Developers was summarized into five steps and published in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> This five step process can broadly be categorized as:</p>
<ol>
<li>Translation of uncertainty to an answerable question and includes critical questioning, study design and levels of evidence<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>Systematic retrieval of the best evidence available<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Critical appraisal of evidence for <a href="/wiki/Internal_validity" title="Internal validity">internal validity</a> that can be broken down into aspects regarding:<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
<ul>
<li>Systematic errors as a result of selection bias, information bias and confounding</li>
<li>Quantitative aspects of diagnosis and treatment</li>
<li>The effect size and aspects regarding its precision</li>
<li>Clinical importance of results</li>
<li>External validity or generalizability</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Application of results in practice<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>Evaluation of performance<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_reviews">Evidence reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Evidence reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systematic reviews of published research studies is a major part of the evaluation of particular treatments. The <a href="/wiki/Cochrane_Collaboration" class="mw-redirect" title="Cochrane Collaboration">Cochrane Collaboration</a> is one of the best-known programs that conducts systematic reviews. Like other collections of systematic reviews, it requires authors to provide a detailed and repeatable plan of their literature search and evaluations of the evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-Tanjong2009_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanjong2009-51">[51]</a></sup> Once all the best evidence is assessed, treatment is categorized as (1) likely to be beneficial, (2) likely to be harmful, or (3) evidence did not support either benefit or harm.</p>
<p>A 2007 analysis of 1,016 systematic reviews from all 50 Cochrane Collaboration Review Groups found that 44% of the reviews concluded that the intervention was likely to be beneficial, 7% concluded that the intervention was likely to be harmful, and 49% concluded that evidence did not support either benefit or harm. 96% recommended further research.<sup id="cite_ref-Mapping2007_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mapping2007-52">[52]</a></sup> A 2001 review of 160 Cochrane systematic reviews (excluding complementary treatments) in the 1998 database revealed that, according to two readers, 41.3% concluded positive or possibly positive effect, 20% concluded evidence of no effect, 8.1% concluded net harmful effects, and 21.3% of the reviews concluded insufficient evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-Ezzo2001_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ezzo2001-53">[53]</a></sup> A review of 145 <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a> Cochrane reviews using the 2004 database revealed that 38.4% concluded positive effect or possibly positive (12.4%) effect, 4.8% concluded no effect, 0.69% concluded harmful effect, and 56.6% concluded insufficient evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-IOM2005_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOM2005-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessing_the_quality_of_evidence">Assessing the quality of evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Assessing the quality of evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Levels_of_evidence" title="Levels of evidence">Levels of evidence</a></div>
<p>Evidence quality can be assessed based on the source type (from <a href="/wiki/Meta-analyses" class="mw-redirect" title="Meta-analyses">meta-analyses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systematic_reviews" class="mw-redirect" title="Systematic reviews">systematic reviews</a> of <a href="/wiki/Triple-blind" class="mw-redirect" title="Triple-blind">triple-blind</a> randomized <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical trials">clinical trials</a> with concealment of allocation and no attrition at the top end, down to <a href="/wiki/Conventional_wisdom" title="Conventional wisdom">conventional wisdom</a> at the bottom), as well as other factors including statistical validity, clinical relevance, currency, and peer-review acceptance. Evidence-based medicine categorizes different types of clinical evidence and rates or grades them<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> according to the strength of their freedom from the various biases that beset medical research. For example, the strongest evidence for therapeutic interventions is provided by systematic review of <a href="/wiki/Randomized" class="mw-redirect" title="Randomized">randomized</a>, triple-blind, <a href="/wiki/Placebo-controlled_trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Placebo-controlled trials">placebo-controlled trials</a> with allocation concealment and complete follow-up involving a homogeneous patient population and medical condition. In contrast, patient testimonials, case reports, and even expert opinion (however some critics have argued that expert opinion "does not belong in the rankings of the quality of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a> because it does not represent a form of empirical evidence" and continue that "expert opinion would seem to be a separate, complex type of knowledge that would not fit into hierarchies otherwise limited to empirical evidence alone.")<sup id="cite_ref-Tonelli99_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli99-56">[56]</a></sup> have little value as proof because of the placebo effect, the biases inherent in observation and reporting of cases, difficulties in ascertaining who is an expert and more.</p>
<p>Several organizations have developed grading systems for assessing the quality of evidence. An example that put forth by the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF).<sup id="cite_ref-USPrevServTaskForce_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USPrevServTaskForce-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Level I: Evidence obtained from at least one properly designed <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trial</a>.</li>
<li>Level II-1: Evidence obtained from well-designed controlled trials without <a href="/wiki/Randomization" title="Randomization">randomization</a>.</li>
<li>Level II-2: Evidence obtained from well-designed <a href="/wiki/Cohort_study" title="Cohort study">cohort</a> or <a href="/wiki/Case-control" class="mw-redirect" title="Case-control">case-control</a> analytic studies, preferably from more than one center or research group.</li>
<li>Level II-3: Evidence obtained from multiple time series designs with or without the intervention. Dramatic results in uncontrolled trials might also be regarded as this type of evidence.</li>
<li>Level III: Opinions of respected authorities, based on clinical experience, descriptive studies, or reports of expert committees.</li>
</ul>
<p>Another example of a system for grading evidence is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxford_CEBM_Levels_of_Evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Oxford CEBM Levels of Evidence (page does not exist)">Oxford (UK) CEBM Levels of Evidence</a>. Most of the evidence ranking schemes grade evidence for therapy and prevention, but not for diagnostic tests, prognostic markers, or harm. The Oxford CEBM Levels of Evidence addresses this issue and provides 'Levels' of evidence for claims about prognosis, diagnosis, treatment benefits, treatment harms, and screening. The original CEBM Levels was first released in September 2000 for Evidence-Based On Call to make the process of finding evidence feasible and its results explicit. In 2011, the Oxford CEBM Levels were redesigned by an international team to make it more understandable and to take into account recent developments in evidence ranking schemes. The Oxford CEBM Levels of Evidence have been used by patients, clinicians and also to develop clinical guidelines including recommendations for the optimal use of phototherapy and topical therapy in <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and guidelines for the use of the BCLC staging system for diagnosing and monitoring <a href="/wiki/Hepatocellular_carcinoma" title="Hepatocellular carcinoma">hepatocellular carcinoma</a> in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_recommendations">Categories of recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Categories of recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In guidelines and other publications, recommendation for a clinical service is classified by the balance of risk versus benefit of the service and the level of evidence on which this information is based. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force uses:<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Level A: Good scientific evidence suggests that the benefits of the clinical service substantially outweigh the potential risks. Clinicians should discuss the service with eligible patients.</li>
<li>Level B: At least fair scientific evidence suggests that the benefits of the clinical service outweighs the potential risks. Clinicians should discuss the service with eligible patients.</li>
<li>Level C: At least fair scientific evidence suggests that there are benefits provided by the clinical service, but the balance between benefits and risks are too close for making general recommendations. Clinicians need not offer it unless there are individual considerations.</li>
<li>Level D: At least fair scientific evidence suggests that the risks of the clinical service outweighs potential benefits. Clinicians should not routinely offer the service to asymptomatic patients.</li>
<li>Level I: Scientific evidence is lacking, of poor quality, or conflicting, such that the risk versus benefit balance cannot be assessed. Clinicians should help patients understand the uncertainty surrounding the clinical service.</li>
</ul>
<p>A system was developed by the GRADE working group and takes into account more dimensions than just the quality of medical research.<sup id="cite_ref-GRADE_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRADE-61">[61]</a></sup> It requires users of GRADE (short for Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation) who are performing an assessment of the quality of evidence, usually as part of a systematic review, to consider the impact of different factors on their confidence in the results. Authors of GRADE tables, grade the quality of evidence into four levels, on the basis of their confidence in the observed effect (a numerical value) being close to what the true effect is. The confidence value is based on judgements assigned in five different domains in a structured manner.<sup id="cite_ref-balshem2011_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balshem2011-62">[62]</a></sup> The GRADE working group defines 'quality of evidence' and 'strength of recommendations' based on the quality as two different concepts which are commonly confused with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-balshem2011_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balshem2011-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Systematic reviews may include Randomized Controlled trials that have low risk of bias, or, observational studies that have high risk of bias. In the case of Randomized controlled trials, the quality of evidence is high, but can be downgraded in five different domains.<sup id="cite_ref-GRADE_Handbook_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRADE_Handbook-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Risk of bias: Is a judgement made on the basis of the chance that bias in included studies has influenced the estimate of effect.</li>
<li>Imprecision: Is a judgement made on the basis of the chance that the observed estimate of effect could change completely.</li>
<li>Indirectness: Is a judgement made on the basis of the differences in characteristics of how the study was conducted and how the results are actually going to be applied.</li>
<li>Inconsistency: Is a judgement made on the basis of the variability of results across the included studies.</li>
<li>Publication bias: Is a judgement made on the basis of the question whether all the research evidence has been taken to account.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the case of observational studies, the quality of evidence starts of lower and may be upgraded in three domains in addition to being subject to downgrading.<sup id="cite_ref-GRADE_Handbook_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRADE_Handbook-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Large effect: This is when methodologically strong studies show that the observed effect is so large that the probability of it changing completely is less likely.</li>
<li>Plausible confounding would change the effect: This is when despite the presence of a possible confounding factor which is expected to reduce the observed effect, the effect estimate still shows significant effect.</li>
<li>Dose response gradient: This is when the intervention used becomes more effective with increasing dose. This suggests that a further increase will likely bring about more effect.</li>
</ul>
<p>Meaning of the levels of quality of evidence as per GRADE:<sup id="cite_ref-balshem2011_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-balshem2011-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>High Quality Evidence: The authors are very confident that the estimate that is presented lies very close to the true value. One could interpret it as "there is very low probability of further research completely changing the presented conclusions."</li>
<li>Moderate Quality Evidence: The authors are confident that the presented estimate lies close to the true value, but it is also possible that it may be substantially different. One could also interpret it as: further research may completely change the conclusions.</li>
<li>Low Quality Evidence: The authors are not confident in the effect estimate and the true value may be substantially different. One could interpret it as "further research is likely to change the presented conclusions completely."</li>
<li>Very low quality Evidence: The authors do not have any confidence in the estimate and it is likely that the true value is substantially different from it. One could interpret it as "new research will most probably change the presented conclusions completely."</li>
</ul>
<p>Guideline panelists may make <b>strong</b> or <b>weak recommendations</b> on the basis of further criteria. Some of the important criteria are:<sup id="cite_ref-GRADE_Handbook_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRADE_Handbook-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Balance between desirable and undesirable effects (not considering cost)</li>
<li>Quality of the evidence</li>
<li>Values and preferences</li>
<li>Costs (resource utilization)</li>
</ul>
<p>Despite the differences between systems, the purposes are the same: to guide users of clinical research information on which studies are likely to be most valid. However, the individual studies still require careful critical appraisal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_measures">Statistical measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Statistical measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence-based medicine attempts to express clinical benefits of tests and treatments using mathematical methods. Tools used by practitioners of evidence-based medicine include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Likelihood ratio
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Likelihood_ratios_in_diagnostic_testing" title="Likelihood ratios in diagnostic testing">Likelihood ratios in diagnostic testing</a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Pre-test_odds" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-test odds">pre-test odds</a> of a particular diagnosis, multiplied by the likelihood ratio, determines the <a href="/wiki/Pre-_and_post-test_probability" title="Pre- and post-test probability">post-test odds</a>. (Odds can be calculated from, and then converted to, the [more familiar] probability.) This reflects <a href="/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem" title="Bayes' theorem">Bayes' theorem</a>. The differences in likelihood ratio between clinical tests can be used to prioritize clinical tests according to their usefulness in a given clinical situation.</li>
<li>AUC-ROC The area under the <a href="/wiki/Receiver_operating_characteristic" title="Receiver operating characteristic">receiver operating characteristic</a> curve (AUC-ROC) reflects the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_and_specificity" title="Sensitivity and specificity">sensitivity and specificity</a> for a given test. High-quality tests will have an AUC-ROC approaching 1, and high-quality publications about clinical tests will provide information about the AUC-ROC. Cutoff values for positive and negative tests can influence specificity and sensitivity, but they do not affect AUC-ROC.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Number_needed_to_treat" title="Number needed to treat">Number needed to treat</a> (NNT)/<a href="/wiki/Number_needed_to_harm" title="Number needed to harm">Number needed to harm</a> (NNH). Number needed to treat or number needed to harm are ways of expressing the effectiveness and safety, respectively, of interventions in a way that is clinically meaningful. NNT is the number of people who need to be treated in order to achieve the desired outcome (e.g. survival from cancer) in one patient. For example, if a treatment increases the chance of survival by 5%, then 20 people need to be treated in order to have 1 additional patient survive due to the treatment. The concept can also be applied to diagnostic tests. For example, if 1339 women age 50–59 have to be invited for breast cancer screening over a ten-year period in order to prevent one woman from dying of breast cancer,<sup id="cite_ref-BandolierStatins2004_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BandolierStatins2004-64">[64]</a></sup> then the NNT for being invited to breast cancer screening is 1339.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_clinical_trials">Quality of clinical trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Quality of clinical trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence-based medicine attempts to objectively evaluate the quality of clinical research by critically assessing techniques reported by researchers in their publications.</p>
<ul>
<li>Trial design considerations. High-quality studies have clearly defined eligibility criteria and have minimal missing data.</li>
<li>Generalizability considerations. Studies may only be applicable to narrowly defined patient populations and may not be generalizable to other clinical contexts.</li>
<li>Follow-up. Sufficient time for defined outcomes to occur can influence the prospective study outcomes and the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_power" title="Statistical power">statistical power</a> of a study to detect differences between a treatment and control arm.</li>
<li>Power. A mathematical calculation can determine if the number of patients is sufficient to detect a difference between treatment arms. A negative study may reflect a lack of benefit, or simply a lack of sufficient quantities of patients to detect a difference.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_and_criticism">Limitations and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Limitations and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although evidence-based medicine is regarded as the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard_(test)" title="Gold standard (test)">gold standard</a> of <a href="/wiki/Conventional_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Conventional medicine">clinical practice</a>, there are a number of limitations and criticisms of its use.<sup id="cite_ref-Timmermans2005_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timmermans2005-65">[65]</a></sup> Two widely cited categorization schemes for the various published critiques of EBM include the three-fold division of Straus and McAlister ("limitations universal to the practice of medicine, limitations unique to evidence-based medicine and misperceptions of evidence-based-medicine")<sup id="cite_ref-Straus-McAlister-2000_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Straus-McAlister-2000-66">[66]</a></sup> and the five-point categorization of Cohen, Stavri and Hersh (EBM is a poor philosophic basis for medicine, defines evidence too narrowly, is not evidence-based, is limited in usefulness when applied to individual patients, or reduces the autonomy of the doctor/patient relationship).<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen-2004_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-2004-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In no particular order, some published objections include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The theoretical ideal of EBM (that every narrow clinical question, of which hundreds of thousands can exist, would be answered by meta-analysis and systematic reviews of multiple RCTs) faces the limitation that research (especially the RCTs themselves) is expensive; thus, in reality, for the foreseeable future, there will always be much more demand for EBM than supply, and the best humanity can do is to triage the application of scarce resources.</li>
<li>Research produced by EBM, such as from <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trials</a> (RCTs), may not be relevant for all treatment situations.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid11489034_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11489034-68">[68]</a></sup> Research tends to focus on specific populations, but individual persons can vary substantially from population norms. Since certain population segments have been historically under-researched (racial minorities and people with co-morbid diseases), evidence from RCTs may not be generalizable to those populations.<sup id="cite_ref-orvyhi_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orvyhi-69">[69]</a></sup> Thus EBM applies to groups of people, but this should not preclude clinicians from using their personal experience in deciding how to treat each patient. One author advises that "the knowledge gained from clinical research does not directly answer the primary clinical question of what is best for the patient at hand" and suggests that evidence-based medicine should not discount the value of clinical experience.<sup id="cite_ref-Tonelli99_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tonelli99-56">[56]</a></sup> Another author stated that "the practice of evidence-based medicine means integrating individual clinical expertise with the best available external clinical evidence from systematic research."<sup id="cite_ref-Sackett96_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sackett96-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>Research can be influenced by biases such as <a href="/wiki/Publication_bias" title="Publication bias">publication bias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a>. For example, studies with conflicts due to industry funding are more likely to favor their product.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>There is a lag between when the RCT is conducted and when its results are published.<sup id="cite_ref-CaseReport_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CaseReport-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>There is a lag between when results are published and when these are properly applied.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypocognition" title="Hypocognition">Hypocognition</a> (the absence of a simple, consolidated mental framework that new information can be placed into) can hinder the application of EBM.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)" class="mw-redirect" title="Value (personal and cultural)">Values</a>: while patient values are considered in the original definition of EBM, the importance of values is not commonly emphasized in EBM training, a potential problem under current study.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_of_evidence_in_clinical_settings">Application of evidence in clinical settings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evidence-based_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Application of evidence in clinical settings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the ongoing challenges with evidence-based medicine is that some healthcare providers do not follow the evidence. This happens partly because the current balance of evidence for and against treatments shifts constantly, and it is impossible to learn about every change.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup> Even when the evidence is unequivocally against a treatment, it usually takes ten years for other treatments to be adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup> In other cases, significant change can require a generation of physicians to retire or die, and be replaced by physicians who were trained with more recent evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another major cause of physicians and other healthcare providers treating patients in ways unsupported by the evidence is that the these healthcare providers are subject to the same cognitive biases as all other humans. They may reject the evidence because they have a vivid memory of a rare but shocking outcome (the <a href="/wiki/Availability_heuristic" title="Availability heuristic">availability heuristic</a>), such as a patient dying after refusing treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup> They may overtreat to "do something" or to address a patient's emotional needs.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup> They may worry about malpractice charges based on a discrepancy between what the patient expects and what the evidence recommends.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup> They may also overtreat or provide ineffective treatments because the treatment feels biologically plausible.<sup id="cite_ref-Epstein_78-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
