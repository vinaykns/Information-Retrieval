<p><br />
<b>Maternal health</b> is the health of women during <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a>, and the postpartum period. It encompasses the <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> dimensions of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-conception_,counseling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pre-conception ,counseling (page does not exist)">preconception</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prenatal_care" title="Prenatal care">prenatal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Postnatal" class="mw-redirect" title="Postnatal">postnatal</a> care in order to ensure a positive and fulfilling experience in most cases and reduce maternal morbidity and mortality in other cases.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_United_Nations_Population_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="The United Nations Population Fund">The United Nations Population Fund</a> (UNFPA) estimated that 289,000 women died of pregnancy or childbirth related causes in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> These causes range from severe bleeding to obstructed labour, all of which have highly effective interventions. As women have gained access to family planning and skilled birth attendance with backup emergency obstetric care, the global maternal mortality ratio has fallen from 380 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births in 1990 to 210 deals per 100,000 live births in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> This has resulted in many countries halving their maternal death rates.</p>
<p>While there has been a decline in world-wide mortality rates much more has to be done. High rates still exist particularly in impoverished communities with over 85% living in Africa and Southern Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> The effect of a mother's death results in vulnerable families, and their <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">infants</a>, if they survive childbirth, are more likely to die before reaching their second birthday.</p>
<p>Four elements are essential to maternal death prevention.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> First, prenatal care. It is recommended that expectant mothers receive at least four antenatal visits to check and monitor the health of mother and foetus. Second, skilled birth attendance with emergency backup such as doctors, nurses and midwives who have the skills to manage normal deliveries and recognize the onset of complications. Third, emergency obstetric care to address the major causes of maternal death which are haemorrhage, sepsis, unsafe abortion, hypertensive disorders and obstructed labour. Lastly, postnatal care which is the six weeks following delivery. During this time bleeding, sepsis and hypertensive disorders can occur and newborns are extremely vulnerable in the immediate aftermath of birth. Therefore, follow-up visits by a health worker is assess the health of both mother and child in the postnatal period is strongly recommended.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determinants">Determinants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Determinants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MDG5.svg" class="image"><img alt="Logo depicting pregnant woman, used in Millennium Development Goal 5, maternal health" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MDG5.svg/220px-MDG5.svg.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MDG5.svg/330px-MDG5.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/MDG5.svg/440px-MDG5.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="149" data-file-height="149" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MDG5.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Logo of <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goal" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennium Development Goal">Millennium Development Goal</a> 5</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to a <a href="/wiki/UNFPA" class="mw-redirect" title="UNFPA">UNFPA</a> report, "A woman's chance of dying or becoming disabled during pregnancy and childbirth is closely connected to her social and economic status, the norms and values of her culture, and the geographic remoteness of her home. Generally speaking, the poorer and more marginalized a woman is, the greater her risk of death. In fact, maternal mortality rates reflect disparities between wealthy and poor countries more than any other measure of health. A woman's lifetime risk of dying as a result of pregnancy or childbirth is 1 in 39 in Sub-Saharan Africa, as compared to 1 in 4,700 in industrialized countries."<sup id="cite_ref-Determinants_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Determinants-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty.2Faccess_to_healthcare">Poverty/access to healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Poverty/access to healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The risk for maternal death (during pregnancy or childbirth) in sub-Saharan Africa is 175 times higher than in developed countries, and risk for pregnancy-related illnesses and negative consequences after birth is even higher. Poverty, maternal health, and outcomes for the child are all interconnected. Neonatal deaths in developing countries account for 98% of worldwide yearly neonatal deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-Filippi_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filippi-4">[4]</a></sup> That being said, poverty is detrimental to the health of both mother and child.</p>
<p>Women living in poverty-stricken areas are more likely to be obese and engage in unhealthy behaviors such as cigarette smoking and <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">drug use</a>, are less likely to engage in or even have access to legitimate prenatal care, and are at a significantly higher risk for adverse outcomes for both the mother and child.<sup id="cite_ref-Timmermans_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timmermans-5">[5]</a></sup> A study conducted in Kenya observed that common maternal health problems in poverty-stricken areas include hemorrhaging, anemia, hypertension, malaria, placenta retention, premature labor, prolonged/complicated labor, and pre-eclampsia.<sup id="cite_ref-Izugbara_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Izugbara-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Generally, adequate <a href="/wiki/Prenatal_care" title="Prenatal care">prenatal care</a> encompasses medical care and educational, social, and nutritional services during pregnancy.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup> Although there are a variety of reasons women choose not to engage in proper prenatal care, 71% of low-income women in a US national study had difficulties getting access to prenatal care when they sought it out.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, immigrants and Hispanic women are at higher risk than white or black women for receiving little to no prenatal care, where level of education is also an indicator (since education and race are correlated). Adolescents are least likely to receive any prenatal care at all. Throughout several studies, women and adolescents ranked inadequate finances and lack of transportation as the most common barriers to receiving proper prenatal care.<sup id="cite_ref-Curry_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Curry-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Income is strongly correlated with quality of prenatal care.<sup id="cite_ref-Curry_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Curry-8">[8]</a></sup> Sometimes, proximity to healthcare facilities and access to transportation have significant effects on whether or not women have access to prenatal care. An analysis conducted on maternal healthcare services in Mali found that women who lived in rural areas, far away from healthcare facilities were less likely to receive prenatal care than those who lived in urban areas. Furthermore, researchers found an even stronger relationship between lack of transportation and prenatal and delivery care.<sup id="cite_ref-Gage_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gage-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition to proximity being a predictor of prenatal care access, Materia and colleagues found similar results for proximity and antenatal care in rural Ethiopa.<sup id="cite_ref-Materia_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Materia-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HIV.2FAIDS">HIV/AIDS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: HIV/AIDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maternal HIV rates vary around the world, ranging from 1% to 40%, with African and Asian countries having the highest rates.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre2-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> can be transmitted to the offspring during the <a href="/wiki/Prenatal_development" title="Prenatal development">prenatal period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a>. If a mother is infected with the HIV/AIDS virus, there is a 25% chance that she will pass on the virus to her offspring if she does not receive proper treatment during pregnancy; on the other hand, if a mother is treated during her pregnancy, there is a 98% chance that her baby will not become infected.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a>, the last decade has seen a large increase in death among young children due to HIV/AIDS contracted from their parents,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> especially in countries where poverty is high and education levels are low.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Although several preventative measures do exist, cost and infrastructure are two central problems that international organizations and health agencies find when trying to implement solutions to the problem of mother-to-child HIV transmission in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre-14">[14]</a></sup> Having HIV/AIDS while pregnant can also cause heightened health risks for the mother. A large concern for HIV-positive pregnant women is the risk of contracting tuberculosis (TB) and/or malaria, in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maternal_weight">Maternal weight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Maternal weight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gestational weight gain should typically fall between 11-20 pounds in order to improve outcomes for both mother and child.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Increased rates of hypertension, diabetes, respiratory complications, and infections are prevalent in cases of maternal obesity and can have detrimental effects on pregnancy outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Obesity is an extremely strong risk factor for gestational diabetes.<sup id="cite_ref-Chu_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chu-17">[17]</a></sup> Research has found that obese mothers who lose weight (at least 10 pounds) in-between pregnancies reduce the risk of gestational diabetes during their next pregnancy, whereas mothers who gain weight actually increase their risk.<sup id="cite_ref-Glazer_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glazer-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oral_health">Oral health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oral health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Maternal oral health</b> has been shown to effect the well-being of both the expectant mother and her unborn <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>.</p>
<p>The 2000 Surgeon's General Report stressed the interdependence of oral health on the overall health and well-being of an individual.<sup id="cite_ref-Surgeon_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surgeon-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oral_health" class="mw-redirect" title="Oral health">Oral health</a> is especially essential during perinatal period and the future development of the child.<sup id="cite_ref-Dental_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dental-20">[20]</a></sup> Proper management of oral health has benefits to both mother and child. Furthermore, lack of understanding or maintenance of good oral health for pregnant women may have adverse effects on them and their children. Hence, it is imperative to educate mothers regarding the significance of oral health. Moreover, collaboration and support among physicians across various fields, especially among family practitioners and obstetricians, is essential in addressing the concerns for maternal oral health.<sup id="cite_ref-National_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2007, the Maternal Oral Health Project was developed to provide routine oral care to low-income pregnant women in Nassau County, NY. Since its inception, the program has treated more than 2,000 pregnant women, many of whom had significant gum and/or tooth problems.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oral health has numerous implications on overall general health and the quality of life of an individual. The <i>Surgeon General's Report</i> lists various systemic diseases and conditions that have oral manifestations.<sup id="cite_ref-Surgeon_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surgeon-19">[19]</a></sup> The oral cavity serves as both a site of and a gateway entry of disease for microbial infections, which can affect general health status. In addition, some studies have demonstrated a relationship between periodontal diseases and diabetes, cardiovascular disease, stroke, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. Furthermore, the report establishes a relationship between oral health and quality of life, including functional, psychosocial, and economic indicators. Poor oral health can affect diet, nutrition, sleep, psychological status, social interaction, school, and work.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_and_effects">Benefits and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Benefits and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/UNFPA" class="mw-redirect" title="UNFPA">UNFPA</a>, maternal deaths would be reduced by about two-thirds, from 287,000 to 105,000, if needs for modern family planning and maternal and newborn health care were met.<sup id="cite_ref-Determinants_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Determinants-3">[3]</a></sup> Therefore, investing in family planning and improved maternal health care brings many benefits including reduced risks of complications and improvement in health for mothers and their children. Education is also critical with research showing "that women with no education were nearly three times more likely to die during pregnancy and childbirth than women who had finished secondary school." <sup id="cite_ref-Determinants_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Determinants-3">[3]</a></sup> Evidence shows that women who are better educated tend to have healthier children. Education would also improve employment opportunities for women which results in improving their status, contributing to family savings, reducing poverty and contributing to economic growth. All of these invests bring significant benefits and effects not only for women and girls but also their children, families, communities and their country.</p>
<p>Protection and control of oral health and diseases safeguards a woman's health and quality of life before and during pregnancy.<sup id="cite_ref-OHRC_Consensus_Statement_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHRC_Consensus_Statement-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, it has the potential to decrease the transmission of pathogenic bacteria that occurs from mother to child.<sup id="cite_ref-Dental_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dental-20">[20]</a></sup> Along with pregnancy, come physiological changes for a woman. The changes, including fluctuating hormones, increase the woman's susceptibility to oral infections such as periodontal disease. This disease impairs the body's ability to repair and maintain soft tissues.<sup id="cite_ref-National_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-21">[21]</a></sup> It also causes indirect damage through bacterial induction of both inflammatory and immune responses of the host.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessa_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessa-24">[24]</a></sup> During pregnancy, mild inflammation of the gums, "pregnancy gingivitis", is quite common and if left untreated can lead to periodontal disease. There have been an increased number of studies establishing associations between, periodontal disease and negative health outcomes, which include tooth loss, cardiovascular disease, stroke, poor diabetes control, and adverse birth outcomes. For example, one such study found that moderate or severe periodontal disease early in pregnancy was associated with delivery of small-for-gestational-age infant.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessb_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessb-25">[25]</a></sup> Other studies have also found an association between periodontal disease and development of pre-eclampsia and pre-term births.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessa_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessa-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another notable oral disease pertinent to maternal child health is dental caries. Dental caries is the process of tooth decay, and the development of what is commonly known as cavities.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessa_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessa-24">[24]</a></sup> Dental carries are transmitted from mother to child vertically; colonization of cariogenic bacteria primarily occurs from mother to child through saliva-sharing activities. Maternal oral flora can ultimately foretell oral flora in offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessc_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessc-26">[26]</a></sup> In addition, other maternal factors such as social, behavioral, and biological factors can predispose a child's experience with tooth-decay.<sup id="cite_ref-Boggessa_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boggessa-24">[24]</a></sup> Some of these factors include the lack of knowledge a mother possesses concerning oral health, which can influence the development of caries among her children. Compared to children whose mothers have good oral health, children whose mothers have bad oral health are five times as likely to have poor oral health.<sup id="cite_ref-National_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-21">[21]</a></sup> Poor maintenance of oral health has profound implications on the development of children. As mentioned in the Surgeon's General Report, oral health affects the quality of life, especially children, with respect to functional, psychological, economic, and overall emotional well-being of an individual.<sup id="cite_ref-Surgeon_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surgeon-19">[19]</a></sup> To demonstrate the adverse effects of poor oral health, take for example the consequences a simple cavity can have on a child. First, it is painful. This might cause a child to miss school or have poor concentration, eventually compromising school performance. In addition, due to the pain, it might result in poor weight gain or growth. Also, children may exhibit reduced self-esteem because of cosmetic issues. Furthermore, it can affect language and impair speech. Impaired speech development can also result in low self-esteem. Finally, cavities although easily preventable, can pose a financial burden of a family. Public dental services are scarce and costly to individuals who lack dental insurance. It may also result in unwarranted visits to emergency department. Poor oral health permeates into other aspects of life, posing threats to overall well-being, if not handled timely and effectively</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaboration_and_education">Collaboration and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collaboration and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The significance of oral health is apparent, however, many women do not receive dental services before, during, and after pregnancy, even with obvious signs of oral disease.<sup id="cite_ref-OHRC_Consensus_Statement_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHRC_Consensus_Statement-23">[23]</a></sup> There are several factors at play regarding pregnant women not seeking dental care, including the role of the health care system and disposition of the woman herself. There is a common misconception that it is not safe to obtain dental services while pregnant. Many prenatal and oral health providers have limited knowledge about the impact and safety of delivering dental services; hence they might delay or withhold treatment during pregnancy.<sup id="cite_ref-Dental_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dental-20">[20]</a></sup> Moreover, some prenatal providers are not aware of the importance of oral health on overall general health, thus failing to refer their patients to dental providers.<sup id="cite_ref-OHRC_Consensus_Statement_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHRC_Consensus_Statement-23">[23]</a></sup> First and foremost, the misconception regarding the impact of dental services while a woman is pregnant needs to be purged. There is a consensus that prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of oral diseases are highly beneficial and can be performed on pregnant women having no added fetal or maternal risk when compared to the risk of providing no oral care.<sup id="cite_ref-OHRC_Consensus_Statement_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHRC_Consensus_Statement-23">[23]</a></sup> Equally important is establishing collaboration among clinicians, especially maternal health providers, with other dental providers. There should be coordination among general health and oral health providers, especially because of the interdependence of the two fields.<sup id="cite_ref-Surgeon_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surgeon-19">[19]</a></sup> Thus, it is imperative to educate and train health providers of the significance of oral health, designing methods to incorporate in their respective practices. Providers most provide education to pregnant women addressing the importance of oral health, because these women ultimately control the fate of themselves and their offspring. For example, providers can illustrate to mothers how to reduce cavities by wiping down the gums of their children with a soft cloth after breastfeeding or bottle-feeding.<sup id="cite_ref-Dental_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dental-20">[20]</a></sup> Bestowing knowledge and practical applications of good oral health maintenance measures to mothers can help improve overall health of the mother and child. There are still other factors in play when analyzing the low use of dental services by pregnant women, particularly prevalent among ethnic and racial minorities. A major factor is the lack of insurance and or access to dental services.<sup id="cite_ref-Surgeon_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surgeon-19">[19]</a></sup> For this reason, more data needs to be collected and analyzed so that programs are set up to effectively to reach all segments of the population.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_child_health_and_development">Effects on child health and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects on child health and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prenatal_health">Prenatal health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Prenatal health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prenatal care is an important part of basic maternal health care. It is recommended expectant mothers receive at least four antenatal visits, in which a health worker can check for signs of ill health – such as underweight, anaemia or infection – and monitor the health of the foetus.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> During these visits, women are counseled on nutrition and hygiene to improve their health prior to, and following, delivery. They can also develop a birth plan laying out how to reach care and what to do in case of an emergency.</p>
<p>Poverty, malnutrition, and substance abuse may contribute to impaired cognitive, motor, and behavioral problems across childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-Hurt_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurt-27">[27]</a></sup> In other words, if a mother is not in optimal health during the prenatal period (the time while she is pregnant) and/or the fetus is exposed to teratogen(s), the child is more likely to experience health or developmental difficulties, or death. The environment in which the mother provides for the embryo/fetus is critical to its wellbeing well after gestation and birth.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Teratology" title="Teratology">teratogen</a> is "any agent that can potentially cause a birth defect or negatively alter cognitive and behavioral outcomes." Dose, genetic susceptibility, and time of exposure are all factors for the extent of the effect of a teratogen on an embryo or fetus.<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prescription drugs taken during pregnancy such as streptomycin, tetracycline, some antidepressants, progestin, synthetic estrogen, and Accutane,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> as well as over-the-counter drugs such as diet pills, can result in teratogenic outcomes for the developing embryo/fetus. Additionally, high dosages of aspirin are known to lead to maternal and fetal bleeding, although low-dose aspirin is usually not harmful.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marret_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marret-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newborns whose mothers use heroin during the gestational period often exhibit withdrawal symptoms at birth and are more likely to have attention problems and health issues as they grow up.<sup id="cite_ref-Blandthorn_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blandthorn-33">[33]</a></sup> Use of stimulants like methamphetamine and cocaine during pregnancy are linked to a number of problems for the child such as <a href="/wiki/Low_birth_weight" title="Low birth weight">low birth weight</a> and small head circumference and motor and cognitive developmental delays, as well as behavioral problems across childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry found that 6 year-olds whose mothers had smoked during pregnancy scored lower on an intelligence test than children whose mothers had not.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldschmidt_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldschmidt-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cigarette smoking during pregnancy can have a multitude of detrimental effects on the health and development of the offspring. Common results of smoking during pregnancy include pre-term births, low birth weights, fetal and neonatal deaths, respiratory problems, and <a href="/wiki/Sudden_infant_death_syndrome" title="Sudden infant death syndrome">sudden infant death syndrome</a> (SIDS),<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup> as well as increased risk for cognitive impairment, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and other behavioral problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Abbott_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbott-39">[39]</a></sup> Also, in a study published in the International Journal of Cancer, children whose mothers smoked during pregnancy experienced a 22% risk increase for non-Hodgkin lymphoma.<sup id="cite_ref-Antonopoulos_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antonopoulos-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although alcohol use in careful moderation (one to two servings a few days a week) during pregnancy are not generally known to cause <a href="/wiki/Fetal_alcohol_spectrum_disorder" title="Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder">fetal alcohol spectrum disorder</a> (FASD), the US Surgeon General advises against the consumption of alcohol at all during pregnancy.<sup id="cite_ref-Cheng_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng-41">[41]</a></sup> Excessive alcohol use during pregnancy can cause FASD, which commonly consist of physical and cognitive abnormalities in the child such as facial deformities, defective limbs, face, and heart, learning problems, below average intelligence, and intellectual disability (ID).<sup id="cite_ref-Painter_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Painter-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although HIV/AIDS can be transmitted to offspring at different times, the most common time that mothers pass on the virus is during pregnancy. During the perinatal period, the embryo/fetus can contract the virus through the placenta.<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gestational_diabetes" title="Gestational diabetes">Gestational diabetes</a> is directly linked with obesity in offspring through adolescence.<sup id="cite_ref-Pettitt_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pettitt-44">[44]</a></sup> Additionally, children whose mothers had diabetes are more likely to develop <a href="/wiki/Type_II_diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Type II diabetes">Type II diabetes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dabelea_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dabelea-45">[45]</a></sup> Mothers who have gestational diabetes have a high chance of giving birth to very large infants (10 pounds or more).<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because the embryo or fetus's nutrition is based on maternal protein, vitamin, mineral, and total caloric intake, infants born to malnourished mothers are more likely to exhibit malformations. Additionally, maternal stress can affect the fetus both directly and indirectly. When a mother is under stress, physiological changes occur in the body that could harm the developing fetus. Additionally, the mother is more likely to engage in behaviors that could negatively affect the fetus, such as tobacco smoking, drug use, and alcohol abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childbirth">Childbirth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Childbirth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herpes_genitalis" class="mw-redirect" title="Herpes genitalis">Genital herpes</a> is passed to the offspring through the birth canal during delivery.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In pregnancies where the mother is infected with the virus, 25% of babies delivered through an infected birth canal become brain damaged, and 1/3 die.<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup> HIV/AIDS can also be transmitted during childbirth through contact with the mother's body fluids.<sup id="cite_ref-Santrock_28-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santrock-28">[28]</a></sup> Mothers in developed countries may often elect to undergo a caesarean section to reduce the risk of transmitting the virus through the birth canal, but this option is not always available in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postpartum_period">Postpartum period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Postpartum period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Globally, more than eight million of the 136 million women giving birth each year suffer from excessive bleeding after childbirth.<sup id="cite_ref-Medicines_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medicines-48">[48]</a></sup> This condition—medically referred to as postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)—causes one out of every four maternal deaths that occur annually and accounts for more maternal deaths than any other individual cause.<sup id="cite_ref-Medicines_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medicines-48">[48]</a></sup> Deaths due to postpartum hemorrhage disproportionately affect women in developing countries.</p>
<p>For every woman who dies from causes related to pregnancy, an estimated 20 to 30 encounter serious complications.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> At least 15 per cent of all births are complicated by a potentially fatal condition.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-2">[2]</a></sup> Women who survive such complications often require lengthy recovery times and may face lasting physical, psychological, social and economic consequences. Although many of these complications are unpredictable, almost all are treatable.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_period" title="Postpartum period">postpartum period</a>, many mothers breastfeed their infants. Transmission of HIV/AIDS through breastfeeding is a huge issue in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, namely in African countries.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre-14">[14]</a></sup> The majority of infants who contract HIV through breast milk do so within the first six weeks of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Hollander_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hollander-49">[49]</a></sup> However, in healthy mothers, there are many benefits for infants who are breastfed. The World Health Organization recommends that mothers breastfeed their children for the first two years of life, whereas the American Academy of Pediatrics and the American Academy of Family Physicians recommend that mothers do so for at least the first six months, and continue as long as is mutually desired.<sup id="cite_ref-Stuebe.26Schwarz_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuebe.26Schwarz-50">[50]</a></sup> Infants who are breastfed by healthy mothers (not infected with HIV/AIDS) are less prone to infections such as <i>Haemophilus influenza, Streptococcus pneunoniae, Vibrio cholerae, Escherichia coli, Giardia lamblia</i>, group B streptococci, <i>Staphylococcus epidermidis</i>, rotavirus, respiratory syncytial virus and herpes simplex virus-1, as well as gastrointestinal and lower respiratory tract infections and otitis media. Lower rates of infant mortality are observed in breastfed babies in addition to lower rates of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Decreases in obesity and diseases such as childhood metabolic disease, asthma, atopic dermatitis, Type I diabetes, and childhood cancers are also seen in children who are breastfed.<sup id="cite_ref-Stuebe.26Schwarz_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuebe.26Schwarz-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_effects_for_the_mother">Long-term effects for the mother</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Long-term effects for the mother">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Maternal_death" title="Maternal death">Maternal death</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
</div>
<p>In many developing countries, <a href="/wiki/Complications_of_pregnancy" title="Complications of pregnancy">complications of pregnancy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a> are the leading causes of <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">death among women</a> of reproductive age. A woman dies from complications from childbirth approximately every minute.<sup id="cite_ref-UNICEF_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNICEF-51">[51]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, in its <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Report" title="World Health Report"><i>World Health Report 2005</i></a>, poor maternal conditions account for the fourth leading cause of death for women worldwide, after HIV/AIDS, malaria, and tuberculosis.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Most maternal deaths and injuries are caused by biological processes, not from disease, which can be prevented and have been largely eradicated in the <a href="/wiki/Developed_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed world">developed world</a> — such as <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_hemorrhage" class="mw-redirect" title="Postpartum hemorrhage">postpartum hemorrhaging</a>, which causes 34% of maternal deaths in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing world">developing world</a> but only 13% of maternal deaths in developed countries.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although high-quality, accessible health care has made maternal death a rare event in developed countries, where only 1% of maternal deaths occur, these complications can often be fatal in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing world">developing world</a> because single most important intervention for safe motherhood is to make sure that a trained provider with midwifery skills is present at every birth, that transport is available to referral services, and that quality emergency obstetric care is available.<sup id="cite_ref-UNICEF_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNICEF-51">[51]</a></sup> In 2008 342,900 women died while pregnant or from childbirth worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Although a high number, this was a significant drop from 1980, when 526,300 women died from the same causes. This improvement was caused by lower pregnancy rates in some countries; higher income, which improves nutrition and access to health care; more education for women; and the increasing availability of <a href="/wiki/Birth_attendant" title="Birth attendant">"skilled birth attendants"</a> — people with training in basic and emergency obstetric care — to help women give birth. The situation was especially led by improvements in large countries like India and China, which helped to drive down the overall death rates. In India, the government started paying for prenatal and delivery care to ensure access, and saw successes in reducing maternal mortality, so much so that India is cited as the major reason for the decreasing global rates of maternal mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT20100413_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20100413-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maternal health problems also include complications from childbirth that do not result in death. For every woman that dies during childbirth, approximately 20 suffer from <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Injury" title="Injury">injury</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Almost 50% of the births in developing countries still take place without a <a href="/wiki/Birth_attendant" title="Birth attendant">medically skilled attendant</a> to aid the mother, and the ratio is even higher in South Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-UNICEF_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNICEF-51">[51]</a></sup> Women in Sub-Saharan Africa mainly rely on <a href="/wiki/Traditional_birth_attendant" title="Traditional birth attendant">traditional birth attendants</a> (TBAs), who have little or no formal health care training. In recognition of their role, some countries and non-governmental organizations are making efforts to train TBAs in maternal health topics, in order to improve the chances for better health outcomes among mothers and babies.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Breastfeeding provides women with several long-term benefits. Women who breastfeed experience better glucose levels, lipid metabolism, and blood pressure, and lose pregnancy weight faster than those who do not. Additionally, women who breastfeed experience lower rates of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, and type 2 diabetes.<sup id="cite_ref-Stuebe.26Schwarz_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuebe.26Schwarz-50">[50]</a></sup> However, it is important to keep in mind that breastfeeding provides substantial benefits to women who are not infected with HIV. In countries where HIV/AIDS rates are high, such as South Africa and Kenya, the virus is a leading cause of maternal mortality, especially in mothers who breastfeed.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre-14">[14]</a></sup> A complication is that many HIV-infected mothers cannot afford formula, and thus have no way of preventing transmission to the child through breast milk or avoiding health risks for themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-Hollander_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hollander-49">[49]</a></sup> In cases like this, mothers have no choice but to breastfeed their infants regardless of their knowledge of the harmful effects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_situation">Global situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Global situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:462px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/460px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" width="460" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/690px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/920px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="820" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Maternal_Mortality_Rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal Mortality Rate">Maternal Mortality Rate</a> worldwide, as defined by the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births from any cause related to or aggravated by pregnancy or its management, excluding accidental or incidental causes.<sup id="cite_ref-CIA2010_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIA2010-58">[58]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Joint_Commission_on_Accreditation_of_Healthcare_Organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organizations">Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organizations</a> calls <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">maternal mortality</a> a "<a href="/wiki/Sentinel_event" title="Sentinel event">sentinel event</a>", and uses it to assess the quality of a health care system.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJoint_Commission2010_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoint_Commission2010-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maternal mortality data is said to be an important indicator of overall health system quality because pregnant women survive in sanitary, safe, well-staffed and stocked facilities. If new mothers are thriving, it indicates that the health care system is doing its job. If not, problems likely exist.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Garret, increasing maternal survival, along with life expectancy, is an important goal for the world health community, as they show that other health issues are also improving. If these areas improve, disease-specific improvements are also better able to positively impact populations.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Worldwide, the <a href="/wiki/Maternal_death" title="Maternal death">Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)</a> has decreased, with South-East Asia seeing the most dramatic decrease of 59% and Africa seeing a decline of 27%. There are no regions that are on track to meet the Millennium Development Goal of decreasing maternal mortality by 75% by the year 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUN2015_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUN2015-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_solutions">Proposed solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maternal_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Proposed solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> estimated that a total of 3.00 US dollars per person a year can provide basic family planning, maternal and neonatal health care to women in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Many non-profit organizations have programs educating the public and gaining access to emergency obstetric care for mothers in developing countries. The United Nations Population Fund (UNPFA) recently began its Campaign on Accelerated Reduction of Maternal Mortality in Africa (CARMMA), focusing on providing quality healthcare to mothers. One of the programs within CARMMA is Sierra Leone providing free healthcare to mothers and children. This initiative has widespread support from African leaders and was started in conjunction with the African Union Health Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Improving maternal health is the fifth of the United Nations' eight <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs), targeting a reduction in the number of women dying during pregnancy and childbirth by three quarters by 2015, notably by increasing the usage of skilled birth attendants, contraception and family planning.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERosenfield_et_al2006_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosenfield_et_al2006-67">[67]</a></sup> The current decline of maternal deaths is only half of what is necessary to achieve this goal, and in several regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa the maternal mortality rate is actually increasing. However, one country that may meet their MDG 5 is Nepal, which has it appears reduced its maternal mortality by more than 50% since the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> As the 2015 deadline for the MDG's approaches, an understanding of the policy developments leading to the inclusion of maternal health within the MDG's is essential for future advocacy efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Decreasing the rates of maternal mortality and morbidity in developing countries is important because poor maternal health is both an indicator and a cause of extreme poverty. According to Tamar Manuelyan Atinc, Vice President for Human Development at the World Bank:<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Maternal deaths are both caused by poverty and are a cause of it. The costs of childbirth can quickly exhaust a family's income, bringing with it even more financial hardship."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Developed countries had rates of maternal mortality similar to those of developing countries until the early 20th century, therefore several lessons can be learned from the west. During the 19th century Sweden had high levels of maternal mortality, and there was a strong support within the country to reduce mortality rate to fewer than 300 per 100,000 live births. The Swedish government began public health initiatives to train enough midwives to attend all births. This approach was also later used by Norway, Denmark, and the Netherlands who also experienced similar successes.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Increasing contraceptive usage and family planning also improves maternal health through reduction in numbers of higher risk pregnancies. In Nepal a strong emphasis was placed on providing family planning to rural regions and it was shown to be effective.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Madagascar saw a dramatic increase in contraceptive use after instituting a nationwide family planning program, the rate of contraceptive use increased from 5.1% in 1992 to 29% in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
