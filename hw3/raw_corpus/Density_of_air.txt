			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Density of air</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>density of air</b> (air density) is the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> per unit <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>. Air density, like air pressure, decreases with increasing altitude. It also changes with variation in temperature or <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> and at 15&#160;Â°C air has a density of approximately 1.225 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram_per_cubic_metre" title="Kilogram per cubic metre">kg/m<sup>3</sup></a> (0.001225 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, 0.0023769 <a href="/wiki/Slug_(mass)" title="Slug (mass)">slug</a>/(cu ft), 0.0765 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a>/(cu ft)) according to ISA (<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a>).</p>
<p>The air density is a property used in many branches of science as <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-aero_01en_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_01en-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aero_02en_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_02en-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aero_03en_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_03en-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gravimetric_analysis" title="Gravimetric analysis">gravimetric analysis</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-grav_01en_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grav_01en-4">[4]</a></sup> the air-conditioning<sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup> industry; atmospheric research and meteorology;<sup id="cite_ref-mete_01en_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_01en-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mete_02en_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_02en-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mete_03en_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_03en-8">[8]</a></sup> the agricultural engineering in their modeling and tracking of Soil-Vegetation-Atmosphere-Transfer (SVAT) models;<sup id="cite_ref-agri_01en_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_01en-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agri_02en_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_02en-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agri_03en_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_03en-11">[11]</a></sup> and the engineering community that deals with compressed air<sup id="cite_ref-eng_01en_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eng_01en-12">[12]</a></sup> from industry utility, heating, drying and cooling processes<sup id="cite_ref-eng_01en_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eng_01en-12">[12]</a></sup> in industries like cooling towers, vacuum and deep vacuum processes,<sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup> high pressure processes,<sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup> gas and light oil combustion processes<sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eng_01en_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eng_01en-12">[12]</a></sup> that power turbine-powered airplanes, gas turbine-powered generators and heating furnaces, and air conditioning<sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup> from deep mines to space capsules.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Density_of_air_calculations">Density of air calculations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Density_of_air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Density of air calculations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on the measuring instruments, use, area of expertise and necessary rigor of the result different calculation criteria and sets of equations for the calculation of the density of air are used. This topic are some examples of calculations with the main variables involved, the amounts presented throughout these examples are properly referenced usual values, different values can be found in other references depending on the criteria used for the calculation . Furthermore we must pay attention to the fact that air is a mixture of gases and the calculation always simplify, to a greater or lesser extent, the properties of the mixture and the values for the composition according to the criteria of calculation.<sup id="cite_ref-aero_01en_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_01en-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aero_02en_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_02en-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aero_03en_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero_03en-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-grav_01en_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grav_01en-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hvac_01en_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvac_01en-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mete_01en_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_01en-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mete_02en_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_02en-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mete_03en_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mete_03en-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agri_01en_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_01en-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agri_02en_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_02en-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agri_03en_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agri_03en-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eng_01en_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eng_01en-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity_.28water_vapor.29">Humidity (water vapor)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Density_of_air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Humidity (water vapor)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">Humidity</a></div>
<p>The addition of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> to air (making the air humid) reduces the density of the air, which may at first appear counter-intuitive. This occurs because the molar mass of water (18 g/mol) is less than the molar mass of dry air<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[note 2]</a></sup> (around 29 g/mol). For any gas, at a given temperature and pressure, the number of molecules present is constant for a particular volume (see <a href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Avogadro's Law">Avogadro's Law</a>). So when water molecules (water vapor) are added to a given volume of air, the dry air molecules must decrease by the same number, to keep the pressure or temperature from increasing. Hence the mass per unit volume of the gas (its density) decreases.</p>
<p>The density of humid air may be calculated as a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gases</a>. In this case, the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a>.
