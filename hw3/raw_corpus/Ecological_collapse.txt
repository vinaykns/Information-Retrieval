			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecological collapse</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Ecological collapse</b> refers to a situation where an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> suffers a drastic, possibly permanent, reduction in <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> for all organisms, often resulting in <a href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass extinction">mass extinction</a>. Usually, an ecological collapse is precipitated by a disastrous event occurring on a short time scale.</p>
<p>Ecosystems have the ability to rebound from a disruptive agent. The difference between collapse or a gentle rebound is determined by two factors —- the toxicity of the introduced element and the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_resilience" title="Ecological resilience">resiliency of the original ecosystem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Basic_Civil_Engineering_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Civil_Engineering-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through natural selection the planet's species have continuously adapted to change through variation in their biological composition and distribution. Mathematically it can be demonstrated that greater numbers of different biological factors tend to dampen fluctuations in each of the individual factors.<sup id="cite_ref-Basic_Civil_Engineering_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basic_Civil_Engineering-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists can predict tipping points for ecological collapse. The most frequently used model for predicting <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food web</a> collapse is called R50, which is a reliable measurement model for food web robustness.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_examples">Causes and examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes and examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although, there is no single cause for ecological collapse, attributing factors include <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Asteroid impact">asteroid impacts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">extremely large volcanic eruptions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a>. The snowball effect of these attributing factors and ecological collapse are demonstrated within the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>. <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">Prehistoric</a> examples include the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Rainforest_Collapse" class="mw-redirect" title="Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse">Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass extinctions">mass extinctions</a>. For example, effects of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> as a contributing factor towards ecological collapse are demonstrated in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_events" title="Ordovician–Silurian extinction events">Ordovician–Silurian extinction events</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A possible cause of the Ordovician Extinction was global cooling which affected the habitats of marine life. Consequently, sea creatures such as trilobites, brachiopods, and graptolites became extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, Karabonov and colleagues conducted a study to show how during the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> (LGM), alternations in the environment and climate led to ecological collapse in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Hovsgol" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Hovsgol">Lake Hovsgol</a> which then led to species evolution in these systems.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The collapse of Hovsgol's ecosystem during the LGM brought forth a new ecosystem, with limited biodiversity in species and low levels of endemism, in Hovsgol during the Holocene. Karabonov's study also shows that ecological collapse during LGM in Lake Hovsgol led to higher levels of diversity and higher levels of endemism as a byproduct of evolution following the ecological collapse of the LGM<sup>[5]</sup>. The Ordovician Extinction event and Lake Baikal and Hovsgol demonstrate two effects of ecological collapse on prehistoric environments.</p>
<p>Historic examples include the <a href="/wiki/Collapse_of_the_Atlantic_northwest_cod_fishery" title="Collapse of the Atlantic northwest cod fishery">collapse of the Grand Banks cod</a> in the early 1990s, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>.</p>
<p>Important pressures contributing to current and future ecological collapse include <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat loss">habitat loss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_degradation" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat degradation">degradation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">fragmentation</a>, overgrazing, <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of ecosystems by humans, human industrial growth and overpopulation,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainforest_collapse">Rainforest collapse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rainforest collapse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainforest collapse refers to the actual past and theoretical future ecological collapse of <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>. It may involve <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a> to the point where little rainforest <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a> is left, and rainforest species only survive in isolated refugia. Habitat fragmentation can be caused by roads. When humans start to cut down the trees for logging, secondary roads are created that will go unused after its primary use. Once abandoned, the plants of the rainforest will find it difficult to grow back in that area.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Forest fragmentation also opens the path for illegal hunting. Species have a hard time finding a new place to settle in these fragments causing ecological collapse. This leads to extinction of many animals in the rainforest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carboniferous_period">Carboniferous period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carboniferous period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_rainforest_collapse" title="Carboniferous rainforest collapse">Carboniferous rainforest collapse</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period, <a href="/wiki/Coal_forest" title="Coal forest">coal forests</a>, great <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wetland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tropical wetland (page does not exist)">tropical wetlands</a>, extended over much of Euramerica (Europe and America). This land supported towering <a href="/wiki/Lycopsid" class="mw-redirect" title="Lycopsid">lycopsids</a> which fragmented and collapsed abruptly.<sup id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse-9">[9]</a></sup> The collapse of the rainforests during the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> has been attributed to <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous_rainforest_collapse#Possible_causes" title="Carboniferous rainforest collapse">multiple causes</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Specifically, at this time climate became cooler and drier, conditions that are not favourable to the growth of rainforests and much of the biodiversity within them. This sudden collapse affected several large groups including lycopsids and amphibians. Reptiles prospered in the new environment due to adaptations that let them thrive in drier conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A classic pattern of <a href="/wiki/Forest_fragmentation" class="mw-redirect" title="Forest fragmentation">forest fragmentation</a> is occurring in many <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a> including those of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon</a>, specifically a 'fishbone' pattern formed by the development of roads into the forest. This is of great concern, not only because of the loss of a <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a> with many untapped resources and wholesale death of living organisms, but also because plant and animal species extinction is known to correlate with habitat fragmentation.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenzweig_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenzweig-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">Overgrazing</a> was found to cause land degradation, specifically in Southern Europe, which is another driver of ecological collapse and natural landscape loss. Proper management of pastoral landscapes can mitigate risk of desertification.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010 roughly 170 million gallons of oil was dumped into the Gulf of Mexico when BP's Deepwater Horizon oil rig exploded. The effects of the BP oil spill are still being felt today, more than 8,000 marine birds, sea turtles and marine mammals were found dead or injured within months of the clean up effort. The impact of this disaster has unbalanced the food web of the environment. The oil spill occurred at the height of breeding season and as result affected egg and larval animals to the worst extent wiping entire age classes. This loss of a generation down the line will prove dire for future predators of the ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, a major concern for marine biologists is the effects of ecological collapse on the coral reefs (who based on fossil evidence are more vulnerable to extinction but also demonstrate greater resilience<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>). An effect of global climate change is the rising sea levels which can lead to reef drowing or <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> Human activity, such as fishing, mining, deforestation, etc., serves as a threat for coral reefs by affecting the niche of the coral reefs. For example, Edinger and colleagues<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> demonstrate a correlation between a loss in diversity of coral reefs by 30-60% and human activity such as sewage and/or industrial pollution.</p>
<p>The ocean is in great danger of collapse. In a study of 154 different marine fish species, David Byler found out that many factors such as overfishing, climate change, and fast growth of fish populations will cause ecosystem collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> When humans fish, they usually will fish the populations of the higher trophic levels such as salmon and tuna. The depletion of these trophic levels allow the lower trophic level to overpopulate, or populate very rapidly. For example, when the population of catfish is depleting due to overfishing, plankton will then overpopulate because their natural predator is being killed off. This causes an issue called eutrophication. Since the population all consumes oxygen the dissolved oxygen(DO) levels will plummet. The DO levels dropping will cause all the species in that area to have to leave, or they will suffocate. This along with climate change, and ocean acidification can cause the collapse of an ecosystem.</p>
<p>Although global climate change and human dominance are inevitable, humans can institute greater self-awareness in order to aid the habits of creatures such as coral reefs.</p>
<p>Some scientists predict that a global ecological collapse will occur after 50% of the natural landscape is gone due to human development.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the causes of ecological collapse are due to factors unique to their environment, they all for the most part share similar ramifications such as loss in biodiversity, trophic cascades, and even extinction. For example, the urbanization and deforestation of the South east Asian Pacific has led to the extinction of three plant species and eight animal species in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
