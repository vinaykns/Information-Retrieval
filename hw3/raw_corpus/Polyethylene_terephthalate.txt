<p><b>Polyethylene terephthalate</b> (sometimes written poly(ethylene terephthalate)), commonly abbreviated <b>PET</b>, <b>PETE</b>, or the obsolete PETP or PET-P, is the most common <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> resin of the <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> family and is used in <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">fibers</a> for clothing, <a href="/wiki/Packaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Packaging">containers</a> for liquids and foods, <a href="/wiki/Thermoforming" title="Thermoforming">thermoforming</a> for manufacturing, and in combination with glass fiber for engineering <a href="/wiki/Resins" class="mw-redirect" title="Resins">resins</a>.</p>
<p>It may also be referred to by the brand name <b>Dacron</b>; in Britain, <b>Terylene</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or, in Russia and the former Soviet Union, <b>Lavsan</b>.</p>
<p>The majority of the world's PET production is for synthetic fibers (in excess of 60%), with bottle production accounting for about 30% of global demand.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the context of textile applications, PET is referred to by its common name, <i><a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a></i>, whereas the acronym <i>PET</i> is generally used in relation to packaging. Polyester makes up about 18% of world polymer production and is the fourth-most-produced <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> (PE), <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> (PP) and <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> (PVC) are first, second and third, respectively.</p>
<p>PET consists of <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymerized</a> units of the monomer ethylene terephthalate, with repeating (C<sub>10</sub>H<sub>8</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) units. PET is commonly <a href="/wiki/PET_bottle_recycling" title="PET bottle recycling">recycled</a>, and has the number "1" as its <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">recycling symbol</a>.</p>
<p>Depending on its processing and thermal history, polyethylene terephthalate may exist both as an amorphous (transparent) and as a <a href="/wiki/Semi-crystalline_polymer" class="mw-redirect" title="Semi-crystalline polymer">semi-crystalline polymer</a>. The semicrystalline material might appear transparent (particle size less than 500&#160;<a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>) or opaque and white (particle size up to a few <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometers</a>) depending on its crystal structure and particle size.</p>
<p>The monomer <a href="/wiki/Bis(2-hydroxyethyl)_terephthalate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate">bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate</a> can be synthesized by the <a href="/wiki/Esterification" class="mw-redirect" title="Esterification">esterification</a> reaction between <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> with water as a byproduct, or by <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> reaction between <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> as a byproduct. Polymerization is through a <a href="/wiki/Polycondensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Polycondensation">polycondensation</a> reaction of the monomers (done immediately after esterification/transesterification) with water as the byproduct.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because PET is an excellent water and moisture barrier material, plastic bottles made from PET are widely used for <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>). For certain specialty bottles, such as those designated for beer containment, PET sandwiches an additional <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_alcohol" title="Polyvinyl alcohol">polyvinyl alcohol</a> (PVOH) layer to further reduce its oxygen permeability.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BoPET" title="BoPET">Biaxially oriented PET</a> film (often known by one of its trade names, "Mylar") can be aluminized by <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporating</a> a <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_deposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Thin-film deposition">thin film</a> of metal onto it to reduce its permeability, and to make it reflective and opaque (<a href="/wiki/Metallized_polyethylene_terephthalate" class="mw-redirect" title="Metallized polyethylene terephthalate">MPET</a>). These properties are useful in many applications, including flexible food <a href="/wiki/Packaging_and_labeling" title="Packaging and labeling">packaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">thermal insulation</a>. See: "<a href="/wiki/Space_blanket" title="Space blanket">space blankets</a>". Because of its high mechanical strength, PET film is often used in tape applications, such as the carrier for <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a> or backing for <a href="/wiki/Pressure-sensitive_tape" title="Pressure-sensitive tape">pressure-sensitive adhesive tapes</a>.</p>
<p>Non-oriented PET sheet can be <a href="/wiki/Thermoforming" title="Thermoforming">thermoformed</a> to make packaging trays and <a href="/wiki/Blister_packs" class="mw-redirect" title="Blister packs">blister packs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> If crystallizable PET is used, the trays can be used for <a href="/wiki/TV_dinner" title="TV dinner">frozen dinners</a>, since they withstand both freezing and oven baking temperatures. As opposed to amorphous PET, which is transparent, crystallizable PET or CPET tends to be black in colour.</p>
<p>When filled with glass <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_(composite)" title="Aggregate (composite)">particles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fiber-reinforced_plastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber-reinforced plastic">fibres</a>, it becomes significantly stiffer and more durable.</p>
<p>PET is also used as a substrate in thin film solar cells.</p>
<p>Terylene (a trademark formed by inversion of (polyeth)ylene ter(ephthalate)) is also spliced into bell rope tops to help prevent wear on the ropes as they pass through the ceiling.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PET was patented in 1941 by <a href="/wiki/John_Rex_Whinfield" title="John Rex Whinfield">John Rex Whinfield</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Tennant_Dickson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James Tennant Dickson (page does not exist)">James Tennant Dickson</a> and their employer the <a href="/wiki/Calico_Printers%27_Association" title="Calico Printers' Association">Calico Printers' Association</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England. <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">E. I. DuPont de Nemours</a> in Delaware, USA, first used the trademark Mylar in June 1951 and received registration of it in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is still the best-known name used for polyester film. The current owner of the trademark is DuPont Teijin Films US, a partnership with a Japanese company.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Soviet Union, PET was first manufactured in the laboratories of the Institute of High-Molecular Compounds of the <a href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">USSR Academy of Sciences</a> in 1949, and its name "Lavsan" is an acronym thereof (<b>ла</b>боратории Института <b>в</b>ысокомолекулярных <b>с</b>оединений <b>А</b>кадемии <b>н</b>аук СССР).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The PET bottle was patented in 1973 by <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Wyeth_(inventor)" title="Nathaniel Wyeth (inventor)">Nathaniel Wyeth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lettenmaier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Lettenmaier.jpg/220px-Lettenmaier.jpg" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Lettenmaier.jpg/330px-Lettenmaier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Lettenmaier.jpg/440px-Lettenmaier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="1260" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lettenmaier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sailcloth" title="Sailcloth">Sailcloth</a> is typically made from PET fibers also known as polyester or under the brand name Dacron; colorful lightweight <a href="/wiki/Spinnaker" title="Spinnaker">spinnakers</a> are usually made of <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>PET in its natural state is a colorless, semi-crystalline resin. Based on how it is processed, PET can be semi-rigid to rigid, and it is very lightweight. It makes a good gas and fair moisture barrier, as well as a good barrier to alcohol (requires additional "barrier" treatment) and solvents. It is strong and <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">impact-resistant</a>. PET becomes white when exposed to chloroform and also certain other chemicals such as toluene.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>About 60% crystallization is the upper limit for commercial products, with the exception of polyester fibers. Clear products can be produced by rapidly cooling molten polymer below T<sub>g</sub> <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass transition temperature">glass transition temperature</a> to form an <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous solid</a>. Like glass, amorphous PET forms when its molecules are not given enough time to arrange themselves in an orderly, crystalline fashion as the melt is cooled. At room temperature the molecules are frozen in place, but, if enough heat energy is put back into them by heating above T<sub>g</sub>, they begin to move again, allowing crystals to <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleate</a> and grow. This procedure is known as solid-state crystallization.</p>
<p>When allowed to cool slowly, the molten polymer forms a more crystalline material. This material has <a href="/wiki/Spherulite" title="Spherulite">spherulites</a> containing many small <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">crystallites</a> when crystallized from an amorphous solid, rather than forming one large single crystal. Light tends to scatter as it crosses the boundaries between crystallites and the amorphous regions between them. This scattering means that crystalline PET is opaque and white in most cases. <a href="/wiki/Cold_drawing" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold drawing">Fiber drawing</a> is among the few industrial processes that produce a nearly single-crystal product.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intrinsic_viscosity">Intrinsic viscosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intrinsic viscosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most important characteristics of PET is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_viscosity" title="Intrinsic viscosity">intrinsic viscosity</a> (IV).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The intrinsic viscosity of the material, found by extrapolating to zero concentration of relative viscosity to concentration which is measured in <a href="/wiki/Deciliter" class="mw-redirect" title="Deciliter">deciliters</a> per gram (dℓ/g). Intrinsic viscosity is dependent upon the length of its polymer chains but has no units due to being extrapolated to zero concentration. The longer the polymer chains the more entanglements between chains and therefore the higher the viscosity. The average chain length of a particular batch of resin can be controlled during <a href="/wiki/Polycondensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Polycondensation">polycondensation</a>.</p>
<p>The intrinsic viscosity range of PET:<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fiber grade</p>
<dl>
<dd>0.40–0.70 Textile</dd>
<dd>0.72–0.98 Technical, <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a> cord</dd>
</dl>
<p>Film grade</p>
<dl>
<dd>0.60–0.70 <a href="/wiki/BoPET" title="BoPET">BoPET</a> (biaxially oriented PET film)</dd>
<dd>0.70–1.00 <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sheet" class="extiw" title="wikt:sheet">Sheet</a> grade for <a href="/wiki/Thermoforming" title="Thermoforming">thermoforming</a></dd>
</dl>
<p>Bottle grade</p>
<dl>
<dd>0.70–0.78 Water bottles (flat)</dd>
<dd>0.78–0.85 Carbonated soft drink grade</dd>
</dl>
<p>Monofilament, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_plastic" title="Engineering plastic">engineering plastic</a></p>
<dl>
<dd>1.00–2.00</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drying">Drying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PET is <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a>, meaning that it absorbs water from its surroundings. However, when this "damp" PET is then heated, the water <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzes</a> the PET, decreasing its resilience. Thus, before the resin can be processed in a molding machine, it must be dried. Drying is achieved through the use of a <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a> or dryers before the PET is fed into the processing equipment.</p>
<p>Inside the dryer, hot dry air is pumped into the bottom of the hopper containing the resin so that it flows up through the pellets, removing moisture on its way. The hot wet air leaves the top of the hopper and is first run through an after-cooler, because it is easier to remove moisture from cold air than hot air. The resulting cool wet air is then passed through a desiccant bed. Finally, the cool dry air leaving the desiccant bed is re-heated in a process heater and sent back through the same processes in a closed loop. Typically, residual moisture levels in the resin must be less than 50 parts per million (parts of water per million parts of resin, by weight) before processing. Dryer residence time should not be shorter than about four hours. This is because drying the material in less than 4 hours would require a temperature above 160&#160;°C, at which level <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> would begin inside the pellets before they could be dried out.</p>
<p>PET can also be dried in compressed air resin dryers. Compressed air dryers do not reuse drying air. Dry, heated compressed air is circulated through the PET pellets as in the desiccant dryer, then released to the atmosphere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copolymers">Copolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Copolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to pure (<a href="/wiki/Homopolymer" class="mw-redirect" title="Homopolymer">homopolymer</a>) PET, PET modified by <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymerization</a> is also available.</p>
<p>In some cases, the modified properties of copolymer are more desirable for a particular application. For example, <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane_dimethanol" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclohexane dimethanol">cyclohexane dimethanol</a> (CHDM) can be added to the polymer backbone in place of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>. Since this building block is much larger (6 additional carbon atoms) than the ethylene glycol unit it replaces, it does not fit in with the neighboring chains the way an ethylene glycol unit would. This interferes with crystallization and lowers the polymer's melting temperature. In general, such PET is known as PETG or PET-G (Polyethylene terephthalate glycol-modified; Eastman Chemical, SK Chemicals, and Artenius Italia are some PETG manufacturers). PETG is a clear amorphous thermoplastic that can be injection molded or sheet extruded. It can be colored during processing.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG/220px-Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG" width="220" height="89" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG/330px-Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG/440px-Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG 2x" data-file-width="446" data-file-height="180" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Phthalic_acid_isomers.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Replacing terephthalic acid (right) with isophthalic acid (center) creates a kink in the PET chain, interfering with <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a> and lowering the polymer's <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Another common modifier is <a href="/wiki/Isophthalic_acid" title="Isophthalic acid">isophthalic acid</a>, replacing some of the 1,4-(<i>para-</i>) linked <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalate</a> units. The 1,2-(<i>ortho-</i>) or 1,3-(<i><a href="/wiki/Arene_substitution_patterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Arene substitution patterns">meta</a></i>-) linkage produces an angle in the chain, which also disturbs crystallinity.</p>
<p>Such copolymers are advantageous for certain molding applications, such as <a href="/wiki/Thermoforming" title="Thermoforming">thermoforming</a>, which is used for example to make tray or blister packaging from co-PET film, or amorphous PET sheet (A-PET) or PETG sheet. On the other hand, crystallization is important in other applications where mechanical and dimensional stability are important, such as seat belts. For PET bottles, the use of small amounts of isophthalic acid, CHDM, <a href="/wiki/Diethylene_glycol" title="Diethylene glycol">diethylene glycol</a> (DEG) or other comonomers can be useful: if only small amounts of comonomers are used, crystallization is slowed but not prevented entirely. As a result, bottles are obtainable via <a href="/wiki/Stretch_blow_molding" class="mw-redirect" title="Stretch blow molding">stretch blow molding</a> ("SBM"), which are both clear and crystalline enough to be an adequate barrier to aromas and even gases, such as carbon dioxide in carbonated beverages.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polyethylene terephthalate is produced from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-polyesters_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polyesters-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The former is a <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> reaction, whereas the latter is an <a href="/wiki/Esterification" class="mw-redirect" title="Esterification">esterification</a> reaction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimethyl_terephthalate_process">Dimethyl terephthalate process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dimethyl terephthalate process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> process, this compound and excess ethylene glycol are reacted in the melt at 150–200&#160;°C with a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic catalyst</a>. <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>OH) is removed by distillation to drive the reaction forward. Excess ethylene glycol is distilled off at higher temperature with the aid of vacuum. The second transesterification step proceeds at 270–280&#160;°C, with continuous distillation of ethylene glycol as well.<sup id="cite_ref-polyesters_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polyesters-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation">Degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PET is subjected to various types of degradations during processing. The main degradations that can occur are hydrolytic, and probably most important, thermal oxidation. When PET degrades, several things happen: discoloration, chain <a href="/wiki/Bond_cleavage" title="Bond cleavage">scissions</a> resulting in reduced molecular weight, formation of <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">cross-links</a> ("gel" or "fish-eye" formation). Discoloration is due to the formation of various chromophoric systems following prolonged thermal treatment at elevated temperatures. This becomes a problem when the optical requirements of the polymer are very high, such as in packaging applications. The thermal and thermooxidative degradation results in poor processibility characteristics and performance of the material.</p>
<p>One way to alleviate this is to use a <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymer</a>. Comonomers such as CHDM or <a href="/wiki/Isophthalic_acid" title="Isophthalic acid">isophthalic acid</a> lower the melting temperature and reduce the degree of crystallinity of PET (especially important when the material is used for bottle manufacturing). Thus, the resin can be plastically formed at lower temperatures and/or with lower force. This helps to prevent degradation, reducing the acetaldehyde content of the finished product to an acceptable (that is, unnoticeable) level. See <a href="#Copolymers">copolymers</a>, above. Another way to improve the stability of the polymer is to use stabilizers, mainly antioxidants such as <a href="/wiki/Phosphites" class="mw-redirect" title="Phosphites">phosphites</a>. Recently, molecular level stabilization of the material using nanostructured chemicals has also been considered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acetaldehyde">Acetaldehyde</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Acetaldehyde">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">Acetaldehyde</a> is a colorless, volatile substance with a fruity smell. Although it forms naturally in some fruit, it can cause an off-taste in bottled water. Acetaldehyde forms by degradation of PET through the mishandling of the material. High temperatures (PET decomposes above 300&#160;°C or 570&#160;°F), high pressures, extruder speeds (excessive shear flow raises temperature), and long barrel residence times all contribute to the production of acetaldehyde. When acetaldehyde is produced, some of it remains dissolved in the walls of a container and then <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuses</a> into the product stored inside, altering the taste and aroma. This is not such a problem for non-consumables (such as shampoo), for fruit juices (which already contain acetaldehyde), or for strong-tasting drinks like soft drinks. For bottled water, however, low acetaldehyde content is quite important, because, if nothing masks the aroma, even extremely low concentrations (10–20 parts per billion in the water) of acetaldehyde can produce an off-taste.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antimony">Antimony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Antimony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">Antimony</a> (Sb) is a <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloid</a> element that is used as a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in the form of compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Antimony_trioxide" title="Antimony trioxide">antimony trioxide</a> (Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) or antimony triacetate in the production of PET. After manufacturing, a detectable amount of antimony can be found on the surface of the product. This residue can be removed with washing. Antimony also remains in the material itself and can, thus, migrate out into food and drinks. Exposing PET to boiling or microwaving can increase the levels of antimony significantly, possibly above USEPA maximum contamination levels.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The drinking water limit assessed by WHO is 20 parts per billion (WHO, 2003), and the drinking water limit in the USA is 6 parts per billion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although antimony trioxide is of low toxicity when taken orally,<sup id="cite_ref-who.int_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-17">[17]</a></sup> its presence is still of concern. The Swiss <a href="/wiki/Federal_Office_of_Public_Health" title="Federal Office of Public Health">Federal Office of Public Health</a> investigated the amount of antimony migration, comparing waters bottled in PET and glass: The antimony concentrations of the water in PET bottles were higher, but still well below the allowed maximum concentration. The Swiss Federal Office of Public Health concluded that small amounts of antimony migrate from the PET into bottled water, but that the health risk of the resulting low concentrations is negligible (1% of the "<a href="/wiki/Tolerable_daily_intake" title="Tolerable daily intake">tolerable daily intake</a>" determined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a>). A later (2006) but more widely publicized study found similar amounts of antimony in water in PET bottles.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The WHO has published a risk assessment for antimony in drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-who.int_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fruit juice concentrates (for which no guidelines are established), however, that were produced and bottled in PET in the UK were found to contain up to 44.7&#160;µg/L of antimony, well above the EU limits for <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">tap water</a> of 5&#160;µg/L.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradation">Biodegradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Biodegradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least one species of bacterium in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nocardia" title="Nocardia">Nocardia</a></i> can degrade PET with an <a href="/wiki/Esterase" title="Esterase">esterase</a> enzyme.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Japanese scientists have isolated a bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Ideonella_sakaiensis" title="Ideonella sakaiensis">Ideonella sakaiensis</a></i> that possesses two enzymes which can break down the PET into smaller pieces that the bacterium can digest. A colony of <i>I. sakaiensis</i> can disintegrate a plastic film in about six weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commentary published in <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Health_Perspectives" title="Environmental Health Perspectives">Environmental Health Perspectives</a></i> in April 2010 suggested that PET might yield <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptors</a> under conditions of common use and recommended research on this topic.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Proposed mechanisms include leaching of <a href="/wiki/Phthalates" class="mw-redirect" title="Phthalates">phthalates</a> as well as leaching of <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>. Article published in <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Environmental_Monitoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of Environmental Monitoring">Journal of Environmental Monitoring</a></i> in April 2012 concludes that antimony concentration in <a href="/wiki/Deionized_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Deionized water">deionized water</a> stored in PET bottles stays within EU's acceptable limit even if stored briefly at temperatures up to 60&#160;°C (140&#160;°F), while bottled contents (water or soft drinks) may occasionally exceed the EU limit after less than a year of storage at room temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bottle_processing_equipment">Bottle processing equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bottle processing equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are two basic molding methods for PET bottles, one-step and two-step. In two-step molding, two separate machines are used. The first machine injection molds the preform, which resembles a test tube, with the bottle-cap threads already molded into place. The body of the tube is significantly thicker, as it will be inflated into its final shape in the second step using <a href="/wiki/Stretch_blow_molding" class="mw-redirect" title="Stretch blow molding">stretch blow molding</a>.</p>
<p>In the second step, the preforms are heated rapidly and then inflated against a two-part mold to form them into the final shape of the bottle. Preforms (uninflated bottles) are now also used as robust and unique containers themselves; besides novelty candy, some <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> chapters distribute them as part of the <a href="/wiki/Vial_of_Life" title="Vial of Life">Vial of Life</a> program to homeowners to store medical history for emergency responders. Another increasingly common use for the preforms are containers in the outdoor activity <a href="/wiki/Geocaching" title="Geocaching">geocaching</a>.</p>
<p>In one-step machines, the entire process from raw material to finished container is conducted within one machine, making it especially suitable for molding non-standard shapes (custom molding), including jars, flat oval, flask shapes, etc. Its greatest merit is the reduction in space, product handling and energy, and far higher visual quality than can be achieved by the two-step system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In year 2016, it was estimated that 56 million tons of PET are produced each year.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> While most thermoplastics can, in principle, be recycled, <a href="/wiki/PET_bottle_recycling" title="PET bottle recycling">PET bottle recycling</a> is more practical than many other plastic applications because of the high value of the resin and the almost exclusive use of PET for widely used water and carbonated soft drink bottling. PET has a <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a> of 1.<sup id="cite_ref-ac_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ac-27">[27]</a></sup> The prime uses for recycled PET are polyester <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">fiber</a>, strapping, and non-food containers.<sup id="cite_ref-ac_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ac-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the recyclability of PET and the relative abundance of <a href="/wiki/Post-consumer_waste" title="Post-consumer waste">post-consumer waste</a> in the form of bottles, PET is rapidly gaining market share as a carpet fiber. <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_Industries" title="Mohawk Industries">Mohawk Industries</a> released everSTRAND in 1999, a 100% post-consumer recycled content PET fiber. Since that time, more than 17 billion bottles have been recycled into carpet fiber.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Pharr Yarns, a supplier to numerous carpet manufacturers including Looptex, Dobbs Mills, and Berkshire Flooring,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> produces a BCF (bulk continuous filament) PET carpet fiber containing a minimum of 25% post-consumer recycled content.</p>
<p>PET, as with many plastics, is also an excellent candidate for thermal disposal (<a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incineration</a>), as it is composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, with only trace amounts of catalyst elements (but no sulfur). PET has the energy content of soft coal.</p>
<p>When recycling polyethylene terephthalate or PET or polyester, in general two ways have to be differentiated:</p>
<ol>
<li>The chemical recycling back to the initial raw materials purified <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalic acid</a> (PTA) or <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> (DMT) and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> (EG) where the polymer structure is destroyed completely, or in process intermediates like <a href="/wiki/Bis(2-hydroxyethyl)_terephthalate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate">bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate</a></li>
<li>The mechanical recycling where the original polymer properties are being maintained or reconstituted.</li>
</ol>
<p>Chemical recycling of PET will become cost-efficient only applying high capacity recycling lines of more than 50,000 tons/year. Such lines could only be seen, if at all, within the production sites of very large polyester producers. Several attempts of industrial magnitude to establish such chemical recycling plants have been made in the past but without resounding success. Even the promising chemical recycling in Japan has not become an industrial breakthrough so far. The two reasons for this are: at first, the difficulty of consistent and continuous waste bottles sourcing in such a huge amount at one single site, and, at second, the steadily increased prices and price volatility of collected bottles. The prices of baled bottles increased for instance between the years 2000 and 2008 from about 50 Euro/ton to over 500 Euro/ton in 2008.</p>
<p>Mechanical recycling or direct circulation of PET in the polymeric state is operated in most diverse variants today. These kinds of processes are typical of small and medium-size industry. Cost-efficiency can already be achieved with plant capacities within a range of 5000–20,000 tons/year. In this case, nearly all kinds of recycled-material feedback into the material circulation are possible today. These diverse recycling processes are being discussed hereafter in detail.</p>
<p>Besides chemical contaminants and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">degradation</a> products generated during first processing and usage, mechanical impurities are representing the main part of quality depreciating impurities in the recycling stream. Recycled materials are increasingly introduced into manufacturing processes, which were originally designed for new materials only. Therefore, efficient sorting, separation and cleaning processes become most important for high quality recycled polyester.</p>
<p>When talking about polyester recycling industry, we are concentrating mainly on recycling of PET bottles, which are meanwhile used for all kinds of liquid packaging like water, carbonated soft drinks, juices, beer, sauces, detergents, household chemicals and so on. Bottles are easy to distinguish because of shape and consistency and separate from waste plastic streams either by automatic or by hand-sorting processes. The established polyester recycling industry consists of three major sections:</p>
<ul>
<li>PET bottle collection and waste separation: waste logistics</li>
<li>Production of clean bottle flakes: flake production</li>
<li>Conversion of PET flakes to final products: flake processing</li>
</ul>
<p>Intermediate product from the first section is baled bottle waste with a PET content greater than 90%. Most common trading form is the bale but also bricked or even loose, pre-cut bottles are common in the market. In the second section, the collected bottles are converted to clean PET bottle flakes. This step can be more or less complex and complicated depending on required final flake quality. During the third step, PET bottle flakes are processed to any kind of products like film, bottles, fiber, filament, strapping or intermediates like pellets for further processing and engineering plastics.</p>
<p>Besides this external (post-consumer) polyester bottle recycling, numbers of internal (pre-consumer) recycling processes exist, where the wasted polymer material does not exit the production site to the free market, and instead is reused in the same production circuit. In this way, fiber waste is directly reused to produce fiber, preform waste is directly reused to produce preforms, and film waste is directly reused to produce film.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PET_bottle_recycling">PET bottle recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: PET bottle recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PET_bottle_recycling" title="PET bottle recycling">PET bottle recycling</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Purification_and_decontamination">Purification and decontamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Purification and decontamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The success of any recycling concept is hidden in the efficiency of purification and decontamination at the right place during processing and to the necessary or desired extent.</p>
<p>In general, the following applies: The earlier in the process foreign substances are removed, and the more thoroughly this is done, the more efficient the process is.</p>
<p>The high <a href="/wiki/Plasticization" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasticization">plasticization</a> temperature of PET in the range of 280&#160;°C (536&#160;°F) is the reason why almost all common organic impurities such as <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polylactic_acid" title="Polylactic acid">PLA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyolefin" title="Polyolefin">polyolefin</a>, chemical wood-pulp and paper fibers, <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate" title="Polyvinyl acetate">polyvinyl acetate</a>, melt adhesive, coloring agents, sugar, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> residues are transformed into colored degradation products that, in their turn, might release in addition reactive degradation products.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Then, the number of defects in the polymer chain increases considerably. The particle size distribution of impurities is very wide, the big particles of 60–1000&#160;µm—which are visible by naked eye and easy to filter—representing the lesser evil, since their total surface is relatively small and the degradation speed is therefore lower. The influence of the microscopic particles, which—because they are many—increase the frequency of defects in the polymer, is relatively greater.</p>
<p>The motto "What the eye does not see the heart cannot grieve over" is considered to be very important in many recycling processes. Therefore, besides efficient sorting, the removal of visible impurity particles by melt filtration processes plays a particular part in this case.</p>

<p>In general, one can say that the processes to make PET bottle flakes from collected bottles are as versatile as the different waste streams are different in their composition and quality. In view of technology there isn't just one way to do it. Meanwhile, there are many engineering companies that are offering flake production plants and components, and it is difficult to decide for one or other plant design. Nevertheless, there are processes that are sharing most of these principles. Depending on composition and impurity level of input material, the general following process steps are applied.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Bale opening, briquette opening</li>
<li>Sorting and selection for different colors, foreign polymers especially PVC, foreign matter, removal of film, paper, glass, sand, soil, stones, and metals</li>
<li>Pre-washing without cutting</li>
<li>Coarse cutting dry or combined to pre-washing</li>
<li>Removal of stones, glass, and metal</li>
<li>Air sifting to remove film, paper, and labels</li>
<li>Grinding, dry and / or wet</li>
<li>Removal of low-density polymers (cups) by density differences</li>
<li>Hot-wash</li>
<li>Caustic wash, and surface etching, maintaining intrinsic viscosity and decontamination</li>
<li>Rinsing</li>
<li>Clean water rinsing</li>
<li>Drying</li>
<li>Air-sifting of flakes</li>
<li>Automatic flake sorting</li>
<li>Water circuit and water treatment technology</li>
<li>Flake quality control</li>
</ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impurities_and_material_defects">Impurities and material defects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Impurities and material defects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The number of possible impurities and material defects that accumulate in the polymeric material is increasing permanently—when processing as well as when using polymers—taking into account a growing service lifetime, growing final applications and repeated recycling. As far as recycled PET bottles are concerned, the defects mentioned can be sorted in the following groups:</p>
<ol>
<li>Reactive polyester OH- or COOH- end groups are transformed into dead or non-reactive end groups, e.g. formation of vinyl ester end groups through dehydration or decarboxylation of terephthalate acid, reaction of the OH- or COOH- end groups with mono-functional degradation products like mono-carbonic acids or alcohols. Results are decreased reactivity during re-polycondensation or re-SSP and broadening the molecular weight distribution.</li>
<li>The end group proportion shifts toward the direction of the COOH end groups built up through a thermal and oxidative degradation. The results are decrease in reactivity, and increase in the acid autocatalytic decomposition during thermal treatment in presence of humidity.</li>
<li>Number of polyfunctional macromolecules increases. Accumulation of gels and long-chain branching defects.</li>
<li>Number, concentration, and variety of nonpolymer-identical organic and inorganic foreign substances are increasing. With every new thermal stress, the organic foreign substances will react by decomposition. This is causing the liberation of further degradation-supporting substances and coloring substances.</li>
<li>Hydroxide and peroxide groups build up at the surface of the products made of polyester in presence of air (oxygen) and humidity. This process is accelerated by ultraviolet light. During an ulterior treatment process, hydro peroxides are a source of oxygen radicals, which are source of oxidative degradation. Destruction of hydro peroxides is to happen before the first thermal treatment or during plasticization and can be supported by suitable additives like antioxidants.</li>
</ol>
<p>Taking into consideration the above-mentioned chemical defects and impurities, there is an ongoing modification of the following polymer characteristics during each recycling cycle, which are detectable by chemical and physical laboratory analysis.</p>
<p>In particular:</p>
<ul>
<li>Increase of COOH end-groups</li>
<li>Increase of color number b</li>
<li>Increase of haze (transparent products)</li>
<li>Increase of oligomer content</li>
<li>Reduction in filterability</li>
<li>Increase of by-products content such as acetaldehyde, formaldehyde</li>
<li>Increase of extractable foreign contaminants</li>
<li>Decrease in color L</li>
<li>Decrease of <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_viscosity" title="Intrinsic viscosity">intrinsic viscosity</a> or dynamic viscosity</li>
<li>Decrease of crystallization temperature and increase of crystallization speed</li>
<li>Decrease of the mechanical properties like tensile strength, elongation at break or <a href="/wiki/Elastic_modulus" title="Elastic modulus">elastic modulus</a></li>
<li>Broadening of molecular weight distribution</li>
</ul>
<p>The recycling of PET-bottles is meanwhile an industrial standard process that is offered by a wide variety of engineering companies.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing_examples_for_recycled_polyester">Processing examples for recycled polyester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Processing examples for recycled polyester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recycling processes with polyester are almost as varied as the manufacturing processes based on primary pellets or melt. Depending on purity of the recycled materials, polyester can be used today in most of the polyester manufacturing processes as blend with virgin polymer or increasingly as 100% recycled polymer. Some exceptions like BOPET-film of low thickness, special applications like optical film or yarns through FDY-spinning at &gt; 6000 m/min, microfilaments, and micro-fibers are produced from virgin polyester only.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_re-pelletizing_of_bottle_flakes">Simple re-pelletizing of bottle flakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Simple re-pelletizing of bottle flakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This process consists of transforming bottle waste into flakes, by drying and crystallizing the flakes, by plasticizing and filtering, as well as by pelletizing. Product is an amorphous re-granulate of an intrinsic viscosity in the range of 0.55–0.7 dℓ/g, depending on how complete pre-drying of PET flakes has been done.</p>
<p>Special feature are: Acetaldehyde and oligomers are contained in the pellets at lower level; the viscosity is reduced somehow, the pellets are amorphous and have to be crystallized and dried before further processing.</p>
<p>Processing to:</p>
<ul>
<li>A-PET film for <a href="/wiki/Thermoforming" title="Thermoforming">thermoforming</a></li>
<li>Addition to PET virgin production</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BoPET" title="BoPET">BoPET</a> packaging film</li>
<li>PET Bottle <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> by SSP</li>
<li>Carpet yarn</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Engineering_plastic" title="Engineering plastic">Engineering plastic</a></li>
<li>Filaments</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Non-woven" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-woven">Non-woven</a></li>
<li>Packaging stripes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Staple_fibre" class="mw-redirect" title="Staple fibre">Staple fibre</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Choosing the re-pelletizing way means having an additional conversion process that is, at the one side, energy-intensive and cost-consuming, and causes thermal destruction. At the other side, the pelletizing step is providing the following advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intensive melt filtration</li>
<li>Intermediate quality control</li>
<li>Modification by additives</li>
<li>Product selection and separation by quality</li>
<li>Processing flexibility increased</li>
<li>Quality uniformization.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture_of_PET-pellets_or_flakes_for_bottles_.28bottle_to_bottle.29_and_A-PET">Manufacture of PET-pellets or flakes for bottles (bottle to bottle) and A-PET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Manufacture of PET-pellets or flakes for bottles (bottle to bottle) and A-PET">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This process is, in principle, similar to the one described above; however, the pellets produced are directly (continuously or discontinuously) crystallized and then subjected to a solid-state polycondensation (SSP) in a tumbling drier or a vertical tube reactor. During this processing step, the corresponding intrinsic viscosity of 0.80–0.085 dℓ/g is rebuild again and, at the same time, the acetaldehyde content is reduced to &lt; 1 ppm.</p>
<p>The fact that some machine manufacturers and line builders in Europe and USA make efforts to offer independent recycling processes, e.g. the so-called bottle-to-bottle (B-2-B) process, such as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bepet.com">BePET</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.starlinger.com">Starlinger</a>, URRC or BÜHLER, aims at generally furnishing proof of the "existence" of the required extraction residues and of the removal of model contaminants according to FDA applying the so-called challenge test, which is necessary for the application of the treated polyester in the food sector. Besides this process approval it is nevertheless necessary that any user of such processes has to constantly check the FDA-limits for the raw materials manufactured by himself for his process.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_conversion_of_bottle_flakes">Direct conversion of bottle flakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Direct conversion of bottle flakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In order to save costs, an increasing number of polyester intermediate producers like spinning mills, strapping mills, or cast film mills are working on the direct use of the PET-flakes, from the treatment of used bottles, with a view to manufacturing an increasing number of polyester intermediates. For the adjustment of the necessary viscosity, besides an efficient drying of the flakes, it is possibly necessary to also reconstitute the viscosity through <a href="/wiki/Polycondensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Polycondensation">polycondensation</a> in the melt phase or solid-state polycondensation of the flakes. The latest PET flake conversion processes are applying twin screw extruders, multi-screw extruders or multi-rotation systems and coincidental vacuum degassing to remove moisture and avoid flake pre-drying. These processes allow the conversion of undried PET flakes without substantial viscosity decrease caused by hydrolysis.</p>
<p>With regard to the consumption of PET bottle flakes, the main portion of about 70% is converted to fibers and filaments. When using directly secondary materials such as bottle flakes in spinning processes, there are a few processing principles to obtain.</p>
<p>High-speed spinning processes for the manufacture of POY normally need a viscosity of 0.62–0.64 dℓ/g. Starting from bottle flakes, the viscosity can be set via the degree of drying. The additional use of TiO<sub>2</sub> is necessary for full dull or semi dull yarn. In order to protect the spinnerets, an efficient filtration of the melt is, in any case is necessary. For the time-being, the amount of POY made of 100% recycling polyester is rather low because this process requires high purity of spinning melt. Most of the time, a blend of virgin and recycled pellets is used.</p>
<p>Staple fibers are spun in an intrinsic viscosity range that lies rather somewhat lower and that should be between 0.58 and 0.62 dℓ/g. In this case, too, the required viscosity can be adjusted via drying or vacuum adjustment in case of vacuum extrusion. For adjusting the viscosity, however, an addition of chain length modifier like <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diethylene_glycol" title="Diethylene glycol">diethylene glycol</a> can also be used.</p>
<p>Spinning non-woven—in the fine titer field for textile applications as well as heavy spinning non-woven as basic materials, e.g. for roof covers or in road building—can be manufactured by spinning bottle flakes. The spinning viscosity is again within a range of 0.58–0.65 dℓ/g.</p>
<p>One field of increasing interest where recycled materials are used is the manufacture of high-tenacity packaging stripes, and monofilaments. In both cases, the initial raw material is a mainly recycled material of higher intrinsic viscosity. High-tenacity packaging stripes as well as monofilament are then manufactured in the melt spinning process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling_to_the_monomers">Recycling to the monomers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Recycling to the monomers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Polyethylene terephthalate can be depolymerized to yield the constituent monomers. After purification, the monomers can be used to prepare new polyethylene terephthalate. The ester bonds in polyethylene terephthalate may be cleaved by hydrolysis, or by transesterification. The reactions are simply the reverse of those used <a href="#Production">in production</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_glycolysis">Partial glycolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Partial glycolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Partial glycolysis (transesterification with ethylene glycol) converts the rigid polymer into short-chained oligomers that can be melt-filtered at low temperature. Once freed of the impurities, the oligomers can be fed back into the production process for polymerization.</p>
<p>The task consists in feeding 10–25% bottle flakes while maintaining the quality of the bottle pellets that are manufactured on the line. This aim is solved by degrading the PET bottle flakes—already during their first plasticization, which can be carried out in a single- or multi-screw extruder—to an intrinsic viscosity of about 0.30 dℓ/g by adding small quantities of ethylene glycol and by subjecting the low-viscosity melt stream to an efficient filtration directly after plasticization. Furthermore, temperature is brought to the lowest possible limit. In addition, with this way of processing, the possibility of a chemical decomposition of the hydro peroxides is possible by adding a corresponding P-stabilizer directly when plasticizing. The destruction of the hydro peroxide groups is, with other processes, already carried out during the last step of flake treatment for instance by adding H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The partially glycolyzed and finely filtered recycled material is continuously fed to the esterification or prepolycondensation reactor, the dosing quantities of the raw materials are being adjusted accordingly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_glycolysis.2C_methanolysis.2C_and_hydrolysis">Total glycolysis, methanolysis, and hydrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polyethylene_terephthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Total glycolysis, methanolysis, and hydrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The treatment of polyester waste through total glycolysis to fully convert the polyester to <a href="/wiki/Bis(2-hydroxyethyl)_terephthalate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate">bis(2-hydroxyethyl) terephthalate</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH)<sub>2</sub>). This compound is purified by vacuum distillation, and is one of the intermediates used in polyester manufacture (see <a href="#Production">production</a>). The reaction involved is as follows:</p>
<dl>
<dd>[(CO)C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>O)]<sub>n</sub> + <i>n</i> HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH → <i>n</i> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH)<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>This recycling route has been executed on an industrial scale in Japan as experimental production.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Similar to total glycolysis, methanolysis converts the polyester to <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a>, which can be filtered and vacuum distilled:</p>
<dl>
<dd>[(CO)C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>O)]<sub>n</sub> + <i>2n</i> CH<sub>3</sub>OH → <i>n</i> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>Methanolysis is only rarely carried out in industry today because polyester production based on dimethyl terephthalate has shrunk tremendously, and many dimethyl terephthalate producers have disappeared.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also as above, polyethylene terephthalate can be hydrolyzed to terephthalic acid and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> under high temperature and pressure. The resultant crude terephthalic acid can be purified by <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry)" title="Recrystallization (chemistry)">recrystallization</a> to yield material suitable for re-polymerization:</p>
<dl>
<dd>[(CO)C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>O)]<sub>n</sub> + 2<i>n</i> H<sub>2</sub>O → <i>n</i> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>H)<sub>2</sub> + <i>n</i> HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH</dd>
</dl>
<p>This method does not appear to have been commercialized yet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
