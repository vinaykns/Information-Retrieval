			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cult</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Cult_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Cult (disambiguation)">Cult (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The term <b>cult</b> usually refers to a <a href="/wiki/Social_group" title="Social group">social group</a> defined by their <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> beliefs, or <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">common interest</a> in a particular personality, object or <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">goal</a>. The term itself is controversial and has divergent definitions in <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">academia</a> and has been an ongoing source of contention among scholars across several fields of study.<sup id="cite_ref-ZablockiRobbins_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZablockiRobbins-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richardson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson-2">[2]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Sociological_classifications_of_religious_movements#Cult_typology" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociological classifications of religious movements">sociological classifications of religious movements</a>, a cult is a social group with <a href="/wiki/Deviance_(sociology)" title="Deviance (sociology)">socially deviant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Novelty" title="Novelty">novel</a> beliefs and practices,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> although this is often unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Other researchers present a less-organized picture of cults on the basis that cults arise spontaneously around novel beliefs and practices.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The word "cult" has always been controversial because it is (in a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> sense) considered a subjective term, used as an <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_hominem" title="Ad hominem">ad hominem</a></i> attack against groups with differing doctrines or practices.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Groups said to be cults range in size from local groups with a few members to international organizations with millions.<sup id="cite_ref-barker1999_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barker1999-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the 1930s, cults became the object of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological study</a> in the context of the study of religious behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> From the 1940s the <a href="/wiki/Christian_countercult_movement" title="Christian countercult movement">Christian countercult movement</a> has opposed some <a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">sects</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movement" title="New religious movement">new religious movements</a>, and labelled them as cults for their "un-Christian" <a href="/wiki/Heterodoxy" title="Heterodoxy">unorthodox beliefs</a>. The secular <a href="/wiki/Anti-cult_movement" title="Anti-cult movement">anti-cult movement</a> began in the 1970s and opposed certain groups, often charging them with <a href="/wiki/Mind_control" title="Mind control">mind control</a> and partly motivated in reaction to acts of violence committed by some of their members. Some of the claims and actions of the anti-cult movements have been disputed by scholars and by the news media, leading to further public controversy.</p>
<p>The term "new religious movement" refers to religions which have appeared since the mid-1800s. Many, but not all, have been considered to be cults. Sub-categories of cults include: <a href="/wiki/Doomsday_cult" title="Doomsday cult">Doomsday cults</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> cults, destructive cults, <a href="/wiki/Racist" class="mw-redirect" title="Racist">racist</a> cults, <a href="/wiki/Polygamist" class="mw-redirect" title="Polygamist">polygamist</a> cults, and <a href="/wiki/Terrorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> cults. Governmental reactions to cult-related issues have also been a source of controversy.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminological_history">Terminological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cult_(religious_practice)" title="Cult (religious practice)">Cult (religious practice)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociological_classifications_of_religious_movements" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociological classifications of religious movements">Sociological classifications of religious movements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holiness_movement" title="Holiness movement">Holiness movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">Faith healing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-cult_movement" title="Anti-cult movement">Anti-cult movement</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ritual_abuse_panic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ritual abuse panic">ritual abuse panic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Church-sect_continuum.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Church-sect_continuum.svg/400px-Church-sect_continuum.svg.png" width="400" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Church-sect_continuum.svg/600px-Church-sect_continuum.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Church-sect_continuum.svg/800px-Church-sect_continuum.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="660" data-file-height="280" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Church-sect_continuum.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Howard_P._Becker" title="Howard P. Becker">Howard P. Becker</a>'s church-sect typology, based on <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Troeltsch" title="Ernst Troeltsch">Ernst Troeltsch</a> original theory and upon which the modern concept of cults, sects, and new religious movements is based.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The word "cult" was originally used not to describe a group of religionists, but for the act of <a href="/wiki/Worship" title="Worship">worship</a> or religious ceremony. It was first used in the early 17th century, borrowed via the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>culte,</i> from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>cultus</i> (worship). This, in turn, was derived from the adjective <i>cultus</i> (inhabited, cultivated, worshiped), based on the verb <i>colere</i> (care, cultivate).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The word "culture" is also derived from the Latin words <i>cultura</i> and <i>cultus</i>, which in general terms refers to the customary beliefs, social forms and material traits of a religious or social group.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the literal sense of the word in English is still in use, a derived sense of "excessive devotion" arose in the 19th century. The terms <i>cult</i> and <i>cultist</i> came to be used in <a href="/wiki/Medical_literature" title="Medical literature">medical literature</a> in the United States in the 1930s for what would now be termed <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">faith healing</a>, especially for the US <a href="/wiki/Holiness_movement" title="Holiness movement">Holiness movement</a>. This experienced a surge of popularity at the time, but extended to other forms of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the English speaking world, the word often carries derogatory connotations, but in other European languages, it is used as English speakers use the word "religion," sometimes causing confusion for English speakers reading material translated from other languages.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> When actually referring to what would be defined as a cult in English, the word "sect" (or similar <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a>) is normally used.</p>
<p>By the late 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_countercult_movement" title="Christian countercult movement">Christian countercult movement</a> began applying the term <i>cult</i> to what would formerly have been termed <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This usage became mainstream by the 1960s, via the best-selling <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_of_the_Cults" title="The Kingdom of the Cults">The Kingdom of the Cults</a></i> (1965). This terminological development, which had so far been characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_United_States" title="Religion in the United States">religious sociology of the United States</a>, entered international use with the <a href="/wiki/Satanic_ritual_abuse" title="Satanic ritual abuse">"ritual abuse" moral panic</a> of the 1980s, which originated in the United States. The late 1980s and early 1990s saw the <a href="/wiki/Satanic_ritual_abuse#International_spread" title="Satanic ritual abuse">international spread</a> throughout most of the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a> and some parts of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also from the 1990s, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">discrimination discourse</a> at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Culture_war#United_States_of_America" title="Culture war">US "culture war"</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neopaganism_in_the_United_States" title="Neopaganism in the United States">US neopagan</a> religions, especially <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a>, began to protest through literature their classification as cults as <a href="/wiki/Discrimination_against_Neopagans" class="mw-redirect" title="Discrimination against Neopagans">discriminatory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Because of this usage of "cult" began to be discouraged in favour of the neutral <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movement" title="New religious movement">new religious movement</a> in sociological literature.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Proponents of such an approach have sometimes been denounced as "procult apologists" by members of the anti-cult movement.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most sociologists and scholars of religion also began to reject the word "cult" altogether because of its negative connotations in mass culture.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Some began to advocate the use of new terms like "new religious movement", "alternative religion" or "novel religion" to describe most of the groups that had come to be referred to as "cults",<sup id="cite_ref-Goldman_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman-22">[22]</a></sup> yet none of these terms have had much success in popular culture or in the media. Other scholars have pushed to redeem the word "cult" as one fit for neutral academic discourse.<sup id="cite_ref-Bainbridge1996_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bainbridge1996-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a survey study containing 258 participants, negative perceptions of the terms "new religious movement", "cult" and "satanic cult" were found. However, these terms differed significantly (i.e., not due to chance) in how negatively the participants perceived them. "New religious movement" was found to be the most favourable term, followed by "cult" and then "Satanic cult".<sup id="cite_ref-Ogloff92_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogloff92-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_religious_movements">New religious movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New religious movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movement" title="New religious movement">New religious movement</a></div>
<p>A new religious movement (NRM) is a religious community or spiritual group of modern origins (since the mid-1800s), which has a peripheral place within its society's dominant religious culture. NRMs can be novel in origin or part of a wider religion, in which case they are distinct from pre-existing <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">denominations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke.2C_Peter_B_2006_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke.2C_Peter_B_2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-siegler2007_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siegler2007-26">[26]</a></sup> Scholars have estimated that NRMs, of which some but not all have been labelled as cults, number in the tens of thousands worldwide, most of which originated in Asia or Africa. The great majority have only a few members, some have thousands and only very few have more than a million.<sup id="cite_ref-barker1999_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barker1999-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2007, religious scholar Elijah Siegler commented that, although no NRM had become the dominant faith in any country, many of the concepts which they had first introduced (often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">New Age</a>" ideas) have become part of worldwide mainstream culture.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarly_studies">Scholarly studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scholarly studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Max_Weber_1894.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Max_Weber_1894.jpg/200px-Max_Weber_1894.jpg" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Max_Weber_1894.jpg/300px-Max_Weber_1894.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Max_Weber_1894.jpg/400px-Max_Weber_1894.jpg 2x" data-file-width="406" data-file-height="542" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Max_Weber_1894.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> (1864–1920), one of the first scholars to study cults.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> (1864–1920) found that cults based on <a href="/wiki/Charisma" title="Charisma">charismatic</a> leadership often follow the <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_authority#Routinizing_charisma" title="Charismatic authority">routinization of charisma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of a "cult" as a <a href="/wiki/Sociological_classifications_of_religious_movements" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociological classifications of religious movements">sociological classification</a> was introduced in 1932 by American sociologist <a href="/wiki/Howard_P._Becker" title="Howard P. Becker">Howard P. Becker</a> as an expansion of German theologian <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Troeltsch" title="Ernst Troeltsch">Ernst Troeltsch</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sociological_classifications_of_religious_movements#Sociological_Church-Sect_typology" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociological classifications of religious movements">church-sect typology</a>. Troeltsch's aim was to distinguish between three main types of religious behavior: churchly, <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystical</a>. Becker created four categories out of Troeltsch's first two by splitting <i>church</i> into "<a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">ecclesia</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">denomination</a>", and <i>sect</i> into "<a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">sect</a>" and "cult".<sup id="cite_ref-SwatosEncy_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SwatosEncy-29">[29]</a></sup> Like Troeltsch's "mystical religion", Becker's cults were small religious groups lacking in organization and emphasizing the private nature of personal beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-CampbellEncy_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CampbellEncy-30">[30]</a></sup> Later sociological formulations built on these characteristics, placing an additional emphasis on cults as <a href="/wiki/Deviant" class="mw-redirect" title="Deviant">deviant</a> religious groups "deriving their inspiration from outside of the predominant religious culture".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This is often thought to lead to a high degree of tension between the group and the more mainstream culture surrounding it, a characteristic shared with religious sects.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In this sociological terminology, <i>sects</i> are products of religious <a href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a> and therefore maintain a continuity with <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">traditional</a> beliefs and practices, while <i>cults</i> arise spontaneously around novel beliefs and practices.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1960s, sociologist <a href="/wiki/John_Lofland_(sociologist)" title="John Lofland (sociologist)">John Lofland</a> lived with <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Young_Oon_Kim" title="Young Oon Kim">Young Oon Kim</a> and some of the first American <a href="/wiki/Unification_Church" title="Unification Church">Unification Church</a> members in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, during which he studied their activities in trying to promote their beliefs and win new members.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Lofland noted that most of their efforts were ineffective and that most of the people who joined did so because of personal relationships with other members, often family relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Lofland published his findings in 1964 as a doctoral thesis entitled: "The World Savers: A Field Study of Cult Processes", and in 1966 in book form by <a href="/wiki/Prentice-Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Prentice-Hall">Prentice-Hall</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Doomsday_Cult:_A_Study_of_Conversion,_Proselytization,_and_Maintenance_of_Faith" title="Doomsday Cult: A Study of Conversion, Proselytization, and Maintenance of Faith">Doomsday Cult: A Study of Conversion, Proselytization and Maintenance of Faith</a></i>. It is considered to be one of the most important and widely cited studies of the process of religious conversion.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Roy_Wallis" title="Roy Wallis">Roy Wallis</a> (1945–1990) argued that a cult is characterized by "<a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemological</a> individualism", meaning that "the cult has no clear locus of final authority beyond the individual member". Cults, according to Wallis, are generally described as "oriented towards the problems of individuals, loosely structured, tolerant [and] non-exclusive", making "few demands on members", without possessing a "clear distinction between members and non-members", having "a rapid turnover of membership" and as being transient collectives with vague boundaries and fluctuating belief systems. Wallis asserts that cults emerge from the "cultic milieu".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978 Bruce Campbell noted that cults are associated with beliefs in a <a href="/wiki/Divine" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine">divine</a> <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">element</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individual</a>. It is either <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">Soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">Self</a>, or <a href="/wiki/True_Self" title="True Self">True Self</a>. Cults are inherently ephemeral and loosely organized. There is a major theme in many of the recent works that show the relationship between cults and <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a>. Campbell brings two major types of cults to attention. One is mystical and the other is instrumental. This can divide the cults into being either <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">occult</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical" class="mw-redirect" title="Metaphysical">metaphysical</a> assemblies. On the basis that Campbell proposes about cults, they are non-traditional religious groups based on belief in a divine element in the individual. There is also a third type. This is service-oriented. Campbell states that "the kinds of stable forms which evolve in the development of religious organization will bear a significant relationship to the content of the religious experience of the founder or founders." <sup id="cite_ref-Bruce_Campbell_1978_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruce_Campbell_1978-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dick_Anthony" title="Dick Anthony">Dick Anthony</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Forensic_psychologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Forensic psychologist">forensic psychologist</a> known for his criticism of <a href="/wiki/Brainwashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Brainwashing">brainwashing</a> theory of conversion,<sup id="cite_ref-Oldenburg_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oldenburg-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> has defended some so-called cults, and in 1988 argued that involvement in such movements may often have beneficial, rather than harmful effects, saying "There's a large research literature published in mainstream journals on the mental health effects of new religions. For the most part the effects seem to be positive in any way that's measurable."<sup id="cite_ref-Sipchen_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sipchen-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In their 1996 book <i>Theory of Religion</i>, American sociologists <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Stark" title="Rodney Stark">Rodney Stark</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Sims_Bainbridge" title="William Sims Bainbridge">William Sims Bainbridge</a> propose that the formation of cults can be explained through the <a href="/wiki/Rational_choice_theory" title="Rational choice theory">rational choice theory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stark1996_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stark1996-44">[44]</a></sup> In <i>The Future of Religion</i> they comment "...in the beginning, all religions are obscure, tiny, deviant cult movements".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Marc_Galanter_(psychiatrist)" title="Marc Galanter (psychiatrist)">Marc Galanter</a>, Professor of Psychiatry at <a href="/wiki/NYU" class="mw-redirect" title="NYU">NYU</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> typical reasons why people join cults include a search for <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Quest" title="Quest">spiritual quest</a>. Stark and Bainbridge, in discussing the process by which individuals join new religious groups, have even questioned the utility of the concept of <i>conversion</i>, suggesting that <i>affiliation</i> is a more useful concept.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movements_and_cults_in_popular_culture" title="New religious movements and cults in popular culture">New religious movements and cults in popular culture</a></div>
<p>Beginning in the 1700s authors in the English-speaking world began introducing members of cults as <a href="/wiki/Antagonists" class="mw-redirect" title="Antagonists">antagonists</a>. <a href="/wiki/Satanists" class="mw-redirect" title="Satanists">Satanists</a>, sects of the <a href="/wiki/Mormon_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Mormon movement">Mormon movement</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thuggees" class="mw-redirect" title="Thuggees">Thuggees</a> were popular choices. In the Twentieth century concern for the rights and feelings of religious minorities led authors to most often invent fictional cults for their villains to be members of.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Fictional cults continue to be popular in film, television, and gaming in the same way; while some popular works treat real cults and new religious movements in a serious manner.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-cult_movements">Anti-cult movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anti-cult movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_countercult_movement">Christian countercult movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Christian countercult movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christian_countercult_movement" title="Christian countercult movement">Christian countercult movement</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Walter_Ralston_Martin_ca_1983.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7e/Walter_Ralston_Martin_ca_1983.png" width="150" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="150" data-file-height="188" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Walter_Ralston_Martin_ca_1983.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Walter_Ralston_Martin" title="Walter Ralston Martin">Walter Martin</a> (1928–1989), American author and leading figure in the <a href="/wiki/Christian_countercult_movement" title="Christian countercult movement">Christian countercult movement</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 1940s, the long held opposition by some established Christian denominations to non-Christian religions and/or supposedly <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretical</a>, or counterfeit, Christian sects crystallized into a more organized <a href="/wiki/Christian_countercult_movement" title="Christian countercult movement">Christian countercult movement</a> in the United States. For those belonging to the movement, all religious groups claiming to be Christian, but deemed outside of Christian <a href="/wiki/Orthodoxy" title="Orthodoxy">orthodoxy</a>, were considered cults.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Christian cults are new religious movements which have a Christian background but are considered to be <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theologically</a> deviant by members of other Christian churches.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In his influential book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_of_the_Cults" title="The Kingdom of the Cults">The Kingdom of the Cults</a></i> (first published in the United States in 1965), Christian scholar <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ralston_Martin" title="Walter Ralston Martin">Walter Martin</a> defines Christian cults as groups that follow the personal interpretation of an individual, rather than the understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> accepted by <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainstream Christianity">mainstream Christianity</a>. He mentions <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Science" title="Christian Science">Christian Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism" title="Unitarian Universalism">Unitarian Universalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unity_Church" title="Unity Church">Unity</a> as examples.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Christian countercult movement asserts that Christian sects whose beliefs are partially or wholly not in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> are erroneous. It also states that a religious sect can be considered a cult if its beliefs involve a denial of what they view as any of the essential <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> teachings such as <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> himself as a person, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Jesus" title="Ministry of Jesus">ministry of Jesus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Miracles_of_Jesus" title="Miracles of Jesus">miracles of Jesus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Crucifixion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Resurrection of Christ">Resurrection of Christ</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Coming of Christ">Second Coming of Christ</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rapture" title="Rapture">Rapture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Countercult literature usually expresses doctrinal or theological concerns and a <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apologetics" title="Apologetics">apologetic</a> purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> It presents a rebuttal by emphasizing the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> against the beliefs of non-fundamental Christian sects. Christian countercult activist writers also emphasize the need for Christians to <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">evangelize</a> to followers of cults.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secular_anti-cult_movement">Secular anti-cult movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Secular anti-cult movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anti-cult_movement" title="Anti-cult movement">Anti-cult movement</a></div>
<p>In the early 1970s, a secular opposition movement to groups considered cults had taken shape. The organizations that formed the secular "<a href="/wiki/Anti-cult_movement" title="Anti-cult movement">anti-cult movement</a>" (ACM) often acted on behalf of relatives of "cult" <a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion" title="Religious conversion">converts</a> who did not believe their loved ones could have altered their lives so drastically by their own free will. A few <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologists</a> and sociologists working in this field suggested that <a href="/wiki/Brainwashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Brainwashing">brainwashing</a> techniques were used to maintain the loyalty of cult members,<sup id="cite_ref-refRichardsonIntrovigne_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refRichardsonIntrovigne-59">[59]</a></sup> while others rejected the idea. The belief that cults brainwashed their members became a unifying theme among cult critics and in the more extreme corners of the anti-cult movement techniques like the sometimes forceful "<a href="/wiki/Deprogramming" title="Deprogramming">deprogramming</a>" of cult members was practiced.<sup id="cite_ref-ShupeEncy_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShupeEncy-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Secular cult opponents belonging to the anti-cult movement usually define a "cult" as a group that tends to manipulate, exploit, and control its members. Specific factors in cult behavior are said to include manipulative and authoritarian <a href="/wiki/Mind_control" title="Mind control">mind control</a> over members, <a href="/wiki/Commune" title="Commune">communal</a> and totalistic organization, aggressive <a href="/wiki/Proselytizing" class="mw-redirect" title="Proselytizing">proselytizing</a>, systematic programs of <a href="/wiki/Indoctrination" title="Indoctrination">indoctrination</a>, and perpetuation in <a href="/wiki/Middle-class" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle-class">middle-class</a> communities.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BromleyEncy_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BromleyEncy-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In the mass media, and among average citizens, "cult" gained an increasingly negative connotation, becoming associated with things like <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnapping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brainwashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Brainwashing">brainwashing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychological_abuse" title="Psychological abuse">psychological abuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">criminal activity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mass_suicide" title="Mass suicide">mass suicide</a>. While most of these negative qualities usually have real documented precedents in the activities of a very small minority of new religious groups, mass culture often extends them to any religious group viewed as culturally deviant, however peaceful or law abiding it may be.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richardson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wright_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>While some psychologists were receptive to these theories, sociologists were for the most part sceptical of their ability to explain conversion to <a href="/wiki/New_religious_movement" title="New religious movement">NRMs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BarkerAReview_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BarkerAReview-70">[70]</a></sup> In the late 1980s, psychologists and sociologists started to abandon theories like brainwashing and <a href="/wiki/Mind-control" class="mw-redirect" title="Mind-control">mind-control</a>. While scholars may believe that various less dramatic <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercive</a> psychological mechanisms could influence group members, they came to see conversion to new religious movements principally as an act of a <a href="/wiki/Rational_choice_theory" title="Rational choice theory">rational choice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayella_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayella-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_to_the_anti-cult_movements">Reactions to the anti-cult movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reactions to the anti-cult movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of the increasingly pejorative use of the words "cult" and "cult leader" since the cult debate of the 1970s, some academics, in addition to groups referred to as cults, argue that these are words to be avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Wessinger" title="Catherine Wessinger">Catherine Wessinger</a> (<a href="/wiki/Loyola_University_New_Orleans" title="Loyola University New Orleans">Loyola University New Orleans</a>) has stated that the word "cult" represents just as much prejudice and antagonism as racial slurs or derogatory words for women and homosexuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Wessinger_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wessinger-75">[75]</a></sup> She has argued that it is important for people to become aware of the bigotry conveyed by the word, drawing attention to the way it dehumanises the group's members and their children.<sup id="cite_ref-Wessinger_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wessinger-75">[75]</a></sup> Labeling a group as subhuman, she says, becomes a justification for violence against it.<sup id="cite_ref-Wessinger_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wessinger-75">[75]</a></sup> She also says that labeling a group a "cult" makes people feel safe, because the "violence associated with religion is split off from conventional religions, projected onto others, and imagined to involve only aberrant groups".<sup id="cite_ref-Wessinger_75-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wessinger-75">[75]</a></sup> This fails to take into account that child abuse, sexual abuse, financial extortion and warfare have also been committed by believers of mainstream religions, but the pejorative "cult" stereotype makes it easier to avoid confronting this uncomfortable fact.<sup id="cite_ref-Wessinger_75-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wessinger-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sociologist Amy Ryan has argued for the need to differentiate those groups that may be dangerous from groups that are more benign.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Ryan notes the sharp differences between definition from cult opponents, who tend to focus on negative characteristics, and those of sociologists, who aim to create definitions that are value-free. The movements themselves may have different definitions of religion as well. <a href="/wiki/George_Chryssides" title="George Chryssides">George Chryssides</a> also cites a need to develop better definitions to allow for common ground in the debate. In <i>Defining Religion in American Law</i>, Bruce J. Casino presents the issue as crucial to international human rights laws. Limiting the definition of religion may interfere with freedom of religion, while too broad a definition may give some dangerous or abusive groups "a limitless excuse for avoiding all unwanted legal obligations".<sup id="cite_ref-Casino-DefiningReligion_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Casino-DefiningReligion-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Psychological_Association_rejection_of_brainwashing_theory">American Psychological Association rejection of brainwashing theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: American Psychological Association rejection of brainwashing theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Singer" title="Margaret Singer">Margaret Singer</a>, a leading anti-cultist who also had studied the political brainwashing of Korean prisoners of war,<sup id="cite_ref-refocus.org_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refocus.org-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clarke_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-79">[79]</a></sup> was asked by the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a> (APA) to chair a <a href="/wiki/Taskforce" class="mw-redirect" title="Taskforce">taskforce</a> called the <a href="/wiki/APA_Task_Force_on_Deceptive_and_Indirect_Techniques_of_Persuasion_and_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="APA Task Force on Deceptive and Indirect Techniques of Persuasion and Control">APA Task Force on Deceptive and Indirect Techniques of Persuasion and Control</a> (DIMPAC) to investigate whether brainwashing or "coercive persuasion" did indeed play a role in recruitment by cults. Before the taskforce had submitted its final report, the APA submitted on 10 February 1987 an <i><a href="/wiki/Amicus_curi%C3%A6" class="mw-redirect" title="Amicus curiæ">amicus curiæ</a></i> brief in an ongoing court case related to brainwashing. Although the amicus curiæ brief written by the APA denies the credibility of the brainwashing theory, the APA submitted the brief under "intense pressure by a consortium of pro-religion scholars (a.k.a. NRM scholars)".<sup id="cite_ref-zablocki-p168_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zablocki-p168-80">[80]</a></sup> The brief repudiated Singer's theories on "coercive persuasion" and suggested that brainwashing theories were without <a href="/wiki/Empirical" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical">empirical</a> proof.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Afterward the APA filed a motion to withdraw its signature from the brief, since Singer's final report had not been completed.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 May 1987, the APA's Board of Social and Ethical Responsibility for Psychology (BSERP) rejected the DIMPAC report because the report "lacks the scientific rigor and evenhanded critical approach necessary for APA imprimatur", and concluded that "after much consideration, BSERP does not believe that we have sufficient information available to guide us in taking a position on this issue."<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Zablocki" title="Benjamin Zablocki">Benjamin Zablocki</a> and Alberto Amitrani interpreted the APA's response as meaning that there was no unanimous decision on the issue either way, suggesting also that Singer retained the respect of the psychological community after the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-zablocki-p168_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zablocki-p168-80">[80]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two critical letters from external reviewers <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Beit-Hallahmi" title="Benjamin Beit-Hallahmi">Benjamin Beit-Hallahmi</a> and Jeffery D. Fisher accompanied the rejection memo. The letters criticized "brainwashing" as an unrecognized theoretical concept and Singer's reasoning as so flawed that it was "almost ridiculous."<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> After her findings were rejected, Singer sued the APA in 1992 for "defamation, frauds, aiding and abetting and conspiracy" and lost.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> After that time U.S. courts consistently rejected testimonies about mind control and manipulation, stating that such theories were not part of accepted mainline <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/Frye_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Frye Standard">Frye Standard</a> of 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subcategories">Subcategories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Subcategories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Destructive_cults">Destructive cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Destructive cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>"Destructive cult" has generally referred to groups whose members have, through deliberate action, physically injured or killed other members of their own group or other people. The <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Consultants_on_Religious_Tolerance" title="Ontario Consultants on Religious Tolerance">Ontario Consultants on Religious Tolerance</a> limit use of the term to specifically refer to religious groups that "have caused or are liable to cause loss of life among their membership or the general public".<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">Psychologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Langone" title="Michael Langone">Michael Langone</a>, executive director of the anti-cult group <a href="/wiki/International_Cultic_Studies_Association" title="International Cultic Studies Association">International Cultic Studies Association</a>, defines a destructive cult as "a highly manipulative group which exploits and sometimes physically and/or psychologically damages members and recruits".<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Gordon_Clark" title="John Gordon Clark">John Gordon Clark</a> cited <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> systems of governance and an emphasis on money making as characteristics of a destructive cult.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> In <i>Cults and the Family</i> the authors cite Shapiro, who defines a "destructive cultism" as a <a href="/wiki/Psychopathy" title="Psychopathy">sociopathic</a> <a href="/wiki/Syndrome" title="Syndrome">syndrome</a>, whose distinctive qualities include: "behavioral and personality changes, loss of personal identity, cessation of scholastic activities, estrangement from family, disinterest in society and pronounced mental control and enslavement by cult leaders".<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the opinion of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Zablocki" title="Benjamin Zablocki">Benjamin Zablocki</a>, a Professor of Sociology at <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_University" title="Rutgers University">Rutgers University</a>, destructive cults are at high risk of becoming abusive to members. He states that this is in part due to members' adulation of <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_authority" title="Charismatic authority">charismatic</a> leaders contributing to the leaders becoming corrupted by power.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> According to Barrett, the most common accusation made against destructive cults is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Reender_Kranenborg" title="Reender Kranenborg">Kranenborg</a>, some groups are risky when they advise their members not to use regular medical care.<sup id="cite_ref-Kranenborg-1996_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kranenborg-1996-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some researchers have criticized the usage of the term "destructive cult", writing that it is used to describe groups which are not necessarily harmful in nature to themselves or others. In his book <i>Understanding New Religious Movements</i>, John A. Saliba writes that the term is overgeneralized. Saliba sees the <a href="/wiki/Peoples_Temple" title="Peoples Temple">Peoples Temple</a> as the "paradigm of a destructive cult", where those that use the term are implying that other groups will also commit <a href="/wiki/Mass_suicide" title="Mass suicide">mass suicide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-saliba_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saliba-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writing in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Misunderstanding_Cults_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="Misunderstanding Cults (book)">Misunderstanding Cults: Searching for Objectivity in a Controversial Field</a></i>, contributor Julius H. Rubin complains that the term has been used to discredit certain groups in the <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">court of public opinion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ZablockiRobbins_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZablockiRobbins-1">[1]</a></sup> In his work <i>Cults in Context</i> author Lorne L. Dawson writes that although the <a href="/wiki/Unification_Church" title="Unification Church">Unification Church</a> "has not been shown to be violent or volatile", it has been described as a destructive cult by "anticult crusaders".<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> In 2002, the German government was held by Germany's <a href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court" title="Federal Constitutional Court">Federal Constitutional Court</a> to have <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamed</a> the <a href="/wiki/Osho_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Osho movement">Osho movement</a> by referring to it, among other things, as a "destructive cult" with no factual basis.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doomsday_cults">Doomsday cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Doomsday cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>"Doomsday cult" is an expression used to describe groups who believe in <a href="/wiki/Apocalypticism" title="Apocalypticism">Apocalypticism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millenarianism" title="Millenarianism">Millenarianism</a>, and can refer both to groups that predict <a href="/wiki/Disaster" title="Disaster">disaster</a>, and to those that attempt to bring it about.<sup id="cite_ref-jenkins_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenkins-98">[98]</a></sup> A 1997 psychological study by Festinger, Riecken, and Schachter found that people turned to a cataclysmic <a href="/wiki/World_view" title="World view">world view</a> after they had repeatedly failed to find meaning in mainstream movements.<sup id="cite_ref-pargament_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pargament-99">[99]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leon_Festinger" title="Leon Festinger">Leon Festinger</a> and his colleagues had observed members of a small <a href="/wiki/UFO_religion" title="UFO religion">UFO religion</a> called the <a href="/wiki/When_Prophecy_Fails" title="When Prophecy Fails">Seekers</a> for several months, and recorded their conversations both prior to and after a failed prophecy from their charismatic leader.<sup id="cite_ref-stangor_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stangor-100">[100]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-petty_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petty-102">[102]</a></sup> Their work was later published in the book <i><a href="/wiki/When_Prophecy_Fails" title="When Prophecy Fails">When Prophecy Fails: A Social and Psychological Study of a Modern Group that Predicted the Destruction of the World</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-festinger_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-festinger-103">[103]</a></sup> In the late 1980s doomsday cults were a major topic of news reports, with some reporters and commentators considering them to be a serious threat to society.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_cults">Political cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Political cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A political cult is a cult with a primary interest in <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political action</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Groups that some writers have termed as "political cults", mostly advocating <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">far-left</a> or <a href="/wiki/Right-wing_politics" title="Right-wing politics">far-right</a> agendas, have received some attention from journalists and scholars. In their 2000 book <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Edge_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="On the Edge (book)">On the Edge: Political Cults Right and Left</a></i>, Dennis Tourish and <a href="/wiki/Tim_Wohlforth" title="Tim Wohlforth">Tim Wohlforth</a> discuss about a dozen organizations in the United States and Great Britain that they characterize as cults.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> In a separate article Tourish says that in his usage:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The word cult is not a term of abuse, as this paper tries to explain. It is nothing more than a shorthand expression for a particular set of practices that have been observed in a variety of dysfunctional organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/LaRouche_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="LaRouche Movement">LaRouche Movement</a><sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gino_Parente" class="mw-redirect" title="Gino Parente">Gino Parente</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Federation" title="National Labor Federation">National Labor Federation</a> (NATLFED)<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> are examples of political groups that have been described as "cults", based in the United States; another is Marlene Dixon's now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Workers_Party" title="Democratic Workers Party">Democratic Workers Party</a> (a critical history of the DWP is given in <i><a href="/wiki/Bounded_Choice" title="Bounded Choice">Bounded Choice</a></i> by Janja A. Lalich, a sociologist and former DWP member).<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<p>The followers of <a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand" title="Ayn Rand">Ayn Rand</a> were characterized as a "cult" by economist <a href="/wiki/Murray_N._Rothbard" class="mw-redirect" title="Murray N. Rothbard">Murray N. Rothbard</a> during her lifetime, and later by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Shermer" title="Michael Shermer">Michael Shermer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rothbard_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rothbard-112">[112]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> The core group around Rand was called the "Collective" and is now defunct (the chief group disseminating Rand's ideas today is the <a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand_Institute" title="Ayn Rand Institute">Ayn Rand Institute</a>). Although the Collective advocated an individualist philosophy, Rothbard claimed they were organized in the manner of a "Leninist" organization.<sup id="cite_ref-rothbard_112-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rothbard-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Workers_Revolutionary_Party_(UK)" title="Workers Revolutionary Party (UK)">Workers Revolutionary Party</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Trotskyist" class="mw-redirect" title="Trotskyist">Trotskyist</a> group led by the late <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Healy" title="Gerry Healy">Gerry Healy</a> and strongly supported by actress <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Redgrave" title="Vanessa Redgrave">Vanessa Redgrave</a>, has been described by others, who have been involved in the Trotskyist movement, as having been a cult or as displaying cult-like characteristics in the 1970s and 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> It is also described as such by Tourish and Wohlforth in their writings.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> In his review of Tourish and Wohlforth's book, Bob Pitt, a former member of the WRP concedes that it had a "cult-like character" but argues that rather than being typical of the far left, this feature actually made the WRP atypical and "led to its being treated as a pariah within the revolutionary left itself".<sup id="cite_ref-Pitt_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitt-116">[116]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Workers' Struggle">Workers' Struggle</a> (LO, <i>Lutte ouvrière</i>) in France, publicly headed by <a href="/wiki/Arlette_Laguiller" title="Arlette Laguiller">Arlette Laguiller</a> but revealed in the 1990s to be directed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Barcia" title="Robert Barcia">Robert Barcia</a>, has often been criticized as a cult, for example by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Cohn-Bendit" title="Daniel Cohn-Bendit">Daniel Cohn-Bendit</a> and his older brother Gabriel Cohn-Bendit, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Humanit%C3%A9" title="L'Humanité">L'Humanité</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lib%C3%A9ration" title="Libération">Libération</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book <i>Les Sectes Politiques: 1965–1995</i> (translation: <i>Political cults: 1965–1995</i>), French writer Cyril Le Tallec considered some religious groups as cults involved in politics, including the <a href="/wiki/League_for_Catholic_Counter-Reformation" title="League for Catholic Counter-Reformation">League for Catholic Counter-Reformation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Office_of_Cluny" title="Cultural Office of Cluny">Cultural Office of Cluny</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Acropolis" title="New Acropolis">New Acropolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dka_Gakkai" class="mw-redirect" title="Sōka Gakkai">Sōka Gakkai</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Light_Mission" title="Divine Light Mission">Divine Light Mission</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_the_Defense_of_Tradition,_Family_and_Property" title="American Society for the Defense of Tradition, Family and Property">Tradition Family Property (TFP)</a>, Longo-Mai, the Supermen Club and the Association for Promotion of the Industrial Arts (Solazaref).<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990 Lucy Patrick commented: "Although we live in a democracy, cult behavior manifests itself in our unwillingness to question the judgment of our leaders, our tendency to devalue outsiders and to avoid dissent. We can overcome cult behavior, he says, by recognizing that we have dependency needs that are inappropriate for mature people, by increasing anti-authoritarian education, and by encouraging personal autonomy and the free exchange of ideas."<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygamist_cults">Polygamist cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Polygamist cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cults that teach and practice <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamy</a>, marriage between more than two people, most often <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">polygyny</a>, one man having multiple wives, have long been noted, although they are a minority. It has been estimated that there are around 50,000 members of polygamist cults in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> Often polygamist cults are viewed negatively by legal authorities and by society, sometimes including related mainstream denominations, because of perceived links to possible domestic abuse and child abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1890, the president of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-Day_Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints">Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilford_Woodruff" title="Wilford Woodruff">Wilford Woodruff</a>, issued a public declaration (the <a href="/wiki/1890_Manifesto" title="1890 Manifesto">Manifesto</a>) announcing that the LDS Church had discontinued new plural marriages. <a href="/wiki/Anti-Mormonism" title="Anti-Mormonism">Anti-Mormon sentiment</a> waned, as did opposition to statehood for <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Smoot_Hearings" class="mw-redirect" title="Smoot Hearings">Smoot Hearings</a> in 1904, which documented that the LDS Church was still practicing polygamy spurred the church to issue a <a href="/wiki/Second_Manifesto" title="Second Manifesto">Second Manifesto</a> again claiming that it had ceased performing new plural marriages. By 1910 the LDS Church <a href="/wiki/Excommunicate" class="mw-redirect" title="Excommunicate">excommunicated</a> those who entered into or performed new plural marriages.<sup id="cite_ref-UHE-Polygamy_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UHE-Polygamy-122">[122]</a></sup> Enforcement of the 1890 Manifesto caused various <a href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schism (religion)">splinter groups</a> to leave the LDS Church in order to continue the practice of plural marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Jesus_Christ_Restored_(Ontario)" title="Church of Jesus Christ Restored (Ontario)">Church of Jesus Christ Restored</a> is a small sect in the <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter Day Saint movement</a> based at <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth,_Ontario" title="Chatsworth, Ontario">Chatsworth, Ontario</a>, Canada. It has been referred to in the news media as a polygamous cult and has been the subject of criminal investigation by local authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-OSST_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSST-124">[124]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CTV_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTV-125">[125]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racist_cults">Racist cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Racist cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Sociologist and historian <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Patterson" title="Orlando Patterson">Orlando Patterson</a> has described the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a>, which arose in the <a href="/wiki/American_South" class="mw-redirect" title="American South">American South</a> after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, as a heretical Christian cult, and their persecution of African Americans and others as a form of <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> Secret <a href="/wiki/Aryan" title="Aryan">Aryan</a> cults in Germany and Austria in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries had a strong influence on the rise of <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> Modern <a href="/wiki/Skinhead" title="Skinhead">Skinhead</a> groups in the United States tend to use the same recruitment techniques as destructive cults.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorist_cults">Terrorist cults</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Terrorist cults">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the book <i>Jihad and Sacred Vengeance: Psychological Undercurrents of History</i>, <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">psychiatrist</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_A._Olsson" title="Peter A. Olsson">Peter A. Olsson</a> compares <a href="/wiki/Osama_bin_Laden" title="Osama bin Laden">Osama bin Laden</a> to certain cult leaders including <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones" title="Jim Jones">Jim Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Koresh" title="David Koresh">David Koresh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shoko_Asahara" title="Shoko Asahara">Shoko Asahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Applewhite" title="Marshall Applewhite">Marshall Applewhite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luc_Jouret" title="Luc Jouret">Luc Jouret</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Di_Mambro" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Di Mambro">Joseph Di Mambro</a>, and says that each of these individuals fit at least eight of the nine criteria for <a href="/wiki/Narcissistic_personality_disorder" title="Narcissistic personality disorder">narcissistic personality disorder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-piven_130-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-piven-130">[130]</a></sup> In the book <i>Seeking the Compassionate Life: The Moral Crisis for Psychotherapy and Society</i> authors Goldberg and Crespo also refer to Osama bin Laden as a "destructive cult leader".<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a 2002 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a> (APA), anti-cultist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Hassan" title="Steven Hassan">Steven Hassan</a> said that <a href="/wiki/Al_Qaida" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Qaida">Al Qaida</a> fulfills the characteristics of a destructive cult. He added: "We need to apply what we know about destructive mind-control cults, and this should be a priority with the war on terrorism. We need to understand the psychological aspects of how people are recruited and indoctrinated so we can slow down recruitment. We need to help counsel former cult members and possibly use some of them in the war against terrorism."<sup id="cite_ref-dittmann_132-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dittmann-132">[132]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an article on <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">Al-Qaida</a> published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, journalist <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ann_Sieghart" title="Mary Ann Sieghart">Mary Ann Sieghart</a> wrote that al-Qaida resembles a "classic cult", commenting: "Al-Qaida fits all the official definitions of a cult. It indoctrinates its members; it forms a closed, totalitarian society; it has a self-appointed, messianic and charismatic leader; and it believes that the ends justify the means."<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Shining_Path" title="Shining Path">Shining Path</a> <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerrilla">guerrilla</a> movement active in Peru in the 1980s and 1990s has been described variously as a "cult"<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> and an intense "cult of personality".<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Tigers" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamil Tigers">Tamil Tigers</a> have also been qualified as such by French magazine <a href="/wiki/L%27Express_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="L'Express (France)">L'Express</a>'<sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Mujahedin_of_Iran" title="People's Mujahedin of Iran">People's Mujahedin of Iran</a>, a leftist guerrilla movement based in Iraq, has been controversially described as a political cult and as a movement that is abusive towards its own members.<sup id="cite_ref-137" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-140" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> Former Mujaheddin member and now author and academic Dr. Masoud Banisadr stated in a May 2005 speech in Spain: "If you ask me: are all cults a terrorist organisation? My answer is no, as there are many peaceful cults at present around the world and in the history of mankind. But if you ask me are all terrorist organisations some sort of cult, my answer is yes. Even if they start as [an] ordinary modern political party or organisation, to prepare and force their members to act without asking any moral questions and act selflessly for the cause of the group and ignore all the ethical, cultural, moral or religious codes of the society and humanity, those organisations have to change into a cult. Therefore to understand an extremist or a terrorist organisation one has to learn about a cult."<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> In 2003, the group ordered some of its members to <a href="/wiki/Self-immolation" title="Self-immolation">set themselves on fire</a>, two of whom died.<sup id="cite_ref-142" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_developments">Regional developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Regional developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The application of the labels "cult" or "sect" to religious movements in government documents signifies the popular and negative use of the term "cult" in <a href="/wiki/English_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="English (language)">English</a> and a functionally similar use of words translated as "sect" in several European languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Richardson01_143-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson01-143">[143]</a></sup> Sociologists critical to this negative politicized use of the word "cult" argue that it may adversely impact the religious freedoms of group members.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis1996_144-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1996-144">[144]</a></sup> At the height of the counter-cult movement and ritual abuse scare of the 1990s, some governments published <a href="/wiki/Governmental_lists_of_cults_and_sects" title="Governmental lists of cults and sects">lists of cults</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-145" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> While these documents utilize similar terminology they do not necessarily include the same groups nor is their assessment of these groups based on agreed criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-Richardson01_143-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson01-143">[143]</a></sup> Other governments and world bodies also report on new religious movements but do not use these terms to describe the groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Richardson01_143-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson01-143">[143]</a></sup> Since the 2000s, some governments have again distanced themselves from such classifications of religious movements.<sup id="cite_ref-146" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> While the official response to new religious groups has been mixed across the globe, some governments aligned more with the critics of these groups to the extent of distinguishing between "legitimate" religion and "dangerous", "unwanted" cults in <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-refRichardsonIntrovigne_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refRichardsonIntrovigne-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Edelman_147-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edelman-147">[147]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For centuries, governments in China have categorized certain religions as <i>xiejiao</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">邪教</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">xiéjiào</span></i>) – sometimes translated as "evil cult" or as "heterodox teaching".<sup id="cite_ref-Pennyreligion_148-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennyreligion-148">[148]</a></sup> In imperial China, the classification of a religion as <i>xiejiao</i> did not necessarily mean that a religion’s teachings were believed to be false or inauthentic, but rather, the label was applied to religious groups that were not authorized by the state, or that were seen as challenging the legitimacy of the state.<sup id="cite_ref-Pennyreligion_148-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennyreligion-148">[148]</a></sup> In modern China, the term <i>xiejiao</i> continues to be used to denote teachings that the government disapproves of, and these groups face suppression and punishment by authorities. Fourteen different groups in China have been listed by the ministry of public security as <i>xiejiao</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-149" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> In addition, in 1999, Chinese authorities denounced the <a href="/wiki/Falun_Gong" title="Falun Gong">Falun Gong</a> spiritual practice as a heretical teaching, and began a campaign to eliminate it. According to <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Persecution_of_Falun_Gong" title="Persecution of Falun Gong">persecution of Falun Gong</a> includes a multifaceted propaganda campaign,<sup id="cite_ref-CRS2006_150-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRS2006-150">[150]</a></sup> a program of enforced ideological conversion and re-education, as well as a variety of extralegal coercive measures, such as arbitrary arrests, <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labour">forced labour</a>, and physical <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a>, sometimes resulting in death.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty1_151-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty1-151">[151]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Interior_Ministry" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Interior Ministry">Russian Interior Ministry</a> prepared a list of "extremist groups." At the top of the list were Islamic groups outside of "traditional Islam," which is supervised by the Russian government. Next listed were <a href="/wiki/Neopaganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Neopaganism">"Pagan cults"</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-152" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> In 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Ministry_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Ministry of Justice">Russian Ministry of Justice</a> created a council which it named "Council of Experts Conducting State Religious Studies Expert Analysis." The new council listed 80 large sects which it considered potentially dangerous to Russian society, and mentioned that there were thousands of smaller ones. Large sects listed included: <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, and what were called "neo-Pentecostals."<sup id="cite_ref-153" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, the scientific status of the "<a href="/wiki/Mind_control" title="Mind control">brainwashing theory</a>" became a central topic in U.S. court cases where the theory was used to try to justify the use of the forceful <a href="/wiki/Deprogramming" title="Deprogramming">deprogramming</a> of cult members.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis.2C_2004_154-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis.2C_2004-154">[154]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davis1996_144-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1996-144">[144]</a></sup> Meanwhile, sociologists critical of these theories assisted advocates of <a href="/wiki/Religious_freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious freedom">religious freedom</a> in defending the legitimacy of new religious movements in court.<sup id="cite_ref-refRichardsonIntrovigne_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refRichardsonIntrovigne-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Edelman_147-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edelman-147">[147]</a></sup> In the United States religious activities of cults are protected under the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_of_the_United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="First Amendment of the United States Constitution">First Amendment of the United States Constitution</a>, which prohibits governmental <a href="/wiki/Establishment_of_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Establishment of religion">establishment of religion</a> and protects <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">freedom of religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_assembly" title="Freedom of assembly">freedom of assembly</a>. However, no religious or cult members are granted any special immunity from criminal charges.<sup id="cite_ref-Ogloff92_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogloff92-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Europe">Western Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cult&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Western Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>France and Belgium have taken policy positions which accept "brainwashing" theories uncritically, while other European nations, like Sweden and Italy, are cautious about brainwashing and have adopted more neutral responses to new religions.<sup id="cite_ref-155" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> Scholars have suggested that outrage following the mass murder/suicides perpetuated by the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Solar_Temple" title="Order of the Solar Temple">Solar Temple</a><sup id="cite_ref-refRichardsonIntrovigne_59-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refRichardsonIntrovigne-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robbins2002_156-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robbins2002-156">[156]</a></sup> as well as the more latent <a href="/wiki/Xenophobic" class="mw-redirect" title="Xenophobic">xenophobic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-American" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-American">anti-American</a> attitudes have contributed significantly to European anti-cult positions.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckford1998_157-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckford1998-157">[157]</a></sup> In the 1980s clergymen and officials of the French government expressed concern that some <a href="/wiki/Religious_order" title="Religious order">orders</a> and other groups within the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> would be adversely affected by anti-cult laws then being considered.<sup id="cite_ref-158" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup></p>
