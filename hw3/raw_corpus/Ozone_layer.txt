			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ozone layer</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>The <b>ozone layer</b> or <b>ozone shield</b> is a region of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> that absorbs most of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV) radiation. It contains high concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (O<sub>3</sub>) in relation to other parts of the atmosphere, although still small in relation to other gases in the stratosphere. The ozone layer contains less than 10 parts per million of ozone, while the average ozone concentration in Earth's atmosphere as a whole is about 0.3 parts per million. The ozone layer is mainly found in the lower portion of the stratosphere, from approximately 20 to 30 kilometres (12 to 19&#160;mi) above Earth, although its thickness varies seasonally and geographically.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ozone layer was discovered in 1913 by the French physicists <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fabry" title="Charles Fabry">Charles Fabry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Buisson" title="Henri Buisson">Henri Buisson</a>. Measurements of the sun showed that the radiation sent out from its surface and reaching the ground on Earth is usually consistent with the spectrum of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> with a temperature in the range of 5,500–6,000 K (5,227 to 5,727&#160;°C), except that there was no radiation below a wavelength of about 310&#160;nm at the ultraviolet end of the spectrum. It was deduced that the missing radiation was being absorbed by something in the atmosphere. Eventually the spectrum of the missing radiation was matched to only one known chemical, ozone.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Its properties were explored in detail by the British meteorologist <a href="/wiki/G._M._B._Dobson" title="G. M. B. Dobson">G. M. B. Dobson</a>, who developed a simple <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">spectrophotometer</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Dobson_spectrometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Dobson spectrometer">Dobsonmeter</a>) that could be used to measure stratospheric ozone from the ground. Between 1928 and 1958, Dobson established a worldwide network of ozone monitoring stations, which continue to operate to this day. The "<a href="/wiki/Dobson_unit" title="Dobson unit">Dobson unit</a>", a convenient measure of the <a href="/wiki/Area_density" title="Area density">amount</a> of ozone overhead, is named in his honor.</p>
<p>The ozone layer absorbs 97 to 99 percent of the Sun's medium-frequency ultraviolet light (from about 200&#160;<a href="/wiki/Nanometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanometer">nm</a> to 315&#160;nm <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>), which otherwise would potentially damage exposed life forms near the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United Nations General Assembly has designated September 16 as the <a href="/wiki/International_Day_for_the_Preservation_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="International Day for the Preservation of the Ozone Layer">International Day for the Preservation of the Ozone Layer</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> also has a thin ozone layer at an altitude of 100 kilometers from the planet's surface.<sup id="cite_ref-venus_ozone_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venus_ozone-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozone_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The photochemical mechanisms that give rise to the ozone layer were discovered by the British physicist <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Chapman_(mathematician)" title="Sydney Chapman (mathematician)">Sydney Chapman</a> in 1930. Ozone in the Earth's stratosphere is created by ultraviolet light striking ordinary <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> containing two oxygen <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> (O<sub>2</sub>), splitting them into individual oxygen atoms (atomic oxygen); the atomic oxygen then combines with unbroken O<sub>2</sub> to create ozone, O<sub>3</sub>. The ozone molecule is unstable (although, in the stratosphere, long-lived) and when ultraviolet light hits ozone it splits into a molecule of O<sub>2</sub> and an individual atom of oxygen, a continuing process called the <a href="/wiki/Ozone-oxygen_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Ozone-oxygen cycle">ozone-oxygen cycle</a>. Chemically, this can be described as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>O<sub>2</sub> + ℎν<sub>uv</sub> → 2O</dd>
<dd>O + O<sub>2</sub> ↔ O<sub>3</sub></dd>
</dl>
<p>About 90 percent of the ozone in the atmosphere is contained in the stratosphere. Ozone concentrations are greatest between about 20 and 40 kilometres (66,000 and 131,000&#160;ft), where they range from about 2 to 8 parts per million. If all of the ozone were compressed to the pressure of the air at sea level, it would be only 3 millimetres (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> inch) thick.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

Essentially all UVC (100–280&#160;nm) is blocked by dioxygen (from 100–200&#160;nm) or else by ozone (200–280&#160;nm) in the atmosphere. The shorter portion of the UV-C band and the more energetic UV above this band causes the formation of the ozone layer, when single oxygen atoms produced by UV <a href="/wiki/Photolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Photolysis">photolysis</a> of dioxygen (below 240&#160;nm) react with more dioxygen. The ozone layer also blocks most, but not quite all, of the sunburn-producing UV-B (280–315&#160;nm) band, which lies in the wavelengths longer than UV-C. The band of UV closest to visible light, UV-A (315–400&#160;nm), is hardly affected by ozone, and most of it reaches the ground. UV-A does not primarily cause skin reddening, but there is evidence that it causes long-term skin damage.</div>
</div>

<p>Although the concentration of the ozone in the ozone layer is very small, it is vitally important to life because it absorbs biologically harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation coming from the sun. Extremely short or vacuum UV (10–100&#160;nm) is screened out by nitrogen. UV radiation capable of penetrating nitrogen is divided into three categories, based on its wavelength; these are referred to as UV-A (400–315&#160;nm), UV-B (315–280&#160;nm), and UV-C (280–100&#160;nm).</p>
<p>UV-C, which is very harmful to all living things, is entirely screened out by a combination of dioxygen (&lt; 200&#160;nm) and ozone (&gt; about 200&#160;nm) by around 35 kilometres (115,000&#160;ft) altitude. UV-B radiation can be harmful to the skin and is the main cause of <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a>; excessive exposure can also cause cataracts, immune system suppression, and genetic damage, resulting in problems such as <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancer</a>. The ozone layer (which absorbs from about 200&#160;nm to 310&#160;nm with a maximal absorption at about 250&#160;nm)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> is very effective at screening out UV-B; for radiation with a wavelength of 290&#160;nm, the intensity at the top of the atmosphere is 350 million times stronger than at the Earth's surface. Nevertheless, some UV-B, particularly at its longest wavelengths, reaches the surface, and is important for the skin's production of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a>.</p>
<p>Ozone is transparent to most UV-A, so most of this longer-wavelength UV radiation reaches the surface, and it constitutes most of the UV reaching the Earth. This type of UV radiation is significantly less harmful to DNA, although it may still potentially cause physical damage, premature aging of the skin, indirect genetic damage, and skin cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The thickness of the ozone layer—that is, the total amount of ozone in a column overhead—varies by a large factor worldwide, being in general smaller near the equator and larger towards the poles. It also varies with season, being in general thicker during the spring and thinner during the autumn. The reasons for this latitude and seasonal dependence are complicated, which involve in atmospheric circulation patterns as well as solar intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since stratospheric ozone is produced by solar UV radiation, one might expect to find the highest ozone levels over the tropics and the lowest over polar regions. The same argument would lead one to expect the highest ozone levels in the summer and the lowest in the winter. The observed behavior is very different: most of the ozone is found in the mid-to-high latitudes of the northern and southern hemispheres, and the highest levels are found in the spring, not summer, and the lowest in the autumn, not winter in the northern hemisphere. During winter, the ozone layer actually increases in depth. This puzzle is explained by the prevailing stratospheric wind patterns, known as the <a href="/wiki/Brewer-Dobson_circulation" title="Brewer-Dobson circulation">Brewer-Dobson circulation</a>. While most of the ozone is indeed created over the tropics, the stratospheric circulation then transports it poleward and downward to the lower stratosphere of the high latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup> However, owing to the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_hole" class="mw-redirect" title="Ozone hole">ozone hole</a> phenomenon, the lowest amounts of column ozone found anywhere in the world are over the Antarctic in the southern spring period of September and October and to a lesser extent over the Arctic in the northern spring period of March, April, and May.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg/300px-Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg" width="300" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg/450px-Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="504" data-file-height="375" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nimbus_ozone_Brewer-Dobson_circulation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The ozone layer is higher in altitude in the tropics, and lower in altitude outside the tropics, especially in the polar regions. This altitude variation of ozone results from the slow circulation that lifts the ozone-poor air out of the troposphere into the stratosphere. As this air slowly rises in the tropics, ozone is produced as the sun overhead photolyzes oxygen molecules. As this slow circulation levels off and flows towards the mid-latitudes, it carries the ozone-rich air from the tropical middle stratosphere to the lower stratosphere middle and high latitudes . The high ozone concentrations at high latitudes are due to the accumulation of ozone at lower altitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Brewer-Dobson circulation moves very slowly. The time needed to lift an air parcel by 1&#160;km in the lower tropical stratosphere is about 2 months (18 m per day).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, horizontal poleward transport in the lower stratosphere is much faster and amounts to approximately 100&#160;km per day in the northern hemisphere whilst it is only half as much in the southern hemisphere (~51&#160;km per day).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Even though ozone in the lower tropical stratosphere is produced at a very slow rate, the lifting circulation is so slow that ozone can build up to relatively high levels by the time it reaches 26 kilometres (16&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ozone amounts over the continental United States (25°N to 49°N) are highest in the northern spring (April and May). These ozone amounts fall over the course of the summer to their lowest amounts in October, and then rise again over the course of the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-wmo2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo2010-11">[11]</a></sup> Again, wind transport of ozone is principally responsible for the seasonal changes of these higher latitude ozone patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The total column amount of ozone generally increases as we move from the tropics to higher latitudes in both hemispheres. However, the overall column amounts are greater in the northern hemisphere high latitudes than in the southern hemisphere high latitudes. In addition, while the highest amounts of column ozone over the Arctic occur in the northern spring (March–April), the opposite is true over the Antarctic, where the lowest amounts of column ozone occur in the southern spring (September–October).<sup id="cite_ref-APH_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APH-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depletion">Depletion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozone_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Depletion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Ozone depletion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif/300px-Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif" width="300" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif/450px-Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif/600px-Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="932" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Future_ozone_layer_concentrations.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NASA projections of stratospheric ozone concentrations if chlorofluorocarbons had not been banned.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ozone layer can be depleted by free radical catalysts, including <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO), <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O), <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> (OH), atomic <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl), and atomic <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> (Br). While there are natural sources for all of these <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">species</a>, the concentrations of chlorine and bromine increased markedly in recent decades because of the release of large quantities of man-made <a href="/wiki/Organohalogen" class="mw-redirect" title="Organohalogen">organohalogen</a> compounds, especially <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs) and <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">bromofluorocarbons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These highly stable compounds are capable of surviving the rise to the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, where Cl and Br <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radicals</a> are liberated by the action of ultraviolet light. Each radical is then free to initiate and catalyze a chain reaction capable of breaking down over 100,000 ozone molecules. By 2009, nitrous oxide was the largest ozone-depleting substance (ODS) emitted through human activities.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png/220px-TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png" width="220" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png/330px-TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png/440px-TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png 2x" data-file-width="721" data-file-height="375" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TOMS_Global_Ozone_65N-65S.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Levels of atmospheric ozone measured by satellite show clear seasonal variations and appear to verify their decline over time.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The breakdown of ozone in the stratosphere results in reduced absorption of ultraviolet radiation. Consequently, unabsorbed and dangerous ultraviolet radiation is able to reach the Earth’s surface at a higher intensity. Ozone levels have dropped by a worldwide average of about 4 percent since the late 1970s. For approximately 5 percent of the Earth's surface, around the north and south poles, much larger seasonal declines have been seen, and are described as "ozone holes".<sup id="cite_ref-wmo2010_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo2010-11">[11]</a></sup> The discovery of the annual depletion of ozone above the Antarctic was first announced by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Farman" title="Joe Farman">Joe Farman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Gardiner" title="Brian G. Gardiner">Brian Gardiner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Shanklin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jonathan Shanklin">Jonathan Shanklin</a>, in a paper which appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> on May 16, 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozone_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_and_climate_change" title="Ozone depletion and climate change">Ozone depletion and climate change</a></div>
<p>To support successful regulation attempts, the ozone case was communicated to lay persons "with easy-to-understand bridging metaphors derived from the popular culture" and related to "immediate risks with everyday relevance". The specific metaphors used in the discussion (ozone shield, ozone hole) proved quite useful<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and, compared to global climate change, the ozone case was much more seen as a "hot issue" and imminent risk.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Lay people were cautious about a depletion of the ozone layer and the risks of skin cancer.</p>
<p>In 1978, the United States, Canada and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> enacted bans on <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFC</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray" title="Aerosol spray">aerosol sprays</a> that damage the ozone layer. The European Community rejected an analogous proposal to do the same. In the U.S., chlorofluorocarbons continued to be used in other applications, such as refrigeration and industrial cleaning, until after the discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole in 1985. After negotiation of an international treaty (the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>), CFC production was capped at 1986 levels with commitments to long-term reductions.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Since that time, the treaty was amended to ban CFC production after 1995 in the developed countries, and later in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Today, all of the world's 197 countries have signed the treaty. Beginning January 1, 1996, only recycled and stockpiled CFCs were available for use in developed countries like the US. This production phaseout was possible because of efforts to ensure that there would be substitute chemicals and technologies for all ODS uses.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 2, 2003, scientists announced that the global depletion of the ozone layer may be slowing down because of the international regulation of ozone-depleting substances. In a study organized by the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a>, three satellites and three ground stations confirmed that the upper-atmosphere ozone-depletion rate slowed down significantly during the previous decade. Some breakdown can be expected to continue because of ODSs used by nations which have not banned them, and because of gases which are already in the stratosphere. Some ODSs, including CFCs, have very long atmospheric lifetimes, ranging from 50 to over 100 years. It has been estimated that the ozone layer will recover to 1980 levels near the middle of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-wmo2010_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo2010-11">[11]</a></sup> A gradual trend toward "healing" was reported in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-healing_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healing-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compounds containing C–H bonds (such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochlorofluorocarbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrochlorofluorocarbon">hydrochlorofluorocarbons</a>, or HCFCs) have been designed to replace CFCs in certain applications. These replacement compounds are more reactive and less likely to survive long enough in the atmosphere to reach the stratosphere where they could affect the ozone layer. While being less damaging than CFCs, HCFCs can have a negative impact on the ozone layer, so they are also being phased out.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> These in turn are being replaced by <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrofluorocarbons">hydrofluorocarbons</a> (HFCs) and other compounds that do not destroy stratospheric ozone at all.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_astronomy">Implications for astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozone_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implications for astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As ozone in the atmosphere prevents most energetic ultraviolet radiation reaching the surface of the earth, astronomical data in these wavelengths has to be gathered from satellites orbiting above the atmosphere and ozone layer. Most of the light from young hot stars is in the ultraviolet and so study of these wavelengths is important for studying the origins of galaxies. The Galaxy Evolution Explorer, <a href="/wiki/GALEX" title="GALEX">GALEX</a>, is an orbiting ultraviolet space telescope launched on April 28, 2003, which operated until early 2012.</p>
