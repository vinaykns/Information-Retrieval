<p>The <b>Institution of Mechanical Engineers</b> (<b>IMechE</b>) is an independent <a href="/wiki/Engineering_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering society">engineering society</a>, headquartered in central London, that represents <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical engineers">mechanical engineers</a>. With over 113,000 members in 140 countries, working across industries such as railway, automotive, aerospace, manufacturing, energy, biomedical and construction, the cInstitution is licensed by the <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Council" title="Engineering Council">Engineering Council</a> (EngC) to assess candidates for inclusion on its Register of <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartered Engineer">Chartered Engineers</a>.</p>
<p>The Institution was founded at the Queen's Hotel, Birmingham, by <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> in 1847 (following an informal meeting at locomotive designer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Beyer" title="Charles Beyer">Charles Beyer</a>'s house in Cecil Street, Manchester). It received a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> in 1930. The Institution's headquarters, purpose-built for the Institution in 1899, are situated at One <a href="/wiki/Birdcage_Walk" title="Birdcage Walk">Birdcage Walk</a>, London. The first woman to be elected to the Institution was <a href="/wiki/Verena_Holmes" title="Verena Holmes">Verena Holmes</a> in 1919.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GeorgeStephenson.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/GeorgeStephenson.PNG/220px-GeorgeStephenson.PNG" width="220" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/GeorgeStephenson.PNG/330px-GeorgeStephenson.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/GeorgeStephenson.PNG/440px-GeorgeStephenson.PNG 2x" data-file-width="764" data-file-height="1094" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GeorgeStephenson.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
George Stephenson</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Institution of Mechanical Engineers was founded on 27 January 1847, in the Queen's Hotel next to <a href="/wiki/Curzon_Street_railway_station" title="Curzon Street railway station">Curzon Street railway station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> by the railway pioneer <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-Cragg_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cragg-1">[1]</a></sup> The founding of the Institution is said to have been spurred by outrage that <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a>, the most famous mechanical engineer of the age, had been refused admission to the <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Civil_Engineers" title="Institution of Civil Engineers">Institution of Civil Engineers</a> unless he sent in "a probationary essay as proof of his capacity as an engineer".<sup id="cite_ref-Pullin_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pullin-2">[2]</a></sup> However this account has been challenged as an exaggeration: though there was certainly coolness between Stephenson and the Institution of Civil Engineers, it is more likely that the motivation behind the founding of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers was simply the need for a specific home for the growing number of mechanical engineers employed in the burgeoning railway and manufacturing industries.<sup id="cite_ref-Pullin_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pullin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Beyer" title="Charles Beyer">Charles Beyer</a> proposed that George Stephenson become the Institution's first President in 1847,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> followed by his son, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson" title="Robert Stephenson">Robert Stephenson</a>, in 1849. Beyer became Vice President and was one of the first to present papers to the Institution;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Geach" title="Charles Geach">Charles Geach</a> was the IMechE's first treasurer. Throughout the 19th and 20th centuries some of Britain's most notable engineers held the position of President, including <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth" title="Joseph Whitworth">Joseph Whitworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Siemens" title="Carl Wilhelm Siemens">Carl Wilhelm Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Harry_Ricardo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Harry Ricardo">Sir Harry Ricardo</a>. It operated from premises in Birmingham until 1877 when it moved to London, taking up its present headquarters on Birdcage Walk in 1899.<sup id="cite_ref-imeche.org_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="One_Birdcage_Walk">One Birdcage Walk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: One Birdcage Walk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg/220px-One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg/330px-One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg/440px-One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg 2x" data-file-width="448" data-file-height="298" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:One_Birdcage_Walk.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One Birdcage Walk</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon its move to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1877, the Institution rented premises at No 10 Victoria Chambers, where it remained for 20 years. In 1895 the Institution bought a plot of land at Storey's Gate, on the eastern end of <a href="/wiki/Birdcage_Walk" title="Birdcage Walk">Birdcage Walk</a>, for £9,500.<sup id="cite_ref-imeche.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche.org-5">[5]</a></sup> Architect Basil Slade looked to the newly completed Admiralty buildings facing the site for inspiration. The building was designed in the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne, 'streaky bacon', style</a> in red brick and <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a>. Inside, there were several features that were state of the art for the time, including a <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a>, a 54-inch fan in the lecture theatre for driving air into the building; an <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">electric lift</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator_Company" title="Otis Elevator Company">Otis Elevator Company</a>, and a Synchronome master-clock, which controlled all house timepieces. In 1933 architect <a href="/wiki/James_Miller_(architect)" title="James Miller (architect)">James Miller</a>, who also designed the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Civil_Engineers" title="Institution of Civil Engineers">Institution of Civil Engineers</a>, remodelled the building, expanding the library and introducing electric lighting.</p>
<p>The building would go on to host the first public presentation of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Whittle" title="Frank Whittle">Frank Whittle's</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a> in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1943 it became the venue for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Electrical_and_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Royal Electrical and Mechanical Engineers">Royal Electrical and Mechanical Engineers' (REME)</a> planning of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation Overlord</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Normandy" title="Invasion of Normandy">invasion of Normandy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-imeche.org_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.onebirdcagewalk.com/home">One Birdcage Walk</a> hosts events, lectures, seminars and meetings in 17 conference and meeting rooms named after notable former members of the Institution, such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Whittle" title="Frank Whittle">Frank Whittle</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Algernon_Parsons" title="Charles Algernon Parsons">Charles Parsons</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership_Grades_and_Post-nominals">Membership Grades and Post-nominals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Membership Grades and Post-nominals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following are membership grades with <a href="/wiki/Post-nominal_letters" title="Post-nominal letters">post-nominals</a>&#160;:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Affiliate</i>: (no post-nominal) The grade for students, apprentices and those interested in or involved in mechanical engineering who do not meet the requirements for the following grades.</li>
<li><b>AMIMechE</b>: <i>Associate Member of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers</i>: this is the grade for graduates (of acceptable degrees or equivalents in engineering, mathematics or science)</li>
<li><b>MIMechE</b>: <i>Member of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers</i>. For those who meet the educational and professional requirements for registration as a <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer_(UK)" title="Chartered Engineer (UK)">Chartered Engineer</a> (CEng) or <a href="/wiki/Incorporated_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Incorporated Engineer">Incorporated Engineer</a> (IEng) or <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Technician" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering Technician">Engineering Technician</a> (EngTech) in Mechanical Engineering .</li>
<li><b>FIMechE</b>: <i><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Fellow of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers">Fellow of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers</a></i>. This is the highest class of elected membership, and is awarded to individuals who have demonstrated exceptional commitment to and innovation in mechanical engineering.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Heritage_Awards" title="Engineering Heritage Awards">Engineering Heritage Awards</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Heritage_Awards" title="Engineering Heritage Awards">Engineering Heritage Awards</a> were created in 1984 to help recognise and promote the value of artefacts, locations, collections and landmarks of significant engineering importance.</p>
<p>Along with The Manufacturer, the Institution also runs The Manufacturer MX Awards,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Formula_Student" title="Formula Student">Formula Student</a>, the world's largest student motorsport event.</p>

Annual dinner of the Institution in the carriage works of the Midland Railway at Derby in 1898. Samuel Johnson, the railway's Chief Mechanical Engineer was the institution president.</div>

<p>As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there have been 131 presidents of the Institution, who since 1922 have been elected annually for one year. The first president was <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a>, followed by his son <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson" title="Robert Stephenson">Robert</a>. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth" title="Joseph Whitworth">Joseph Whitworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Penn_(engineer)" title="John Penn (engineer)">John Penn</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William Armstrong</a> are the only persons to have served two terms. <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Liversidge" title="Pamela Liversidge">Pamela Liversidge</a> in 1997-98 was the first female president and Professor Isobel Pollock became the second in May 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_Committees">Engineering Committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Engineering Committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Institution of Mechanical Engineers has a number of committees that work to promote and develop thought leadership in different industry sectors. The Institution has 8 divisions: - Aerospace, Automobile, Biomedical Engineering Association, Construction &amp; Building Services, Cross-sector Technologies, Manufacturing Industries, Power Industries, Process Industries and Railway.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biomedical Engineering Association (BmEA) aims to bring together key workers from both medicine and engineering to discuss the latest advances and issues, to enable networking among different industry leaders, and to promote the field of Medical Engineering, also known as <a href="/wiki/Bioengineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioengineering">Bioengineering</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_Engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomedical Engineering">Biomedical Engineering</a>, to government, healthcare professionals and the wider public. This committee offers:</p>
<ul>
<li>seminars, lectures and conferences every year;</li>
<li>the <i>Journal of Engineering in Medicine</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>the annual Student Project Competition.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <b>Railway Division</b> was formed in 1969 when IMechE amalgamated with the Institution of Locomotive Engineers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
