<p><b>Patrick Moore</b> (born 1947) is a Canadian <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a> and former president of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> Canada.</p>
<p>He has sharply and publicly differed with many policies of major environmental groups, including <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> itself on other issues including forestry, biotechnology, aquaculture, and the use of chemicals for many applications.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> According to Greenpeace, he is "a paid spokesman for the nuclear industry, the logging industry, and genetic engineering industry" and is an outspoken proponent of nuclear energy and skeptical of sole human responsibility for climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace-Patrick-Moore-Info_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace-Patrick-Moore-Info-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore was born in 1947, in <a href="/wiki/Port_Alice,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Port Alice, British Columbia">Port Alice, British Columbia</a> and raised in <a href="/wiki/Winter_Harbour,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter Harbour, British Columbia">Winter Harbour</a>, on Vancouver Island. He is the third generation of a British Columbian family with a long history in forestry and fishing. His father, W. D. Moore, was the president of the B.C. Truck Loggers Association and past president of the Pacific Logging Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Moore obtained a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> from the Institute of Animal Resource Ecology, <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> under the direction of Dr. <a href="/wiki/C.S._Holling" class="mw-redirect" title="C.S. Holling">C.S. Holling</a> and forest ecologist Hamish Kimmins.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greenpeace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <i>Greenpeace: How a Group of Ecologists, Journalists, and Visionaries Changed the World</i> by Rex Wyler, the <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Make_a_Wave_Committee" title="Don't Make a Wave Committee">Don't Make a Wave Committee</a> was formed in January 1970 by <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Stowe" title="Dorothy Stowe">Dorothy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irving_Stowe" title="Irving Stowe">Irving Stowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Metcalfe" title="Ben Metcalfe">Ben Metcalfe</a>, Marie and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bohlen" title="Jim Bohlen">Jim Bohlen</a>, Paul Cote, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hunter_(journalist)" title="Robert Hunter (journalist)">Bob Hunter</a> and incorporated in October 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Committee had formed to plan opposition to the testing of a one megaton hydrogen bomb in 1969 by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">United States Atomic Energy Commission</a> on <a href="/wiki/Amchitka" title="Amchitka">Amchitka</a> Island in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutians" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleutians">Aleutians</a>. Moore joined the committee in 1971 and, as Greenpeace co-founder Bob Hunter wrote, “Moore was quickly accepted into the inner circle on the basis of his scientific background, his reputation [as an environmental activist], and his ability to inject practical, no-nonsense insights into the discussions.”<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore traveled to Alaska on advanced research with Jim Bohlen, attending Wave Committee meetings. In 1971, Moore was a member of the crew of the <i>Phyllis Cormack</i>, a chartered fishing boat which the Committee sent across the North Pacific in order to draw attention to the US testing of a 5 megaton bomb planned for September of that year. <i>Greenpeace</i> was the name given to the boat for the voyage and it would be the first of the many Greenpeace protests.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following the first voyage, key crew members decided to formally change the name of the Don't Make a Wave Committee to the Greenpeace Foundation. These decision makers included founders Bob Hunter, Rod Marining and <a href="/wiki/Ben_and_Dorothy_Metcalfe" class="mw-redirect" title="Ben and Dorothy Metcalfe">Ben Metcalfe</a> as well as Patrick Moore.<sup id="cite_ref-greenpeace.org_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace.org-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following US President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s cancellation of the remaining hydrogen bomb tests planned for Amchitka Island in early 1972, Greenpeace turned its attention to French atmospheric nuclear testing at <a href="/wiki/Mururoa" class="mw-redirect" title="Mururoa">Mururoa</a> Atoll in the South Pacific. In May 1972, Moore traveled to New York with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bohlen" title="Jim Bohlen">Jim Bohlen</a> and Marie Bohlen to lobby the key United Nations delegations from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Rim" title="Pacific Rim">Pacific Rim</a> countries involved. Moore then went to Europe together with Ben Metcalfe, Dorothy Metcalfe, Lyle Thurston and Rod Marining where they received an audience with Pope Paul VI and protested at <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre Dame de Paris">Notre Dame Cathedral</a> in Paris. In June, they attended the first <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">UN Conference on the Environment</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> where they convinced New Zealand to propose a vote condemning French nuclear testing, which passed with a strong majority.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore again crewed the <i>Phyllis Cormack</i> in 1975 during the first campaign to save whales, as Greenpeace met the Soviet whaling fleet off the coast of California. During the confrontation, film footage was caught of the Soviet whaling boat firing a harpoon over the heads of Greenpeace members in a <a href="/wiki/Zodiac_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Zodiac Group">Zodiac</a> inflatable and into the back of a female <a href="/wiki/Sperm_whale" title="Sperm whale">sperm whale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The film footage made the evening news the next day on all three US national networks, initiating Greenpeace's debut on the world media stage, and prompting a swift rise in public support of the charity.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Patrick Moore and Bob Hunter appeared on Dr. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wattenburg" title="Bill Wattenburg">Bill Wattenburg</a>'s talk radio show on <a href="/wiki/KGO_(AM)" title="KGO (AM)">KGO</a> and appealed for a lawyer to help them incorporate a branch office in San Francisco and to manage donations. David Tussman, a young lawyer, volunteered to help Moore, Hunter, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Spong" title="Paul Spong">Paul Spong</a> set up an office at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Mason" title="Fort Mason">Fort Mason</a>. The Greenpeace Foundation of America (since changed to Greenpeace USA), then became the major fundraising center for the expansion of Greenpeace worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-GPHistory_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPHistory-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency_of_Greenpeace_Foundation_in_Canada">Presidency of Greenpeace Foundation in Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presidency of Greenpeace Foundation in Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1977, Bob Hunter stepped down as president of the Greenpeace Foundation and Patrick Moore was elected president. He inherited an organization that was deeply in debt.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Greenpeace organizations began to form throughout North America, including cities such as Toronto, Montreal, Seattle, Portland, Los Angeles, Boston, and San Francisco. Not all of these offices accepted the authority of the founding organization in Canada. Moore's presidency and governance style proved controversial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for the whole sentence (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Moore and his chosen board in Vancouver called for two meetings to formalize his governance proposals. During this time David Tussman, together with the rest of the founders, early activists of Greenpeace, and the majority of Greenpeace staff-members announced that the board of the San Francisco group intended to separate Patrick Moore's Greenpeace Foundation from the rest of the Greenpeace movement. After efforts to settle the matter failed, the Greenpeace Foundation filed a civil lawsuit in San Francisco charging that the San Francisco group was in violation of trademark and copyright by using the Greenpeace name without permission of the Greenpeace Foundation.</p>
<p>The lawsuit was settled at a meeting on 10 October 1979, in the offices of lawyer David Gibbons in Vancouver. Attending were Moore, Hunter, <a href="/wiki/David_McTaggart" title="David McTaggart">David McTaggart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rex_Weyler" title="Rex Weyler">Rex Weyler</a>, and about six others. At this meeting it was agreed that Greenpeace International would be created. This meant that Greenpeace would remain a single organization rather than an amorphous collection of individual offices. McTaggart who had come to represent all the other Greenpeace groups against the Greenpeace Foundation, was named Chairman. Moore became President of Greenpeace Canada (the new name for Greenpeace Foundation) and a director of Greenpeace International. Other directors were appointed from the US, France, the UK, and the Netherlands. He served for nine years as President of Greenpeace Canada, as well as six years as a Director of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenpeace International">Greenpeace International</a>.</p>
<p>In 1985, Moore was on board the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Warrior_(1978)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow Warrior (1978)">Rainbow Warrior</a> <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_Rainbow_Warrior" title="Sinking of the Rainbow Warrior">when it was bombed and sunk by the French government</a>. He and other directors of Greenpeace International were greeting the ship off the coast of New Zealand on its way to protest French nuclear testing at <a href="/wiki/Mururoa_Atoll" class="mw-redirect" title="Mururoa Atoll">Mururoa Atoll</a>. Expedition photographer, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pereira" title="Fernando Pereira">Fernando Pereira</a>, was killed. Greenpeace's media presence peaked again.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Greenpeace">After Greenpeace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After Greenpeace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, after leaving Greenpeace over differences in policy, Moore established a family <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquaculture of salmon">salmon farming</a> business, Quatsino Seafarms, at his home in <a href="/wiki/Winter_Harbour,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter Harbour, British Columbia">Winter Harbour</a>. He commented that he had left Greenpeace because it "took a sharp turn to the political left" and "evolved into an organization of extremism and politically motivated agendas".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In this year he was also elected president of the British Columbia Salmon Farmers Association. From 1990-4 he was appointed to the British Columbia Round Table on the Environment and the Economy<sup id="cite_ref-gspm_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gspm-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and founded and chaired the B.C. Carbon Project. In 1991, he joined the board of the Forest Alliance of BC, an initiative of the CEOs of the major forest companies in British Columbia. As chair of the Sustainable Forestry Committee of the Forest Alliance he spent ten years developing the Principles of Sustainable Forestry, which were later adopted by much of the industry.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wired_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1991, Moore also founded Greenspirit to "promote sustainable development from a scientific environmental platform".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2002, Tom Tevlin and Trevor Figueiredo joined Moore in the formation of the environmental consultancy company Greenspirit Strategies Ltd.</p>
<p>Moore served for four years as Vice President of Environment for Waterfurnace International manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal</a> heat pumps.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2000, Moore published <i>Green Spirit - Trees are the Answer</i>, a photo-book on forests and the role they can play in solving some current environmental problems. He also made two appearances on <i><a href="/wiki/Penn_%26_Teller:_Bullshit!" title="Penn &amp; Teller: Bullshit!">Penn &amp; Teller: Bullshit!</a></i> in episodes <i>Environmental Hysteria</i> (2003) and <i>Endangered Species</i> (2005). In 2006, Moore became co-chair (with <a href="/wiki/Christine_Todd_Whitman" title="Christine Todd Whitman">Christine Todd Whitman</a>) of a new industry-funded initiative, the Clean and Safe Energy Coalition, which promotes increased use of nuclear energy.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2010, Moore was recruited to represent the Indonesian logging firm <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pulp_%26_Paper" title="Asia Pulp &amp; Paper">Asia Pulp &amp; Paper</a> (APP), a multi-national accused by activist groups of widespread and illegal rainforest clearance practices, although this is strongly disputed by Moore.<sup id="cite_ref-APP_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APP-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, Moore criticized what he saw as scare tactics and disinformation employed by some within the environmental movement, saying that the environmental movement "abandoned science and logic in favor of emotion and sensationalism."<sup id="cite_ref-herald_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-31">[31]</a></sup> Moore contends that for the environmental movement "most of the really serious problems have been dealt with", seeking now to "invent doom and gloom scenarios".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He suggests they romanticise peasant life as part of an anti-industrial campaign to prevent development in less-developed countries, which he describes as "anti-human".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-penn_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penn-34">[34]</a></sup> Moore was interviewed in the 2007 film documentary <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Global_Warming_Swindle" title="The Great Global Warming Swindle">The Great Global Warming Swindle</a></i>, in which he expressed similar views. In 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> reported on his writings for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> arguing against the theory that mankind was causing <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, noting his advocacy for the felling of tropical <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a> and the planting of <a href="/wiki/Genetically_engineered" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically engineered">genetically engineered</a> crops.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> He has expressed his positive views of logging on the Greenspirit website.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2010, Moore was commissioned by forestry giant <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pulp_and_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Pulp and Paper">Asia Pulp and Paper</a> to report on its logging activity in Indonesia's rainforests, resulting in a glowing review.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Moore was opposed to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> in the 1970s<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> when he "believed that nuclear energy was synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_holocaust" title="Nuclear holocaust">nuclear holocaust</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-28">[28]</a></sup> and "everything nuclear was evil",<sup id="cite_ref-GreenpeaceWrong_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenpeaceWrong-39">[39]</a></sup> but has since<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> come to be in favor of it.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wapo_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NuclearEnergyYesPlease_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NuclearEnergyYesPlease-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore is supported by the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Energy_Institute" title="Nuclear Energy Institute">Nuclear Energy Institute</a> (NEI), a national organization of pro-nuclear industries and in 2009 he chaired their <i>Clean and Safe Energy Coalition</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nuclear_Energy_Institute_article_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nuclear_Energy_Institute_article-43">[43]</a></sup> As chair, he suggested that the mainstream media and the environmentalist movement is not as opposed to nuclear energy as in decades past.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>He argues that any realistic plan to reduce reliance on <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions would require increased use of nuclear energy to supply <a href="/wiki/Baseload" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseload">baseload</a> power.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GreenpeaceWrong_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenpeaceWrong-39">[39]</a></sup> He has also criticized the costs and reliability of <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Moore calls global warming the "most difficult issue facing the scientific community today in terms of being able to actually predict with any kind of accuracy what's going to happen".<sup id="cite_ref-penn_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penn-34">[34]</a></sup> In 2006, he wrote to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> arguing there was "no scientific proof" that mankind was causing global warming<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and believes that it "has a much better correlation with changes in solar activity than CO<sub>2</sub> levels".<sup id="cite_ref-newsletter.co.uk_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsletter.co.uk-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore has stated that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and the melting of glaciers is not necessarily a negative event because it creates more <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> and the use of forest products drives up demand for wood and spurs the planting of more trees.<sup id="cite_ref-honolulu_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honolulu-48">[48]</a></sup> Rather than <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>, Moore advocates <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MassiveChange_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassiveChange-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Moore testified to the U.S. Congress on the subject of Global Warming. “There is no scientific proof that human emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2) are the dominant cause of the minor warming of the Earth’s atmosphere over the past 100 years,” according to Moore’s testimony. “Today, we live in an unusually cold period in the history of life on earth and there is no reason to believe that a warmer climate would be anything but beneficial for humans and the majority of other species.” Moore continued, "The fact that we had both higher temperatures and an ice age at a time when CO2 emissions were 10 times higher than they are today fundamentally contradicts the certainty that human-caused CO2 emissions are the main cause of global warming. When modern life evolved over 500 million years ago, CO2 was more than 10 times higher than today, yet life flourished at this time,” he added. “Then an Ice Age occurred 450 million years ago when CO2 was 10 times higher than today... Humans just aren’t capable of predicting global temperature changes".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>A March 2014 episode of the American program <i><a href="/wiki/Hannity" title="Hannity">Hannity</a></i> featured Moore making the statement that the Earth "has not warmed for the last 17 years" in a debate with <a href="/wiki/Pundit" title="Pundit">pundit</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Beckel" title="Bob Beckel">Bob Beckel</a>. <a href="/wiki/Politifact" class="mw-redirect" title="Politifact">Politifact</a> rated Moore's assertion "mostly false", remarking that a significant net warming over that time-frame had occurred even though the spread was relatively flat as well as that Moore cherry-picked the time-frame to obscure the overall heating trend.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 26, 2015 multiple videos were posted on <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.prageru.com/what-we-do">PragerU</a> with Moore presenting with the title of 'Co-Founder of Greenpeace'. Included is a 5:40 video titled <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.prageru.com/courses/environmental-science/why-i-left-greenpeace">"Why I Left Greenpeace"</a> that explains his version of his involvement and departure from Greenpeace. Other videos presented by Moore include <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.prageru.com/courses/environmental-science/what-they-havent-told-you-about-climate-change">"What They Haven't Told You about Climate Change"</a> (at 1:34 he states "Temperatures and carbon dioxide levels do not show a strong correlation." with a chart), "Are GMOs Good or Bad?", "The Truth about CO2" and "Trees Are the Answer".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_foods">Genetically modified foods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Genetically modified foods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, Moore addressed a <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology_Industry_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Biotechnology Industry Organization">Biotechnology Industry Organization</a> conference in <a href="/wiki/Waikiki" title="Waikiki">Waikiki</a> saying, "There's no getting away from the fact that over 6 billion people wake up each day on this planet with real needs for food, energy and materials", and need <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically engineered crops</a> to this end.<sup id="cite_ref-honolulu_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honolulu-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moore supports the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Golden_rice" title="Golden rice">golden rice</a> to prevent <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A_deficiency" title="Vitamin A deficiency">vitamin A deficiency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MassiveChange_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassiveChange-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects_of_glyphosate">Health effects of glyphosate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Health effects of glyphosate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During March 2015 in an interview by French investigative journalist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Moreira" title="Paul Moreira">Paul Moreira</a>, which was first broadcast on French television station <a href="/wiki/Canal%2B" title="Canal+">Canal+</a> in September 2014, Moore was asked about the safety of the herbicide <a href="/wiki/Glyphosate" title="Glyphosate">glyphosate</a>. Moore told <a href="/wiki/Paul_Moreira" title="Paul Moreira">Paul Moreira</a> that one "could drink a whole quart of it" without any harm. When Moore was challenged to drink a glass of the weedkiller, he refused, and ended the interview.<sup id="cite_ref-Monsanto-Lobbyist-Herbicide-Safe-to-Drink-Runs-Away-When-Offered-Some_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monsanto-Lobbyist-Herbicide-Safe-to-Drink-Runs-Away-When-Offered-Some-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Watch-GMO-Advocate-Claim-Weed-Killer-Safe-to-Drink-but-Refuse-to-Drink_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watch-GMO-Advocate-Claim-Weed-Killer-Safe-to-Drink-but-Refuse-to-Drink-53">[53]</a></sup> The interview came shortly after the release of a <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) report adding glyphosate to a list of <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen#International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="Carcinogen">probable carcinogens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moore-Roundup-Safe-Refuse-Drink_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-Roundup-Safe-Refuse-Drink-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Roundup-Safe-Enough-to-Drink-Refuse-Drink_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roundup-Safe-Enough-to-Drink-Refuse-Drink-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Carcinogenicity_of_tetrachlorvinphos.2C_parathion.2C_malathion.2C_diazinon.2C_and_glyphosate_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carcinogenicity_of_tetrachlorvinphos.2C_parathion.2C_malathion.2C_diazinon.2C_and_glyphosate-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore's views and change of stance (see above) have evoked controversy in environmentalist arenas. He is accused of having "abruptly turned his back on the environmental movement" and "being a mouthpiece for some of the very interests Greenpeace was founded to counter".<sup id="cite_ref-wired_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> His critics point out Moore's business relations with "polluters and <a href="/wiki/Clearfelling" class="mw-redirect" title="Clearfelling">clear-cutters</a>" through his consultancy.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-25">[25]</a></sup> Moore has earned his living since the early 1990s primarily by consulting for, and publicly speaking for a wide variety of corporations and lobby groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Energy_Institute" title="Nuclear Energy Institute">Nuclear Energy Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nuclear_Energy_Institute_article_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nuclear_Energy_Institute_article-43">[43]</a></sup> Monte Hummel, MScF, President, <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> Canada has claimed that Moore's book, <i>Pacific Spirit</i>, is a collection of "<a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a> and dubious assumptions."</p>
<p>The writer and environmental activist <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> has written critically of Moore's work with the Indonesian logging firm <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pulp_%26_Paper" title="Asia Pulp &amp; Paper">Asia Pulp &amp; Paper</a> (APP). Moore was hired as a consultant to write an environmental 'inspection report' on APP operations. According to Monbiot, Moore's company is not a monitoring firm and the consultants used were experts in public relations, not tropical ecology or Indonesian law. Monbiot has said that sections of the report were directly copied from an APP PR brochure.<sup id="cite_ref-APP_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APP-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Information_and_Resource_Service" title="Nuclear Information and Resource Service">Nuclear Information and Resource Service</a>, an anti-nuclear group, criticized Moore saying that his comment in 1976 that "it should be remembered that there are employed in the nuclear industry some very high-powered public relations organizations. One can no more trust them to tell the truth about nuclear power than about which brand of toothpaste will result in this apparently insoluble problem" was seen as forecasting his own future.<sup id="cite_ref-nirs.org_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nirs.org-59">[59]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Journalism_Review" title="Columbia Journalism Review">Columbia Journalism Review</a> editorial criticizes the press for uncritically printing "pro-nuclear songs" such as Moore's, citing his role as a paid spokesperson of the nuclear industry.<sup id="cite_ref-nirs.org_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nirs.org-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
