<p><b>Nuclear proliferation</b> is the spread of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>, fissionable material, and weapons-applicable nuclear technology and information to nations not recognized as "Nuclear Weapon States" by the <i>Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons</i>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a> or NPT. Proliferation has been opposed by many nations with and without nuclear weapons, the governments of which fear that more countries with nuclear weapons may increase the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear warfare</a> (up to and including the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Countervalue" title="Countervalue">countervalue</a>" targeting of civilians with nuclear weapons), de-stabilize international or regional relations, or infringe upon the national <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nation-state" class="mw-redirect" title="Nation-state">states</a>.</p>
<p>Four countries besides the five recognized Nuclear Weapons States have acquired, or are presumed to have acquired, nuclear weapons: <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. None of these four is a party to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">NPT</a>, although <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> acceded to the NPT in 1985, then withdrew in 2003 and conducted announced <a href="/wiki/List_of_nuclear_weapons_tests_of_North_Korea" title="List of nuclear weapons tests of North Korea">nuclear tests</a> in 2006, 2009, 2013 and 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> One critique of the NPT is that it is discriminatory in recognizing as nuclear weapon states only those countries that tested nuclear weapons before 1968 and requiring all other states joining the treaty to forswear nuclear weapons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Research into the development of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> was undertaken during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>), Germany, Japan, and the USSR. The United States was the first and is the only country to have used a nuclear weapon in war, when it used <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">two bombs against Japan</a> in August 1945. With their loss during the war, Germany and Japan ceased to be involved in any nuclear weapon research. In August 1949, the <a href="/wiki/RDS-1" title="RDS-1">USSR tested a nuclear weapon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_and_the_United_Kingdom" title="Nuclear weapons and the United Kingdom">United Kingdom tested a nuclear weapon</a> in October 1952. <a href="/wiki/France_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="France and weapons of mass destruction">France developed a nuclear weapon</a> in 1960. The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> detonated a nuclear weapon in 1964. India exploded a nuclear device in 1974, and Pakistan conducted a series of nuclear weapon tests in May 1998, following tests by India earlier that month. In 2006, North Korea conducted its <a href="/wiki/2006_North_Korean_nuclear_test" title="2006 North Korean nuclear test">first nuclear test</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-proliferation_efforts">Non-proliferation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Non-proliferation efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early efforts to prevent nuclear proliferation involved intense government secrecy, the wartime acquisition of known <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> stores (the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Combined_Development_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Combined Development Trust (page does not exist)">Combined Development Trust</a>), and at times even outright <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a>—such as the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_heavy_water_sabotage" title="Norwegian heavy water sabotage">bombing of a heavy-water facility</a> thought to be used for a German nuclear program. None of these efforts were explicitly public, because the weapon developments themselves were kept secret until the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombings_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" title="Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">bombing of Hiroshima</a>.</p>
<p>Earnest international efforts to promote nuclear non-proliferation began soon after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Truman Administration</a> proposed the <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Plan" title="Baruch Plan">Baruch Plan</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of 1946, named after <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Baruch" title="Bernard Baruch">Bernard Baruch</a>, America's first representative to the United Nations Atomic Energy Commission. The Baruch Plan, which drew heavily from the <a href="/wiki/Acheson%E2%80%93Lilienthal_Report" title="Acheson–Lilienthal Report">Acheson–Lilienthal Report</a> of 1946, proposed the verifiable dismantlement and destruction of the U.S. nuclear arsenal (which, at that time, was the only nuclear arsenal in the world) after all governments had cooperated successfully to accomplish two things: (1) the establishment of an "international atomic development authority," which would actually own and control all military-applicable nuclear materials and activities, and (2) the creation of a system of automatic sanctions, which not even the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">U.N. Security Council</a> could veto, and which would proportionately punish states attempting to acquire the capability to make nuclear weapons or fissile material.</p>
<p>Baruch's plea for the destruction of nuclear weapons invoked basic moral and religious intuitions. In one part of his address to the UN, Baruch said, "Behind the black portent of the new atomic age lies a hope which, seized upon with faith, can work out our salvation. If we fail, then we have damned every man to be the slave of Fear. Let us not deceive ourselves. We must elect World Peace or World Destruction.... We must answer the world's longing for peace and security."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With this remark, Baruch helped launch the field of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_ethics" title="Nuclear ethics">nuclear ethics</a>, to which many policy experts and scholars have contributed.</p>
<p>Although the Baruch Plan enjoyed wide international support, it failed to emerge from the UNAEC because the Soviet Union planned to veto it in the Security Council. Still, it remained official American policy until 1953, when <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">President Eisenhower</a> made his "<a href="/wiki/Atoms_for_Peace" title="Atoms for Peace">Atoms for Peace</a>" proposal before the U.N. General Assembly. Eisenhower's proposal led eventually to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) in 1957. Under the "Atoms for Peace" program thousands of scientists from around the world were educated in nuclear science and then dispatched home, where many later pursued secret weapons programs in their home country.<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Efforts to conclude an international agreement to limit the spread of nuclear weapons did not begin until the early 1960s, after four nations (the United States, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom and France) had acquired nuclear weapons (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons" title="List of states with nuclear weapons">List of states with nuclear weapons</a> for more information). Although these efforts stalled in the early 1960s, they renewed once again in 1964, after China detonated a nuclear weapon. In 1968, governments represented at the <a href="/wiki/Eighteen_Nation_Disarmament_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Eighteen Nation Disarmament Committee">Eighteen Nation Disarmament Committee</a> (ENDC) finished negotiations on the text of the NPT. In June 1968, the U.N. General Assembly endorsed the NPT with General Assembly Resolution 2373 (XXII), and in July 1968, the NPT opened for signature in Washington, DC, London and Moscow. The NPT entered into force in March 1970.</p>
<p>Since the mid-1970s, the primary focus of non-proliferation efforts has been to maintain, and even increase, international control over the <a href="/wiki/Fissile_material" title="Fissile material">fissile material</a> and specialized technologies necessary to build such devices because these are the most difficult and expensive parts of a nuclear weapons program. The main materials whose generation and distribution is controlled are highly <a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enriched uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. Other than the acquisition of these special materials, the scientific and technical means for weapons construction to develop rudimentary, but working, nuclear explosive devices are considered to be within the reach of industrialized nations.</p>
<p>Since its founding by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in 1957, the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) has promoted two, sometimes contradictory, missions: on the one hand, the Agency seeks to promote and spread internationally the use of civilian nuclear energy; on the other hand, it seeks to prevent, or at least detect, the diversion of civilian nuclear energy to nuclear weapons, nuclear explosive devices or purposes unknown. The IAEA now operates a safeguards system as specified under Article III of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a> (NPT) of 1968, which aims to ensure that civil stocks of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>, as well as facilities and technologies associated with these nuclear materials, are used only for peaceful purposes and do not contribute in any way to proliferation or nuclear weapons programs. It is often argued that proliferation of nuclear weapons to many other states has been prevented by the extension of assurances and mutual defence treaties to these states by nuclear powers, but other factors, such as national prestige, or specific historical experiences, also play a part in hastening or stopping nuclear proliferation.<sup id="cite_ref-test_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-test-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_use_technology">Dual use technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dual use technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dual-use_technology" title="Dual-use technology">Dual-use technology</a> refers to the possibility of military use of civilian <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> technology. Many technologies and materials associated with the creation of a nuclear power program have a dual-use capability, in that several stages of the nuclear fuel cycle allow diversion of nuclear materials for nuclear weapons. When this happens a nuclear power program can become a route leading to the atomic bomb or a public annex to a secret bomb program. The crisis over <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_program_of_Iran" title="Nuclear program of Iran">Iran’s nuclear activities</a> is a case in point.<sup id="cite_ref-dfall2009_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfall2009-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many UN and US agencies warn that building more nuclear reactors unavoidably increases nuclear proliferation risks.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A fundamental goal for American and global security is to minimize the proliferation risks associated with the expansion of nuclear power. If this development is "poorly managed or efforts to contain risks are unsuccessful, the nuclear future will be dangerous".<sup id="cite_ref-dfall2009_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfall2009-7">[7]</a></sup> For nuclear power programs to be developed and managed safely and securely, it is important that countries have domestic “<a href="/wiki/Good_governance" title="Good governance">good governance</a>” characteristics that will encourage proper nuclear operations and management:<sup id="cite_ref-dfall2009_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfall2009-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>These characteristics include low degrees of corruption (to avoid officials selling materials and technology for their own personal gain as occurred with the <a href="/wiki/A.Q._Khan" class="mw-redirect" title="A.Q. Khan">A.Q. Khan</a> smuggling network in Pakistan), high degrees of political stability (defined by the World Bank as “likelihood that the government will be destabilized or overthrown by unconstitutional or violent means, including politically-motivated violence and terrorism”), high governmental effectiveness scores (a World Bank aggregate measure of “the quality of the civil service and the degree of its independence from political pressures [and] the quality of policy formulation and implementation”), and a strong degree of regulatory competence.<sup id="cite_ref-dfall2009_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfall2009-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_cooperation">International cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International cooperation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_on_the_Non-Proliferation_of_Nuclear_Weapons">Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Non-Proliferation_of_Nuclear_Weapons" title="Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons">Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons</a></div>
<p>At present, 189 countries are States Parties to the <i>Treaty on the Nonproliferation of Nuclear Weapons</i>, more commonly known as the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty or NPT. These include the five Nuclear Weapons States (NWS) recognized by the NPT: the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>Notable non-signatories to the NPT are <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (the latter two have since tested nuclear weapons, while Israel is considered by most to be an unacknowledged nuclear weapons state). <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> was once a signatory but withdrew in January 2003. The legality of North Korea's withdrawal is debatable but as of 9 October 2006, North Korea clearly possesses the capability to make a nuclear explosive device.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Atomic_Energy_Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International Atomic Energy Agency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a></div>
<p>The IAEA was established on 29 July 1957 to help nations develop nuclear energy for peaceful purposes. Allied to this role is the administration of safeguards arrangements to provide assurance to the international community that individual countries are honoring their commitments under the treaty. Though established under its own international treaty, the IAEA reports to both the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">Security Council</a>.</p>
<p>The IAEA regularly inspects civil nuclear facilities to verify the accuracy of documentation supplied to it. The agency checks inventories, and samples and analyzes materials. Safeguards are designed to deter diversion of nuclear material by increasing the risk of early detection. They are complemented by controls on the export of sensitive technology from countries such as UK and United States through voluntary bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Suppliers_Group" title="Nuclear Suppliers Group">Nuclear Suppliers Group</a>. The main concern of the IAEA is that uranium not be enriched beyond what is necessary for commercial civil plants, and that <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> which is produced by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> not be refined into a form that would be suitable for bomb production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope_of_safeguards">Scope of safeguards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scope of safeguards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Brazilian%E2%80%93Argentine_Agency_for_Accounting_and_Control_of_Nuclear_Materials" title="Brazilian–Argentine Agency for Accounting and Control of Nuclear Materials">Brazilian–Argentine Agency for Accounting and Control of Nuclear Materials</a></div>
<p>Traditional safeguards are arrangements to account for and control the use of nuclear materials. This verification is a key element in the international system which ensures that uranium in particular is used only for peaceful purposes.</p>
<p>Parties to the NPT agree to accept technical safeguard measures applied by the IAEA. These require that operators of nuclear facilities maintain and declare detailed accounting records of all movements and transactions involving nuclear material. Over 550 facilities and several hundred other locations are subject to regular inspection, and their records and the nuclear material being audited. Inspections by the IAEA are complemented by other measures such as surveillance cameras and instrumentation.</p>
<p>The inspections act as an alert system providing a warning of the possible diversion of nuclear material from peaceful activities. The system relies on;</p>
<ol>
<li>Material Accountancy – tracking all inward and outward transfers and the flow of materials in any nuclear facility. This includes sampling and analysis of nuclear material, on-site inspections, and review and verification of operating records.</li>
<li>Physical Security – restricting access to nuclear materials at the site.</li>
<li>Containment and Surveillance – use of seals, automatic cameras and other instruments to detect unreported movement or tampering with nuclear materials, as well as spot checks on-site.</li>
</ol>
<p>All NPT non-weapons states must accept these full-scope safeguards. In the five weapons states plus the non-NPT states (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>), facility-specific safeguards apply. IAEA inspectors regularly visit these facilities to verify completeness and accuracy of records.</p>
<p>The terms of the NPT cannot be enforced by the IAEA itself, nor can nations be forced to sign the treaty. In reality, as shown in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, safeguards can be backed up by diplomatic, political and economic measures.</p>
<p>While traditional safeguards easily verified the correctness of formal declarations by suspect states, in the 1990s attention turned to what might not have been declared. While accepting safeguards at declared facilities, Iraq had set up elaborate equipment elsewhere in an attempt to enrich uranium to weapons grade. North Korea attempted to use research reactors (not commercial electricity-generating reactors) and a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">reprocessing</a> plant to produce some weapons-grade plutonium.</p>
<p>The weakness of the NPT regime lay in the fact that no obvious diversion of material was involved. The uranium used as fuel probably came from indigenous sources, and the nuclear facilities were built by the countries themselves without being declared or placed under safeguards. Iraq, as an NPT party, was obliged to declare all facilities but did not do so. Nevertheless, the activities were detected and brought under control using international diplomacy. In Iraq, a military defeat assisted this process.</p>
<p>In North Korea, the activities concerned took place before the conclusion of its NPT safeguards agreement. With North Korea, the promised provision of commercial power reactors appeared to resolve the situation for a time, but it later withdrew from the NPT and declared it had nuclear weapons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_Protocol">Additional Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Additional Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993 a program was initiated to strengthen and extend the classical safeguards system, and a model protocol was agreed by the IAEA Board of Governors 1997. The measures boosted the IAEA's ability to detect undeclared nuclear activities, including those with no connection to the civil fuel cycle.</p>
<p>Innovations were of two kinds. Some could be implemented on the basis of IAEA's existing legal authority through safeguards agreements and inspections. Others required further legal authority to be conferred through an <b>Additional Protocol</b>. This must be agreed by each non-weapons state with IAEA, as a supplement to any existing comprehensive safeguards agreement. Weapons states have agreed to accept the principles of the model additional protocol.</p>
<p>Key elements of the model Additional Protocol:</p>
<ul>
<li>The IAEA is to be given considerably more information on nuclear and nuclear-related activities, including R &amp; D, production of uranium and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> (regardless of whether it is traded), and nuclear-related imports and exports.</li>
<li>IAEA inspectors will have greater rights of access. This will include any suspect location, it can be at short notice (e.g., two hours), and the IAEA can deploy environmental sampling and remote monitoring techniques to detect illicit activities.</li>
<li>States must streamline administrative procedures so that IAEA inspectors get automatic visa renewal and can communicate more readily with IAEA headquarters.</li>
<li>Further evolution of safeguards is towards evaluation of each state, taking account of its particular situation and the kind of nuclear materials it has. This will involve greater judgement on the part of IAEA and the development of effective methodologies which reassure NPT States.</li>
</ul>
<p>As of 3 July 2015, 146 countries have signed Additional Protocols and 126 have brought them into force. The IAEA is also applying the measures of the Additional Protocol in Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of_Action" title="Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action">Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_program_of_Iran" title="Nuclear program of Iran">Iran</a> has agreed to implement its protocol provisionally. Among the leading countries that have not signed the Additional Protocol are Egypt, which says it will not sign until Israel accepts comprehensive IAEA safeguards,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and Brazil, which opposes making the protocol a requirement for international cooperation on enrichment and reprocessing,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but has not ruled out signing.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_safeguards">Limitations of safeguards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Limitations of safeguards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The greatest risk from nuclear weapons proliferation comes from countries which have not joined the NPT and which have significant unsafeguarded nuclear activities; <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> fall within this category. While safeguards apply to some of their activities, others remain beyond scrutiny.</p>
<p>A further concern is that countries may develop various sensitive <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> cycle facilities and research reactors under full safeguards and then subsequently opt out of the NPT. Bilateral agreements, such as insisted upon by <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> for sale of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, address this by including fallback provisions, but many countries are outside the scope of these agreements. If a nuclear-capable country does leave the NPT, it is likely to be reported by the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">IAEA</a> to the UN Security Council, just as if it were in breach of its safeguards agreement. Trade sanctions would then be likely.</p>
<p>IAEA safeguards can help ensure that <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> supplied as nuclear fuel and other nuclear supplies do not contribute to nuclear weapons proliferation. In fact, the worldwide application of those safeguards and the substantial world trade in uranium for nuclear <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> make the proliferation of nuclear weapons much less likely.</p>
<p>The Additional Protocol, once it is widely in force, will provide credible assurance that there are no undeclared nuclear materials or activities in the states concerned. This will be a major step forward in preventing nuclear proliferation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_developments">Other developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Suppliers_Group" title="Nuclear Suppliers Group">Nuclear Suppliers Group</a> communicated its guidelines, essentially a set of export rules, to the IAEA in 1978. These were to ensure that transfers of nuclear material or equipment would not be diverted to unsafeguarded nuclear fuel cycle or nuclear explosive activities, and formal government assurances to this effect were required from recipients. The Guidelines also recognised the need for physical protection measures in the transfer of sensitive facilities, technology and weapons-usable materials, and strengthened retransfer provisions. The group began with seven members&#160;– the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>&#160;– but now includes 46 countries including all five <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear weapons states">nuclear weapons states</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Framework_for_Nuclear_Energy_Cooperation" title="International Framework for Nuclear Energy Cooperation">International Framework for Nuclear Energy Cooperation</a> is an international project involving 25 partner countries, 28 observer and candidate partner countries, and the International Atomic Energy Agency, the Generation IV International Forum, and the European Commission. Its goal is to "[..] provide competitive, commercially-based services as an alternative to a state’s development of costly, proliferation-sensitive facilities, and address other issues associated with the safe and secure management of used fuel and radioactive waste."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Kenneth D. Bergeron's <i>Tritium on Ice: The Dangerous New Alliance of Nuclear Weapons and Nuclear Power</i>, tritium is not classified as a "special nuclear material" but rather as a by-product. It is seen as an important litmus test on the seriousness of the United States' intention to nuclear disarm. This radioactive super-heavy <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_hydrogen" title="Isotopes of hydrogen">hydrogen isotope</a> is used to boost the efficiency of fissile materials in nuclear weapons. The United States resumed tritium production in 2003 for the first time in 15 years. This could indicate that there is a potential nuclear arm stockpile replacement since the isotope naturally <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">decays</a>.</p>
<p>In May 1995, NPT parties reaffirmed their commitment to a Fissile Materials Cut-off Treaty to prohibit the production of any further fissile material for weapons. This aims to complement the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Test_Ban_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty</a> of 1996 (not entered into force as of 2011) and to codify commitments made by the United States, the UK, France and Russia to cease production of weapons material, as well as putting a similar ban on China. This treaty will also put more pressure on Israel, India and Pakistan to agree to international verification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 9 August 2005, <a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">Ayatollah</a> <a href="/wiki/Ali_Khamenei" title="Ali Khamenei">Ali Khamenei</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">fatwa</a> forbidding the production, stockpiling and use of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>. Khamenei's official statement was made at the meeting of the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) in Vienna.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of February 2006 <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> formally announced that <a href="/wiki/Uranium_enrichment" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium enrichment">uranium enrichment</a> within their borders has continued. Iran claims it is for peaceful purposes but the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> claim the purpose is for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> research and construction.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NPT_Non_Signatories">NPT Non Signatories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: NPT Non Signatories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> have been "threshold" countries in terms of the international non-proliferation regime. They possess or are quickly capable of assembling one or more nuclear weapons. They have remained outside the 1970 NPT. They are thus largely excluded from trade in nuclear plant or materials, except for safety-related devices for a few safeguarded facilities.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pokhran-II" title="Pokhran-II">In May 1998 India</a> and Pakistan each exploded several nuclear devices underground. This heightened concerns regarding an arms race between them, with <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> involving the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, an acknowledged nuclear weapons state. Both countries are opposed to the NPT as it stands, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> has consistently attacked the Treaty since its inception in 1970 labeling it as a lopsided treaty in favor of the nuclear powers.</p>
<p>Relations between the two countries are tense and hostile, and the risks of nuclear conflict between them have long been considered quite high. <a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a> is a prime cause of bilateral tension, its sovereignty being in dispute since 1948. There is persistent low level bilateral military conflict due to alleged backing of insurgency by Pakistan in India, and correspondingly Indian sponsoring of terrorism in Pakistan <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Indian_government_sponsoring_MQM_in_Pakistan_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_government_sponsoring_MQM_in_Pakistan-17">[17]</a></sup> along with the disputed status of Kashmir.</p>
<p>Both engaged in a <a href="/wiki/Conventional_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Conventional arms">conventional arms</a> race in the 1980s, including sophisticated technology and equipment capable of delivering nuclear weapons. In the 1990s the arms race quickened. In 1994 India reversed a four-year trend of reduced allocations for defence, and despite its much smaller economy, Pakistan was expected to push its own expenditures yet higher. Both have lost their patrons: India, the former USSR, and Pakistan, the United States.</p>
<p>But it is the growth and modernization of China's nuclear arsenal and its assistance with Pakistan's nuclear power programme and, reportedly, with missile technology, which exacerbate Indian concerns. In particular, as viewed by Indian strategists, Pakistan is aided by China's <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="People's Liberation Army">People's Liberation Army</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation">

<p>Nuclear power for civil use is well established in India. Its civil nuclear strategy has been directed towards complete independence in the nuclear fuel cycle, necessary because of its outspoken rejection of the NPT. This self-sufficiency extends from uranium exploration and mining through fuel fabrication, heavy water production, reactor design and construction, to reprocessing and waste management. It has a small fast <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactor</a> and is planning a much larger one. It is also developing technology to utilise its abundant resources of thorium as a nuclear fuel.</p>
<p>India has 14 small nuclear power reactors in commercial operation, two larger ones under construction, and ten more planned. The 14 operating ones (2548 MWe total) comprise:</p>
<ul>
<li>two 150 MWe BWRs from the United States, which started up in 1969, now use locally enriched uranium and are under safeguards,</li>
<li>two small Canadian PHWRs (1972 &amp; 1980), also under safeguards, and</li>
<li>ten local PHWRs based on Canadian designs, two of 150 and eight 200 MWe.</li>
<li>two new 540 MWe and two 700 MWe plants at Tarapur (known as TAPP: <a href="/wiki/Tarapur_Atomic_Power_Station" title="Tarapur Atomic Power Station">Tarapur Atomic Power Station</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>The two under construction and two of the planned ones are 450 MWe versions of these 200 MWe domestic products. Construction has been seriously delayed by financial and technical problems. In 2001 a final agreement was signed with Russia for the country's first large nuclear power plant, comprising two VVER-1000 reactors, under a Russian-financed US$3 billion contract. The first unit is due to be commissioned in 2007. A further two Russian units are under consideration for the site. Nuclear power supplied 3.1% of India's electricity in 2000.</p>
<p>Its weapons material appears to come from a Canadian-designed 40MW "research" reactor which started up in 1960, well before the NPT, and a 100MW indigenous unit in operation since 1985. Both use local uranium, as India does not import any nuclear fuel. It is estimated that India may have built up enough weapons-grade plutonium for a hundred nuclear warheads.</p>
<p>It is widely believed that the nuclear programs of India and Pakistan used CANDU reactors to produce fissionable materials for their weapons; however, this is not accurate. Both Canada (by supplying the 40 MW research reactor) and the United States (by supplying 21 tons of heavy water) supplied India with the technology necessary to create a nuclear weapons program, dubbed CIRUS (Canada-India Reactor, United States). Canada sold India the reactor on the condition that the reactor and any by-products would be <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nci.org/06nci/04/Canada-India%20CIRUS%20agreement.htm">"employed for peaceful purposes only."</a>. Similarly, the United States sold India heavy water for use in the reactor <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nci.org/06nci/04/US-India%20CIRUS%20agreement.htm">"only... in connection with research into and the use of atomic energy for peaceful purposes"</a>. India, in violation of these agreements, used the Canadian-supplied reactor and American-supplied heavy water to produce plutonium for their first nuclear explosion, <a href="/wiki/Smiling_Buddha" title="Smiling Buddha">Smiling Buddha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nwa-inwptb_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwa-inwptb-18">[18]</a></sup> The Indian government controversially justified this, however, by claiming that Smiling Buddha was a "peaceful nuclear explosion."</p>
<p>The country has at least three other research reactors including the tiny one which is exploring the use of thorium as a nuclear fuel, by breeding fissile U-233. In addition, an advanced heavy-water thorium cycle is under development.</p>
<p>India <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear testing">exploded a nuclear device</a> in 1974, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Smiling_Buddha" title="Smiling Buddha">Smiling Buddha</a> test, which it has consistently claimed was for peaceful purposes. Others saw it as a response to China's nuclear weapons capability. It was then universally perceived, notwithstanding official denials, to possess, or to be able to quickly assemble, nuclear weapons. In 1999 it deployed its own <a href="/wiki/Agni-II" title="Agni-II">medium-range missile</a> and has developed an <a href="/wiki/Agni-III" title="Agni-III">intermediate-range missile</a> capable of reaching targets in China's industrial heartland.</p>
<p>In 1995 the United States quietly intervened to head off a proposed nuclear test. However, in 1998 there were five more tests in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Shakti" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Shakti">Operation Shakti</a>. These were unambiguously military, including one claimed to be of a sophisticated thermonuclear device, and their declared purpose was "to help in the design of nuclear weapons of different yields and different delivery systems".</p>
<p>Indian security policies are driven by:</p>
<ul>
<li>its determination to be recognized as a dominant power in the region</li>
<li>its increasing concern with China's expanding nuclear weapons and missile delivery programmes</li>
<li>its concern with Pakistan's capability to deliver nuclear weapons deep into India</li>
</ul>
<p>It perceives nuclear weapons as a cost-effective political counter to China's nuclear and conventional weaponry, and the effects of its nuclear weapons policy in provoking Pakistan is, by some accounts, considered incidental. India has had an unhappy relationship with China. After an uneasy ceasefire ended the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Indian_War" title="Sino-Indian War">1962 war</a>, relations between the two nations were frozen until 1998. Since then a degree of high-level contact has been established and a few elementary confidence-building measures put in place. China still occupies some territory which it captured during the aforementioned war, claimed by India, and India still occupies some territory claimed by China. Its nuclear weapon and missile support for Pakistan is a major bone of contention.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> met with India <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_India" title="Prime Minister of India">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Manmohan_Singh" title="Manmohan Singh">Manmohan Singh</a> to discuss India's involvement with nuclear weapons. The two countries agreed that the United States would give nuclear power assistance to India.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Over the several years, the Nuclear power infrastructure has been well established by Pakistan which is dedicated for the industrial and <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Pakistan" title="Economy of Pakistan">economic development</a> of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Pakistan_Atomic_Energy_Commission_report_to_IAEA_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pakistan_Atomic_Energy_Commission_report_to_IAEA-20">[20]</a></sup> Its current <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_programme%E2%80%932050" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear power programme–2050">nuclear policy</a> is directed and aimed to promote the socio-economic development of the people as a "foremost priority";<sup id="cite_ref-Pakistan_Tribune_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pakistan_Tribune-21">[21]</a></sup> and to fulfill the energy, economic, and industrial needs from the nuclear sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Pakistan_Tribune_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pakistan_Tribune-21">[21]</a></sup> Currently, there are three operational mega-commercial nuclear power plants while three larger ones are under construction.<sup id="cite_ref-Pakistan_Atomic_Energy_Commission_report_to_IAEA_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pakistan_Atomic_Energy_Commission_report_to_IAEA-20">[20]</a></sup> The nuclear power supplies 787MW (roughly ~3.6%) of electricity as of 2012, and the country has projected to produce 8800MW electricity by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-Directorate-General_for_Nuclear_Power_Generation_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Directorate-General_for_Nuclear_Power_Generation-22">[22]</a></sup> Infrastructure established by the IAEA and the U.S. in the 1950s–1960s were based on peaceful research and development and economic prosperity of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the civil-sector nuclear power was established in the 1950s, the country has an active nuclear weapons program which was started in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995-23">[23]</a></sup> The bomb program has its roots after <a href="/wiki/East_Pakistan" title="East Pakistan">East-Pakistan</a> gained its <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_Liberation_War" title="Bangladesh Liberation War">independence</a> as <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> after <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s successful <a href="/wiki/Indian_Armed_Forces" title="Indian Armed Forces">intervention</a> led to a <a href="/wiki/Instrument_of_Surrender_(1971)" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrument of Surrender (1971)">decisive victory</a> on <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawn_Newspapers.2C_1995-23">[23]</a></sup> This large-scale but clandestine atomic bomb project was directed towards the development of ingenious development of reactor and military-grade plutonium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1974, when India surprised the outer world with its successful detonation of its own bomb, codename <i><a href="/wiki/Smiling_Buddha" title="Smiling Buddha">Smiling Buddha</a></i>, it became "imperative for Pakistan" to pursue the weapons research.<sup id="cite_ref-Dawn_News_Interviews_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawn_News_Interviews-24">[24]</a></sup> According to leading scientist in the program, it became clear once India detonated the bomb, "<a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_Third_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Newton's Third Law">Newton's third law</a>" came into "operation", from then on it was a classic case of "<a href="/wiki/Action_and_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Action and reaction">action and reaction</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Dawn_News_Interviews_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dawn_News_Interviews-24">[24]</a></sup> Earlier efforts were directed towards mastering the plutonium technology from France, but plutonium route was partially slowed down when the plan was failed after the U.S. intervention to cancel the project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Contrary to popular perception, Pakistan did not forego the "plutonium" route and covertly continued its indegenious research under Munir Khan and it succeeded with plutonium route in the early 1980s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Reacting on India's nuclear test (<a href="/wiki/Smiling_Buddha" title="Smiling Buddha">Smiling Buddha</a>), Bhutto and the country's elite political and military science circle sensed this test as final and dangerous anticipation to Pakistan's "moral and physical existence."<sup id="cite_ref-Time_Magazine.2C_1985_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Magazine.2C_1985-25">[25]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Aziz_Ahmed" title="Aziz Ahmed">Aziz Ahmed</a> on his side, Bhutto launched a serious diplomatic offense and aggressively maintained at the session of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Pakistan was exposed to a kind of "<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear threat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_blackmail" title="Nuclear blackmail">blackmail</a>" unparalleled elsewhere. ... If the world's community failed to provide political insurance to Pakistan and other countries against the nuclear blackmail, these countries would be constraint to launch atomic bomb programs of their own! ... [A]ssurances provided by the United Nations were not "Enough!"...&#160;</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, <small>statement written in "<i>Eating Grass</i>"</small>, source<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_University_Press_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_University_Press-26">[26]</a></sup></cite></div>

<p>After 1974, <a href="/wiki/Zulfikar_Ali_Bhutto" title="Zulfikar Ali Bhutto">Bhutto's government</a> redoubled its effort, this time equally focused on uranium and plutonium.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_Times.2C_2007_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Times.2C_2007-27">[27]</a></sup> Pakistan had established science directorates in almost all of her <a href="/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_Pakistan" title="List of diplomatic missions of Pakistan">embassies</a> in the important countries of the world, with theoretical physicist S.A. Butt being the director.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_Times.2C_2007_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Times.2C_2007-27">[27]</a></sup> Abdul Qadeer Khan then established a network through Dubai to smuggle URENCO technology to <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Research_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering Research Laboratories">Engineering Research Laboratories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bomb_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bomb-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cbsnews_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbsnews-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Earlier, he worked with <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fysisch_Dynamisch_Onderzoekslaboratoriu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fysisch Dynamisch Onderzoekslaboratoriu (page does not exist)">Physics Dynamics Research Laboratories</a></i> (FDO), a subsidiary of the Dutch firm VMF-Stork based in Amsterdam. Later after joining, the <a href="/wiki/Urenco" class="mw-redirect" title="Urenco">Urenco</a>, he had access through photographs and documents of the technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> Against the popular perception, the technology that A.Q. Khan had brought from Urenco was based on first generation civil rector technology, filled with many serious technical errors, though it was authentic and vital link for centrifuge project of the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After the British Government stopped the British subsidiary of the American Emerson Electric Co. from shipping the components to Pakistan, he describes his frustration with a supplier from Germany as: "That man from the German team was unethical.<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> When he did not get the order from us, he wrote a letter to a <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> member and questions were asked in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">[British] Parliament</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1978, his efforts were paid off and made him into a national hero.<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1981, as a tribute, President General <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Zia-ul-Haq" title="Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq">Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq</a>, renamed the <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Research_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering Research Laboratories">research institute</a> after <a href="/wiki/A._Q._Khan_Research_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="A. Q. Khan Research Laboratories">his name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catherine_Collins_and_Douglas_Frantz_2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1996, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Pakistan" title="Prime Minister of Pakistan">Prime minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Benazir_Bhutto" title="Benazir Bhutto">Benazir Bhutto</a> made it clear that "if India conducts a nuclear test, Pakistan could be forced to "follow suit".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NTI.3B_Pakistan_Television_.28PTV.29_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTI.3B_Pakistan_Television_.28PTV.29-35">[35]</a></sup> In 1997, her statement was echoed by Prime minister <a href="/wiki/Nawaz_Sharif" title="Nawaz Sharif">Nawaz Sharif</a> who maintained to the fact that: "Since 1972, [P]akistan had progressed significantly, and we have left that stage (developmental) far behind. Pakistan will not be made a "hostage" to India by signing the CTBT, before (India).!"<sup id="cite_ref-NTI_publications.2C_September_1997_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTI_publications.2C_September_1997-36">[36]</a></sup> In May 1998, within weeks of India's nuclear tests, Pakistan announced that it had conducted six underground tests in the Chagai Hills, five on the 28th and one on the 30th of that month. Seismic events consistent with these claims were recorded.</p>
<p>In 2004, the revelation of A.Q. Khan's efforts led the exposure of many defunct European consortium who defied export restrictions in the 1970s, and many of defunct Dutch companies exported thousands of centrifuges to Pakistan as early as 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-Craig_S._Smith_2004_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craig_S._Smith_2004-37">[37]</a></sup> Many centrifuge components were apparently manufactured in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> <a href="/wiki/Scomi_Precision_Engineering_nuclear_scandal" title="Scomi Precision Engineering nuclear scandal">Scomi Precision Engineering</a> with the assistance of South Asian and German companies, and used a UAE-based computer company as a false front.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was widely believed to have direct involvement of the government of Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29-39">[39]</a></sup> This claim could not be verified due to the refusal of the government of Pakistan to allow IAEA to interview the alleged head of the nuclear black market, who happened to be no other than A.Q. Khan. Confessing his crimes later a month on national television, he bailed out the government by taking full responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29-39">[39]</a></sup> Independent investigation conducted by <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies" title="International Institute for Strategic Studies">IISS</a> confirmed that he had control over the import-export deals, and his acquisition activities were largely unsupervised by Pakistan governmental authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29-39">[39]</a></sup> All of his activities went undetected for several years. He duly confessed of running the atomic proliferation ring from Pakistan to Iran and North Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> He was immediately given presidential immunity.<sup id="cite_ref-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29-39">[39]</a></sup> Exact nature of the involvement at the governmental level is still unclear, but the manner in which the government acted cast doubt on the sincerity of Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_International_Institute_For_Strategic_Studies_.28IISS.29-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Korea">North Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: North Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North_Korea_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction#Nuclear_weapons" title="North Korea and weapons of mass destruction">North Korea and weapons of mass destruction §&#160;Nuclear weapons</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Peoples_Republic_of_Korea" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Peoples Republic of Korea">Democratic Peoples Republic of Korea</a> (or better known as <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>), joined the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Non-Proliferation_of_Nuclear_Weapons" title="Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons">NPT</a> in 1985 and had subsequently signed a <a href="/wiki/IAEA_safeguards" title="IAEA safeguards">safeguards</a> agreement with the IAEA. However, it was believed that North Korea was diverting plutonium extracted from the fuel of its reactor at Yongbyon, for use in nuclear weapons. The subsequent confrontation with IAEA on the issue of inspections and suspected violations, resulted in North Korea threatening to withdraw from the NPT in 1993. This eventually led to negotiations with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Agreed_Framework_(1994)" class="mw-redirect" title="Agreed Framework (1994)">Agreed Framework of 1994</a>, which provided for IAEA safeguards being applied to its reactors and spent fuel rods. These spent fuel rods were sealed in canisters by the United States to prevent North Korea from extracting plutonium from them. North Korea had to therefore freeze its plutonium programme.</p>
<p>During this period, Pakistan-North Korea <a href="/wiki/North_Korea%E2%80%93Pakistan_relations" title="North Korea–Pakistan relations">cooperation</a> in missile technology transfer was being established. A high level delegation of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_military" class="mw-redirect" title="Pakistan military">Pakistan military</a> visited North Korea in August–September 1992, reportedly to discuss the supply of missile technology to Pakistan. In 1993, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Pakistan" title="Prime Minister of Pakistan">PM</a> <a href="/wiki/Benazir_Bhutto" title="Benazir Bhutto">Benazir Bhutto</a> repeatedly traveled to China, and the paid <a href="/wiki/State_visit" title="State visit">state visit</a> to North Korea. The visits are believed to be related to the subsequent acquisition technology to developed its Ghauri system by Pakistan. During the period 1992–1994, A.Q. Khan was reported to have visited North Korea thirteen times. The missile cooperation program with North Korea was under <a href="/wiki/A._Q._Khan_Research_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="A. Q. Khan Research Laboratories">Dr. A. Q. Khan Research Laboratories</a>. At this time China was under U.S. pressure not to supply the <a href="/wiki/Dongfeng_(missile)" title="Dongfeng (missile)">M Dongfeng</a> series of missiles to Pakistan. It is believed by experts that possibly with Chinese connivance and facilitation, the latter was forced to approach North Korea for missile transfers. Reports indicate that North Korea was willing to supply missile sub-systems including rocket motors, inertial guidance systems, control and testing equipment for US$50 million.</p>
<p>It is not clear what North Korea got in return. Joseph S. Bermudez Jr. in <i><a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Defence_Weekly" title="Jane's Defence Weekly">Jane's Defence Weekly</a></i> (27 November 2002) reports that Western analysts had begun to question what North Korea received in payment for the missiles; many suspected it was the nuclear technology. The KRL was in charge of both uranium program and also of the missile program with North Korea. It is therefore likely during this period that cooperation in nuclear technology between Pakistan and North Korea was initiated. Western intelligence agencies began to notice exchange of personnel, technology and components between KRL and entities of the North Korean 2nd Economic Committee (responsible for weapons production).</p>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> report on 18 October 2002 quoted U.S. intelligence officials having stated that Pakistan was a major supplier of critical equipment to North Korea. The report added that equipment such as gas centrifuges appeared to have been "part of a barter deal" in which North Korea supplied Pakistan with missiles. Separate reports indicate (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Times" title="The Washington Times">The Washington Times</a></i>, 22 November 2002) that U.S. intelligence had as early as 1999 picked up signs that North Korea was continuing to develop nuclear arms. Other reports also indicate that North Korea had been working covertly to develop an enrichment capability for nuclear weapons for at least five years and had used technology obtained from Pakistan (Washington Times, 18 October 2002).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> is also thought to possess an arsenal of potentially up to several hundred nuclear warheads based on estimates of the amount of fissile material produced by Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This has never been openly confirmed or denied however, due to Israel's <a href="/wiki/Policy_of_deliberate_ambiguity" title="Policy of deliberate ambiguity">policy of deliberate ambiguity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>An Israeli nuclear installation is located about ten kilometers to the south of <a href="/wiki/Dimona" title="Dimona">Dimona</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Negev_Nuclear_Research_Center" title="Negev Nuclear Research Center">Negev Nuclear Research Center</a>. Its construction commenced in 1958, with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> assistance. The official reason given by the Israeli and French governments was to build a nuclear reactor to power a "<a href="/wiki/Desalination_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Desalination plant">desalination plant</a>", in order to "green the Negev". The purpose of the Dimona plant is widely assumed to be the manufacturing of nuclear weapons, and the majority of defense experts have concluded that it does in fact do that.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the Israeli government refuses to confirm or deny this publicly, a policy it refers to as "ambiguity".</p>
<p>Norway sold 20 tonnes of heavy water needed for the reactor to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in 1959 and 1960 in a secret deal. There were no "safeguards" required in this deal to prevent usage of the heavy water for non-peaceful purposes. The British newspaper <i>Daily Express</i> accused Israel of working on a bomb in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> intelligence community discovered the purpose of the Dimona plant in the early 1960s, it demanded that Israel agree to international inspections. Israel agreed, but on a condition that U.S., rather than IAEA, inspectors were used, and that Israel would receive advanced notice of all inspections.</p>
<p>Some claim that because Israel knew the schedule of the inspectors' visits, it was able to hide the alleged purpose of the site from the inspectors by installing temporary false walls and other devices before each inspection. The inspectors eventually informed the U.S. government that their inspections were useless due to Israeli restrictions on what areas of the facility they could inspect. In 1969, the United States terminated the inspections.</p>
<p>In 1986, <a href="/wiki/Mordechai_Vanunu" title="Mordechai Vanunu">Mordechai Vanunu</a>, a former technician at the Dimona plant, revealed to the media some evidence of Israel's nuclear program. Israeli agents arrested him from Italy, drugged him and transported him to Israel, and an Israeli court then tried him in secret on charges of <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a> and <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and sentenced him to eighteen years imprisonment. He was freed on 21 April 2004, but was severely limited by the Israeli government. He was arrested again on 11 November 2004, though formal charges were not immediately filed.</p>
<p>Comments on photographs taken by <a href="/wiki/Mordechai_Vanunu" title="Mordechai Vanunu">Mordechai Vanunu</a> inside the <a href="/wiki/Negev_Nuclear_Research_Center" title="Negev Nuclear Research Center">Negev Nuclear Research Center</a> have been made by prominent scientists. British nuclear weapons scientist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Barnaby" title="Frank Barnaby">Frank Barnaby</a>, who questioned Vanunu over several days, estimated Israel had enough plutonium for about 150 weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-barnaby-opinion_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnaby-opinion-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ted_Taylor_(physicist)" title="Ted Taylor (physicist)">Ted Taylor</a>, a bomb designer employed by the United States has confirmed the several hundred warhead estimate based on Vanunu's photographs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to Lieutenant Colonel Warner D. Farr in a report to the USAF Counterproliferation Center while France was previously a leader in nuclear research "Israel and France were at a similar level of expertise after WWII, and Israeli scientists could make significant contributions to the French effort."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 1986 <a href="/wiki/Francis_Perrin" title="Francis Perrin">Francis Perrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commissariat_%C3%A0_l%27%C3%89nergie_Atomique" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissariat à l'Énergie Atomique">French high-commissioner for atomic energy</a> from 1951 to 1970 stated that in 1949 Israeli scientists were invited to the <a href="/wiki/Saclay" title="Saclay">Saclay</a> nuclear research facility, this cooperation leading to a joint effort including sharing of knowledge between French and Israeli scientists especially those with knowledge from the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Israel_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Israel and weapons of mass destruction">Israel and weapons of mass destruction</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_arms_control_in_South_Asia">Nuclear arms control in South Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Nuclear arms control in South Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lahore_Summit" class="mw-redirect" title="Lahore Summit">Lahore Summit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agra_summit" title="Agra summit">Agra summit</a></div>
<p>The public stance of the two states on non-proliferation differs markedly. Pakistan has initiated a series of regional security proposals. It has repeatedly proposed a nuclear free zone in South Asia and has proclaimed its willingness to engage in nuclear disarmament and to sign the Non-Proliferation Treaty if India would do so. It has endorsed a United States proposal for a regional five power conference to consider non-proliferation in South Asia.</p>
<p>India has taken the view that solutions to regional security issues should be found at the international rather than the regional level, since its chief concern is with China. It therefore rejects Pakistan's proposals.</p>
<p>Instead, the '<a href="/w/index.php?title=Gandhi_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gandhi Plan (page does not exist)">Gandhi Plan</a>', put forward in 1988, proposed the revision of the Non-Proliferation Treaty, which it regards as inherently discriminatory in favor of the nuclear-weapon States, and a timetable for complete nuclear weapons disarmament. It endorsed early proposals for a Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty and for an international convention to ban the production of highly enriched uranium and plutonium for weapons purposes, known as the 'cut-off' convention.</p>
<p>The United States for some years, especially under the <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Clinton</a> administration, pursued a variety of initiatives to persuade India and Pakistan to abandon their nuclear weapons programs and to accept comprehensive international safeguards on all their nuclear activities. To this end, the Clinton administration proposed a conference of the five nuclear-weapon states, Japan, Germany, India and Pakistan.</p>
<p>India refused this and similar previous proposals, and countered with demands that other potential weapons states, such as Iran and North Korea, should be invited, and that regional limitations would only be acceptable if they were accepted equally by China. The United States would not accept the participation of Iran and North Korea and these initiatives have lapsed.</p>
<p>Another, more recent approach, centers on 'capping' the production of fissile material for weapons purposes, which would hopefully be followed by 'roll back'. To this end, India and the United States jointly sponsored a UN General Assembly resolution in 1993 calling for negotiations for a 'cut-off' convention. Should India and Pakistan join such a convention, they would have to agree to halt the production of fissile materials for weapons and to accept international verification on their relevant nuclear facilities (enrichment and reprocessing plants). It appears that India is now prepared to join negotiations regarding such a Cut-off Treaty, under the UN Conference on Disarmament.</p>
<p>Bilateral confidence-building measures between India and Pakistan to reduce the prospects of confrontation have been limited. In 1990 each side ratified a treaty not to attack the other's nuclear installations, and at the end of 1991 they provided one another with a list showing the location of all their nuclear plants, even though the respective lists were regarded as not being wholly accurate. Early in 1994 India proposed a bilateral agreement for a 'no first use' of nuclear weapons and an extension of the 'no attack' treaty to cover civilian and industrial targets as well as nuclear installations.</p>
<p>Having promoted the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Test_Ban_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty</a> since 1954, India dropped its support in 1995 and in 1996 attempted to block the Treaty. Following the 1998 tests the question has been reopened and both Pakistan and India have indicated their intention to sign the CTBT. Indian ratification may be conditional upon the five weapons states agreeing to specific reductions in nuclear arsenals. The UN Conference on Disarmament has also called upon both countries "to accede without delay to the Non-Proliferation Treaty", presumably as non-weapons states.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2004 and 2005, Egypt disclosed past undeclared nuclear activities and material to the IAEA. In 2007 and 2008, high enriched and low enriched uranium particles were found in environmental samples taken in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-SS2008_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS2008-50">[50]</a></sup> In 2008, the IAEA states Egypt's statements were consistent with its own findings.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In May 2009, <i>Reuters</i> reported that the IAEA was conducting further investigation in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iran_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction#Nuclear_weapons" title="Iran and weapons of mass destruction">Iran and weapons of mass destruction §&#160;Nuclear weapons</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_program_of_Iran" title="Nuclear program of Iran">Nuclear program of Iran</a></div>

href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">IAEA</a> Board of Governors voted in a rare non-consensus decision to find Iran in non-compliance with its NPT Safeguards Agreement and to report that non-compliance to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">UN Security Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IAEAIran2005_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAEAIran2005-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Up to the late 1980s it was generally assumed that any undeclared nuclear activities would have to be based on the diversion of nuclear material from safeguards. States acknowledged the possibility of nuclear activities entirely separate from those covered by safeguards, but it was assumed they would be detected by national intelligence activities. There was no particular effort by IAEA to attempt to detect them.</p>
<p>Iraq had been making efforts to secure a nuclear potential since the 1960s. In the late 1970s a specialised plant, <a href="/wiki/Osiraq" class="mw-redirect" title="Osiraq">Osiraq</a>, was constructed near Baghdad. The plant was attacked during the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a> and was <a href="/wiki/Operation_Opera" title="Operation Opera">destroyed by Israeli bombers</a> in June 1981.</p>
<p>Not until the 1990 NPT Review Conference did some states raise the possibility of making more use of (for example) provisions for "special inspections" in existing NPT Safeguards Agreements. Special inspections can be undertaken at locations other than those where safeguards routinely apply, if there is reason to believe there may be undeclared material or activities.</p>
<p>After inspections in Iraq following the UN <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> cease-fire resolution showed the extent of Iraq's clandestine nuclear weapons program, it became clear that the IAEA would have to broaden the scope of its activities. Iraq was an NPT Party, and had thus agreed to place all its nuclear material under IAEA safeguards. But the inspections revealed that it had been pursuing an extensive clandestine uranium enrichment programme, as well as a nuclear weapons design programme.</p>
<p>The main thrust of Iraq's uranium enrichment program was the development of technology for <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_isotope_separation" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromagnetic isotope separation">electromagnetic isotope separation</a> (EMIS) of indigenous uranium. This uses the same principles as a <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass spectrometer">mass spectrometer</a> (albeit on a much larger scale). Ions of uranium-238 and uranium-235 are separated because they describe arcs of different radii when they move through a magnetic field. This process was used in the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> to make the highly enriched uranium used in the <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">Hiroshima bomb</a>, but was abandoned soon afterwards.</p>
<p>The Iraqis did the basic research work at their nuclear research establishment at Tuwaitha, near <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>, and were building two full-scale facilities at Tarmiya and Ash Sharqat, north of Baghdad. However, when the war broke out, only a few separators had been installed at Tarmiya, and none at Ash Sharqat.</p>
<p>The Iraqis were also very interested in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle#Enrichment" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">centrifuge enrichment</a>, and had been able to acquire some components including some carbon-fibre rotors, which they were at an early stage of testing. In May 1998, <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> reported that <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Qadeer_Khan" title="Abdul Qadeer Khan">Abdul Qadeer Khan</a> had sent Iraq centrifuge designs, which were apparently confiscated by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Monitoring,_Verification_and_Inspection_Commission" title="United Nations Monitoring, Verification and Inspection Commission">UNMOVIC</a> officials. Iraqi officials said "the documents were authentic but that they had not agreed to work with A. Q. Khan, fearing an <a href="/wiki/ISI_(Pakistan)" class="mw-redirect" title="ISI (Pakistan)">ISI</a> sting operation, due to <a href="/wiki/Iraq%E2%80%93Pakistan_relations" title="Iraq–Pakistan relations">strained relations</a> between two countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Pakistan" title="Government of Pakistan">Government of Pakistan</a> and A. Q. Khan strongly denied this allegation whilst the government declared the evidence to be "fraudulent".<sup id="cite_ref-International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies_.28ISSI.29_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-International_Institute_for_Strategic_Studies_.28ISSI.29-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>They were clearly in violation of their NPT and safeguards obligations, and the IAEA Board of Governors ruled to that effect. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">UN Security Council</a> then ordered the IAEA to remove, destroy or render harmless Iraq's nuclear weapons capability. This was done by mid-1998, but Iraq then ceased all cooperation with the UN, so the IAEA withdrew from this work.</p>
<p>The revelations from Iraq provided the impetus for a very far-reaching reconsideration of what safeguards are intended to achieve.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Libya">Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Libya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Libya_and_nuclear_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Libya and nuclear technology">Libya and nuclear technology</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> possesses ballistic missiles and previously pursued nuclear weapons <a href="/wiki/History_of_Libya_under_Muammar_Gaddafi" title="History of Libya under Muammar Gaddafi">under the leadership</a> of <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>. On 19 December 2003, Gaddafi announced that Libya would voluntarily eliminate all materials, equipment and programs that could lead to internationally proscribed weapons, including weapons of mass destruction and long-range ballistic missiles.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LibyaTimeline_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LibyaTimeline-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NuclearThreat_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NuclearThreat-72">[72]</a></sup> Libya signed the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a> (NPT) in 1968 and ratified it in 1975, and concluded a safeguards agreement with the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In March 2004, the IAEA Board of Governors welcomed Libya's decision to eliminate its formerly undeclared nuclear program, which it found had violated Libya's safeguards agreement, and approved Libya's Additional Protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-LibyaTimeline_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LibyaTimeline-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The United States and the United Kingdom assisted Libya in removing equipment and material from its nuclear weapons program, with independent verification by the IAEA.<sup id="cite_ref-NuclearThreat_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NuclearThreat-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Myanmar">Myanmar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Myanmar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A report in the <i>Sydney Morning Herald</i> and <i>Searchina</i>, a Japanese newspaper, report that two <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmarese</a> defectors saying that the Myanmar junta was secretly building a nuclear reactor and plutonium extraction facility with North Korea's help, with the aim of acquiring its first nuclear bomb in five years. According to the report, "The secret complex, much of it in caves tunnelled into a mountain at Naung Laing in northern Burma, runs parallel to a civilian reactor being built at another site by <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> that both the Russians and Burmese say will be put under international safeguards."<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In 2002, Myanmar had notified IAEA of its intention to pursue a civilian nuclear programme. Later, Russia announced that it would build a nuclear reactor in Myanmar. There have also been reports that two Pakistani scientists, from the AQ Khan stable, had been dispatched to Myanmar where they had settled down, to help Myanmar's project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Recently, the <a href="/wiki/David_Albright" title="David Albright">David Albright</a>-led <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Science_and_International_Security" title="Institute for Science and International Security">Institute for Science and International Security</a> (ISIS) rang alarm bells about Myanmar attempting a nuclear project with North Korean help.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If true, the full weight of international pressure will be brought against Myanmar, said officials familiar with developments. But equally, the information that has been peddled by the defectors is also "preliminary" and could be used by the west to turn the screws on Myanmar—on democracy and human rights issues—in the run-up to the elections in the country in 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During an <a href="/wiki/ASEAN" class="mw-redirect" title="ASEAN">ASEAN</a> meeting in Thailand in July 2009, US secretary of state <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> highlighted concerns of the North Korean link. "We know there are also growing concerns about military cooperation between <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> which we take very seriously," Clinton said.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> However, in 2012, after contact with the American president, Barack Obama, the Burmese leader, Thein Sein, renounced military ties with DPRK (North Korea).<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Korea_2">North Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: North Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK) acceded to the NPT in 1985 as a condition for the supply of a nuclear power station by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>. However, it delayed concluding its NPT Safeguards Agreement with the IAEA, a process which should take only 18 months, until April 1992.</p>
<p>During that period, it brought into operation a small gas-cooled, graphite-moderated, natural-uranium (metal) fuelled "Experimental Power Reactor" of about 25 <a href="/wiki/MWt" class="mw-redirect" title="MWt">MWt</a> (5 <a href="/wiki/MWe" class="mw-redirect" title="MWe">MWe</a>), based on the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnox" title="Magnox">Magnox</a> design. While this was a well-suited design to start a wholly indigenous nuclear reactor development, it also exhibited all the features of a small plutonium production reactor for weapons purposes. North Korea also made substantial progress in the construction of two larger reactors designed on the same principles, a prototype of about 200 MWt (50 MWe), and a full-scale version of about 800 MWt (200 MWe). They made only slow progress; construction halted on both in 1994 and has not resumed. Both reactors have degraded considerably since that time and would take significant efforts to refurbish.</p>
<p>In addition it completed and commissioned a reprocessing plant that makes the Magnox <a href="/wiki/Spent_nuclear_fuel" title="Spent nuclear fuel">spent nuclear fuel</a> safe, recovering <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. That plutonium, if the fuel was only irradiated to a very low burn-up, would have been in a form very suitable for weapons. Although all these facilities at <a href="/wiki/Yongbyon_Nuclear_Scientific_Research_Center" title="Yongbyon Nuclear Scientific Research Center">Yongbyon</a> were to be under safeguards, there was always the risk that at some stage, the DPRK would withdraw from the NPT and use the plutonium for weapons.</p>
<p>One of the first steps in applying NPT safeguards is for the IAEA to verify the initial stocks of uranium and plutonium to ensure that all the nuclear materials in the country have been declared for safeguards purposes. While undertaking this work in 1992, IAEA inspectors found discrepancies which indicated that the reprocessing plant had been used more often than the DPRK had declared, which suggested that the DPRK could have weapons-grade plutonium which it had not declared to the IAEA. Information passed to the IAEA by a Member State (as required by the IAEA) supported that suggestion by indicating that the DPRK had two undeclared waste or other storage sites.</p>
<p>In February 1993 the IAEA called on the DPRK to allow special inspections of the two sites so that the initial stocks of nuclear material could be verified. The DPRK refused, and on 12 March announced its intention to withdraw from the NPT (three months' notice is required). In April 1993 the IAEA Board concluded that the DPRK was in non-compliance with its safeguards obligations and reported the matter to the UN Security Council. In June 1993 the DPRK announced that it had "suspended" its withdrawal from the NPT, but subsequently claimed a "special status" with respect to its safeguards obligations. This was rejected by IAEA.</p>
<p>Once the DPRK's non-compliance had been reported to the UN Security Council, the essential part of the IAEA's mission had been completed. Inspections in the DPRK continued, although inspectors were increasingly hampered in what they were permitted to do by the DPRK's claim of a "special status". However, some 8,000 corroding fuel rods associated with the experimental reactor have remained under close surveillance.</p>
<p>Following bilateral negotiations between the United States and the DPRK, and the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Agreed_Framework" title="Agreed Framework">Agreed Framework</a> in October 1994, the IAEA has been given additional responsibilities. The agreement requires a freeze on the operation and construction of the DPRK's plutonium production reactors and their related facilities, and the IAEA is responsible for monitoring the freeze until the facilities are eventually dismantled. The DPRK remains uncooperative with the IAEA verification work and has yet to comply with its safeguards agreement.</p>
<p>While Iraq was defeated in a war, allowing the UN the opportunity to seek out and destroy its nuclear weapons programme as part of the cease-fire conditions, the DPRK was not defeated, nor was it vulnerable to other measures, such as <a href="/wiki/Trade_sanctions" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade sanctions">trade sanctions</a>. It can scarcely afford to import anything, and sanctions on vital commodities, such as oil, would either be ineffective or risk provoking war.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, the DPRK was persuaded to stop what appeared to be its nuclear weapons programme in exchange, under the agreed framework, for about US$5 billion in energy-related assistance. This included two 1000 MWe light water nuclear power reactors based on an advanced U.S. System-80 design.</p>
<p>In January 2003 the DPRK withdrew from the NPT. In response, a series of discussions among the DPRK, the United States, and China, a series of six-party talks (the parties being the DPRK, the ROK, China, Japan, the United States and Russia) were held in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>; the first beginning in April 2004 concerning North Korea's weapons program.</p>
<p>On 10 January 2005, North Korea declared that it was in the possession of nuclear weapons. On 19 September 2005, the fourth round of the Six-Party Talks ended with a joint statement in which North Korea agreed to end its nuclear programs and return to the NPT in exchange for diplomatic, energy and economic assistance. However, by the end of 2005 the DPRK had halted all six-party talks because the United States froze certain DPRK international financial assets such as those in a bank in Macau.</p>
<p>On 9 October 2006, North Korea announced that it has performed its first-ever <a href="/wiki/2006_North_Korean_nuclear_test" title="2006 North Korean nuclear test">nuclear weapon test</a>. On 18 December 2006, the six-party talks finally resumed. On 13 February 2007, the parties announced "Initial Actions" to implement the 2005 joint statement including shutdown and disablement of North Korean nuclear facilities in exchange for energy assistance. Reacting to UN sanctions imposed after missile tests in April 2009, North Korea withdrew from the six-party talks, restarted its nuclear facilities and conducted a <a href="/wiki/2009_North_Korean_nuclear_test" title="2009 North Korean nuclear test">second nuclear test</a> on 25 May 2009.</p>
<p>On 12 February 2013, North Korea conducted an <a href="/wiki/2013_North_Korean_nuclear_test" title="2013 North Korean nuclear test">underground nuclear explosion</a> with an estimated yield of 6 to 7 kilotonnes. The detonation registered a magnitude 4.9 disturbance in the area around the epicenter.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Russia_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction#Nuclear_weapons" title="Russia and weapons of mass destruction">Russia and weapons of mass destruction §&#160;Nuclear weapons</a></div>
<p>Security of nuclear weapons in Russia remains a matter of concern. According to high-ranking Russian <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Intelligence_Service_(Russia)" title="Foreign Intelligence Service (Russia)">SVR</a> defector <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Tretyakov_(intelligence_officer)" title="Sergei Tretyakov (intelligence officer)">Tretyakov</a>, he had a meeting with two Russian businessman representing a state-created <i>C-W</i> corporation in 1991. They came up with a project of destroying large quantities of chemical wastes collected from Western countries at the island of <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> (a test place for Soviet nuclear weapons) using an underground nuclear blast. The project was rejected by Canadian representatives, but one of the businessmen told Tretyakov that he keeps his own nuclear bomb at his <a href="/wiki/Dacha" title="Dacha">dacha</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>. Tretyakov thought that man was insane, but the "businessmen" (Vladimir K. Dmitriev) replied: "Do not be so naive. With economic conditions the way they are in Russia today, anyone with enough money can buy a nuclear bomb. It's no big deal really".<sup id="cite_ref-Comrade_J_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Comrade_J-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1991, South Africa acceded to the NPT, concluded a comprehensive safeguards agreement with the IAEA, and submitted a report on its nuclear material subject to safeguards.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> At the time, the state had a nuclear power programme producing nearly 10% of the country's electricity, whereas Iraq and North Korea only had research reactors.</p>
<p>The IAEA's initial verification task was complicated by South Africa's announcement that between 1979 and 1989 it built and then dismantled a number of nuclear weapons. South Africa asked the IAEA to verify the conclusion of its weapons programme. In 1995 the IAEA declared that it was satisfied all materials were accounted for and the weapons programme had been terminated and dismantled.</p>
<p>South Africa has signed the NPT, and now holds the distinction of being the only known state to have indigenously produced nuclear weapons, and then verifiably dismantled them.<sup id="cite_ref-fas-sanwp_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas-sanwp-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Syria">Syria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Syria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Syria_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Syria and weapons of mass destruction">Syria and weapons of mass destruction</a></div>
<p>On 6 September 2007, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> bombed an officially unidentified site in Syria which it later asserted was a nuclear reactor under construction (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Operation_Orchard" title="Operation Orchard">Operation Orchard</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-Globalsecurity_Airstrike_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Globalsecurity_Airstrike-81">[81]</a></sup> The alleged reactor was not asserted to be operational and it was not asserted that nuclear material had been introduced into it.<sup id="cite_ref-SS2008_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS2008-50">[50]</a></sup> Syria said the site was a military site and was not involved in any nuclear activities.<sup id="cite_ref-SS2008_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS2008-50">[50]</a></sup> The IAEA requested Syria to provide further access to the site and any other locations where the debris and equipment from the building had been stored.<sup id="cite_ref-SS2008_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS2008-50">[50]</a></sup> Syria denounced what it called the Western "fabrication and forging of facts" in regards to the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> IAEA Director General Mohamed ElBaradei criticized the strikes and deplored that information regarding the matter had not been shared with his agency earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-IAEAHaaretz_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAEAHaaretz-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakout_capability">Breakout capability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Breakout capability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a state that does not possess nuclear weapons, the capability to produce one or more weapons quickly and with little warning is called a breakout capability.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/23px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png" width="23" height="15" class="thumbborder" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/35px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/Flag_of_Japan.svg/45px-Flag_of_Japan.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" />&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, with its civil nuclear infrastructure and experience, has a stockpile of separated plutonium that could be fabricated into weapons relatively quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Flag_of_Iran.svg/23px-Flag_of_Iran.svg.png" width="23" height="13" class="thumbborder" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Flag_of_Iran.svg/35px-Flag_of_Iran.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Flag_of_Iran.svg/46px-Flag_of_Iran.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="630" data-file-height="360" />&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, according to some observers, may be seeking (or have already achieved) a breakout capability, with its stockpile of low-enriched uranium and its capability to enrich further to weapons grade.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_for_and_against_proliferation">Arguments for and against proliferation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Arguments for and against proliferation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_peace" title="Nuclear peace">Nuclear peace</a></div>
<p>There has been much debate in the academic study of International Security as to the advisability of proliferation. In the late 1950s and early 1960s, Gen. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Marie_Gallois" title="Pierre Marie Gallois">Pierre Marie Gallois</a> of France, an adviser to Charles DeGaulle, argued in books like <i>The Balance of Terror: Strategy for the Nuclear Age</i> (1961) that mere possession of a nuclear arsenal, what the French called the <i><a href="/wiki/Force_de_frappe" class="mw-redirect" title="Force de frappe">force de frappe</a></i>, was enough to ensure deterrence, and thus concluded that the spread of nuclear weapons could increase international stability.</p>
<p>Some very prominent <a href="/wiki/Neorealism_(international_relations)" title="Neorealism (international relations)">neo-realist</a> scholars, such as <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Waltz" title="Kenneth Waltz">Kenneth Waltz</a>, Emeritus Professor of Political Science at <a href="/wiki/UC_Berkeley" class="mw-redirect" title="UC Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a> and Adjunct Senior Research Scholar at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a>, R. Wendell Harrison Distinguished Service Professor of Political Science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, continue to argue along the lines of Gallois (though these scholars rarely acknowledge their intellectual debt to Gallois and his contemporaries). Specifically, these scholars advocate some forms of nuclear proliferation, arguing that it will decrease the likelihood of war, especially in troubled regions of the world. Aside from the majority opinion which opposes proliferation in any form, there are two schools of thought on the matter: those, like Mearsheimer, who favor selective proliferation,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and those such as Waltz, who advocate a laissez-faire attitude to programs like North Korea's.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_proliferation">Total proliferation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Total proliferation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In embryo, Waltz argues that the logic of <a href="/wiki/Mutually_assured_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutually assured destruction">mutually assured destruction</a> (MAD) should work in all security environments, regardless of historical tensions or recent hostility. He sees the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> as the ultimate proof of MAD logic&#160;– the only occasion when enmity between two Great Powers did not result in military conflict. This was, he argues, because nuclear weapons promote caution in decision-makers. Neither Washington nor Moscow would risk a nuclear apocalypse to advance territorial or power goals, hence a peaceful stalemate ensued (Waltz and Sagan (2003), p.&#160;24). Waltz believes there to be no reason why this effect would not occur in all circumstances.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selective_proliferation">Selective proliferation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Selective proliferation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>John Mearsheimer would not support Waltz's optimism in the majority of potential instances; however, he has argued for nuclear proliferation as policy in certain places, such as post–Cold War Europe. In two famous articles, Professor Mearsheimer opines that Europe is bound to return to its pre–Cold War environment of regular conflagration and suspicion at some point in the future. He advocates arming both Germany and Ukraine with nuclear weaponry in order to achieve a balance of power between these states in the east and France/UK in the west. If this does not occur, he is certain that war will eventually break out on the European continent.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another separate argument against Waltz's open proliferation and in favor of Mearsheimer's selective distribution is the possibility of nuclear terrorism. Some countries included in the aforementioned laissez-faire distribution could predispose the transfer of nuclear materials or a bomb falling into the hands of groups not affiliated with any governments. Such countries would not have the political will or ability to safeguard attempts at devices being transferred to a third party. Not being deterred by self-annihilation, terrorism groups could push forth their own nuclear agendas or be used as shadow fronts to carry out the attack plans by mentioned unstable governments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_both_positions">Arguments against both positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Arguments against both positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are numerous arguments presented against both selective and total proliferation, generally targeting the very neorealist assumptions (such as the primacy of <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">military security</a> in state agendas, the weakness of <a href="/wiki/International_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="International institution">international institutions</a>, and the long-run unimportance of <a href="/wiki/Economic_integration" title="Economic integration">economic integration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> to state strategy) its proponents tend to make. With respect to Mearsheimer's specific example of Europe, many <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Neoliberalism in international relations">neoliberals</a> argue that the <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">economic integration of Europe</a> through the development of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has made war in most of the European continent so disastrous economically so as to serve as an effective deterrent. <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Constructivism in international relations">Constructivists</a> take this one step further, frequently arguing that the development of EU political institutions has led or will lead to the development of a nascent <a href="/wiki/European_identity" class="mw-redirect" title="European identity">European identity</a>, which most states on the European continent wish to partake in to some degree or another, and which makes all states within or aspiring to be within the EU regard war between them as unthinkable.</p>
<p>As for Waltz, the general opinion is that most states are not in a position to safely guard against nuclear use, that he underestimates the long-standing antipathy in many regions, and that weak states will be unable to prevent – or will actively provide for – the disastrous possibility of nuclear terrorism. Waltz has dealt with all of these objections at some point in his work; though to many, he has not adequately responded (Betts (2000)).</p>
<p>The Learning Channel documentary Doomsday: "On The Brink" illustrated 40 years of U.S. and Soviet nuclear weapons accidents. Even the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_rocket_incident" title="Norwegian rocket incident">Norwegian rocket incident</a> demonstrated a potential scenario in which Russian democratization and military downsizing at the end of the Cold War did not eliminate the danger of accidental nuclear war through command and control errors. After asking: might a future Russian ruler or renegade Russian general be tempted to use nuclear weapons to make foreign policy? The documentary writers revealed a greater danger of Russian security over its nuclear stocks, but especially the ultimate danger of human nature to want the ultimate weapon of mass destruction to exercise political and military power. Future world leaders might not understand how close the Soviets, Russians, and Americans were to doomsday, how easy it all seemed because apocalypse was avoided for a mere 40 years between rivals, politicians not terrorists, who loved their children and did not want to die, against 30,000 years of human prehistory. History and military experts agree that proliferation can be slowed, but never stopped (technology cannot be uninvented).<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proliferation_begets_proliferation">Proliferation begets proliferation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Proliferation begets proliferation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proliferation begets proliferation is a concept described by <a href="/wiki/Scott_Sagan" title="Scott Sagan">Scott Sagan</a> in his article, "Why Do States Build Nuclear Weapons?". This concept can be described as a strategic <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain reaction</a>. If one state produces a nuclear weapon it creates almost a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a> within the region. States in the region will seek to acquire nuclear weapons to balance or eliminate the security threat. Sagan describes this reaction best in his article when he states, “Every time one state develops nuclear weapons to balance against its main rival, it also creates a nuclear threat to another region, which then has to initiate its own nuclear weapons program to maintain its national security”.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Going back through history we can see how this has taken place. When the United States demonstrated that it had nuclear power capabilities after the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the Russians started to develop their program in preparation for the Cold War. With the Russian military buildup, France and the United Kingdom perceived this as a security threat and therefore they pursued nuclear weapons (Sagan, pg 71).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran_2">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Former Iranian President <a href="/wiki/Mahmoud_Ahmadinejad" title="Mahmoud Ahmadinejad">Mahmoud Ahmadinejad</a> has been a frequent critic of the concept of nuclear <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> as it has been put into practice by several countries, particularly the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In an interview with <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>'s Christiane Amanpour, Ahmadinejad said that <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> was "against 'nuclear apartheid,' which means some have the right to possess it, use the fuel, and then sell it to another country for 10 times its value. We're against that. We say clean energy is the right of all countries. But also it is the duty and the responsibility of all countries, including ours, to set up frameworks to stop the proliferation of it." Hours after that interview, he spoke passionately in favor of Iran's right to develop nuclear technology, claiming the nation should have the same liberties.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Iran is a signatory of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a> and claims that any work done in regards to nuclear technology is related only to civilian uses, which is acceptable under the treaty. Iran violated its safeguards obligations under the treaty<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> by performing <a href="/wiki/Uranium-enrichment" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium-enrichment">uranium-enrichment</a> in secret, after which the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> ordered Iran to suspend all uranium-enrichment until July 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India_2">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_proliferation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> has also been discussed in the context of nuclear apartheid. India has consistently attempted to pass measures that would call for full international disarmament, however they have not succeeded due to protests from those states that already have nuclear weapons. In light of this, India viewed nuclear weapons as a necessary right for all nations as long as certain states were still in possession of nuclear weapons. India stated that nuclear issues were directly related to national security.</p>
<p>Years before India's first underground nuclear test in 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Nuclear-Test-Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty</a> was passed. Some have argued that coercive language was used in an attempt to persuade India to sign the treaty, which was pushed for heavily by neighboring China.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> India viewed the treaty as a means for countries that already had nuclear weapons, primarily the five nations of the United Nations Security Council, to keep their weapons while ensuring that no other nations could develop them.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
