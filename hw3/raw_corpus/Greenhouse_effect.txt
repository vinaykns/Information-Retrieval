			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenhouse effect</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>greenhouse effect</b> is the process by which radiation from a planet's atmosphere warms the planet's surface to a temperature above what it would be without its atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-ipccar4syr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipccar4syr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ipcc-AR4WG1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-AR4WG1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>If a planet's atmosphere contains radiatively active gases (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>) the atmosphere will radiate energy in all directions. Part of this radiation is directed towards the surface, warming it. The downward component of this radiation – that is, the strength of the greenhouse effect – will depend on the atmosphere's temperature and on the amount of greenhouse gases that the atmosphere contains.</p>
<p>On Earth, the atmosphere is warmed by absorption of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> thermal radiation from the underlying surface (heated by the sun), absorption of shorter wavelength <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">radiant energy</a> from the sun, and <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> heat fluxes from the surface. Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere radiate energy, some of which is directed to the surface and lower atmosphere. The mechanism that produces this difference between the actual surface temperature and the effective temperature is due to the atmosphere and is known as the greenhouse effect.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Earth’s natural greenhouse effect is critical to supporting life. Human activities, primarily the burning of fossil fuels and clearing of forests, have intensified the natural greenhouse effect, causing <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mechanism is named after a faulty analogy with the effect of solar radiation passing through glass and warming a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a>. The way a greenhouse retains heat is fundamentally different, as a greenhouse works by reducing airflow and retaining warm air inside the structure.<sup id="cite_ref-ipcc-AR4WG1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-AR4WG1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schroeder_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wood1909_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wood1909-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a></div>
<p>The existence of the greenhouse effect was argued for by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> in 1824. The argument and the evidence were further strengthened by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Pouillet" title="Claude Pouillet">Claude Pouillet</a> in 1827 and 1838 and reasoned from experimental observations by <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> in 1859, who measured the radiative properties of specific greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The effect was more fully quantified by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> in 1896, who made the first quantitative prediction of global warming due to a hypothetical doubling of atmospheric carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-anrev_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anrev-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the term "greenhouse" was not used to refer to this effect by any of these scientists; the term was first used in this way by <a href="/wiki/Nils_Ekholm" class="mw-redirect" title="Nils Ekholm">Nils Gustaf Ekholm</a> in 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span></h2>
<p>Earth receives energy from the Sun in the form of <a href="/wiki/UV" class="mw-redirect" title="UV">ultraviolet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visible_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Visible light">visible</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared" class="mw-redirect" title="Near-infrared">near-infrared</a> radiation. Of the total amount of solar energy available at the top of the atmosphere, about 26% is reflected to space by the atmosphere and clouds and 19% is absorbed by the atmosphere and clouds. Most of the remaining energy is absorbed at the surface of Earth. <a href="/wiki/Wien%27s_law" class="mw-disambig" title="Wien's law">Because the Earth's surface is colder</a> than the photosphere of the Sun, it radiates at <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">wavelengths that are much longer</a> than the wavelengths that were absorbed. Most of this thermal radiation is absorbed by the atmosphere, thereby warming it. In addition to the absorption of solar and thermal radiation, the atmosphere gains heat by sensible and latent heat fluxes from the surface. The atmosphere radiates energy both upwards and downwards; the part radiated downwards is absorbed by the surface of Earth. This leads to a higher equilibrium temperature than if the atmosphere were absent.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_Spectrum.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/220px-Solar_Spectrum.png" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/330px-Solar_Spectrum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/440px-Solar_Spectrum.png 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="595" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_Spectrum.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> spectrum for direct light at both the top of Earth's atmosphere and at sea level</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An ideal thermally conductive <a href="/wiki/Blackbody" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a> at the same distance from the Sun as Earth would have a temperature of about 5.3&#160;°C. However, because Earth reflects about 30%<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard-12">[12]</a></sup> of the incoming sunlight, this idealized planet's <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> (the temperature of a blackbody that would emit the same amount of radiation) would be about −18&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IPCC4_ch01_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC4_ch01-14">[14]</a></sup> The surface temperature of this hypothetical planet is 33&#160;°C below Earth's actual surface temperature of approximately 14&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The basic mechanism can be qualified in a number of ways, none of which affect the fundamental process. The atmosphere near the surface is largely opaque to thermal radiation (with important exceptions for "window" bands), and most heat loss from the surface is by <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> transport. Radiative energy losses become increasingly important higher in the atmosphere, largely because of the decreasing concentration of water vapor, an important greenhouse gas. It is more realistic to think of the greenhouse effect as applying to a "surface" in the mid-<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, which is effectively coupled to the surface by a <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>. The simple picture also assumes a steady state, but in the real world, there are variations due to the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal cycle</a> as well as the seasonal cycle and weather disturbances. Solar heating only applies during daytime. During the night, the atmosphere cools somewhat, but not greatly, because its emissivity is low. <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">Diurnal temperature changes</a> decrease with height in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Within the region where radiative effects are important, the description given by the <a href="/wiki/Idealized_greenhouse_model" title="Idealized greenhouse model">idealized greenhouse model</a> becomes realistic. Earth's surface, warmed to a temperature around 255&#160;K, radiates long-wavelength, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> heat in the range of 4–100&#160;μm.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell-1989_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-1989-16">[16]</a></sup> At these wavelengths, greenhouse gases that were largely transparent to incoming solar radiation are more absorbent.<sup id="cite_ref-Mitchell-1989_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-1989-16">[16]</a></sup> Each layer of atmosphere with greenhouses gases absorbs some of the heat being radiated upwards from lower layers. It reradiates in all directions, both upwards and downwards; in equilibrium (by definition) the same amount as it has absorbed. This results in more warmth below. Increasing the concentration of the gases increases the amount of absorption and reradiation, and thereby further warms the layers and ultimately the surface below.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC4_ch01_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC4_ch01-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greenhouse gases—including most diatomic gases with two different atoms (such as carbon monoxide, CO) and all gases with three or more atoms—are able to absorb and emit infrared radiation. Though more than 99% of the dry atmosphere is IR transparent (because the main constituents—N<sub>2</sub>, O<sub>2</sub>, and Ar—are not able to directly absorb or emit infrared radiation), intermolecular collisions cause the energy absorbed and emitted by the greenhouse gases to be shared with the other, non-IR-active, gases.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png/500px-CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" width="500" height="74" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png 1.5x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="107" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Atmospheric gases only absorb some wavelengths of energy but are transparent to others. The absorption patterns of water vapor (blue peaks) and carbon dioxide (pink peaks) overlap in some wavelengths. (Illustration NASA, Robert Rohde)<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_climate_forcings_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_climate_forcings-17">[17]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By their percentage contribution to the greenhouse effect on Earth the four major gases are:<sup id="cite_ref-realclimate.org_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-realclimate.org-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kiehl197_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kiehl197-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, 36–70%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, 9–26%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, 4–9%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, 3–7%</li>
</ul>
<p>It is not physically realistic to assign a specific percentage to each gas because the absorption and emission bands of the gases overlap (hence the ranges given above). The major non-gas contributor to Earth's greenhouse effect, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forcing" title="Cloud forcing">clouds</a>, also absorb and emit infrared radiation and thus have an effect on the radiative properties of the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-kiehl197_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kiehl197-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_climate_change">Role in climate change</span></h2>

<p>Strengthening of the greenhouse effect through human activities is known as the enhanced (or <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>) greenhouse effect.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This increase in <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> from human activity is attributable mainly to increased atmospheric carbon dioxide levels.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> According to the latest Assessment Report from the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, "<i>atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide are unprecedented in at least the last 800,000 years. Their effects, together with those of other anthropogenic drivers, have been detected throughout the climate system and are extremely likely to have been the dominant cause of the observed warming since the mid-20th century</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> is produced by fossil fuel burning and other activities such as <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> production and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_deforestation" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical deforestation">tropical deforestation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_deforestation_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_deforestation-23">[23]</a></sup> Measurements of CO<sub>2</sub> from the Mauna Loa observatory show that concentrations have increased from about 313 parts per million (ppm)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in 1960 to about 389 ppm in 2010. It reached the 400 ppm milestone on May 9, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The current observed amount of CO<sub>2</sub> exceeds the geological record maxima (~300 ppm) from ice core data.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The effect of combustion-produced carbon dioxide on the global climate, a special case of the greenhouse effect first described in 1896 by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a>, has also been called the <a href="/wiki/Callendar_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Callendar effect">Callendar effect</a>.</p>
<p>Over the past 800,000 years,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide#Variation_in_the_past" title="Carbon dioxide">ice core data</a> shows that carbon dioxide has varied from values as low as 180 ppm to the pre-industrial level of 270 ppm.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoclimatologists">Paleoclimatologists</a> consider variations in carbon dioxide concentration to be a fundamental factor influencing climate variations over this time scale.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowen_.282005.29.2C_Thin_Ice_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen_.282005.29.2C_Thin_Ice-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_greenhouses">Real greenhouses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RHSGlasshouse.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/220px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/330px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/440px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2628" data-file-height="1953" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RHSGlasshouse.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A modern <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">Greenhouse</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wisley_Garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Wisley Garden">RHS Wisley</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The "greenhouse effect" of the atmosphere is named by analogy to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> which become warmer in sunlight. The explanation given in most sources for the warmer temperature in an actual greenhouse is that incident <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> in the visible, long-wavelength <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> and short-wavelength <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> range of the spectrum passes through the glass roof and walls and is absorbed by the floor, earth, and contents, which become warmer and re-emit the energy as longer-wavelength infrared radiation. Glass and other materials used for greenhouse walls do not transmit infrared radiation, so the infrared cannot escape via <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer</a>. As the structure is not open to the atmosphere, heat also cannot escape via <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">convection</a>, so the temperature inside the greenhouse rises.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The greenhouse effect, due to infrared-opaque "<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>" including carbon dioxide and methane instead of glass, also affects Earth as a whole; there is no convective cooling because no significant amount of air escapes from Earth.</p>
<p>However, the mechanism by which the atmosphere retains heat—the "greenhouse effect"—is different; a greenhouse is not primarily warmed by the "greenhouse effect".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A greenhouse works primarily by allowing sunlight to warm surfaces inside the structure, but then preventing absorbed heat from leaving the structure through convection. The "greenhouse effect" heats Earth because greenhouse gases absorb outgoing radiative energy, heating the atmosphere which then emits radiative energy with some of it going back towards Earth.</p>
<p>A greenhouse is built of any material that passes sunlight, usually glass, or plastic. It mainly warms up because the sun warms the ground and contents inside, which then warms the air in the greenhouse. The air continues to heat up because it is confined within the greenhouse, unlike the environment outside the greenhouse where warm air near the surface rises and mixes with cooler air aloft. This can be demonstrated by opening a small window near the roof of a greenhouse: the temperature will drop considerably. It was demonstrated experimentally (<a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Wood" title="Robert W. Wood">R. W. Wood</a>, 1909) that a "greenhouse" with a cover of <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">rock salt</a> (which is transparent to infrared) heats up an enclosure similarly to one with a glass cover.<sup id="cite_ref-wood1909_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wood1909-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus greenhouses work primarily by preventing <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> cooling.<sup id="cite_ref-Schroeder_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recent quantitative studies suggest that the effect of infrared radiative cooling is not negligibly small, and may have economic implications in a heated greenhouse. Analysis of issues of near-infrared radiation in a greenhouse with screens of a high coefficient of reflection concluded that installation of such screens reduced heat demand by about 8%, and application of dyes to transparent surfaces was suggested. Composite less-reflective glass, or less effective but cheaper anti-reflective coated simple glass, also produced savings.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bodies_other_than_Earth">Bodies other than Earth</span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, there are also greenhouse effects on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus#Atmosphere_and_climate" title="Venus">Venus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. The greenhouse effect on Venus is particularly large because its dense atmosphere consisting mainly of carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Titan has an <a href="/wiki/Anti-greenhouse_effect" title="Anti-greenhouse effect">anti-greenhouse effect</a>, in that its atmosphere absorbs solar radiation but is relatively transparent to infrared radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Pluto" title="Atmosphere of Pluto">Pluto</a> is also colder than would be expected because evaporation of nitrogen cools it.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a> occurs if <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedbacks</a> lead to the evaporation of all greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Kasting_1991_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasting_1991-39">[39]</a></sup> A runaway greenhouse effect involving carbon dioxide and water vapor is thought to have occurred on Venus.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
