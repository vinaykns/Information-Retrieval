<p><b>Pearson PLC</b> is a British <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. It was founded as a construction business in the 1840s. It shut down its construction activities in the 1920s and switched to publishing. It is the largest education company and the largest book publisher in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Pearson has a primary listing on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>. It has a secondary listing on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/American_Depository_Receipts" class="mw-redirect" title="American Depository Receipts">American Depository Receipts</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_business:_1844_to_the_1920s">Construction business: 1844 to the 1920s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction business: 1844 to the 1920s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company was founded by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pearson" title="Samuel Pearson">Samuel Pearson</a> in 1844 as a building and engineering concern operating in Yorkshire under the name <i>S. Pearson &amp; Son</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1880, control passed to his grandson <a href="/wiki/Weetman_Pearson,_1st_Viscount_Cowdray" title="Weetman Pearson, 1st Viscount Cowdray">Weetman Dickinson Pearson</a>, an engineer, who in 1890 moved the business to London and turned it into one of the world's largest construction companies.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> Another of its prominent engineers was <a href="/wiki/Ernest_William_Moir" title="Ernest William Moir">Ernest William Moir</a> who, after working for Pearson on tunnels in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, became the contractor's agent on construction of the <a href="/wiki/Blackwall_Tunnel" title="Blackwall Tunnel">Blackwall Tunnel</a> under the River Thames in London between 1892 and 1897.<sup id="cite_ref-Blackwall_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blackwall-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-History_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The company also built the Admiralty Harbour at <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a>, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Halifax">Halifax</a> Dry Dock in Canada, the <a href="/wiki/East_River_Tunnels" title="East River Tunnels">East River Railway Tunnels</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the Mexican Grand Canal that drained <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarril_Trans%C3%ADstmico" title="Ferrocarril Transístmico">Tehuantepec Railway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and railways and harbours around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In November 1915, the firm began construction of <a href="/wiki/HM_Factory,_Gretna" title="HM Factory, Gretna">HM Factory, Gretna</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/Cordite" title="Cordite">cordite</a> factory in the UK during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The construction business was shut down in the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Among its final projects was completion of the <a href="/wiki/Sennar_Dam" title="Sennar Dam">Sennar Dam</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, in 1925.<sup id="cite_ref-Engineer_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engineer-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publishing_business:_1920s_to_1990">Publishing business: 1920s to 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Publishing business: 1920s to 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1919, the firm acquired a 45% stake in the London branch of merchant bankers <a href="/wiki/Lazard" title="Lazard">Lazard Brothers</a>, an interest which was increased to 80% in 1932 during the depression years. Pearson continued to hold a 50% stake until 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1921, Pearson purchased a number of local daily and weekly newspapers in the United Kingdom, which it combined to form the Westminster Press group.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1957, it bought the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-history_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> and acquired a 50% stake in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>. It purchased the publisher <a href="/wiki/Longman" title="Longman">Longman</a> in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company was first listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> It went on to buy paperback publisher <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin</a> in 1970,<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup> and in 1972, children's imprint <a href="/wiki/Ladybird_Books" title="Ladybird Books">Ladybird Books</a>. In 1986, Pearson invested in the <a href="/wiki/British_Satellite_Broadcasting" title="British Satellite Broadcasting">British Satellite Broadcasting</a> consortium, which, a few years later merged with <a href="/wiki/Sky_Television_plc" title="Sky Television plc">Sky TV</a> to form a new company, <a href="/wiki/BSkyB" class="mw-redirect" title="BSkyB">British Sky Broadcasting</a> (BSkyB).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_to_2000">1990 to 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990 to 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1990s, Pearson acquired a number of TV production and broadcasting assets (including former <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> franchisee <a href="/wiki/Thames_Television" title="Thames Television">Thames Television</a>) and sold most of its non-media assets, under the leadership of future U.S. Congressman <a href="/wiki/Bob_Turner_(American_politician)" title="Bob Turner (American politician)">Bob Turner</a>. Westminster Press was sold to <a href="/wiki/Newsquest" title="Newsquest">Newsquest</a> in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Pearson acquired the education division of <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a> in 1996 from <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation" title="News Corporation">News Corporation</a> <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and acquired book publishers <a href="/wiki/Scott_Foresman" title="Scott Foresman">Scott Foresman &amp; Co.</a> in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1998 Pearson acquired <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Prentice Hall Textbooks/Simon &amp; Schuster Trade Books</a> from Viacom and merged it with its own education unit, <a href="/wiki/Addison-Wesley" title="Addison-Wesley">Addison-Wesley</a> Longman to form Pearson Education.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_2005">2000 to 2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000 to 2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson acquired <a href="/wiki/Dorling_Kindersley" title="Dorling Kindersley">Dorling Kindersley</a>, the illustrated reference publisher and integrated it within Penguin, in March 2000<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and then bought acquired National Computer Systems (NCS) in September 2000 so entering the educational assessment and school management systems market in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Pearson sold its 22% stake in <a href="/wiki/RTL_Group" title="RTL Group">RTL Group</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> for 1.5 billion Euros, and then purchased <a href="/wiki/Rough_Guides" title="Rough Guides">Rough Guides</a>, the travel publisher, and brought it under Penguin. Pearson acquired <a href="/wiki/Edexcel" title="Edexcel">Edexcel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-marking_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marking-23">[23]</a></sup> a provider of qualifications in the UK, in 2003 and acquired about 80% stake in Meximerica Media Inc for $16.5 million for the swelling U.S. Hispanic market in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pearson purchased a series of other testing and assessment businesses, including Knowledge Technologies in 2004,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> AGS in 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and National Evaluation Systems<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and Promissor in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_to_2012">2005 to 2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2005 to 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson acquired National Evaluation Systems, a provider of customised state assessments for teacher certification in the US, in April 2006<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and announced that it had agreed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Harcourt_Assessment" title="Harcourt Assessment">Harcourt Assessment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harcourt_(publisher)" title="Harcourt (publisher)">Harcourt Education International</a> from <a href="/wiki/Reed_Elsevier" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed Elsevier">Reed Elsevier</a> for $950m in cash in May 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Pearson then completed the acquisition of Harcourt Assessment, merging the acquired businesses into Pearson Assessment &amp; Information.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Pearson also acquired eCollege, a digital learning technology group for $477m in May 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2008, Pearson announced the sale of its Pearson Data Management Division (formerly the scanner manufacturing and servicing division of NCS Inc.) to <a href="/wiki/Optical_answer_sheet" title="Optical answer sheet">Scantron</a> Corporation (part of <a href="/wiki/M%26F_Worldwide" title="M&amp;F Worldwide">M&amp;F Worldwide</a>) which had been its main competitor.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pearson acquired Wall Street English for $145m in 2009<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and bought the school learning systems division of Sistema Educacional Brasileiro (SEB) for $497m in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Also in 2010, Pearson sold its 61% stake in Interactive Data to investment funds managed by <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_Partners" title="Silver Lake Partners">Silver Lake Partners</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warburg_Pincus" title="Warburg Pincus">Warburg Pincus</a> for $2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2011, Pearson announced the creation of <a href="/wiki/Pearson_College_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pearson College (UK)">Pearson College</a>, a British degree provider based in London.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Also in 2011, Pearson acquired Connections Education and agreed to sell its 50% stake in FTSE International Limited to the London Stock Exchange for £450 million.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2011, Pearson also increased its stake in TutorVista, such that it had a 76% stake, for $127 million.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pearson entered into talks with rival conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann" title="Bertelsmann">Bertelsmann</a>, over the possibility of combining their respective publishing companies, <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Group" title="Penguin Group">Penguin Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a> in October 2012. The houses are considered two of the "Big Six" publishing companies.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> On 29 October 2012, Pearson said it would merge <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> with <a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann" title="Bertelsmann">Bertelsmann</a>'s Random House to create the world's biggest consumer <a href="/wiki/Book" title="Book">book</a> publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Then in May 2012 Pearson announced its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/GlobalEnglish" title="GlobalEnglish">GlobalEnglish Corporation</a>, an American <a href="/wiki/Business_English" title="Business English">Business English</a> software and solutions company, in an all-cash transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_to_2015">2013 to 2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2013 to 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2013, Pearson announced a new restructuring plan to invest in digital learning and emerging markets, after predicting weaker earnings.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The change supports the decoupling of the Penguin consumer publishing business into a separate entity with Random House (forming Penguin Random House). The new structure combines the separate education companies, Pearson International and Pearson North America under one Pearson company. Pearson will organize around three global lines of business – School, Higher Education and Professional. The Financial Times Group and Pearson English will form part of Pearson Professional.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2014, Pearson announced it had cut 4,000 jobs, representing 10% of the company's workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pearson announced on 23 July 2015 that it had agreed to sell the FT Group, which includes business daily <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i>, to Japanese media group <a href="/wiki/Nihon_Keizai_Shimbun" class="mw-redirect" title="Nihon Keizai Shimbun">Nikkei</a> for £844 million, or $1.32 billion. The sale does not include FT Group's London property at One Southwark Bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In August 2015, Pearson's sold its 50% stake in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> to the <a href="/wiki/Agnelli" title="Agnelli">Agnelli</a> family for £469 million<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> who previously held 4.7% of the group.<sup id="cite_ref-BrandRepublic_2015-07-28_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrandRepublic_2015-07-28-49">[49]</a></sup> The remaining 50% of The Economist Group is owned by the <a href="/wiki/Schroders" title="Schroders">Schroders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_family" title="Cadbury family">Cadburys</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_family" title="Rothschild family">Rothschilds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BrandRepublic_2015-07-28_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrandRepublic_2015-07-28-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_to_present">2016 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2016 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 5 November 2015, Pearson announced it was <a href="/wiki/Rebranding" title="Rebranding">rebranding</a>, including creating a new logo, with a "100% focus" on education.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pearson is now organised into three main business groupings: Pearson School, Pearson Higher Education and Pearson Professional (includes Pearson English). In 2011 Pearson generated total revenues of £5.9 billion, of which £4,390 million were from Pearson Education, £1,045 million from Penguin Group, and £427 million from Financial Times Group, since sold.<sup id="cite_ref-annrep_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annrep-52">[52]</a></sup> In 2011, 60% of Pearson's revenues were generated in North America, 23% in Europe, 11% in Asia, and 6% in the rest of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-annrep_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annrep-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pearson_School">Pearson School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pearson School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson School was created when the School businesses from Pearson International and Pearson North America were merged in 2013. They provide textbooks and digital technologies to teachers and students across school ages. Pearson's school brands include BTEC, Bug Club, Edexcel, Fronter, GradPoint, Schoolnet, and SuccessNet...</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pearson_Higher_Education">Pearson Higher Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pearson Higher Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson Higher Education was created when the Higher Education businesses from Pearson International and Pearson North America were merged in 2013. They provide textbooks and digital technologies to teachers and students across Higher Education. Pearson's higher education brands include eCollege, Mastering/MyLabs, Revel, online tutoring (<a href="/wiki/Smarthinking" title="Smarthinking">Smarthinking</a>), and Financial Times Publishing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pearson_Professional">Pearson Professional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pearson Professional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson Professional includes Pearson English and Pearson VUE.</p>
<p>Pearson English provides textbooks, digital technologies and education services to teachers and students across English-language learning. Pearson English is made up of:</p>
<p>– Pearson ELT (English language Teaching): English language textbooks and e-learning</p>
<p>– Pearson English Business Solutions (formerly Global English): English language e-learning for large corporations</p>
<p>– Wall Street English: English language schools for business professionals</p>
<p>Pearson VUE is a provider in electronic testing for regulatory and certification boards. It delivers certification testing in over 150 countries.</p>
<p>The Financial Times (FT Group) formed part of this Pearson division until it was sold to Nikkei.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Concerns exist around the amount of influence Pearson, a commercial company, has on public education.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Other concerns are around tax avoidance, the high value contracts, and in one instance, laying off teachers to offset the high costs of testing.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson owns <a href="/wiki/Edexcel" title="Edexcel">Edexcel</a>, an education and examination board.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Edexcel has produced qualifications which link to Pearson texts, although Edexcel also continues to endorse textbooks published by other companies.<sup id="cite_ref-marking_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marking-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libyan_Investment_Authority">Libyan Investment Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Libyan Investment Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2010, Pearson Plc received notification that the Libyan Investment Authority (LIA) founded by Muammar Gaddafi's son Saif al-Islam Gaddafi as a sovereign fund, had acquired 24,431,000 shares within the company via Euroclear. On further investigation, Pearson said the LIA may have acquired an additional 2,141,179 shares, resulting in a total interest of 26,572,179 shares. At the time, this represented a major holding of 3.27% within the company and the investment was worth around £280 million.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_Avoidance">Tax Avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pearson_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tax Avoidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pearson has been criticised for using offshore <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">tax avoidance</a> schemes involving a host of companies at a service address in Luxembourg.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
