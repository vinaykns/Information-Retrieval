<p>The <b>Club of Rome</b> is a global <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> that deals with a variety of international issues, including the world economic system, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and environmental degradation. Founded in 1968 at <a href="/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei" title="Accademia dei Lincei">Accademia dei Lincei</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, the Club of Rome describes itself as "a group of world citizens, sharing a common concern for the future of humanity." It consists of current and former heads of state, UN bureaucrats, high-level politicians and government officials, diplomats, scientists, economists and business leaders from around the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It raised considerable public attention in 1972 with its report <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>. The club states that its mission is "to act as a global catalyst for change through the identification and analysis of the crucial problems facing humanity and the communication of such problems to the most important public and private decision makers as well as to the general public."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since 1 July 2008, the organization has its headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Winterthur" title="Winterthur">Winterthur</a>, Switzerland.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Club_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Club of Rome was founded in April 1968<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Aurelio_Peccei" title="Aurelio Peccei">Aurelio Peccei</a>, an Italian industrialist,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_King_(scientist)" title="Alexander King (scientist)">Alexander King</a>, a Scottish <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a>. It was formed when a small international group of people from the fields of academia, civil society, diplomacy, and industry, met at a villa in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, hence the name.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hasan_%C3%96zbekhan" title="Hasan Özbekhan">Hasan Özbekhan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erich_Jantsch" title="Erich Jantsch">Erich Jantsch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Christakis" title="Alexander Christakis">Alexander Christakis</a> were responsible for conceptualizing the original prospectus of the Club of Rome titled <i>The Predicament of Mankind</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Club of Rome raised considerable public attention with its report <i><a href="/wiki/Limits_to_Growth" class="mw-redirect" title="Limits to Growth">Limits to Growth</a></i>, which has sold 30 million copies in more than 30 translations,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> making it the best-selling environmental book in world history.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Published in 1972 and presented for the first time at the <a href="/wiki/St._Gallen_Symposium" title="St. Gallen Symposium">International Students' Committee (ISC) annual Management Symposium</a> in St. Gallen, Switzerland, it predicted that <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> could not continue indefinitely because of the limited availability of natural resources, particularly <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> increased public concern about this problem. However, even before <i>Limits to Growth</i> was published, <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Pestel" title="Eduard Pestel">Eduard Pestel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mihajlo_Mesarovic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mihajlo Mesarovic">Mihajlo Mesarovic</a> of <a href="/wiki/Case_Western_Reserve_University" title="Case Western Reserve University">Case Western Reserve University</a> had begun work on a far more elaborate model (it distinguished ten world regions and involved 200,000 equations compared with 1000 in the Meadows model). The research had the full support of the Club and the final publication, <i>Mankind at the Turning Point</i> was accepted as the official <i>Second Report</i> to the Club of Rome in 1974. In addition to providing a more refined regional breakdown, Pestel and Mesarovic had succeeded in integrating social as well as technical data. The <i>Second Report</i> revised the scenarios of the original <i>Limits to Growth</i> and gave a more optimistic prognosis for the future of the environment, noting that many of the factors were within human control and therefore that environmental and economic catastrophe were preventable or avoidable, hence the title.</p>
<p>In 1991, the Club published <i><a href="/wiki/The_First_Global_Revolution" title="The First Global Revolution">The First Global Revolution</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It analyses the problems of humanity, calling these collectively or in essence the 'problematique'. It notes (laments) that, historically, social or political unity has commonly been motivated by enemies in common: "The need for enemies seems to be a common historical factor. Some states have striven to overcome domestic failure and internal contradictions by blaming external enemies. The ploy of finding a scapegoat is as old as mankind itself - when things become too difficult at home, divert attention to adventure abroad. Bring the divided nation together to face an outside enemy, either a real one, or else one invented for the purpose. With the disappearance of the traditional enemy, the temptation is to use religious or ethnic minorities as scapegoats, especially those whose differences from the majority are disturbing."<sup id="cite_ref-King70_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King70-9">[9]</a></sup> "Every state has been so used to classifying its neighbours as friend or foe, that the sudden absence of traditional adversaries has left governments and public opinion with a great void to fill. New enemies have to be identified, new strategies imagined, and new weapons devised."<sup id="cite_ref-King70_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King70-9">[9]</a></sup> "In searching for a common enemy against whom we can unite, we came up with the idea that pollution, the threat of global warming, water shortages, famine and the like, would fit the bill. In their totality and their interactions these phenomena do constitute a common threat which must be confronted by everyone together. But in designating these dangers as the enemy, we fall into the trap, which we have already warned readers about, namely mistaking symptoms for causes. All these dangers are caused by human intervention in natural processes, and it is only through changed attitudes and behaviour that they can be overcome. The real enemy then is humanity itself."<sup id="cite_ref-King115_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King115-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 the Club of Rome established a <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a>, called <i>tt30</i>, comprising around 30 men and women ages 25–35. It aimed to identify and solve problems in the world, from a younger perspective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A study by Graham Turner of the research organisation <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" class="mw-redirect" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a> in Australia in 2008 found that "30 years of historical data compare favorably with key features of a business-as-usual scenario called the ‘‘standard run’’ scenario, which results in collapse of the global system midway through the 21st century."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Club_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to its website, the Club of Rome is composed of "scientists, economists, businessmen, international high civil servants, heads of state and former heads of state from all five continents who are convinced that the future of humankind is not determined once and for all and that each human being can contribute to the improvement of our societies."</p>
<p>The Club of Rome is a membership organization and has different membership categories. Full members, of which the Club of Rome currently has 106,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> are engaged in the research activities and projects of the Club of Rome and contribute to decision-making processes during the Club of Rome's annual General Assembly. Of the full members, 12 are elected to form the Executive Committee of the Club,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which sets the general direction and the agenda of the Club. Of the Executive Committee, two are elected as co-presidents, currently <a href="/wiki/Anders_Wijkman" title="Anders Wijkman">Anders Wijkman</a> (Sweden) and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ulrich_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker" title="Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker">Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker</a> (Germany), and two as vice-presidents, currently Dr. <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Peccei" title="Roberto Peccei">Roberto Peccei</a> (Italy and USA) and <a href="/wiki/Susana_Catalina_Chac%C3%B3n_Dom%C3%ADnguez" title="Susana Catalina Chacón Domínguez">Susana Catalina Chacón Domínguez</a> (Mexico). The secretary-general is equally elected amongst the members of the Executive Committee, and is currently <a href="/wiki/Graeme_Maxton" title="Graeme Maxton">Graeme Maxton</a> of Scotland. He is responsible for the day-to-day operation of the club at its headquarters in Winterthur, Switzerland. Other full members include: <a href="/wiki/Ugo_Bardi" title="Ugo Bardi">Ugo Bardi</a>, Juan Luis Cebrian, Obiora Ike, <a href="/wiki/Mugur_Is%C4%83rescu" title="Mugur Isărescu">Mugur Isărescu</a>, Esko Kalimo, <a href="/wiki/Ashok_Khosla" title="Ashok Khosla">Ashok Khosla</a>, Claude Martin, <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Peccei" title="Roberto Peccei">Roberto Peccei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorgen_Randers" class="mw-redirect" title="Jorgen Randers">Jorgen Randers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Suter" title="Keith Suter">Keith Suter</a>. Next to full members, the Club of Rome also has associate members, which equally participate in research and projects but have no vote in the annual General Assembly, as well as honorary members. Notable honorary members include <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_of_the_Netherlands" title="Beatrix of the Netherlands">Princess Beatrix of the Netherlands</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orio_Giarini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Orio Giarini (page does not exist)">Orio Giarini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Henrique_Cardoso" title="Fernando Henrique Cardoso">Fernando Henrique Cardoso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>, King <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos I of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horst_K%C3%B6hler" title="Horst Köhler">Horst Köhler</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manmohan_Singh" title="Manmohan Singh">Manmohan Singh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The annual General Assembly of 2016 took place in Berlin, Germany, between 10th and 11th of November. Among the guest speakers were former German President <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wulff" title="Christian Wulff">Christian Wulff</a>, current German Minister for Economic Cooperation and Development <a href="/wiki/Gerd_M%C3%BCller" title="Gerd Müller">Gerd Müller</a>, as well as Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Yunus" title="Muhammad Yunus">Muhammad Yunus</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_associations">National associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Club_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Club of Rome currently has national associations in 35 countries and territories, including Argentina, Australia, Austria, Belgium, Brazil, Bulgaria, Canada, Croatia, Czech Republic, Estonia, Finland, Georgia, Germany, Greece, Hungary, India, Italy, Japan, Jordan, Mexico, Nepal, Nigeria, Poland, Puerto Rico, Romania, Russia, Serbia, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, The Netherlands, Turkey, Ukraine, USA, Venezuela.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The mission of the national associations is to spread the ideas and vision of the Club of Rome in their respective countries, to offer solutions and to lobby for a more sustainable and just economy in the respective national context, and to support the international secretariat of the Club of Rome with the organization of events, such as the annual general assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_activities">Current activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Club_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009, the organization established a three-year program on "A New Path for World Development". In a flyer describing the project, it declared "The global issues which were the focus of the 1972 Report, "Limits to Growth" are even more severe and urgent today." The project has five issue areas: Environment and Resources, Globalization, International Development, Social Transformation, Peace and Security.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2010, nine reports to The Club of Rome have been published: <a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Economy" title="The Blue Economy">The Blue Economy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gunter_Pauli" title="Gunter Pauli">Gunter Pauli</a>, 2010), Factor Five (<a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ulrich_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker" title="Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker">Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker</a>, Charlie Hargroves, Michael H. Smith, Cheryl Desha, Peter Stasinopoulos, 2010), Bankrupting Nature (<a href="/wiki/Anders_Wijkman" title="Anders Wijkman">Anders Wijkman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johan_Rockstr%C3%B6m" title="Johan Rockström">Johan Rockström</a>, 2012), 2052 (<a href="/wiki/Jorgen_Randers" class="mw-redirect" title="Jorgen Randers">Jorgen Randers</a>, 2012), Extracted (<a href="/wiki/Ugo_Bardi" title="Ugo Bardi">Ugo Bardi</a>, 2014), Change the Story, Change the Future (<a href="/wiki/David_Korten" title="David Korten">David Korten</a>, 2015), On the Edge (<a href="/wiki/Claude_Martin" title="Claude Martin">Claude Martin</a>, 2015), To Choose Our Future (<a href="/wiki/Ashok_Khosla" title="Ashok Khosla">Ashok Khosla</a>, 2015), and Reinventing Prosperity (<a href="/wiki/Graeme_Maxton" title="Graeme Maxton">Graeme Maxton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jorgen_Randers" class="mw-redirect" title="Jorgen Randers">Jorgen Randers</a>, 2016). Moreover, the Club has released studies on the social benefits of a circular economy for Finland, Sweden, France, the Netherlands, Spain, Poland, and Czech Republic. Currently, members of The Club of Rome are working on two new Reports called "Come On" and " A Finer Future".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, the Club of Rome also initiated a new youth project called "Reclaim Economics". According to its website, with this project the Club of Rome "wants to inspire and support students, activists, intellectuals, artists, video-makers, teachers, professors and many others to help shift the teaching of economics away from the mathematical pseudo-science it has become. The well-being of people and the sustainability of the planet need to lie at the core of all economic thinking."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worldviews">Worldviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Club_of_Rome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Worldviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The club's worldview is based on three things. Firstly, it has a global perspective in viewing different situations, with the awareness that there is an increasing interdependency of countries. Secondly, it seeks holistic solutions understanding the different interactions in contemporary problems. Finally, the club has a long-term perspective for solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
