<p><b>Water treatment</b> is any process that makes <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> more acceptable for a specific end-use. The end use may be <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking</a>, industrial water supply, irrigation, river flow maintenance, water recreation or many other uses, including being safely returned to the environment. Water treatment removes <a href="/wiki/Contaminant" class="mw-redirect" title="Contaminant">contaminants</a> and undesirable components, or reduces their concentration so that the water becomes fit for its desired end-use.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_for_drinking_water_production">Treatment for drinking water production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Treatment for drinking water production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">Water purification</a></div>
<p>Treatment for <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> production involves the removal of contaminants from raw water to produce water that is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Purity" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Purity">pure</a> enough for human consumption without any short term or long term risk of any adverse health effect. Substances that are removed during the process of drinking water treatment include <a href="/wiki/Suspended_solids" title="Suspended solids">suspended solids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>.</p>
<p>The processes involved in removing the contaminants include physical processes such as <a href="/wiki/Settling" title="Settling">settling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_process" title="Chemical process">chemical processes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Disinfection" class="mw-redirect" title="Disinfection">disinfection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">coagulation</a> and biological processes such as <a href="/wiki/Slow_sand_filter" title="Slow sand filter">slow sand filtration</a>.</p>
<p>Measures taken to ensure water quality not only relate to the treatment of the water, but to its conveyance and distribution after treatment. It is therefore common practice to keep residual disinfectants in the treated water to kill bacteriological contamination during distribution.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) guidelines are a general set of standards intended to apply where better local standards are not implemeted. More rigorous standards apply across Europe, the USA and in most other developed countries. followed throughout the world for drinking water quality requirements.</p>

<p>A combination selected from the following processes is used for municipal drinking water treatment worldwide:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pre-<a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">chlorination</a> for algae control and arresting biological growth</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aeration" title="Aeration">Aeration</a> along with pre-chlorination for removal of dissolved iron when present with small amounts relatively of manganese</li>
<li>Coagulation for <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">flocculation</a> or slow-sand filtration</li>
<li>Coagulant aids, also known as <a href="/wiki/Polyelectrolyte" title="Polyelectrolyte">polyelectrolytes</a> – to improve coagulation and for more robust floc formation</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">Sedimentation</a> for solids separation that is removal of suspended solids trapped in the floc</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">Filtration</a> to remove particles from water either by passage through a sand bed that can be washed and reused or by passage through a purpose designed filter that may be washable.</li>
<li>Disinfection for killing bacteria viruses and other pathogens.</li>
</ul>
<p>Technologies for potable water and other uses are well developed, and generalized designs are available from which treatment processes can be selected for pilot testing on the specific source water. In addition, a number of private companies provide patented technological solutions for treatment of specific contaminants. Automation of water and waste-water treatment is common in the developed world. Source water quality through the seasons, scale and environmental impact can dictate capital costs and operating costs. End use of the treated water dictates the necessary quality monitoring technologies, and locally available skills typically dictate the level of automation adopted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polluted_water_treatment">Polluted water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Polluted water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">Wastewater treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg/300px-Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg" width="300" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg/450px-Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg/600px-Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bragan%C3%A7a43.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sewage treatment plant in northern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Wastewater treatment is the process that removes the majority of the <a href="/wiki/Contaminant" class="mw-redirect" title="Contaminant">contaminants</a> from wastewater or <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> and produces both a liquid effluent suitable for disposal to the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Sludge" title="Sludge">sludge</a>. Biological processes can be employed in the treatment of wastewater and these processes may include, for example, <a href="/wiki/Aerated_lagoon" title="Aerated lagoon">aerated lagoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activated_sludge" title="Activated sludge">activated sludge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Slow_sand_filter" title="Slow sand filter">slow sand filters</a>. To be effective, sewage must be conveyed to a treatment plant by appropriate <a href="/wiki/Sewage_collection_and_disposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Sewage collection and disposal">pipes and infrastructure</a> and the process itself must be subject to regulation and controls. Some wastewaters require different and sometimes specialized treatment methods. At the simplest level, treatment of sewage and most wastewaters is carried out through separation of <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> from <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>, usually by <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation_(water_treatment)" title="Sedimentation (water treatment)">sedimentation</a>. By progressively converting dissolved material into solids, usually a biological floc, which is then settled out, an effluent stream of increasing purity is produced.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_Primer_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Primer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_water_and_wastewater_treatment">Industrial water and wastewater treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial water and wastewater treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_water_treatment" title="Industrial water treatment">Industrial water treatment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">Industrial wastewater treatment</a></div>
<p>Two of the main processes of industrial water treatment are <i>boiler water treatment</i> and <i>cooling water treatment</i>. A lack of proper water treatment can lead to the reaction of solids and bacteria within pipe work and boiler housing. Steam boilers can suffer from scale or <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> when left untreated. Scale deposits can lead to weak and dangerous machinery, while additional fuel is required to heat the same level of water because of the rise in thermal resistance. Poor quality dirty water can become a breeding ground for bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Legionella" title="Legionella">Legionella</a></i> causing a risk to public health.</p>
<p>With the proper treatment, a significant proportion of industrial on-site wastewater might be reusable. This can save money in three ways: lower charges for lower water consumption, lower charges for the smaller volume of effluent water discharged and lower energy costs due to the recovery of heat in recycled wastewater.</p>
<p>Corrosion in low pressure boilers can be caused by dissolved oxygen, acidity and excessive alkalinity. Water treatment therefore should remove the dissolved oxygen and maintain the boiler water with the appropriate pH and alkalinity levels. Without effective water treatment, a cooling water system can suffer from scale formation, corrosion and fouling and may become a breeding ground for harmful bacteria. This reduces efficiency, shortens plant life and makes operations unreliable and unsafe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_water_treatment">Domestic water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Domestic water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water supplied to domestic properties may be further treated before use, often using an in-line treatment process. Such treatments can include <a href="/wiki/Water_softening" title="Water softening">water softening</a> or ion exchange. Many propriety systems also claim to remove residual disinfectants and <a href="/wiki/Toxic_heavy_metal" title="Toxic heavy metal">heavy metal</a> ions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Desalination">Desalination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Desalination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">Desalination</a></div>
<p>Saline water can be treated to yield fresh water. Two main processes are used, <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>. Both methods require high energy inputs and are usually only used where fresh water is difficult to source.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Field_processes">Field processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Field processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portable_water_purification" title="Portable water purification">Portable water purification</a></div>
<p>Living away from drinking water supplies often requires some form of portable water treatment process. These can vary in complexity from the simple addition of a disinfectant tablet in a hiker's water bottle through to complex multi-stage processes carried by boat or plane to disaster areas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultra_pure_water_production">Ultra pure water production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ultra pure water production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some industries such as the production of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_wafer" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicon wafer">silicon wafers</a>, space technology and many high quality <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgical</a> process require <a href="/wiki/Ultrapure_water" title="Ultrapure water">ultrapure water</a>. The production of such water typically involves many stages, and can include reverse osmosis, <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> and several distillation stages using solid <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> apparatus.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_water_supply_and_sanitation" title="History of water supply and sanitation">History of water supply and sanitation</a></div>
<p>Early water treatment methods still used included sand filtration and chlorination. The first documented use of <a href="/wiki/Sand_filter" title="Sand filter">sand filters</a> to purify the water supply dates to 1804, when the owner of a bleachery in <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Paisley, Scotland">Paisley, Scotland</a>, John Gibb, installed an experimental filter, selling his unwanted surplus to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This method was refined in the following two decades, and it culminated in the first treated public water supply in the world, installed by the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Waterworks_Company" title="Chelsea Waterworks Company">Chelsea Waterworks Company</a> in London in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-Chelsea_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chelsea-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_countries">Developing countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_treatment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Developing countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2006, <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a> are estimated to have caused 1.8 million deaths each year. These deaths are attributable to inadequate public <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> systems and in these cases, proper <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewerage</a> (or other options such as <a href="/wiki/Greywater_treatment" class="mw-redirect" title="Greywater treatment">small-scale wastewater treatment</a>) that must be installed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">Appropriate technology</a> options in water treatment include both community-scale and household-scale <a href="/wiki/Point-of-use" class="mw-redirect" title="Point-of-use">point-of-use</a> (POU) designs.<sup id="cite_ref-cawstHWT_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cawstHWT-9">[9]</a></sup> Such designs may employ <a href="/wiki/SODIS" class="mw-redirect" title="SODIS">solar water disinfection</a> methods, using solar irradiation to inactivate harmful waterborne microorganisms directly, mainly by the UV-A component of the solar spectrum, or indirectly through the presence of an oxide <a href="/wiki/Photocatalysis" title="Photocatalysis">photocatalyst</a>, typically supported <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">TiO<sub>2</sub></a> in its <a href="/wiki/Anatase" title="Anatase">anatase</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> phases.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Despite progress in <a href="/wiki/SODIS" class="mw-redirect" title="SODIS">SODIS</a> technology, military surplus water treatment units like the <a href="/wiki/ERDLator" title="ERDLator">ERDLator</a> are still frequently used in developing countries. Newer military style <a href="/wiki/Reverse_Osmosis_Water_Purification_Unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverse Osmosis Water Purification Unit">Reverse Osmosis Water Purification Units</a> (ROWPU) are portable, self-contained water treatment plants are becoming more available for public use.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>For waterborne disease reduction to last, water treatment programs that research and development groups start in <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> must be sustainable by the citizens of those countries. This can ensure the efficiency of such programs after the departure of the research team, as monitoring is difficult because of the remoteness of many locations.</p>
