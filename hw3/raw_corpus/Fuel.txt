<p>A <b>fuel</b> is any material that can be made to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">react</a> with other substances so that it releases <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a> as <a href="/wiki/Heat_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat energy">heat</a> or to be used for <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a>. The concept was originally applied solely to those materials capable of releasing <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> but has since also been applied to other sources of heat energy such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>).</p>
<p>The heat energy released by reactions of fuels is converted into <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> via a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>. Other times the heat itself is valued for warmth, <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>, or industrial processes, as well as the illumination that comes with combustion. Fuels are also used in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisms">organisms</a> in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, where organic molecules are oxidized to release usable energy. <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">Hydrocarbons</a> and related oxygen-containing molecules are by far the most common source of fuel used by humans, but other substances, including radioactive metals, are also utilized.</p>
<p>Fuels are contrasted with other substances or devices storing potential energy, such as those that directly release <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheels</a>, springs, compressed air, or water in a reservoir).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known use of fuel was the combustion of wood or sticks by <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> near 2,000,000 (two million) years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Throughout most of human history fuels derived from plants or animal fat were only used by humans. <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">Charcoal</a>, a wood derivative, has been used since at least 6,000 BCE for melting metals. It was only supplanted by <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>, derived from coal, as European forests started to become depleted around the 18th century. Charcoal briquettes are now commonly used as a fuel for <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">barbecue</a> cooking.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coal was first used as a fuel around 1000 BCE in China. With the energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> that could be released through <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but the concept development of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> in the United Kingdom in 1769, coal came into more common use as a power source. Coal was later used to drive ships and <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotives</a>. By the 19th century, gas extracted from coal was being used for street lighting in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. In the 20th and 21st centuries, the primary use of coal is to generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, providing 40% of the world's electrical power supply in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fossil fuels were rapidly adopted during the industrial revolution, because they were more concentrated and flexible than traditional energy sources, such as water power. They have become a pivotal part of our contemporary society, with most countries in the world burning fossil fuels in order to produce power.</p>
<p>Currently the trend has been towards renewable fuels, such as <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy#Biofuel" title="Renewable energy">biofuels</a> like alcohols.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chemical fuels are substances that release energy by reacting with substances around them, most notably by the process of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>. Most of the chemical energy released in combustion was not stored in the chemical bonds of the fuel, but in the weak double bond of molecular oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-Schmidt-Rohr_15_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-Rohr_15-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_fuel">Solid fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solid fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">Solid fuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_anthracite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Coal_anthracite.jpg/220px-Coal_anthracite.jpg" width="220" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Coal_anthracite.jpg/330px-Coal_anthracite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/72/Coal_anthracite.jpg 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="337" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_anthracite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coal is an important solid fuel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Solid fuel refers to various types of <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> material that are used as fuel to produce <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> and provide <a href="/wiki/Heating" class="mw-redirect" title="Heating">heating</a>, usually released through <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>. Solid fuels include <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Wood_fuel" title="Wood fuel">wood fuel</a>), <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexamine_fuel_tablet" title="Hexamine fuel tablet">Hexamine fuel tablets</a>, and pellets made from wood (see <a href="/wiki/Wood_pellets" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood pellets">wood pellets</a>), <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grains</a>. <a href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid-fuel rocket">Solid-fuel rocket</a> technology also uses solid fuel (see <a href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel#Solid_propellants" class="mw-redirect" title="Rocket fuel">solid propellants</a>). Solid fuels have been used by humanity for many years to <a href="/wiki/Making_fire" class="mw-redirect" title="Making fire">create fire</a>. Coal was the fuel source which enabled the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, from firing <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a>, to running <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a>. Wood was also extensively used to run <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a>. Both peat and coal are still used in <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> today. The use of some solid fuels (e.g. coal) is restricted or prohibited in some urban areas, due to unsafe levels of toxic emissions. The use of other solid fuels such as wood is decreasing as heating technology and the availability of good quality fuel improves. In some areas, <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">smokeless coal</a> is often the only solid fuel used. In Ireland, peat <a href="/wiki/Briquette" title="Briquette">briquettes</a> are used as smokeless fuel. They are also used to start a coal fire.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_fuels">Liquid fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Liquid fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">Liquid fuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benzinepomp002.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Benzinepomp002.jpg/220px-Benzinepomp002.jpg" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Benzinepomp002.jpg/330px-Benzinepomp002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/Benzinepomp002.jpg 2x" data-file-width="352" data-file-height="288" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Benzinepomp002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Gasoline_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasoline station">gasoline station</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Liquid fuels are combustible or energy-generating molecules that can be harnessed to create <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a>, usually producing <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>; they also must take the shape of their container. It is the fumes of liquid fuels that are flammable instead of the fluid.</p>
<p>Most liquid fuels in widespread use are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossilized remains</a> of dead plants and animals by exposure to heat and pressure in the Earth's crust. However, there are several types, such as hydrogen fuel (for <a href="/wiki/Automotive" class="mw-redirect" title="Automotive">automotive</a> uses), ethanol, jet fuel and biodiesel which are all categorized as a liquid fuel. <a href="/wiki/Emulsified_fuel" title="Emulsified fuel">Emulsified fuels</a> of oil-in-water such as <a href="/wiki/Orimulsion" title="Orimulsion">orimulsion</a> have been developed a way to make heavy oil fractions usable as liquid fuels. Many liquid fuels play a primary role in transportation and the economy.</p>
<p>Some common properties of liquid fuels are that they are easy to transport, and can be handled with relative ease. Also they are relatively easy to use for all engineering applications, and home use. Fuels like kerosene are rationed in some countries, for example available in government subsidized shops in India for home use.</p>
<p>Conventional diesel is similar to gasoline in that it is a mixture of aliphatic hydrocarbons extracted from petroleum. Kerosene is used in <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamps</a> and as a fuel for cooking, heating, and small engines. <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a>, composed chiefly of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, can only exist as a liquid at very low temperatures (regardless of pressure), which limits its direct use as a liquid fuel in most applications. <a href="/wiki/LP_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="LP gas">LP gas</a> is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>, both of which are easily compressible gases under standard atmospheric conditions. It offers many of the advantages of <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> (CNG), but is denser than air, does not burn as cleanly, and is much more easily compressed. Commonly used for cooking and space heating, LP gas and compressed propane are seeing increased use in motorized vehicles; propane is the third most commonly used motor fuel globally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaseous_fuels">Gaseous fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gaseous fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">Fuel gas</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Propane_tank_20lb.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Propane_tank_20lb.jpg/170px-Propane_tank_20lb.jpg" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Propane_tank_20lb.jpg/255px-Propane_tank_20lb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Propane_tank_20lb.jpg/340px-Propane_tank_20lb.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1973" data-file-height="2575" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Propane_tank_20lb.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 20-pound (<span class="nowrap">9.1 kg</span>) propane cylinder</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fuel gas is any one of a number of fuels that under ordinary conditions are <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a>. Many fuel gases are composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, or mixtures thereof. Such gases are sources of potential <a href="/wiki/Heat_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat energy">heat energy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Light_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Light energy">light energy</a> that can be readily transmitted and distributed through pipes from the point of origin directly to the place of consumption. Fuel gas is contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid fuels">liquid fuels</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">solid fuels</a>, though some fuel gases are <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">liquefied</a> for storage or transport. While their gaseous nature can be advantageous, avoiding the difficulty of transporting solid fuel and the dangers of spillage inherent in liquid fuels, it can also be dangerous. It is possible for a fuel gas to be undetected and collect in certain areas, leading to the risk of a <a href="/wiki/Gas_explosion" title="Gas explosion">gas explosion</a>. For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Odorizer" title="Odorizer">odorizers</a> are added to most fuel gases so that they may be detected by a distinct smell. The most common type of fuel gas in current use is <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels">Biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a> can be broadly defined as solid, liquid, or gas fuel consisting of, or derived from <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. Biomass can also be used directly for heating or power—known as <i>biomass fuel</i>. Biofuel can be produced from any carbon source that can be replenished rapidly e.g. plants. Many different plants and plant-derived materials are used for biofuel manufacture.</p>
<p>Perhaps the earliest fuel employed by humans is wood. Evidence shows controlled fire was used up to 1.5 million years ago at <a href="/wiki/Swartkrans" title="Swartkrans">Swartkrans</a>, South Africa. It is unknown which hominid species first used fire, as both <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus" title="Australopithecus">Australopithecus</a></i> and an early species of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Homo (genus)">Homo</a></i> were present at the sites.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As a fuel, wood has remained in use up until the present day, although it has been superseded for many purposes by other sources. Wood has an <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of 10–20 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">MJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently biofuels have been developed for use in automotive transport (for example <a href="/wiki/Bioethanol" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioethanol">Bioethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a>), but there is widespread public debate about how carbon efficient these fuels are.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuels">Fossil fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fossil fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">Fossil fuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Extraction_of_oil.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Extraction_of_oil.jpg/220px-Extraction_of_oil.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Extraction_of_oil.jpg/330px-Extraction_of_oil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Extraction_of_oil.jpg/440px-Extraction_of_oil.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="334" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Extraction_of_oil.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Extraction of petroleum</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fossil fuels are <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, primarily <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid_petroleum" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid petroleum">liquid petroleum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>), formed from the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilized remains</a> of ancient plants and animals<sup id="cite_ref-elements1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elements1-8">[8]</a></sup> by exposure to high heat and pressure in the absence of oxygen in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> over hundreds of millions of years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Commonly, the term fossil fuel also includes hydrocarbon-containing <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> that are not derived entirely from biological sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Tar_sands" class="mw-redirect" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a>. These latter sources are properly known as <i>mineral fuels</i>.</p>
<p>Fossil fuels contain high percentages of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and include <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> They range from <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> materials with low <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>:<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> ratios like <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, to liquid petroleum to nonvolatile materials composed of almost pure carbon, like <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">anthracite</a> coal. Methane can be found in <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fields, alone, associated with oil, or in the form of <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane clathrates">methane clathrates</a>. Fossil fuels formed from the <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilized remains</a> of dead plants<sup id="cite_ref-elements1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elements1-8">[8]</a></sup> by exposure to heat and pressure in the Earth's crust over millions of years.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Petroleum#Biogenic_theory" title="Petroleum">biogenic theory</a> was first introduced by German scholar <a href="/wiki/Georg_Agricola" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg Agricola">Georg Agricola</a> in 1556 and later by <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Lomonosov" title="Mikhail Lomonosov">Mikhail Lomonosov</a> in the 18th century.</p>
<p>It was estimated by the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">Energy Information Administration</a> that in 2007 primary sources of energy consisted of petroleum 36.0%, coal 27.4%, natural gas 23.0%, amounting to an 86.4% share for fossil fuels in primary energy consumption in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Non-fossil sources in 2006 included <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> 6.3%, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a> 8.5%, and others (<a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood_fuel" title="Wood fuel">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">waste</a>) amounting to 0.9%.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> World energy consumption was growing about 2.3% per year.</p>
<p>Fossil fuels are <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-renewable resources">non-renewable resources</a> because they take millions of years to form, and reserves are being depleted much faster than new ones are being made. So we must conserve these fuels and use them judiciously. The production and use of fossil fuels raise environmental concerns. A global movement toward the generation of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> is therefore under way to help meet increased energy needs. The burning of fossil fuels produces around 21.3 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> (21.3 <a href="/wiki/Gigatonne" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigatonne">gigatonnes</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) per year, but it is estimated that natural processes can only absorb about half of that amount, so there is a net increase of 10.65 billion tonnes of atmospheric carbon dioxide per year (one tonne of atmospheric carbon is equivalent to 44/12 or 3.7 tonnes of carbon dioxide).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Carbon dioxide is one of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> that enhances <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> and contributes to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, causing the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">average surface temperature</a> of the Earth to rise in response, which <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">the vast majority of climate scientists</a> agree will cause major <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">adverse effects</a>. Fuels are a source of energy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear">Nuclear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nuclear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">Nuclear fuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:251px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg" width="249" height="139" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="249" data-file-height="139" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<b>CANDU fuel bundles</b> Two <a href="/wiki/CANDU" class="mw-redirect" title="CANDU">CANDU</a> ("CANada Deuterium Uranium") fuel bundles, each about 50<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>cm long and 10<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>cm in diameter</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nuclear fuel is any material that is consumed to derive <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a>. Technically speaking, All matter can be a nuclear fuel because any element under the right conditions will release nuclear energy,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Fuel#Dubious" title="Talk:Fuel">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> but the materials commonly referred to as nuclear fuels are those that will produce energy without being placed under extreme duress. Nuclear fuel is a material that can be 'burned' by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> to derive <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a>. <i>Nuclear fuel</i> can refer to the fuel itself, or to physical objects (for example bundles composed of <b>fuel rods</b>) composed of the fuel material, mixed with structural, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderating</a>, or neutron reflecting materials.</p>
<p>Most nuclear fuels contain heavy <a href="/wiki/Fissile" class="mw-redirect" title="Fissile">fissile</a> elements that are capable of nuclear fission. When these fuels are struck by neutrons, they are in turn capable of emitting neutrons when they break apart. This makes possible a self-sustaining <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain reaction</a> that releases energy with a controlled rate in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> or with a very rapid uncontrolled rate in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapon</a>.</p>
<p>The most common fissile nuclear fuels are <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a> (<sup>235</sup>U) and <a href="/wiki/Pu-239" class="mw-redirect" title="Pu-239">plutonium-239</a> (<sup>239</sup>Pu). The actions of mining, refining, purifying, using, and ultimately disposing of nuclear fuel together make up the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">nuclear fuel cycle</a>. Not all types of nuclear fuels create power from nuclear fission. <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-238" title="Plutonium-238">Plutonium-238</a> and some other elements are used to produce small amounts of nuclear power by <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generators</a> and other types of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_battery" title="Atomic battery">atomic batteries</a>. Also, light <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclides</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> (<sup>3</sup>H) can be used as fuel for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>. Nuclear fuel has the highest <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of all practical fuel sources.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>The most common type of nuclear fuel used by humans is heavy <a href="/wiki/Fissile" class="mw-redirect" title="Fissile">fissile</a> elements that can be made to undergo <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain reactions</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear fission reactor</a>; <i>nuclear fuel</i> can refer to the material or to physical objects (for example fuel bundles composed of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_rod" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel rod">fuel rods</a>) composed of the fuel material, perhaps mixed with structural, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderating</a>, or neutron reflecting materials. The most common fissile nuclear fuels are <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235"><sup>235</sup>U</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pu-239" class="mw-redirect" title="Pu-239"><sup>239</sup>Pu</a>, and the actions of mining, refining, purifying, using, and ultimately disposing of these elements together make up the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle" title="Nuclear fuel cycle">nuclear fuel cycle</a>, which is important for its relevance to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> generation and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fusion">Fusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fuels that produce energy by the process of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> are currently not utilized by humans but are the main source of fuel for <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Fusion fuels tend to be light elements such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> which will combine easily. Energy is required to start fusion by raising temperature so high all materials would turn into plasma, and allow nuclei to collide and stick together with each other before repelling due to electric charge. This process is called fusion and it can give out energy.</p>
<p>In stars that undergo nuclear fusion, fuel consists of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic nuclei">atomic nuclei</a> that can release energy by the absorption of a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>. In most stars the fuel is provided by hydrogen, which can combine to form <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Proton-proton_chain_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Proton-proton chain reaction">proton-proton chain reaction</a> or by the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a>. When the hydrogen fuel is exhausted, nuclear fusion can continue with progressively heavier elements, although the net energy released is lower because of the smaller difference in nuclear binding energy. Once <a href="/wiki/Iron-56" title="Iron-56">iron-56</a> or <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_nickel" title="Isotopes of nickel">nickel-56</a> nuclei are produced, no further energy can be obtained by nuclear fusion as these have the highest nuclear binding energies. The elements then on use up energy instead of giving off energy when fused. Therefore, fusion stops and the star dies. In attempts by humans, fusion is only carried out with hydrogen (isotope of 2 and 3) to form helium-4 as this reaction gives out the most net energy. Electric confinement (<a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a>), <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement" class="mw-redirect" title="Inertial confinement">inertial confinement</a>(heating by laser) and heating by strong electric currents are the popular methods used. .<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
