<p><b>Lockheed Martin</b> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:LMT">LMT</a>) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> global <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">defense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">advanced technologies</a> company with worldwide interests. It was formed by the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_integration" title="Horizontal integration">merger</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed Corporation</a> with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> in March 1995. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metropolitan_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Metropolitan Area">Washington, DC, area</a>. Lockheed Martin employs 126,000 people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Martin-2015-page-10_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Martin-2015-page-10-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marillyn_Hewson" title="Marillyn Hewson">Marillyn Hewson</a> is the current President and Chief Executive Officer.</p>
<p>Lockheed Martin is one of the largest companies in the aerospace, defense, security, and technologies industry. It is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense contractor">defense contractor</a> based on revenue for fiscal year 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Top_20_Defense_Contractors_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top_20_Defense_Contractors-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2013, 78% of Lockheed Martin's revenues came from military sales;<sup id="cite_ref-DefenceIQ_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DefenceIQ-6">[6]</a></sup> it topped the <a href="/wiki/Top_100_US_Federal_Contractors" class="mw-redirect" title="Top 100 US Federal Contractors">list of US federal government contractors</a> and received nearly 10% of the funds paid out by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Pentagon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2009 US government contracts accounted for $38.4&#160;billion (85%), foreign government contracts $5.8&#160;billion (13%), and commercial and other contracts for $900 million (2%).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lockheed Martin operates in five business segments: Aeronautics, Information Systems &amp; Global Solutions, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Missiles_%26_Fire_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Missiles &amp; Fire Control">Missiles and Fire Control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Mission_Systems_and_Sensors" title="Lockheed Martin Mission Systems and Sensors">Rotary and Mission Systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Space_Systems" title="Lockheed Martin Space Systems">Space Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Martin-2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Martin-2014-9">[9]</a></sup> The company received the <a href="/wiki/Collier_Trophy" title="Collier Trophy">Collier Trophy</a> six times, including in 2001 for being part of developing the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_X-35" title="Lockheed Martin X-35">X-35</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35B</a> <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_LiftSystem" title="Rolls-Royce LiftSystem">LiftFan Propulsion System</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-LMCollier_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LMCollier-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LiftFanPatent2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiftFanPatent2-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collier_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collier-12">[12]</a></sup> and most recently in 2006 for leading the team that developed the <a href="/wiki/F-22_Raptor" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a> fighter jet. Lockheed Martin is currently developing the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a> and leads the international supply chain, leads the team for the development and implementation of technology solutions for the new <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Fence" title="Space Fence">Space Fence</a> (<a href="/wiki/AFSSS" class="mw-redirect" title="AFSSS">AFSSS</a> replacement),<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceFence_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceFence-13">[13]</a></sup> and is the primary contractor for the development of the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion (spacecraft)</a> command module.<sup id="cite_ref-OrionSpacecraft_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OrionSpacecraft-14">[14]</a></sup> The company also invests in healthcare systems, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">intelligent energy distribution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">compact nuclear fusion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LockheedMartinFusion_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LockheedMartinFusion-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Merger talks between <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> began in March 1994, with the companies announcing their $10&#160;billion planned merger on August 30, 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The deal was finalized on March 15, 1995, when the two companies' shareholders approved the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The segments of the two companies not retained by the new company formed the basis for the present <a href="/wiki/L-3_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="L-3 Communications">L-3 Communications</a>, a mid-size defense contractor in its own right. Lockheed Martin also later spun off the materials company <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta_Materials" title="Martin Marietta Materials">Martin Marietta Materials</a>.</p>
<p>Both companies contributed important products to the new portfolio. Lockheed products included the <a href="/wiki/Trident_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident missile">Trident missile</a>, <a href="/wiki/P-3_Orion" class="mw-redirect" title="P-3 Orion">P-3 Orion</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/F-22_Raptor" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a>, <a href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" class="mw-redirect" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a>, <a href="/wiki/A-4AR_Fightinghawk" class="mw-redirect" title="A-4AR Fightinghawk">A-4AR Fightinghawk</a> and the DSCS-3 satellite. Martin Marietta products included <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan rockets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> (management contract acquired in 1993), <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank" title="Space Shuttle external tank">Space Shuttle External Tank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viking_1" title="Viking 1">Viking 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viking_2" title="Viking 2">Viking 2</a> landers, the Transfer Orbit Stage (under subcontract to <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a>) and various satellite models.</p>
<p>On April 22, 1996, Lockheed Martin completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Loral_Corporation" title="Loral Corporation">Loral Corporation</a>'s defense electronics and system integration businesses for $9.1&#160;billion, the deal having been announced in January. The remainder of Loral became <a href="/wiki/Loral_Space_%26_Communications" title="Loral Space &amp; Communications">Loral Space &amp; Communications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin abandoned plans for a $8.3&#160;billion merger with <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> on July 16, 1998, due to government concerns over the potential strength of the new group; Lockheed/Northrop would have had control of 25% of the Department of Defense's procurement budget.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Climate_Orbiter" title="Mars Climate Orbiter">Mars Climate Orbiter</a>, Lockheed Martin incorrectly provided NASA with software using measurements in <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">US Customary force units</a> when metric was expected; this resulted in the loss of the Orbiter at a cost of $125&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The development of the spacecraft cost $193.1&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In May 2001, Lockheed Martin sold <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Platform_Solutions" class="mw-redirect" title="BAE Systems Platform Solutions">Lockheed Martin Control Systems</a> to <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>. On November 27, 2000, Lockheed completed the sale of its <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Aerospace_Electronic_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Aerospace Electronic Systems">Aerospace Electronic Systems</a> business to BAE Systems for $1.67&#160;billion, a deal announced in July 2000. This group encompassed <a href="/wiki/Sanders_Associates" title="Sanders Associates">Sanders Associates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Systems" title="Fairchild Systems">Fairchild Systems</a>, and Lockheed Martin Space Electronics &amp; Communications.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2001, Lockheed Martin won the contract to build the <a href="/wiki/F-35_Lightning_II" class="mw-redirect" title="F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a>; this was the largest fighter aircraft procurement project since the F-16, with an initial order of 3,000 aircraft. In 2001, Lockheed Martin settled a nine–year investigation conducted by NASA's Office of Inspector General with the assistance of the Defense Contract Audit Agency. The company paid the United States government $7.1&#160;million based on allegations that its predecessor, Lockheed Engineering Science Corporation, submitted false lease costs claims to NASA.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 12, 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> reported that when Robert Stevens took control of Lockheed Martin in 2004, he faced the dilemma that within 10 years, 100,000 of the about 130,000 Lockheed Martin employees – more than three-quarters – would be retiring.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On August 31, 2006, Lockheed Martin won a $3.9&#160;billion contract from NASA to design and build the CEV capsule, later named <a href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Orion (Constellation program)"><i>Orion</i></a> for the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> rocket in the Constellation Program. In 2009, NASA reduced the capsule crew requirements from the initial six seats to four for transport to the International Space Station.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 13, 2008, Lockheed Martin acquired the government business unit of Nantero, Inc., a company that had developed methods and processes for incorporating <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon nanotubes">carbon nanotubes</a> in next-generation electronic devices.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Martin_Acquires_Nantero_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Martin_Acquires_Nantero-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2009, Lockheed Martin bought Unitech.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 18, 2010, Lockheed Martin announced that it would be closing its <a href="/wiki/Eagan,_Minnesota" title="Eagan, Minnesota">Eagan, Minnesota</a> location by 2013 to reduce costs and optimize capacity at its locations nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In January 2011, Lockheed Martin agreed to pay the US Government $2&#160;million to settle allegations that the company submitted <a href="/wiki/False_Claims_Act" title="False Claims Act">false claims</a> on a U.S. government contract for that amount. The allegations came from a contract with the Naval Oceanographic Office Major Shared Resource Center in Mississippi.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> On May 25, 2011 Lockheed Martin bought the first Quantum Computing System from <a href="/wiki/D-Wave_Systems" title="D-Wave Systems">D-Wave Systems</a>. Lockheed Martin and D-Wave will collaborate to realize the benefits of a computing platform based upon a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computing" title="Quantum computing">quantum annealing processor</a>, as applied to some of Lockheed Martin's most challenging computation problems. Lockheed Martin established a multi-year contract that includes one system, maintenance and services. Potentially an important milestone for both companies.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 28, 2011 it was reported that a cyber-attack using <a href="/wiki/SecurID#March_2011_system_compromise" class="mw-redirect" title="SecurID">previously stolen EMC files</a> had broken through to sensitive materials at the contractor.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It is unclear if the Lockheed incident is the specific prompt whereby on June 1, 2011, the new United States military strategy, makes explicit that a cyberattack is <i><a href="/wiki/Casus_belli" title="Casus belli">casus belli</a></i> for a traditional act of war.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 10, 2012, Lockheed Martin announced it was cutting its workforce by 740 workers to reduce costs and remain competitive as necessary for future growth.<sup id="cite_ref-cuts_740_jobs_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cuts_740_jobs-36">[36]</a></sup> On August 2, 2012, the Vice President for Business Development, George Standridge stated that his company has offered 6 more C-130J aircraft to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Air_Force" title="Indian Air Force">Indian Air Force</a>, for which discussions are underway with the Indian Government.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> On November 27, 2012, Lockheed Martin announced that <a href="/wiki/Marillyn_Hewson" title="Marillyn Hewson">Marillyn Hewson</a> will become the corporation's chief executive officer on January 1, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 7, 2013, Lockheed Martin Canada announced that it will be acquiring the engine maintenance, repair and overhaul assets from <a href="/wiki/Aveos_Fleet_Performance" title="Aveos Fleet Performance">Aveos Fleet Performance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. On July 3, 2013, Lockheed Martin announced that it was partnering with <a href="/wiki/DreamHammer" title="DreamHammer">DreamHammer</a> to use the company's software for integrated command and control of its unmanned aerial vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Bell_Helicopter" title="Bell Helicopter">Bell Helicopter</a> to propose the <a href="/wiki/V-280_Valor" class="mw-redirect" title="V-280 Valor">V-280 Valor</a> <a href="/wiki/Tiltrotor" title="Tiltrotor">tiltrotor</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Future_Vertical_Lift" title="Future Vertical Lift">Future Vertical Lift</a> (FVL) program.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In September 2013, Lockheed Martin acquired the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>-based tech firm, <a href="/wiki/Amor_Group" title="Amor Group">Amor Group</a>, saying the deal would aid its plans to expand internationally and into non-defence markets.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> On November 14, 2013, Lockheed announced they would be closing their <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron, Ohio</a> facility laying off 500 employees and transferring other employees to other locations.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2014, Lockheed Martin acquired Beontra AG, a provider of integrated planning and demand forecasting tools for airport, planning to expand their business in commercial airport information technology solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Also in March 2014, Lockheed Martin announced its acquisition of Industrial Defender Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On June 2, 2014 Lockheed Martin received a Pentagon contract to build a space fence that would track debris, keeping it from damaging satellites and spacecrafts.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In September 2014, Lockheed agreed a deal to sell 40 <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35 fighter jets</a> to <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> for delivery between 2018 and 2021, for a fee in the region of $7.06 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 20, 2015, Lockheed Martin announced plans to purchase <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_Aircraft" title="Sikorsky Aircraft">Sikorsky Aircraft</a> from <a href="/wiki/United_Technologies_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="United Technologies Corporation">United Technologies Corporation</a> at a cost of $7.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a> has criticized the acquisition as causing a reduction in competition.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In November 2015, the acquisition received final approval from the Chinese government,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> with a total cost of $9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Dan Schulz was named the president of Lockheed Martin's Sikorsky company.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin has shown<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> sketches for a twin-engine, <a href="/wiki/Blended_wing_body" title="Blended wing body">blended wing body</a> strategic airlifter similar in size to the C-5.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> On March 31, 2015, the US Navy awarded Lockheed Martin a contract worth $362 million for construction of <a href="/wiki/Freedom-class_littoral_combat_ship" title="Freedom-class littoral combat ship">Freedom-class</a> ship LCS 21 and $79 million for advanced procurement for LCS 23. The Freedom-class ships are built by Fincantieri Marinette Marine in <a href="/wiki/Marinette,_Wisconsin" title="Marinette, Wisconsin">Marinette, Wisconsin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In December 2015, Lockheed won a $867 million seven-year contract to train <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>’s next generation of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_pilots&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Military pilots (page does not exist)">military pilots</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption_investigations">Corruption investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corruption investigations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 3, 2012, the U.S. Justice Department said that Lockheed Martin had agreed to settle allegations that the defense contractor had sold overpriced perishable tools used on many contracts. The DoJ said the allegations were based specifically on the subsidiary Tools &amp; Metals Inc's inflation of costs between 1998 and 2005, which Lockheed Martin then passed on to the U.S. government under its contracts. Further, in March 2006, Todd B. Loftis, a former TMI president, was sentenced to 87 months in prison and ordered to pay a fine of $20 million following his guilty plea.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 20, 2013, Lockheed Martin Corp complied with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._District_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. District Court">U.S. District Court</a> in New York, agreeing to pay a $19.5 million lawsuit to conclude a <a href="/wiki/Securities_fraud" title="Securities fraud">securities fraud</a> <a href="/wiki/Class-action" class="mw-redirect" title="Class-action">class-action</a> legal battle that had accused the company of deceiving shareholders in regards to expectations for the company's information technology division.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 20, 2014, Lockheed Martin Integrated Systems agreed to settle a <a href="/wiki/False_Claims_Act" title="False Claims Act">False Claims Act</a> lawsuit paying $27.5 million to finalize allegations that it had knowingly overbilled the taxpayer for work performed by company staff who did not hold the relevant, essential qualifications for the contract.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, Lt. Gen. Christopher Bogdan criticized the company's F-35 fighter jet program. The general said: "I want them both to start behaving like they want to be around for 40 years...I want them to take on some of the risk of this program. I want them to invest in cost reductions. I want them to do the things that will build a better relationship. I'm not getting all that love yet."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The criticism comes in the wake of previous criticism from former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Defense Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gates" title="Robert Gates">Robert Gates</a> regarding the same program.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_contracts">Government contracts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Government contracts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lockheed Martin received $36&#160;billion in government contracts in 2008 alone, more than any company in history. It does work for more than two dozen government agencies from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">Department of Agriculture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>. It's involved in surveillance and information processing for the <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> (IRS), the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA), <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">Postal Service</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-MoJo_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJo-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2013, Lockheed announced it expected to finalise a $2.2 billion contract with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> for two advanced military communications satellites.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_2">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lockheed Martin is listed as the largest U.S. government contractor and "ranks third for number of incidents, and twenty-first for size of settlements on the 'contractor misconduct' database maintained by the <a href="/wiki/Project_on_Government_Oversight" class="mw-redirect" title="Project on Government Oversight">Project on Government Oversight</a>, a Washington, D.C.-based watchdog group."<sup id="cite_ref-MoJo_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJo-65">[65]</a></sup> Since 1995, the company has agreed to pay $606&#160;million to settle 59 instances of misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying">Lobbying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Lobbying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Politico" title="Politico">Politico</a></i>, Lockheed Martin has "a political network that is already the envy of its competitors", and its contracts enjoy wide bipartisan support in the U.S. Congress thanks to it having "perfected the strategy of spreading jobs on weapons programs in key states and congressional districts".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The company's 2010 lobbying expenditure by the third quarter was $9.9&#160;million (2009 total: $13.7&#160;million).<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through its <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committee</a> (PAC), the company provides low levels of financial support to candidates who advocate national defense and relevant business issues.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> It is "the top contributor to the incoming <a href="/wiki/House_Armed_Services_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="House Armed Services Committee">House Armed Services Committee</a> chairman, <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Buck_McKeon" title="Buck McKeon">Buck McKeon</a> of California, giving more than $50,000 in the most recent election cycle. It also topped the list of donors to Sen. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Inouye" title="Daniel Inouye">Daniel Inouye</a> (D-HI), the chair of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Appropriations_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate Appropriations Committee">Senate Appropriations Committee</a> before his death in 2012."<sup id="cite_ref-MoJo_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJo-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lockheed Martin Employees Political Action Committee is one of the 50 largest in the country, according to FEC data. With contributions from 3,000 employees, it donates $500,000 a year to about 260 House and Senate candidates. For the 2004 election cycle,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="needs attention and maybe moving to &quot;History&quot;. This info is old (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Lockheed's PAC contributed $350,279 to federal candidates, with about 62 percent going to Republicans, according to the Center for Responsive Politics. That compares with $515,000 from General Dynamics' political action committee and $122,850 from BAE Systems North America, the center's data showed.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Senior management consists of the CEO, COO and CFO, and Executive Vice Presidents (EVP) of five business areas.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The EVPs are responsible for managing major programs.</p>
<p>Employees in each program are organized into four tiers: Tier1 –Program Manager/VP, Tier2-Functional Teams (Finance, Chief Engineer, Quality, Operations, etc.), Tier3-Integrated Product Teams (IPTs) (Weapon System Development, Weapon System Integration, etc.), and Tier4-detailed product development. Floor or touch workers belong to component assembly teams.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin manages and maintains its relationship with these touch workers through its supervisors and unions.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lockheed Martin manages employees through its Full Spectrum Leadership and LM21 programs.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The LM21 program relies on <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> principles, which are techniques to improve efficiency. Senior management constructs leadership councils and assigns managers to facilitate <a href="/wiki/Kaizen" title="Kaizen">Kaizen</a> events, which target specific processes for improvement. A manager facilitates teams and processes stakeholders and suppliers to streamline process implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tier2 Functional Leads and Tier3 IPT Leads report to Tier1. IPT leads are responsible for entire systems or products defined by the contract’s Statement of Work.<sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>To control quality, Lockheed Martin trains and builds IPT teams.<sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_80-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-80">[80]</a></sup> and ensures that work is executed correctly through a Technical Performance Measure (TPM) system which emphasizes its Lean and 6 Sigma processes. Middle management uses commitment mechanisms that parallel high commitment and human relations theory.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>Floor employees assemble aircraft using Flow-to-<a href="/wiki/Takt_time" title="Takt time">takt</a> lean manufacturing process<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> which uses properties from both division of labor and scientific management. By separating tasks based on parts, Lockheed Martin utilizes the division of labor theory,<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> specialization on a specific area creates efficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_Helix_methodology">Double Helix methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Double Helix methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Double Helix methodology</b> is a systems development methodology used by Lockheed Martin. It combines experimentation, technology, and a warfighter's <a href="/wiki/Concept_of_Operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Concept of Operations">concept of operations</a> to create new tactics and weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-DoubleHelixInfo_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoubleHelixInfo-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
