			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Celsius</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Celsius_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Celsius (disambiguation)">Celsius (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Celsius</b>, also known as <b>centigrade</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-AKA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AKA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> scale and <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit of measurement">unit of measurement</a> for <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. As an <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a>, it is used by most countries in the world. It is named after the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> (1701–1744), who developed a similar temperature scale. The <b>degree Celsius</b> (<b>°C</b>) can refer to a specific temperature on the Celsius scale as well as a unit to indicate a temperature <a href="/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)" title="Interval (mathematics)">interval</a>, a difference between two temperatures or an <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a>. Before being renamed to honour Anders Celsius in 1948, the unit was called <i>centigrade</i>, from the Latin <i>centum</i>, which means 100, and <i>gradus</i>, which means steps.</p>
<p>The scale is based on 0° for the freezing point of water and 100° for the boiling point of water at 1 atm pressure. This scale is widely taught in schools today. By international agreement the unit "degree Celsius" and the Celsius scale are currently defined by two different temperatures: <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water" title="Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water">VSMOW</a> (specially purified water). This definition also precisely relates the Celsius scale to the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a> scale, which defines the <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">thermodynamic temperature</a> with symbol K. Absolute zero, the lowest temperature possible, is defined as being precisely 0&#160;K and −273.15&#160;°C. The temperature of the triple point of water is defined as precisely 273.16&#160;K (0.01&#160;°C; 32.02&#160;°F) at 611.657 pascals (6.11657&#160;mbar; 0.00603659&#160;atm) pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-BIPMbrocure_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMbrocure-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>This definition fixes the magnitude of both the degree Celsius and the kelvin as precisely 1 part in 273.16 (approximately 0.00366) of the difference between absolute zero and the triple point of water. Thus, it sets the magnitude of one degree Celsius and that of one kelvin as exactly the same. Additionally, it establishes the difference between the two scales' null points as being precisely 273.15 degrees (<span class="nowrap">−273.15 °C = 0 K</span> and <span class="nowrap">0 °C = 273.15 K</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-nistphys_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nistphys-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:65px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Celsius_original_thermometer.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ad/Celsius_original_thermometer.png" width="63" height="427" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="63" data-file-height="427" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Celsius_original_thermometer.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration of Anders Celsius's original thermometer. Note the reversed scale, where 0 is the boiling point of water and 100 is its freezing point.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1742, Swedish astronomer <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> (1701–1744) created a temperature scale which was <i>the reverse</i> of the scale now known by the name "Celsius": <i>0</i> represented the boiling point of water, while <i>100</i> represented the freezing point of water.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In his paper <i>Observations of two persistent degrees on a thermometer</i>, he recounted his experiments showing that the melting point of ice is essentially unaffected by pressure. He also determined with remarkable precision how the boiling point of water varied as a function of atmospheric pressure. He proposed that the zero point of his temperature scale, being the boiling point, would be calibrated at the mean barometric pressure at mean sea level. This pressure is known as one <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">BIPM</a>'s 10th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> (CGPM) later defined one standard atmosphere to equal precisely <span style="white-space:nowrap">1<span style="margin-left:0.25em">013</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em">250</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em"><a href="/wiki/Dyne" title="Dyne">dynes</a></span></span> per <a href="/wiki/Square_centimetre" class="mw-redirect" title="Square centimetre">square centimetre</a> (101.325&#160;<a href="/wiki/KPa" class="mw-redirect" title="KPa">kPa</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-bipm_1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bipm_1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1743, the <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyonnais</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Christin" title="Jean-Pierre Christin">Jean-Pierre Christin</a>, permanent secretary of the <i>Académie des sciences, belles-lettres et arts de Lyon</i><sup><a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_sciences,_belles-lettres_et_arts_de_Lyon" class="extiw" title="fr:Académie des sciences, belles-lettres et arts de Lyon">FR</a></sup>, working independently of Celsius, developed a scale where zero represented the freezing point of water and 100 represented the boiling point of water.<sup id="cite_ref-EOC_1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOC_1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TFOFDOWAC_1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TFOFDOWAC_1-9">[9]</a></sup> On 19 May 1743 he published the design of a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_thermometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury thermometer">mercury thermometer</a>, the "Thermometer of Lyon" built by the craftsman Pierre Casati that used this scale.<sup id="cite_ref-MSLDDMDLT_1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSLDDMDLT_1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IDJ_1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IDJ_1-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MPLHDSEDBA_1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPLHDSEDBA_1-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1744, coincident with the death of Anders Celsius, the Swedish botanist <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolus Linnaeus">Carolus Linnaeus</a> (1707–1778) reversed Celsius's scale.<sup id="cite_ref-.27Linnaeus.27_thermometer_1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-.27Linnaeus.27_thermometer_1-13">[13]</a></sup> His custom-made "linnaeus-thermometer", for use in his greenhouses, was made by Daniel Ekström, Sweden's leading maker of scientific instruments at the time and whose workshop was located in the basement of the Stockholm observatory. As often happened in this age before modern communications, numerous physicists, scientists, and instrument makers are credited with having independently developed this same scale;<sup id="cite_ref-Physics_HTbook_1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_HTbook_1-14">[14]</a></sup> among them were Pehr Elvius, the secretary of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences (which had an instrument workshop) and with whom Linnaeus had been corresponding; Daniel Ekström<sup><a href="https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Ekstr%C3%B6m" class="extiw" title="sv:Daniel Ekström">[SV]</a></sup>, the instrument maker; and Mårten Strömer (1707–1770) who had studied astronomy under Anders Celsius.</p>
<p>The first known Swedish document<sup id="cite_ref-Linn.C3.A6us_.26_his_Garden_1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linn.C3.A6us_.26_his_Garden_1-15">[15]</a></sup> reporting temperatures in this modern "forward" Celsius scale is the paper <i>Hortus Upsaliensis</i> dated 16 December 1745 that Linnaeus wrote to a student of his, Samuel Nauclér. In it, Linnaeus recounted the temperatures inside the orangery at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Uppsala_Botanical_Garden" title="University of Uppsala Botanical Garden">University of Uppsala Botanical Garden</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>...since the caldarium (the hot part of the greenhouse) by the angle of the windows, merely from the rays of the sun, obtains such heat that the thermometer often reaches 30 degrees, although the keen gardener usually takes care not to let it rise to more than 20 to 25 degrees, and in winter not under 15 degrees...</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Centigrade.2C_hectograde_and_Celsius">Centigrade, hectograde and Celsius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Centigrade, hectograde and Celsius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the 19th century, the scientific and <a href="/wiki/Thermometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermometry">thermometry</a> communities worldwide referred to this scale as the centigrade scale. Temperatures on the centigrade scale were often reported simply as <i>degrees</i> or, when greater specificity was desired, as <i>degrees centigrade</i> (symbol: °C). Because the term <i>centigrade</i> was also the Spanish and French language name for a unit of angular measurement (1/100 of a right angle) and had a similar connotation in other languages, the term <i>centesimal degree</i> (known as the <i><b><a href="/wiki/Gradian" title="Gradian">gradian</a></b></i>, "grad" or "gon": 1ᵍ = 0.9°, 100ᵍ = 90°) was used when very precise, unambiguous language was required by international standards bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">BIPM</a>. More properly, what was defined as "centigrade" then would now be "<i>hectograde</i>". Furthermore, in the context here, centigrade/hectograde is referring to the whole 0-100 range, <b>not</b> the given part thereof, hence "20° centigrade" means "20ᵍ per 100 gradians" (or 20% hectograde), not its literal description, "0.2 gradians".</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>(To be descriptively correct, "20° centigrade"</i> should <i>be "20° hectocentigrade", or just "20 gradians" {20ᵍ}.)</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>To eliminate such confusion, the 9th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">CGPM</a> and the CIPM (<a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">Comité international des poids et mesures</a>) formally adopted "degree Celsius" in 1948,<sup id="cite_ref-bipmagreement_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bipmagreement-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OED_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-17">[a]</a></sup> formally keeping the recognized degree symbol, rather than adopting the gradian/centesimal degree symbol.</p>
<p>For scientific use, "Celsius" is the term usually used, with "centigrade" otherwise continuing to be in common but decreasing use, especially in informal contexts in English-speaking countries.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED_2-18">[17]</a></sup> It was not until February 1985 that the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Weather" title="BBC Weather">forecasts issued by the BBC</a> switched from "centigrade" to "Celsius".<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_change_1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_change_1-19">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_symbol_typesetting">Name and symbol typesetting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name and symbol typesetting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "degree Celsius" has been the only SI unit whose full unit name contains an uppercase letter since the <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a> for temperature, the kelvin, became the proper name in 1967 replacing the term <i>degrees Kelvin</i>. The plural form is <i>degrees Celsius</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-UOTTK_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UOTTK-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The general rule of the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM)</a> is that the numerical value always precedes the unit, and a space is always used to separate the unit from the number, <span class="nowrap">e.g. "30.2 °C"</span> (not "30.2°C" or "30.2°&#160;C").<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> Thus the value of the quantity is the product of the number and the unit, the space being regarded as a multiplication sign (just as a space between units implies multiplication). The only exceptions to this rule are for the unit <a href="/wiki/Degree_symbol" title="Degree symbol">symbols for degree</a>, minute, and second for plane angle (°, ′, and ″, respectively), for which no space is left between the numerical value and the unit symbol.<sup id="cite_ref-SI_unit_conventions_1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SI_unit_conventions_1-26">[24]</a></sup> Other languages, and various publishing houses, may follow different typographical rules.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode_character">Unicode character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unicode character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> provides the Celsius symbol at codepoint <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2103</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">℃</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">degree celsius</span>. However, this is a <a href="/wiki/Unicode_compatibility_characters" title="Unicode compatibility characters">compatibility character</a> provided for roundtrip compatibility with legacy encodings. The Unicode standard explicitly discourages the use of this character: "In normal use, it is better to represent degrees Celsius "°C" with a sequence of <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+00B0</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">°</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">degree sign</span> + <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+0043</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">C</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">latin capital letter c</span>, rather than <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+2103</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">℃</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">degree celsius</span>. For searching, treat these two sequences as identical."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperatures_and_intervals">Temperatures and intervals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temperatures and intervals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The degree Celsius is a special name for the kelvin for use in expressing Celsius temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2_on_20161129113316_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2_on_20161129113316-28">[26]</a></sup> The degree Celsius is also subject to the same rules as the kelvin with regard to the use of its unit name and symbol. Thus, besides expressing specific temperatures along its scale (e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Gallium</a> melts at 29.7646&#160;°C" and "The temperature outside is 23 degrees Celsius"), the degree Celsius is also suitable for expressing temperature <i>intervals</i>: differences between temperatures or their uncertainties (e.g. "The output of the heat exchanger is hotter by 40 degrees Celsius", and "Our standard uncertainty is ±3&#160;°C").<sup id="cite_ref-bipm_3_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bipm_3-29">[27]</a></sup> Because of this dual usage, one must not rely upon the unit name or its symbol to denote that a quantity is a temperature interval; it must be unambiguous through context or explicit statement that the quantity is an interval.<sup id="cite_ref-bipm-4_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bipm-4-30">[c]</a></sup> This is sometimes solved by using the symbol °C (pronounced "degrees Celsius") for a temperature, and C° (pronounced "Celsius degrees") for a temperature interval, although this usage is non-standard.<sup id="cite_ref-UPWMP_1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UPWMP_1-31">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>What is often confusing about the Celsius measurement is that it follows an interval system but not a ratio system; that it follows a relative scale not an absolute scale. This is put simply by illustrating that while 10&#160;°C and 20&#160;°C have the same interval difference as 20&#160;°C and 30&#160;°C the temperature 20&#160;°C is not twice the air heat energy as 10&#160;°C. As this example shows, degrees Celsius is a useful interval measurement but does not possess the characteristics of ratio measures like weight or distance.<sup id="cite_ref-BAGTDAAD_1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAGTDAAD_1-32">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coexistence_of_Kelvin_and_Celsius_scales">Coexistence of Kelvin and Celsius scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coexistence of Kelvin and Celsius scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In science and in engineering, the Celsius scale and the Kelvin scale are often used in combination in close contexts, e.g. "...a measured value was 0.01023&#160;°C with an uncertainty of 70&#160;µK...". This practice is permissible because the magnitude of the degree Celsius is equal to that of the kelvin.</p>
<p>Notwithstanding the official endorsement provided by decision <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.fr/en/CGPM/db/13/3/">#3 of Resolution 3 of the 13th CGPM</a>, which stated "a temperature interval may also be expressed in degrees Celsius", the practice of simultaneously using both °C and K remains widespread throughout the scientific world as the use of <a href="/wiki/Metric_prefix#List_of_SI_prefixes" title="Metric prefix">SI-prefixed</a> forms of the degree Celsius (such as "µ°C" or "microdegrees Celsius") to express a temperature interval has not been well-adopted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Melting_and_boiling_points_of_water">Melting and boiling points of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celsius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Melting and boiling points of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One effect of defining the Celsius scale at the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water" title="Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water">Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water</a> (<a href="/wiki/VSMOW" class="mw-redirect" title="VSMOW">VSMOW</a>, 273.16&#160;K and 0.01&#160;°C), and at <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a> (0&#160;K and −273.15&#160;°C), is that neither the <a href="/wiki/Water_(properties)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (properties)">melting</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Water_(properties)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (properties)">boiling</a> point of water under one standard atmosphere (101.325&#160;kPa) remains a defining point for the Celsius scale. In 1948 when the 9th General Conference on Weights and Measures (<a href="/wiki/CGPM" class="mw-redirect" title="CGPM">CGPM</a>) in Resolution 3 first considered using the triple point of water as a defining point, the triple point was so close to being 0.01&#160;°C greater than water's known melting point, it was simply defined as precisely 0.01&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-bipm3res_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bipm3res-33">[30]</a></sup> However, current measurements show that the difference between the triple and melting points of VSMOW is actually very slightly (&lt;0.001&#160;°C) greater than 0.01&#160;°C. Thus, the actual melting point of ice is very slightly (less than a thousandth of a degree) below 0&#160;°C. Also, defining water's triple point at 273.16&#160;K precisely defined the magnitude of each 1&#160;°C increment in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">absolute thermodynamic temperature scale</a> (referencing absolute zero). Now decoupled from the actual boiling point of water, the value "100&#160;°C" is hotter than 0&#160;°C – in absolute terms – by a factor of <i>precisely</i> <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">373.15</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">273.15</span></span> (approximately 36.61% thermodynamically hotter). When adhering <i>strictly</i> to the two-point definition for calibration, the boiling point of VSMOW under one standard atmosphere of pressure is actually 373.1339&#160;K (99.9839&#160;°C). When calibrated to <a href="/wiki/International_Temperature_Scale_of_1990" title="International Temperature Scale of 1990">ITS-90</a> (a calibration standard comprising many definition points and commonly used for high-precision instrumentation), the boiling point of VSMOW is slightly less, about 99.974&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-LSBU_1_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSBU_1-34">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>This boiling-point difference of 16.1 millikelvin between the Celsius scale's original definition and the current one (based on absolute zero and the triple point) has little practical meaning in common daily applications because water's boiling point is very sensitive to variations in barometric pressure. For example, an altitude change of only 28&#160;cm (11&#160;in) causes the boiling point to change by one millikelvin.</p>

The OED also cites this 1928 reporting of a temperature: "My altitude was about 5,800 metres, the temperature was 28° Celsius." However, dictionaries seek to find the earliest use of a word or term and are not a useful resource as regards to the terminology used throughout the history of science. According to several writings of Dr. Terry Quinn CBE FRS, Director of the BIPM (1988–2004), including <i>Temperature Scales from the early days of thermometry to the 21st century</i> (<cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.imeko.org/publications/tc12-2004/IMEKO-TC12-2004-PL-001.pdf">"here"</a> <span style="font-size:85%;">(PDF)</span>.</cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3ACelsius&amp;rft.btitle=here&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.imeko.org%2Fpublications%2Ftc12-2004%2FIMEKO-TC12-2004-PL-001.pdf&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook" class="Z3988"><span style="display:none;">&#160;</span></span>&#160;<small>(146&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kibibyte" title="Kibibyte">KiB</a>)</small>) as well as <i>Temperature</i> (2nd Edition/1990/Academic Press/0125696817), the term <i>Celsius</i> in connection with the centigrade scale was not used whatsoever by the scientific or thermometry communities until after the CIPM and CGPM adopted the term in 1948. The 1948 adoption of <i>Celsius</i> accomplished three objectives:</span>

<dd>3. &#160;&#160;&#160;The new name eliminated the ambiguity of the term "centigrade", freeing it to refer exclusively to the French-language name for the unit of angular measurement.</dd>
</dl>
