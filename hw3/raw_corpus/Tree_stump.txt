<p>After a <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> has been cut and felled, the <b>stump</b> or <b>tree stump</b> is usually a small remaining portion of the trunk with the <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> still in the ground. Stumps may show the age-defining rings of a tree. The study of these rings is known as <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration">Regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tree_stump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stumps (both those on the ground and stumps of removed branches) are sometimes able to regenerate into new trees. Often, a <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous tree</a> that has been cut will re-sprout in multiple places around the edge of the stump or from the roots.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Depending on whether the tree is being removed permanently or whether the <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> is expected to recover, this can be either desirable or undesirable. Stump sprouts can grow very quickly and sometimes become viable trees themselves either for aesthetics or timber, due to the existing root structure; however, the cut portion of the trunk may weaken the sprouts and introduce disease into the newly forming tree(s).</p>

<p>The process of deliberately cutting a tree to a stump to regrow is known as <a href="/wiki/Coppicing" title="Coppicing">coppicing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stump_removal">Stump removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tree_stump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stump removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tree stumps can be difficult to remove from the ground. They can be dug out, shredded with a <a href="/wiki/Stump_grinder" title="Stump grinder">stump grinder</a> or burnt.</p>
<p>A common method for stump removal is to use one of the many chemical stump removal products, so long as immediate results are not needed. These stump removers are mostly made of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a> (KNO<sub>3</sub>) and act by rapidly increasing the decay of the stump. After an average of 4–6 weeks, the stump will be rotten through and easily fragmented in manageable pieces. If time is a limiting factor, setting fire to the stump is effective because once the potassium nitrate has been absorbed it acts as an effective oxidizer.</p>
<p>Historically, an explosive called <i>stumping powder</i> was used to blast stumps into bits.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stump_harvesting">Stump harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tree_stump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stump harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In plantation forests in parts of Europe, stumps are sometimes pulled out of the ground using a specially adapted tracked <a href="/wiki/Excavator" title="Excavator">excavator</a>, to supply wood fuel for <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> power stations. <a href="/wiki/Stump_harvesting" title="Stump harvesting">Stump harvesting</a> may provide an increasing component of the woody material required by the biomass power sector.</p>
