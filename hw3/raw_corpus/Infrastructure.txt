<p><b>Infrastructure</b> refers to the fundamental facilities and systems serving a country, city, or area,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> including the services and facilities necessary for its <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> to function.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It typically characterises technical structures such as <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a>, and so forth, and can be defined as "the physical components of interrelated systems providing <a href="/wiki/Commodities" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities">commodities</a> and services essential to enable, sustain, or enhance societal <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">living conditions</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_infrastructure" title="History of infrastructure">History of infrastructure</a></div>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Online_Etymology_Dictionary" title="Online Etymology Dictionary">Online Etymology Dictionary</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the word infrastructure has been used in English since 1887 and in French since 1875, originally meaning "The installations that form the basis for any operation or system".<sup id="cite_ref-opendb.net_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-opendb.net-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word was imported from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, where it means <i><a href="/wiki/Subgrade" title="Subgrade">subgrade</a></i>, the native material underneath a constructed pavement or railway. The word is a combination of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> prefix "infra", meaning "below", and "structure". The military use of the term achieved currency in the United States after the formation of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> in the 1940s, and by 1970 was adopted by <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planners</a> in its modern civilian sense .<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term came to prominence in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the 1980s following the publication of <i>America in Ruins</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which initiated a public-policy discussion of the nation's "infrastructure crisis", purported to be caused by decades of inadequate investment and poor maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a>. This crisis discussion contributed to an increase in infrastructure <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a> and maintenance planning in the US.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Public-policy discussions have been hampered by lack of a precise definition for infrastructure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 1987 <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> panel adopted the term "public works infrastructure", referring to:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"...&#160;both specific functional modes – highways, streets, roads, and bridges; mass transit; airports and airways; water supply and water resources; wastewater management; solid-waste treatment and disposal; electric power generation and transmission; telecommunications; and hazardous waste management – and the combined system these modal elements comprise. A comprehension of infrastructure spans not only these public works facilities, but also the operating procedures, management practices, and development policies that interact together with societal demand and the physical world to facilitate the transport of people and goods, provision of water for drinking and a variety of other uses, safe disposal of society's waste products, provision of energy where it is needed, and transmission of information within and between communities."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The OECD also classifies communications as a part of infrastructure .<sup id="cite_ref-OECD_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American Society of Civil Engineers has not defined the term, though issuing a US "Infrastructure Report Card" every 2-4 years.<sup id="cite_ref-asce17_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asce17-10">[10]</a></sup> As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> they grade 16 categories, namely Aviation, Bridges, Dams, Drinking Water, Energy, Hazardous Waste, Inland Waterways, Levees, Parks &amp; Recreation, Ports, Rail, Roads, Schools, Solid Waste, Transit and Wastewater.<sup id="cite_ref-asce17_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asce17-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:4</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hard_infrastructure" title="Hard infrastructure">Hard infrastructure</a> refers to the physical networks necessary for the functioning of a modern industrial nation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Soft_infrastructure" title="Soft infrastructure">Soft infrastructure</a> may refer to the <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> which are required to maintain an economy,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> like health, and cultural and social standards of a country, such as the <a href="/wiki/Financial_system" title="Financial system">financial system</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Education_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Education system">education system</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Health_care_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Health care system">health care system</a>, the system of government, and <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement" title="Law enforcement">law enforcement</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Emergency_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency services">emergency services</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-opendb.net_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-opendb.net-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since March 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since March 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Critical_infrastructure" title="Critical infrastructure">critical infrastructure</a> distinguishes those infrastructure elements that, if significantly damaged or destroyed, would cause serious disruption of a system or organization. <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">Storm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> damage leading to loss of certain transportation routes in a city, for example bridges crossing a river, that would make it impossible for people to evacuate, and for <a href="/wiki/Emergency_service" title="Emergency service">emergency services</a> to operate, would be deemed critical infrastructure. Similarly, an on-line booking system might be critical infrastructure for an <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a>. These elements of infrastructure are the focus of recovery efforts in the aftermath of natural disasters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>infrastructure</i> may be confused with the following overlapping or related concepts.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Land_improvement" class="mw-redirect" title="Land improvement">Land improvement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land development</a> are general terms that in some contexts may include infrastructure, but in the context of a discussion of infrastructure would refer only to smaller scale systems or works that are not included in infrastructure, because they are typically limited to a single <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">parcel of land</a>, and are owned and operated by the land owner. For example, an irrigation canal that serves a region or district would be included with infrastructure, but the private irrigation systems on individual land parcels would be considered land improvements, not infrastructure. Service connections to municipal service and public utility networks would also be considered land improvements, not infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a> includes government-owned and operated infrastructure as well as public buildings, such as schools and court houses. Public works generally refers to physical assets needed to deliver <a href="/wiki/Public_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Public services">public services</a>. Public services include both infrastructure and services generally provided by government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_and_financing">Ownership and financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ownership and financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_and_economics" title="Infrastructure and economics">Infrastructure and economics</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/50px-Mergefrom.svg.png" width="50" height="20" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/75px-Mergefrom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/100px-Mergefrom.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="50" data-file-height="20" /></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">It has been suggested that <i><a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_and_economics" title="Infrastructure and economics">Infrastructure and economics</a></i> be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Merging" title="Wikipedia:Merging">merged</a> into this section. (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Infrastructure" title="Talk:Infrastructure">Discuss</a>) <small><i>Proposed since March 2015.</i></small></span></td>

<p>Infrastructure may be owned and managed by <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> or by private companies, such as sole <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">public utility</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway</a> companies. Generally, most roads, major ports and airports, water distribution systems and sewage networks are publicly owned, whereas most energy and telecommunications networks are privately owned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Publicly owned infrastructure may be paid for from taxes, tolls, or metered user fees, whereas private infrastructure is generally paid for by metered user fees.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Major investment projects are generally financed by the issuance of long-term <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Government owned and operated infrastructure may be developed and operated in the <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Public-private_partnership" class="mw-redirect" title="Public-private partnership">public-private partnerships</a>, in addition to in the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a>. As of 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> in the United States for example, public spending on infrastructure has varied between 2.3% and 3.6% of GDP since 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/List_of_financial_institutions_that_invest_in_infrastructure" title="List of financial institutions that invest in infrastructure">financial institutions invest in infrastructure</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>, the word <i>infrastructure</i> was exclusively used to describe public assets that facilitate production, but not private assets of the same purpose. In post-Keynesian times, the word has grown in popularity and has been applied with increasing generality to suggest the internal framework discernible in any technology system or <a href="/wiki/Business_organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Business organisation">business organisation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_the_term">Uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_and_construction">Engineering and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Engineering and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineers">Engineers</a> generally limit the term "infrastructure" to describe <a href="/wiki/Fixed_assets" class="mw-redirect" title="Fixed assets">fixed assets</a> that are in the form of a large network, in other words, <a href="/wiki/Hard_infrastructure" title="Hard infrastructure">hard infrastructure</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Efforts to devise more generic definitions of infrastructures have typically referred to the network aspects of most of the structures, and to the accumulated value of investments in the networks as assets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One such definition from 1998 defined infrastructure as the network of assets "where the system as a whole is intended to be maintained indefinitely at a specified standard of service by the continuing replacement and refurbishment of its components".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_defense_and_economic_development">Civil defense and economic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Civil defense and economic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense_by_country" title="Civil defense by country">Civil defense by country</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Civil_defense" title="Civil defense">Civil defense</a> planners and <a href="/wiki/Development_economics" title="Development economics">developmental economists</a> generally refer to both hard and soft infrastructure, including <a href="/wiki/Public_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Public services">public services</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Schools">schools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hospitals" class="mw-redirect" title="Hospitals">hospitals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency services">emergency services</a> such as police and fire fighting, and basic <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a>. The notion of <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure-based_development" title="Infrastructure-based development">Infrastructure-based development</a> combining long-term infrastructure investments by government agencies at central and regional levels with <a href="/wiki/Public_private_partnership" class="mw-redirect" title="Public private partnership">public private partnerships</a> has proven popular among Asian- notably <a href="/wiki/Singaporean" class="mw-redirect" title="Singaporean">Singaporean</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a>, Mainland European and Latin American economists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">Military</a> strategists use the term infrastructure to refer to all building and permanent installations necessary for the support of military forces, whether they are stationed in bases, being deployed or engaged in operations. For example barracks, headquarters, airfields, communications facilities, stores of military equipment, port installations, and maintenance stations.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban">Urban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Urban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Urban</i> or <i>municipal infrastructure</i> refers to hard infrastructure systems generally owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>, such as streets, water distribution, and sewers. It may also include some of the facilities associated with soft infrastructure, such as parks, public pools, schools, hospitals and libraries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_infrastructure">Green infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Green infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">Green infrastructure</a> is a concept that highlights the importance of the natural environment in decisions about <a href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" class="mw-redirect" title="Land use planning">land use planning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In particular there is an emphasis on the "life support" functions provided by a network of natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Interconnectivity" title="Interconnectivity">interconnectivity</a> to support long-term <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. Examples include clean <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and healthy soils, as well as the more <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentric</a> functions such as <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a> and providing shade and shelter in and around towns and cities. The concept can be extended to apply to the management of <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> runoff at the local level through the use of natural systems, or engineered systems that mimic natural systems, to treat polluted <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">runoff</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxism">Marxism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Marxism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, the term infrastructure is sometimes used as a synonym for "base" in the <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectic</a> synthetic pair <i><a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">base and superstructure</a></i>. However the Marxist notion of base is broader than the non-Marxist use of the term infrastructure, and some soft infrastructure, such as laws, governance, regulations and standards, would be considered by Marxists to be part of the superstructure, not the base.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">Communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term infrastructure may refer to informal and formal channels of communication, <a href="/wiki/Political" class="mw-redirect" title="Political">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networks</a>, or beliefs held by members of particular groups, as well as <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, software development tools. Still underlying these more conceptual uses is the idea that infrastructure provides organizing structure and support for the system or organization it serves, whether it is a city, a nation, a corporation, or a collection of people with common interests. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_management" title="Information technology management">IT infrastructure</a>, research infrastructure, terrorist infrastructure, employment infrastructure and tourism infrastructure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_developing_world">In the developing world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In the developing world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to researchers at the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a>, the lack of infrastructure in many <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> represents one of the most significant limitations to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> and achievement of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals (MDGs)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> Infrastructure investments and maintenance can be very expensive, especially in such as areas as landlocked, rural and sparsely populated countries in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> It has been argued that infrastructure investments contributed to more than half of Africa's improved growth performance between 1990 and 2005, and increased investment is necessary to maintain growth and tackle <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> The returns to investment in infrastructure are very significant, with on average thirty to forty percent returns for <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> (ICT) investments, over forty percent for <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> generation, and eighty percent for <a href="/wiki/Roads" class="mw-redirect" title="Roads">roads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_differences">Regional differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Regional differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The demand for infrastructure, both by consumers and by companies is much higher than the amount invested.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> There are severe constraints on the supply side of the provision of infrastructure in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The infrastructure financing gap between what is invested in <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> (around US$48 billion) and what is needed (US$228 billion) is around US$180 billion every year.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, three percent of <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> (around US$71 billion) would need to be invested in infrastructure in order to satisfy demand, yet in 2005, for example, only around two percent was invested leaving a financing gap of approximately US$24 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Africa, in order to reach the seven percent annual growth calculated to be required to meet the MDGs by 2015 would require infrastructure investments of about fifteen percent of GDP, or around US$93 billion a year.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Fragile_state" title="Fragile state">fragile states</a>, over thirty-seven percent of GDP would be required.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_funding">Sources of funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrastructure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sources of funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The source of financing varies significantly across sectors.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> Some sectors are dominated by government spending, others by <a href="/wiki/Development_aid" title="Development aid">overseas development aid (ODA)</a>, and yet others by private investors.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, governments spend around US$9.4 billion out of a total of US$24.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, governments represent almost all spending. In <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> a majority of investment is government spending. In <a href="/wiki/Information_and_communication_technologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Information and communication technologies">ICT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>, the private sector represents the majority of capital expenditure.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> Overall, between them aid, the private sector, and non-<a href="/wiki/OECD" class="mw-redirect" title="OECD">OECD</a> financiers exceed government spending.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> The private sector spending alone equals state capital expenditure, though the majority is focused on ICT infrastructure investments.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup> External financing increased in the 2000s (decade) and in Africa alone external infrastructure investments increased from US$7 billion in 2002 to US$27 billion in 2009. <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, in particular, has emerged as an important investor.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_23-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
