<p><b>Seawater</b>, or <b>salt water</b>, is water from a sea or ocean. On average, seawater in the world's oceans has a <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> of about 3.5% (35&#160;g/L, or 0.600 M) This means that every kilogram (roughly one litre by volume) of seawater has approximately 35 grams (1.2&#160;oz) of <a href="/wiki/Sea_salt" title="Sea salt">dissolved salts</a> (predominantly <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">+<br /></span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br /></span></span>) <a href="/wiki/Ions" class="mw-redirect" title="Ions">ions</a>). Average density at the surface is 1.025&#160;kg/l. Seawater is <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">denser</a> than both <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> and pure water (density 1.0&#160;kg/l at 4&#160;°C (39&#160;°F)) because the dissolved salts increase the mass by a larger proportion than the volume. The <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">freezing point</a> of seawater decreases as salt concentration increases. At typical salinity, it freezes at about −2&#160;°C (28&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The coldest seawater ever recorded (in a liquid state) was in 2010, in a stream under an <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a>, and measured −2.6&#160;°C (27.3&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Seawater <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> is typically limited to a range between 7.5 and 8.4.<sup id="cite_ref-Chester_Marine_Geochem_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chester_Marine_Geochem-3">[3]</a></sup> However, there is no universally accepted reference pH-scale for seawater and the difference between measurements based on different reference scales may be up to 0.14 units.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salinity">Salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Although the vast majority of seawater has a salinity of between 31 g/kg and 38 g/kg, seawater is not uniformly saline throughout the world. Where mixing occurs with fresh water runoff from river mouths, near melting glaciers or vast amounts precipitation (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a>), seawater can be substantially less saline. The most saline open sea is the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, where high rates of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, low <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and low river run-off, and confined circulation result in unusually salty water. The salinity in isolated bodies of water (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>) can be considerably greater still. Historically, several salinity scales were used to approximate the absolute salinity of seawater. A popular scale was the "Practical Salinity Scale" where salinity was measured in "practical salinity units (psu)". The current standard for salinity is the "Reference Salinity" scale <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with the salinity expressed in units of "g/kg".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermophysical_properties_of_Seawater">Thermophysical properties of Seawater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermophysical properties of Seawater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of surface seawater ranges from about 1020 to 1029&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup>, depending on the temperature and salinity. At a temperature of 25&#160;°C, salinity of 35 g/kg and 1 atm pressure, the density of seawater is 1023.6 &#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nayar_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nayar-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MIT_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-8">[8]</a></sup> Deep in the ocean, under high pressure, seawater can reach a density of 1050&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup> or higher. The density of seawater also changes with salinity. Brines generated by seawater desalination plants can have salinities up to 120 g/kg. The density of typical seawater brine of 120 g/kg salinity at 25&#160;°C and atmospheric pressure is 1088&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nayar_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nayar-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MIT_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-8">[8]</a></sup> Seawater <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> is limited to the range 7.5 to 8.4. The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in seawater is about 1,500&#160;m/s (whereas speed of sound is usually around 330&#160;m/s in air at roughly 1000hPa pressure, 1 atmosphere), and varies with water temperature, salinity, and pressure. The thermal conductivity of seawater is 0.6&#160;W/mK at 25&#160;°C and a salinity of 35&#160;g/kg.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The thermal conductivity decreases with increasing salinity and increases with increasing temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Seawater contains more dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> than all types of freshwater.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the ratios of solutes differ dramatically. For instance, although seawater contains about 2.8 times more <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> than river water based on <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molarity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percentage</a> of bicarbonate in seawater as a ratio of <i>all</i> dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> is far lower than in river water. Bicarbonate ions also constitute 48% of river water solutes but only 0.14% of all seawater ions.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pinet_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinet-12">[12]</a></sup> Differences like these are due to the varying <a href="/wiki/Residence_time_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Residence time (fluid dynamics)">residence times</a> of seawater solutes; <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> have very long residence times, while <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> (vital for <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> formation) tends to precipitate much more quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-Pinet_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinet-12">[12]</a></sup> The most abundant dissolved ions in seawater are sodium, chloride, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> and calcium.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_concentration" title="Osmotic concentration">osmolarity</a> is about 1000 mOsm/l.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Small amounts of other substances are found including <a href="/wiki/Amino_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> at concentrations up to 2 micrograms of nitrogen atoms per liter,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which are thought to have played a key role in the <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">origin of life</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_components">Microbial components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microbial components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research in 1957 by the <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a> sampled water in both <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neritic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neritic">neritic</a> locations in the Pacific Ocean. Direct microscopic counts and cultures were used, the direct counts in some cases showing up to 10 000 times that obtained from cultures. These differences were attributed to the occurrence of bacteria in aggregates, selective effects of the culture media, and the presence of inactive cells. A marked reduction in bacterial culture numbers was noted below the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>, but not by direct microscopic observation. Large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Spirilli" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirilli">spirilli</a>-like forms were seen by microscope but not under cultivation. The disparity in numbers obtained by the two methods is well known in this and other fields.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the 1990s, improved techniques of detection and identification of microbes by probing just small snippets of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, enabled researchers taking part in the <a href="/wiki/Census_of_Marine_Life" title="Census of Marine Life">Census of Marine Life</a> to identify thousands of previously unknown microbes usually present only in small numbers. This revealed a far greater diversity than previously suspected, so that a litre of seawater may hold more than 20,000 species. Dr. Mitchell Sogin from the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a> feels that "the number of different kinds of bacteria in the oceans could eclipse five to 10 million."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bacteria are found at all depths in the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>, as well as in the sediments, some being aerobic, others anaerobic. Most are free-swimming, but some exist as <a href="/wiki/Symbionts" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbionts">symbionts</a> within other organisms – examples of these being bioluminescent bacteria. <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">Cyanobacteria</a> played an important role in the evolution of ocean processes, enabling the development of <a href="/wiki/Stromatolites" class="mw-redirect" title="Stromatolites">stromatolites</a> and oxygen in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Some bacteria interact with <a href="/wiki/Diatoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a>, and form a critical link in the cycling of silicon in the ocean. One anaerobic species, <i><a href="/wiki/Thiomargarita_namibiensis" title="Thiomargarita namibiensis">Thiomargarita namibiensis</a></i>, plays an important part in the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulphide" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen sulphide">hydrogen sulphide</a> eruptions from diatomaceous sediments off the Namibian coast, and generated by high rates of phytoplankton growth in the <a href="/wiki/Benguela_Current" title="Benguela Current">Benguela Current</a> upwelling zone, eventually falling to the seafloor.</p>
<p>Bacteria-like <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a> surprised marine microbiologists by their survival and thriving in extreme environments, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrothermal vents">hydrothermal vents</a> on the ocean floor. Alkalotolerant marine bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas" title="Pseudomonas">Pseudomonas</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vibrio" title="Vibrio">Vibrio</a></i> spp. survive in a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> range of 7.3 to 10.6, while some species will grow only at pH 10 to 10.6.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Archaea also exist in pelagic waters and may constitute as much as half the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, clearly playing an important part in oceanic processes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2000 sediments from the ocean floor revealed a species of Archaea that breaks down <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, an important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> gas and a major contributor to atmospheric warming.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Some bacteria break down the rocks of the sea floor, influencing seawater chemistry. Oil spills, and runoff containing human sewage and chemical pollutants have a marked effect on microbial life in the vicinity, as well as harbouring pathogens and toxins affecting all forms of marine life. The protist <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellates" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinoflagellates">dinoflagellates</a> may at certain times undergo population explosions called blooms or <a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">red tides</a>, often after human-caused pollution. The process may produce <a href="/wiki/Metabolites" class="mw-redirect" title="Metabolites">metabolites</a> known as biotoxins, which move along the ocean food chain, tainting higher-order animal consumers.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Pandoravirus_salinus" title="Pandoravirus salinus">Pandoravirus salinus</a></i>, a species of very large virus, with a genome much larger than that of any other virus species, was discovered in 2013. Like the other very large viruses <i><a href="/wiki/Mimivirus" title="Mimivirus">Mimivirus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Megavirus" title="Megavirus">Megavirus</a></i>, <i>Pandoravirus</i> infects amoebas, but its genome, containing 1.9 to 2.5 megabases of DNA, is twice as large as that of <i>Megavirus</i>, and it differs greatly from the other large viruses in appearance and in genome structure.</p>
<p>In 2013 researchers from <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Aberdeen University">Aberdeen University</a> announced that they were starting a hunt for undiscovered chemicals in organisms that have evolved in deep sea trenches, hoping to find "the next generation" of antibiotics, anticipating an "antibiotic apocalypse" with a dearth of new infection-fighting drugs. The EU-funded research will start in the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Trench" class="mw-redirect" title="Atacama Trench">Atacama Trench</a> and then move on to search trenches off New Zealand and Antarctica.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ocean has a long history of human waste disposal on the assumption that its vast size makes it capable of absorbing and diluting all noxious material.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> While this may be true on a small scale, the large amounts of sewage routinely dumped has damaged many coastal ecosystems, and rendered them life-threatening. Pathogenic viruses and bacteria occur in such waters, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vibrio_cholerae" title="Vibrio cholerae">Vibrio cholerae</a></i> the cause of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">hepatitis A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_E" title="Hepatitis E">hepatitis E</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polio" class="mw-redirect" title="Polio">polio</a>, along with protozoans causing <a href="/wiki/Giardiasis" title="Giardiasis">giardiasis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidiosis" title="Cryptosporidiosis">cryptosporidiosis</a>. These pathogens are routinely present in the ballast water of large vessels, and are widely spread when the ballast is discharged.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">Scientific theories</a> behind the origins of sea salt started with Sir <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">Edmond Halley</a> in 1715, who proposed that salt and other minerals were carried into the sea by rivers after rainfall washed it out of the ground. Upon reaching the ocean, these salts concentrated as more salt arrived over time (see <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">Hydrologic cycle</a>). Halley noted that most lakes that don't have ocean outlets (such as the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basin</a>), have high salt content. Halley termed this process "continental weathering".</p>
<p>Halley's theory was partly correct. In addition, sodium leached out of the ocean floor when the ocean formed. The presence of salt's other dominant ion, chloride, results from <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a> of chloride (as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>) with other gases from Earth's interior via <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a>. The sodium and chloride ions subsequently became the most abundant constituents of sea salt.</p>
<p>Ocean salinity has been stable for billions of years, most likely as a consequence of a chemical/<a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic</a> system which removes as much salt as is deposited; for instance, sodium and chloride sinks include <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporite</a> deposits, pore-water burial, and reactions with seafloor <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pinet_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinet-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:133</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_impacts">Human impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Human impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a>, rising atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, excess nutrients, and pollution in many forms are altering global oceanic geochemistry. Rates of change for some aspects greatly exceed those in the historical and recent geological record. Major trends include an increasing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidity</a>, reduced subsurface oxygen in both near-shore and pelagic waters, rising coastal nitrogen levels, and widespread increases in <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> and persistent organic pollutants. Most of these perturbations are tied either directly or indirectly to human fossil fuel combustion, fertilizer, and industrial activity. Concentrations are projected to grow in coming decades, with negative impacts on ocean biota and other marine resources.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most striking features of this is <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, resulting from increased CO<sub>2</sub> uptake of the oceans related to higher atmospheric concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> and higher temperatures,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> because it severely affects <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_consumption">Human consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sea_water_poisoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea water poisoning">Sea water poisoning</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">Desalination</a></div>
<p>Accidentally consuming small quantities of clean seawater is not harmful, especially if the seawater is taken along with a larger quantity of fresh water. However, drinking seawater to maintain hydration is counterproductive; more water must be excreted to eliminate the salt (via <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>) than the amount of water obtained from the seawater itself.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Urinary_system" title="Urinary system">renal system</a> actively regulates sodium chloride in the blood within a very narrow range around 9 g/L (0.9% by weight).</p>
<p>In most open waters concentrations vary somewhat around typical values of about 3.5%, far higher than the body can tolerate and most beyond what the kidney can process. A point frequently overlooked<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>, in claims that the kidney can excrete NaCl in Baltic concentrations (2%), is that the gut cannot absorb water at such concentrations, so that there is no benefit in drinking such water. Drinking seawater temporarily increases blood's NaCl concentration. This signals the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> to excrete sodium, but seawater's sodium concentration is above the kidney's maximum concentrating ability. Eventually the blood's sodium concentration rises to toxic levels, removing water from cells and interfering with <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerve</a> conduction, ultimately producing fatal <a href="/wiki/Non-epileptic_seizure" title="Non-epileptic seizure">seizure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrhythmia" title="Cardiac arrhythmia">cardiac arrhythmia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Survival_skills" title="Survival skills">Survival manuals</a> consistently advise against drinking seawater.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A summary of 163 <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(shipboard)" title="Lifeboat (shipboard)">life raft</a> voyages estimated the risk of death at 39% for those who drank seawater, compared to 3% for those who did not. The effect of seawater intake on rats confirmed the negative effects of drinking seawater when dehydrated.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The temptation to drink seawater was greatest for sailors who had expended their supply of fresh water, and were unable to capture enough rainwater for drinking. This frustration was described famously by a line from <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rime_of_the_Ancient_Mariner" title="The Rime of the Ancient Mariner">The Rime of the Ancient Mariner</a></i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>"Water, water, everywhere,<br />
And all the boards did shrink;<br />
Water, water, everywhere,<br />
Nor any drop to drink."</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Although humans cannot survive on seawater, some people claim that up to two cups a day, mixed with fresh water in a 2:3 ratio, produces no ill effect. The French physician <a href="/wiki/Alain_Bombard" title="Alain Bombard">Alain Bombard</a> survived an ocean crossing in a small Zodiak rubber boat using mainly raw fish meat, which contains about 40 percent water (like most living tissues), as well as small amounts of seawater and other provisions harvested from the ocean. His findings were challenged, but an alternative explanation was not given. In his 1948 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Kon-Tiki" class="mw-redirect" title="Kon-Tiki">Kon-Tiki</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Thor_Heyerdahl" title="Thor Heyerdahl">Thor Heyerdahl</a> reported drinking seawater mixed with fresh in a 2:3 ratio during the 1947 expedition.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A few years later, another adventurer, <a href="/wiki/William_Willis_(sailor)" title="William Willis (sailor)">William Willis</a>, claimed to have drunk two cups of seawater and one cup of fresh per day for 70 days without ill effect when he lost part of his water supply.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Russell_(doctor)" title="Richard Russell (doctor)">Richard Russell</a> advocated the practice's medical use in the UK, and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Quinton" title="René Quinton">René Quinton</a> expanded the advocation of the practice other countries, notably France, in the 20th century. Currently, the practice is widely used in Nicaragua and other countries, supposedly taking advantage of the latest medical discoveries.</p>
<p>Most ocean-going vessels <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalinate</a> <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable</a> water from seawater using processes such as <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_distillation" title="Vacuum distillation">vacuum distillation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Multi-stage_flash_distillation" title="Multi-stage flash distillation">multi-stage flash distillation</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Evaporator_(marine)" title="Evaporator (marine)">evaporator</a>, or more recently by <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a>. These energy-intensive processes were not usually available during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>. Larger sailing warships with large crews, such as <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Nelson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/HMS_Victory" title="HMS Victory">HMS&#160;<i>Victory</i></a>, were fitted with distilling apparatuses in their <a href="/wiki/Galley_(kitchen)" title="Galley (kitchen)">galleys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rippon.2C_Vol_1.2C_78-79_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rippon.2C_Vol_1.2C_78-79-32">[32]</a></sup> Animals such as fish, whales, <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>, such as penguins and <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a>, can adapt to a high saline habitat. For example, the kidney of the <a href="/wiki/Gerbil" title="Gerbil">desert rat</a> can concentrate sodium far more efficiently than the human kidney.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard">Standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM International</a> has an international standard for <a href="/wiki/Artificial_seawater" title="Artificial seawater">artificial seawater</a>: ASTM D1141-98 (Original Standard ASTM D1141-52). It is used in many research testing labs as a reproducible solution for seawater such as tests on corrosion, oil contamination, and detergency evaluation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
