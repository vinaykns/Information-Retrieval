<p><i><b>Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America</b></i> (<i><b>PNAS</b></i>) is the official <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, published since 1915. With broad coverage, spanning the biological, physical, and social sciences, the journal publishes original research<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> alongside scientific reviews, commentaries, and letters. In 1999–2009, the last period for which data are available, <i>PNAS</i> was the second most cited journal across all fields of science.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>PNAS</i> is published weekly in print, and daily online in <i>PNAS Early Edition</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>PNAS</i> was established by the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> (NAS) in 1914, with its first issue published in 1915. The NAS itself had been founded in 1863 as a private institution, but <a href="/wiki/Congressional_charter" title="Congressional charter">chartered</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, with the goal to "investigate, examine, experiment, and report upon any subject of science or art". By 1914 the Academy had been well established.</p>
<p>Prior to the inception of PNAS, the National Academy of Sciences published three volumes of organizational transactions, consisting mostly of minutes of meetings and annual reports. In accordance with the guiding principles established by astronomer <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">George Ellery Hale</a>, the foreign secretary of NAS in 1914, <i>PNAS</i> publishes brief first announcements of Academy members' and foreign associates' more important contributions to research and of work that appears to a member to be of particular importance.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peer_review">Peer review</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peer review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All research papers published in <i>PNAS</i> are <a href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> The standard mode is for papers to be submitted directly to <i>PNAS</i> rather than going through an Academy member. Members may handle the peer review process for up to 4 of their own papers per year—this is an open review process because the member selects and communicates directly with the referees. These submissions and reviews, like all for <i>PNAS</i>, are evaluated for publication by the <i>PNAS</i> Editorial Board. Until July 1, 2010, members were allowed to communicate up to 2 papers from non-members to <i>PNAS</i> every year. The review process for these papers was anonymous in that the identities of the referees were not revealed to the authors. Referees were selected by the NAS member.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Garfield_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garfield-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>PNAS</i> eliminated communicated submissions through NAS members as of July 1, 2010, while continuing to make the final decision on all <i>PNAS</i> papers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_use_papers_and_national_security">Dual use papers and national security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dual use papers and national security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, <i>PNAS</i> issued an editorial stating its policy on publication of sensitive material in the life sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>PNAS</i> stated that it would "continue to monitor submitted papers for material that may be deemed inappropriate and that could, if published, compromise the public welfare." This statement was in keeping with the efforts of several other journals.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2005 <i>PNAS</i> published an article titled "Analyzing a bioterror attack on the food supply: The case of botulinum toxin in milk"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> despite objections raised by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The paper was published with a commentary by the president of the Academy at the time, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Alberts" title="Bruce Alberts">Bruce Alberts</a>, titled "Modeling attacks on the food supply".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>PNAS</i> is widely read by researchers, particularly those involved in basic sciences, around the world. <i>PNAS Online</i> receives over 21 million hits per month.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The journal is notable for its policy of making research articles freely available online to everyone six months after publication (<a href="/wiki/Delayed_open_access_journal" title="Delayed open access journal">delayed open access</a>), or immediately if authors have chosen the "open access" option (<a href="/wiki/Hybrid_open_access_journal" title="Hybrid open access journal">hybrid open access</a>). Immediately free online access (without the six-month delay) is available to more than 100 developing countries<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and for some categories of papers such as colloquia. Abstracts, tables of contents, and online supporting information are free. Anyone can sign up to receive free tables of contents by email.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because <i>PNAS</i> is self-sustaining and receives no direct funding from the U.S.&#160;government or the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the journal charges authors <a href="/wiki/Article_Processing_Charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Article Processing Charge">publication fees</a> and subscription fees to offset the cost of the editorial and publication process.</p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">Journal Citation Reports</a></i>, the journal has a 2015 <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> of 9.423.<sup id="cite_ref-WoS_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WoS-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>PNAS</i> is the second most cited scientific journal, with nearly 1.4&#160;million citations from 1999 to 2009 (the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Biological_Chemistry" title="Journal of Biological Chemistry">Journal of Biological Chemistry</a></i> is the most cited journal over this period).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>PNAS</i> has received occasional criticism for releasing papers to <a href="/wiki/Science_journalism" title="Science journalism">science journalists</a> as much as a week before making them available to the general public; this practice is known as a <a href="/wiki/News_embargo" title="News embargo">news embargo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yong_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yong-18">[18]</a></sup> According to critics, this allows mainstream news outlets to misrepresent or exaggerate the implications of experimental findings before the scientific community is able to respond.<sup id="cite_ref-LLL_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LLL-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Science writer <a href="/wiki/Ed_Yong" title="Ed Yong">Ed Yong</a>, on the other hand, has argued that the real problem is not embargoes themselves, but the <a href="/wiki/Press_release" title="Press release">press releases</a> issued by research institutes and universities.<sup id="cite_ref-Yong_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yong-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="PNAS_Plus_and_significance_statements"><i>PNAS</i> Plus and significance statements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: PNAS Plus and significance statements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 2011, <i>PNAS</i> started considering manuscripts for exclusive online publication, "<i>PNAS</i> Plus" papers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> These have a larger maximum page limit (10 rather than 6 pages). Accompanying these papers both online and in print was a one- to two-page summary description written by the authors for a broad readership. Since mid-October 2012, <i>PNAS</i> Plus authors no longer need to submit author summaries and are instead asked to submit a 120-word-maximum statement about the significance of their paper. The significance statement will appear both online and in print.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Since July 15, 2013, the significance statement is required for all research articles.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_science">Sustainability science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sustainability science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006 <i>PNAS</i> launched a new section of the journal dedicated to sustainability science, an emerging field of research dealing with the interactions between natural and social systems, and with how those interactions affect the challenge of sustainability: meeting the needs of present and future generations while substantially reducing poverty and conserving the planet's life support systems. See the Sustainability Science portal <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sustainability.pnas.org/">here</a>.</p>
