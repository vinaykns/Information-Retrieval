<p>The <b>Aguçadoura Wave Farm</b> was the world's first <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a>. It was located 5&#160;km (3&#160;mi) offshore near <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">Póvoa de Varzim</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. The farm was designed to use three <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter" title="Pelamis Wave Energy Converter">Pelamis Wave Energy Converters</a> to convert the motion of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface waves</a> into electricity, totalling to 2.25&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a> in total installed capacity. The farm was officially opened on 23 September 2008, by the Portuguese Minister of Economy.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.pt_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.pt-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The wave farm was shut down two months after the official opening in November 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Pelamis_Sinks_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pelamis_Sinks-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CEO_Exits_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEO_Exits-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pelamis_machines">Pelamis machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pelamis machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/220px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" width="220" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/330px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/440px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3371" data-file-height="1524" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Pelamis machine at the wave park.</div>

<p>Developed by the Scottish company <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Power" title="Pelamis Wave Power">Pelamis Wave Power</a>, the Pelamis machine is made up of connected sections which flex and bend relative to one another as waves run along the structure. This motion is resisted by <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_ram" title="Hydraulic ram">hydraulic rams</a> which pump high pressure oil through <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_machinery" title="Hydraulic machinery">hydraulic motors</a> which in turn drive <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generators</a>. The three machines which made up the Aguçadoura Wave Park were each rated at a peak output of 750&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kilowatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>, giving an installed peak capacity of 2.25&#160;MW, enough to meet the average electricity demand of more than 1,500 Portuguese homes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The average output from a Pelamis machine will depend on the wave resource in a particular area. The higher the resource the higher the average output. According to information on the Pelamis web site, it appears that the average power output for a Pelamis wave machine is about 150&#160;kW. <sup id="cite_ref-Average_Resource_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Average_Resource-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_management">Project management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The project was originally conceived by the Portuguese renewable energy company <a href="/wiki/Enersis" title="Enersis">Enersis</a>, which developed and financed the project and which was subsequently bought by the Australian infrastructure company <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Brown" title="Babcock &amp; Brown">Babcock &amp; Brown</a> in December 2005. In the last quarter of 2008, Babcock &amp; Brown had its shares suspended and has been in a managed process of selling its assets, including the Aguçadoura project. In March 2009, Babcock &amp; Brown went into voluntary administration.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2008, the Pelamis machines were brought back to harbor at <a href="/wiki/Leix%C3%B5es" title="Leixões">Leixões</a> due to a technical problem with some of the bearings for which a solution has been found. However, the machines are likely to remain off-line until a new partner is found to take over Babcock &amp; Brown’s 77% share in the project. This seems unlikely, because according to Pelamis "those machines are sub-optimal" and the owner is trying to sell them.<sup id="cite_ref-CEO_Exits_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEO_Exits-4">[4]</a></sup> Pelamis subsequently focused its efforts on the new P2 machine,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which was tested in Orkney in Scotland beginning in 2010. Pelamis ceased operations in 2014;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the P2 device was dismantled in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
