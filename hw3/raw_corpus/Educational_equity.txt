<p><br />
<b>Educational equity</b>, also referred to as <b>equity in education</b>, is a measure of achievement, fairness, and opportunity in education. The study of education equity is often linked with the study of <a href="/wiki/Excellence_and_equity" title="Excellence and equity">excellence and equity</a>.</p>
<p>Educational equity is dependent on two main factors. The first is <a href="/wiki/Distributive_justice" title="Distributive justice">fairness</a>, which implies that factors specific to one's personal conditions should not interfere with the potential of academic success. The second important factor is <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_(value_and_practice)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inclusion (value and practice)">inclusion</a>, which refers to a comprehensive standard that applies to everyone in a certain education system. These two factors are closely related and are dependent on each other for true academic success of an educational system.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The growing importance of education equity is based on the premise that an individual’s level of education directly correlates to future <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup> Therefore, an academic system that practices educational equity is a strong foundation of a society that is fair and thriving. However, <a href="/wiki/Educational_inequality" title="Educational inequality">inequity in education</a> is challenging to avoid, and can be broken down into inequity due to socioeconomic standing, race, gender or disability.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equity_vs._equality">Equity vs. equality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Equity vs. equality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Often, the terms "equity" and "equality" are interchanged when referring to educational equity. Although similar, there can be important distinctions between the two.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equity">Equity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Equity recognizes that some are at a larger disadvantage than others and aims at compensating for these peoples misfortunes and disabilities to ensure that everyone can attain the same type of healthy lifestyle. Examples of this are: “When libraries offer literacy programs, when schools offer courses in English as a second language, and when foundations target scholarships to students from poor families, they operationalize a belief in equity of access as fairness and as justice”.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> Equity recognizes this uneven playing field and aims to take extra measures by giving those who are in need more than others who are not. Equity aims at making sure that everyone's lifestyle is equal even if it may come at the cost of unequal distribution of access and goods. <a href="/wiki/Social_justice_educational_leadership" title="Social justice educational leadership">Social justice leaders</a> in education strive to ensure equitable outcomes for their students.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equality">Equality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Equality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> defines equality as: “access to channels of communication and sources of information that is made available on even terms to all a level playing field is derived from the concept of fairness as uniform distribution, where everyone is entitled to the same level of access and can avail themselves if they so choose.”<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> In this definition of equality no one person has an unfair advantage. Everyone is given equal opportunities and accessibility and are then free to do what they please with it. However, this is not to say that everyone is then inherently equal. Some people may choose to seize these open and equal opportunities while others let them pass by.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Socio-economic_equity_in_education">Socio-economic equity in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Socio-economic equity in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Income_and_class">Income and class</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Income and class">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Income has always played an important role in shaping academic success. Those who come from a family of a higher <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">socioeconomic status</a> (SES) are privileged with more opportunities than those of lower SES. Those who come from a higher SES can afford things like better tutors, rigorous <a href="/wiki/SAT" title="SAT">SAT</a>/<a href="/wiki/ACT_(test)" title="ACT (test)">ACT</a> prep classes, impressive summer programs, and so on. Parents generally feel more comfortable intervening on behalf of their children to acquire better grades or more qualified teachers (Levitsky). Parents of a higher SES are more willing to donate large sums of money to a certain institution to better improve their child's chances of acceptance, along with other extravagant measures. This creates an unfair advantage and distinct class barrier.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_of_education">Costs of education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Costs of education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The extraordinarily high cost of the many prestigious high schools and universities in the United States makes an attempt at a "level playing field" for all students not so level. High-achieving low-income students do not have the means to attend <a href="/wiki/Selective_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Selective schools">selective schools</a> that better prepare a student for later success. Because of this, low-income students do not even attempt to apply to the top-tier schools for which they are more than qualified. In addition, neighborhoods generally <a href="/wiki/Residential_segregation" class="mw-redirect" title="Residential segregation">segregated</a> by class leave lower-income students in lower-quality schools. For higher-quality schooling, students in low-income areas would be required to take public transport which they do not have the means to pay for. Fewer than 30 percent of students in the bottom quarter of incomes even enroll in a four-year school and among that group, fewer than half graduate.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracking">Tracking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tracking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another contributor to the inequality in the education system is <a href="/wiki/Tracking_(education)" title="Tracking (education)">tracking</a>. Tracking sorts students into different classes or groups based on ability or future plans. The point of tracking is to create an environment in which the student's abilities match both the curriculum as well as the other student's in the class.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This separation, however, creates an inequality within itself. Starting at an extremely young age, the sorting of students mimics a hierarchy similar to what may form later in life. Students are both viewed and treated differently depending on which track they take. The quality of teaching and curricula vary between tracks and as a result, those of the lower track are disadvantaged with inferior resources, teachers, etc. In many cases, tracking stunts students who may develop the ability to excel past their original placement.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_equity_in_education">Racial equity in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Racial equity in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From a scientific point of view, the human species is a single species. It is therefore misleading to use divisive terms such as races and racial groups. Nevertheless, the term racial group is enshrined in legislation, and phrases such as race equality and race relations are in widespread official use.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-5">[5]</a></sup> Racial equity in education means the assignment of students to <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">public schools</a> and within schools without regard to their race. This includes providing students with a full opportunity for participation in all educational programs regardless of their race.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated9_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated9-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The educational system and its response to racial concerns in education vary from country to country. Below are some examples of countries that have to deal with racial discrimination in education.</p>
<ul>
<li>US Department of Education: The Commission on Equity and Excellence in Education issues a seminal report in 2013. It is not a restatement of public education's struggles, nor is it a mere list of recommendations. Rather, this is a declaration of an urgent national mission: to provide equity and excellence in education in American public schools once and for all. This collective wisdom is a historic blueprint for making the dream of equity, and a world-class education, for each and every American child a reality.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated10_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated10-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The struggle for equality of access to formal education and equality of excellent educational outcomes is part of the history of education in this country and is tied up with the economic, political, social history of the peoples who are part of it. From the beginning of this nation, there were many barriers to the schooling and education of girls and racial, national origin, and language groups not from the dominant culture. Approaches and resources for achieving equality and equity in the public schooling of girls and ethnic, racial, and language minority groups are still evolving.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Asia-Pacific Region: Globalization of the economy, increasingly diverse and interconnected populations, and rapid technological change are posing new and demanding challenges to individuals and societies alike. Within the Asia-Pacific region, for example, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, Shanghai-China, and Japan are examples of Asian education systems that have climbed the ladder to the top in both quality and equity indicators.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-9">[9]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_equity_in_education">Gender equity in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gender equity in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gender equity in practicality refers to both male and female concerns, yet most of the gender bias is against women in the developing world. Gender discrimination in education has been very evident and underlying problem in many countries, especially in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> where cultural and societal stigma continue to hinder growth and prosperity for women. <a href="/wiki/Global_Campaign_for_Education" title="Global Campaign for Education">Global Campaign for Education</a> (GCE) followed a survey called "Gender Discrimination in Violation of Rights of Women and Girls" states that one tenth of girls in primary school are 'unhappy' and this number increases to one fifth by the time they reach secondary schools. Some of the reasonings that girls provided include harassment, restorations to freedom, and an inherent lack of opportunities, compared to boys.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (<a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>) understands Education as a " fundamental human right and essential for the exercise of all other human rights. It promotes individual freedom and empowerment and yields important development benefits."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>UN Special Rapporteur Katarina Tomasevki developed the '4A' framework on the Right to Education. The '4A' framework encompasses availability, accessibility, acceptability and adaptability as fundamental to the institution of education. And yet girls in many underdeveloped countries are denied secondary education. Figure on the right shows the discrepancies in secondary education in the world. Countries such as <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> face the highest of inequity when it comes to gender bias.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gender-based inequity in education is not just a phenomenon in developing countries. A <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> article<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> highlights how education systems, especially public school systems, tend to segregate. Boys and girls are often taught with different approach which programs children to think that they are different and deserve different treatment. However, studies show that boys and girls learn differently and therefore should be taught differently. Boys learn better when they are kept moving while girls learn better sitting in one place with silence. Therefore, segregation of gender for this reasoning promotes gender equity in education as both boys and girls have optimized learning.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_gender_discrimination_in_education">Causes of gender discrimination in education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Causes of gender discrimination in education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Service_Overseas" title="Voluntary Service Overseas">VSO</a> is a leading independent international development organization that works towards eliminating poverty and one of the problems they tackle is gender inequity in education.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> VSO published a paper that categorizes the obstacles (or causes) into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Community Level Obstacles: This category primarily relates to the bias displayed for education external to the school environment. This includes restraints due to poverty and child labour, soil-economic constraints, lack of parental involvement and community participation. Harmful practices like child marriage and predetermined gender roles are cultural hindrances.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>School and Education System Level Obstacles: Lack of investment in quality education, inappropriate attitudes and behaviors, lack of female teachers as role models and lack of gender-friendly school environment are all factors that promote gender inequity in education.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_gender_discrimination_on_the_economy">Impact of gender discrimination on the economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Impact of gender discrimination on the economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Education is universally acknowledged as an essential human right because it highly impacts the socio-economic and cultural aspects of a country. Equity in education increases the work force of the nation, therefore increasing <a href="/wiki/National_income" class="mw-redirect" title="National income">national income</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_productivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic productivity">economic productivity</a>, and [gross domestic product]. It reduces fertility and <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">infant mortality</a>, improves child health, increases life expectancy and increases standards of living.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> These are factors that allow <a href="/wiki/Economic_stability" title="Economic stability">economic stability</a> and growth in the future. Above all, female education can increase output levels and allow countries to attain sustainable development. Equity in education of women also reduces the possibilities of trafficking and exploitation of women. UNESCO also refers gender equity as a major factor that allows for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2013/11/gender-inequality">http://www.economist.com/blogs/freeexchange/2013/11/gender-inequality</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> is an article published by <i>The Economist</i>, which says:</p>
<p>"Looking at recently-published UN statistics on gender inequality in education, one observes that the overall picture has improved dramatically over the last decade, but progress has not been even (see chart). Although the developing world on average looks likely to hit the UN’s gender-inequality target, many parts of Africa are lagging behind. While progress is being made in sub-Saharan Africa in primary education, gender inequality is in fact widening among older children. The ratio of girls enrolled in primary school rose from 85 to 93 per 100 boys between 1999 and 2010, whereas it fell from 83 to 82 and from 67 to 63 at the secondary and tertiary levels."</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_equality_in_education_-_a_survey_report_by_England">Race equality in education - a survey report by England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Race equality in education - a survey report by England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The local authorities in England gave a survey report <b>Race equality in education</b> in November 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-27">[27]</a></sup> This report is based on visits by Her Majesty.s Inspectors (HMIs) and additional inspectors to 12 LEAs and 50 schools in England between summer term 2003 to spring term 2005. This report illustrates good practice on race equality in education in a sample of schools and local education authorities (LEAs) surveyed between the summer of 2003 and the spring of 2005. The survey focused on schools and LEAs that were involved effectively in race equality in education. Four areas were examined by inspectors: improving standards and achievement amongst groups of pupils, with reference to the Race Relations (Amendment) Act 2000 (RRAA); the incorporation of race equality concepts into the curriculum in schools; the handling and reporting of race-related incidents in schools; the work of schools and LEAs in improving links with local minority ethnic communities.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_equity_in_education:_how_far_has_South_Africa_come.3F">Racial equity in education: how far has South Africa come?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Racial equity in education: how far has South Africa come?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A major task of South Africa’s new government in 1994 was to promote racial equity in the state education system. This paper evaluates progress towards this goal using three distinct concepts: equal treatment, equal educational opportunity, and educational adequacy. The authors find that the country has succeeded in establishing racial equity defined as equal treatment, primarily through race-blind policies for allocating state funds for schools. Progress measured by the other two criteria, however, has been constrained by the legacy of apartheid, including poor facilities and lack of human capacity in schools serving black students, and by policies such as school fees.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_in_education:_an_argument_for_integrative_analysis">Race in education: an argument for integrative analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Race in education: an argument for integrative analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Race, social class, and gender tend to be treated as separate issues in education literature. We review a sample of education literature from four academic journals, spanning ten years, to determine the extent to which these status groups were integrated. We found little integration. We then provide an example from research on cooperative learning to illustrate how attention to only one status group oversimplifies the analysis of student behavior in school. From findings of studies integrating race and class, and race and gender, we argue that attending only to race, in this example, oversimplifies behavior analysis and may contribute to perpetuation of gender and class biases. To determine to what extent race, social class, and gender are integrated in the education literature, we examined a sample of literature published over a ten-year period and 30 articles focused primarily on race, or on school issues related directly to race, such as desegregation.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_in_educational_equity">Challenges in educational equity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Challenges in educational equity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The long-term social and economic consequences of having little education are more tangible now than ever before. Those without the skills to participate socially and economically in society generate higher costs of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_total_health_expenditure_(PPP)_per_capita" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries by total health expenditure (PPP) per capita">healthcare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Income_support" class="mw-redirect" title="Income support">income support</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_welfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Child welfare">child welfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Societal_structure_and_costs">Societal structure and costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Societal structure and costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While both <a href="/wiki/Basic_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic education">basic education</a> and higher education have both been improved and expanded in the past 50 years, this has not translated to a more equal society in terms of academics. While the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a> has made great strides for women, other groups have not been as fortunate. Generally, <a href="/wiki/Social_mobility" title="Social mobility">social mobility</a> has not increased, while <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">economic inequality</a> has.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup> So, while more students are getting a basic education and even attending universities, a dramatic divide is present and many people are still being left behind.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increase_migration_and_diversity">Increase migration and diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Increase migration and diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As increased immigration causes problems in educational equity for some countries, poor <a href="/wiki/Social_cohesion" class="mw-redirect" title="Social cohesion">social cohesion</a> in other countries is also a major issue. In countries where continued migration causes an issue, the ever-changing social structure of different races makes it difficult to propose a long-term solution to educational equity. On the other hand, many countries with consistent levels of diversity experience long-standing issues of integrating <a href="/wiki/Minorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Minorities">minorities</a>. Challenges for minorities and migrants are often exacerbated as these groups statistically struggle more in terms of both lower academic performance and lower <a href="/wiki/Socio-economic_status" class="mw-redirect" title="Socio-economic status">socio-economic status</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recommendations_and_solutions">Recommendations and solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Recommendations and solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schools and teachers play a valuable role in promoting good race relations between people of different racial groups, eliminating unlawful racial discrimination and promoting equality of outcomes between these groups, but they need support to continue.</p>
<ul>
<li>For Schools: Inclusion of race equality concepts in lessons should be seen as a normal part of effective teaching and learning; local resources in lessons involving race equality, such as work by local black and minority ethnic writers, and in the history of local industrialization, should be used to stimulate pupils' interest and learning. From <b>Race equality in education</b><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>One of the quotations, 'there is nothing more important than closing the gap between national averages and the educational attainments of black kids, Muslim kids, travellers and <a href="/wiki/Gypsy_(term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gypsy (term)">gypsies</a>, and most refugee kids.' But the significance of closing the achievement gap is manifold; it is fundamental to building a sense of belonging and to improving a sense of safety amongst all pupils. All of which are essential to pursuing race equality in schools. From <b>Race equality and education</b><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>For Teachers: All bullying is wrong and causes recipients great distress. Teachers should be alert to potential incidences of bullying and intervene when they become aware of it. There should be a framework within which all members of staff should operate in their response to such incidences. It is not a matter for an individual to deal with on their own. Whilst there are several similarities between racist bullying and other forms of bullying, there are significant differences. It is essential that teachers should be aware of these. Helping children and young people to understand this, and to act against unjustifiable inequalities, is one of the most important challenges facing teachers. ATL believes that this publication will help enable teachers to fulfil this role and help schools meet their legal obligations under the Race Relations (Amendment) Act 2000. From <b>Race equality and education</b><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>For Governments: One of the most efficient educational strategies for governments is to invest early and all the way up to upper secondary. Governments can prevent school failure and reduce dropout using two parallel approaches: eliminating education policies and practices that hinder equity; and targeting low performing disadvantaged schools. But education policies need to be aligned with other government policies, such as housing or welfare, to ensure student success. At this initial educational stage, direct public funding of services is associated with more effective governmental monitoring of early childhood services, advantages of scale, better quality across the country, more effective training for educators and a higher degree of equity in access.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>For Society: Each and every one of us has a personal responsibility to confront these issues and to contribute to a change in societal perceptions and views. Whilst no one individual can act alone, changes in society can be achieved if we all work together. Discrimination is not limited to <a href="/wiki/Skin_colour" class="mw-redirect" title="Skin colour">skin colour</a>, however. The <a href="/wiki/Experiences" class="mw-redirect" title="Experiences">experiences</a>, expectations and opportunities open to an individual can be as much dependent on their <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, age, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>, or the national, cultural and religious traditions into which they were born. The task is not easy. It can be frequently stressful as it involves confronting and challenging others – children, young people, their <a href="/wiki/Parents" class="mw-redirect" title="Parents">parents</a>, and one's <a href="/wiki/Colleagues" class="mw-redirect" title="Colleagues">colleagues</a>. Further, it also involves confronting and challenging oneself.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Race Equality Toolkit: Universities in Scotland first published the Race Equality Toolkit – Learning and Teaching in 2006 in response to strong demand from the universities in Scotland for guidance on meeting their statutory obligations in terms of the Race Relations (Amendment) Act 2000. This resource is designed to assist academic staff, particularly those less familiar with race equality issues, to embed issues of race equality and of fostering good relations as part of learning and teaching and curriculum design. It does not provide a blueprint for how race equality should be addressed in teaching and learning but it encourages the academic staff to self-evaluate, and to review the curriculum and their teaching and assessment methods to create as inclusive a learning environment as possible. The Toolkit, therefore, encourages the institutions to develop the corporate strategies necessary to support individual lecturers and departments in mainstreaming race equality issues.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated8_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated8-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
