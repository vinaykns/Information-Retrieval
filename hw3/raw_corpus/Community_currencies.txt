<p>A <b>community currency</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Complementary_currency" title="Complementary currency">complementary currency</a> that is used by groups with a common bond, like members of a locality, or association, and designed to meet their needs. A community currency may be geography-based, making it a type of <a href="/wiki/Local_currency" title="Local currency">local currency</a>, or it may be used within a business-based, or <a href="/wiki/Online_community" title="Online community">online community</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purposes">Purposes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Community currencies aim at using money as a tool to achieve social or environmental objectives. According to the <a href="/wiki/New_Economics_Foundation" title="New Economics Foundation">New Economics Foundation</a> partner Community Currencies in Action:<sup id="cite_ref-CCIA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCIA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><i>... money is simply a social technology and the ways in which it is designed, produced and controlled – far from being neutral or predetermined factors – all influence the effects it has upon society at large.</i></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="3" class="cquotecite" style="border: none; padding-right: 4%; font-size: smaller; text-align: right;"><cite>— People Powered Money: designing, developing and delivering community currencies.</cite></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Some of the purposes for community currencies identified by Community Currencies in Action<sup id="cite_ref-CCIA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCIA-1">[1]</a></sup> include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Democratizing services and organisations: time credits for volunteering encourage people to actively engage in their community while making services, such as elderly care, more democratic. Zeitvorsoge,<sup id="cite_ref-CCIA_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCIA-1">[1]</a></sup> Makkie<sup id="cite_ref-CCIA_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCIA-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Supporting small and medium enterprises: Community currencies can serve as a means to promote independent shops over large corporations since they keep on circulating locally. They can also help SMEs support each other financially by lending and receiving credit, goods and services within the currency network. Examples are: <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Pound" title="Bristol Pound">Bristol Pound</a>, SoNantes,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> TradeQoin,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chiemgauer" title="Chiemgauer">Chiemgauer</a></li>
<li>Countering inequality and social exclusion: Specially designed currencies can address inequality issues by giving everyone the chance to get involved in their community; for instance by rewarding participation in voluntary programs. (Spice Time Credits,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Makkie)</li>
<li>Addressing environmental impacts: Community currencies can play a role in better valuation of environmental resources and providing an incentive for more sustainable behavior. For example, the Belgian Portemonnee rewards residents for environmentally positive actions such as composting. Reward currencies can also encourage businesses to adopt more environmentally sound practices.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several software packages have been written supporting the management of community currencies.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1998, Richard Kay, a Senior Lecturer at Birmingham City University,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> wrote a "Multi-registry System" specification for routing and processing community currency transactions using an approach designed to be decentralized, with no single point of control or failure, using the <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> for server discovery.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
