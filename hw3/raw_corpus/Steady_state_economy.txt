<p>A <b>steady-state economy</b> is an economy made up of a constant stock of physical wealth (capital) and a constant population size. The term typically refers to the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">national economy</a> of a particular country, but it is also applicable to the economic system of a city, a region, or the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">entire world</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/History_of_economic_thought" title="History of economic thought">history of economic thought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economist</a> <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> of the 18th century was the first economist to theorise on the concept of a <i>stationary state</i> of an economy. Smith conjectured that any national economy in the world would sooner or later settle in a final state of stationarity.</p>
<p>Since the 1970s, the concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b> has been associated mainly with the work of leading <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economist</a> <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>. As Daly's concept of a <i>steady-state</i> includes the ecological analysis of natural resource flows through the economy, his concept differs from the original classical concept of a <i>stationary state</i>. One other difference is that Daly recommends immediate political action to establish the steady-state economy by imposing permanent government restrictions on all resource use, whereas Adam Smith and the other economists of the original classical period of theorising believed that the final stationary state of any economy would settle by itself without any government intervention.</p>
<p>A <b>steady-state economy</b> is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Economic_stagnation" title="Economic stagnation">economic stagnation</a>: Whereas a steady-state economy is established as the result of deliberate political action, economic stagnation is the unexpected and unwelcome failure of a growth economy.</p>
<p>The world's <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">mounting ecological problems</a> have brought about a widening interest in the concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b>. Critics of the steady-state economy object to it by arguing that <a href="/wiki/Sustainability#Decoupling_environmental_degradation_and_economic_growth" title="Sustainability">resource decoupling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Productivity_improving_technologies" title="Productivity improving technologies">technological development</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)#Mechanisms_of_markets" title="Market (economics)">unrestrained operation of market mechanisms</a> are fully capable of overcoming any resource scarcity, any rampant pollution or any overpopulation ever to be encountered on Earth. Proponents of the steady-state economy, on the other hand, rebut that these objections remain insubstantial and mistaken — and that the case for a steady-state economy is gaining leverage every day.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_vision">Definition and vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herman Daly defines his concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b> as an economic system made up of a constant stock of physical wealth (capital) and a constant stock of people (population), both stocks to be maintained by a flow of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> through the system. The first component, the constant stocks, is similar to the concept of the <i>stationary state</i>, originally used in <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economics</a>; the second component, the flow of natural resources, is a new <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological feature</a>, presently also used in the academic discipline of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>. The <i>durability</i> of both of the constant stocks is to be maximized: The more durable the stock of capital is, the smaller the flow of natural resources is needed to maintain the stock; likewise, a 'durable' population means a population enjoying a high life expectancy — something desirable by itself — maintained by a low birth rate and an equally low death rate. Taken together, higher durability translates into better ecology in the system as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14–19</sup></p>
<p>Daly's concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b> is based on the vision that man's economy is an open subsystem embedded in a finite natural environment of scarce resources and fragile ecosystems. The economy is maintained by importing valuable natural resources from the input end and exporting valueless waste and pollution at the output end in a constant and irreversible flow. Any subsystem of a finite nongrowing system must itself at some point also become nongrowing and start maintaining itself in a steady-state as far as possible. This vision is opposed to <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">mainstream neoclassical economics</a>, where the economy is represented by an isolated and circular model with goods and services exchanging endlessly between companies and households, without exhibiting any physical contact to the natural environment.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xiii</sup></p>
<p>In the early 2010s, reviewers sympathetic towards Daly's concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b> have passed the concurrent judgement that although his concept remains beyond what is politically feasible at present, there is room for mainstream thinking and collective action to approach the concept in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-ck01_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ck01-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:549</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-ma01_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ma01-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:84</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-ap01_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:83</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_background:_Exceeding_global_limits_to_growth">Present background: Exceeding global limits to growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Present background: Exceeding global limits to growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">Planetary boundaries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_issues" title="List of environmental issues">List of environmental issues</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">Societal collapse</a></div>

<p>The widening interest in the concept of a <b>steady-state economy</b> has a background in the world's mounting ecological problems: Since the 1990s, measurements have provided evidence that the volume of the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a> far exceeds critical global limits to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> already. According to the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint#Footprint_measurements" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint measure</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sustainability#Carrying_capacity" title="Sustainability">carrying capacity</a> of Earth — that is, Earth's long-term capacity to sustain human populations and consumption levels — was exceeded by some twenty percent in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-be01_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-be01-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:18</sup> By 2015, this figure had increased to some sixty percent.<sup id="cite_ref-gfn01_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gfn01-7">[7]</a></sup> In effect, mankind is now confronted with the vicious dynamics of planetary <a href="/wiki/Overshoot_(population)" title="Overshoot (population)">overshoot-and-collapse</a>. Furthermore, the concept of the <i><a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">anthropocene</a></i> has been proposed by geologists as a new <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a> to describe the significant impact of human activities on Earth's ecosystems. Specifically, the following issues have long been of general concern worldwide:</p>
<p><b>Human overpopulation</b><br />
World population is expected to reach 9.3 billion by 2050, and continue growing thereafter. This massive number of people is already resulting in <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human overpopulation</a>, putting excessive strain on all kinds of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">wildlife habitats</a> everywhere, increasing pollution levels and deteriorating human living conditions. Uncontrolled urbanisation is forcing people to live in congested <a href="/wiki/Shanty_town" title="Shanty town">shanty towns</a>, and large cities swell to become <a href="/wiki/Megacity" title="Megacity">megacities</a> with <a href="/wiki/Megacity#Slums" title="Megacity">slum areas</a> ripe with <a href="/wiki/Megacity#Crime" title="Megacity">high crime rates</a>. Overpopulation may even lead to <a href="/wiki/War#Youth_bulge" title="War">social conflict and violence</a>, when too many people (especially young males) end up competing for too few employment opportunities in <a href="/wiki/Economic_stagnation" title="Economic stagnation">stagnating economies</a>.</p>
<p><b>Air pollution and global warming</b><br />
Air pollution emanating from motor vehicles and industrial plants is <a href="/wiki/Pollution#Human_health" title="Pollution">damaging public health and increasing mortality rates</a>. The concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> in the atmosphere is evidently the source of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming and climate change</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">extreme regional weather patterns</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise#Effects" title="Sea level rise">rising sea levels</a> caused by the warming may deteriorate future living conditions in many — if not all — parts of the world. The warming already poses a <a href="/wiki/Climate_security#General" title="Climate security">security threat to many nations</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans#Security" title="Effects of global warming on humans">'threat multiplier' to geo-political stability</a>.</p>
<p><b>Depletion of non-renewable minerals</b><br />
Non-renewable <a href="/wiki/Mineral_resource_classification" title="Mineral resource classification">mineral reserves</a> are currently extracted at high and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development#Insubstantial_stretching_of_the_term" title="Sustainable development">unsustainable rates</a> from <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth#Crust" title="Structure of the Earth">the Earth's crust</a>. Hence, the remaining reserves are likely to <a href="/wiki/Peak_minerals#Future_production" title="Peak minerals">become ever more costly to extract in the near future</a>, and will reach depletion at some point. The era of (relatively) peaceful economic expansion that has prevailed globally since World War II may be interrupted by unexpected <a href="/wiki/Supply_shock" title="Supply shock">supply shocks</a>; or simply be succeeded by the all-too-expected <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_peak_theory#Hubbert_peaks" title="Hubbert peak theory">peaking depletion paths</a> of <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peak_minerals" title="Peak minerals">other valuable minerals</a>.</p>
<p><b>Excessive use of renewable resources</b><br />
Excessive use of otherwise <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource#Classification" title="Natural resource">renewable</a> resources are undermining <a href="/wiki/Ecological_stability" title="Ecological stability">ecological stability</a> worldwide. Between 2000 and 2012 alone, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">some fourteen percent of what amounts to Earth's original forest cover was deforested</a>. Tropical rainforests have been subject to <a href="/wiki/Rainforest#Deforestation" title="Rainforest">deforestation at a rapid pace for decades</a> — especially in <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_by_region#Africa" title="Deforestation by region">West and Central Africa</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_of_the_Amazon_rainforest" title="Deforestation of the Amazon rainforest">Brazil</a> — mostly due to <a href="/wiki/Deforestation#Causes" title="Deforestation">subsistence farming, population pressure and urbanization</a>. Population pressure is also exerting much strain on the <a href="/wiki/Soil_retrogression_and_degradation#Consequences_of_soil_regression_and_degradation" title="Soil retrogression and degradation">world's soil systems, leading to land degradation</a>, mostly in developing countries. Global erosion rates on conventional cropland are presently estimated to <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment#Agricultural_land_loss_and_soil_erosion" title="Human impact on the environment">exceed soil creation rates by more than one order of magnitude</a>. Widespread overuse of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> result in <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity#Overview_of_regions_suffering_crisis_impacts" title="Water scarcity">water deficits in many countries of the world</a>. By 2025, <a href="/wiki/Water_security#Fresh_Water" title="Water security">the living conditions of two-thirds of the world's population could be stressed by water scarcity</a>.</p>
<p><b>Endangered wildlife and loss of biodiversity</b><br />
The destructive impact of human activity on <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">wildlife habitats</a> worldwide is accelerating the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene#Biodiversity" title="Anthropocene">extinction of rare species</a>, thereby substantially reducing <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity#Species_loss_rates" title="Biodiversity">Earth's biodiversity of plants and animals</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">natural nitrogen cycle</a> is heavily overloaded by <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation#Industrial_nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">industrial nitrogen fixation and use</a>, thereby <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_nitrogen_cycle#Impacts_of_anthropogenic_inputs_on_the_nitrogen_cycle" title="Human impact on the nitrogen cycle">disrupting most known types of ecosystems</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution#Plastic_debris" title="Marine pollution">accumulating pollution of plastic debris in the oceans</a> leads to entanglement, suffocation and ingestion of aquatic life. The <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidification of the oceans</a> due to concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the atmosphere is resulting in <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification#Impacts_on_oceanic_calcifying_organisms" title="Ocean acidification">impedes oceanic calcifying organisms</a>. <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a> caused by global warming is <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear#Climate_change" title="Polar bear">endangering the polar bear</a>.</p>
<p>These mounting concerns have prompted an increasing number of philosophers, economists — besides <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> — and natural scientists to point to obvious global limits to <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, and to question — or even oppose — the prevailing political orthodoxy of persistently pursuing growth.<sup id="cite_ref-bc01_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bc01-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jm02_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jm02-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dm01_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dm01-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rh01_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rh01-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-js01_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-js01-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pe01_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pe01-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tj01_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tj01-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sl01_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sl01-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-do01_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-do01-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mh01_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mh01-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rb01_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rb01-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jg01_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jg01-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jd01_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jd01-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-av01_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-av01-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gd01_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gd01-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pr01_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pr01-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For centuries, economists from <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economists</a> like <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> down to present-day <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economists</a> have considered a transition from a growing economy to a stable (stationary, steady) one.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_stationary_state_in_classical_economics">The stationary state in classical economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The stationary state in classical economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">Classical economics</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tendency_of_the_rate_of_profit_to_fall" title="Tendency of the rate of profit to fall">Tendency of the rate of profit to fall</a></div>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> and onwards, economists in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical period of economic theorising</a> described the general development of society in terms of a contrast between the scarcity of arable agricultural land on the one hand, and the growth of population and capital on the other hand. The incomes from gross production were distributed as rents, profits and wages among landowners, capitalists and labourers respectively, and these three classes were incessantly engaged in the struggle for increasing their own share. The accumulation of capital (net investments) would sooner or later come to an end as the <a href="/wiki/Tendency_of_the_rate_of_profit_to_fall" title="Tendency of the rate of profit to fall">rate of profit fell to a minimum or to nil</a>. At that point, the economy would settle in a final stationary state with a constant population size and a constant stock of capital.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:295</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adam_Smith.27s_concept_of_the_stationary_state">Adam Smith's concept of the stationary state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adam Smith's concept of the stationary state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p><a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i>, published in 1776, laid the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economics</a> in Britain. Smith thereby disseminated and established a concept that has since been a cornerstone in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> throughout most of the world: In a <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">liberal capitalist society</a>, provided with a stable institutional and legal framework, an '<a href="/wiki/Invisible_hand" title="Invisible hand">invisible hand</a>' will ensure that the enlightened self-interest of all members of society will contribute to the growth and prosperity of society as a whole, thereby leading to an 'obvious and simple system of natural liberty'.<sup id="cite_ref-as01_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-as01-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:349f, 533f</sup></p>
<p>Smith was convinced of the beneficial effect of the enlightened self-interest on the wealth of nations; but he was less certain this wealth would grow forever. Smith observed that any country in the world found itself in either a 'progressive', a 'stationary', or a 'declining' state: Although England was wealthier than its North American colonies, wages were higher in the latter place as wealth in North America was growing faster than in England; hence, North America was in the 'cheerful and hearty' progressive state. In China, on the other hand, wages were low, the condition of poor people was scantier than in any nation in Europe, and more marriages were contracted here because the 'horrid' killing of newborn babies was permitted and even widely practised; hence, China was in the 'dull' stationary state, although it did not yet seem to be declining. In nations situated in the 'melancholic' declining state, the higher ranks of society would fall down and settle for occupation amid the lower ranks, while the lowest ranks would either subsist on a miserable and insufficient wage, resort to begging or crime, or slide into starvation and early death. Bengal and some other English settlements in the East Indies possibly found themselves in this state, Smith reckoned.<sup id="cite_ref-as01_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-as01-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59–68</sup></p>
<p>Smith pointed out that as wealth was growing in any nation, the rate of profit would tend to fall and investment opportunities would diminish. In a nation that had thereby reached this 'full complement of riches', society would finally settle in a stationary state with a constant stock of people and capital. In an 18th-century anticipation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>, Smith described the state as follows:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">In a country which had acquired that full complement of riches which the nature of its soil and climate, and its situation with respect to other countries, allowed it to acquire; which could, therefore, advance no further, and which was not going backwards, both the wages of labour and the profits of stock would probably be very low. In a country fully peopled in proportion to what either its territory could maintain or its stock employ, the competition for employment would necessarily be so great as to reduce the wages of labour to what was barely sufficient to keep up the number of labourers, and, the country being already fully peopled, that number could never be augmented. In a country fully stocked in proportion to all the business it had to transact, as great a quantity of stock would be employed in every particular branch as the nature and extent of the trade would admit. The competition, therefore, would everywhere be as great, and consequently the ordinary profit as low as possible."<sup id="cite_ref-as01_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-as01-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:78</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>According to Smith, Holland seemed to be approaching this stationary state, although at a much higher level than in China.<sup id="cite_ref-as01_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-as01-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:79</sup> Smith was unable to provide any contemporary examples of a nation in the world that had in fact reached the full complement of riches and thus had settled in stationarity, because, as he conjectured, "... perhaps no country has ever yet arrived at this degree of opulence."<sup id="cite_ref-as01_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-as01-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:78</sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>In the early 19th century, <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a> was the leading economist of the day and the champion of British <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism"><i>laissez-faire</i> liberalism</a>. According to Ricardo, the limits to growth were ever present due to scarcity of arable agricultural land. In the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> on the European continent, the British economy was approaching the stationary state as population was growing, plots of land with decreasing fertility were put into agricultural use, and the rents of the rural landowning class were crowding out the profits of the urban capitalists. This was the broad outline of Ricardo's controversial land rent theory, published in his <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Principles_of_Political_Economy_and_Taxation" title="On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation">On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation</a></i> in 1817.<sup id="cite_ref-mb01_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb01-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ricardo believed that the only way for Britain to avoid the stationary state was to increase its volume of <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a>. The country should export more of its industrial products and start importing cheap agricultural products from abroad in turn. However, this course of development was impeded by the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Laws" title="Corn Laws">Corn Laws</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Protectionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Protectionist">protectionist</a> two-sided measure of <a href="/wiki/Subsidies" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsidies">subsidies</a> on corn exports and <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> on corn imports. These laws seemed to be hampering both the <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Internationalization" title="Internationalization">internationalization</a> of the British economy, but the laws were nonetheless upheld by a Parliament dominated by a landowning class only too eager to preserve its own position and income in society. In the 1820s, Ricardo and his followers — Ricardo himself died in 1823 — directed much of their fire at the Corn Laws in order to have them repealed.<sup id="cite_ref-mb01_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb01-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Corn Laws were not repealed before 1846. In the meantime, the British economy kept growing, a fact that effectively undermined the credibility and thrust of Ricardian economics in Britain; but Ricardo had by now established himself as the first stationary state theorist in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_economic_thought" title="History of economic thought">history of economic thought</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88f</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Stuart_Mill.27s_concept_of_the_stationary_state">John Stuart Mill's concept of the stationary state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: John Stuart Mill's concept of the stationary state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Liberalism#Classical_and_modern" title="Liberalism">Liberalism §&#160;Classical and modern</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism#Classical_utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">Utilitarianism §&#160;Classical utilitarianism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Political_Economy" title="Principles of Political Economy">Principles of Political Economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/On_Liberty" title="On Liberty">On Liberty</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">Utilitarianism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JohnStuartMill.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/JohnStuartMill.JPG/170px-JohnStuartMill.JPG" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/JohnStuartMill.JPG/255px-JohnStuartMill.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/JohnStuartMill.JPG/340px-JohnStuartMill.JPG 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JohnStuartMill.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mill believed the future stationary state was both inevitable, necessary and desirable</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>John Stuart Mill was the leading economist, philosopher and social reformer in the middle of 19th century Britain. His economics treatise on the <i><a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Political_Economy" title="Principles of Political Economy">Principles of Political Economy</a></i>, published in 1848, attained status as the standard textbook in economics throughout the English-speaking world until the turn of the century.<sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:179</sup></p>
<p>A champion of <a href="/wiki/Liberalism#Classical_and_modern" title="Liberalism">classical liberalism</a>, Mill believed that an ideal society should allow all individuals to pursue their own good without any interference from others or from government.<sup id="cite_ref-jsm02_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm02-27">[27]</a></sup> Also a <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism#Classical_utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarian philosopher</a>, Mill regarded the 'Greatest Happiness Principle' as the ultimate ideal for a harmonious society:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">As the means of making the nearest approach to this ideal, utility would enjoin, first, that laws and social arrangements should place the happiness ... of every individual, as nearly as possible in harmony with the interest of the whole; and secondly, that education and opinion, which have so vast a power over human character, should so use that power as to establish in the mind of every individual an indissoluble association between his own happiness and the good of the whole; ...<sup id="cite_ref-jsm03_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm03-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:19</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Mill's concept of the stationary state was strongly coloured by these ideals.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:213</sup> Mill conjectured that the stationary state of society was not too far away in the future:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">It must always have been seen, more or less distinctly, by political economists, that the increase of wealth is not boundless; that at the end of what they term the progressive state lies the stationary state, that all progress in wealth is but a postponement of this, and that each step in advance is an approach to it. We have now been led to recognize that this ultimate goal is at all times near enough to be fully in view; that we are always on the verge of it, and that, if we have not reached it long ago, it is because the goal itself flies before us.<sup id="cite_ref-jsm01_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm01-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:592</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Contrary to both Smith and Ricardo before him, Mill took an optimistic view on the future stationary state. Mill could not "... regard the stationary state of capital and wealth with the unaffected aversion so generally manifested toward it by political economists of the old school."<sup id="cite_ref-jsm01_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm01-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:593</sup> Instead, Mill attributed many important qualities to this future state, he even believed the state would bring about "... a very considerable improvement on our present condition."<sup id="cite_ref-jsm01_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm01-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:593</sup> According to Mill, the stationary state was at one and the same time inevitable, necessary and desirable: It was <i>inevitable</i>, because the accumulation of capital would bring about a falling rate of profit that would diminish investment opportunities and hamper further accumulation; it was also <i>necessary</i>, because mankind had to learn how to reduce its size and its level of consumption within the boundaries set by nature and by employment opportunities; finally, the stationary state was <i>desirable</i>, as it would ease the introduction of public income redistibution schemes, create more equality and put an end to man's ruthless struggle to get by — instead, the human spirit would be liberated to the benefit of more elevated social and cultural activities, 'the graces of life'.<sup id="cite_ref-jsm01_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jsm01-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:592–596</sup></p>
<p>Hence, Mill was able to express all of his liberal ideals for mankind through his concept of the stationary state.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14f</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:213</sup> It has been argued that Mill essentially made a quality-of-life argument for the stationary state.<sup id="cite_ref-ma01_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ma01-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:79</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_developments_in_economics_since_Mill">Main developments in economics since Mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Main developments in economics since Mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">Neoclassical economics</a></div>
<p>When the influence of John Stuart Mill and his <i>Principles</i> declined, the classical-liberalist period of economic theorising came to an end. By the turn of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> had emerged to dominate economics. This development led to the exclusion of any concern with natural resource scarcity in economic modelling and analysis:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marx6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Marx6.jpg/170px-Marx6.jpg" width="170" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Marx6.jpg/255px-Marx6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Marx6.jpg/340px-Marx6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="345" data-file-height="504" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marx6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Marx replaced the concept of a stationary state with a vision of a communist society that would bring about abundance for everybody</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>Although a classical economist in his own right, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> abandoned the earlier concept of a stationary state and replaced it with his own unique vision of <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a>, according to which human societies pass through several <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">'modes of production'</a>, eventually leading to <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>. In each mode of production, man's increasing mastery over nature and the '<a href="/wiki/Productive_forces" title="Productive forces">productive forces</a>' of society develop to a point where the class struggle bursts into revolutions, followed by the establishment of a new mode of production. In opposition to his liberalist predecessors in the field, Marx did not regard natural resource scarcity as a factor constraining future economic growth; instead, the capitalist mode of production was to be overturned before the productive forces of society could fully develop, bringing about an abundance of goods in a new society based on the principle of "from each according to ability, to each according to need" — that is, communism. It was assumed that communism would overcome any resource scarcity ever to be encountered.<sup id="cite_ref-rg01_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rg01-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:292</sup> For ideological reasons, then, orthodox Marxism has mostly been opposed to any concern with natural resource scarcity ever since Marx's own day.<sup id="cite_ref-rg01_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rg01-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:57–65</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-jm01_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jm01-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:218–225</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-pb01_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pb01-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:5f</sup> However, the march of history has been hard on this orthodoxy: By 1991, German sociologist <a href="/wiki/Reiner_Grundmann" title="Reiner Grundmann">Reiner Grundmann</a> was able to make the sweeping observation that "Orthodox Marxism has vanished from the scene, <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">leftism</a> has turned green, and Marxists have become ecologists."<sup id="cite_ref-rg01_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rg01-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:52</sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a>, on the other hand, the classical preoccupation with society's long term growth and development was abandoned altogether; instead, economic analysis came to focus on the study of the relationship between given ends and given scarce means, forming the concept of <a href="/wiki/General_equilibrium_theory" title="General equilibrium theory">general equilibrium theory</a> within an essentially static framework. Hence, neoclassical economics achieved greater generality, but only by asking easier questions; and any concern with natural resource scarcity was neglected.<sup id="cite_ref-mb02_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb02-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:295–299</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-rc01_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rc01-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:55–57</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Taken together, it has been argued that "... if Judeo-Christian <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheism</a> took nature out of religion, Anglo-American economists (after about 1880) took nature out of economics."<sup id="cite_ref-ap01_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:88</sup> In effect, modern <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economics</a> — and <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a> in particular — has been described as "ecologically illiterate."<sup id="cite_ref-tj01_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tj01-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:123</sup> Almost one century later, nature was reintegrated into economics by Herman Daly in his concept of a steady-state economy (<a href="#Herman_Daly.27s_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy">see below</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Maynard_Keynes.27s_concept_of_reaching_saturation">John Maynard Keynes's concept of reaching saturation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: John Maynard Keynes's concept of reaching saturation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">Macroeconomics</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Keynes_1933.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Keynes_1933.jpg/170px-Keynes_1933.jpg" width="170" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Keynes_1933.jpg/255px-Keynes_1933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Keynes_1933.jpg 2x" data-file-width="289" data-file-height="300" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Keynes_1933.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Keynes predicted that capital accumulation would soon reach saturation and bring about a quasi-stationary community</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift#Kuhnian_paradigm_shifts" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm founder</a> of modern <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomics</a>, and is widely considered today to be the most influential economist of the 20th century. Keynes rejected the basic tenet of classical economics that <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics#Keynes_and_the_classics" title="Keynesian economics">free markets would lead to full employment by themselves</a>. Consequently, he recommended government intervention to stimulate <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a> in the economy, a macroeconomic policy now known as <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>. Keynes also believed that capital accumulation would reach saturation at some point in the future.</p>
<p>In his essay from 1930 on <i>The Economic Possibilities of Our Grandchildren</i>, Keynes ventured to look one hundred years ahead into the future and predict the standard of living in the 21st century. Writing at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, Keynes rejected the prevailing "bad attack of economic pessimism" of his own time and foresaw that by 2030, the grandchildren of his generation would live in a state of abundance, where saturation would have been reached. People would find themselves liberated from such economic activities as saving and capital accumulation, and be able to get rid of 'pseudo-moral principles' — avarice, exaction of interest, love of money — that had characterized <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalistic societies</a> so far. Instead, people would devote themselves to the true art of life, to live "wisely and agreeably and well." Mankind would finally have solved "the economic problem," that is, the struggle for existence.<sup id="cite_ref-jmk01_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jmk01-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lp01_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lp01-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2, 11</sup></p>
<p>The similarity between John Stuart Mill's concept of the stationary state (<a href="#John_Stuart_Mill.27s_concept_of_the_stationary_state">see above</a>) and Keynes's predictions in this essay has been noted.<sup id="cite_ref-lp01_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lp01-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:15</sup> It has been argued that although Keynes was right about future growth rates, he underestimated the inequalities prevailing today, both within and across countries. He was also wrong in predicting that greater wealth would induce more leisure spent; in fact, the reverse trend seems to be true.<sup id="cite_ref-lp01_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lp01-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3–6</sup></p>
<p>In his <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a></i>, Keynes looked only one generation ahead into the future and predicted that state intervention balancing aggregate demand would by then have caused capital accumulation to reach the point of saturation. The marginal efficiency of capital as well as the rate of interest would both be brought down to zero, and — if population was not increasing rapidly — society would finally "... attain the conditions of a quasi-stationary community where change and progress would result only from changes in technique, taste, population and institutions ..."<sup id="cite_ref-jmk02_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jmk02-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:138f</sup> Keynes believed this development would bring about the disappearance of the rentier class, something he welcomed: Keynes argued that rentiers incurred no sacrifice for their earnings, and their savings did not lead to productive investments unless aggregate demand in the economy was sufficiently high. "I see, therefore, the rentier aspect of capitalism as a transitional phase which will disappear when it has done its work."<sup id="cite_ref-jmk02_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jmk02-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:237</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_economics">Ecological economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecological economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> recognized the connection between physical laws and economic activity and wrote about it in 1971 in his <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Magnum_opus_on_The_Entropy_Law_and_the_Economic_Process" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">The Entropy Law and the Economic Process</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ngr01_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ngr01-37">[37]</a></sup> His premise was that the second law of thermodynamics, the entropy law, determines what is possible in the economy. Georgescu-Roegen explained that useful, low-entropy energy and materials are dissipated in transformations that occur in economic processes, and they return to the environment as high-entropy wastes. The economy, then, functions as conduit for converting natural resources into goods, services, human satisfaction, and waste products. Increasing entropy in the economy places profound limits on the scale it can achieve and maintain.</p>
<p>Around the same time that Georgescu-Roegen published his magnum opus, many other economists, most notably <a href="/wiki/E.F._Schumacher" class="mw-redirect" title="E.F. Schumacher">E.F. Schumacher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Boulding" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenneth Boulding">Kenneth Boulding</a>, were writing about the environmental effects of economic growth and suggesting alternative models to the neoclassical growth paradigm. Schumacher proposed <i>Buddhist Economics</i> in an essay of the same name in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a></i>. Schumacher's economic model is grounded in sufficiency of consumption, opportunities for people to participate in useful and fulfilling work, and vibrant community life marked by peace and cooperative endeavors.<sup id="cite_ref-es01_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-es01-38">[38]</a></sup> Boulding used the spaceship as a metaphor for the planet in his prominent essay, <i>The Economics of the Coming Spaceship Earth</i>. He recognized the material and energy constraints of the economy and proposed a shift from the cowboy economy to the spaceman economy. In the cowboy economy, success is gauged by the quantity and speed of production and consumption. In the spaceman economy, by contrast, "what we are primarily concerned with is stock maintenance, and any technological change which results in the maintenance of a given total stock with a lessened throughput (that is, less production and consumption) is clearly a gain."<sup id="cite_ref-kb01_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kb01-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, a student of Georgescu-Roegen, built upon his mentor's work and combined limits-to-growth arguments, theories of welfare economics, ecological principles, and the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> into a model he called steady-state economics (<a href="#Herman_Daly.27s_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy">see below</a>). He later joined forces with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a>, AnnMari Jansson, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Martinez_Alier" title="Joan Martinez Alier">Joan Martinez Alier</a>, and others to develop the field of ecological economics.<sup id="cite_ref-ir01_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ir01-40">[40]</a></sup> In 1990, these prominent professors established the <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Ecological_Economics" title="International Society for Ecological Economics">International Society for Ecological Economics</a>. The three founding positions of the society and the field of ecological economics are: (1) The human economy is embedded in nature, and economic processes are actually biological, physical, and chemical processes and transformations. (2) Ecological economics is meeting place for researchers committed to environmental issues. (3) Ecological economics requires trans-disciplinary work to describe economic processes in relation to physical reality.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a> has become the field of study most closely linked with the concept of a steady-state economy. Ecological economists have developed a robust body of theory and evidence on the biophysical limits of economic growth and the requirements of a sustainable economy.<sup id="cite_ref-hd04_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd04-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mc01_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc01-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Herman_Daly.27s_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy">Herman Daly's concept of a steady-state economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Herman Daly's concept of a steady-state economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> has been the world's leading proponent of a steady-state economy.<sup id="cite_ref-ma01_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ma01-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:81f</sup> Throughout his career, Daly has published several books and articles on the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hd03_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd03-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:117–124</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-hd05_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd05-44">[44]</a></sup> He has also founded a center for the advancement of the steady-state economy.<sup id="cite_ref-casse_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-casse-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to two independent comparative studies of American Daly's steady-state economics versus the later, competing school of <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a> from continental Europe, no differences of analytical substance exist between the two schools; only, Daly's <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucratic</a> — or even <a href="/wiki/Technocracy" title="Technocracy">technocratic</a> — top-down management of the economy fares badly with the more radical <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> appeal of degrowth, as championed by French political scientist <a href="/wiki/Serge_Latouche" title="Serge Latouche">Serge Latouche</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ck01_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ck01-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:549</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-ap01_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:146–148</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/240px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" width="240" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/360px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/480px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="421" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Natural resources flow through the economy and end up as waste and pollution</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The premise underlying Daly's concept of a steady-state economy is that the economy is an open subsystem of a finite and non-growing ecosystem (Earth's natural environment). The economy is maintained by importing low-entropy matter-energy (resources) from nature; these resources are put through the economy, being transformed and manufactured into goods along the way; eventually, the throughput of matter-energy is exported to the environment as high-entropy waste and pollution. <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">Recycling</a> of material resources is possible, but only by using up some energy resources as well as an additional amount of other material resources; and energy resources, in turn, cannot be recycled at all, but are dissipated as <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a>. Out of necessity, then, any subsystem of a fixed nongrowing system must itself at some point also become nongrowing.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xiii</sup></p>
<p>Daly argues that nature has provided basically two sources of wealth at man's disposal, namely a stock of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Mineral_resource_classification" title="Mineral resource classification">mineral resources</a> and a flow of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>. An 'asymmetry' between these two sources of wealth exist in that we may — within some practical limits — extract the mineral stock at a rate of our own choosing (that is, rapidly), whereas the flow of solar energy reaches Earth at a rate beyond human control. Since the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> will continue to shine on Earth at a fixed rate for billions of years to come, it is the terrestrial mineral stock — and not the Sun — that constitutes the crucial scarcity factor regarding man's economic future.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:21f</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cottonopolis1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cottonopolis1.jpg/240px-Cottonopolis1.jpg" width="240" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cottonopolis1.jpg/360px-Cottonopolis1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cottonopolis1.jpg/480px-Cottonopolis1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="746" data-file-height="506" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cottonopolis1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Unintentionally, the Industrial Revolution has thrown modern man out of equilibrium with the rest of the biosphere</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Daly points out that today's global ecological problems are rooted in man's historical record: Until the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> that took place in Britain in the second half of the 18th century, man lived within the limits imposed by what Daly terms a 'solar-income budget': The <a href="/wiki/Palaeolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeolithic">Palaeolithic</a> tribes of hunter-gatherers and the later agricultural societies of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and onwards subsisted primarily — though not exclusively — on Earth's <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, powered by an ample supply of renewable energy, received from the Sun. The Industrial Revolution changed this situation completely, as man began extracting the terrestrial mineral stock at a rapidly increasing rate. The original solar-income budget was thereby broken and supplemented by the new, but much scarcer source of wealth. Mankind still lives in the after-effect of this revolution.</p>
<p>Daly cautions that more than two hundred years of worldwide industrialisation is now confronting mankind with a range of problems pertaining to the future existence and survival of our species:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">The entire evolution of the biosphere has occurred around a fixed point — the constant solar-energy budget. Modern man is the only species to have broken the solar-income budget constraint, and this has thrown him out of equilibrium with the rest of the biosphere. Natural cycles have become overloaded, and new materials have been produced for which no natural cycles exist. Not only is geological capital being depleted, but the basic life-support services of nature are impaired in their functioning by too large a throughput from the human sector.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:23</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Following the work of <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a>, Daly argues that the laws of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> restrict all <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">human technologies</a> and apply to all economic systems:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">Entropy</a> is the basic physical coordinate of scarcity. Were it not for entropy, we could burn the same gallon of gasoline over and over, and our capital stock would never wear out. Technology is unable to rise above the basic laws of physics, so there is no question of ever 'inventing' a way to recycle energy.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:36</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>In Daly's view, mainstream economists tend to regard natural resource scarcity as only a relative phenomenon, while human needs and wants are granted absolute status: It is believed that the <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)#Mechanisms_of_markets" title="Market (economics)">price mechanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technological development</a> (however defined) is capable of overcoming any scarcity ever to be faced on Earth; it is also believed that all human wants could and should be treated alike as absolutes, from the most basic necessities of life to the extravagant and insatiable craving for luxuries. Daly terms this belief 'growthmania', which he finds pervasive in modern society. In opposition to the dogma of growthmania, Daly submits that "... there <i>is</i> such a thing as absolute scarcity, and there <i>is</i> such a thing as purely relative and trivial wants".<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41</sup> Once it is recognised that scarcity is imposed by nature in an absolute form by the laws of thermodynamics and the finitude of Earth; and that some human wants are only relative and not worthy of satisfying; then we are all well on the way to the paradigm of a steady-state economy, Daly concludes.</p>
<p><span id="Daly.27s_institutions"></span></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg/240px-Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg" width="240" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg/360px-Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg/480px-Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingham_Canyon_April_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The inevitable exhaustion of mined resources could be postponed by imposing permanent quantitative restrictions on the economy</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Consequently, Daly recommends that a system of permanent government restrictions on the economy is established as soon as possible, a steady-state economy. Whereas the <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economists</a> believed that the final stationary state would settle by itself as the rate of profit fell and capital accumulation came to an end (<a href="#The_stationary_state_in_classical_economics">see above</a>), Daly wants to create the steady-state politically by establishing three institutions of the state as a superstructure on top of the present market economy:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first institution is to correct inequality to some extent by putting minimum and maximum limits on incomes, maximum limits on wealth, and then redistribute accordingly.</li>
<li>The second institution is to stabilise the population by issuing transferable reproduction licenses to all fertile women at a level corresponding with the general replacement fertility in society.</li>
<li>The third institution is to stabilise the level of capital by issuing and selling depletion quotas that impose quantitative restrictions on the flow of resources through the economy. Quotas effectively minimise the throughput of resources necessary to maintain any given level of capital (as opposed to taxes, that merely alter the prevailing price structure).</li>
</ul>
<p>The purpose of these three institutions is to stop and prevent further growth by combining what Daly calls "a nice reconciliation of efficiency and equity" and providing "the ecologically necessary macrocontrol of growth with the least sacrifice in terms of microlevel freedom and variability."<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup></p>
<p>Among the generation of his teachers, Daly ranks <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_E._Boulding" title="Kenneth E. Boulding">Kenneth E. Boulding</a> as the two economists he has learned the most from.<sup id="cite_ref-hd02_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd02-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:xvi</sup> However, both Georgescu-Roegen and Boulding have assessed that a steady-state economy may serve only as a temporary societal arrangement for mankind when facing the long-term issue of global mineral resource exhaustion: Even with a constant stock of people and capital, and a minimised (yet constant) flow of resources put through the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a>, the Earth's mineral stock will still be exhausted, although at a slower rate than is presently the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-ngr02_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ngr02-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:366–369</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-kb02_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kb02-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:165–167</sup></p>
<p><span id="Daly.27s_credo"></span> Responding specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Criticising_Daly.27s_steady-state_economics" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">criticism levelled at him by Georgescu-Roegen</a>, Daly concedes that a steady-state economy will serve only to postpone, and not to prevent, the inevitable mineral resource exhaustion: "A steady-state economy cannot last forever, but neither can a growing economy, nor a declining economy".<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:369</sup> A frank and committed <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a>, Daly further argues that...</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">... the steady-state economy is based on the assumption that <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">creation</a> will have an end — that it is finite temporally as well as spatially. ... Only <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> can raise any part of his creation out of time and into <a href="/wiki/Eternity" title="Eternity">eternity</a>. As mere stewards of creation, all we can do is to avoid wasting the limited capacity of creation to support present and future life.<sup id="cite_ref-hd01_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:370</sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Later, other economists in the field have agreed that not even a steady-state economy can last forever on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-mf01_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mf01-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:105–107</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-mb03_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mb03-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:270</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-av01_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-av01-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:37</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_and_rebuttals_thereof">Criticisms and rebuttals thereof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steady-state_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Criticisms and rebuttals thereof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability#Decoupling_environmental_degradation_and_economic_growth" title="Sustainability">Sustainability §&#160;Decoupling environmental degradation and economic growth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jevons_paradox" title="Jevons paradox">Jevons paradox</a></div>
<p>Critics of the idea of limits to growth present two main arguments:</p>
<ol>
<li>Technological progress (however defined) can overcome the limits to growth.</li>
<li>The economy can be de-materialized so that it grows without using more and more resources.</li>
</ol>
<p>These can be called the technological optimist and <a href="/wiki/Eco-economic_decoupling" title="Eco-economic decoupling">decoupling</a> arguments respectively.</p>
<p>Decoupling means achieving higher levels of economic output with lower levels of material and energy input.<sup id="cite_ref-ew01_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew01-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ew02_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ew02-51">[51]</a></sup> Proponents of decoupling cite transition to an information economy as proof of decoupling. Evidence shows that economies have achieved some success at relative decoupling. As an example, the amount of carbon dioxide emitted per dollar of economic production has decreased over time. But those gains have come amidst the background condition of increasing GDP. Even with decreases in the <a href="/wiki/Resource_intensity" title="Resource intensity">resource intensity</a> of GDP, economies are still using more resources. Carbon dioxide emissions from fossil fuels have increased by 80% since 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-tj01_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tj01-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological economists also observe that an economy is structured like an ecosystem — it has a trophic structure that controls flows of energy and materials. In nature, the producers are plants, which literally produce their own food in the process of photosynthesis. Herbivores consume plants, and carnivores consume herbivores. Omnivores may eat plants or animals, and some species function as service providers, such as scavengers and decomposers. The human economy follows the same natural laws. The producers are the agricultural and extractive sectors, such as logging, mining, and fishing. Surplus in these sectors allows for the division of labor, economic growth, and the flow of resources to other economic sectors. Analogous to herbivores, some economic sectors, such as manufacturing, consume the raw materials of the producers. Higher level manufacturers are analogous to carnivores. The economy also features service providers, such as chefs, janitors, bankers, and purveyors of information. The key point is that the economy tends to grow as an integrated whole. More manufacturing and more services requires more agricultural and extractive surplus. The trophic structure of the economy puts limits on how much of an economy's resources can be dedicated to creating and distributing information.<sup id="cite_ref-bc01_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bc01-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both technological optimists and proponents of decoupling cite efficiency of resource use as a way to mitigate the problems associated with economic growth. But history has shown that when technological progress increases the efficiency with which a resource is used, the rate of consumption of that resource actually tends to rise. This phenomenon is called the <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effect (conservation)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jevons_paradox" title="Jevons paradox">Jevons paradox</a>. A recent extensive historical analysis of technological efficiency improvements has conclusively shown that energy and materials use efficiency improvements were almost always outpaced by economic growth, resulting in a net increase in resource use and associated pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-mh01_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mh01-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:chap. 5</sup> <sup id="cite_ref-cc01_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc01-52">[52]</a></sup> Furthermore, there are inherent thermodynamic (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>) and practical limits to all efficiency improvements. For example, there are certain minimum unavoidable material requirements for growing food, and there are limits to making automobiles, houses, furniture, and other products lighter and thinner without the risk of losing their necessary functions.<sup id="cite_ref-mh01_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mh01-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:111</sup></p>
<p>Since it is both theoretically and practically impossible to increase resource use efficiencies indefinitely, it is equally impossible to have continued and infinite economic growth without a concomitant increase in resource depletion and environmental pollution, i.e., economic growth and resource depletion can be decoupled to some degree over the short run but not the long run. Consequently, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> and others in the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a> community have advocated that long-term sustainability require the transition to a steady-state economy in which total GDP remains more or less constant (<a href="#Herman_Daly.27s_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy">see above</a>).</p>
<p>Some critics of zero growth claim that it does not go far enough. They argue that <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a> and fundamental changes to our economic system are needed to attain sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-tt01_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tt01-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
