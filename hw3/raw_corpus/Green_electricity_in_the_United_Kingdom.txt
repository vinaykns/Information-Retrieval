			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Green electricity in the United Kingdom</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg/200px-Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg/300px-Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg/400px-Greenpark_wind_turbine_arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1712" data-file-height="2288" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A wind turbine at <a href="/wiki/Green_Park,_Berkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Park, Berkshire">Greenpark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, generating green electricity for approx 1000 homes.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are a number of suppliers offering 'green' electricity in the United Kingdom. In theory these types of tariffs help to lower carbon emissions by increasing consumer demand for green electricity and encouraging more renewables to be built. Since OFGEM's 2014 regulations there are now set criteria defining what can be classified as a green source product.<sup id="cite_ref-ofgem.gov.uk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ofgem.gov.uk-1">[1]</a></sup> As well as holding sufficient <a href="/wiki/Guarantee_of_origin" title="Guarantee of origin">guarantee of origin certificates</a> to cover the electricity sold to consumers, suppliers are also required to show <a href="/wiki/Additionality" title="Additionality">additionality</a> by contributing to wider environmental and low carbon funds.</p>
<p>Care needs to be taken in selecting a <a href="/wiki/Green_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Green energy">green energy</a> supplier. A <a href="/wiki/National_Consumer_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="National Consumer Council">National Consumer Council</a> report in December 2006 concluded that many green tariffs are not delivering the environmental benefits they claim to, and that consumers may not be making the positive contribution they think they are.<sup id="cite_ref-NCC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCC-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shades_of_green">Shades of green</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shades of green">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewables_Obligation">Renewables Obligation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Renewables Obligation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, suppliers are legally obliged to purchase a proportion of their electricity from renewable sources under the <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Obligation">Renewables Obligation</a> and there is a danger that energy suppliers may sell such green electricity under a premium 'green energy' tariff, rather than sourcing additional green electricity supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition to selling the green electricity to the consumer, it is also possible for the supplier to sell the associated <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Obligation_Certificate" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Obligation Certificate">Renewables Obligation Certificates</a> to another supplier who has failed to meet their quota, rather than 'retiring' the certificate from the marketplace.<sup id="cite_ref-FOEjuly05_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOEjuly05-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On several occasions in May 2016, Britain burned no coal for electricity for the first time since 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_Change_Levy">Climate Change Levy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate Change Levy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-domestic consumers can avoid paying the <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Levy" title="Climate Change Levy">Climate Change Levy</a> by acquiring Levy Exemption Certificates from renewable energy suppliers. Since these are not required by domestic consumers, it is possible for the supplier to sell the certificates to the non-domestic sector, as well as selling the renewables obligation certificate and the electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOEjuly05_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOEjuly05-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_offsetting">Carbon offsetting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carbon offsetting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offset</a></div>
<p>Prior to the OFGEM green supply regulations, some green energy tariffs involved <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offsetting</a>, rather than purchasing or investing in renewable energy,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> a practice that does not meet with universal approval. <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a>, an English environmentalist and writer, has compared carbon offsets to the practice of purchasing <a href="/wiki/Indulgence" title="Indulgence">indulgences</a> during the Middle Ages, whereby people believed they could purchase forgiveness for their sins (instead of actually repenting and not sinning anymore). Monbiot also says that carbon offsets are an excuse for business as usual with regard to pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reporting_Requirements">Reporting Requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reporting Requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2008, Defra announced that with effect from the current financial year (2008/9), Green Electricity is to be treated the same as average grid electricity for CO<sub>2</sub> reporting purposes. Previously, it was treated as if there were no CO<sub>2</sub> emissions associated with it.</p>
<p>Explaining the change, the Minister responsible (Hilary Benn) said<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> "I want to make sure that the green tariff market, which has grown rapidly over recent years, is clear for consumers and businesses about the precise benefit their tariff brings. Many energy suppliers offer green tariffs to businesses and domestic customers who want to make a contribution to environmental projects or help tackle climate change, but these differ in what they deliver." He added "It is increasingly difficult to demonstrate that buying a renewable electricity tariff is offering additional carbon emissions reductions compared with what suppliers are required to source to meet the Renewables Obligation. I have therefore decided that we will change the voluntary corporate reporting guidelines to bring them into line with current best practice and provide coherent carbon accounting. This will mean that for the reporting year 2008-9, best practice is expected to be for businesses to use a grid average rate - average rate of carbon emissions associated with electricity transmitted on the national grid - unless their supplier can prove the carbon benefits are additional." This does not affect green electricity generated on site (e.g. from photovoltaics or a micro-wind turbine), which can continue to be reported as producing zero emissions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification_schemes">Certification schemes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Certification schemes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1999 the <a href="/wiki/Future_Energy" title="Future Energy">Future Energy</a> national certification scheme for green electricity was launched, however it was closed in 2002, apparently because too few suppliers were prepared to accept the proposed revised requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the absence of a replacement national scheme in the UK, the international <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Green_Energy_Standard" title="Eugene Green Energy Standard">Eugene Network</a>, whose main purpose was to give accreditation to national green energy schemes, supported <a href="/wiki/Ecotricity" title="Ecotricity">Ecotricity</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and indicates that <a href="/wiki/Good_Energy" title="Good Energy">Good Energy</a> would be ready for accreditation should a <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Green_Energy_Standard" title="Eugene Green Energy Standard">Eugene Standard</a> compliant scheme be introduced in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Eugene standard was discontinued in February 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> leaving no internationally recognised means of comparing green electricity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ofgem.27s_2007-8_Proposals">Ofgem's 2007-8 Proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ofgem's 2007-8 Proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an attempt to end some of the premium priced offerings by suppliers which were doing no more than repackaging electricity that they had to buy under the Renewables Obligation, the UK Government's Office of Gas and Electricity Markets <a href="/wiki/Ofgem" class="mw-redirect" title="Ofgem">Ofgem</a> proposed a voluntary scheme for independently verified energy ratings<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> for electricity suppliers. Initially this was intended to include a detailed analysis of the carbon content of the supply, but some critics felt that this lacked sufficient emphasis on additionality, and the actual energy used by consumers from the grid was always a mix of brown and green sources.</p>
<p>After running a series of workshops in early 2008, their proposals turned away from a focus on the source of electricity (and its associated <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions), to look instead at whether an environmental benefit is demonstrated. Essentially, Ofgem suggest that this can only be done if the supplier invests part of payment received into one of the following activities:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>installation of energy efficiency technologies (outside of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Emission_Reduction_Target" title="Carbon Emission Reduction Target">Carbon Emission Reduction Target</a> - CERT programme);</li>
<li>other consumer behaviour measures, such as active demand management or voluntary <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">smart metering</a>;</li>
<li>support for renewable heat installations;</li>
<li>additional investment in smaller-scale (e.g. community based) renewable electricity projects - a limit of 1MW has been suggested for wind farms - or in other low carbon sources including <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">CHP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a>;</li>
<li>retirement of EU allowances under the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Emission_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emission Trading Scheme">EU Emissions Trading Scheme</a> (EU ETS);</li>
<li>purchase of carbon offsets in line with Defra’s Quality Assurance Scheme for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">Carbon Offsetting</a>, typically offsets from developing countries under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a>;</li>
<li>research and development into emerging renewable technologies; or</li>
<li>contribution to an environmental charity.</li>
</ul>
<p>The initial draft of the guidelines would have excluded most of the products currently available on the UK market, including offerings from renewable energy specialists <a href="/wiki/Good_Energy" title="Good Energy">Good Energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecotricity" title="Ecotricity">Ecotricity</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Green_Energy_Supply_Certification_Scheme">The Green Energy Supply Certification Scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Green Energy Supply Certification Scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After consultation, Ofgem introduced modified Guidelines in February 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These placed a greater emphasis on the matching principle, requiring that all electricity sold under the Scheme had to be matched by supplies from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable sources</a> (and excluding other low-carbon sources such as CHP or nuclear). The additionality criteria were simplified slightly to comprise:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>carbon offsets accredited either under the UK Government's Quality Assurance Scheme (QAS) or under the <a href="/wiki/CDM_Gold_Standard" title="CDM Gold Standard">Gold Standard</a></li>
<li>green energy schemes, mainly those that donate money to charities or to trust funds established to invest in new renewable projects – generally at a community level, and required to be independent of the suppliers</li>
<li>energy efficiency schemes, making savings above those from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Emission_Reduction_Target" title="Carbon Emission Reduction Target">CERT</a> including savings in customers' premises through lower energy use</li>
<li>unspecified other measures, producing equivalent carbon savings</li>
</ul>
<p>Certified tariffs are subject to an annual audit, to ensure that the matching and additionality criteria have been met. Consumers must also be given information on the suppliers overall fuel mix at the point of sale. Although aimed mainly at domestic consumers, the scheme can also certify tariffs aimed at small businesses with an annual energy consumption below 50,000kWh.</p>
<p>The Scheme is controlled by an independent Panel chaired by <a href="/wiki/Solitaire_Townsend" title="Solitaire Townsend">Solitaire Townsend</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Foundation" title="National Energy Foundation">National Energy Foundation</a> were appointed as the secretariat.</p>
<p>The Scheme was launched in February 2010, with the six big UK energy suppliers as members as well as <a href="/wiki/Good_Energy" title="Good Energy">Good Energy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ecotricity" title="Ecotricity">Ecotricity</a> elected not to join the scheme citing concerns about the additionality rules.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> All suppliers offer domestic tariffs, but only some have offered small business tariffs under the scheme.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_OFGEM_regulation">Current OFGEM regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current OFGEM regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In response to concerns about the lack of regulation in the market for green electricity tariffs and in order to protect consumers, OFGEM has consulted and enacted changes to the green tariff market in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-ofgem.gov.uk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ofgem.gov.uk-1">[1]</a></sup> Whereas previously the green tariff products were not subject to specific regulations, all retailers must now prove the green credentials of their products to OFGEM. The regulations have three components:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><b>Electricity source</b> - retailers need to hold sufficient Renewable Energy Guarantee of Origin Certificates to match the quantity of electricity sold. Retailers are required to submit proof of this to the regulator.</li>
<li><b>Additionality</b> - retailers also need to invest additional funds into other environmental projects.</li>
<li><b>Transparency</b> - retailers are required to inform consumers of how their tariffs benefit the environment.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_controversy">Areas of controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Areas of controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been several areas of controversy regarding green electricity sales in the UK. These have been related to both the concept as a whole, which has been subject to criticism, but have usually manifested as disputes between the two leading green electricity companies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_of_Renewable_Obligation_Certificates_.28ROCs.29">Retirement of Renewable Obligation Certificates (ROCs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Retirement of Renewable Obligation Certificates (ROCs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009 the founder of Ecotricity, Dale Vince, criticised Good Energy for their failure to retire a sufficient quantity of ROCs.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Good Energy had previously promised to retire 5% of its ROCs, but Vince claimed they had consistently retired fewer ROCs than promised. He backed this up with evidence from OFGEM. It appears that after this dispute Good Energy did increase the number of ROCs it retired, but now seems to have ceased to do this entirely.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising_Standards_Authority_Case">Advertising Standards Authority Case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_electricity_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Advertising Standards Authority Case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016 Ecotricity claimed in an advertisement that it was the greenest energy supplier. This claim was disputed by Tesla Motors and subsequently thrown out.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The judgement faced criticism from Good Energy, with its managing director David Brooks saying “The ASA appears to have based its decision purely on data provided by Ecotricity without considering whether that data represents an objective view. Obviously, the criteria Ecotricity has chosen to measure itself against other companies has been selected to suit Ecotricity. We don’t think the ASA has considered what it calls “suitable comparative data” before reaching its conclusions." <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
