			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Conceptual framework</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For the concept in aesthetics and art criticism, see <a href="/wiki/The_Conceptual_Framework" title="The Conceptual Framework">The Conceptual Framework</a>. For the concept in computing, see <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_schema" title="Conceptual schema">Conceptual schema</a>. For the concept in philosophy, see <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">Paradigm</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>conceptual framework</b> is an analytical tool with several variations and contexts. It is used to make conceptual distinctions and organize ideas. Strong conceptual frameworks capture something real and do this in a way that is easy to remember and apply.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Berlin" title="Isaiah Berlin">Isaiah Berlin</a> used the metaphor of a "fox" and a "hedgehog" to make conceptual distinctions in how important philosophers and authors view the world.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Berlin describes hedgehogs as those who use a single idea or organizing principle to view the world (such as <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">Fyodor Dostoyevsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>). Foxes, on the other hand, incorporate a type of pluralism and view the world through multiple, sometimes conflicting, lenses (examples include <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">Molière</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">Honoré de Balzac</a>).</p>
<p>Economists use the conceptual framework of "<a href="/wiki/Supply_(economics)" title="Supply (economics)">supply</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Demand_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand (economics)">demand</a>" to distinguish between the behavior and incentive systems of firms and consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Like many conceptual frameworks, supply and demand can be presented through visual or graphical representations (see <a href="/wiki/Demand_curve" title="Demand curve">demand curve</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conceptual_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of the term <i>conceptual framework</i> crosses both scale (large and small theories)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and contexts (social science,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> marketing,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> applied science,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> art<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> etc.). Its explicit definition and application can therefore vary.</p>
<p>Conceptual frameworks are particularly useful as organizing devices in empirical research. One set of scholars has applied the notion of conceptual framework to <a href="/wiki/Deductive" class="mw-redirect" title="Deductive">deductive</a>, empirical research at the micro- or individual study level.<sup id="cite_ref-Shields_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-books.google.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They employ <a href="/wiki/American_football_plays" title="American football plays">American football plays</a> as a useful metaphor to clarify the meaning of <i>conceptual framework</i> (used in the context of a deductive empirical study).</p>
<p>Likewise, conceptual frameworks are abstract representations, connected to the research project's goal that direct the collection and analysis of data (on the plane of observation – the ground). Critically, a football play is a "plan of action" tied to a particular, timely, purpose, usually summarized as long or short yardage.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Shields and Rangarajan argue that it is this tie to "purpose" that make <a href="/wiki/American_football_plays" title="American football plays">American football plays</a> such a good metaphor. They define a conceptual framework as "the way ideas are organized to achieve a research project's purpose".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Like football plays conceptual frameworks are connected to a research purpose. <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">Explanation</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> is the most common type of research purpose employed in empirical research. The formal <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> is the framework associated with <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">explanation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Explanatory research usually focuses on "why" or "what caused" a phenomenon to occur. Formal hypotheses posit possible explanations (answers to the why question) that are tested by collecting data and assessing the evidence (usually quantitative using statistical tests). For example, Kai Huang<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> wanted to determine what factors contributed to residential fires in U.S. cities. Three factors were posited to influence residential fires. These factors (environment, population and building characteristics) became the hypotheses or conceptual framework he used to achieve his purpose – explain factors that influenced home fires in U.S. cities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conceptual_framework&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several types of conceptual frameworks have been identified,<sup id="cite_ref-Shields_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-books.google.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and line up with a research purpose in the following ways:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Working_hypothesis" title="Working hypothesis">Working hypothesis</a> – <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">Exploration</a> or <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_research" title="Exploratory research">Exploratory research</a></li>
<li>Descriptive categories – <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">Description</a> or <a href="/wiki/Descriptive_research" title="Descriptive research">Descriptive research</a></li>
<li>Practical ideal type – <a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">Analysis (Gauging)</a></li>
<li>Models of <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a> – <a href="/wiki/Decision_making" class="mw-redirect" title="Decision making">Decision making</a></li>
<li>Formal hypothesis – <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">Explanation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">Prediction</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Note that Shields and Rangarajan (2013) do not claim that the above are the only framework-purpose pairing. Nor do they claim the system is applicable to <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive</a> forms of empirical research. Rather, the conceptual framework-research purpose pairings they propose are useful and provide new scholars a point of departure to develop their own <a href="/wiki/Research_design" title="Research design">research design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.com_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Frameworks have also been used to explain <a href="/wiki/Conflict_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Conflict theory">conflict theory</a> and the balance necessary to reach what amounts to resolution. Within these conflict frameworks, visible and invisible variables function under concepts of relevance. Boundaries form and within these boundaries, tensions regarding laws and chaos (or freedom) are mitigated. These frameworks often function like cells, with sub-frameworks, stasis, evolution and revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anomalistics" title="Anomalistics">Anomalies</a> may exist without adequate "lenses" or "filters" to see them and may become visible only when the tools exist to define them.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
