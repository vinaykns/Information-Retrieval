			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Electric boat</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">This article is about electrically powered boats in general. For the company called "Electric Boat", see <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">General Dynamics Electric Boat</a>.</div>

</div>
</div>
<p>While a significant majority of <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">water vessels</a> are powered by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sail</a> power and gasoline engines also remaining popular, boats powered by <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> have been used for over 120 years. <b>Electric boats</b> were very popular from the 1880s<sup id="cite_ref-thames_map_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thames_map-1">[1]</a></sup> until the 1920s, when the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> took dominance. Since the <a href="/wiki/Energy_crisis" title="Energy crisis">energy crises</a> of the 1970s, interest in this quiet and potentially <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable</a> marine energy source has been increasing steadily again, especially as <a href="/wiki/Solar_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a> became available, for the first time making possible <a href="/wiki/Motorboat" title="Motorboat">motorboats</a> with an infinite range like <a href="/wiki/Sailboats" class="mw-redirect" title="Sailboats">sailboats</a>. The first practical <b>solar boat</b> was probably constructed in 1975 in England.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first electric sailboat which mades the round-the-world tour, including the Panama Canal, with only green technologies is <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ecosailingproject.com/en/">EcoSailingProject</a> thanks to <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.naviwatt.fr/en/">Naviwatt</a></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early">Early</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:148px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg/146px-Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg" width="146" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg/220px-Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg/293px-Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="819" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moritz_Hermann_von_Jacobi_1856.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Moritz_von_Jacobi" title="Moritz von Jacobi">Moritz von Jacobi</a>, inventor of an early electric boat.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An early electric boat was developed by the German inventor <a href="/wiki/Moritz_von_Jacobi" title="Moritz von Jacobi">Moritz von Jacobi</a> in 1839 in <a href="/wiki/St_Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petersburg">St Petersburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. It was a 24-foot (7.3&#160;m) boat which carried 14 passengers at 3&#160;mph. It was successfully demonstrated to Emperor <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_I_of_Russia" title="Nicholas I of Russia">Nicholas I of Russia</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Neva_River" title="Neva River">Neva River</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_Age">Golden Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Golden Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It took more than 30 years of battery and motor development before the electric boat became a practical proposition. This method of propulsion enjoyed something of a golden age from about 1880 to 1920, when gasoline-powered <a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard motors</a> became the dominant method.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gustave_Trouv%C3%A9" title="Gustave Trouvé">Gustave Trouvé</a>, French electrical engineer, patented a small electric motor in 1880. He initially suggested that the motor could power a set of paddle wheels to propel boats on the water, and later argued for the use of a <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a>, instead.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Immisch_motor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Immisch_motor.jpg/250px-Immisch_motor.jpg" width="250" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Immisch_motor.jpg/375px-Immisch_motor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Immisch_motor.jpg/500px-Immisch_motor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="270" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Immisch_motor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Electric motor designed by <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Immisch" title="Moritz Immisch">Immisch &amp; Co.</a>, who established the first fleet of electric launches in London.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An Austrian emigre to Britain, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Reckenzaun" title="Anthony Reckenzaun">Anthony Reckenzaun</a>, was instrumental in the development of the first practical electric boats. While working as an <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> for the Electrical Power Storage Company, he undertook much original and pioneering work on various forms of electric traction. In 1882 he designed the first significant electric launch driven by <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">storage batteries</a>, and named the boat <i>Electricity</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The boat had a steel hull and was over 7 metres long. The batteries and electric equipment were concealed from view underneath the seating area, increasing the passenger accommodation. The boats were used for leisure excursions up and down the River Thames and provided a very smooth, clean and quiet trip. The boat could run for six hours and operate at an average speed of 8 miles per hour.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Moritz_Immisch" title="Moritz Immisch">Moritz Immisch</a> established his company in 1882 in partnership with <a href="/wiki/William_Keppel,_7th_Earl_of_Albemarle" title="William Keppel, 7th Earl of Albemarle">William Keppel, 7th Earl of Albemarle</a>, specializing in the application of electric motors to transportation. The company employed <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Volk" title="Magnus Volk">Magnus Volk</a> as a manager in the development of their electric launch department. After 12 months of experimental work starting in 1888 with a <a href="/wiki/Randan" title="Randan">randan</a> <a href="/wiki/Skiff" title="Skiff">skiff</a>, the firm commissioned the construction of hulls which they equipped with electrical apparatus. The world's first fleet of electric launches for hire, with a chain of electrical <a href="/wiki/Charging_station" title="Charging station">charging stations</a>, was established along the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> in the 1880s. An 1893 pleasure map of the Thames shows 8 "charging stations for electric launches" between <a href="/wiki/Kew" title="Kew">Kew</a> (<a href="/wiki/Strand-on-the-Green" title="Strand-on-the-Green">Strand-on-the-Green</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caversham,_Berkshire" title="Caversham, Berkshire">Caversham</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-thames_map_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thames_map-1">[1]</a></sup> The company built its headquarters on the island called <a href="/wiki/Platt%27s_Eyot" title="Platt's Eyot">Platt's Eyot</a>.</p>
<p>From 1889 until just before the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> the boating season and <a href="/wiki/Regatta" title="Regatta">regattas</a> saw the silent <a href="/wiki/Electric_boats" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric boats">electric boats</a> plying their way up and downstream.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Early_electric_launch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/29/Early_electric_launch.jpg/260px-Early_electric_launch.jpg" width="260" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/29/Early_electric_launch.jpg/390px-Early_electric_launch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/29/Early_electric_launch.jpg/520px-Early_electric_launch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="255" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Early_electric_launch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Early electric launch on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, built by William Sargeant.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The company's electric launches were widely used by the rich as a conveyance along the river. Grand ships were constructed of teak or mahogany and furnished luxuriously, with stained glass windows, silk curtains and velvet cushions. William Sargeant was commissioned by Immisch's company to build the <i>Mary Gordon</i> in 1898 for <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Council" title="Leeds City Council">Leeds City Council</a> for use on the <a href="/wiki/Roundhay_Park" title="Roundhay Park">Roundhay Park</a> Lake - the boat still survives and is <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.marygordon.org.uk/?page_id=21">currently being restored</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This 70 foot long luxury pleasure craft could carry up to 75 passengers in comfort. Launches were exported elsewhere - they were used in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and all over the world.</p>
<p>In the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">Chicago World Fair</a> 55 launches developed from <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Reckenzaun" title="Anthony Reckenzaun">Anthony Reckenzaun</a>'s work carried more than a million passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Electric boats had an early period of popularity between around 1890 and 1920, before the emergence of the internal combustion engine drove them out of most applications.</p>
<p>Most of the electric boats of this era were small passenger boats on non-tidal waters at a time when the only power alternative was <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg/220px-Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg/330px-Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg/440px-Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Koenigssee_Schoenau-Anlegepier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An electric passenger launch on Lake Königssee in Germany</div>

<p>With the advent of the gasoline-powered <a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard motor</a>, the use of electric power on boats declined from the 1920s. However, in a few situations, the use of electric boats has persisted from the early 20th century to the present day. One of these is on the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigssee" title="Königssee">Königssee</a> lake, near <a href="/wiki/Berchtesgaden" title="Berchtesgaden">Berchtesgaden</a> in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Here the lake is considered so environmentally sensitive that steam and motor boats have been prohibited since 1909. Instead the <a href="/wiki/Bayerische_Seenschifffahrt" title="Bayerische Seenschifffahrt">Bayerische Seenschifffahrt</a> company and its predecessors have operated a fleet of electric launches to provide a public passenger service on the lake.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first electrically powered submarines were built in the 1890s, such as the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Peral_Submarine" title="Peral Submarine">Peral submarine</a>, launched in 1888.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Since then, electric power has been used almost exclusively for the powering of submarines underwater (traditionally by batteries), although diesel was used for directly powering the propeller while on the surface until the development of <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_transmission" title="Diesel-electric transmission">diesel-electric transmission</a> by the US Navy in 1928, in which the propeller was always powered by an electric motor, energy coming from batteries while submerged or diesel generator while surfaced.</p>
<p>The use of combined fuel and electric propulsion (<i><a href="/wiki/Combined_diesel-electric_and_gas" title="Combined diesel-electric and gas">combined diesel-electric or gas</a></i>, or CODLOG) has gradually been extended over the years to the extent that some modern liners such as the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Mary_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Mary 2">Queen Mary 2</a> use only electric motors for the actual propulsion, powered by diesel and gas turbine engines. The advantages include being able to run the fuel engines at an optimal speed at all times and being able to mount the electric motor in a <a href="/wiki/Azimuth_thruster" title="Azimuth thruster">pod</a> which may be rotated by 360° for increased manoeuvrability. Note that this is not actually an <i>electric boat</i>, but rather a variant of <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric_transmission" title="Diesel-electric transmission">diesel-electric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turbine-electric_transmission" title="Turbine-electric transmission">turbine-electric</a> propulsion, similar to the diesel or electric propulsion used on <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a> since <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">WWI</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of electricity alone to power boats stagnated apart from their <a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard</a> use as <a href="/wiki/Trolling_motor" title="Trolling motor">trolling motors</a> until the Duffy Electric Boat Company of California started mass-producing small electric craft in 1968. Duffy Boats has produced over 10,000 electric powered boats to date and is producing well over 300 per year today. It wasn't until the 1980s that the British Electric Boat Association <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> was formed and solar powered boats started to emerge.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main components of the drive system of any electrically powered boat are similar in all cases, and similar to the options available for any <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charger">Charger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Charger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Electric energy has to be obtained for the battery bank from some source.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg/220px-Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg/330px-Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d6/Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg 2x" data-file-width="407" data-file-height="307" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_on_yacht_at_sea.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Solar panels deployed on a small yacht at sea.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Mains_electricity" title="Mains electricity">mains</a> <a href="/wiki/Battery_charger" title="Battery charger">charger</a></b> allows the boat to be charged from shore-side power when available. Shore-based power stations are subject to much stricter environmental controls than the average marine diesel or outboard motor. By purchasing <a href="/wiki/Green_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Green electricity">green electricity</a> it is possible to operate electric boats using <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable</a> or <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic module">Solar panels</a></b> can be built into the boat in reasonable areas in the deck, cabin roof or as awnings. Some solar panels, or photovoltaic arrays, can be flexible enough to fit to slightly curved surfaces and can be ordered in unusual shapes and sizes. Nonetheless, the heavier, rigid mono-crystalline types are more efficient in terms of energy output per square meter. The efficiency of solar panels rapidly decreases when they are not pointed directly at the sun, so some way of tilting the arrays while under way is very advantageous.</li>
<li><b>Towed generators</b> are common on long-distance <a href="/wiki/Cruising_(maritime)" title="Cruising (maritime)">cruising</a> yachts and can generate a lot of power when travelling under sail. If an electric boat has sails as well, and will be used in deep water (deeper than about 15&#160;m or 50&#160;ft), then a towed generator can help build up battery charge while sailing (there is no point in trailing such a generator while under electric propulsion as the extra <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> from the generator would <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">waste more electricity than it generates</a>). Some electric power systems use the free-wheeling drive propeller to generate charge through the drive motor when sailing, but this system, including the design of the propeller and any gearing, cannot be optimised for both functions. It may be better locked off or feathered while the towed generator's more efficient turbine gathers energy.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wind_turbines" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbines">Wind turbines</a></b> are common on <a href="/wiki/Cruising_(maritime)" title="Cruising (maritime)">cruising</a> yachts and can be very well suited to electric boats. There are safety considerations regarding the spinning blades, especially in a strong wind. It is important that the boat is big enough that the turbine can be mounted out of the way of all passengers and crew under all circumstances, including when alongside and when coming alongside a dock, a bank or a pier. It is also important that the boat is big enough and stable enough that the <i>top hamper</i> created by the turbine on its pole or mast does not compromise its stability in a strong wind or gale. Large enough wind generators could produce a completely wind-powered electric boat. No such boats are yet known although a few <i>mechanical</i> wind turbine powered boats exist.</li>
<li>In hybrid electric boats, it boat has an <b>internal combustion engine</b> anyway, then its <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a> will provide significant charge when it is running. Two schemes are in use: the combustion engine and the electric motor are both coupled to the drive (parallel hybrid), or the combustion engine drives a separate generator only, for charging the storage batteries (series hybrid).</li>
</ul>
<p>In all cases, a <b><a href="/wiki/Charge_controller" title="Charge controller">charge regulator</a></b> is needed. This ensures that the batteries are charged at the maximum rate that they safely can stand when the power is available. It also ensures that they are not overcharged when nearing full charge and not overheated when a large charge current becomes available.</p>

<p>There have been significant technical advances in battery technology in recent years, and more are to be expected in the future.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lead-acid_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead-acid batteries">Lead-acid batteries</a></b> may still be the most viable option at the moment (2008). Deep-cycle, 'traction' batteries are the obvious choice. They are heavy and bulky, but not much more so than the diesel engine, tanks and fittings that they may replace. They need to be securely mounted, low down and centrally situated in the boat. It is essential that they <i>cannot</i> move around under <i>any</i> circumstances. Care must be taken that there is no risk of the strong acid being spilled in the event of a capsize as this could be very dangerous. Venting of explosive hydrogen and oxygen gases is also necessary. Typical lead-acid batteries must be kept topped-up with distilled water.</li>
<li><b>Valve-regulated lead-acid (<a href="/wiki/VRLA" class="mw-redirect" title="VRLA">VRLA</a>) batteries</b>, usually known as sealed lead-acid, <a href="/wiki/Gel_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Gel battery">Gel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Absorbed_Glass_Mat" class="mw-redirect" title="Absorbed Glass Mat">AGM</a> batteries, minimize the risk of spillage, and gases are only vented when the batteries are overcharged. These batteries require minimal maintenance, as they cannot and usually do not need to be refilled with water.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Nickel_metal_hydride" class="mw-redirect" title="Nickel metal hydride">Nickel metal hydride</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion</a></b> and other battery types are becoming available, but are still expensive. These are the kind of batteries currently common in rechargeable hand tools like drills and screwdrivers, but they are relatively new to this environment. They require different charge controllers to those that suit lead-acid types.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">Lithium-ion</a></b> in this case usually means lithium iron phosphate batteries, which although are heavier than other lithium-ion, is safer for marine application. They are expensive but in applications which need reliability and ruggedness like ferries which run most of the day (10–12 hours/day) this is the best option. It has a much longer life - 5 to 7 years life-cycle.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow batteries</a></b> may provide significant advantages in years to come. Today (2010) however they are still expensive and require specialist equipment and knowledge.</li>
</ul>
<p>The size of the battery bank determines the <i>range</i> of the boat under electric power. The speed at which the boat is motored also affects range – a lower speed can make a big difference to the energy required to move a hull. Other factors that affect range include sea-state, currents, windage and any charge that can be reclaimed while under way, for example by solar panels in full sun. A wind turbine in a good wind will help, and motor-sailing in any wind could do so even more.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed_controller">Speed controller</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Speed controller">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To make the boat usable and maneuverable, a simple-to-operate forward/stop/backwards speed controller is needed. This must be efficient—i.e. it must not get hot and waste energy at any speed—and it must be able to stand the full current that could conceivably flow under any full-load condition. One of the most common types of speed controllers uses <a href="/wiki/Pulse-width_modulation" title="Pulse-width modulation">pulse-width modulation</a> (PWM). PWM controllers send high frequency pulses of power to the motor(s). As more power is needed the pulses become longer in duration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_motor">Electric motor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electric motor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> technologies are in use. Traditional field-wound DC motors were and still are used. Today many boats use lightweight permanent magnet DC motors. The advantage of both types is that while the speed can be controlled electronically, this is not a requirement. Some boats use AC motors or permanent magnet brushless motors. The advantages of these are the lack of commutators which can wear out or fail and the often lower currents allowing thinner cables; the disadvantages are the total reliance on the required electronic controllers and the usually high voltages which require a high standard of insulation.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drive_train">Drive train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drive train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditional boats use an inboard motor powering a propeller through a propeller shaft complete with bearings and seals. Often a gear reduction is incorporated in order to be able to use a larger more efficient propeller. This can be a traditional gear box, coaxial planetary gears or a <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">transmission</a> with belts or chains. Because of the inevitable loss associated with gearing, many drives eliminate it by using slow high-torque motors. The electric motor can be encapsulated into a pod with the propeller and fixed outside the hull (saildrive) or on an outboard fixture (outboard motor).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li><b>Canal, river and lake</b> boats. Electric boats, with their limited range and performance, have tended to be used mostly on inland waterways, where their complete lack of local pollution is a significant advantage. Electric drives are also available as auxiliary propulsion for sailing yachts on inland waters.</li>
<li><b>Electric outboards</b> and trolling motors have been available for some years at prices from about $100 (US) up to several thousand. These require external batteries in the bottom of the boat, but are otherwise practical one-piece items. Most available electric outboards are not as efficient as custom drives, but are optimised for their intended use, e.g. for inland waterway fishermen. They are quiet and they do not pollute the water or the air, so they do not scare away or harm fish, birds and other wildlife. Combined with modern waterproof battery packs, electric outboards are also ideal for yacht tenders and other inshore pleasure boats.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cruising_(maritime)" title="Cruising (maritime)">Cruising</a> yachts</b> usually have an auxiliary engine, and there are two main uses for it: One is to power ahead or motor-sail at sea when the wind is light or from the wrong direction. The other is to provide the last 10 minutes or so of propulsion when the boat is in port and needs to be manoeuvred into a tight berth in a crowded and confined marina or harbour. Electric propulsion is not suitable for prolonged cruising at full power although the power required to motor slowly in light airs and calm seas is small. Regarding the second case, electric drives are ideally suited as they can be finely controlled and can provide substantial power for short periods of time.</li>
</ul>

<li>Commercial <b><a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferries</a></b>. Norway's first <a href="https://no.wikipedia.org/wiki/MF_%C2%ABAmpere%C2%BB" class="extiw" title="no:MF «Ampere»">battery-electric ferry is the Ampere</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> with capacity for 120 cars and 12 trucks. As of November 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it has operated for 106,000 km. Its battery holds 1 <a href="/wiki/MWh" class="mw-redirect" title="MWh">MWh</a> of energy, but the 9 minute charge time is sometimes not enough, and more battery capacity is to be installed. Norway has scheduled several other electric and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_ferry" title="Hybrid ferry">hybrid ferry</a> projects.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Based on operational data, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> concludes in a <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle analysis">life cycle analysis</a> that 61 of Norway's 112 diesel ferry routes could be replaced by electric ferries with a payback time of 5 years. The analysis includes auxiliary costs such as chargers, grid, and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Other projects are considered in Canada, Sweden and Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/India%27s_First_Solar_Ferry" class="mw-redirect" title="India's First Solar Ferry">India's First Solar Ferry</a>, a 75-passenger boat, that is powered by sun and grid charging with lithium batteries, is under construction and expected to be in operation by July, 2016. Based on the predictions of consumption the payback time is 3 years.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_ferry" title="Hybrid ferry">Diesel-electric hybrid</a></b>. There is a third potential use for a diesel auxiliary and that is to charge the batteries, when they suddenly start to wane far from shore in the middle of the night, or at anchor after some days of living aboard. In this case, where this kind of use is to be expected, perhaps on a larger cruising yacht, then a combined diesel-electric solution may be designed from the start. The diesel engine is installed with the prime purpose of charging the battery banks, and the electric motor with that of propulsion. There is some reduction in efficiency if motoring for long distances as the diesel's power is converted first to electricity and then to motion, but there is a balancing saving every time the wind-, sail- and solar-charged batteries are used for manoeuvring and for short journeys without starting the diesel. There is the flexibility of being able to start the diesel as a pure generator whenever required. The main losses are in weight and installation cost, but on the bigger cruising boats that may sit at anchor running large diesels for hours every day, these are not too big an issue, compared to the savings that can be made at other times. An example is the fishing boat Selfa El-Max 1099,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> with 135 kWh battery and 80kW diesel generator.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">LNG</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Platform_supply_vessel" title="Platform supply vessel">supply vessel</a> started operation in 2016 with a 653 kWh/1600 kW battery acting as <a href="/wiki/Operating_reserve" title="Operating reserve">spinning reserve</a> during <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_positioning" title="Dynamic positioning">dynamic positioning</a>, saving 15-30% fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Solar powered</b>. A boat propelled by direct solar energy is a marine <a href="/wiki/Solar_vehicle" title="Solar vehicle">solar vehicle</a>. The available sunlight is almost always converted to electricity by solar cells, temporarily stored in accumulator batteries, and used to drive a propeller through an electric motor. Power levels are usually on the order of a few hundred watts to a few kilowatts. Solar powered boats started to become known around 1985 and in 1995 the first commercial solar passenger boats appeared.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Solar powered boats have been used successfully at sea. The first crossing of the Atlantic Ocean was achieved in the winter of 2006/2007 by the solar catamaran <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sun21&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sun21 (page does not exist)">Sun21</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> (see also <a href="/wiki/List_of_solar-powered_boats" title="List of solar-powered boats">List of solar-powered boats</a>)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wired_electric_boats">Wired electric boats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Wired electric boats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg/220px-Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg/330px-Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg/440px-Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Strausberg_Faehre_Steffi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The electric ferry <i>Steffi</i> on the Straussee, 30&#160;km east of Berlin.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A special category of electric boats are the vessels receiving their electrical power by wire. One or two wires are fixed over the water and the boat can make contact with them to draw electric current. In case of a single wire the electrical circuit has to be closed by the water itself, giving rise to a larger resistance and corrosion of the electrodes. In case of two wires no electric current has to be sent through the water, but the twin wires, which cause a short-circuit whenever they come into contact with each other, complicate the construction.</p>
<p>Naturally the boat has to stay close to the wire and therefore it is limited in its maneuverability. For ferries and on narrow canals this is no problem. The <a href="/wiki/Straussee_Ferry" title="Straussee Ferry">Straussee Ferry</a> in Strausberg, Germany is an example. It crosses a lake along a 370&#160;m trajectory. It is powered by 170&#160;V from a single overhead wire. In the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mauvages_tunnel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mauvages tunnel (page does not exist)">Mauvages tunnel</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunnel_de_Mauvages" class="extiw" title="fr:Tunnel de Mauvages">fr</a>)</span> on the <a href="/wiki/Marne-Rhine_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Marne-Rhine Canal">Marne-Rhine Canal</a> a bipolar overhead line provides 600&#160;V DC to an electrical tug, pulling itself and several ships through the 4877&#160;m tunnel along a submerged chain. This prevents the buildup of diesel exhaust fumes in the tunnel. Another example was the experimental <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_tug_Teltow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Electrical tug Teltow (page does not exist)">electrical tug Teltow</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teltow_(Schiff)" class="extiw" title="de:Teltow (Schiff)">de</a>)</span> on the Kleinmachnower See, 17&#160;km south-west of Berlin. It was used from 1903 till 1910 and had current collection poles based on those used on trolley buses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_and_embodied_energy">Pollution and embodied energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pollution and embodied energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">Embodied energy</a></div>
<p>All the component parts of any boat have to be manufactured and will eventually have to be disposed of. Some pollution and use of other energy sources are inevitable during these stages of the boat's life and electric boats are no exception. The benefits to the global environment that are achieved by the use of electric propulsion are manifested during the working life of the boat, which can be many years. These benefits are also most directly felt in the sensitive and very beautiful environments in which such a boat is used.</p>
<p>The May 2010 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_Boat_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Classic Boat (magazine)">Classic Boat</a></i> magazine carried a pro and con article entitled <i>Electric debate</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CB263_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CB263-31">[31]</a></sup> Jamie Campbell argued against electric boating on four main counts, which were rebuffed by Kevin Desmond and Ian Rutter of the Electric Boat Association. Jamie Campbell asserted that electric propulsion can no more be justified afloat than a <a href="/wiki/British_Seagull" title="British Seagull"><i>Seagull</i></a> outboard motor, proposing <a href="/wiki/Boat_building" title="Boat building">wooden</a> <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> boats and rowing <a href="/wiki/Dinghy" title="Dinghy">dinghies</a> as "by far the most environmentally sensitive and renewable options for recreational boating".</p>
<dl>
<dt>Electricity production</dt>
<dd>Campbell asserts that the lack of pollution from an electric boat "reeks of <a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">nimbyism</a>" as "the discharge is all in someone else's back yard" and that the provision of re-charging points may involve digging up miles of habitat. Desmond responds that while there is no doubt that rechargeable batteries derive their energy from power stations (when not charged on board by solar and wind generation), noisier internal-combustion-engined boats obtain their fuel from even further away and that, once installed a power cable is less environmentally disruptive than a petrol station. Rutter notes that electric boats tend to recharge overnight, using 'base load'.</dd>
<dt>Efficiency</dt>
<dd>While there are losses in the charge/discharge cycle and in the conversion of electricity to motive power, Rutter points out that most electric boats need only about 1.5&#160;kW or 2&#160;hp to cruise at 5&#160;mph, a common maximum river speed and that a 30&#160;hp petrol or diesel engine producing only 2&#160;hp is considerably more inefficient. While Campbell refers to heavy batteries requiring a "load-bearing hull" and "cranky, even unseaworthy vessels", Desmond points out that electric boaters tend to prefer efficient, low-wash hull forms that are more friendly to river banks.</dd>
<dt>Pollution</dt>
<dd>Campbell discusses the pollution that "traditional" batteries put into the water when a boat sinks, but Desmond says that electric boats are no more liable to sinking than other types and lists the leakage of fuel, engine oil and coolant additives as inevitable when an internal-combustion-engined boat sinks. Rutter points to the "very nasty cocktail of pollutants" that come out of a diesel wet exhaust in normal use.</dd>
<dt>Battery manufacture</dt>
<dd>Campbell mentions "all manner of noxious chemicals ... involved in battery manufacture", but Rutter describes them as being "lead and sulphuric acid with a few extra trace metals in a modest plastic box" with a potential lifetime of 10–12 years. Desmond says that the US has a 98% recycling rate for lead acid batteries and that the battery and lead-smelting industries observe some of the tightest pollution control standards in the world.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The article mentions 25% and 30% discounts being offered to electric boaters by the UK <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Broads_Authority" title="Broads Authority">Broads Authority</a> and that battery powered vehicles have <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>5</sub></span> the carbon footprint of their petrol equivalents. It is claimed that a typical recharge after a day's cruising costs £1.50, without the use of solar or wind power.<sup id="cite_ref-CB263_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CB263-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2016 life-cycle study in Norway states that electric ferries and hybrid offshore supply ships compensate for the environmental effects of producing lithium-ion batteries in less than 2 months.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_ships">Solar ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electric_boat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg/220px-Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg/330px-Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg/440px-Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5616" data-file-height="3744" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Planetsolar-miami-12.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/T%C3%BBranor_PlanetSolar" title="Tûranor PlanetSolar">PlanetSolar</a>, the world's largest solar-powered boat and the first ever solar electric vehicle to circumnavigate the globe (in 2012).</div>

<p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BBranor_PlanetSolar" title="Tûranor PlanetSolar">Tûranor PlanetSolar</a>, a 35 metre long, 26 metre wide <a href="/wiki/Catamaran" title="Catamaran">catamaran</a> yacht powered by 537 square metres of solar panels, was unveiled. On 4 May 2012 it completed a 60,023 kilometres (37,297&#160;mi) <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigation</a> of the Earth in <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> after 585 days and visiting 28 different countries, without using any fossil fuel. It is so far the largest solar-powered boat ever built.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>India's first solar ferry, a 75-passenger boat fully powered by sun, is under construction. It is expected to be completed by the middle of 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Japan's biggest shipping line <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Yusen" title="Nippon Yusen">Nippon Yusen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Oil_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nippon Oil Corporation">Nippon Oil Corporation</a> said solar panels capable of generating 40 kilowatts of electricity would be placed on top of a 60,000 tonne <a href="/wiki/Car_carrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Car carrier">car carrier</a> ship to be used by <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Motor_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Toyota Motor Corporation">Toyota Motor Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> yacht company <a href="/wiki/Wally_Yachts" title="Wally Yachts">Wally</a> has announced a "gigayacht" designed for billionaires torn between buying a mansion and a superyacht.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The <i>Why 58 x 38</i> is designed to have an autonomous cruising range of 12,000 miles at 12 knots by means of 900m<sup>2</sup> of solar panels which generate 150&#160;kW to assist the diesel-electric motors and optional <a href="/wiki/Skysails" class="mw-redirect" title="Skysails">Skysails</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
