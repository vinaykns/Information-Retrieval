<p><b>Alta Wind Energy Center</b> (AWEC), also known as <b>Mojave Wind Farm</b>, is the third largest onshore wind energy project in the world. The Alta Wind Energy Center is a <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farm</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Pass" title="Tehachapi Pass">Tehachapi Pass</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Mountains" title="Tehachapi Mountains">Tehachapi Mountains</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kern_County,_California" title="Kern County, California">Kern County, California</a>. As of 2013, it is the largest wind farm in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with a combined installed capacity of 1,547&#160;MW (2,075,000&#160;hp). The project, being developed near <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Pass_Wind_Farm" title="Tehachapi Pass Wind Farm">Tehachapi Pass Wind Farm</a>— site of the first large-scale wind farms installed in the U.S. in the 1970s and 1980s—is "a powerful illustration of the growing size and scope of modern wind projects".<sup id="cite_ref-alta_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alta-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Edison" title="Southern California Edison">Southern California Edison</a> has agreed to a 25-year <a href="/wiki/Power_purchase_agreement" title="Power purchase agreement">power purchase agreement</a> for the power produced as part of the power purchase agreements for up to 1,500&#160;MW (2,000,000&#160;hp) or more of power generated from new projects to be built in the Tehachapi area. The project will "reduce carbon dioxide emissions by more than 5.2 million metric tons, which is equivalent to taking 446,000 cars off the road".<sup id="cite_ref-alta_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alta-2">[2]</a></sup> A total of 3,000&#160;MW (4,000,000&#160;hp) is planned.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wind farm is being developed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terra-Gen_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terra-Gen Power (page does not exist)">Terra-Gen Power</a> which closed a US$1.2&#160;billion financing deal in July 2010 with partners that included <a href="/wiki/Citibank" title="Citibank">Citibank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barclays_Capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Barclays Capital">Barclays Capital</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Credit_Suisse" title="Credit Suisse">Credit Suisse</a>. After many delays, the first phase began construction in 2010. Financing for additional phases of $650 million was secured in April 2012. Construction of the Alta Wind Energy Center is expected to create more than 3,000&#160;domestic manufacturing, construction, and maintenance jobs, and contribute more than one billion dollars to the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-alta_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alta-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alta_Wind_Energy_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The original "Alta-Oak Creek Mojave Project" plan consisted of up to 320&#160;<a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> occupying a 9,000-acre (36&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) area while producing 800&#160;MW (1,100,000&#160;hp) of power. That project was originally developed by Oak Creek Energy Systems under the contract with Terra-Gen, but the project development was later transferred to Terra-Gen. The project then became the first development under the umbrella of Alta Wind Energy Center.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Alta-Oak Creek Mojave Project is generally located at the south side of the Oak Creek Road, starting a few miles west of the state route 14 in <a href="/wiki/Mojave,_California" title="Mojave, California">Mojave</a> and continuing westward along the Oak Creek Road to the west side of the Tehachapi-Willow Springs Road.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Wind_Energy_Association" title="American Wind Energy Association">American Wind Energy Association</a>, two initial projects were completed in fall 2010, the 150&#160;MW (200,000&#160;hp) Alta II (Vestas) project using 50 3&#160;MW (4,000&#160;hp) generators and the 150&#160;MW Alta I project using 100 1.5&#160;MW generators.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Three subsequent projects 150&#160;MW Alta III, 102&#160;MW (137,000&#160;hp) Alta IV, and 168&#160;MW Alta V projects were completed in the 2nd quarter of 2011 using 50, 34, and 56 3&#160;MW Vestas V-90 wind turbines. Alta VIII and Alta VI projects, comprising 300&#160;MW (400,000&#160;hp), were completed in late 2011/early 2012. Alta VII and IX projects comprising 300&#160;MW (400,000&#160;hp), were completed in December 2012 (bringing the total to 1,320&#160;MW (1,770,000&#160;hp)), but they were renamed to Pinyon Pines Wind I and Pinyon Pines Wind II.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>138&#160;MW (185,000&#160;hp) Alta X and 90&#160;MW (120,000&#160;hp) Alta XI are under development with scheduled completion in late 2013, 180&#160;MW (240,000&#160;hp) Alta XIII in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
