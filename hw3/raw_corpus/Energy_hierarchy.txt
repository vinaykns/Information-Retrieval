<p>The <b>Energy Hierarchy</b> is a classification of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> options, prioritised to assist progress towards a more sustainable energy system. It is a similar approach to the <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">waste hierarchy</a> for minimising resource depletion, and adopts a parallel sequence.</p>
<p>The highest priorities cover the prevention of unnecessary energy usage both through eliminating waste and improving <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>. The sustainable production of energy resources is the next priority. Depletive and waste-producing energy generation options are the lowest priority.</p>
<p>For an energy system to be sustainable: the resources applied to producing the energy must be capable of lasting indefinitely; <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy conversion">energy conversion</a> should produce no harmful by-products, including net emissions, nor wastes which cannot be fully recycled; and it must be capable of meeting reasonable energy demands.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_saving">Energy saving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Energy saving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">Energy conservation</a></div>
<p>The top priority under the Energy Hierarchy is energy conservation or the prevention of unnecessary use of energy. This category includes eliminating waste by turning off unneeded lights and appliances and by avoiding unnecessary journeys. Heat loss from buildings is a major source of energy wastage,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> so improvements to building insulation and air-tightness can make a significant contribution to energy conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many countries have agencies to encourage energy saving.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">Energy efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The second priority under the energy hierarchy is to ensure that energy that is used is produced and consumed efficiently. Energy efficiency has two main aspects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_efficiency_of_energy_consumption">Conversion efficiency of energy consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conversion efficiency of energy consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">Efficient energy use</a></div>
<p>Energy efficiency is the ratio of the productive output of a device to the energy it consumes.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Energy efficiency was a lower priority when energy was cheap and awareness of its environmental impact was low. In 1975 the average fuel economy of a car in the US was under 15 miles per gallon<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_vehicles_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_vehicles-6">[6]</a></sup> Incandescent light bulbs, which were the most common type until the late 20th century, waste 90% of their energy as heat, with only 10% converted to useful light.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, energy efficiency has become a priority.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The last reported average fuel efficiency of US cars had almost doubled from the 1975 level;<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_vehicles_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_vehicles-6">[6]</a></sup> LED lighting is now being promoted which are between five and ten times more efficient than incandescents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Many household appliances are now required to display <a href="/wiki/Energy_input_labeling" title="Energy input labeling">labels</a> to show their energy efficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_efficiency_of_energy_production">Conversion efficiency of energy production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conversion efficiency of energy production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">Energy conversion efficiency</a></div>
<p>Losses are incurred when energy is harvested from the natural resource from which it is derived, such as fossil fuels, radioactive materials, solar radiation or other sources. Most electricity production is in thermal power stations, where much of the source energy is lost as heat. The average efficiency of world electricity production in 2009 was c.37%.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A priority in the Energy Hierarchy is to improve the efficiency of energy conversion, whether in traditional power stations<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> or by improving the <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_station#System_performance" title="Photovoltaic power station">performance ratio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_stations" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic power stations">Photovoltaic power stations</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and other energy sources.</p>
<p>Overall efficiency and sustainability can also be improved by capacity- or <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation#Fuel_switching" title="Climate change mitigation">fuel-switching</a> from less efficient, less sustainable resources to better ones; but this is mainly covered under the <a href="#Low_impact_energy_production">fourth level</a> of the hierarchy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_energy_production">Sustainable energy production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sustainable energy production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a></div>
<p>Renewable energy describes naturally occurring, theoretically inexhaustible sources of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These sources are treated as being inexhaustible, or naturally replenished, and fall into two classes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elemental_renewables">Elemental renewables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elemental renewables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first class of renewables derive from climatic or elemental sources,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> such as sunlight, wind, waves, tides or rainfall (hydro power). Geothermal energy from the heat of the earth's core also falls in this category.</p>
<p>These are treated as being inexhaustible because most derive ultimately from energy emanating from the sun, which has an estimated life of 6.5 billion years.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio-energy">Bio-energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bio-energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The other main class of renewables, bioenergy,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> derives from biomass, where the relatively short growing cycle means that usage is replenished by new growth. Bioenergy is usually converted by combustion, and therefore gives rise to carbon emissions. It is treated as carbon neutral overall, because an equivalent amount of carbon dioxide will have been extracted from the atmosphere during the growing cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bioenergy sources can be solid, such as wood and <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a>; liquid, such as biofuels; or gaseous, such as biomethane from anaerobic digestion.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_impact_energy_production">Low impact energy production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Low impact energy production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low_carbon_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Low carbon power">Low carbon power</a></div>
<p>The next priority in the hierarchy covers energy sources that are not entirely sustainable, but have a low environmental impact. These include the use of fossil fuels with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nuclear energy is sometimes treated as a low impact source, because it has low carbon emissions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_impact_energy_production">High impact energy production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: High impact energy production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lowest priority under the energy hierarchy is energy production using unsustainables sources, such as unabated fossil fuels. Some also place nuclear energy in this category, rather than the one above, because of the required management/storage of highly hazardous <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a> over extremely long (hundreds of thousands of years or more) timeframes <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and depletion of uranium resources.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a consensus that the share of such energy sources must decline.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within this tier, there are possibilities for limiting adverse impacts by <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation#Fuel_switching" title="Climate change mitigation">switching</a> from the most damaging fuel sources, such as coal, to less emittive sources, such as gas.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many suggest that when such high impact energy usage has been minimised, the effects of any unavoidable residual usage should be counterbalanced by emissions <a href="/wiki/Offsetting" class="mw-redirect" title="Offsetting">offsetting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_energy_hierarchy">Origins of the energy hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Origins of the energy hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Energy Hierarchy was first proposed in 2005 by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Wolfe_(engineer)" title="Philip Wolfe (engineer)">Philip Wolfe</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> when he was Director General of the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Association" title="Renewable Energy Association">Renewable Energy Association</a>. This first version had three levels; energy efficiency, renewables and traditional energy production. It was endorsed and adopted in 2006 by a consortium of institutions, associations and other bodies in the Sustainable Energy Manifesto.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Subsequently the concept has been adopted and refined by others in the energy industry<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and in government.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
