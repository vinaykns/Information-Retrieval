			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Liquefied natural gas</h1>

<p><b>Liquefied natural gas</b> (<b>LNG</b>) is <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (predominantly <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, CH<sub>4</sub>, with some mixture of <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6f</sub>) that has been converted to liquid form for ease of storage or transport. It takes up about 1/600th the volume of natural gas in the gaseous state. It is <a href="/wiki/Odorless" class="mw-redirect" title="Odorless">odorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">colorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corrosive_substance" title="Corrosive substance">non-corrosive</a>. Hazards include flammability after vaporization into a gaseous state, freezing and <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">liquefaction</a> process involves removal of certain components, such as dust, <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, water, and heavy <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, which could cause difficulty downstream. The natural gas is then <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensed</a> into a liquid at close to atmospheric pressure by cooling it to approximately −162&#160;°C (−260&#160;°F); maximum transport pressure is set at around 25&#160;kPa (4&#160;psi).</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LNG_Process_En.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/LNG_Process_En.png/300px-LNG_Process_En.png" width="300" height="469" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/LNG_Process_En.png/450px-LNG_Process_En.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/LNG_Process_En.png/600px-LNG_Process_En.png 2x" data-file-width="663" data-file-height="1037" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LNG_Process_En.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A typical LNG process. The gas is first extracted and transported to a processing plant where it is purified by removing any condensates such as water, oil, mud, as well as other gases such as CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>S. An LNG process train will also typically be designed to remove trace amounts of mercury from the gas stream to prevent mercury amalgamizing with aluminium in the cryogenic heat exchangers. The gas is then cooled down in stages until it is liquefied. LNG is finally stored in storage tanks and can be loaded and shipped.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>LNG achieves a higher reduction in volume than <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> (CNG) so that the (volumetric) <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of LNG is 2.4&#160;times greater than that of CNG or 60 percent that of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This makes LNG cost efficient to transport over long distances where pipelines do not exist. Specially designed <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> sea vessels (<a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a>) or cryogenic road tankers are used for its transport. LNG is principally used for transporting natural gas to markets, where it is regasified and distributed as pipeline natural gas. It can be used in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">natural gas vehicles</a>, although it is more common to design vehicles to use <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a>. Its relatively high cost of production and the need to store it in expensive cryogenic tanks have hindered widespread commercial use. Despite these drawbacks, on energy basis LNG production is expected to hit 10% of the global crude production by 2020.(see <a href="#Trade">LNG Trade</a>)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_energy_content_and_energy_density">Specific energy content and energy density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Specific energy content and energy density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The heating value depends on the source of gas that is used and the process that is used to liquefy the gas. The range of heating value can span +/- 10 to 15 percent. A typical value of the <a href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Higher heating value">higher heating value</a> of LNG is approximately 50&#160;MJ/kg or 21,500&#160;BTU/lb.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A typical value of the <a href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower heating value">lower heating value</a> of LNG is 45&#160;MJ/kg or 19,350&#160;BTU/lb.</p>
<p>For the purpose of comparison of different fuels the heating value may be expressed in terms of energy per volume which is known as the <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> expressed in MJ/liter. The density of LNG is roughly 0.41&#160;kg/liter to 0.5&#160;kg/liter, depending on temperature, pressure, and composition,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> compared to water at 1.0&#160;kg/liter. Using the median value of 0.45&#160;kg/liter, the typical energy density values are 22.5 MJ/liter (based on higher heating value) or 20.3 MJ/liter (based on lower heating value).</p>
<p>The (volume-based) energy density of LNG is approximately 2.4 times greater than that of CNG which makes it economical to transport natural gas by ship in the form of LNG. The energy density of LNG is comparable to <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> but is only 60 percent that of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> and 70 percent that of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Experiments on the properties of gases started early in the seventeenth century. By the middle of the seventeenth century Robert Boyle had derived the inverse relationship between the pressure and the volume of gases. About the same time, Guillaume Amontons started looking into temperature effects on gas. Various gas experiments continued for the next 200 years. During that time there were efforts to liquefy gases. Many new facts on the nature of gases had been discovered. For example, early in the nineteenth century <a href="/wiki/Cagniard_de_la_Tour" class="mw-redirect" title="Cagniard de la Tour">Cagniard de la Tour</a> had shown there was a temperature above which a gas could not be liquefied. There was a major push in the mid to late nineteenth century to liquefy all gases. A number of scientists including Michael Faraday, James Joule, and William Thomson (Lord Kelvin), did experiments in this area. In 1886 Karol Olszewski liquefied methane, the primary constituent of natural gas. By 1900 all gases had been liquefied except <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">helium</a> which was liquefied in 1908.</p>
<p>The first large scale liquefaction of natural gas in the U.S. was in 1918 when the U.S. government liquefied natural gas as a way to extract helium, which is a small component of some natural gas. This helium was intended for use in British dirigibles for World War I. The liquid natural gas (LNG) was not stored, but regasified and immediately put into the gas mains.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The key patents having to do with natural gas liquefaction were in 1915 and the mid-1930s. In 1915 Godfrey Cabot patented a method for storing liquid gases at very low temperatures. It consisted of a Thermos bottle type design which included a cold inner tank within an outer tank; the tanks being separated by insulation. In 1937 Lee Twomey received patents for a process for large scale liquefaction of natural gas. The intention was to store natural gas as a liquid so it could be used for shaving peak energy loads during cold snaps. Because of large volumes it is not practical to store natural gas, as a gas, near atmospheric pressure. However, if it can be liquefied it can be stored in a volume 600 times smaller. This is a practical way to store it but the gas must be stored at −260&#160;°F (−162&#160;°C).</p>
<p>There are two processes for liquefying natural gas in large quantities. The first is the cascade process, in which the natural gas is cooled by another gas which in turn has been cooled by still another gas, hence named the "cascade" process. There are usually two cascade cycles prior to the liquid natural gas cycle. The other method is the Linde process, with a variation of the Linde process, called the Claude process, being sometimes used. In this process, the gas is cooled regeneratively by continually passing it through an orifice until it is cooled to temperatures at which it liquefies. The cooling of gas by expanding it through an orifice was developed by James Joule and William Thomson and is known as the Joule-Thomson effect. Lee Twomey used the cascade process for his patents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_operations_in_the_United_States">Commercial operations in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial operations in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The East Ohio Gas Company built a full-scale commercial liquid natural gas (LNG) plant in Cleveland, Ohio, in 1940 just after a successful pilot plant built by its sister company, Hope Natural Gas Company of West Virginia. This was the first such plant in the world. Originally it had three spheres, approximately 63 feet in diameter containing LNG at −260&#160;°F. Each sphere held the equivalent of about 50 million cubic feet of natural gas. A fourth tank, a cylinder, was added in 1942. It had an equivalent capacity of 100 million cubic feet of gas. The plant operated successfully for three years. The stored gas was regasified and put into the mains when cold snaps hit and extra capacity was needed. This precluded the denial of gas to some customers during a cold snap.</p>
<p>The Cleveland plant failed on October 20, 1944 when the cylindrical tank ruptured spilling thousands of gallons of LNG over the plant and nearby neighborhood. The gas evaporated and caught fire, which caused 130 fatalities.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The fire delayed further implementation of LNG facilities for several years. However, over the next 15 years new research on low-temperature alloys, and better insulation materials, set the stage for a revival of the industry. It restarted in 1959 when a U.S. World War II Liberty ship, the <i>Methane Pioneer</i>, converted to carry LNG, made a delivery of LNG from the U.S. Gulf coast to energy starved Great Britain. In June 1964, the world's first purpose-built LNG carrier, the "Methane Princess" entered service.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Soon after that a large natural gas field was discovered in Algeria. International trade in LNG quickly followed as LNG was shipped to France and Great Britain from the Algerian fields. One more important attribute of LNG had now been exploited. Once natural gas was liquefied it could not only be stored more easily, but it could be transported. Thus energy could now be shipped over the oceans via LNG the same way it was shipped by oil.</p>
<p>The US LNG industry restarted in 1965 when a series of new plants were built in the U.S. The building continued through the 1970s. These plants were not only used for peak-shaving, as in Cleveland, but also for base-load supplies for places that never had natural gas prior to this. A number of import facilities were built on the East Coast in anticipation of the need to import energy via LNG. However, a recent boom in U.S. natural gas production (2010-2014), enabled by <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a> (“fracking”), has many of these import facilities being considered as export facilities. The first U.S. LNG export was completed in early 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-bizjournals.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bizjournals.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The natural gas fed into the LNG plant will be treated to remove water, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and other components that will <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freeze</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>) under the low temperatures needed for storage or be destructive to the liquefaction facility. LNG typically contains more than 90 percent&#160;<a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. It also contains small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>, some heavier <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, and nitrogen. The purification process can be designed to give almost 100 percent&#160;<a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. One of the risks of LNG is <a href="/wiki/Rapid_phase_transition" title="Rapid phase transition">a rapid phase transition explosion</a> (RPT), which occurs when cold LNG comes into contact with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most important infrastructure needed for LNG production and transportation is an LNG plant consisting of one or more <a href="/wiki/LNG_train" title="LNG train">LNG trains</a>, each of which is an independent unit for gas liquefaction. The largest LNG train now in operation is in Qatar. These facilities recently reached a safety milestone, completing 12 years of operations on its offshore facilities without a Lost Time Incident.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Until recently it was the Train&#160;4 of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_LNG" title="Atlantic LNG">Atlantic LNG</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> with a production capacity of 5.2 million metric ton per annum (mmtpa),<sup id="cite_ref-upstream2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upstream2-11">[11]</a></sup> followed by the <a href="/wiki/SEGAS_LNG" title="SEGAS LNG">SEGAS LNG</a> plant in Egypt with a capacity of 5&#160;mmtpa. In July 2014, Atlantic LNG celebrated its 3000th cargo of LNG at the company’s liquefaction facility in Trinidad.<sup id="cite_ref-lngindustry.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lngindustry.com-12">[12]</a></sup> The Qatargas&#160;II plant has a production capacity of 7.8&#160;mmtpa for each of its two trains. LNG sourced from Qatargas II will be supplied to Kuwait, following the signing of an agreement in May 2014 between Qatar Liquefied Gas Company and Kuwait Petroleum Corp.<sup id="cite_ref-lngindustry.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lngindustry.com-12">[12]</a></sup> LNG is loaded onto ships and delivered to a regasification terminal, where the LNG is allowed to expand and reconvert into gas. Regasification terminals are usually connected to a storage and pipeline distribution network to distribute natural gas to local distribution companies (LDCs) or independent power plants (IPPs).</p>

<p>The LNG industry developed slowly during the second half of the last century because most LNG plants are located in remote areas not served by pipelines, and because of the large costs to treat and transport LNG. Constructing an LNG plant costs at least $1.5&#160;billion per 1&#160;mmtpa capacity, a receiving terminal costs $1&#160;billion per 1 bcf/day throughput capacity and LNG vessels cost $200 million–$300 million.</p>
<p>In the early 2000s, prices for constructing LNG plants, receiving terminals and vessels fell as new technologies emerged and more players invested in liquefaction and regasification. This tended to make LNG more competitive as a means of energy distribution, but increasing material costs and demand for construction contractors have put upward pressure on prices in the last few years. The standard price for a 125,000 cubic meter LNG vessel built in European and Japanese shipyards used to be US$250&#160;million. When Korean and Chinese shipyards entered the race, increased competition reduced profit margins and improved efficiency—reducing costs by 60 percent. Costs in US dollars also declined due to the devaluation of the currencies of the world's largest shipbuilders: the Japanese yen and Korean won.</p>
<p>Since 2004, the large number of orders increased demand for shipyard slots, raising their price and increasing ship costs. The per-ton construction cost of an LNG liquefaction plant fell steadily from the 1970s through the 1990s. The cost reduced by approximately 35 percent. However, recently the cost of building liquefaction and regasification terminals doubled due to increased cost of materials and a shortage of skilled labor, professional engineers, designers, managers and other white-collar professionals.</p>
<p>Due to natural gas shortage concerns in the northeastern U.S. and surplus nature gas in the rest of the country, many new LNG import and export terminals are being contemplated in the United States. Concerns about the safety of such facilities create controversy in some regions where they are proposed. One such location is in the Long Island Sound between Connecticut and Long Island. <a href="/wiki/Broadwater_Energy" title="Broadwater Energy">Broadwater Energy</a>, an effort of <a href="/wiki/TransCanada_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="TransCanada Corp.">TransCanada Corp.</a> and Shell, wishes to build an LNG import terminal in the sound on the New York side. Local politicians including the Suffolk County Executive raised questions about the terminal. In 2005, New York Senators <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Schumer" title="Chuck Schumer">Chuck Schumer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> also announced their opposition to the project.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Several import terminal proposals along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> were also met with high levels of resistance and questions. On Sep. 13, 2013 the U.S. Department of Energy approved <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Cove_Point_LNG" title="Dominion Cove Point LNG">Dominion Cove Point</a>'s application to export up to 770 million cubic feet per day of LNG to countries that do not have a free trade agreement with the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-ogj.com_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogj.com-20">[20]</a></sup> In May 2014, the FERC concluded its environmental assessment of the Cove Point LNG project, which found that the proposed natural gas export project could be built and operated safely.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Another LNG terminal is currently proposed for <a href="/wiki/Elba_Island" title="Elba Island">Elba Island</a>, Ga.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Plans for three LNG export terminals in the U.S. Gulf Coast region have also received conditional Federal approval.<sup id="cite_ref-ogj.com_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogj.com-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In Canada, an LNG export terminal is under construction near <a href="/wiki/Guysborough" class="mw-redirect" title="Guysborough">Guysborough</a>, Nova Scotia.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_aspects">Commercial aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Trade">Global Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Global Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the commercial development of an LNG value chain, LNG suppliers first confirm sales to the downstream buyers and then sign long-term contracts (typically 20–25 years) with strict terms and structures for gas pricing. Only when the customers are confirmed and the development of a greenfield project deemed economically feasible, could the sponsors of an LNG project invest in their development and operation. Thus, the LNG liquefaction business has been limited to players with strong financial and political resources. Major international oil companies (IOCs) such as <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a>, <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a>, <a href="/wiki/BG_Group" title="BG Group">BG Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>, and national oil companies (NOCs) such as <a href="/wiki/Pertamina" title="Pertamina">Pertamina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">Petronas</a> are active players.</p>
<p>LNG is shipped around the world in specially constructed <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">seagoing vessels</a>. The trade of LNG is completed by signing an SPA (sale and purchase agreement) between a supplier and receiving terminal, and by signing a GSA (gas sale agreement) between a receiving terminal and end-users. Most of the contract terms used to be <a href="/wiki/Incoterms#Delivered_Ex_Ship" title="Incoterms">DES or ex ship</a>, holding the seller responsible for the transport of the gas. With low shipbuilding costs, and the buyers preferring to ensure reliable and stable supply, however, contracts with <a href="/wiki/FOB_(shipping)" title="FOB (shipping)">FOB</a> terms increased. Under such terms the buyer, who often owns a vessel or signs a long-term charter agreement with independent carriers, is responsible for the transport.</p>
<p>LNG purchasing agreements used to be for a long term with relatively little flexibility both in price and volume. If the annual contract quantity is confirmed, the buyer is obliged to take and pay for the product, or pay for it even if not taken, in what is referred to as the obligation of <a href="/wiki/Take-or-pay_contract" title="Take-or-pay contract">take-or-pay contract</a> (TOP).</p>
<p>In the mid-1990s, LNG was a buyer's market. At the request of buyers, the SPAs began to adopt some flexibilities on volume and price. The buyers had more upward and downward flexibilities in TOP, and short-term SPAs less than 16 years came into effect. At the same time, alternative destinations for cargo and arbitrage were also allowed. By the turn of the 21st century, the market was again in favor of sellers. However, sellers have become more sophisticated and are now proposing sharing of arbitrage opportunities and moving away from S-curve pricing. There has been much discussion regarding the creation of an "OGEC" as a natural gas equivalent of <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a>. Russia and <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, countries with the largest and the third largest natural gas reserves in the world, have finally supported such move.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Until 2003, LNG prices have closely followed oil prices. Since then, LNG prices in Europe and Japan have been lower than oil prices, although the link between LNG and oil is still strong. In contrast, prices in the US and the UK have recently skyrocketed, then fallen as a result of changes in supply and storage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In late 1990s and in early 2000s, the market shifted for buyers, but since 2003 and 2004, it has been a strong seller's market, with net-back as the best estimation for prices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>Research from QNB Group in 2014 shows that robust global demand is likely to keep LNG prices high for at least the next few years.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The current surge in <a href="/wiki/Unconventional_oil" title="Unconventional oil">unconventional oil</a> and gas in the U.S. has resulted in lower gas prices in the U.S. This has led to discussions in Asia' oil linked gas markets to import gas based on Henry Hub index.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Recent high level conference in Vancouver, the Pacific Energy Summit 2013 <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://nbr.org/research/activity.aspx?id=292#.UZD-IStARpE">Pacific Energy Summit 2013</a> convened policy makers and experts from Asia and the U.S. to discuss LNG trade relations between these regions.</p>
<p>Receiving terminals exist in about 18 countries, including India, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, China, Greece, Belgium, Spain, Italy, France, the UK, the US, Chile, and the Dominican Republic, among others. Plans exist for Argentina, Brazil, Uruguay, Canada, Ukraine and others to also construct new receiving (gasification) terminals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LNG_Project_Screening">LNG Project Screening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: LNG Project Screening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Base load (large scale, &gt;1 MTPA) LNG projects require natural gas reserves,<sup id="cite_ref-engineersaustralia.org.au_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engineersaustralia.org.au-27">[27]</a></sup> buyers<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and financing. Using proven technology and a proven contractor is extremely important for both investors and buyers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Gas reserves required: 1 tcf of gas required per Mtpa of LNG over 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-engineersaustralia.org.au_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engineersaustralia.org.au-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary use of LNG is to simplify transport of natural gas from the source to a destination. On the large scale, this is done when the source and the destination are across an ocean from each other. It can also be used when adequate pipeline capacity is not available. For large scale transport uses, the LNG is typically regassified at the receiving end and pushed into the local natural gas pipeline infrastructure.</p>
<p>- LNG can also be used to meet peak demand when the normal pipeline infrastructure can meet most demand needs, but not the peak demand needs. These plants are typically called LNG Peak Shaving Plants as the purpose is to shave off part of the peak demand from what is required out of the supply pipeline.</p>
<p>- LNG can be used to fuel internal combustion engines. LNG is in the early stages of becoming a mainstream fuel for transportation needs. It is being evaluated and tested for over-the-road trucking,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> off-road,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> marine, and train applications.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There are known problems with the fuel tanks and delivery of gas to the engine,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> but despite these concerns the move to LNG as a transportation fuel has begun.</p>
<p><br /></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_LNG_to_fuel_large_over-the-road_trucks">Use of LNG to fuel large over-the-road trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Use of LNG to fuel large over-the-road trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China has been a leader in the use of LNG vehicles<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> with over 100,000 LNG powered vehicles on the road as of Sept 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States the beginnings of a public LNG Fueling capability is being put in place. An alternative fuelling centre tracking site shows 84 public truck LNG fuel centres as of Dec 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It is possible for large trucks to make cross country trips such as Los Angeles to Boston and refuel at public refuelling stations every 500 miles. The 2013 National Trucker's Directory lists approximately 7,000 truckstops,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> thus approximately 1% of US truckstops have LNG available.</p>
<p>As of December 2014 LNG fuel and NGV's have not been taken to very quickly within Europe and it is questionable whether LNG will ever become the fuel of choice among fleet operators.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> During the year 2015, Netherlands introduced LNG powered trucks in transport sector.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Australian government is planning to develop an LNG highway to utilise the locally produced LNG and replace the imported diesel fuel used by interstate haulage vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the year 2015, India also made small beginning by transporting LNG by LNG powered road tankers in Kerala state.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Japan, the world’s largest importer of LNG, is set to use of LNG as road transport fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_LNG_to_fuel_high-horsepower.2Fhigh-torque_engines">Use of LNG to fuel high-horsepower/high-torque engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Use of LNG to fuel high-horsepower/high-torque engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In internal combustion engines the volume of the cylinders is a common measure of the power of an engine. Thus a 2000cc engine would typically be more powerful than a 1800cc engine, but that assumes a similar air-fuel mixture is used. Also If, via a turbocharger as an example, the 1800cc engine were using an air-fuel mixture that was significantly more energy dense, then it might be able to produce more power than a 2000cc engine burning a less energy dense air-fuel mixture. Unfortunately turbochargers are both complex and expensive. Thus it becomes clear for high-horsepower/high-torque engines a fuel that can inherently be used to create a more energy dense air-fuel mixture is preferred because a smaller and simpler engine can be used to produce the same power.</p>
<p>With traditional gasoline and diesel engines the energy density of the air-fuel mixture is limited because the liquid fuels do not mix well in the cylinder. Further, gasoline and diesel auto-ignite<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> at temperatures and pressures relevant to engine design. An important part of traditional engine design is designing the cylinders, compression ratios, and fuel injectors such that pre-ignition is avoided,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> but at the same time as much fuel as possible can be injected, become well mixed, and still have time to complete the combustion process during the power stroke.</p>
<p>Natural gas does not auto-ignite at pressures and temperatures relevant to traditional gasoline and diesel engine design, thus providing more flexibility in the design of a natural gas engine. Methane, the main component of natural gas, has an autoignition temperature of 580C/1076F,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> whereas gasoline and diesel autoignite at approximately 250C and 210C respectively.</p>
<p>With a compressed natural gas (CNG) engine, the mixing of the fuel and the air is more effective since gases typically mix well in a short period of time, but at typical CNG compression pressures the fuel itself is less energy dense than gas or diesel thus the end result is a lower energy dense air-fuel mixture. Thus for the same cylinder displacement engine, a non turbocharged CNG powered engine is typically less powerful than a similarly sized gas or diesel engine. For that reason turbochargers are popular on European CNG cars.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Despite that limitation, the 12 liter Cummins Westport ISX12G engine<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> is an example of a CNG capable engine designed to pull tractor/trailer loads up to 80,000 lbs showing CNG can be used in most if not all on-road truck applications. The original ISX G engines incorporated a turbocharger to enhance the air-fuel energy density.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>LNG offers a unique advantage over CNG for more demanding high-horsepower applications by eliminating the need for a turbocharger. Because LNG boils at approximately -160C, by using a simple heat exchanger a small amount of LNG can be converted to its gaseous form at extremely high pressure with the use of little or no mechanical energy. A properly designed high-horsepower engine can leverage this extremely high pressure energy dense gaseous fuel source to create a higher energy density air-fuel mixture than can be efficiently created with a CNG powered engine. The end result when compared to CNG engines is more overall efficiency in high-horsepower engine applications when high-pressure direct injection technology is used. The Westport HDMI2<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> fuel system is an example of a high-pressure direct injection technology that does not require a turbocharger if teamed with appropriate LNG heat exchanger technology. The Volvo Trucks 13-liter LNG engine<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> is another example of a LNG engine leveraging advanced high pressure technology.</p>
<p>Westport recommends CNG for engines 7 liters or smaller and LNG with direct injection for engines between 20 and 150 liters. For engines between 7 and 20 liters either option is recommended. See slide 13 from there NGV BRUXELLES – INDUSTRY INNOVATION SESSION presentation<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Container shipping company, Maersk Group has decided to introduce LNG fuel driven container ships.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> DEME Group has contracted Wärtsilä to power its new generation ‘Antigoon’ class dredger with dual fuel (DF) engines.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> High horsepower engines in the oil drilling, mining, locomotive, and marine fields have been or are being developed.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Paul Blomerous has written a paper<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> concluding as much as 40 Million tonnes per annum of LNG (approximately 26.1 billion gallons/year or 71 million gallons/day) could be required just to meet the global needs of the high-horsepower engines by 2025 to 2030.</p>
<p>As of the end of 1st quarter 2015 Prometheus Energy Group Inc claims to have delivered over 100 million gallons of LNG within the previous 4 years into the industrial market,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and is continuing to add new customers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade">Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global trade in LNG is growing rapidly from negligible in 1970 to what is expected to be a globally meaningful amount by 2020. As a reference, the 2014 global production of crude oil was 92 million barrels per day<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> or 186.4 quads/yr (quadrillion BTUs/yr).</p>
<p>In 1970, global LNG trade was of 3 billion cubic metres (bcm) (0.11 quads).<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup> In 2011, it was 331 bcm (11.92 quads).<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup> The U.S. started exporting LNG in February 2016. The Black &amp; Veatch Oct 2014 forecast is that by 2020, the U.S. alone will export between 10 Bcf/d (3.75 quads/yr) and 14 Bcf/d (5.25 quads/yr).<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> E&amp;Y projects global LNG demand could hit 400 mtpa (19.7 quads) by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-Global_LNG_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Global_LNG-60">[60]</a></sup> If that occurs, the LNG market will be roughly 10% the size of the global crude oil market, and that does not count the vast majority of natural gas which is delivered via pipeline directly from the well to the consumer.</p>
<p>In 2004, LNG accounted for 7 percent of the world’s natural gas demand.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The global trade in LNG, which has increased at a rate of 7.4 percent per year over the decade from 1995 to 2005, is expected to continue to grow substantially.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.ca.gov_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.ca.gov-62">[62]</a></sup> LNG trade is expected to increase at 6.7 percent per year from 2005 to 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.ca.gov_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.ca.gov-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until the mid-1990s, LNG demand was heavily concentrated in Northeast Asia: Japan, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. At the same time, Pacific Basin supplies dominated world LNG trade.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.ca.gov_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.ca.gov-62">[62]</a></sup> The world-wide interest in using natural gas-fired combined cycle generating units for electric power generation, coupled with the inability of North American and North Sea natural gas supplies to meet the growing demand, substantially broadened the regional markets for LNG. It also brought new Atlantic Basin and Middle East suppliers into the trade.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.ca.gov_62-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.ca.gov-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 2011, there were 18 LNG exporting countries and 25 LNG importing countries. The three biggest LNG exporters in 2011 were Qatar (75.5 MT), Malaysia (25 MT) and Indonesia (21.4 MT). The three biggest LNG importers in 2011 were Japan (78.8 MT), South Korea (35 MT) and UK (18.6 MT).<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> LNG trade volumes increased from 140 MT in 2005 to 158 MT in 2006, 165 MT in 2007, 172 MT in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Global LNG production was 246 MT in 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> most of which was used in trade between countries.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> During the next several years there would be significant increase in volume of LNG Trade.<sup id="cite_ref-Global_LNG_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Global_LNG-60">[60]</a></sup> For example, about 59 MTPA of new LNG supply from six new plants came to market just in 2009, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Northwest Shelf Train 5: 4.4 MTPA</li>
<li>Sakhalin II: 9.6 MTPA</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yemen_LNG" title="Yemen LNG">Yemen LNG</a>: 6.7 MTPA</li>
<li>Tangguh: 7.6 MTPA</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Qatargas" title="Qatargas">Qatargas</a>: 15.6 MTPA</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rasgas" class="mw-redirect" title="Rasgas">Rasgas</a> Qatar: 15.6 MTPA</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2006, Qatar became the world's biggest exporter of LNG.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup> As of 2012, Qatar is the source of 25 percent of the world's LNG exports.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Investments in U.S. export facilities were increasing by 2013, these investments were spurred by increasing <a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">shale gas</a> production in the United States and a large price differential between natural gas prices in the U.S. and those in Europe and Asia. Cheniere Energy became the first company in the United States to receive permission and export LNG in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-bizjournals.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bizjournals.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imports">Imports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Imports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1964, the UK and France made the first LNG trade, buying gas from <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, witnessing a new era of energy.</p>
<p>Today, only 19 countries export LNG.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compared with the crude oil market, in 2013 the natural gas market was about 72 percent of the crude oil market (measured on a heat equivalent basis),<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> of which LNG forms a small but rapidly growing part. Much of this growth is driven by the need for clean fuel and some substitution effect due to the high price of oil (primarily in the heating and electricity generation sectors).</p>
<p>Japan, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, Spain, France, Italy and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> import large volumes of LNG due to their shortage of energy. In 2005, Japan imported 58.6 million tons of LNG, representing some 30 percent of the LNG trade around the world that year. Also in 2005, South Korea imported 22.1 million tons, and in 2004 Taiwan imported 6.8 million tons. These three major buyers purchase approximately two-thirds of the world's LNG demand. In addition, Spain imported some 8.2 mmtpa in 2006, making it the third largest importer. France also imported similar quantities as Spain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster</a> in March 2011 Japan became a major importer accounting for one third of the total.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT51713_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT51713-68">[68]</a></sup> European LNG imports fell by 30 percent in 2012, and are expected to fall further by 24 percent in 2013, as South American and Asian importers pay more.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_diversion">Cargo diversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cargo diversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on the LNG SPAs, LNG is destined for pre-agreed destinations, and diversion of that LNG is not allowed. However, if Seller and Buyer make a mutual agreement, then the diversion of the cargo is permitted—subject to sharing the additional profit created by such a diversion. In the European Union and some other jurisdictions, it is not permitted to apply the profit-sharing clause in LNG SPAs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_of_LNG_plants">Cost of LNG plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cost of LNG plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For an extended period of time, design improvements in liquefaction plants and tankers had the effect of reducing costs.</p>
<p>In the 1980s, the cost of building an LNG liquefaction plant cost $350 per tpa (tonne per year). In 2000s, it was $200/tpa. In 2012, the costs can go as high as $1,000/tpa, partly due to the increase in the price of steel.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>As recently as 2003, it was common to assume that this was a “learning curve” effect and would continue into the future. But this perception of steadily falling costs for LNG has been dashed in the last several years.<sup id="cite_ref-energy.ca.gov_62-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy.ca.gov-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>The construction cost of greenfield LNG projects started to skyrocket from 2004 afterward and has increased from about $400 per ton per year of capacity to $1,000 per ton per year of capacity in 2008.</p>
<p>The main reasons for skyrocketed costs in LNG industry can be described as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>Low availability of <a href="/wiki/Engineering,_procurement,_and_construction_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering, procurement, and construction management">EPC</a> contractors as result of extraordinary high level of ongoing petroleum projects worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Hashimoto_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hashimoto-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>High raw material prices as result of surge in demand for raw materials.</li>
<li>Lack of skilled and experienced workforce in LNG industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Hashimoto_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hashimoto-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Devaluation of US dollar.</li>
</ol>
<p>The 2007–2008 <a href="/wiki/Global_Financial_Crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Global Financial Crisis">global financial crisis</a> caused a general decline in raw material and equipment prices, which somewhat lessened the construction cost of LNG plants. However, by 2012 this was more than offset by increasing demand for materials and labor for the LNG market.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Small-scale_liquefaction_plants">Small-scale liquefaction plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Small-scale liquefaction plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Small-scale liquefaction plants are suitable for peakshaving on natural gas pipelines, transportation fuel, or for deliveries of natural gas to remote areas not connected to pipelines.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> They typically have a compact size, are fed from a natural gas pipeline, and are located close to the location where the LNG will be used. This proximity decreases transportation and LNG product costs for consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WR2015_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WR2015-72">[72]</a></sup> It also avoids the additional greenhouse gas emissions generated during long transportation.</p>
<p>The small-scale LNG plant also allows localized peakshaving to occur—balancing the availability of natural gas during high and low periods of demand. It also makes it possible for communities without access to natural gas pipelines to install local distribution systems and have them supplied with stored LNG.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LNG_pricing">LNG pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: LNG pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three major pricing systems in the current LNG contracts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Oil indexed contract used primarily in Japan, Korea, Taiwan and China;</li>
<li>Oil, oil products and other energy carriers indexed contracts used primarily in Continental Europe;<sup id="cite_ref-Hughes_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-74">[74]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>Market indexed contracts used in the US and the UK.;</li>
</ul>
<p>The formula for an indexed price is as follows:</p>
<p>CP = BP + β X</p>
<ul>
<li>BP: constant part or base price</li>
<li>β: gradient</li>
<li>X: indexation</li>
</ul>
<p>The formula has been widely used in Asian LNG SPAs, where base price refers to a term that represents various non-oil factors, but usually a constant determined by negotiation at a level which can prevent LNG prices from falling below a certain level. It thus varies regardless of oil price fluctuation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Henry_Hub_Plus">Henry Hub Plus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Henry Hub Plus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some LNG buyers have already signed contracts for future US-based cargos at Henry Hub-linked prices.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Cheniere Energy’s LNG export contract pricing consists of a fixed fee (liquefaction tolling fee) plus 115% of Henry Hub per <a href="/wiki/MMBtu" class="mw-redirect" title="MMBtu">MMBtu</a> of LNG.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Tolling fees in the Cheniere contracts vary: $2.25/MMBtu with BG Group signed in 2011; $2.49/MMBtu with Spain's GNF signed in 2012; and $3.00/MMBtu with South Korea's Kogas and Centrica signed in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_parity">Oil parity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Oil parity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oil parity is the LNG price that would be equal to that of crude oil on a <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">Barrel of oil equivalent</a> basis. If the LNG price exceeds the price of crude oil in <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">BOE</a> terms, then the situation is called broken oil parity. A coefficient of 0.1724 results in full oil parity. In most cases the price of LNG is less than the price of crude oil in <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">BOE</a> terms. In 2009, in several spot cargo deals especially in East Asia, oil parity approached the full oil parity or even exceeds oil parity.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In January 2016, the spot LNG price (5.461 US$/mmbtu) has broken oil parity when the Brent crude price (≤32 US$/bbl) has fallen steeply.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> By the end of June 2016, LNG price has fallen by nearly 50% below its oil parity price making it more economical than more polluting diesel/gas oil in transport sector.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="S-curve">S-curve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: S-curve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many formulae include an <a href="/wiki/Logistic_function" title="Logistic function">S-curve</a>, where the price formula is different above and below a certain oil price, to dampen the impact of high oil prices on the buyer, and low oil prices on the seller. Bulk of the LNG trade is governed by long term contracts. When the spot LNG prices are cheaper than long term oil price indexed contracts, profitable LNG end use is to power mobile engines for replacing costly gasoline and diesel consumption.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="JCC_and_ICP">JCC and ICP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: JCC and ICP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In most of the East Asian LNG contracts, price formula is indexed to a basket of crude imported to Japan called the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Crude_Cocktail" title="Japan Crude Cocktail">Japan Crude Cocktail</a> (JCC). In Indonesian LNG contracts, price formula is linked to <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Crude_Price" title="Indonesian Crude Price">Indonesian Crude Price</a> (ICP).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brent_and_other_energy_carriers">Brent and other energy carriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Brent and other energy carriers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In continental Europe, the price formula indexation does not follow the same format, and it varies from contract to contract. Brent crude price (B), heavy fuel oil price (HFO), light fuel oil price (LFO), gas oil price (GO), coal price, electricity price and in some cases, consumer and producer price indexes are the indexation elements of price formulas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_review">Price review</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Price review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Usually there exists a clause allowing parties to trigger the price revision or price reopening in LNG SPAs. In some contracts there are two options for triggering a price revision. regular and special. Regular ones are the dates that will be agreed and defined in the LNG SPAs for the purpose of price review.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_LNG">Quality of LNG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Quality of LNG">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LNG quality is one of the most important issues in the LNG business. Any gas which does not conform to the agreed specifications in the sale and purchase agreement is regarded as “off-specification” (off-spec) or “off-quality” gas or LNG. Quality regulations serve three purposes:<sup id="cite_ref-Com.qa_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Com.qa-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>1 - to ensure that the gas distributed is non-corrosive and non-toxic, below the upper limits for H<sub>2</sub>S, total sulphur, CO<sub>2</sub> and Hg content;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>2 - to guard against the formation of liquids or hydrates in the networks, through maximum water and hydrocarbon dewpoints;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>3 - to allow interchangeability of the gases distributed, via limits on the variation range for parameters affecting combustion: content of inert gases, calorific value, <a href="/wiki/Wobbe_index" title="Wobbe index">Wobbe index</a>, Soot Index, Incomplete Combustion Factor, Yellow Tip Index, etc.</dd>
</dl>
<p>In the case of off-spec gas or LNG the buyer can refuse to accept the gas or LNG and the seller has to pay liquidated damages for the respective off-spec gas volumes.</p>
<p>The quality of gas or LNG is measured at delivery point by using an instrument such as a gas chromatograph.</p>
<p>The most important gas quality concerns involve the sulphur and mercury content and the calorific value. Due to the sensitivity of liquefaction facilities to sulfur and mercury elements, the gas being sent to the liquefaction process shall be accurately refined and tested in order to assure the minimum possible concentration of these two elements before entering the liquefaction plant, hence there is not much concern about them.</p>
<p>However, the main concern is the heating value of gas. Usually natural gas markets can be divided in three markets in terms of heating value:<sup id="cite_ref-Com.qa_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Com.qa-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Asia (Japan, Korea, Taiwan) where gas distributed is rich, with a gross calorific value (GCV) higher than 43 MJ/m<sup>3</sup>(n), i.e. 1,090 Btu/scf,</li>
<li>the UK and the US, where distributed gas is lean, with a GCV usually lower than 42 MJ/m<sup>3</sup>(n), i.e. 1,065 Btu/scf,</li>
<li>Continental Europe, where the acceptable GCV range is quite wide: approx. 39 to 46 MJ/m<sup>3</sup>(n), i.e. 990 to 1,160 Btu/scf.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are some methods to modify the heating value of produced LNG to the desired level. For the purpose of increasing the heating value, injecting propane and butane is a solution. For the purpose of decreasing heating value, nitrogen injecting and extracting butane and propane are proved solutions. Blending with gas or LNG can be a solutions; however all of these solutions while theoretically viable can be costly and logistically difficult to manage in large scale. Lean LNG price in terms of mmbtu is lower to the rich LNG price.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquefaction_technology">Liquefaction technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Liquefaction technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several liquefaction processes available for large, baseload LNG plants:<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Liquefin<sup>TM</sup> - designed by <a href="/wiki/Air_Liquide" title="Air Liquide">Air Liquide</a></li>
<li>AP-C3MR<sup>TM</sup> - designed by <a href="/wiki/Air_Products_%26_Chemicals" title="Air Products &amp; Chemicals">Air Products &amp; Chemicals</a>, Inc. (APCI)</li>
<li>Cascade - designed by ConocoPhillips</li>
<li>AP-X<sup>®</sup> - designed by <a href="/wiki/Air_Products_%26_Chemicals" title="Air Products &amp; Chemicals">Air Products &amp; Chemicals</a>, Inc. (APCI)</li>
<li>DMR (Dual Mixed Refrigerant)</li>
<li>SMR (Single Mixed Refrigerant)</li>
<li>MFC® (mixed fluid cascade) - designed by <a href="/wiki/Linde_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Linde AG">Linde</a></li>
<li>PRICO<sup>®</sup> (SMR) - designed by Black &amp; Veatch</li>
</ol>
<p>As of January 2016, global nominal LNG liquefaction capacity was 301.5 MTPA (million tonnes per annum), and liquefaction capacity under construction was 142 MTPA.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>The majority of these trains use either APCI AP-C3MR<sup>TM</sup> or Cascade technology for the liquefaction process. The other processes, used in a small minority of some liquefaction plants, include Shell's DMR (double-mixed refrigerant) technology and the Linde technology.</p>
<p>APCI technology is the most-used liquefaction process in LNG plants: out of 100 liquefaction trains onstream or under-construction, 86 trains with a total capacity of 243 MMTPA have been designed based on the APCI process. Philips Cascade process is the second most-used, used in 10 trains with a total capacity of 36.16 MMTPA. The Shell DMR process has been used in three trains with total capacity of 13.9 MMTPA; and, finally, the Linde/Statoil process is used in the Snohvit 4.2 MMTPA single train.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Floating_liquefied_natural_gas" title="Floating liquefied natural gas">Floating liquefied natural gas</a> (FLNG) facilities float above an offshore gas field, and produce, liquefy, store and transfer LNG (and potentially LPG and condensate) at sea before carriers ship it directly to markets. The first FLNG facility is now in development by Shell,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> due for completion in around 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Egtank.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Egtank.jpg/220px-Egtank.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Egtank.jpg/330px-Egtank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Egtank.jpg/440px-Egtank.jpg 2x" data-file-width="488" data-file-height="322" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Egtank.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
LNG storage tank at <a href="/wiki/EG_LNG" title="EG LNG">EG LNG</a></div>

<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/LNG_storage_tank" title="LNG storage tank">LNG storage tanks</a> are typically full containment type, which has a <a href="/wiki/Prestressed_concrete" title="Prestressed concrete">prestressed concrete</a> outer wall and a high-nickel steel inner tank, with extremely efficient insulation between the walls. Large tanks are low aspect ratio (height to width) and cylindrical in design with a domed steel or concrete roof. Storage pressure in these tanks is very low, less than 10&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> (1.45&#160;psig). Sometimes more expensive underground tanks are used for storage. Smaller quantities (say 700&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (190,000 US&#160;gallons) and less), may be stored in horizontal or vertical, vacuum-jacketed, pressure vessels. These tanks may be at pressures anywhere from less than 50&#160;kPa to over 1,700&#160;kPa (7&#160;psig to 250&#160;psig).</p>
<p>LNG must be kept cold to remain a liquid, independent of pressure. Despite efficient insulation, there will inevitably be some heat leakage into the LNG, resulting in vaporisation of the LNG. This boil-off gas acts to keep the LNG cold. The boil-off gas is typically compressed and exported as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, or it is reliquefied and returned to storage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>LNG is transported in specially designed ships with double <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hulls</a> protecting the cargo systems from damage or leaks. There are several special leak test methods available to test the integrity of an LNG vessel's membrane cargo tanks.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tankers cost around US$200 million each.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_58-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transportation and supply is an important aspect of the gas business, since natural gas reserves are normally quite distant from consumer markets. Natural gas has far more volume than oil to transport, and most gas is transported by pipelines. There is a natural gas pipeline network in the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, Europe and North America. Natural gas is less dense, even at higher pressures. Natural gas will travel much faster than oil through a high-pressure pipeline, but can transmit only about a fifth of the amount of energy per day due to the lower density. Natural gas is usually liquefied to LNG at the end of the pipeline, prior to shipping.</p>
<p>Short LNG pipelines for use in moving product from LNG vessels to onshore storage are available. Longer pipelines, which allow vessels to offload LNG at a greater distance from port facilities are under development. This requires pipe in pipe technology due to requirements for keeping the LNG cold.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>LNG is transported using both tanker truck,<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> railway tanker, and purpose built ships known as <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a>. LNG will be sometimes taken to <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> temperatures to increase the tanker capacity. The first commercial <a href="/wiki/Ship-to-ship_transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Ship-to-ship transfer">ship-to-ship transfer</a> (STS) transfers were undertaken in February 2007 at the Flotta facility in Scapa Flow<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> with 132,000 m<sup>3</sup> of LNG being passed between the vessels Excalibur and Excelsior. Transfers have also been carried out by <a href="/wiki/Exmar_Shipmanagement" title="Exmar Shipmanagement">Exmar Shipmanagement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> gas tanker owner in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, which involved the transfer of LNG from a conventional LNG carrier to an LNG regasification vessel (LNGRV). Prior to this commercial exercise LNG had only ever been transferred between ships on a handful of occasions as a necessity following an incident.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/SIGTTO" title="SIGTTO">SIGTTO</a> - the Society of International Gas Tanker and Terminal Operators is the responsible body for LNG operators around the world and seeks to disseminate knowledge regarding the safe transport of LNG at sea.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides LNG vessels, LNG is also used in <a href="/wiki/Aviation_fuel#LNG" title="Aviation fuel">some aircraft</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminals">Terminals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Terminals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_LNG_terminals" title="List of LNG terminals">List of LNG terminals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas_terminal" title="Liquefied natural gas terminal">Liquefied natural gas terminal</a></div>
<p>Liquefied natural gas is used to transport natural gas over long distances, often by sea. In most cases, LNG terminals are purpose-built ports used exclusively to export or import LNG.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration">Refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The insulation, as efficient as it is, will not keep LNG cold enough by itself. Inevitably, heat leakage will warm and vapourise the LNG. Industry practice is to store LNG as a boiling <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogen</a>. That is, the liquid is stored at its <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> for the pressure at which it is stored (atmospheric pressure). As the vapour boils off, heat for the <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase change</a> cools the remaining liquid. Because the insulation is very efficient, only a relatively small amount of boil off is necessary to maintain temperature. This phenomenon is also called <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">auto-refrigeration</a>.</p>
<p>Boil off gas from land based <a href="/wiki/LNG_storage_tank" title="LNG storage tank">LNG storage tanks</a> is usually compressed and fed to <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> pipeline networks. Some <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a> use boil off gas for fuel.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> could be considered the most environmentally friendly fossil fuel, because it has the lowest CO<sub>2</sub> emissions per unit of energy and because it is suitable for use in high efficiency <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> power stations. For an equivalent amount of heat, burning natural gas produces about 30 percent less <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> than burning <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and about 45 per cent less than burning <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> On a per kilometre transported basis, emissions from LNG are lower than piped natural gas, which is a particular issue in Europe, where significant amounts of gas are piped several thousand kilometres from Russia. However, emissions from natural gas transported as LNG are higher than for natural gas produced locally to the point of combustion as emissions associated with transport are lower for the latter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, on the West Coast of the United States, where up to three new LNG importation terminals were proposed prior to the U.S. fracking boom, environmental groups, such as <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Environment" title="Pacific Environment">Pacific Environment</a>, Ratepayers for Affordable Clean Energy (RACE), and <a href="/wiki/Rising_Tide_North_America" title="Rising Tide North America">Rising Tide</a> had moved to oppose them.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> They claimed that, while natural gas power plants emit approximately half the carbon dioxide of an equivalent coal power plant, the natural gas combustion required to produce and transport LNG to the plants adds 20 to 40 percent more carbon dioxide than burning natural gas alone.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> A 2015 peer reviewed study evaluated the full end to end life cycle of LNG produced in the U.S. and consumed in Europe or Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> It concluded that global CO2 production would be reduced due to the resulting reduction in other fossil fuels burned.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_accidents">Safety and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefied_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Safety and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural gas is a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Combustibility" title="Combustibility">combustible</a> substance. To ensure safe and reliable operation, particular measures are taken in the design, construction and operation of LNG facilities.</p>
<p>In its liquid state, LNG is not explosive and can not burn. For LNG to burn, it must first vaporize, then mix with air in the proper proportions (the <a href="/wiki/Flammability_limit" title="Flammability limit">flammable range</a> is 5 percent to 15 percent), and then be ignited. In the case of a leak, LNG vaporizes rapidly, turning into a gas (methane plus trace gases), and mixing with air. If this mixture is within the <a href="/wiki/Flammability_limit" title="Flammability limit">flammable range</a>, there is risk of ignition which would create <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> hazards.</p>
<p>Gas venting from vehicles powered by LNG may create a flammability hazard if parked indoors for longer than a week. Additionally, due to its low temperature, refueling a LNG-powered vehicle requires training to avoid the risk of <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>LNG tankers have sailed over 100 million miles without a shipboard death or even a major accident.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several on-site accidents involving or related to LNG are listed below:</p>
<ul>
<li>1944, Oct. 20. The <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_East_Ohio_Gas_explosion" title="Cleveland East Ohio Gas explosion">East Ohio Natural Gas Co.</a> experienced a failure of an LNG tank in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/US" class="mw-redirect" title="US">US</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CHIV_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHIV-98">[98]</a></sup> 128 people perished in the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_East_Ohio_Gas_explosion" title="Cleveland East Ohio Gas explosion">explosion and fire</a>. The tank did not have a dike retaining wall, and it was made during World War II, when metal rationing was very strict. The steel of the tank was made with an extremely low amount of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, which meant the tank was brittle when exposed to the cryogenic nature of LNG. The tank ruptured, spilling LNG into the city sewer system. The LNG vaporized and turned into gas, which exploded and burned.</li>
<li>1979, Oct. 6, <a href="/wiki/Lusby,_Maryland" title="Lusby, Maryland">Lusby, Maryland</a>, US. A pump seal failed at the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Cove_Point_LNG" title="Dominion Cove Point LNG">Cove Point LNG</a> import facility, releasing natural gas vapors (not LNG), which entered and settled in an electrical conduit.<sup id="cite_ref-CHIV_98-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHIV-98">[98]</a></sup> A worker switched off a circuit breaker, which ignited the gas vapors. The resulting explosion killed a worker, severely injured another and caused heavy damage to the building. A safety analysis was not required at the time, and none was performed during the planning, design or construction of the facility.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> National fire codes were changed as a result of the accident.</li>
<li>2004, Jan. 19, <a href="/wiki/Skikda" title="Skikda">Skikda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. Explosion at Sonatrach LNG liquefaction facility.<sup id="cite_ref-CHIV_98-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHIV-98">[98]</a></sup> 27 killed, 56 injured, three LNG trains destroyed, a marine berth was damaged and 2004 production was down 76 percent for the year. Total loss was US$900 million. A steam boiler that was part of an LNG liquefaction train exploded triggering a massive hydrocarbon gas explosion. The explosion occurred where propane and ethane refrigeration storage were located. Site distribution of the units caused a domino effect of explosions.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> It remains unclear if LNG or LNG vapour, or other hydrocarbon gases forming part of the liquefaction process initiated the explosions. One report, of the US Government Team Site Inspection of the Sonatrach Skikda LNG Plant in Skikda, Algeria, March 12–16, 2004, has cited it was a leak of hydrocarbons from the refrigerant (liquefaction) process system.</li>
<li class="mw-empty-elt"></li>
</ul>
