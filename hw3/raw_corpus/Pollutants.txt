			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pollutant</h1>

<p>A <b>pollutant</b> is a substance or energy introduced into the environment that has undesired effects, or adversely affects the usefulness of a resource. A pollutant may cause long- or short-term damage by changing the growth rate of plant or animal species, or by interfering with human amenities, comfort, health, or property values. Some pollutants are <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodegradable">biodegradable</a> and therefore will not persist in the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a> in the long term. However the degradation products of some pollutants are themselves polluting such as the products DDE and DDD produced from degradation of <a href="/wiki/DDT" class="mw-redirect" title="DDT">DDT</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_types_of_pollutants_in_nature">Different types of pollutants in nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Different types of pollutants in nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stock_pollutants">Stock pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stock pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pollutants, towards which the environment has little or no <a href="/wiki/Absorptive_capacity" title="Absorptive capacity">absorptive capacity</a> are called <i>stock pollutants</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup> (e.g. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Persistent_synthetic_chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Persistent synthetic chemicals (page does not exist)">persistent synthetic chemicals</a>, non-<a href="/wiki/Biodegradable" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodegradable">biodegradable</a> plastics, and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>). Stock pollutants accumulate in the environment over time. The damage they cause increases as more pollutant is emitted, and persists as the pollutant accumulates. Stock pollutants can create a burden for future generations, by passing on damage that persists well after the ‘benefits' received from incurring that damage; have been forgotten.<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fund_pollutants">Fund pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fund pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fund_pollutants" class="mw-redirect" title="Fund pollutants">Fund pollutants</a> are those for which the environment has some absorptive capacity. Fund pollutants do not cause damage to the environment unless the emission rate exceeds the receiving environment's absorptive capacity (e.g. carbon dioxide, which is absorbed by plants and oceans).<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup> Fund pollutants are not destroyed, but rather converted into less harmful substances, or diluted/dispersed to non-harmful concentrations.<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_pollutants">Notable pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Notable pollutants include the following groups:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">Persistent organic pollutants</a> POP</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Persistent_Pharmaceutical_Pollutant" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Persistent Pharmaceutical Pollutant">Environmental Persistent Pharmaceutical Pollutants</a> EPPP</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">Volatile organic compounds</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_pollution">Light pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Light pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">Light pollution</a> is the impact that anthropogenic light has on the visibility of the night sky. It also encompasses</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_light_pollution" title="Ecological light pollution">ecological light pollution</a> which describes the effect of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial light">artificial light</a> on individual <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> and on the structure of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> as a whole.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zones_of_influence">Zones of influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zones of influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pollutants can also be defined by their zones of influence, both horizontally and vertically.<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_zone">Horizontal zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Horizontal zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The horizontal zone refers to the area that is damaged by a pollutant. Local pollutants cause damage near the emission source. Regional pollutants cause damage further from the emission source.<sup id="cite_ref-Tietenberg_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tietenberg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_zone">Vertical zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vertical zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vertical zone is referred to whether the damage is ground-level or atmospheric. Surface pollutants cause damage by concentrations of the pollutant accumulating near the Earth's surface Global pollutants cause damage by concentrations in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_and_monitoring_of_pollution" title="Regulation and monitoring of pollution">Regulation and monitoring of pollution</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pollutants can cross international borders and therefore international regulations are needed for their control. The <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention_on_Persistent_Organic_Pollutants" title="Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants">Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants</a>, which entered into force in 2004, is an international legally binding agreement for the control of persistent organic pollutants. <a href="/wiki/Pollutant_Release_and_Transfer_Register" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollutant Release and Transfer Register">Pollutant Release and Transfer Registers</a> (PRTR) are systems to collect and disseminate information on environmental releases and transfers of toxic chemicals from industrial and other facilities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Pollutant_Emission_Register" title="European Pollutant Emission Register">European Pollutant Emission Register</a> is a type of PRTR providing access to information on the annual emissions of industrial facilities in the Member States of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, as well as Norway.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pollutant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Clean Air Act standards.</b> Under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Ambient_Air_Quality_Standards" title="National Ambient Air Quality Standards">National Ambient Air Quality Standards</a> (NAAQS) are developed by the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) for six common air pollutants, also called "criteria pollutants": <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a>; <a href="/wiki/Smog#Photochemical_smog" title="Smog">smog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ground-level_ozone" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground-level ozone">ground-level ozone</a>; <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a>; <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Emissions_Standards_for_Hazardous_Air_Pollutants" title="National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants">National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants</a> are additional emission standards that are set by EPA for toxic air pollutants.</p>
<p><b>Clean Water Act standards.</b> Under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>, EPA promulgated national standards for municipal <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> plants, also called <i><a href="/wiki/Publicly_owned_treatment_works" title="Publicly owned treatment works">publicly owned treatment works</a>,</i> in the <i>Secondary Treatment Regulation.</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> National standards for industrial dischargers are called <i><a href="/wiki/Effluent_guidelines" title="Effluent guidelines">Effluent guidelines</a></i> (for existing sources) and <a href="/wiki/New_Source_Performance_Standard#Clean_Water_Act" title="New Source Performance Standard">New Source Performance Standards</a>, and currently cover over 50 industrial categories.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In addition, the Act requires <a href="/wiki/U.S._states" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. states">states</a> to publish <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act#Water_Quality_Standards_Program" title="Clean Water Act">water quality standards</a> for individual water bodies to provide additional protection where the national standards are insufficient.</p>
<p><b>RCRA standards.</b> The <a href="/wiki/Resource_Conservation_and_Recovery_Act" title="Resource Conservation and Recovery Act">Resource Conservation and Recovery Act</a> (RCRA) regulates the management, transport and disposal of <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Underground_storage_tank" title="Underground storage tank">underground storage tanks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A tank or piping network that has at least 10 percent of its volume underground is known as an underground storage tank (UST). They often store substances such as petroleum, that are harmful to the surrounding environment should it become contaminated.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
