<p><b>Sustainable development indicators</b> (SDI) are measures of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, and attempt to quantify beyond the generic concept. Though there are disagreements among those from different disciplines (and influenced by different political beliefs about the nature of the good society), these disciplines and international organizations have each offered measures or <a href="/wiki/Economic_indicator" title="Economic indicator">indicators</a> of how to measure the concept.</p>
<p>While sustainability indicators, indices and reporting systems gained growing popularity in both the public and private sectors, their effectiveness in influencing actual policy and practices often remains limited.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrics_and_indices">Metrics and indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Metrics and indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various ways of operationalizing or measuring sustainability have been developed. During the last 10 years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> there has been an expansion of interest in SDI systems, both in industrialized and, albeit to a lesser extent, in <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. SDIs are seen as useful in a wide range of settings, by a wide range of actors: international and intergovernmental bodies; national governments and government departments; economic sectors; administrators of geographic or ecological regions; communities; nongovernmental organizations; and the private sector.</p>
<p>SDI processes are underpinned and driven by the increasing need for improved quality and regularly produced information with better spatial and temporal resolution. Accompanying this need is the requirement, brought in part by the information revolution, to better differentiate between information that matters in any given policy context versus information that is of secondary importance or irrelevant.</p>
<p>A large and still growing number of attempts to create aggregate measures of various aspects of sustainability created a stable of indices that provide a more nuanced perspective on development than economic aggregates such as GDP. Some of the most prominent of these include the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI) of the United Nations Development Programme&#160;(UNDP); the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a> of <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a> and its partner organizations; the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Sustainability_Index" title="Environmental Sustainability Index">Environmental Sustainability Index</a>&#160;(ESI) and the pilot <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a>&#160;(EPI) reported under the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a>&#160;(WEF); or the <a href="/wiki/Genuine_Progress_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Genuine Progress Index">Genuine Progress Index</a>&#160;(GPI) calculated at the national or sub-national level. Parallel to these initiatives, political interest in producing a <a href="/wiki/Green_GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="Green GDP">green GDP</a> that would take at least the cost of pollution and natural capital depletion into account has grown, even if implementation is held back by the reluctance of policymakers and statistical services arising mostly from a concern about conceptual and technical challenges.</p>
<p>At the heart of the debate over different indicators are not only different disciplinary approaches but also different views of <a href="/wiki/Regional_development" title="Regional development">development</a>. Some indicators reflect the ideology of <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> that seek to define and measure progress on whether different countries or cultures agree to accept industrial technologies in their eco-systems.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Other approaches, like those that start from international treaties on cultural rights of indigenous peoples to maintain traditional cultures, measure the ability of those cultures to maintain their traditions within their eco-systems at whatever level of productivity they choose.</p>
<p>The Lempert-Nguyen indicator, devised in 2008 for practitioners, starts with the standards for sustainable development that have been agreed upon by the international community and then looks at whether intergovernmental organizations such as the UNDP and other development actors are applying these principles in their projects and work as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Lempert_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lempert-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In using sustainability indicators, it is important to distinguish between three types of sustainability that are often mentioned in international development:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sustainability of a culture (human system) within its resources and environment;</li>
<li>Sustainability of a specific stream of benefits or productivity (usually just an economic measure); and</li>
<li>Sustainability of a particular institution or project without additional assistance (<a href="/wiki/Institutionalization" class="mw-redirect" title="Institutionalization">institutionalization</a> of an input).</li>
</ul>
<p>The following list is not exhaustive but contains the major points of view:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Daly_Rules.22_approach">"Daly Rules" approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: &quot;Daly Rules&quot; approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>University of Maryland School of Public Policy professor and former Chief Economist for the World Bank Herman E. Daly (working from theory initially developed by Romanian economist <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> and laid out in his 1971 opus "The Entropy Law and the Economic Process") suggests the following three operational rules defining the condition of ecological (thermodynamic) sustainability:</p>
<ol>
<li>Renewable resources such as fish, soil, and groundwater must be used no faster than the rate at which they regenerate.</li>
<li>Nonrenewable resources such as minerals and fossil fuels must be used no faster than renewable substitutes for them can be put into place.</li>
<li>Pollution and wastes must be emitted no faster than natural systems can absorb them, recycle them, or render them harmless.</li>
</ol>
<p>Some commentators have argued that the "Daly Rules", being based on <a href="/wiki/Ecological_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological theory">ecological theory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Thermodynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of Thermodynamics">Laws of Thermodynamics</a>, should perhaps be considered implicit or foundational for the many other systems that are advocated, and are thus the most straightforward system for operationalization of the Bruntland Definition. In this view, the Bruntland Definition and the Daly Rules can be seen as complementary—Bruntland provides the ethical goal of non-depletion of natural capital, Daly details parsimoniously how this ethic is operationalized in physical terms. The system is rationally complete, and in agreement with physical laws. Other definitions may thus be superfluous, or mere glosses on the immutable thermodynamic reality.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are numerous other definitions and systems of operationalization for sustainability, and there has been competition for influence between them, with the unfortunate result that, in the minds of some observers at least, sustainability has no agreed-upon definition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_Step_approach">Natural Step approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural Step approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Natural_Step" title="The Natural Step">The Natural Step</a></div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">Brundtland Commission</a>'s report, one of the first initiatives to bring scientific principles to the assessment of sustainability was by Swedish cancer scientist <a href="/wiki/Karl-Henrik_Rob%C3%A8rt" title="Karl-Henrik Robèrt">Karl-Henrik Robèrt</a>. Robèrt coordinated a consensus process to define and operationalize sustainability. At the core of the process lies a consensus on what Robèrt came to call the natural step framework. The framework is based on a definition of sustainability, described as <a href="/wiki/The_Natural_Step#System_conditions_of_sustainability" title="The Natural Step">the system conditions of sustainability</a> (as derived from <a href="/wiki/System_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="System theory">System theory</a>). In the natural step framework, a sustainable society does not systematically increase concentrations of <i>substances extracted from the Earth's crust</i>, or <i>substances produced by society</i>; that <i>does not degrade the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a></i> and in which people have the <i>capacity to meet their needs worldwide.</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_footprint_approach">Ecological footprint approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecological footprint approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a></div>
<p>Ecological footprint accounting, based on the biological concept of <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>, tracks the amount of land and water area a human population, needed to produce the resources the population consumes and to absorb its waste, under prevailing technology. This amount then is compared to available <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a>, in the world or in that region. The biocapacity represents the area able to regenerate resources and assimilate waste. <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a> publishes every year results for all nations captured in UN statistics.</p>
<p>The algorithms of ecological footprint accounts have been used in combination with the <a href="/wiki/Emergy" title="Emergy">emergy</a> methodology (S. Zhao, Z. Li and W. Li 2005), and a <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_metric_and_indices" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainability metric and indices">sustainability index</a> has been derived from the latter. They have also been combined with a measure of <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>, for instance through the "<a href="/wiki/Happy_Planet_Index" title="Happy Planet Index">Happy Planet Index</a>"&#160;(HPI) calculated for 178 nations (Marks et al., 2006). The Happy Planet Index calculates how many happy life years each country is able to generate per <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectare</a> of ecological footprint.</p>
<p>One of the striking conclusions to emerge from ecological footprint accounting is that it would be necessary to have 4 or 5 back-up planets engaged in nothing but agriculture for all those alive today to live a western lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Footprint analysis is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/I_%3D_PAT" title="I = PAT">I&#160;=&#160;PAT</a> equation that, itself, can be considered a metric.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropological-cultural_approach">Anthropological-cultural approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anthropological-cultural approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though sustainable development has become a concept that biologists and ecologists have measured from an eco-system point of view and that the business community has measured from a perspective of energy and resource efficiencies and consumption, the discipline of <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a> is itself founded on the concept of sustainability of human groups within ecological systems. At the basis of the definition of <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> is whether a human group is able to transmit its values and continue several aspects of that lifestyle for at least three generations. The measurement of culture, by anthropologists, is itself a measure of sustainability and it is also one that has been codified by international agreements and treaties like the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a> of 1992 and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Declaration_on_the_Rights_of_Indigenous_Peoples" title="Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples">Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples</a> to maintain a cultural group's choice of lifestyles within their lands and ecosystems.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Terralingua" title="Terralingua">Terralingua</a>, an organization of anthropologists and linguists working to protect <a href="/w/index.php?title=Biocultural&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Biocultural (page does not exist)">biocultural</a> diversity, with a focus on language, has devised a sert of measures with <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> for measuring the survivability of languages and cultures in given eco-systems.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Lempert–Nguyen indicator of sustainable development, developed in 2008 by David Lempert and Hue Nhu Nguyen, is one that incorporates and integrates these cultural principles with international law.<sup id="cite_ref-Lempert_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lempert-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Circles_of_Sustainability_approach">Circles of Sustainability approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Circles of Sustainability approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of agencies including the <a href="/wiki/UN_Global_Compact" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Global Compact">UN Global Compact</a> Cities Programme, <a href="/wiki/World_Vision" class="mw-redirect" title="World Vision">World Vision</a> and Metropolis have since 2010 begun using the <a href="/wiki/Circles_of_Sustainability" title="Circles of Sustainability">Circles of Sustainability</a> approach that sets up a four-domain framework for choosing appropriate indicators. Rather than designating the indicators that have to be used like most other approaches, it provides a framework to guide decision-making on what indicators are most useful. The framework is arranged around four domains - economics, ecology, politics and culture - which are then subdivided into seven analytically derived sub-domains for each domain. Indicators are linked to each sub-domain. By choosing culture as one of its key domains, the approach takes into account the emphasis of the 'Anthropological' approach (above), but retains a comprehensive sense of sustainability. The approach can be used to map any other sustainability indicator set.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This is foundationally different from the Global Reporting Initiative Index (below) which uses a triple-bottom-line organizing framework, and is most relevant to corporate reporting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Reporting_Initiative_Index">Global Reporting Initiative Index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global Reporting Initiative Index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_Reporting_Initiative" title="Global Reporting Initiative">Global Reporting Initiative</a></div>
<p>In 1997 the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) was started as a multi-stakeholder process and independent institution whose mission has been "to develop and disseminate globally applicable <b>Sustainability Reporting Guidelines</b>". The GRI uses ecological footprint analysis and became independent in 2002. It is an official collaborating centre of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) and during the tenure of <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, it cooperated with the <a href="/wiki/UN_Secretary-General" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Secretary-General">UN Secretary-General</a>'s Global Compact.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy.2C_Emergy_and_Sustainability_Index">Energy, Emergy and Sustainability Index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energy, Emergy and Sustainability Index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1956 Dr. <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Howard T. Odum</a> of the University of Florida coined the term <a href="/wiki/Emergy" title="Emergy">Emergy</a> and devised the accounting system of embodied energy.</p>
<p>In 1997, <a href="/wiki/Systems_ecology" title="Systems ecology">systems ecologists</a> M.T. Brown and S. Ulgiati published their formulation of a quantitative Sustainability Index (SI) as a ratio of the <a href="/wiki/Emergy" title="Emergy">emergy</a> (spelled with an "m", i.e. "<a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a>", not simply "energy") yield ratio (EYR) to the environmental loading ratio (ELR). Brown and Ulgiati also called the sustainability index the "Emergy Sustainability Index" (ESI), "an index that accounts for yield, renewability, and environmental load. It is the incremental emergy yield compared to the environmental load".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Sustainability Index =&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">Emergy Yield Ratio</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">Environmental Loading Ratio</span></span> =&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>EYR</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>ELR</i></span></span></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>NOTE:</b> The numerator is called "e<b>m</b>ergy" and is spelled with an "<b>m</b>". It is an abbreviation of the term, "embodied energy". The numerator is NOT "energy yield ratio", which is a different concept.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Writers like Leone (2005) and Yi et al. have also recently suggested that the emergy sustainability index has significant utility. In particular, Leone notes that while the GRI measures behavior, it fails to calculate supply constraints the emergy methodology aims to calculate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Sustainability_Index">Environmental Sustainability Index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental Sustainability Index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Sustainability_Index" title="Environmental Sustainability Index">Environmental Sustainability Index</a></div>
<p>In 2004, a joint initiative of the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_Environmental_Law_and_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale Center for Environmental Law and Policy">Yale Center for Environmental Law and Policy</a> (YCELP) and the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_International_Earth_Science_Information_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Center for International Earth Science Information Network">Center for International Earth Science Information Network</a> (CIESIN) of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General_Joint_Research_Centre_(European_Commission)" class="mw-redirect" title="Directorate-General Joint Research Centre (European Commission)">Directorate-General Joint Research Centre (European Commission)</a> also attempted to construct an Environmental Sustainability Index&#160;(ESI).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This was formally released in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, Switzerland, at the annual meeting of the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> (WEF) on 28 January 2005. The report on this index made a comparison of the WEF ESI to other sustainability indicators such as the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a> Index. However, there was no mention of the emergy sustainability index.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IISD_Sample_Policy_Framework">IISD Sample Policy Framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: IISD Sample Policy Framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996 the <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Sustainable_Development" title="International Institute for Sustainable Development">International Institute for Sustainable Development</a> (IISD) developed a <i>Sample Policy Framework</i>, which proposed that a sustainability index "...would give decision-makers tools to rate policies and programs against each other" (1996, p.&#160;9). Ravi Jain (2005)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> argued that, "The ability to analyze different alternatives or to assess progress towards sustainability will then depend on establishing measurable entities or metrics used for sustainability."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_dashboard">Sustainability dashboard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sustainability dashboard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.iisd.org/">International Institute for Sustainable Development</a> has produced a "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.iisd.org/cgsdi/dashboard.asp">Dashboard of Sustainability</a>", "a free, non-commercial software package that illustrates the complex relationships among economic, social and environmental issues". This is based on <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.iisd.org/pdf/2005/measure_indicators_sd_way_forward.pdf">Sustainable Development Indicators</a> Prepared for the United Nations Division for Sustainable Development (UN-DSD)DECEMBER 2005.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WBCSD_approach">WBCSD approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: WBCSD approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Business_Council_for_Sustainable_Development" title="World Business Council for Sustainable Development">World Business Council for Sustainable Development</a> (WBCSD), founded in 1995, has formulated the business case for sustainable development and argues that "sustainable development is good for <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> and business is good for sustainable development". This view is also maintained by proponents of the concept of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">industrial ecology</a>. The theory of industrial ecology declares that <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> should be viewed as a series of interlocking man-made ecosystems interfacing with the natural <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">global ecosystem</a>.</p>
<p>According to some economists, it is possible for the concepts of sustainable development and competitiveness to merge if enacted wisely, so that there is not an inevitable trade-off.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This merger is motivated by the following six observations (Hargroves &amp; Smith 2005):</p>
<ol>
<li>Throughout the economy there are widespread untapped potential <a href="/wiki/Resource_productivity" title="Resource productivity">resource productivity</a> improvements to be made to be coupled with effective design.</li>
<li>There has been a significant shift in understanding over the last three decades of what creates lasting competitiveness of a firm.</li>
<li>There is now a critical mass of enabling technologies in eco-innovations that make integrated approaches to sustainable development economically viable.</li>
<li>Since many of the costs of what economists call ‘environmental externalities’ are passed on to governments, in the long-term sustainable development strategies can provide multiple benefits to the tax payer.</li>
<li>There is a growing understanding of the multiple benefits of valuing social and natural capital, for both moral and economic reasons, and including them in measures of national well-being.</li>
<li>There is mounting evidence to show that a transition to a sustainable economy, if done wisely, may not harm economic growth significantly, in fact it could even help it. Recent research by ex-<a href="/wiki/Wuppertal_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Wuppertal Institute">Wuppertal Institute</a> member Joachim Spangenberg, working with neo-classical economists, shows that the transition, if focused on improving resource productivity, leads to higher economic growth than business as usual, while at the same time reducing pressures on the environment and enhancing <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a>.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life-cycle_assessment">Life-cycle assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Life-cycle assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life-cycle assessment</a></div>
<p>Life-cycle assessment is a "composite measure of sustainability."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It analyses the environmental performance of products and services through all phases of their life cycle: extracting and processing raw materials; manufacturing, transportation and distribution; use, re-use, maintenance; recycling, and final disposal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_enterprise_approach">Sustainable enterprise approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sustainable enterprise approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Building on the work of the <a href="/wiki/World_Business_Council_for_Sustainable_Development" title="World Business Council for Sustainable Development">World Business Council for Sustainable Development</a>, businesses began to see the needs of environmental and social systems as opportunities for business development and contribution to stakeholder value. This approach has manifested itself in three key areas of strategic intent: 'sustainable innovation', <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" title="Human development (humanity)">human development</a>, and '<a href="/wiki/Bottom_of_the_pyramid" title="Bottom of the pyramid">bottom of the pyramid</a>' business strategies. Now, as businesses have begun the shift toward sustainable enterprise, many business schools are leading the research and education of the next generation of business leaders. Companies have introduced key development indicators to set targets and track progress on sustainable development. Some key players<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_livelihoods_approach">Sustainable livelihoods approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sustainable livelihoods approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Inclusive_business" title="Inclusive business">Inclusive business</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worldchanging" title="Worldchanging">Worldchanging</a></div>
<p>Another application of the term sustainability has been in the Sustainable Livelihoods Approach, developed from conceptual work by <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a>, and the UK's Institute for Development Studies <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.ids.ac.uk">http://www.ids.ac.uk</a>. This was championed by the UK's <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">Department for International Development</a>(DFID), <a href="/wiki/UNDP" class="mw-redirect" title="UNDP">UNDP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO) as well as <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_for_Assistance_and_Relief_Everywhere" class="mw-redirect" title="Cooperative for Assistance and Relief Everywhere">CARE</a>, <a href="/wiki/OXFAM" class="mw-redirect" title="OXFAM">OXFAM</a> and the African Institute for Community-Driven Development, Khanya-aicdd <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.khanya-aicdd.org">http://www.khanya-aicdd.org</a>. Key concepts include the Sustainable Livelihoods (SL) Framework, a holistic way of understanding livelihoods, the SL principles, as well as six governance issues developed by Khanya-aicdd.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A wide range of information resources on Sustainable Livelihoods Approaches can be found at Livelihoods Connect <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.livelihoods.org">http://www.livelihoods.org</a></p>
<p>Some analysts view this measure with caution because they believe that it has a tendency to take one part of the footprint analysis and I&#160;=&#160;PAT equation (productivity) and to focus on the sustainability of economic returns to an economic sector rather than on the sustainability of the entire population or culture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FAO_types_of_sustainability">FAO types of sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: FAO types of sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United Nations <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a>&#160;(FAO) has identified considerations for technical cooperation that affect three types of sustainability:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Institutional sustainability</b>. Can a strengthened institutional structure continue to deliver the results of technical cooperation to end users? The results may not be sustainable if, for example, the planning authority that depends on the technical cooperation loses access to top management, or is not provided with adequate resources after the technical cooperation ends. Institutional sustainability can also be linked to the concept of social sustainability, which asks how the interventions can be sustained by social structures and institutions;</li>
<li><b>Economic and financial sustainability</b>. Can the results of technical cooperation continue to yield an economic benefit after the technical cooperation is withdrawn? For example, the benefits from the introduction of new crops may not be sustained if the constraints to marketing the crops are not resolved. Similarly, economic, as distinct from financial, sustainability may be at risk if the end users continue to depend on heavily <a href="/wiki/Subsidized" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsidized">subsidized</a> activities and inputs.</li>
<li><b>Ecological sustainability</b>. Are the benefits to be generated by the technical cooperation likely to lead to a deterioration in the physical environment, thus indirectly contributing to a fall in production, or well-being of the groups targeted and their society?</li>
</ul>
<p>Some ecologists have emphasised a fourth type of sustainability:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Energetic sustainability</b>. This type of sustainability is often concerned with the production of energy and mineral resources. Some researchers have pointed to trends they say document the limits of production. See <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_peak" class="mw-redirect" title="Hubbert peak">Hubbert peak</a> for example.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Development_sustainability.22_approaches">"Development sustainability" approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_metrics_and_indices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: &quot;Development sustainability&quot; approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sustainability is relevant to international development projects. One definition of development sustainability is "the continuation of benefits after major assistance from the donor has been completed" (Australian Agency for International Development 2000). Ensuring that development projects are sustainable can reduce the likelihood of them collapsing after they have just finished; it also reduces the financial cost of development projects and the subsequent social problems, such as dependence of the stakeholders on external donors and their resources. All development assistance, apart from temporary emergency and humanitarian relief efforts, should be designed and implemented with the aim of achieving sustainable benefits. There are ten key factors that influence development sustainability.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Participation and ownership. Get the stakeholders (men and women) to genuinely participate in design and implementation. Build on their initiatives and demands. Get them to monitor the project and periodically evaluate it for results.</li>
<li>Capacity building and training. Training stakeholders to take over should begin from the start of any project and continue throughout. The right approach should both motivate and transfer skills to people.</li>
<li>Government policies. Development projects should be aligned with local government policies.</li>
<li>Financial. In some countries and sectors, financial sustainability is difficult in the medium term. Training in local fundraising is a possibility, as is identifying links with the private sector, charging for use, and encouraging policy reforms.</li>
<li>Management and organization. Activities that integrate with or add to local structures may have better prospects for sustainability than those that establish new or parallel structures.</li>
<li>Social, gender and culture. The introduction of new ideas, technologies and skills requires an understanding of local decision-making systems, gender divisions and cultural preferences.</li>
<li>Technology. All outside equipment must be selected with careful consideration given to the local finance available for maintenance and replacement. Cultural acceptability and the local capacity to maintain equipment and buy spare parts are vital.</li>
<li>Environment. Poor rural communities that depend on natural resources should be involved in identifying and managing environmental risks. Urban communities should identify and manage waste disposal and pollution risks.</li>
<li>External political and economic factors. In a weak economy, projects should not be too complicated, ambitious or expensive.</li>
<li>Realistic duration. A short project may be inadequate for solving entrenched problems in a sustainable way, particularly when behavioural and institutional changes are intended. A long project, may on the other hand, promote dependence.</li>
</ol>
<p>The definition of sustainability as "the continuation of benefits after major assistance from the donor has been completed" (Australian Agency for International Development 2000) is echoed by other definitions (World Bank, USAID). The concept has however evolved as it has become of interest to non grant-making institutions. Sustainability in development refers to <i>processes</i> and <i>relative</i> increases in local capacity and performance while foreign assistance decreases or shifts (not necessarily disappears). The objective of sustainable development is open to various interpretations.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
