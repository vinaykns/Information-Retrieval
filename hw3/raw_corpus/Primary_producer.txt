<p><b>Primary producers</b> are organisms in an ecosystem that produce <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> from inorganic compounds (<a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a>). In almost all cases these are photosynthetically active organisms (plants, <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> and a number of other unicellular organisms; see article on <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>). However, there are examples of <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (unicellular organisms) that produce biomass from the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of inorganic chemical compounds (<a href="/wiki/Chemoautotroph" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemoautotroph">chemoautotrophs</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> in the deep ocean.</p>
<p>Fungi and other organisms that gain their biomass from oxidizing organic materials are called <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposers</a> and are not primary producers. However, <a href="/wiki/Lichens" class="mw-redirect" title="Lichens">lichens</a> located in tundra climates are an exceptional example of a primary producer that, by mutualistic symbiosis, combine photosynthesis by algae (or additionally nitrogen fixation by cyanobacteria) with the protection of a decomposer fungus</p>
