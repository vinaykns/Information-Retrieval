			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Vegetable</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>In everyday usage, a <b>vegetable</b> is any part of a plant that is consumed by humans as food as part of a meal. The term <i>vegetable</i> is somewhat arbitrary, and largely defined through culinary and cultural tradition. It normally excludes other food derived from plants such as <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> grains, but includes seeds such as <a href="/wiki/Pulse_(legume)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulse (legume)">pulses</a>. The original meaning of the word <i>vegetable</i>, still used in biology, was to describe all types of plant, as in the terms "vegetable kingdom" and "vegetable matter".</p>
<p>Originally, vegetables were collected from the wild by <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a> and entered cultivation in several parts of the world, probably during the period 10,000 BC to 7,000 BC, when a new <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">agricultural way of life</a> developed. At first, plants which grew locally would have been cultivated, but as time went on, trade brought exotic crops from elsewhere to add to domestic types. Nowadays, most vegetables are grown all over the world as climate permits, and crops may be cultivated in protected environments in less suitable locations. <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_China" title="Agriculture in China">China</a> is the largest producer of vegetables and global trade in agricultural products allows consumers to purchase vegetables grown in faraway countries. The scale of production varies from <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence farmers</a> supplying the needs of their family for food, to <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusinesses</a> with vast acreages of single-product crops. Depending on the type of vegetable concerned, harvesting the crop is followed by grading, storing, processing, and marketing.</p>
<p>Vegetables can be eaten either raw or cooked and play an important role in human nutrition, being mostly low in fat and carbohydrates, but high in vitamins, minerals and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>. Many nutritionists encourage people to consume plenty of fruit and vegetables, five or more portions a day often being recommended.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg/220px-Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg/330px-Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg/440px-Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vegetable_garden_at_Ham_House_Estate_-_geograph.org.uk_-_4530.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Domestic <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_garden" title="Kitchen garden">vegetable garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vegetable" class="extiw" title="wikt:vegetable">vegetable</a></i> was first recorded in English in the early 15th century. It comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Online_Etymology_Dictionary_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Online_Etymology_Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> and was originally applied to all plants; the word is still used in this sense in biological contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-swedenborg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swedenborg-2">[2]</a></sup> It derives from <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>vegetabilis</i> "growing, flourishing" (i.e. of a plant), a semantic change from a <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> meaning "to be enlivening, quickening".<sup id="cite_ref-Online_Etymology_Dictionary_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Online_Etymology_Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The meaning of "vegetable" as a "plant grown for food" was not established until the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1767, the word was specifically used to mean a "plant cultivated for food, an edible herb or root". The year 1955 saw the first use of the shortened, slang term "veggie".<sup id="cite_ref-Online_Etymology_Dictionary2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Online_Etymology_Dictionary2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>, the word <i>vegetable</i> is used in scientific and technical contexts with a different and much broader meaning, namely of "related to plants" in general, edible or not — as in <i>vegetable matter</i>, <i>vegetable kingdom</i>, <i>vegetable origin</i>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-swedenborg_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swedenborg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/220px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/330px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/440px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png 2x" data-file-width="1458" data-file-height="1068" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Venn_diagram" title="Venn diagram">Venn diagram</a> shows the overlap in the terminology of "vegetables" in a culinary sense and "fruits" in the botanical sense.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The exact definition of "vegetable" may vary simply because of the many parts of a plant consumed as food worldwide&#160;– roots, tubers, bulbs, <a href="/wiki/Corm" title="Corm">corms</a>, stems, leaf stems, leaf sheaths, leaves, buds, flowers, fruits, and seeds. The broadest definition is the word's use adjectivally to mean "matter of plant origin" to distinguish it from "animal", meaning "matter of animal origin". More specifically, a vegetable may be defined as "any plant, part of which is used for food",<sup id="cite_ref-Dictionary.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.com-5">[5]</a></sup> a secondary meaning then being "the edible part of such a plant".<sup id="cite_ref-Dictionary.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.com-5">[5]</a></sup> A more precise definition is "any plant part consumed for food that is not a fruit or seed, but including mature fruits that are eaten as part of a main meal".<sup id="cite_ref-Sinha_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinha-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iarc_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iarc-7">[7]</a></sup> Falling outside these definitions are edible fungi (such as <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushrooms</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Edible_seaweed" title="Edible seaweed">edible seaweed</a> which, although not parts of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, are often treated as vegetables.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In everyday language, the words "<a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>" and "vegetable" are mutually exclusive. "Fruit" has a precise <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanical</a> meaning, being a part that developed from the <a href="/wiki/Ovary_(botany)" title="Ovary (botany)">ovary</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a>. This is considerably different from the word's culinary meaning. While <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plum" title="Plum">plums</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a> are "fruit" in both senses, many items commonly called "vegetables", such as <a href="/wiki/Eggplant" title="Eggplant">eggplants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">bell peppers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, are botanically fruits. The question of whether the tomato is a fruit or a vegetable found its way into the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">United States Supreme Court</a> in 1893. The court ruled unanimously in <i><a href="/wiki/Nix_v._Hedden" title="Nix v. Hedden">Nix v. Hedden</a></i> that a tomato is correctly identified as, and thus taxed as, a vegetable, for the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Tariff_of_1883" title="Tariff of 1883">Tariff of 1883</a> on imported produce. The court did acknowledge, however, that, botanically speaking, a tomato is a fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Before the advent of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, humans were <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>. They foraged for edible fruit, nuts, stems, leaves, corms, and tubers, <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenged</a> for dead animals and <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunted</a> living ones for food.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">Forest gardening</a> in a tropical jungle clearing is thought to be the first example of agriculture; useful plant species were identified and encouraged to grow while undesirable species were removed. Plant breeding through the selection of strains with desirable traits such as large fruit and vigorous growth soon followed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> While the first evidence for the domestication of grasses such as wheat and barley has been found in the <a href="/wiki/Fertile_Crescent" title="Fertile Crescent">Fertile Crescent</a> in the Middle East, it is likely that various peoples around the world started growing crops in the period 10,000 BC to 7,000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">Subsistence agriculture</a> continues to this day, with many rural farmers in Africa, Asia, South America, and elsewhere using their plots of land to produce enough food for their families, while any surplus produce is used for exchange for other goods.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout recorded history, the rich have been able to afford a varied diet including meat, vegetables and fruit, but for poor people, meat was a luxury and the food they ate was very dull, typically comprising mainly some <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staple</a> product made from rice, rye, barley, wheat, millet or maize. The addition of vegetable matter provided some variety to the diet. The staple diet of the <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztecs</a> in Central America was <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> and they cultivated <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capsicum" title="Capsicum">peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (plant)">squashes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amaranth" title="Amaranth">amaranth</a> seeds to supplement their <a href="/wiki/Tortilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Tortilla">tortillas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porridge" title="Porridge">porridge</a>. In Peru, the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Incas</a> subsisted on maize in the lowlands and potatoes at higher altitudes. They also used seeds from <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>, supplementing their diet with peppers, tomatoes, and avocados.<sup id="cite_ref-Lambert_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/History_of_China#Ancient_China" title="History of China">Ancient China</a>, rice was the staple crop in the south and wheat in the north, the latter made into <a href="/wiki/Dumpling" title="Dumpling">dumplings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noodle" title="Noodle">noodles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pancake" title="Pancake">pancakes</a>. Vegetables used to accompany these included <a href="/wiki/Yam_(vegetable)" title="Yam (vegetable)">yams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broad_bean" class="mw-redirect" title="Broad bean">broad beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turnip" title="Turnip">turnips</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_onion" class="mw-redirect" title="Spring onion">spring onions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>. The diet of the ancient Egyptians was based on <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>, often contaminated with sand which wore away their teeth. Meat was a luxury but fish was more plentiful. These were accompanied by a range of vegetables including marrows, broad beans, lentils, onions, leeks, garlic, radishes, and lettuces.<sup id="cite_ref-Lambert_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The mainstay of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> diet was bread, and this was accompanied by goat's cheese, olives, figs, fish, and occasionally meat. The vegetables grown included onions, garlic, cabbages, melons, and lentils.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Rome</a>, a thick porridge was made of <a href="/wiki/Emmer_wheat" class="mw-redirect" title="Emmer wheat">emmer wheat</a> or beans, accompanied by green vegetables but little meat, and fish was not esteemed. The Romans grew broad beans, peas, onions and turnips and ate the leaves of <a href="/wiki/Beetroot" title="Beetroot">beets</a> rather than their roots.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition_and_health">Nutrition and health</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg/220px-Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg/330px-Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg/440px-Ipomoea_stir_fry.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg/220px-Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg/330px-Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg/440px-Vegetable_Cart_in_Guntur.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>

<p>Vegetables play an important role in human nutrition. Most are low in fat and calories but are bulky and filling.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> They supply dietary fiber and are important sources of essential vitamins, minerals, and trace elements. Particularly important are the antioxidant vitamins <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">C</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_E" title="Vitamin E">E</a>. When vegetables are included in the diet, there is found to be a reduction in the incidence of cancer, stroke, cardiovascular disease, and other chronic ailments.<sup id="cite_ref-Kerala_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerala-21">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Terry2011_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terry2011-22">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> Research has shown that, compared with individuals who eat less than three servings of fruits and vegetables each day, those that eat more than five servings have an approximately twenty percent lower risk of developing coronary heart disease or stroke.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-24">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutritional</a> content of vegetables varies considerably; some contain useful amounts of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> though generally they contain little <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Li2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li2008-25">[23]</a></sup> and varying proportions of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B6" title="Vitamin B6">vitamin B<sub>6</sub></a>; <a href="/wiki/Provitamin" title="Provitamin">provitamins</a>; <a href="/wiki/Dietary_mineral" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary mineral">dietary minerals</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.</p>
<p>However, vegetables often also contain <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">toxins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antinutrient" title="Antinutrient">antinutrients</a> which interfere with the absorption of nutrients. These include <a href="/wiki/Solanine" title="Solanine">α-solanine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaconine" title="Chaconine">α-chaconine</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_inhibitor" title="Enzyme inhibitor">enzyme inhibitors</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Cholinesterase" title="Cholinesterase">cholinesterase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">protease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, etc.), <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linamarin" title="Linamarin">cyanide precursors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>, and others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These toxins are natural defenses, used to ward off the insects, predators and fungi that might attack the plant. Some beans contain <a href="/wiki/Phytohaemagglutinin" title="Phytohaemagglutinin">phytohaemagglutinin</a>, and cassava roots contain <a href="/wiki/Cyanogenic_glycoside" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyanogenic glycoside">cyanogenic glycoside</a> as do <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_shoot" title="Bamboo shoot">bamboo shoots</a>. These toxins can be deactivated by adequate cooking. Green potatoes contain <a href="/wiki/Glycoalkaloid" title="Glycoalkaloid">glycoalkaloids</a> and should be avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fruit and vegetables, particularly leafy vegetables, have been implicated in nearly half the <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastrointestinal infections</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Norovirus" title="Norovirus">norovirus</a> in the United States. These foods are commonly eaten raw and may become contaminated during their preparation by an infected food handler. Hygiene is important when handling foods to be eaten raw, and such products need to be properly cleaned, handled, and stored to limit contamination.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_recommendations">Dietary recommendations</span></h3>
<p>The USDA Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommends consuming five to nine servings of fruit and vegetables daily.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> The total amount consumed will vary according to age and gender, and is determined based upon the standard portion sizes typically consumed, as well as general nutritional content. Potatoes are not included in the count as they are mainly providers of starch. For most vegetables and vegetable juices, one serving is half of a cup and can be eaten raw or cooked. For leafy greens, such as <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinach" title="Spinach">spinach</a>, a single serving is typically a full cup.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> A variety of products should be chosen as no single fruit or vegetable provides all the nutrients needed for health.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>International dietary guidelines are similar to the ones established by the USDA. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, for example, recommends the consumption of five to six servings of vegetables daily.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> French recommendations provide similar guidelines and set the daily goal at five servings.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> In India, the daily recommendation for adults is 275 grams (9.7&#160;oz) of vegetables per day.<sup id="cite_ref-Kerala_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerala-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_garden" title="Kitchen garden">Kitchen garden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_farming" title="Vegetable farming">Vegetable farming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_(10672860713).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg/220px-AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg/330px-AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg/440px-AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_%2810672860713%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AUSAID_SOUTH_AFRICA_(10672860713).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Growing vegetables in South Africa</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Vegetables have been part of the human diet from <a href="/wiki/Time_immemorial" title="Time immemorial">time immemorial</a>. Some are staple foods but most are accessory foodstuffs, adding variety to meals with their unique flavors and at the same time, adding nutrients necessary for health. Some vegetables are <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennials</a> but most are <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annuals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biennial_plant" title="Biennial plant">biennials</a>, usually harvested within a year of sowing or planting. Whatever system is used for growing crops, cultivation follows a similar pattern; preparation of the soil by loosening it, removing or burying weeds, and adding organic manures or fertilisers; sowing seeds or planting young plants; tending the crop while it grows to reduce weed competition, control pests, and provide sufficient water; harvesting the crop when it is ready; sorting, storing, and marketing the crop or eating it fresh from the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-RHS_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RHS-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different soil types suit different crops, but in general in temperate climates, sandy soils dry out fast but warm up quickly in the spring and are suitable for early crops, while heavy clays retain moisture better and are more suitable for late season crops. The growing season can be lengthened by the use of <a href="/wiki/Horticultural_fleece" title="Horticultural fleece">fleece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloche_(agriculture)" title="Cloche (agriculture)">cloches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic_mulch" title="Plastic mulch">plastic mulch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polytunnel" title="Polytunnel">polytunnels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RHS_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RHS-33">[31]</a></sup> In hotter regions, the production of vegetables is constrained by the climate, especially the pattern of rainfall, while in temperate zones, it is constrained by the temperature and day length.<sup id="cite_ref-Field_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Field-34">[32]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg/220px-John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg/330px-John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg/440px-John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2390" data-file-height="1477" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_tractor_between_cabbage_rows.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Weeding cabbages in Colorado, US</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On a domestic scale, the spade, fork, and hoe are the tools of choice while on commercial farms a range of mechanical equipment is available. Besides tractors, these include <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">ploughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harrow_(tool)" title="Harrow (tool)">harrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drill" title="Drill">drills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transplanter" title="Transplanter">transplanters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultivator" title="Cultivator">cultivators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> equipment, and <a href="/wiki/Forage_harvester" title="Forage harvester">harvesters</a>. New techniques are changing the cultivation procedures involved in growing vegetables with computer monitoring systems, <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> locators, and self-steer programs for driverless machines giving economic benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-Field_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Field-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting">Harvesting</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg/220px-Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg/330px-Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg/440px-Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvesting_-_geograph.org.uk_-_290336.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Harvesting beetroot in the United Kingdom</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When a vegetable is harvested, it is cut off from its source of water and nourishment. It continues to transpire and loses moisture as it does so, a process most noticeable in the wilting of green leafy crops. Harvesting root vegetables when they are fully mature improves their storage life, but alternatively, these root crops can be left in the ground and harvested over an extended period. The harvesting process should seek to minimise damage and bruising to the crop. Onions and garlic can be dried for a few days in the field and root crops such as potatoes benefit from a short maturation period in warm, moist surroundings, during which time wounds heal and the skin thickens up and hardens. Before marketing or storage, grading needs to be done to remove damaged goods and select produce according to its quality, size, ripeness, and color.<sup id="cite_ref-Dixie_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dixie-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span></h3>
<p>All vegetables benefit from proper post harvest care. A large proportion of vegetables and perishable foods are lost after harvest during the storage period. These losses may be as high as thirty to fifty percent in developing countries where adequate cold storage facilities are not available. The main causes of loss include spoilage caused by moisture, moulds, micro-organisms, and vermin.<sup id="cite_ref-PrakashGarg_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrakashGarg-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerooide_aardappels.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gerooide_aardappels.JPG/220px-Gerooide_aardappels.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gerooide_aardappels.JPG/330px-Gerooide_aardappels.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gerooide_aardappels.JPG/440px-Gerooide_aardappels.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4272" data-file-height="2848" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerooide_aardappels.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Temporary storage of potatoes in the Netherlands</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Storage can be short-term or long-term. Most vegetables are perishable and short-term storage for a few days provides flexibility in marketing. During storage, leafy vegetables lose <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> in them degrades rapidly. A few products such as potatoes and onions have better keeping qualities and can be sold when higher prices may be available, and by extending the marketing season, a greater total volume of crop can be sold. If refrigerated storage is not available, the priority for most crops is to store high-quality produce, to maintain a high humidity level, and to keep the produce in the shade.<sup id="cite_ref-Dixie_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dixie-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Proper post-harvest storage aimed at extending and ensuring shelf life is best effected by efficient <a href="/wiki/Cold_chain" title="Cold chain">cold chain</a> application.<sup id="cite_ref-pxk_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pxk-37">[35]</a></sup> Cold storage is particularly useful for vegetables such as cauliflower, eggplant, lettuce, radish, spinach, potatoes, and tomatoes, the optimum temperature depending on the type of produce. There are temperature-controlling technologies that do not require the use of electricity such as evaporative cooling.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinha_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinha-6">[6]</a></sup> Storage of fruit and vegetables in controlled atmospheres with high levels of carbon dioxide or high oxygen levels can inhibit microbial growth and extend storage life.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The irradiation of vegetables and other agricultural produce by <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> can be used to preserve it from both microbial infection and insect damage, as well as from physical deterioration. It can extend the storage life of food without noticeably changing its properties.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span></h3>
<p>The objective of preserving vegetables is to extend their availability for consumption or marketing purposes. The aim is to harvest the food at its maximum state of palatability and nutritional value, and preserve these qualities for an extended period. The main causes of deterioration in vegetables after they are gathered are the actions of naturally-occurring <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and the spoilage caused by <a href="/wiki/Micro-organism" class="mw-redirect" title="Micro-organism">micro-organisms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MAFF_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAFF-40">[38]</a></sup> Canning and freezing are the most commonly used techniques, and vegetables preserved by these methods are generally similar in nutritional value to comparable fresh products with regards to <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_E" title="Vitamin E">vitamin E</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dietary_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary element">minerals</a>. and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg/220px-Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg/330px-Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg/440px-Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg 2x" data-file-width="929" data-file-height="721" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bean_field_Seabrook_Farm1a33785v.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bean field and canning factory, New Jersey, US</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">Canning</a> is a process during which the enzymes in vegetables are deactivated and the micro-organisms present killed by heat. The sealed can excludes air from the foodstuff to prevent subsequent deterioration. The lowest necessary heat and the minimum processing time are used in order to prevent the mechanical breakdown of the product and to preserve the flavor as far as is possible. The can is then able to be stored at ambient temperatures for a long period.<sup id="cite_ref-MAFF_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAFF-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frozen_food" title="Frozen food">Freezing</a> vegetables and maintaining their temperature at below −10&#160;°C (14&#160;°F) will prevent their spoilage for a short period, whereas a temperature of −18&#160;°C (0&#160;°F) is required for longer-term storage. The enzyme action will merely be inhibited, and <a href="/wiki/Blanching_(cooking)" title="Blanching (cooking)">blanching</a> of suitably sized prepared vegetables before freezing mitigates this and prevents off-flavors developing. Not all micro-organisms will be killed at these temperatures and after thawing the vegetables should be used promptly because otherwise, any microbes present may proliferate.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup></p>

Sun-drying tomatoes in Greece</div>

<p>Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Drying_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drying (food)">sun drying</a> has been used for some products such as tomatoes, mushrooms, and beans, spreading the produce on racks and turning the crop at intervals. This method suffers from several disadvantages including lack of control over drying rates, spoilage when drying is slow, contamination by dirt, wetting by rain, and attack by rodents, birds, and insects. These disadvantages can be alleviated by using <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar powered</a> driers.<sup id="cite_ref-PrakashGarg_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrakashGarg-36">[34]</a></sup> The dried produce must be prevented from reabsorbing moisture during storage.<sup id="cite_ref-MAFF_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAFF-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>High levels of both <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> can preserve food by preventing micro-organisms from growing. Green beans can be salted by layering the pods with salt, but this method of preservation is unsuited to most vegetables. Marrows, beetroot, carrot, and some other vegetables can be boiled with sugar to create jams.<sup id="cite_ref-MAFF_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAFF-40">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">Vinegar</a> is widely used in food preservation; a sufficient concentration of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> prevents the development of destructive micro-organisms, a fact made use of in the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chutney" title="Chutney">chutneys</a> and relishes.<sup id="cite_ref-MAFF_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAFF-40">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">Fermentation</a> is another method of preserving vegetables for later use. <a href="/wiki/Sauerkraut" title="Sauerkraut">Sauerkraut</a> is made from chopped cabbage and relies on <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> bacteria which produce compounds that are inhibitory to the growth of other micro-organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinha_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinha-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards">Standards</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) sets international standards to ensure that products and services are safe, reliable, and of good quality. There are a number of ISO standards regarding fruits and vegetables.<sup id="cite_ref-fvdp_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvdp-44">[42]</a></sup> ISO 1991-1:1982 lists the botanical names of sixty-one species of plants used as vegetables along with the common names of the vegetables in English, French, and Russian.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> ISO 67.080.20 covers the storage and transport of vegetables and their derived products.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup></p>
