			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecosystem services</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>Humankind benefits in a multitude of ways from <a href="/wiki/Ecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>. Collectively, these benefits are becoming known as <b>ecosystem services</b>. Ecosystem services are regularly involved in the provisioning of clean <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a> of wastes. While scientists and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmentalists">environmentalists</a> have discussed ecosystem services implicitly for decades, the ecosystem services concept itself was popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> (MA) in the early 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005-1">[1]</a></sup> This grouped ecosystem services into four broad categories: <i>provisioning</i>, such as the production of food and water; <i>regulating</i>, such as the control of climate and disease; <i>supporting</i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrient cycles">nutrient cycles</a> and crop <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>; and <i>cultural</i>, such as spiritual and recreational benefits. To help inform <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-makers</a>, many ecosystem services are being assigned economic values.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the notion of human dependence on Earth’s ecosystems reaches to the start of <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">homo sapiens</a>’ existence, the term 'natural capital' was first coined by <a href="/wiki/E._F._Schumacher" title="E. F. Schumacher">E.F. Schumacher</a> in 1973 in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small is Beautiful</a></i> <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><i>.</i> Recognition of how ecosystems could provide more complex services to mankind date back to at least <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> (c. 400 BC) who understood that <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> could lead to soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and the drying of springs.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily1997_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily1997-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Modern ideas of ecosystem services probably began with Marsh in 1864 when he challenged the idea that Earth’s natural resources are unbounded by pointing out changes in <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">soil fertility</a> in the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was not until the late 1940s that three key authors – <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn,_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn, Jr">Henry Fairfield Osborn, Jr</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Vogt" title="William Vogt">William Vogt</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> – promoted recognition of human dependence on the environment.</p>
<p>In 1956, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sears" title="Paul Sears">Paul Sears</a> drew attention to the critical role of the ecosystem in processing wastes and recycling nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1970, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich" title="Paul Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a> and Rosa Weigert called attention to "ecological systems" in their environmental science textbook<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and "the most subtle and dangerous threat to man’s existence... the potential destruction, by man’s own activities, of those ecological systems upon which the very existence of the human species depends".</p>
<p>The term "<b>environmental services</b>" was introduced in a 1970 report of the <i>Study of Critical Environmental Problems</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which listed services including insect <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> regulation and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> control. In following years, variations of the term were used, but eventually 'ecosystem services' became the standard in scientific literature.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ecosystem services concept has continued to expand and includes <a href="/wiki/Socio-economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Socio-economic">socio-economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation ethic">conservation</a> objectives, which are discussed below. A history of the concepts and terminology of ecosystem services as of 1997, can be found in Daily's book "Nature’s Services: Societal Dependence on Natural Ecosystems".<sup id="cite_ref-Daily1997_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily1997-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Per the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> (MA), ecosystem services are "the benefits people obtain from ecosystems." The MA also delineated the four categories of ecosystem services—supporting, provisioning, regulating and cultural—discussed below.</p>
<p>By 2010, there had evolved various working definitions and descriptions of ecosystem services in the literature.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> To prevent double counting in ecosystem services audits, for instance, <a href="/wiki/The_Economics_of_Ecosystems_and_Biodiversity" title="The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity">The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity</a> (TEEB) replaced "Supporting Services" in the MA with "Habitat Services" and "ecosystem functions," defined as "a subset of the interactions between ecosystem structure and processes that underpin the capacity of an ecosystem to provide goods and services."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">The Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA)</a> report 2005 defines <i>Ecosystem services</i> as benefits people obtain from ecosystems and distinguishes four categories of ecosystem services, where the so-called supporting services are regarded as the basis for the services of the other three categories.<sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005-1">[1]</a></sup> The following lists represent the definition and samples of each according to the MA:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_services">Supporting services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Supporting services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecosystem services "that are necessary for the production of all other ecosystem services".<sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These include services such as nutrient recycling, primary production and <a href="/wiki/Soil_formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil formation">soil formation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> These services make it possible for the ecosystems to provide services such as food supply, flood regulation and water purification.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisioning_services">Provisioning services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Provisioning services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Products obtained from ecosystems" <sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>food (including <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game</a>), crops, wild foods, and <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a></li>
<li>raw materials (including lumber, skins, fuel wood, organic matter, fodder, and fertilizer)</li>
<li>genetic resources (including crop improvement genes, and health care)</li>
<li>water</li>
<li>minerals (including <a href="/wiki/Diatomite" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatomite">diatomite</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Medication" class="mw-redirect" title="Medication">medicinal resources</a> (including pharmaceuticals, chemical models, and test and assay organisms)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biomass fuels</a>)</li>
<li>ornamental resources (including fashion, handicraft, jewelry, pets, worship, decoration and souvenirs like furs, feathers, ivory, orchids, butterflies, aquarium fish, shells, etc.)</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_services">Cultural services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Nonmaterial benefits people obtain from ecosystems through spiritual enrichment, cognitive development, reflection, recreation, and aesthetic experiences" <sup id="cite_ref-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment_2005:_p.40-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>cultural (including use of nature as motif in books, film, painting, folklore, national symbols, architect, advertising, etc.)</li>
<li>spiritual and historical (including use of nature for religious or heritage value or natural)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreational</a> experiences (including <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a>, outdoor sports, and recreation)</li>
<li>science and education (including use of natural systems for school excursions, and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_discovery" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific discovery">scientific discovery</a>)</li>
<li>Therapeutic (including Ecotherapy, social forestry and animal assisted therapy)</li>
</ul>
<p>There is discussion as to how the concept of <b>cultural ecosystem services</b> can be operationalized. A good review of approaches in landscape aesthetics, cultural heritage, outdoor recreation, and spiritual significance to define and assess cultural values of our environment so that they fit into the ecosystem services approach is given by Daniel et al.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> who vote for models that explicitly link ecological structures and functions with cultural values and benefits. There also is a fundamental <b>critique</b> of the concept of cultural ecosystem services that builds on three arguments:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Pivotal cultural values attaching to the natural/cultivated environment rely on an area’s unique character that cannot be addressed by methods that use universal scientific parameters to determine ecological structures and functions.</li>
<li>If a natural/cultivated environment has symbolic meanings and cultural values the object of these values are not ecosystems but shaped lifeworldly phenomena like mountains, lakes, forests, and, mainly, symbolic landscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Those cultural values do result not from properties produced by ecosystems but are the product of a specific way of seeing within the given cultural framework of symbolic experience.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following examples illustrate the relationships between humans and natural ecosystems through the services derived from them:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, where the quality of drinking water had fallen below standards required by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)</a>, authorities opted to restore the polluted <a href="/wiki/Catskill_Park" title="Catskill Park">Catskill Watershed</a> that had previously provided the city with the ecosystem service of <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a>. Once the input of sewage and pesticides to the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> area was reduced, natural <a href="/wiki/Abiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> processes such as soil <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a> of chemicals, together with biotic recycling via <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> <a href="/wiki/Microorganisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> improved to levels that met government standards. The cost of this investment in <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> was estimated between $1–1.5 billion, which contrasted dramatically with the estimated $6–8 billion cost of constructing a <a href="/wiki/Water_filtration" class="mw-redirect" title="Water filtration">water filtration</a> plant plus the $300 million annual running costs.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">Pollination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">bees</a> is required for 15-30% of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Food production">food production</a>; most large-scale farmers import non-native honey <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">bees</a> to provide this service. One study <sup id="cite_ref-Kremen_2005_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kremen_2005-22">[22]</a></sup> reports that in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California’s</a> agricultural region, it was found that <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">wild bees</a> alone could provide partial or complete <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> services or enhance the services provided by <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">honey bees</a> through behavioral interactions. However, <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensified agricultural practices</a> can quickly erode <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> services through the loss of species and those remaining are unable to compensate for the difference. The results of this study also indicate that the proportion of <a href="/wiki/Chaparral" title="Chaparral">chaparral</a> and <a href="/wiki/California_oak_woodland" title="California oak woodland">oak-woodland habitat</a> available for <a href="/wiki/Bees" class="mw-redirect" title="Bees">wild bees</a> within 1–2&#160;km of a <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> can strongly stabilize and enhance the provision of pollination services, thereby providing a potential insurance policy for farmers of this region.</li>
<li>In watersheds of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Yangtze River">Yangtze River</a> (<a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>), <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">spatial models</a> for water flow through different forest habitats were created to determine potential contributions for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> in the region. By quantifying the relative value of ecological parameters (vegetation-soil-slope complexes), researchers were able to estimate the annual economic benefit of maintaining forests in the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> for power services to be 2.2 times that if it were harvested once for <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the 1980s, mineral water company Vittel (now a brand of Nestlé Waters) faced a critical problem. Nitrates and pesticides were entering the company’s springs in northeastern France. Local farmers had intensified agricultural practices and cleared native vegetation that previously had filtered water before it seeped into the aquifer used by Vittel. This contamination threatened the company’s right to use the “natural mineral water” label under French law.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In response to this business risk, Vittel developed an incentive package for farmers to improve their agricultural practices and consequently reduce water pollution that had affected Vittel's product. For example, Vittel provided subsidies and free technical assistance to farmers in exchange for farmers' agreement to enhance pasture management, reforest catchments, and reduce the use of agrochemicals. This is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Payment_for_ecosystem_services" title="Payment for ecosystem services">Payment for ecosystem services</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>It was counted that to plant 15 000 ha new woodland in UK, if we consider only the value of timber, it would cost £ 79 000 000 which is more than the benefit of £ 65 000 000. If, however, we include all other benefits the trees in lowland could provide (like soil stabilization, wind deflection, recreation wind defense, food production, air purification, carbon storage, wildlife habitat, fuel production, cooling, flood prevention), the costs will increase due to displacing the profitable farmland (would be around £ 231 000 000) but will be overweight by benefits of £ 546 000 000.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Europe, various projects are implemented in order to define the values of concrete ecosystems and to implement this concept into decision making process. For example, "LIFE Viva grass" project aims to due this with grasslands in Baltics.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Understanding of ecosystem services requires a strong foundation in <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, which describes the underlying principles and interactions of organisms and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. Since the scales at which these entities interact can vary from <a href="/wiki/Microbes" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbes">microbes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Landscapes" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscapes">landscapes</a>, milliseconds to millions of years, one of the greatest remaining challenges is the descriptive characterization of energy and material flow between them. For example, the area of a forest floor, the <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> upon it, the microorganisms in the soil and characteristics of the soil itself will all contribute to the abilities of that forest for providing ecosystem services like carbon sequestration, water purification, and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> prevention to other areas within the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a>. Note that it is often possible for multiple services to be bundled together and when benefits of targeted objectives are secured, there may also be ancillary benefits – the same forest may provide <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> for other organisms as well as human recreation, which are also ecosystem services.</p>
<p>The complexity of Earth’s ecosystems poses a challenge for scientists as they try to understand how relationships are interwoven among organisms, processes and their surroundings. As it relates to human ecology, a suggested research agenda <sup id="cite_ref-Kremen_2005_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kremen_2005-22">[22]</a></sup> for the study of ecosystem services includes the following steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>identification of <i>ecosystem service providers</i> (<i>ESP</i>s) – <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> or populations that provide specific ecosystem services – and characterization of their functional roles and relationships;</li>
<li>determination of community structure aspects that influence how ESPs function in their <a href="/wiki/Natural_landscape" title="Natural landscape">natural landscape</a>, such as compensatory responses that stabilize function and non-random extinction sequences which can erode it;</li>
<li>assessment of key environmental (<a href="/wiki/Abiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a>) factors influencing the provision of services;</li>
<li>measurement of the spatial and temporal scales ESPs and their services operate on.</li>
</ol>
<p>Recently, a technique has been developed to improve and standardize the evaluation of ESP functionality by quantifying the relative importance of different species in terms of their efficiency and abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-Balvanera_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balvanera-28">[28]</a></sup> Such parameters provide indications of how species respond to changes in the environment (i.e. predators, resource availability, climate) and are useful for identifying species that are disproportionately important at providing ecosystem services. However, a critical drawback is that the technique does not account for the effects of interactions, which are often both complex and fundamental in maintaining an ecosystem and can involve species that are not readily detected as a priority. Even so, estimating the functional structure of an ecosystem and combining it with information about individual species traits can help us understand the <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a> of an ecosystem amidst environmental change.</p>
<p>Many ecologists also believe that the provision of ecosystem services can be stabilized with <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. Increasing biodiversity also benefits the variety of ecosystem services available to society. Understanding the relationship between biodiversity and an ecosystem's stability is essential to the management of natural resources and their services.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redundancy_hypothesis">Redundancy hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Redundancy hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept of ecological redundancy is sometimes referred to as <i>functional compensation</i> and assumes that more than one species performs a given role within an ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> More specifically, it is characterized by a particular species increasing its efficiency at providing a service when conditions are stressed in order to maintain aggregate stability in the ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> However, such increased dependence on a compensating species places additional stress on the ecosystem and often enhances its susceptibility to subsequent <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbance</a>. The redundancy hypothesis can be summarized as "species redundancy enhances ecosystem resilience".<sup id="cite_ref-Naeem_1998_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Naeem_1998-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another idea uses the analogy of rivets in an airplane wing to compare the exponential effect the loss of each species will have on the function of an ecosystem; this is sometimes referred to as <i>rivet popping</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawton_1994_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawton_1994-32">[32]</a></sup> If only one species disappears, the loss of the ecosystem's efficiency as a whole is relatively small; however if several species are lost, the system essentially collapses as an airplane wing would, were it to lose too many rivets. The hypothesis assumes that species are relatively specialized in their roles and that their ability to compensate for one another is less than in the redundancy hypothesis. As a result, the loss of any species is critical to the performance of the ecosystem. The key difference is the rate at which the loss of species affects total ecosystem function.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portfolio_effect">Portfolio effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portfolio effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A third explanation, known as the <i>portfolio effect</i>, compares biodiversity to stock holdings, where diversification minimizes the volatility of the investment, or in this case, the risk in stability of ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This is related to the idea of <i>response diversity</i> where a suite of species will exhibit differential responses to a given environmental perturbation and therefore when considered together, they create a stabilizing function that preserves the integrity of a service.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several experiments have tested these hypotheses in both the field and the lab. In ECOTRON, a laboratory in the UK where many of the <a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> factors of nature can be simulated, studies have focused on the effects of earthworms and <a href="/wiki/Symbiotic_bacteria" title="Symbiotic bacteria">symbiotic bacteria</a> on plant roots.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawton_1994_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawton_1994-32">[32]</a></sup> These laboratory experiments seem to favor the rivet hypothesis. However, a study on grasslands at Cedar Creek Reserve in Minnesota seems to support the redundancy hypothesis, as have many other field studies.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are questions regarding the environmental and economic values of ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Some people may be unaware of the environment in general and humanity's interrelatedness with the natural environment, which may cause misconceptions. Although environmental awareness is rapidly improving in our contemporary world, ecosystem capital and its flow are still poorly understood, threats continue to impose, and we suffer from the so-called ‘<a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>’.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Many efforts to inform decision-makers of current versus future costs and benefits now involve organizing and translating scientific knowledge to <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, which articulate the consequences of our choices in comparable units of impact on human well-being.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_et_al._2000._38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_et_al._2000.-38">[38]</a></sup> An especially challenging aspect of this process is that interpreting ecological information collected from one spatial-temporal scale does not necessarily mean it can be applied at another; understanding the dynamics of ecological processes relative to ecosystem services is essential in aiding economic decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Weighting factors such as a service’s irreplaceability or bundled services can also allocate economic value such that goal attainment becomes more efficient.</p>
<p>The economic valuation of ecosystem services also involves social communication and information, areas that remain particularly challenging and are the focus of many researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In general, the idea is that although individuals make decisions for any variety of reasons, trends reveal the aggregative preferences of a society, from which the economic value of services can be inferred and assigned. The six major methods for valuing ecosystem services in monetary terms are:<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Avoided cost: Services allow society to avoid costs that would have been incurred in the absence of those services (e.g. waste treatment by <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> habitats avoids health costs)</li>
<li>Replacement cost: Services could be replaced with man-made systems (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Restoration_Ecology" class="mw-redirect" title="Restoration Ecology">restoration</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Catskill_Watershed" class="mw-redirect" title="Catskill Watershed">Catskill Watershed</a> cost less than the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a> plant)</li>
<li>Factor income: Services provide for the enhancement of incomes (e.g. improved <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> increases the commercial take of a <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> and improves the income of fishers)</li>
<li>Travel cost: Service demand may require travel, whose costs can reflect the implied value of the service (e.g. value of <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a> experience is at least what a visitor is willing to pay to get there)</li>
<li>Hedonic pricing: Service demand may be reflected in the prices people will pay for associated goods (e.g. coastal housing prices exceed that of inland homes)</li>
<li>Contingent valuation: Service demand may be elicited by posing hypothetical scenarios that involve some valuation of alternatives (e.g. visitors willing to pay for increased access to national parks)</li>
</ul>
<p>A peer-reviewed study published in 1997 estimated the value of the world's ecosystem services and natural capital to be between US$16–54 trillion per year, with an average of US$33 trillion per year.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, Salles (2011) indicates 'The total value of biodiversity is infinite, so having debate about what is the total value of nature is actually pointless because we can't live without it'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_and_policy">Management and policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Management and policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although monetary pricing continues with respect to the valuation of ecosystem services, the challenges in policy implementation and management are significant and multitudinous. The administration of <a href="/wiki/Common_pool_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Common pool resources">common pool resources</a> is a subject of extensive academic pursuit.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostrom_1990_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrom_1990-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> From defining the problems to finding solutions that can be applied in practical and sustainable ways, there is much to overcome. Considering options must balance present and future human needs, and decision-makers must frequently work from valid but incomplete information. Existing legal policies are often considered insufficient since they typically pertain to human health-based standards that are mismatched with necessary means to protect <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_health" title="Ecosystem health">ecosystem health</a> and services. To improve the information available, one suggestion has involved the implementation of an <i>Ecosystem Services Framework</i> (ESF<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_2000_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_2000-48">[48]</a></sup>), which integrates the biophysical and <a href="/wiki/Socio-economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Socio-economic">socio-economic</a> dimensions of protecting the environment and is designed to guide <a href="/wiki/Institutions" class="mw-redirect" title="Institutions">institutions</a> through multidisciplinary information and jargon, helping to direct strategic choices.</p>
<p>Novel and expedient methods are needed to deal with managing Earth’s ecosystem services. Local to regional collective management efforts might be considered appropriate for services like crop <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> or resources like water.<sup id="cite_ref-Kremen_2005_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kremen_2005-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ostrom_1990_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrom_1990-43">[43]</a></sup> Another approach that has become increasingly popular over the last decade is the marketing of ecosystem services protection. Payment and trading of services is an emerging worldwide small-scale solution where one can acquire credits for activities such as sponsoring the protection of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> sources or the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_ecology" title="Restoration ecology">restoration</a> of ecosystem service providers. In some cases, banks for handling such credits have been established and conservation companies have even gone public on stock exchanges, defining an evermore parallel link with economic endeavors and opportunities for tying into social perceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_et_al._2000._38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_et_al._2000.-38">[38]</a></sup> However, crucial for implementation are clearly defined <a href="/wiki/Land_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Land rights">land rights</a>, which is often lacking in many <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-49">[49]</a></sup> In particular, many forest-rich developing countries suffering <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> experience conflict between different forest stakeholders.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-49">[49]</a></sup> In addition, concerns for such global transactions include inconsistent compensation for services or resources sacrificed elsewhere and misconceived warrants for irresponsible use. Another approach has been focused on protecting ecosystem service ‘<a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">hotspots</a>’. Recognition that the conservation of many ecosystem services aligns with more traditional conservation goals (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>) has led to the suggested merging of objectives for maximizing their mutual success. This may be particularly strategic when employing networks that permit the flow of services across <a href="/wiki/Landscapes" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscapes">landscapes</a>, and might also facilitate securing the financial means to protect services through a diversification of investors.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, in recent years there has been interest in the valuation of ecosystem services provided by <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> production and restoration.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> A keystone species, low in the food chain, bivalve shellfish such as oysters support a complex community of species by performing a number of functions essential to the diverse array of species that surround them. There is also increasing recognition that some shellfish species may impact or control many ecological processes; so much so that they are included on the list of “ecosystem engineers”—organisms that physically, biologically or chemically modify the environment around them in ways that influence the health of other organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Many of the ecological functions and processes performed or affected by shellfish contribute to human well-being by providing a stream of valuable ecosystem services over time by filtering out particulate materials and potentially mitigating water quality issues by controlling <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_pollution" title="Nutrient pollution">excess nutrients</a> in the water.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem-Based_Adaptation_.28EbA.29">Ecosystem-Based Adaptation (EbA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ecosystem-Based Adaptation (EbA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecosystem-Based Adaptation or EbA is an emerging strategy for community development and environmental management that seeks to use an ecosystem services framework to help communities adapt to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> currently defines Ecosystem-Based Adaptation as “the use of biodiversity and ecosystem services to help people adapt to the adverse effects of climate change”, which includes the use of “sustainable management, conservation and restoration of ecosystems, as part of an overall adaptation strategy that takes into account the multiple social, economic and cultural co-benefits for local communities”.<sup id="cite_ref-ebaflagship.org_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebaflagship.org-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> announced that humanity’s impact on the natural world was increasing to levels never before seen, and that the degradation of the planet’s ecosystems would become a major barrier to achieving the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>. In recognition of this fact, Ecosystem-Based Adaptation seeks to use the restoration of ecosystems as a stepping-stone to improving the quality of life in communities experiencing the impacts of climate change. Specifically, this involves the restoration of ecosystems that provide the community with essential services, such as the provisioning of food and water and protection from <a href="/wiki/Storm_surges" class="mw-redirect" title="Storm surges">storm surges</a> and flooding. EbA interventions typically combine elements of both <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to global warming</a> to help address the community’s current and future needs.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Collaborative planning between scientists, policy makers, and community members is an essential element of Ecosystem-Based Adaptation. By drawing on the expertise of outside experts and local residents alike, EbA seeks to develop unique solutions to unique problems, rather than simply replicating past projects.<sup id="cite_ref-ebaflagship.org_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebaflagship.org-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estuarine_and_Coastal_Ecosystem_Services">Estuarine and Coastal Ecosystem Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Estuarine and Coastal Ecosystem Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecosystem services are defined as the gains acquired by humankind from surroundings ecosystems. Four different types of ecosystem services have been distinguished by the scientific body: regulating services, provisioning services, cultural services and supporting services. An ecosystem does not necessarily offer all four types of services simultaneously; but given the intricate nature of any ecosystem, it is usually assumed that humans benefit from a combination of these services. The services offered by diverse types of ecosystems (forests, seas, coral reefs, mangroves, etc.) differ in nature and in consequence. In fact, some services directly affect the livelihood of neighboring human populations (such as fresh water, food or aesthetic value, etc.) while other services affect general environmental conditions by which humans are indirectly impacted (such as climate change, erosion regulation or natural hazard regulation, etc.).<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Estuarine and coastal ecosystems are both marine ecosystems. An <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> is defined as the area in which a river meets the sea or the ocean. The waters surrounding this area are predominantly salty waters or brackish waters; and the incoming river water is dynamically motioned by the tide. An estuary strip may be covered by populations of reed (or similar plants) and/or sandbanks (or similar form or land).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A coastal ecosystem occurs in areas where the sea or ocean waters meet the land.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulating_services_2">Regulating services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Regulating services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regulating services are the “benefits obtained from the regulation of ecosystem processes”.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In the case of coastal and estuarine ecosystems, these services include climate regulation, waste treatment and disease control and natural hazard regulation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_regulation">Climate regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Both the biotic and abiotic ensembles of marine ecosystems play a role in climate regulation. They act as sponges when it comes to gases in the atmosphere, retaining large levels of CO2 and other Green House Gases (methane and nitrous oxide). Marine plants also use CO2 for photosynthesis purposes and help in reducing the atmospheric CO2. The oceans and seas absorb the heat from the atmosphere and redistribute it through the means of water currents, and atmospheric processes, such as evaporation and the reflection of light allow for the cooling and warming of the overlying atmosphere. The ocean temperatures are thus imperative to the regulation of the atmospheric temperatures in any part of the world: “without the ocean, the Earth would be unbearably hot during the daylight hours and frigidly cold, if not frozen, at night”.<sup id="cite_ref-davidsuzuki.org_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidsuzuki.org-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_treatment_and_disease_regulation">Waste treatment and disease regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Waste treatment and disease regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another service offered by marine ecosystem is the treatment of wastes, thus helping in the regulation of diseases. Wastes can be diluted and detoxified through transport across marine ecosystems; pollutants are removed from the environment and stored, buried or recycled in marine ecosystems: “Marine ecosystems break down organic waste through microbial communities that filter water, reduce/limit the effects of eutrophication, and break down toxic hydrocarbons into their basic components such as carbon dioxide, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water”.<sup id="cite_ref-davidsuzuki.org_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidsuzuki.org-58">[58]</a></sup> The fact that waste is diluted with large volumes of water and moves with water currents leads to the regulation of diseases and the reduction of toxics in seafood.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buffer_zones">Buffer zones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Buffer zones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Coastal and estuarine ecosystems act as buffer zones against natural hazards and environmental disturbances, such as floods, cyclones, tidal surges and storms. The role they play is to “[absorb] a portion of the impact and thus [lessen] its effect on the land”.<sup id="cite_ref-davidsuzuki.org_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidsuzuki.org-58">[58]</a></sup> Wetlands, for example, and the vegetation it supports – trees, root mats, etc. – retain large amounts of water (surface water, snowmelt, rain, groundwater) and then slowly releases them back, decreasing the likeliness of floods.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Mangrove forests protect coastal shorelines from tidal erosion or erosion by currents; a process that was studied after the 1999 cyclone that hit India. Villages that were surrounded with mangrove forests encountered less damages than other villages that weren’t protected by mangroves.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisioning_Services_2">Provisioning Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Provisioning Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Provisioning services consist of all “the products obtained from ecosystems”. Marine ecosystems provide people with: wild &amp; cultured seafood, fresh water, fiber &amp; fuel and biochemical &amp; genetic resources.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_products">Marine products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Marine products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Humans consume a large number of products originating from the seas, whether as a nutritious product or for use in other sectors: “More than one billion people worldwide, or one-sixth of the global population, rely on fish as their main source of animal protein. In 2000, marine and coastal fisheries accounted for 12 per cent of world food production”.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-61">[61]</a></sup> Fish and other edible marine products - primarily fish, shellfish, roe and seaweeds – constitute for populations living along the coast the main elements of the local cultural diets, norms and traditions. A very pertinent example would be Sushi, the national food of Japan, which consists mostly of different types of fish and seaweed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_water">Fresh water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Fresh water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Water bodies that are not highly concentrated in salts are referred to as ‘fresh water’ bodies. Fresh water may run through lakes, rivers and streams, to name a few; but it is most prominently found in the frozen state or as soil moisture or buried deep underground. Fresh water is not only important for the survival of humans, but also for the survival of all the existing species of animals, plants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Raw_materials">Raw materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Raw materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Marine creatures provide us with the raw materials needed for the manufacturing of clothing, building materials (lime extracted from coral reefs), ornamental items and personal-use items (luffas, art and jewelry): “The skin of marine mammals for clothing, gas deposits for energy production, lime (extracted from coral reefs) for building construction, and the timber of mangroves and coastal forests for shelter are some of the more familiar uses of marine organisms. Raw marine materials are utilized for non-essential goods as well, such as shells and corals in ornamental items”.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-61">[61]</a></sup> Humans have also referred to processes within marine environments for the production of renewable energy: using the power of waves – or tidal power – as a source of energy for the powering of a turbine, for example.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Oceans and seas are used as sites for offshore oil and gas installations, offshore wind farms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemical_and_genetic_resources">Biochemical and genetic resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Biochemical and genetic resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Biochemical resources are compounds extracted from marine organisms for use in medicines, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics and other biochemical products. Genetic resources are the genetic information found in marine organisms that would later on be used for animal and plant breeding and for technological advances in the biological field. These resources are either directly taken out from an organism – such as fish oil as a source of omega3 –, or used as a model for innovative man-made products: “such as the construction of fiber optics technology based on the properties of sponges. […] Compared to terrestrial products, marine-sourced products tend to be more highly bioactive, likely due to the fact that marine organisms have to retain their potency despite being diluted in the surrounding sea-water”.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_services_2">Cultural services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Cultural services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cultural services relate to the non-material world, as they benefit the benefit recreational, aesthetic, cognitive and spiritual activities, which are not easily quantifiable in monetary terms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspirational">Inspirational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Inspirational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Marine environments have been used by many as an inspiration for their works of art, music, architecture, traditions… Water environments are spiritually important as a lot of people view them as a means for rejuvenation and change of perspective. Many also consider the water as being a part of their personality, especially if they have lived near it since they were kids: they associate it to fond memories and past experiences. Living near water bodies for a long time results in a certain set of water activities that become a ritual in the lives of people and of the culture in the region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation_and_tourism">Recreation and tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Recreation and tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sea sports are very popular among coastal populations: surfing, snorkeling, whale watching, kayaking, recreational fishing…a lot of tourists also travel to resorts close to the sea or rivers or lakes to be able to experience these activities, and relax near the water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_education">Science and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Science and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A lot can be learned from marine processes, environments and organisms – that could be implemented into our daily actions and into the scientific domain. Although much is still yet to still be known about the ocean world: “by the extraordinary intricacy and complexity of the marine environment and how it is influenced by large spatial scales, time lags, and cumulative effects”.<sup id="cite_ref-davidsuzuki.org_58-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidsuzuki.org-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_services_2">Supporting services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Supporting services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supporting services are the services that allow for the other ecosystem services to be present. They have indirect impacts on humans that last over a long period of time. Several services can be considered as being both supporting services and regulating/cultural/provisioning services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_cycling">Nutrient cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Nutrient cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>“Nutrient cycling refers to the storage, cycling, and maintenance of nutrients by organisms and their associated processes”. The ocean is a vast storage pool for these nutrients, such as carbon, nitrogen and phosphorus. The nutrients are absorbed by the basic organisms of the marine food web and are thus transferred from one organism to the other and from one ecosystem to the other. Nutrients are recycled through the life cycle of organisms as they die and decompose, releasing the nutrients into the neighboring environment. “The service of nutrient cycling eventually impacts all other ecosystem services as all living things require a constant supply of nutrients to survive”.<sup id="cite_ref-davidsuzuki.org_58-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidsuzuki.org-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biologically_mediated_habitats">Biologically mediated habitats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Biologically mediated habitats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Biologically mediated habitats are defined as being the habitats that living marine structures offer to other organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> These need not to be designed for the sole purpose of serving as a habitat, but happen to become living quarters whilst growing naturally. For example, coral reefs and mangrove forests are home to numerous species of fish, seaweed and shellfish… The importance of these habitats is that they allow for interactions between different species, aiding the provisioning of marine goods and services. They are also very important for the growth at the early life stages of marine species (breeding and bursary spaces), as they serve as a food source and as a shelter from predators.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_production">Primary production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Primary production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Primary production refers to the production of organic matter, i.e., chemically bound energy, through processes such as photosynthesis and chemosynthesis. The organic matter produced by primary producers forms the basis of all food webs. Further, it generates oxygen, a molecule necessary for life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Ecosystem services degradation can pose a number of risks to corporate performance as well as provide business opportunities through ecosystem restoration and enhancement. Risks and opportunities include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Operational</b></i>
<ul>
<li>Risks such as higher costs for freshwater due to scarcity or lower output for hydroelectric facilities due to siltation</li>
<li>Opportunities such as increasing water-use efficiency or building an on-site wetland to circumvent the need for new water treatment infrastructure</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i><b>Regulatory and legal</b></i>
<ul>
<li>Risks such as new fines, government regulations, or lawsuits from local communities that lose ecosystem services due to corporate activities</li>
<li>Opportunities such as engaging governments to develop policies and incentives to protect or restore ecosystems that provide services a company needs</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i><b>Reputational</b></i>
<ul>
<li>Risks such as retail companies being targeted by nongovernmental organization campaigns for purchasing wood or paper from sensitive forests</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Opportunities such as implementing and communicating sustainable purchasing, operating, or investment practices in order to differentiate corporate brands.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Market and product</b></i>
<ul>
<li>Risks such as customers switching to other suppliers that offer products with lower ecosystem impacts or governments implementing new sustainable procurement policies</li>
<li>Opportunities such as launching new products and services that reduce customer impacts on ecosystems or participating in emerging markets for carbon sequestration and watershed protection other products</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i><b>Financing</b></i>
<ul>
<li>Risks such as banks implementing more rigorous lending requirements for corporate loans</li>
<li>Opportunities such as banks offering more favorable loan terms or investors taking positions in companies supplying products and services that improve resource use efficiency or restore degraded ecosystems</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Many companies are not fully aware of the extent of their dependence and impact on ecosystems and the possible ramifications. Likewise, environmental management systems and environmental due diligence tools are more suited to handle “traditional” issues of pollution and natural resource consumption. Most focus on <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impacts" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impacts">environmental impacts</a>, not dependence. Several newly developed tools and methodologies can help the private sector value and assess ecosystem services. These include Our Ecosystem,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> the Corporate Ecosystem Services Review (ESR),<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Artificial Intelligence for Ecosystem Services (ARIES),<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> the Natural Value Initiative (NVI)<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and InVEST (Integrated Valuation of Ecosystem Services &amp; Tradeoffs) <sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use_change_decisions">Land use change decisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Land use change decisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecosystem services decisions require making complex choices at the intersection of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>. The process of making ecosystem services decisions must consider the interaction of many types of information, honor all <a href="/wiki/Project_stakeholder" title="Project stakeholder">stakeholder</a> viewpoints, including <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_agencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulatory agencies">regulatory agencies</a>, proposal proponents, decision makers, residents, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a>, and measure the impacts on all four parts of the intersection. These decisions are usually <a href="/wiki/Spatial_planning" title="Spatial planning">spatial</a>, always <a href="/wiki/Multi-objective_optimization" title="Multi-objective optimization">multi-objective</a>, and based on uncertain data, models, and estimates. Often it is the combination of the best science combined with the stakeholder values, estimates and opinions that drive the process.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>One analytical study modeled the stakeholders as <a href="/wiki/Software_agent" title="Software agent">agents</a> to support water resource management decisions in the Middle Rio Grande basin of New Mexico. This study focused on modeling the stakeholder inputs across a spatial decision, but ignored uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Another study used <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_method" title="Monte Carlo method">Monte Carlo</a> methods to exercise econometric models of landowner decisions in a study of the effects of land-use change. Here the stakeholder inputs were modeled as random effects to reflect the uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> A third study used a Bayesian <a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">decision support system</a> to both model the uncertainty in the scientific information <a href="/wiki/Bayes_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayes network">Bayes Nets</a> and to assist collecting and fusing the input from stakeholders. This study was about siting wave energy devices off the Oregon Coast, but presents a general method for managing uncertain spatial science and stakeholder information in a decision making environment.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">Remote sensing</a> data and analyses can be used to assess the health and extent of land cover classes that provide ecosystem services, which aids in planning, management, monitoring of stakeholders' actions, and communication between stakeholders.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Baltic countries scientists, nature conservationists and local authorities are <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://vivagrass.eu/about-the-project/">implementing integrated planning approach for grassland ecosystems</a>. They are developing Integrated Planning Tool that will be based on <a href="/wiki/GIS" class="mw-redirect" title="GIS">GIS</a> (geographic information system) technology and put online that will help for planners to choose the best grassland management solution for concrete grassland. It will look holistically at the processes in the countryside and help to find best grassland management solutions by taking into account both natural and socioeconomic factors of the particular site.</p>
