			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental resource management</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Environmental resource management</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a> of the interaction and impact of <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">human societies</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. It is not, as the phrase might suggest, the management of the environment itself. Environmental resources management aims to ensure that <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> are protected and maintained for future human generations, and also maintain ecosystem integrity through considering <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a>) variables.<sup id="cite_ref-Wostl2006_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wostl2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Environmental resource management tries to identify factors affected by conflicts that rise between meeting needs and protecting resources. It is thus linked to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_landscape_management" title="Integrated landscape management">integrated landscape management</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental resource management is an issue of increasing concern, as reflected in its prevalence in seminal texts influencing global <a href="/wiki/Political_sociology" title="Political sociology">sociopolitical</a> frameworks such as the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">Brundtland Commission</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Our_Common_Future" title="Our Common Future">Our Common Future</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which highlighted the integrated nature of environment and <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a>'s annual <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_World_(book_series)" title="State of the World (book series)">State of the World</a> reports.</p>
<p>The environment determines the nature of people, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, and places around the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, affecting behaviour, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and economic practices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TerracesBuffers.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/TerracesBuffers.JPG/220px-TerracesBuffers.JPG" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/TerracesBuffers.JPG/330px-TerracesBuffers.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/TerracesBuffers.JPG/440px-TerracesBuffers.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="2100" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TerracesBuffers.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Improved agricultural practices such as these <a href="/wiki/Terrace_(agriculture)" title="Terrace (agriculture)">terraces</a> in northwest Iowa can serve to preserve soil and improve water quality</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Environmental resource management can be viewed from a variety of perspectives. It involves the management of all components of the <a href="/wiki/Biophysical_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Biophysical environment">biophysical environment</a>, both living (<a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a>) and non-living (<a href="/wiki/Abiotic_component" title="Abiotic component">abiotic</a>), and the relationships among all living <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>. The environment also involves the relationships of the human environment, such as the social, cultural and economic environment, with the biophysical environment. The essential aspects of environmental resource management are ethical, economical, social, and technological. These underlie principles and help make decisions.</p>
<p>The concept of environmental determinism, probabilism and possibilism are significant in the concept of environmental resource management.</p>
<p>Environmental resource management covers many areas in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects">Aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical">Ethical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental resource management strategies are intrinsically driven by conceptions of <a href="/wiki/Human-Environment_interaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Human-Environment interaction">human-nature relationships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> Ethical aspects involve the cultural and social issues relating to the environment, and dealing with changes to it. "All human activities take place in the context of certain types of relationships between society and the bio-physical world (the rest of nature),"<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_193_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991_193-4">[4]</a></sup> and so, there is a great significance in understanding the ethical values of different groups around the world. Broadly speaking, two schools of thought exist in environmental ethics: <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">Anthropocentrism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">Ecocentrism</a>, each influencing a broad spectrum of environmental resource management styles along a continuum.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> These styles perceive "...different evidence, imperatives, and problems, and prescribe different solutions, strategies, technologies, roles for economic sectors, culture, governments, and ethics, etc."<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_193_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991_193-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropocentrism">Anthropocentrism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anthropocentrism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">Anthropocentrism</a></div>
<p>Anthropocentrism, "...an inclination to evaluate reality exclusively in terms of human values,"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> is an ethic reflected in the major interpretations of Western religions and the dominant economic paradigms of the industrialised world.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> Anthropocentrism looks at nature as existing solely for the benefit of humans, and as a commodity to use for the good of humanity and to improve human quality of life.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Anthropocentric environmental resource management is therefore not the conservation of the environment solely for the environment's sake, but rather the conservation of the environment, and ecosystem structure, for humans' sake.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecocentrism">Ecocentrism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecocentrism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">Ecocentrism</a></div>
<p>Ecocentrists believe in the intrinsic value of nature while maintaining that human beings must use and even exploit nature to survive and live.<sup id="cite_ref-Purser_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purser-9">[9]</a></sup> It is this fine ethical line that ecocentrists navigate between fair use and abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-Purser_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purser-9">[9]</a></sup> At an extreme of the ethical scale, ecocentrism includes philosophies such as <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a>, which evolved as a reaction to dominant anthropocentric paradigms.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> "In its current form, it is an attempt to synthesize many old and some new philosophical attitudes about the relationship between nature and human activity, with particular emphasis on ethical, social, and spiritual aspects that have been downplayed in the dominant economic worldview."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg/220px-Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg/330px-Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg/440px-Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1173" data-file-height="776" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gibraltar_East_Side_Water_Catchments_in_1992.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A water harvesting system collects rainwater from the <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_Gibraltar" title="Rock of Gibraltar">Rock of Gibraltar</a> into pipes that lead to tanks excavated inside the rock.</div>

</div>
<p>The economy functions within, and is dependent upon goods and services provided by natural ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Thampa_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thampa-11">[11]</a></sup> The role of the environment is recognized in both <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> theories, yet the environment was a lower priority in economic policies from 1950 to 1980 due to emphasis from policy makers on economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-Thampa_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thampa-11">[11]</a></sup> With the prevalence of environmental problems, many economists embraced the notion that, "If environmental sustainability must coexist for economic sustainability, then the overall system must [permit] identification of an equilibrium between the environment and the economy."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As such, economic policy makers began to incorporate the functions of the natural environment—or <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> — particularly as a sink for wastes and for the provision of raw materials and amenities.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Debate continues among economists as to how to account for natural capital, specifically whether resources can be replaced through knowledge and technology, or whether the environment is a closed system that cannot be replenished and is finite.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Economic models influence environmental resource management, in that management policies reflect beliefs about natural capital scarcity. For someone who believes natural capital is infinite and easily substituted, environmental management is irrelevant to the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, economic paradigms based on neoclassical models of closed economic systems are primarily concerned with resource scarcity, and thus prescribe legalizing the environment as an economic externality for an environmental resource management strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> This approach has often been termed 'Command-and-control'.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup> Colby has identified trends in the development of economic paradigms, among them, a shift towards more <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a> since the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Colby1991_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colby1991-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>"The pairing of significant uncertainty about the behaviour and response of ecological systems with urgent calls for near-term action constitutes a difficult reality, and a common lament" for many <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Manager" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Manager">environmental resource managers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Scientific analysis of the environment deals with several dimensions of ecological uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-Arvai2006_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arvai2006-16">[16]</a></sup> These include: <i>structural uncertainty</i> resulting from the misidentification, or lack of information pertaining to the relationships between ecological variables; <i>parameter uncertainty</i> referring to "uncertainty associated with parameter values that are not known precisely but can be assessed and reported in terms of the likelihood…of experiencing a defined range of outcomes";<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <i>stochastic uncertainty</i> stemming from chance or unrelated factors.<sup id="cite_ref-Arvai2006_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arvai2006-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">Adaptive management</a> <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> is considered a useful framework for dealing with situations of high levels of uncertainty <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> though it is not without its detractors.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common scientific concept and impetus behind environmental resource management is <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>. Simply put, carrying capacity refers to the maximum number of organisms a particular resource can sustain. The concept of carrying capacity, whilst understood by many cultures over history, has its roots in <a href="/wiki/Malthusianism" title="Malthusianism">Malthusian</a> theory. An example is visible in the EU <a href="/wiki/Water_Framework_Directive" title="Water Framework Directive">Water Framework Directive</a>. However, "it is argued that Western scientific knowledge ... is often insufficient to deal with the full complexity of the interplay of variables in environmental resource management.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> These concerns have been recently addressed by a shift in environmental resource management approaches to incorporate different knowledge systems including <a href="/wiki/Traditional_knowledge" title="Traditional knowledge">traditional knowledge</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Raymond2010_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2010-24">[24]</a></sup> reflected in approaches such as adaptive co-management <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> community-based natural resource management <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and transitions management.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> among others.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymond2010_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond2010-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_and_environmental_management" title="Sustainability and environmental management">Sustainability and environmental management</a></div>
<p>Sustainability in environmental resource management involves managing economic, social, and ecological systems both within and outside an organizational entity so it can sustain itself and the system it exists in.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup> In context, sustainability implies that rather than competing for endless growth on a finite planet, development improves quality of life without necessarily consuming more resources.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Sustainably managing environmental resources requires organizational change that instills sustainability values that portrays these values outwardly from all levels and reinforces them to surrounding stakeholders.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup> The end result should be a symbiotic relationship between the sustaining organization, community, and environment.</p>
<p>Many drivers compel environmental resource management to take sustainability issues into account. Today's economic paradigms do not protect the natural environment, yet they deepen human dependency on biodiversity and ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Ecologically, massive <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> <sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> <sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> threaten the stability of ecological systems that humanity depends on.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNEP2002_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP2002-39">[39]</a></sup> Socially, an increasing gap between rich and poor and the global <a href="/wiki/North-South_divide" class="mw-redirect" title="North-South divide">North-South divide</a> denies many access to basic human needs, rights, and education, leading to further environmental destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNEP2002_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP2002-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The planet's unstable condition is caused by many <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic">anthropogenic</a> sources.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-37">[37]</a></sup> As an exceptionally powerful contributing factor to social and environmental change, the modern organisation has the potential to apply environmental resource management with sustainability principals to achieve highly effective outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup> To achieve <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> with environmental resource management an organisation should work within sustainability principles, including social and environmental <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">accountability</a>, long-term planning; a strong, shared vision; a holistic focus; devolved and consensus decision making; broad stakeholder engagement and justice; transparency measures; trust; and flexibility.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_paradigm_shifts">Current paradigm shifts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Current paradigm shifts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To adjust to today's environment of quick social and ecological changes, some organizations have begun to experiment with new tools and concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Daily_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily-44">[44]</a></sup> Those that are more traditional and stick to hierarchical decision making have difficulty dealing with the demand for lateral decision making that supports effective participation.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup> Whether it be a matter of ethics or just strategic advantage organizations are internalizing sustainability principles.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BGG_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGG-45">[45]</a></sup> Some of the world's largest and most profitable corporations are shifting to sustainable environmental resource management: Ford, Toyota, BMW, Honda, Shell, Du Pont, Statoil,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Swiss Re, Hewlett-Packard, and Unilever, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup> An extensive study by the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Consulting_Group" title="Boston Consulting Group">Boston Consulting Group</a> reaching 1,560 business leaders from diverse regions, job positions, expertise in sustainability, industries, and sizes of organizations, revealed the many benefits of sustainable practice as well as its viability.<sup id="cite_ref-BGG_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGG-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is important to note that though sustainability of environmental resource management has improved,<sup id="cite_ref-Avery2010_31-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery2010-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dunphy2007_32-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunphy2007-32">[32]</a></sup> corporate sustainability, for one, has yet to reach the majority of global companies operating in the markets.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP-42">[42]</a></sup> The three major barriers to preventing organizations to shift towards sustainable practice with environmental resource management are not understanding what sustainability is; having difficulty modeling an economically viable case for the switch; and having a flawed execution plan, or a lack thereof.<sup id="cite_ref-BGG_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGG-45">[45]</a></sup> Therefore, the most important part of shifting an organization to adopt sustainability in environmental resource management would be to create a shared vision and understanding of what sustainability is for that particular organization, and to clarify the business case.<sup id="cite_ref-BGG_45-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGG-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stakeholders">Stakeholders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stakeholders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> comprises the general government sector plus all public corporations including the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In environmental resource management the public sector is responsible for administering <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a> and implementing <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a> legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-DERM_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DERM-48">[48]</a></sup> The traditional role of the public sector in environmental resource management is to provide professional judgement through skilled technicians on behalf of the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup> With the increase of intractable environmental problems, the public sector has been led to examine alternative paradigms for managing environmental resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup> This has resulted in the public sector working collaboratively with other sectors (including other governments, private and civil) to encourage sustainable natural resource management behaviours.<sup id="cite_ref-DERM_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DERM-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_sector">Private sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Private sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a> comprises private corporations and non-profit institutions serving households.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The private sector's traditional role in environmental resource management is that of the recovery of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-50">[50]</a></sup> Such private sector recovery groups include mining (minerals and petroleum), forestry and fishery organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-50">[50]</a></sup> Environmental resource management undertaken by the private sectors varies dependent upon the resource type, that being renewable or non-renewable and private and common resources (also see <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Manager" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Manager">Environmental managers</a> from the private sector also need skills to manage collaboration within a dynamic social and political environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_society">Civil society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Civil society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">Civil society</a> comprises associations in which societies voluntarily organise themselves into and which represent a wide range of interests and ties.<sup id="cite_ref-oecd07_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oecd07-51">[51]</a></sup> These can include community-based organisations, indigenous peoples' organisations and non-government organisations (<a href="/wiki/NGO" class="mw-redirect" title="NGO">NGO</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-oecd07_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oecd07-51">[51]</a></sup> Functioning through strong public pressure, civil society can exercise their legal rights against the implementation of resource management plans, particularly land management plans.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup> The aim of civil society in environmental resource management is to be included in the decision-making process by means of <a href="/wiki/Public_participation" title="Public participation">public participation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup> Public participation can be an effective strategy to invoke a sense of social responsibility of natural resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Selin_43-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Selin-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools">Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with all management functions, effective management tools, standards and systems are required. An environmental management standard or system or <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(natural_sciences)" class="mw-redirect" title="Protocol (natural sciences)">protocol</a> attempts to reduce <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental impact</a> as measured by some objective criteria. The <a href="/wiki/ISO_14001" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 14001">ISO 14001</a> standard is the most widely used standard for environmental <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> and is closely aligned to the European <a href="/wiki/Eco-Management_and_Audit_Scheme" title="Eco-Management and Audit Scheme">Eco-Management and Audit Scheme (EMAS)</a>. As a common auditing standard, the <a href="/wiki/ISO_19011" title="ISO 19011">ISO 19011</a> standard explains how to combine this with <a href="/wiki/Quality_management" title="Quality management">quality management</a>.</p>
<p>Other <a href="/wiki/Environmental_management_system" title="Environmental management system">environmental management systems (EMS)</a> tend to be based on the ISO 14001 standard and many extend it in various ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Green Dragon Environmental Management Standard is a five-level EMS designed for smaller organisations for whom ISO 14001 may be too onerous and for larger organisations who wish to implement ISO 14001 in a more manageable step-by-step approach,<sup id="cite_ref-greendrag_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greendrag-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>BS 8555 is a phased standard that can help smaller companies move to ISO 14001 in six manageable steps,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Natural_Step" title="The Natural Step">The Natural Step</a> focuses on basic <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> criteria and helps focus <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> on reducing use of materials or energy use that is unsustainable in the long term,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism" title="Natural Capitalism">Natural Capitalism</a> advises using <a href="/wiki/Accounting_reform" title="Accounting reform">accounting reform</a> and a general <a href="/wiki/Biomimicry" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomimicry">biomimicry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">industrial ecology</a> approach to do the same thing,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">US Environmental Protection Agency</a> has many further terms and standards that it defines as appropriate to large-scale EMS,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/UN" class="mw-redirect" title="UN">UN</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> has encouraged adopting a "<a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>" measurement and management framework,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> <a href="/wiki/Eco-Management_and_Audit_Scheme" title="Eco-Management and Audit Scheme">Eco-Management and Audit Scheme (EMAS)</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other strategies exist that rely on making simple distinctions rather than building top-down management "systems" using <a href="/wiki/Performance_audit" title="Performance audit">performance audits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Full_cost_accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="Full cost accounting">full cost accounting</a>. For instance, Ecological Intelligent Design divides products into <a href="/wiki/Consumable" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumable">consumables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Service_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Service product">service products</a> or durables and <a href="/wiki/Unsaleable" class="mw-redirect" title="Unsaleable">unsaleables</a> — toxic products that no one should buy, or in many cases, do not realize they are buying. By eliminating the unsaleables from the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_outcome" class="mw-redirect" title="Comprehensive outcome">comprehensive outcome</a> of any purchase, better environmental resource management is achieved without <i>systems</i>.</p>
<p>Recent successful cases have put forward the notion of <i>integrated management</i>. It shares a wider approach and stresses out the importance of interdisciplinary assessment. It is an interesting notion that might not be adaptable to all cases.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
