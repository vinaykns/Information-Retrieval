			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Lignocellulosic biomass</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Lignocellulose</b> refers to plant dry matter (<a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>), so called lignocellulosic biomass. It is the most abundantly available raw material on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Biofuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Biofuels">biofuels</a>, mainly <a href="/wiki/Bio-ethanol" class="mw-redirect" title="Bio-ethanol">bio-ethanol</a>. It is composed of carbohydrate polymers (<a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>), and an aromatic polymer (<a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>). These carbohydrate polymers contain different sugar monomers (six and five carbon sugars) and they are tightly bound to lignin. Lignocellulosic biomass can be broadly classified into virgin biomass, waste biomass and energy crops. Virgin biomass includes all naturally occurring terrestrial plants such as trees, bushes and grass. Waste biomass is produced as a low value byproduct of various industrial sectors such as <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> (<a href="/wiki/Corn_stover" title="Corn stover">corn stover</a>, sugarcane <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a>, straw etc.) and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saw_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Saw mill">saw mill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mill</a> discards). <a href="/wiki/Energy_crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy crops">Energy crops</a> are crops with high yield of lignocellulosic biomass produced to serve as a raw material for production of second generation biofuel; examples include switch grass (<i><a href="/wiki/Panicum_virgatum" title="Panicum virgatum">Panicum virgatum</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Elephant_grass" title="Elephant grass">Elephant grass</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedicated_energy_crops">Dedicated energy crops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignocellulosic_biomass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dedicated energy crops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many crops are of interest for their ability to provide high yields of biomass and can be harvested multiple times each year. These include <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a> trees and <i><a href="/wiki/Miscanthus_giganteus" title="Miscanthus giganteus">Miscanthus giganteus</a></i>. The premier energy crop is <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, which is a source of the readily fermentable <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and the lignocellulosic by-product <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignocellulosic_biomass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulp_and_paper_industry">Pulp and paper industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignocellulosic_biomass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pulp and paper industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lignocellulosic biomass is the feedstock for the <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a>. This energy-intensive industry focuses on the separation of the lignin and cellulosic fractions of the biomass.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels">Biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignocellulosic_biomass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lignocellulosic biomass, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Wood_fuel" title="Wood fuel">wood fuel</a>, has a long history as a source of energy. Since the middle of the 20th century, the interest of biomass as a precursor to <i>liquid</i> fuels has increased. To be specific, the <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">fermentation</a> of lignocellulosic biomass to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an attractive route to fuels that supplements the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. Biomass is a carbon-neutral source of energy: Since it comes from plants, the combustion of lignocellulosic ethanol produces no net carbon dioxide into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">earth’s atmosphere</a>. Aside from ethanol, many other lignocellulose-derived fuels are of potential interest, including <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/2,5-Dimethylfuran" title="2,5-Dimethylfuran">dimethylfuran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Valerolactone" title="Gamma-Valerolactone">gamma-Valerolactone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>One barrier to the production of ethanol from biomass is that the sugars necessary for fermentation are trapped inside the lignocellulose. Lignocellulose has evolved to resist degradation and to confer <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolytic</a> stability and structural robustness to the cell walls of the plants. This robustness or "recalcitrance" is attributable to the crosslinking between the polysaccharides (cellulose and hemicellulose) and the lignin via <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> linkages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ester linkages arise between oxidized sugars, the <a href="/wiki/Uronic_acid" title="Uronic acid">uronic acids</a>, and the phenols and phenylpropanols functionalities of the lignin. To extract the fermentable sugars, one must first disconnect the celluloses from the lignin, and then use acid or enzymatic methods to <a href="/wiki/Hydrolyze" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrolyze">hydrolyze</a> the newly freed celluloses to break them down into simple monosaccharides. Another challenge to biomass fermentation is the high percentage of pentoses in the hemicellulose, such as <a href="/wiki/Xylose" title="Xylose">xylose</a>, or wood sugar. Unlike hexoses such as glucose, pentoses are difficult to ferment. The problems presented by the lignin and hemicellulose fractions are the foci of much contemporary research.</p>
<p>A large sector of research into the exploitation of lignocellulosic biomass as a feedstock for bio-ethanol focuses particularly on the fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Trichoderma_reesei" title="Trichoderma reesei">Trichoderma reesei</a></i>, known for its cellulolytic abilities. Multiple avenues are being explored including the design of an optimised cocktail of cellulases and hemicellulases isolated from <i>T. reesei</i>, as well as genetic-engineering-based strain improvement to allow the fungus to simply be placed in the presence of lignocellulosic biomass and break down the matter into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose"><small>D</small>-glucose</a> <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Strain improvement methods have led to strains capable of producing significantly more cellulases than the original QM6a isolate; certain industrial strains are known to produce up to 100g of cellulase per litre of fungus<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> thus allowing for maximal extraction of sugars from lignocellulosic biomass. These sugars can then be fermented, leading to bio-ethanol.</p>
