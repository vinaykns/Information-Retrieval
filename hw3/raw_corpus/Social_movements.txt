<p><b>Social movements</b> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Group_action_(sociology)" title="Group action (sociology)">group action</a>. They are large, sometimes informal, groupings of <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individuals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organizations</a> which focus on specific <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_issue" title="Social issue">social issues</a>. In other words, they carry out, resist, or undo a <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">social change</a>.</p>
<p>Modern Western social movements became possible through education (the wider dissemination of <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>) and increased mobility of labor due to the <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> of 19th century societies.<sup id="cite_ref-w2013_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w2013-1">[1]</a></sup> It is sometimes argued that the freedom of expression, education and relative economic independence prevalent in the modern <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> are responsible for the unprecedented number and scope of various contemporary social movements. However, others point out that many of the social movements of the last hundred years grew up, like the <a href="/wiki/Mau_Mau" class="mw-redirect" title="Mau Mau">Mau Mau</a> in Kenya, to oppose Western colonialism. Either way, social movements have been and continued to be closely connected with <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political systems</a>. Occasionally, social movements have been involved in <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">democratizing</a> nations, but more often they have flourished after democratization. Over the past 200 years, they have become part of a popular and global expression of <a href="/wiki/Dissent" title="Dissent">dissent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-t2004_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2004-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern movements often utilize technology and the internet to mobilize people globally. Adapting to communication trends is a common theme among successful movements. Research is beginning to explore how advocacy organizations linked to social movements in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-Advocacy_2.0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Advocacy_2.0-3">[3]</a></sup> and Canada<sup id="cite_ref-Obar_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obar-4">[4]</a></sup> use <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> to facilitate civic engagement and collective action. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://dx.doi.org/10.13140/RG.2.1.4239.9442">systematic literature review</a> of Buettner &amp; Buettner analyzed the role of Twitter during a wide range of social movements (2007 <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a>, 2009 <a href="/wiki/April_2009_Moldovan_parliamentary_election_protests" title="April 2009 Moldovan parliamentary election protests">Moldova</a>, 2009 <a href="/wiki/2009_student_protests_in_Austria" title="2009 student protests in Austria">Austria student protest</a>, 2009 <a href="/wiki/Gaza%E2%80%93Israel_conflict" title="Gaza–Israel conflict">Israel-Gaza</a>, 2009 <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Green_Movement" title="Iranian Green Movement">Iran green revolution</a>, 2009 <a href="/wiki/2010_G-20_Toronto_summit_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2010 G-20 Toronto summit protests">Toronto G20</a>, 2010 <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Revolution" title="Bolivarian Revolution">Venezuela</a>, 2010 <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart_21" title="Stuttgart 21">Germany Stuttgart21</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Revolution_of_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="Egyptian Revolution of 2011">Egypt</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/2011_England_riots" title="2011 England riots">England</a>, 2011 US <a href="/wiki/Occupy_movement" title="Occupy movement">Occupy movement</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/Anti-austerity_movement_in_Spain" title="Anti-austerity movement in Spain">Spain Indignados</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/Anti-austerity_movement_in_Greece" title="Anti-austerity movement in Greece">Greece Aganaktismenoi movements</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/2011_Rome_demonstration" title="2011 Rome demonstration">Italy</a>, 2011 <a href="/wiki/2011_Wisconsin_protests" title="2011 Wisconsin protests">Wisconsin labor protests</a>, 2012 <a href="/wiki/Gaza%E2%80%93Israel_conflict" title="Gaza–Israel conflict">Israel Hamas</a>, 2013 <a href="/wiki/2013_protests_in_Brazil" title="2013 protests in Brazil">Brazil Vinegar</a>, 2013 <a href="/wiki/Gezi_Park_protests" title="Gezi Park protests">Turkey</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Buettner2016a_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buettner2016a-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">Political science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> have developed a variety of theories and empirical research on social movements. For example, some research in political science highlights the relation between popular movements and the formation of new <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political parties</a> as well as discussing the function of social movements in relation to <a href="/wiki/Agenda-setting_theory" title="Agenda-setting theory">agenda setting</a> and influence on politics.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no single consensus definition of a social movement.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Mario Diani argues that nearly all definitions share three criteria: "a network of informal interactions between a plurality of individuals, groups and/or organizations, engaged in a political or cultural conflict, on the basis of a shared collective identity" <sup id="cite_ref-Diani1992_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diani1992-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tilly" title="Charles Tilly">Charles Tilly</a> defines social movements as a series of contentious performances, displays and campaigns by which ordinary people make collective claims on others.<sup id="cite_ref-t2004_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2004-2">[2]</a></sup> For Tilly, social movements are a major vehicle for ordinary people's participation in public politics.<sup id="cite_ref-t2004-3_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2004-3-8">[8]</a></sup> He argues that there are three major elements to a social movement:<sup id="cite_ref-t2004_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2004-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><b>Campaigns</b>: a sustained, organized public effort making collective claims of target authorities;</li>
<li><b>Repertoire</b> (<a href="/wiki/Repertoire_of_contention" title="Repertoire of contention">repertoire of contention</a>): employment of combinations from among the following forms of <a href="/wiki/Political_action" class="mw-redirect" title="Political action">political action</a>: creation of special-purpose associations and coalitions, public meetings, solemn processions, vigils, rallies, demonstrations, petition drives, statements to and in public media, and pamphleteering; and</li>
<li><b>WUNC displays</b>: participants' concerted public representation of <b>w</b>orthiness, <b>u</b>nity, <b>n</b>umbers, and <b>c</b>ommitments on the part of themselves and/or their constituencies.</li>
</ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sidney_Tarrow" title="Sidney Tarrow">Sidney Tarrow</a> defines a social movement as "collective challenges [to elites, authorities, other groups or cultural codes] by people with common purposes and solidarity in sustained interactions with elites, opponents and authorities." He specifically distinguishes social movements from political parties and <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">advocacy groups</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sociologists John McCarthy and Mayer Zald define as a social movement as "a set of opinions and beliefs in a population which represents preferences for changing some elements of the social structure and/or reward distribution of a society." <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Paul van Seeters and <a href="/wiki/Paul_James_(academic)" title="Paul James (academic)">Paul James</a> defining a social movement entails a few minimal conditions of ‘coming together’:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>(1.) the formation of some kind of collective identity; (2.) the development of a shared normative orientation; (3.) the sharing of a concern for change of the status quo and (4.) the occurrence of moments of practical action that are at least subjectively connected together across time addressing this concern for change. Thus we define a social movement as a form of political association between persons who have at least a minimal sense of themselves as connected to others in common purpose and who come together across an extended period of time to effect social change in the name of that purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes,_Esq.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png/150px-William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png" width="150" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png/225px-William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png/300px-William_Hogarth_-_John_Wilkes%2C_Esq.png 2x" data-file-width="1970" data-file-height="3013" /></a>

Satirical engraving of Wilkes by <a href="/wiki/William_Hogarth" title="William Hogarth">William Hogarth</a>. Wilkes is holding two editions of <i><a href="/wiki/The_North_Briton" title="The North Briton">The North Briton</a></i>.</div>

<p>The early growth of social movements was connected to broad economic and political changes in England in the mid-18th century, including <a href="/wiki/Political_representation" class="mw-redirect" title="Political representation">political representation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Proletarianization" title="Proletarianization">proletarianization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-t2004_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2004-2">[2]</a></sup> The first mass social movement catalyzed around the controversial political figure, <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkes" title="John Wilkes">John Wilkes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As editor of the paper <i><a href="/wiki/The_North_Briton" title="The North Briton">The North Briton</a></i>, Wilkes vigorously attacked the new administration of <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart,_3rd_Earl_of_Bute" title="John Stuart, 3rd Earl of Bute">Lord Bute</a> and the peace terms that the new government accepted at the 1763 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1763)" title="Treaty of Paris (1763)">Treaty of Paris</a> at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>. Charged with <a href="/wiki/Seditious_libel" title="Seditious libel">seditious libel</a>, Wilkes was arrested after the issue of a <a href="/wiki/General_warrant" class="mw-redirect" title="General warrant">general warrant</a>, a move that Wilkes denounced as unlawful - the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chief Justice">Lord Chief Justice</a> eventually ruled in Wilkes favour. As a result of this episode, Wilkes became a figurehead to the growing movement for popular sovereignty among the middle classes - people began chanting, "Wilkes and Liberty" in the streets.</p>
<p>After a later period of exile, brought about by further charges of libel and <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a>, Wilkes stood for the Parliamentary seat at <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Middlesex (UK Parliament constituency)">Middlesex</a>, where most of his support was located.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECash2006204.E2.80.9326_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECash2006204.E2.80.9326-13">[13]</a></sup> When Wilkes was imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Bench_Prison" title="King's Bench Prison">King's Bench Prison</a> on 10 May 1768, a mass movement of support emerged, with large demonstrations in the streets under the slogan "No liberty, no King."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECash2006216.E2.80.9326_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECash2006216.E2.80.9326-14">[14]</a></sup> Stripped of the right to sit in Parliament, Wilkes became an <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">Alderman</a> of <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">London</a> in 1769, and an activist group called the <i>Society for the Supporters of the Bill of Rights</i> began aggressively promoting his policies.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This was the first ever sustained social movement; -it involved public meetings, demonstrations, the distribution of pamphlets on an unprecedented scale and the mass petition march. However, the movement was careful not to cross the line into open rebellion; - it tried to rectify the faults in governance through appeals to existing legal precedents and was conceived of as an extra-Parliamentary form of agitation to arrive at a consensual and constitutional arrangement.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The force and influence of this social movement on the streets of London compelled the authorities to concede to the movement's demands. Wilkes was returned to Parliament, <a href="/wiki/General_warrant" class="mw-redirect" title="General warrant">general warrants</a> were declared as unconstitutional and press freedom was extended to the coverage of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_debate" title="Parliamentary debate">Parliamentary debates</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg/230px-The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg" width="230" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg/345px-The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg/460px-The_Gordon_Riots_by_John_Seymour_Lucas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="614" data-file-height="419" /></a>

<p>A much larger movement of <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Catholic">anti-Catholic</a> protest was triggered by the <a href="/wiki/Papists_Act_1778" title="Papists Act 1778">Papists Act 1778</a>, which eliminated a number of the penalties and disabilities endured by <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in England and Wales">Roman Catholics in England</a>, and formed around <a href="/wiki/Lord_George_Gordon" title="Lord George Gordon">Lord George Gordon</a>, who became the President of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Association">Protestant Association</a> in 1779.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Association had the support of leading Calvinist religious figures, including <a href="/wiki/Rowland_Hill_(preacher)" title="Rowland Hill (preacher)">Rowland Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Middleton" title="Erasmus Middleton">Erasmus Middleton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Rippon" title="John Rippon">John Rippon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Gordon was an articulate propagandist and he inflamed the mob with fears of Papism and a return to <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchical rule</a>. The situation deteriorated rapidly, and in 1780, after a meeting of the Protestant Association, its members subsequently marched on the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Great_Britain" title="House of Commons of Great Britain">House of Commons</a> to deliver a petition demanding the repeal of the Act, which the government refused to do. Soon, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_riots" class="mw-redirect" title="Gordon riots">large riots</a> broke out across London and embassies and Catholic owned businesses were attacked by angry mobs.</p>
<p>Other <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movements</a> that emerged in the late 18th century included the British <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abolitionism in the United Kingdom">abolitionist</a> movement against <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> (becoming one between the sugar boycott of 1791 and the second great petition drive of 1806), and possibly the upheaval surrounding the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolutions</a>. In the opinion of Eugene Black (1963), "...association made possible the extension of the politically effective public. Modern extra parliamentary political organization is a product of the late eighteenth century [and] the history of the age of reform cannot be written without it.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_spread">Growth and spread</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growth and spread">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/300px-William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="300" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/450px-William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/600px-William_Edward_Kilburn_-_View_of_the_Great_Chartist_Meeting_on_Kennington_Common_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2797" data-file-height="1990" /></a>

<p>From 1815, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">Britain</a> after victory in the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> entered a period of social upheaval characterised by the growing maturity of the use of social movements and special-interest associations. <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartism</a> was the first mass movement of the growing working-class in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It campaigned for political reform between 1838 and 1848 with the <a href="/wiki/Chartism#People.27s_Charter_of_1838" title="Chartism">People's Charter of 1838</a> as its manifesto – this called for <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> and the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Secret_ballot" title="Secret ballot">secret ballot</a>, amongst other things. The term "social movements" was introduced in 1848 by the German Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_von_Stein" title="Lorenz von Stein">Lorenz von Stein</a> in his book <i>Socialist and Communist Movements since the Third French Revolution (1848)</i> in which he introduced the term "social movement" into scholarly discussions<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> - actually depicting in this way <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movements</a> fighting for the social rights understood as <a href="/wiki/Welfare_rights" title="Welfare rights">welfare rights</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg/200px-Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg" width="200" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg/300px-Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg/400px-Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1296" data-file-height="1403" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Martin_Luther_King_-_March_on_Washington.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> was a leader in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, one of the most famous social movements of the 20th century.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Labor_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor movement">labor movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist movement">socialist movement</a> of the late 19th century are seen as the prototypical social movements, leading to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> parties and organisations. These tendencies were seen in poorer countries as pressure for reform continued, for example in Russia with the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1905">Russian Revolution of 1905</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">of 1917</a>, resulting in the collapse of the Czarist regime around the end of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.</p>
<p>In 1945, Britain after victory in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> entered a period of radical reform and change. In the post-war period, <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">Feminism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gay_rights_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay rights movement">gay rights movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> emerged, often dubbed the <a href="/wiki/New_Social_Movements" class="mw-redirect" title="New Social Movements">New Social Movements</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> They led, among other things, to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Green_parties" class="mw-redirect" title="Green parties">green parties</a> and organisations influenced by the <a href="/wiki/New_left" class="mw-redirect" title="New left">new left</a>. Some find in the end of the 1990s the emergence of a new global social movement, the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">anti-globalization movement</a>. Some social movement scholars posit that with the rapid pace of globalization, the potential for the emergence of new <i>type</i> of social movement is latent—they make the analogy to national movements of the past to describe what has been termed a <a href="/wiki/Global_citizens_movement" title="Global citizens movement">global citizens movement</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_processes">Key processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Key processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several key processes lie behind the history of social movements. <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">Urbanization</a> led to larger settlements, where people of similar goals could find each other, gather and organize. This facilitated <a href="/wiki/Social_interaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Social interaction">social interaction</a> between scores of people, and it was in urban areas that those early social movements first appeared. Similarly, the process of industrialization which gathered large masses of workers in the same region explains why many of those early social movements addressed matters such as economic wellbeing, important to the <a href="/wiki/Worker_class" class="mw-redirect" title="Worker class">worker class</a>. Many other social movements were created at <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>, where the process of <a href="/wiki/Mass_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass education">mass education</a> brought many people together. With the development of <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> technologies, creation and activities of social movements became easier – from printed pamphlets circulating in the 18th century <a href="/wiki/Coffeehouse" title="Coffeehouse">coffeehouses</a> to <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspapers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>, all those tools became important factors in the growth of the social movements. Finally, the spread of <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Political rights">political rights</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a> made the creation and functioning of social movements much easier.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_social_movement">Types of social movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Types of social movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Types_of_social_movements.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Types_of_social_movements.svg/400px-Types_of_social_movements.svg.png" width="400" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Types_of_social_movements.svg/600px-Types_of_social_movements.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Types_of_social_movements.svg/800px-Types_of_social_movements.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="635" data-file-height="466" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Types_of_social_movements.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Types of social movements.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></div>

<p>Sociologists distinguish between several types of social movement:</p>
<ul>
<li>Scope:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reform_movement" title="Reform movement">reform movement</a> - movements advocating changing some <a href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norm (sociology)">norms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">laws</a>. Examples of such a movement would include a <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> with a goal of increasing <a href="/wiki/Workers_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Workers rights">workers rights</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Green_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Green movement">green movement</a> advocating a set of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> laws, or a movement supporting introduction of a <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> or the right to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>. Some reform movements may aim for a change in custom and moral norms, such as condemnation of <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a> or proliferation of some <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>.</li>
<li>radical movement - movements dedicated to changing <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">value systems</a> in a fundamental way. Examples would include the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a> which demanded full <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> and equality under the law to all Americans, regardless of <a href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> (<i>Solidarność</i>) movement which demanded the transformation of a <a href="/wiki/Stalinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Stalinist">Stalinist</a> political and <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic system</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>; or the <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shack_dweller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shack dweller (page does not exist)">shack dwellers</a>' movement <a href="/wiki/Abahlali_baseMjondolo" title="Abahlali baseMjondolo">Abahlali baseMjondolo</a> which demands the full inclusion of shack dwellers into the life of cities.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Type of change:
<ul>
<li>innovation movement - movements which want to introduce or change particular norms, values, etc. The <a href="/wiki/Singularitarianism" title="Singularitarianism">singularitarianism</a> movement advocating deliberate action to effect and ensure the safety of the <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">technological singularity</a> is an example of an innovation movement.</li>
<li>conservative movement - movements which want to preserve existing norms, values, etc. For example, the anti-technology 19th century <a href="/wiki/Luddite" title="Luddite">Luddites</a> movement or the modern movement opposing the spread of the <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_food" title="Genetically modified food">genetically modified food</a> could be seen as conservative movements in that they aimed to fight specific technological changes.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Targets:
<ul>
<li>group-focus movements - focused on affecting groups or society in general, for example, advocating the change of the political system. Some of these groups transform into or join a <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a>, but many remain outside the reformist party political system.</li>
<li>individual-focused movements - focused on affecting individuals. Most <a href="/wiki/Religious_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious movement">religious movements</a> would fall under this category.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Methods of work:
<ul>
<li>peaceful movements - various movements which use <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolent</a> means of protest as part of a campaign of <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_resistance" title="Nonviolent resistance">nonviolent resistance</a>, also often called <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a>. The American Civil Rights movement, Polish Solidarity movement or the <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonviolent">nonviolent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_disobedience" title="Civil disobedience">civil disobedience</a>-orientated wing of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a> would fall into this category.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>violent movements - various movements which resort to violence; they are usually armed and in extreme cases can take a form of a <a href="/wiki/Paramilitary" title="Paramilitary">paramilitary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terrorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrorist">terrorist</a> organization. Examples: the <a href="/wiki/Rote_Armee_Fraktion" class="mw-redirect" title="Rote Armee Fraktion">Rote Armee Fraktion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaida" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Qaida">Al-Qaida</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Old and new:
<ul>
<li>old movements - movements for change have existed for many centuries. Most of the oldest recognized movements, dating to late 18th and 19th centuries, fought for specific social groups, such as the working class, peasants, whites, aristocrats, Protestants, men. They were usually centered around some <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialistic</a> goals like improving the <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a> or, for example, the political autonomy of the working class.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_social_movements" title="New social movements">new movements</a> - movements which became dominant from the second half of the 20th century - like the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pro-choice" class="mw-redirect" title="Pro-choice">pro-choice movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free_software_movement" title="Free software movement">free software movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gay_rights_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay rights movement">gay rights movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">anti-nuclear movement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alter-globalization" title="Alter-globalization">alter-globalization</a> movement, etc. Sometimes they are known as <a href="/wiki/New_social_movements" title="New social movements">new social movements</a>. They are usually centered around issues that go beyond but are not separate from class.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Range:
<ul>
<li>global movements - social movements with global (<a href="/wiki/International" title="International">transnational</a>) objectives and goals. Movements such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Workingmen%27s_Association" title="International Workingmen's Association">first</a> (where Marx and Bakunin met), <a href="/wiki/Second_International" title="Second International">second</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comintern" class="mw-redirect" title="Comintern">third</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fourth_international" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth international">fourth internationals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Social_Forum" title="World Social Forum">World Social Forum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Peoples%27_Global_Action" title="Peoples' Global Action">Peoples' Global Action</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist movement</a> seek to change society at a global level.</li>
<li>local movements - most of the social movements have a local scope.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> They are focused on local or regional objectives, such as protecting a specific natural area, lobbying for the lowering of tolls in a certain motorway, or preserving a building about to be demolished for gentrification and turning it into a <a href="/wiki/Social_center" title="Social center">social center</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_of_supporters">Identification of supporters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Identification of supporters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A difficulty for scholarship of movements is that for most of them, neither insiders to a movement nor outsiders apply consistent labels or even descriptive phrases. Unless there is a single leader who does that, or a formal system of membership agreements, activists will typically use diverse labels and descriptive phrases that require scholars to discern when they are referring to the same or similar ideas, declare similar goals, adopt similar programs of action, and use similar methods. There can be great differences in the way that is done, to recognize who is and who is not a member or an allied group:</p>
<ul>
<li>Insiders: Often exaggerate the level of support by considering people supporters whose level of activity or support is weak, but also reject those that outsiders might consider supporters because they discredit the cause, or are even seen as adversaries.</li>
<li>Outsiders: Those not supporters who may tend to either underestimate or overestimate the level or support or activity of elements of a movement, by including or excluding those that insiders would exclude or include.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is often outsiders rather than insiders that apply the identifying labels for a movement, which the insiders then may or may not adopt and use to self-identify. For example, the label for the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">levellers</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movement</a> in 17th-century England was applied to them by their antagonists, as a <a href="/wiki/Term_of_disparagement" class="mw-redirect" title="Term of disparagement">term of disparagement</a>. Yet admirers of the movement and its aims later came to use the term, and it is the term by which they are known to history.</p>
<p>Caution must always be exercised in any discussion of amorphous phenomena such as movements to distinguish between the views of insiders and outsiders, supporters and antagonists, each of whom may have their own purposes and agendas in <a href="/wiki/Characterization" title="Characterization">characterization</a> or mischaracterization of it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics_of_social_movements">Dynamics of social movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamics of social movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stages_of_social_movements.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Stages_of_social_movements.svg/400px-Stages_of_social_movements.svg.png" width="400" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Stages_of_social_movements.svg/600px-Stages_of_social_movements.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Stages_of_social_movements.svg/800px-Stages_of_social_movements.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="670" data-file-height="333" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stages_of_social_movements.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stages of social movements.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Social movements are not eternal. They have a <a href="/wiki/Product_life_cycle_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Product life cycle management">life cycle</a>: they are created, they grow, they achieve successes or failures and eventually, they dissolve and cease to exist.</p>
<p>They are more likely to evolve in the time and place which is friendly to the social movements: hence their evident symbiosis with the 19th century proliferation of ideas like individual rights, freedom of speech and civil disobedience. Social movements occur in liberal and authoritarian societies but in different forms. However, there must always be polarizing differences between groups of people: in case of 'old movements', they were the <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">wealth gaps</a>. In case of the 'new movements', they are more likely to be the differences in customs, ethics and values. Finally, the birth of a social movement needs what sociologist <a href="/wiki/Neil_Smelser" title="Neil Smelser">Neil Smelser</a> calls an <i>initiating event</i>: a particular, individual event that will begin a <a href="/wiki/Chain_reaction" title="Chain reaction">chain reaction</a> of events in the given society leading to the creation of a social movement. For example, the Civil Rights Movement grew on the reaction to black woman, <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Parks" title="Rosa Parks">Rosa Parks</a>, riding in the whites-only section of the bus (although she was not acting alone or spontaneously—typically activist leaders lay the groundwork behind the scenes of interventions designed to spark a movement). The Polish Solidarity movement, which eventually <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">toppled the communist regimes of Eastern Europe</a>, developed after trade union activist <a href="/wiki/Anna_Walentynowicz" title="Anna Walentynowicz">Anna Walentynowicz</a> was fired from work. The South African shack dwellers' movement <a href="/wiki/Abahlali_baseMjondolo" title="Abahlali baseMjondolo">Abahlali baseMjondolo</a> grew out of a road blockade in response to the sudden selling off of a small piece of land promised for housing to a developer. Such an event is also described as a <i>volcanic model</i> – a social movement is often created after a large number of people realize that there are others sharing the same value and desire for a particular social change.</p>
<p>One of the main difficulties facing the emerging social movement is spreading the very knowledge that it exists. Second is overcoming the <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">free rider problem</a> – convincing people to join it, instead of following the mentality 'why should I trouble myself when others can do it and I can just reap the benefits after their hard work'.</p>
<p>Many social movements are created around some <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_leader" class="mw-redirect" title="Charismatic leader">charismatic leader</a>, i.e. one possessing <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_authority" title="Charismatic authority">charismatic authority</a>. After the social movement is created, there are two likely phases of recruitment. The first phase will gather the people deeply interested in the primary goal and ideal of the movement. The second phase, which will usually come after the given movement had some successes and is trendy; it would look good on a <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9sum%C3%A9" title="Résumé">résumé</a>. People who join in this second phase will likely be the first to leave when the movement suffers any setbacks and failures.</p>
<p>Eventually, the social crisis can be encouraged by outside elements, like opposition from government or other movements. However, many movements had survived a failure crisis, being revived by some hardcore <a href="/wiki/Activist" class="mw-redirect" title="Activist">activists</a> even after several decades later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_movement_theories">Social movement theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social movement theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_movement_theory" title="Social movement theory">Social movement theory</a></div>
<p>Sociologists have developed several <a href="/wiki/Social_movement_theory" title="Social movement theory">theories</a> related to social movements [Kendall, 2005]. Some of the better-known approaches are outlined below. Chronologically they include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Collective_behavior" title="Collective behavior">collective behavior</a>/<a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action</a> theories (1950s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Relative_deprivation_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Relative deprivation theory">relative deprivation theory</a> (1960s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marxist_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist theory">marxist theory</a> (1880s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Value-added_theory" title="Value-added theory">value-added theory</a> (1960s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Resource_mobilization" title="Resource mobilization">resource mobilization</a> (1970s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Political_process_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Political process theory">political process theory</a> (1980s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">framing theory</a> (1980s) (closely related to <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionist_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Social constructionist theory">social constructionist theory</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_social_movement_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="New social movement theory">new social movement theory</a> (1980s)</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deprivation_theory">Deprivation theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deprivation theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Relative_deprivation_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Relative deprivation theory">Deprivation theory</a> argues that social movements have their foundations among people who feel deprived of some good(s) or resource(s). According to this approach, individuals who are lacking some good, service, or comfort are more likely to organize a social movement to improve (or defend) their conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two significant problems with this theory. First, since most people feel deprived at one level or another almost all the time, the theory has a hard time explaining why the groups that form social movements do when other people are also deprived. Second, the reasoning behind this theory is circular – often the only evidence for deprivation is the social movement. If deprivation is claimed to be the cause but the only evidence for such is the movement, the reasoning is circular.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_society_theory">Mass society theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mass society theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_society_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mass society theory (page does not exist)">Mass society theory</a> argues that social movements are made up of individuals in large societies who feel insignificant or socially detached. Social movements, according to this theory, provide a sense of empowerment and belonging that the movement members would otherwise not have.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very little support has been found for this theory. Aho (1990), in his study of Idaho Christian Patriotism, did not find that members of that movement were more likely to have been socially detached. In fact, the key to joining the movement was having a friend or associate who was a member of the movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_strain_theory">Structural strain theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Structural strain theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strain_theory_(sociology)" title="Strain theory (sociology)">Social strain theory</a>, also known as value-added theory, proposes six factors that encourage social movement development:<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>structural conduciveness - people come to believe their society has problems</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Structural_strain" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural strain">structural strain</a> - people experience deprivation</li>
<li>growth and spread of a solution - a solution to the problems people are experiencing is proposed and spreads</li>
<li>precipitating factors - discontent usually requires a catalyst (often a specific event) to turn it into a social movement</li>
<li>lack of social control - the entity that is to be changed must be at least somewhat open to the change; if the social movement is quickly and powerfully repressed, it may never materialize</li>
<li>mobilization - this is the actual organizing and active component of the movement; people do what needs to be done</li>
</ol>
<p>This theory is also subject to circular reasoning as it incorporates, at least in part, deprivation theory and relies upon it, and social/structural strain for the underlying motivation of social movement activism. However, social movement activism is, like in the case of deprivation theory, often the only indication that there was strain or deprivation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_mobilization_theory">Resource mobilization theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Resource mobilization theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Resource_mobilization_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource mobilization theory">Resource mobilization theory</a> emphasizes the importance of resources in social movement development and success. Resources are understood here to include: knowledge, money, media, labor, solidarity, legitimacy, and internal and external support from power elite. The theory argues that social movements develop when individuals with grievances are able to mobilize sufficient resources to take action.The emphasis on resources offers an explanation why some discontented/deprived individuals are able to organize while others are not.<sup id="cite_ref-mccarthyzald_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mccarthyzald-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast to earlier <a href="/wiki/Collective_behavior" title="Collective behavior">collective behavior</a> perspectives on social movements—which emphasized the role of exceptional levels of deprivation, grievance, or social strain in motivating mass protest—Resource Mobilization perspectives hold "that there is always enough discontent in any society to supply the grass-roots support for a movement if the movement is effectively organized and has at its disposal the power and resources of some established elite group" <sup id="cite_ref-TurnerKillian_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TurnerKillian-34">[34]</a></sup> Movement emergence is contingent upon the aggregation of resources by social movement entrepreneurs and movement organizations, who use these resources to turn collective dissent in to political pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-mccarthyzald_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mccarthyzald-33">[33]</a></sup> Members are recruited through networks; commitment is maintained by building a collective identity, and through interpersonal relationships.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Resource Mobilization Theory views social movement activity as "politics by other means": a rational and strategic effort by ordinary people to change society or politics.<sup id="cite_ref-gamson_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamson-35">[35]</a></sup> The form of the resources shapes the activities of the movement (e.g., access to a TV station will result in the extensive use TV media). Movements develop in contingent <i>opportunity structures</i> that influence their efforts to mobilize; and each movement's response to the opportunity structures depends on the movement's organization and resources<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Critics of this theory argue that there is too much of an emphasis on resources, especially financial resources. Some movements are effective without an influx of money and are more dependent upon the movement members for time and labor (e.g., the civil rights movement in the U.S.).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_process_theory">Political process theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Political process theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Political_process_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Political process theory">Political process theory</a> is similar to resource mobilization in many regards, but tends to emphasize a different component of <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social structure</a> that is important for social movement development: <a href="/wiki/Political_opportunities" class="mw-redirect" title="Political opportunities">political opportunities</a>. Political process theory argues that there are three vital components for movement formation: insurgent consciousness, organizational strength, and political opportunities.</p>
<p>Insurgent consciousness refers back to the ideas of deprivation and grievances. The idea is that certain members of society feel like they are being mistreated or that somehow the system is unjust. The insurgent consciousness is the collective sense of injustice that movement members (or potential movement members) feel and serves as the motivation for movement organization.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J20_democracy_dc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/J20_democracy_dc.jpg/250px-J20_democracy_dc.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/J20_democracy_dc.jpg/375px-J20_democracy_dc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/J20_democracy_dc.jpg/500px-J20_democracy_dc.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2528" data-file-height="1896" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J20_democracy_dc.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photo taken at the 2005 U.S. Presidential inauguration protest.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Organizational strength falls inline with resource-mobilization theory, arguing that in order for a social movement to organize it must have strong leadership and sufficient resources.</p>
<p>Political opportunity refers to the receptivity or vulnerability of the existing political system to challenge. This vulnerability can be the result of any of the following (or a combination thereof):</p>
<ul>
<li>growth of political pluralism</li>
<li>decline in effectiveness of repression</li>
<li>elite disunity; the leading factions are internally fragmented</li>
<li>a broadening of access to institutional participation in political processes</li>
<li>support of organized opposition by elites</li>
</ul>
<p>One of the advantages of the political process theory is that it addresses the issue of timing or emergence of social movements. Some groups may have the insurgent consciousness and resources to mobilize, but because political opportunities are closed, they will not have any success. The theory, then, argues that all three of these components are important.</p>
<p>Critics of the political process theory and resource-mobilization theory point out that neither theory discusses movement culture to any great degree. This has presented culture theorists an opportunity to expound on the importance of culture.</p>
<p>One advance on the political process theory is the <i>political mediation model,</i> which outlines the way in which the political context facing movement actors intersects with the strategic choices that movements make. An additional strength of this model is that it can look at the outcomes of social movements not only in terms of success or failure but also in terms of consequences (whether intentional or unintentional, positive or negative) and in terms of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Collective_benefits&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Collective benefits (page does not exist)">collective benefits</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Framing_perspective">Framing perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Framing perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reflecting the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_turn" title="Cultural turn">cultural turn</a> in the social sciences and humanities more broadly, recent strains of social movement theory and research add to the largely structural concerns seen in the resource mobilization and political process theories by emphasizing the cultural and psychological aspects of social movement processes, such as collectively shared interpretations and beliefs, ideologies, values and other meanings about the world. In doing so, this general cultural approach also attempts to address the <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">free-rider problem</a>. One particularly successful take on some such cultural dimensions is manifested in the <a href="/wiki/Framing_(social_sciences)" title="Framing (social sciences)">framing perspective</a> on social movements.</p>
<p>While both resource mobilization theory and political process theory include, or at least accept, the idea that certain shared understandings of, for example, perceived unjust societal conditions must exist for mobilization to occur at all, this is not explicitly problematized within those approaches. The framing perspective has brought such shared understandings to the forefront of the attempt to understand movement creation and existence by, e.g., arguing that, in order for social movements to successfully mobilize individuals, they must develop an <i>injustice frame</i>. An injustice frame is a collection of ideas and symbols that illustrate both how significant the problem is as well as what the movement can do to alleviate it,</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Like a picture frame, an issue frame marks off some part of the world. Like a building frame, it holds things together. It provides coherence to an array of symbols, images, and arguments, linking them through an underlying organizing idea that suggests what is essential - what consequences and values are at stake. We do not see the frame directly, but infer its presence by its characteristic expressions and language. Each frame gives the advantage to certain ways of talking and thinking, while it places others out of the picture."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Important characteristics of the injustice frames include:<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Facts take on their meaning by being embedded in frames, which render them relevant and significant or irrelevant and trivial.</li>
<li>People carry around multiple frames in their heads.</li>
<li>Successful reframing involves the ability to enter into the worldview of our adversaries.</li>
<li>All frames contain implicit or explicit appeals to moral principles.</li>
</ul>
<p>In emphasizing the injustice frame, culture theory also addresses the free-rider problem. The free-rider problem refers to the idea that people will not be motivated to participate in a social movement that will use up their personal resources (e.g., time, money, etc.) if they can still receive the benefits without participating. In other words, if person X knows that movement Y is working to improve environmental conditions in his neighborhood, he is presented with a choice: join or not join the movement. If he believes the movement will succeed without him, he can avoid participation in the movement, save his resources, and still reap the benefits - this is <i>free-riding</i>. A significant problem for social movement theory has been to explain why people join movements if they believe the movement can/will succeed without their contribution. Culture theory argues that, in conjunction with social networks being an important contact tool, the injustice frame will provide the motivation for people to contribute to the movement.</p>
<p>Framing processes includes three separate components:</p>
<ul>
<li>Diagnostic frame: the movement organization frames what is the problem or what they are critiquing</li>
<li>Prognostic frame: the movement organization frames what is the desirable solution to the problem</li>
<li>Motivational frame: the movement organization frames a "call to arms" by suggesting and encouraging that people take action to solve the problem</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_movement_and_social_networking">Social movement and social networking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Social movement and social networking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For more than ten years, social movement groups have been using the Internet to accomplish organizational goals. It has been argued that the Internet helps to increase the speed, reach and effectiveness of social movement-related communication as well as mobilization efforts, and as a result, it has been suggested that the Internet has had a positive impact on the social movements in general.<sup id="cite_ref-Obar_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obar-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many discussions have been generated recently on the topic of social networking and the effect it may play on the formation and mobilization of social movement.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> For example, the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Coffee_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Coffee Party">Coffee Party</a> first appeared on the social networking site, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>. The party has continued to gather membership and support through that site and file sharing sites, such as <a href="/wiki/Flickr" title="Flickr">Flickr</a>. The <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%932010_Iranian_election_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2009–2010 Iranian election protests">2009–2010 Iranian election protests</a> also demonstrated how social networking sites are making the mobilization of large numbers of people quicker and easier. Iranians were able to organize and speak out against the election of <a href="/wiki/Mahmoud_Ahmadinejad" title="Mahmoud Ahmadinejad">Mahmoud Ahmadinejad</a> by using sites such as <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>. This in turn prompted widespread government censorship of the web and social networking sites.</p>
<p>The sociological study of social movements is quite new. The traditional view of movements often perceived them as chaotic and disorganized, treating activism as a threat to the <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a>. The activism experienced in the 1960s and 1970s shuffled in a new world opinion about the subject. Models were now introduced to understand the organizational and structural powers embedded in social movements.</p>
