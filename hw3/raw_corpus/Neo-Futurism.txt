			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Neo-futurism</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Neo-futurism</b> is a late 20th–early 21st century movement in <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>. It could be seen as a departure from the attitude of <a href="/wiki/Post-modernism" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-modernism">post-modernism</a> and represents an <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealistic</a> belief in a better future and "a need to periodize the modern rapport with the technological".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> movement<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a futuristic rethinking of the aesthetic and functionality of rapidly growing cities. The industrialization that began worldwide following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> gave wind to new streams of thought in life, art and architecture, leading to post-modernism, neo-modernism and then neo-futurism.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Western countries, <a href="/wiki/Futurist_architecture" title="Futurist architecture">futurist architecture</a> evolved into <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Googie_architecture" title="Googie architecture">Googie movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/High-tech_architecture" title="High-tech architecture">high-tech architecture</a>, and finally into Neo-Futurism.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Neo-futuristic urbanists, architects, designers and artists believe in cities releasing emotions, driven by eco-<a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, ethical values and implementing new materials and new technologies to provide a better quality of life for city-dwellers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neo-futurism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pioneered in the late 1960s and early 70s by American architects <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a><sup id="cite_ref-auto_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_C._Portman,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John C. Portman, Jr.">John C. Portman, Jr.</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Finnish-American architect and industrial designer <a href="/wiki/Eero_Saarinen" title="Eero Saarinen">Eero Saarinen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Bundling_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim has too many footnotes for reading to be smooth. <p>Although it was never built, the Fun Palace (1961) interpreted by architect <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Price" title="Cedric Price">Cedric Price</a> as a "giant neo-futurist machine"<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> influenced other architects, notably <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rogers" title="Richard Rogers">Richard Rogers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Centre_Georges_Pompidou" title="Centre Georges Pompidou">Pompidou Centre</a> extended many of Price's ideas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neo-futurism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neo-futurism was relaunched in 2007 after the dissemination of "The Neo-Futuristic City <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">Manifesto</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> included in the candidature presented to the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_International_Expositions" title="Bureau of International Expositions">Bureau of International Expositions</a> (BIE)<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and written by innovation designer <a href="/wiki/Vito_Di_Bari" title="Vito Di Bari">Vito Di Bari</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> a former executive director at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization</a> (UNESCO),<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> to outline his vision for the city of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> at the time of the Universal <a href="/wiki/Expo_2015" title="Expo 2015">Expo 2015</a>. Di Bari defined his neo-futuristic vision as the "cross-pollination of art, cutting edge technologies and ethical values combined to create a pervasively higher quality of life";<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> he referenced the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development#Culture" title="Sustainable development">Fourth Pillar of Sustainable Development Theory</a><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and reported that the name had been inspired by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> report <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Common_Future" title="Our Common Future">Our Common Future</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Cohen" title="Jean-Louis Cohen">Jean-Louis Cohen</a> has defined neo-futurism<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> as "a corollary to technology, being the structures built today byproducts of new materials to create previously impossible forms." Etan J. Ilfeld wrote that in the contemporary neo-futurist aesthetics "the machine becomes an integral element of the creative process itself, and generates the emergence of artistic modes that would have been impossible prior to computer technology."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Reyner Banham's definition of "une architecture autre" is a call for an architecture that technologically overcomes all previous architectures but possessing an expressive form,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> as Banham stated about neo-futuristic "Archigram’s Plug-in Computerized City, form does not have to follow function into oblivion."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neo-futurism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The relaunch of neo-futurism in the 21st century has been creatively inspired by the Iraqi-British <a href="/wiki/Pritzker_Architecture_Prize" title="Pritzker Architecture Prize">Pritzker Architecture Prize</a>-winning architect <a href="/wiki/Zaha_Hadid" title="Zaha Hadid">Zaha Hadid</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Spanish architect <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-auto2_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto3_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> and by Vito DiBari.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neo-Futurist architects, designers and artists are French architect <a href="/w/index.php?title=Denis_Laming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Denis Laming (page does not exist)">Denis Laming</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Laming" class="extiw" title="fr:Denis Laming">fr</a>)</span>;<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a
