<p>A <b>solar vehicle</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> powered completely or significantly by direct <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>. Usually, <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) cells contained in <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> convert the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>'s energy directly into <a href="/wiki/Electric_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric energy">electric energy</a>. The term "solar vehicle" usually implies that solar energy is used to power all or part of a vehicle's <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_propulsion" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle propulsion">propulsion</a>. <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">Solar power</a> may be also used to provide power for communications or controls or other auxiliary functions.</p>
<p>Solar vehicles are not sold as practical day-to-day transportation devices at present, but are primarily demonstration vehicles and engineering exercises, often sponsored by government agencies. However, indirectly solar-charged vehicles are widespread and <a href="/wiki/Electric_boat" title="Electric boat">solar boats</a> are available commercially.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land">Land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cars">Solar cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar cars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_car" title="Solar car">Solar car</a></div>
<p>Solar cars depend on PV cells to convert sunlight into electricity to drive electric motors. Unlike solar thermal energy which converts solar energy to heat, PV cells directly convert sunlight into electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The design of a solar car is severely limited by the amount of energy input into the car. Solar cars are built for <a href="/wiki/Solar_car_racing" title="Solar car racing">solar car races</a> and also for public use <a href="/wiki/List_of_prototype_solar-powered_cars" title="List of prototype solar-powered cars">List of prototype solar-powered cars</a>. Even the best solar cells can only collect limited power and energy over the area of a car's surface. This limits solar cars to ultralight composite bodies to save weight. Solar cars lack the safety and convenience features of conventional vehicles. The first solar family car was built in 2013 by students in the Netherlands. This vehicle is capable of 550 miles on one charge during sunlight. It weighs 850 pounds and has a 1.5kw solar array. Solar vehicles must be light and efficient. 3,000 pound or even 2,000 pound vehicles are less practical. Stella Lux the predecessor to Stella broke a record with a 932 mile single charge range. The Dutch are trying to commercialize this technology. During racing Stella Lux is capable of 700 miles during daylight. At 45&#160;mph Stella Lux has infinite range. This is again due to high efficiency including a <a href="/wiki/Drag_coefficient" title="Drag coefficient">Coefficient of drag</a> of .16. The average family who never drive more than 200 miles a day would never need to charge from the mains. They would only plug in if they wanted to return energy to the grid.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Solar cars are often fitted with gauges and/or <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">wireless telemetry</a>, to carefully monitor the car's energy consumption, solar energy capture and other parameters. Wireless telemetry is typically preferred as it frees the driver to concentrate on driving, which can be dangerous in such a small, lightweight car. The Solar Electric Vehicle system was designed and engineered as an easy to install (2 to 3 hours) integrated accessory system with a custom molded low profile solar module, supplemental battery pack and a proven charge controlling system.</p>
<p>As an alternative, a battery-powered electric vehicle may use a solar array to recharge; the array may be connected to the general electrical distribution grid.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_buses">Solar buses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar buses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_bus" title="Solar bus">Solar bus</a></div>
<p>Solar buses are propulsed by solar energy, all or part of which is collected from stationary solar panel installations. The <a href="/wiki/Tindo" class="mw-redirect" title="Tindo">Tindo</a> bus is a 100% solar bus that operates as <a href="/wiki/Free_public_transport" title="Free public transport">free public transport</a> service in <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> City as an initiative of the City Council.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bus services which use <a href="/wiki/Electric_bus" title="Electric bus">electric buses</a> that are partially powered by solar panels installed on the bus roof, intended to reduce energy consumption and to prolong the life cycle of the rechargable battery of the electric bus, have been put in place in China.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solar buses are to be distinguished from conventional buses in which electric functions of the bus such as lighting, heating or air-conditioning, but not the propulsion itself, are fed by solar energy. Such systems are more widespread as they allow bus companies to meet specific regulations, for example the <a href="/wiki/Anti-idling" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-idling">anti-idling</a> laws that are in force in several of the US states, and can be retrofitted to existing vehicle batteries without changing the conventional engine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-track_vehicles">Single-track vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Single-track vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first solar "cars" were actually tricycles or <a href="/wiki/Quadracycle" title="Quadracycle">Quadracycles</a> built with bicycle technology. These were called solarmobiles at the first solar race, the <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_Sol" title="Tour de Sol">Tour de Sol</a> in Switzerland in 1985. With 72 participants, half used solar power exclusively while the other half used solar-human-powered hybrids. A few true solar bicycles were built, either with a large solar roof, a small rear panel, or a trailer with a solar panel. Later more practical solar bicycles were built with foldable panels to be set up only during parking. Even later the panels were left at home, feeding into the electric mains, and the bicycles charged from the mains. Today highly developed <a href="/wiki/Electric_bicycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric bicycles">electric bicycles</a> are available and these use so little power that it costs little to buy the equivalent amount of solar electricity. The "solar" has evolved from actual hardware to an indirect accounting system. The same system also works for electric motorcycles, which were also first developed for the <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_Sol" title="Tour de Sol">Tour de Sol</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Venturi Astrolab in 2006 was the world's first commercial electro-solar hybrid car, and was originally due to be released in January 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2007 a partnership of Canadian companies led by Hymotion altered a <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a> to use solar cells to generate up to 240 watts of electrical power in full sunshine. This is reported as permitting up to 15&#160;km extra range on a sunny summer day<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> while using only the electric motors.</p>
<p>An inventor from Michigan, USA built a street legal, licensed, insured, solar charged electric scooter in 2005. It had a top speed controlled at a bit over 30&#160;mph, and used fold-out solar panels to charge the batteries while parked.<sup id="cite_ref-scooter_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scooter-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auxiliary_power">Auxiliary power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Auxiliary power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Photovoltaic modules are used commercially as <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit" title="Auxiliary power unit">auxiliary power units</a> on passenger cars<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> in order to ventilate the car, reducing the temperature of the passenger compartment while it is parked in the sun. Vehicles such as the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius#2009.E2.80.93_.28model_ZVW30.29" title="Toyota Prius">Prius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aptera_2_Series#Accessories_and_interior" title="Aptera 2 Series">Aptera 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audi_A8#D2" title="Audi A8">Audi A8</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mazda_929#1990" title="Mazda 929">Mazda 929</a> have had solar <a href="/wiki/Sunroof#Sunroof_Types" title="Sunroof">sunroof</a> options for ventilation purposes.</p>
<p>The area of photovoltaic modules required to power a car with conventional design is too large to be carried on board. A prototype car and trailer has been built <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.solartaxi.com/">Solar Taxi</a>. According to the website, it is capable of 100&#160;km/day using 6m<sup>2</sup> of standard crystalline silicon cells. Electricity is stored using a <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_battery#Zebra_battery" title="Molten salt battery">nickel/salt battery</a>. A stationary system such as a rooftop solar panel, however, can be used to charge conventional electric vehicles.</p>
<p>It is also possible to use solar panels to extend the range of a hybrid or electric car, as incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Fisker_Karma" title="Fisker Karma">Fisker Karma</a>, available as an option on the <a href="/wiki/Chevy_Volt" class="mw-redirect" title="Chevy Volt">Chevy Volt</a>, on the hood and roof of "Destiny 2000" modifications of <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Fiero" title="Pontiac Fiero">Pontiac Fieros</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Italdesign_Quaranta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Italdesign Quaranta (page does not exist)">Italdesign Quaranta</a>, Free Drive EV <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Bug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solar Bug (page does not exist)">Solar Bug</a>, and numerous other electric vehicles, both concept and production. In May 2007 a partnership of Canadian companies led by Hymotion added PV cells to a <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a> to extend the range.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.solarelectricalvehicles.com/">SEV</a> claims 20 miles per day from their combined 215W module mounted on the car roof and an additional 3kWh battery.</p>
<p>On 9 June 2008, the German and French Presidents announced a plan to offer a credit of 6-8g/km of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions for cars fitted with technologies "not yet taken into consideration during the standard measuring cycle of the emissions of a car".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This has given rise to speculation that photovoltaic panels might be widely adopted on autos in the near future<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also technically possible to use photovoltaic technology, (specifically <a href="/wiki/Thermophotovoltaic" title="Thermophotovoltaic">thermophotovoltaic</a> (TPV) technology) to provide motive power for a car. Fuel is used to heat an emitter. The infrared radiation generated is converted to electricity by a low band gap PV cell (e.g. GaSb). A prototype TPV hybrid car was even built. The "Viking 29"<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> was the World’s first thermophotovoltaic (TPV) powered automobile, designed and built by the Vehicle Research Institute (VRI) at Western Washington University. Efficiency would need to be increased and cost decreased to make TPV competitive with fuel cells or internal combustion engines.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Railway presents a low rolling resistance option that would be beneficial of planned journeys and stops.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> PV panels were tested as APUs on Italian rolling stock under EU project.<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.trenitalia.com/en/trenitalia/pvtrain/pvtrain_6/Newsletter1.html">PVTRAIN</a></p>
<p>PVTrain concluded that the most interest for PV in rail transport was on freight cars where on board electrical power would allow new functionality:</p>
<ul>
<li>GPS or other positioning devices, so as to improve its use in fleet management and efficiency.</li>
<li>Electric locks, a video monitor and remote control system for cars with sliding doors, so as to reduce the risk of robbery for valuable goods.</li>
<li>ABS brakes, which would raise the maximum velocity of freight cars to 160&#160;km/h, improving productivity.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Kismaros – Királyrét narrow-gauge line near Budapest has built a solar powered railcar called 'Vili'. With a maximum speed of 25&#160;km/h, 'Vili' is driven by two 7&#160;kW motors capable of regenerative braking and powered by 9.9m2 of PV panels. Electricity is stored in on-board batteries.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In addition to on-board solar panels, there is the possibility to use stationary (off-board) panels to generate electricity specifically for use in transport.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few pilot plants have been built in the framework of the "Heliotram" project, such as the tram depots in Hannover Leinhausen<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and Geneva (Bachet de Pesay).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The 150&#160;kW<sub>p</sub> Geneva site injected 600V DC directly into the tram/trolleybus electricity network provided about 1% of the electricity used by the Geneva transport network at its opening in 1999.</p>
<p>Direct feed to a DC grids avoids losses through DC to AC conversion.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> DC grids are only to be found in electric powered transport: railways, trams and trolleybuses.</p>
<p>Indian railways announced their intention to use on board PV to run air conditioning systems in railway coaches.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, Indian Railways announced it's all set to conduct a trial run by the end of May 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It hopes that an average of 90,800 liters of diesel per train will be saved on an annual basis, which in turn results in reduction of 239 tones of CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>

<p>Solar powered boats have mainly been limited to rivers and canals, but in 2007 an experimental 14m catamaran, the Sun21 sailed the Atlantic from <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> to <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, and from there to New York.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It was the first crossing of the Atlantic powered only by solar.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Japan's biggest shipping line Nippon Yusen KK and <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Oil_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nippon Oil Corporation">Nippon Oil Corporation</a> said solar panels capable of generating 40 kilowatts of electricity would be placed on top of a 60,213 ton <a href="/wiki/Car_carrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Car carrier">car carrier</a> ship to be used by <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Motor_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Toyota Motor Corporation">Toyota Motor Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BBranor_PlanetSolar" title="Tûranor PlanetSolar">Tûranor PlanetSolar</a>, a 30 metre long, 15.2 metre wide <a href="/wiki/Catamaran" title="Catamaran">catamaran</a> yacht powered by 470 square metres of solar panels, was unveiled. It is, so far, the largest solar-powered boat ever built.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2012, PlanetSolar became the first ever solar electric vehicle to <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigate</a> the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-Gieffers_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gieffers-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various demonstration systems have been made. Curiously, none yet takes advantage of the huge power gain that water cooling would bring.</p>
<p>The low power density of current solar panels limits the use of solar propelled vessels, however boats that use sails (which do not generate electricity unlike combustion engines) rely on battery power for electrical appliances (such as refrigeration, lighting and communications). Here solar panels have become popular for recharging batteries as they do not create noise, require fuel and often can be seamlessly added to existing deck space.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Solar ships can refer to solar powered airships or hybrid airships.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is considerable military interest in <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> (UAVs); solar power would enable these to stay aloft for months, becoming a much cheaper means of doing some tasks done today by satellites. In September 2007, the first successful flight for 48h under constant power of a UAV was reported.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This is likely to be the first commercial use for photovoltaics in flight.</p>
<p>Many demonstration solar aircraft have been built, some of the best known by <a href="/wiki/AeroVironment" title="AeroVironment">AeroVironment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unmanned_aerial_vehicles">Unmanned aerial vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unmanned aerial vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/NASA_Pathfinder" title="NASA Pathfinder">Pathfinder and Pathfinder-Plus</a> - This UAV demonstrated that an airplane could stay aloft for an extended period of time fueled purely by solar power.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/NASA_Pathfinder" title="NASA Pathfinder">Helios</a> - Derived from the Pathfinder-Plus, this <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a> powered UAV set a world record for flight at 96,863 feet (29,524 m).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zephyr_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zephyr (aircraft)">Zephyr</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> - built by Qinetiq, this UAV set the unofficial world record for longest duration unmanned flight at over 82 hours on 31 July 2008. Just 15 days after the Solar Impulse flight mentioned above, on 23 July 2010 the Zephyr, a lightweight <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicle</a> engineered by the United Kingdom defence firm <a href="/wiki/QinetiQ" class="mw-redirect" title="QinetiQ">QinetiQ</a>, claimed the endurance record for an unmanned aerial vehicle. It flew in the skies of Arizona for over two weeks (336 hours). It has also soared to over 70,700 feet (21.5&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC20100723_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20100723-39">[39]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space">Space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_powered_spacecraft">Solar powered spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar powered spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg/220px-ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg/330px-ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg/440px-ISS_on_20_August_2001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">Solar panels on spacecraft</a></div>
<p>Solar energy is often used to supply power for satellites and spacecraft operating in the inner solar system since it can supply energy for a long time without excess fuel mass. A <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">Communications satellite</a> contains multiple radio transmitters which operate continually during its life. It would be uneconomic to operate such a vehicle (which may be on-orbit for years) from primary <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>, and refuelling in <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">orbit</a> is not practical. Solar power is not generally used to adjust the satellite's position, however, and the useful life of a communications satellite will be limited by the on-board station-keeping fuel supply.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_propelled_spacecraft">Solar propelled spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Solar propelled spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few spacecraft operating within the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> have used solar power as an energy source for their propulsion system.</p>
<p>All current solar powered spacecraft use <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar panels">solar panels</a> in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Electrically_powered_spacecraft_propulsion" title="Electrically powered spacecraft propulsion">electric propulsion</a>, typically <a href="/wiki/Ion_thruster" title="Ion thruster">ion drives</a> as this gives a very high exhaust velocity, and reduces the propellant over that of a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> by more than a factor of ten. Since propellant is usually the biggest mass on many spacecraft, this reduces launch costs.</p>
<p>Other proposals for solar spacecraft include <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar thermal">solar thermal</a> heating of propellant, typically hydrogen or sometimes water is proposed. An <a href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_tether" title="Electrodynamic tether">electrodynamic tether</a> can be used to change a satellite's orientation or adjust its orbit.</p>
<p>Another concept for solar propulsion in space is the <a href="/wiki/Light_sail" class="mw-redirect" title="Light sail">light sail</a>; this doesn't require conversion of light to electrical energy, instead relying directly on the tiny but persistent <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> of light.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_exploration">Planetary exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Planetary exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the most successful solar-propelled vehicles have been the "rovers" used to explore surfaces of the Moon and Mars. The 1977 <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_programme" title="Lunokhod programme">Lunokhod programme</a> and the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a> used solar power to propel remote controlled vehicles. The operating life of these rovers far exceeded the limits of endurance that would have been imposed, had they been operated on conventional fuels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_vehicle_with_solar_assist">Electric vehicle with solar assist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Electric vehicle with solar assist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Swiss project, called "Solartaxi", has circumnavigated the world. This is the first time in history an electric vehicle (not self sufficient solar vehicle) has gone around the world, covering 50000&#160;km in 18 months and crossing 40 countries. It is a road-worthy electric vehicle hauling a trailer with solar panels, carrying a 6 m² sized solar array. The Solartaxi has <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_battery#Zebra_battery" title="Molten salt battery">Zebra batteries</a>, which permit a range of 400&#160;km without recharging. The car can also run for 200&#160;km without the trailer. Its maximum speed is 90&#160;km/h. The car weighs 500&#160;kg and the trailer weighs 200&#160;kg. According to initiator and tour director <a href="/wiki/Louis_Palmer" title="Louis Palmer">Louis Palmer</a>, the car in mass production could be produced for 16000 Euro. Solartaxi has toured the World from July 2007 till December 2008 to show that solutions to stop global warming are available and to encourage people in pursuing alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Palmer suggests the most economical location for solar panels for an electric car is on building rooftops though,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> likening it to putting money into a bank in one location and withdrawing it in another.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation">

<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><img alt="Unbalanced scales.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/45px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png" width="45" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/68px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/90px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="354" /></div>

<p>There are limits to using photovoltaic (PV) cells for vehicles:</p>
<ul>
<li>Power density: Power from a solar array is limited by the size of the vehicle and area that can be exposed to sunlight. This can also be overcome by adding a flatbed and connecting it to the car and this gives more area for panels for powering the car. While energy can be accumulated in batteries to lower peak demand on the array and provide operation in sunless conditions, the battery adds weight and cost to the vehicle. The power limit can be mitigated by use of conventional electric cars supplied by solar (or other) power, recharging from the electrical grid.</li>
<li>Cost: While sunlight is free, the creation of PV cells to capture that sunlight is expensive. Costs for solar panels are steadily declining (22% cost reduction per doubling of production volume).</li>
<li>Design considerations: Even though sunlight has no lifespan, PV cells do. The lifetime of a solar module is approximately 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Standard photovoltaics often come with a warranty of 90% (from nominal power) after 10 years and 80% after 25 years. Mobile applications are unlikely to require lifetimes as long as building integrated PV and solar parks. Current PV panels are mostly designed for stationary installations. However, to be successful in mobile applications, PV panels need to be designed to withstand vibrations. Also, solar panels, especially those incorporating glass, have significant weight. In order for its addition to be of value, a solar panel must provide energy equivalent to or greater than the energy consumed to propel its weight.</li>
</ul>
