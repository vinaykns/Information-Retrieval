			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Global dimming</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

Dozens of fires burning on the surface (red dots) and a thick pall of smoke and haze (greyish pixels) filling the skies overhead in <a href="/wiki/East_China" title="East China">Eastern China</a>. <p><b>Global dimming</b> is the gradual reduction in the amount of global direct <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface that was observed for several decades after the start of systematic measurements in the 1950s. The effect varies by location, but worldwide it has been estimated to be of the order of a 4% reduction over the three decades from 1960–1990. However, after discounting an anomaly caused by the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in 1991, a very slight reversal in the overall trend has been observed.<sup id="cite_ref-AR4_9_2_2_2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR4_9_2_2_2-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Global dimming is thought to have been caused by an increase in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulates</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfate_aerosol" title="Sulfate aerosol">sulfate aerosols</a> in the atmosphere due to human action.</p>
<p>It has interfered with the <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a> by reducing evaporation and may have reduced rainfall in some areas. Global dimming also creates a <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">cooling</a> effect that may have partially counteracted the effect of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_and_effects">Causes and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">Albedo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Insolation">insolation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_cloud" title="Anthropogenic cloud">Anthropogenic cloud</a></div>
<p>It is thought that global dimming is probably due to the increased presence of <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particles</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> caused by human action.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Aerosols and other particulates absorb solar energy and reflect sunlight back into space. The pollutants can also become <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">nuclei</a> for cloud droplets. Water droplets in <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> <a href="/wiki/Coalescence_(physics)" title="Coalescence (physics)">coalesce</a> around the particles.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Increased pollution causes more particulates and thereby creates clouds consisting of a greater number of smaller droplets (that is, the same amount of water is spread over more droplets). The smaller droplets make clouds more <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">reflective</a>, so that more incoming sunlight is reflected back into space and less reaches the Earth's surface. This same effect also reflects radiation from below, trapping it in the lower atmosphere. In models, these smaller droplets also decrease rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-YunQian_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YunQian-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clouds intercept both heat from the sun and heat radiated from the Earth. Their effects are complex and vary in time, location, and altitude. Usually during the daytime the interception of sunlight predominates, giving a cooling effect; however, at night the re-radiation of heat to the Earth slows the Earth's heat loss, this causes <a href="/wiki/Storms" class="mw-redirect" title="Storms">storms</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> rains and <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">Climate model</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">pyranometer</a></div>
<p>In the late-1960s, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Ivanovich_Budyko" class="mw-redirect" title="Mikhail Ivanovich Budyko">Mikhail Ivanovich Budyko</a> worked with simple two-dimensional energy-balance climate models to investigate the <a href="/wiki/Reflectivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> of ice.<sup id="cite_ref-Budyko_1969_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Budyko_1969-5">[5]</a></sup> He found that the ice-albedo feedback created a positive feedback loop in the Earth's climate system. The more snow and ice, the more solar radiation is reflected back into space and hence the colder Earth grows and the more it snows. Other studies found that pollution or a volcano eruption could provoke the onset of an ice age.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s, <a href="/wiki/Atsumu_Ohmura" title="Atsumu Ohmura">Atsumu Ohmura</a>, a geography researcher at the <a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">Swiss Federal Institute of Technology</a>, found that solar radiation striking the Earth's surface had declined by more than 10% over the three previous decades. His findings appeared to contradict <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> — the global temperature had been generally rising since the 70s. Less light reaching the earth seemed to mean that it should cool. Ohmura published his findings "Secular variation of global radiation in Europe" in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Ohmura_and_Lang_1989_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ohmura_and_Lang_1989-8">[8]</a></sup> This was soon followed by others: Viivi Russak in 1990 "Trends of solar radiation, cloudiness and atmospheric transparency during recent decades in Estonia",<sup id="cite_ref-Russak_1990_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Russak_1990-9">[9]</a></sup> and Beate Liepert in 1994 "Solar radiation in Germany — Observed trends and an assessment of their causes".<sup id="cite_ref-Liepert_et_al._2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liepert_et_al._2004-10">[10]</a></sup> Dimming has also been observed in sites all over the former Soviet Union.<sup id="cite_ref-Abakumova_1996_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abakumova_1996-11">[11]</a></sup> Gerry Stanhill who studied these declines worldwide in many papers (see references) coined the term "global dimming".<sup id="cite_ref-Stanhill_and_Moreshet_1992_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanhill_and_Moreshet_1992-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Independent research in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in the late 1980s showed an apparent reduction in the amount of sunlight,<sup id="cite_ref-LightensUp_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LightensUp-13">[13]</a></sup> despite widespread evidence that the climate was becoming hotter. The rate of dimming varies around the world but is on average estimated at around 2–3% per decade. The trend reversed in the early 1990s. <sup id="cite_ref-AR4_9_2_2_2_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR4_9_2_2_2-1">[1]</a></sup> It is difficult to make a precise measurement, due to the difficulty in accurately <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">calibrating</a> the instruments used, and the problem of spatial coverage. Nonetheless, the effect is almost certainly present.</p>
<p>The effect (2–3%, as above) is due to changes within the Earth's atmosphere; the value of the solar radiation at the top of the atmosphere has not changed by more than a fraction of this amount.<sup id="cite_ref-Eddy_et_al._1982_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eddy_et_al._1982-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg/300px-Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg" width="300" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg/450px-Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg/600px-Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="239" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">Smog</a>, seen here at the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a>, is a likely contributor to global dimming.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The effect varies greatly over the planet, but estimates of the terrestrial surface average value are:</p>
<ul>
<li>5.3% (9 W/m²); over 1958–85 (Stanhill and Moreshet, 1992)<sup id="cite_ref-Stanhill_and_Moreshet_1992_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanhill_and_Moreshet_1992-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>2%/decade over 1964–93 (Gilgen <i>et al.</i>, 1998)<sup id="cite_ref-Gilgen_et_al._1998_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilgen_et_al._1998-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>2.7%/decade (total 20 W/m²); up to 2000 (Stanhill and Cohen, 2001)<sup id="cite_ref-Stanhill_and_Cohen_2001_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanhill_and_Cohen_2001-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>4% over 1961–90 (Liepert 2002)<sup id="cite_ref-Liepert_2002_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liepert_2002-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Note that these numbers are for the terrestrial surface and not really a global average. Whether dimming (or brightening) occurred over the ocean has been a bit of an unknown though a specific measurement (see below, Causes) measured effects some 400 miles (643.7&#160;km) from India over the Indian Ocean towards the Maldives Islands. Regional effects probably dominate but are not strictly confined to the land area, and the effects will be driven by regional air circulation. A 2009 review by Wild et al.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> found that widespread variation in regional and time effects. There was solar brightening beyond 2000 at numerous stations in Europe, the United States, and Korea. The brightening seen at sites in Antarctica during the 1990s, influenced by recovering from the Mount Pinatubo <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic eruption">volcanic eruption</a> in 1991, fades after 2000. The brightening tendency also seems to level off at sites in Japan. In China there is some indication for a renewed dimming, after the stabilization in the 1990s. A continuation of the long-lasting dimming is also noted at the sites in India. Overall, the available data suggest continuation of the brightening beyond the year 2000 at numerous locations, yet less pronounced and coherent than during the 1990s, with more regions with no clear changes or declines. Therefore, globally, greenhouse warming after 2000 may be less modulated by surface <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">solar variations</a> than in prior decades. The largest reductions are found in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> mid-latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Visible light and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation seem to be most affected rather than the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> part of the spectrum.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan_evaporation_data">Pan evaporation data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pan evaporation data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Pan_evaporation" title="Pan evaporation">Pan evaporation</a></div>
<p>Over the last 50 or so years, <a href="/wiki/Pan_evaporation" title="Pan evaporation">pan evaporation</a> has been carefully monitored. For decades, nobody took much notice of the pan evaporation measurements<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. But in the 1990s in Europe, Israel, and North America, scientists spotted something that at the time was considered very strange: the rate of evaporation was falling although they had expected it to increase due to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Roderick_and_Farquhar_2002_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roderick_and_Farquhar_2002-21">[21]</a></sup> The same trend has been observed in China over a similar period. A decrease in solar irradiance is cited as the driving force. However, unlike in other areas of the world, in China the decrease in solar irradiance was not always accompanied by an increase in cloud cover and precipitation. It is believed that <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">aerosols</a> may play a critical role in the decrease of solar irradiance in China.<sup id="cite_ref-Liu_et_al._2004_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu_et_al._2004-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/BBC_Horizon" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Horizon">BBC Horizon</a> producer <a href="/wiki/David_Sington" title="David Sington">David Sington</a> believes that many climate scientists regard the pan evaporation data as the most convincing evidence of solar dimming.<sup id="cite_ref-Sington_2005_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sington_2005-23">[23]</a></sup> Pan evaporation experiments are easy to reproduce with low-cost equipment. There are many pans used for agriculture all over the world and in many instances the data have been collected for nearly a half century. However, pan evaporation depends on factors besides net radiation from the sun. The other two major factors are vapor pressure deficit and wind speed.<sup id="cite_ref-pe_attribution_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pe_attribution-24">[24]</a></sup> The ambient temperature turns out to be a negligible factor. The pan evaporation data corroborates the data gathered by <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometer</a><sup id="cite_ref-Stanhill_and_Cohen_2001_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanhill_and_Cohen_2001-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Roderick_and_Farquhar_2002_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roderick_and_Farquhar_2002-21">[21]</a></sup> and fills in the gaps in the data obtained using <a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">pyranometers</a>. With adjustments to these factors, pan evaporation data has been compared to results of climate simulations.<sup id="cite_ref-Rotstayn_et_al._2006_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotstayn_et_al._2006-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Probable_causes">Probable causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Probable causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Particulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Particulate">Particulate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">Black carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">Contrail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">Volcanic ash</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG/220px-Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG/330px-Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG/440px-Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="864" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sfc.contrail.1.26.01.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> photograph showing aircraft <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a> and natural clouds. The temporary disappearance of contrails over North America due to plane groundings after the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, and the resulting increase in <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">diurnal</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_temperature" title="Atmospheric temperature">temperature range</a> gave <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a> of the effect of thin ice clouds at the Earth's surface.<sup id="cite_ref-contrails_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-contrails-26">[26]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The incomplete combustion of fossil fuels (such as <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>) and wood releases <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> into the air. Though black carbon, most of which is <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>, is an extremely small component of air pollution at land surface levels, the phenomenon has a significant heating effect on the atmosphere at altitudes above two kilometers (6,562&#160;ft). Also, it dims the surface of the ocean by absorbing solar radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experiments in the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> (comparing the atmosphere over the northern and southern islands) in the 1990s showed that the effect of macroscopic pollutants in the atmosphere at that time (blown south from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>) caused about a 10% reduction in sunlight reaching the surface in the area under the <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> cloud — a much greater reduction than expected from the presence of the particles themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Prior to the research being undertaken, predictions were of a 0.5–1% effect from <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate</a> matter; the variation from prediction may be explained by cloud formation with the particles acting as the focus for droplet creation. Clouds are very effective at <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflecting</a> light back out into space.</p>
<p>The phenomenon underlying global dimming may also have regional effects. While most of the earth has warmed, the regions that are downwind from major sources of air pollution (specifically sulfur dioxide emissions) have generally cooled. This may explain the cooling of the eastern United States relative to the warming western part.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>However some research shows that black carbon will increase global warming, being second only to CO<sub>2</sub>. They believe that soot will absorb solar energy and transport it to other areas such as the Himalayas where glacial melting occurs. It can also darken Arctic ice reducing reflectivity and increasing absorption of solar radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some climate scientists have theorized that aircraft <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a> (also called vapor trails) are implicated in global dimming, but the constant flow of air traffic previously meant that this could not be tested. The near-total shutdown of <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil air traffic</a> during the three days following the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a> afforded a unique opportunity in which to observe the climate of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> absent from the effect of contrails. During this period, an increase in <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">diurnal</a> temperature variation of over 1&#160;°C (1.8&#160;°F) was observed in some parts of the U.S., i.e. aircraft contrails may have been raising nighttime temperatures and/or lowering daytime temperatures by much more than previously thought.<sup id="cite_ref-contrails_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-contrails-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Airborne <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> can reflect the Sun's rays back into space and thereby contribute to cooling the planet. Dips in earth temperatures have been observed after large volcano eruptions such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Agung" title="Mount Agung">Mount Agung</a> in Bali that erupted in 1963, El Chichon (Mexico) 1983, <a href="/wiki/Nevado_del_Ruiz" title="Nevado del Ruiz">Ruiz</a> (Colombia) 1985, and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Pinatubo</a> (Philippines) 1991. But even for major eruptions, the ash clouds remain only for relatively short periods.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_reversal_of_the_trend">Recent reversal of the trend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent reversal of the trend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act (United States)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerosol_dimming.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Aerosol_dimming.jpg" width="500" height="207" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="207" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerosol_dimming.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sun-blocking <a href="/wiki/Aerosols" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a> around the world steadily declined (red line) since the 1991 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a>, according to satellite estimates. Credit: Michael Mishchenko, NASA</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wild <i>et al.</i>, using measurements over land, report brightening since 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-LightensUp_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LightensUp-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wild_et_al._2005_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wild_et_al._2005-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wild_et_al._2007_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wild_et_al._2007-33">[33]</a></sup> and Pinker <i>et al.</i><sup id="cite_ref-Pinker_et_al._2005_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinker_et_al._2005-34">[34]</a></sup> found that slight dimming continued over land while brightening occurred over the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Hence, over the land surface, Wild <i>et al.</i> and Pinker <i>et al.</i> disagree. A 2007 <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> sponsored satellite-based study sheds light on the puzzling observations by other scientists that the amount of sunlight reaching Earth's surface had been steadily declining in recent decades, began to reverse around 1990. This switch from a "global dimming" trend to a "brightening" trend happened just as global aerosol levels started to decline.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kerr_2007_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerr_2007-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is likely that at least some of this change, particularly over Europe, is due to decreases in airborne pollution. Most governments of <a href="/wiki/Developed_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed nation">developed nations</a> have taken steps to reduce aerosols released into the atmosphere, which helps reduce global dimming.</p>
<p>Sulfate aerosols have declined significantly since 1970 with the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> in the United States and similar policies in Europe. The Clean Air Act was strengthened in 1977 and 1990. According to the EPA, from 1970 to 2005, total emissions of the six principal air pollutants, including PM’s, dropped by 53% in the US. In 1975, the masked effects of trapped greenhouse gases finally started to emerge and have dominated ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Baseline_Surface_Radiation_Network" title="Baseline Surface Radiation Network">Baseline Surface Radiation Network</a> (BSRN) has been collecting surface measurements. BSRN was started in the early 1990s and updated the archives in this time. Analysis of recent data reveals that the surface of the planet has brightened by about 4% in the past decade. The brightening trend is corroborated by other data, including satellite analyses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_hydrological_cycle">Relationship to hydrological cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to hydrological cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Climate_Change_Attribution.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png/250px-Climate_Change_Attribution.png" width="250" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png/375px-Climate_Change_Attribution.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="573" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Climate_Change_Attribution.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This figure shows the level of agreement between a <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate model</a> driven by five factors and the <a href="/wiki/Historical_temperature_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical temperature record">historical temperature record</a>. The negative component identified as "sulfate" is associated with the aerosol emissions blamed for global dimming.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrological cycle">Hydrological cycle</a></div>
<p>Pollution produced by humans may be seriously weakening the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a> — reducing rainfall and threatening fresh water supplies. A 2001 study by researchers at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography suggests that tiny particles of soot and other pollutants have a significant effect on the hydrological cycle. According to <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">Veerabhadran Ramanathan</a>, "the energy for the hydrological cycle comes from sunlight. As sunlight heats the ocean, water escapes into the atmosphere and falls out as rain. So as aerosols cut down sunlight by large amounts, they may be spinning down the hydrological cycle of the planet."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large scale changes in weather patterns may also have been caused by global dimming. Climate modelers speculatively suggest that this reduction in <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> at the surface may have led to the failure of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> in sub-<a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> during the 1970s and 1980s, together with the associated <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famines</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Sahel_drought" title="Sahel drought">Sahel drought</a>, caused by Northern hemisphere pollution cooling the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rotstayn_and_Lohmann_2002_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotstayn_and_Lohmann_2002-39">[39]</a></sup> Because of this, the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_belt" title="Tropical rain belt">Tropical rain belt</a> may not have risen to its northern latitudes, thus causing an absence of seasonal rains. This claim is not universally accepted and is very difficult to test. However a 2009 Chinese study of 50 years of continuous data found that, though most parts of eastern China saw no significant change in the amount of water held by the atmosphere, light rains had decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-YunQian_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YunQian-4">[4]</a></sup> The researchers then modeled the effect of aerosols and also concluded the overall effect was that water drops in polluted cases are up to 50 percent smaller than in pristine skies. They concluded smaller size impedes the formation of rain clouds, and the falling of light rain is beneficial for agriculture. This was a different effect than reducing solar irradiance, but still a direct result from the presence of aerosols.</p>
<p>The 2001 study by researchers at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography concluded that the imbalance between global dimming and global warming at the surface leads to weaker turbulent heat fluxes to the atmosphere. This means globally reduced evaporation and hence precipitation occur in a dimmer and warmer world, which could ultimately lead to a more humid atmosphere in which it rains less.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>A natural form of large scale environmental shading/dimming has been identified that affected the 2006 northern hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Times" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane season</a>. The <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> study found that several major dust storms in June and July in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert sent dust drifting over the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and through several effects caused cooling of the waters, thus dampening the development of hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_global_warming">Relationship to global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship to global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>
<p>Some scientists now consider that the effects of global dimming have masked the effect of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to some extent and that resolving global dimming may therefore lead to increases in predictions of future temperature rise.<sup id="cite_ref-Andreae_et_al._2005_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andreae_et_al._2005-43">[43]</a></sup> According to Beate Liepert, "We lived in a global warming plus a global dimming world and now we are taking out global dimming. So we end up with the global warming world, which will be much worse than we thought it will be, much hotter."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The magnitude of this masking effect is one of the central problems in climate change with significant implications for future climate changes and policy responses to global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-Andreae_et_al._2005_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andreae_et_al._2005-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interactions between the two theories for climate modification have also been studied, as global warming and global dimming are neither mutually exclusive nor contradictory. In a paper published on March 8, 2005 in the American Geophysical Union's Geophysical Research Letters, a research team led by Anastasia Romanou of Columbia University's Department of Applied Physics and Mathematics, New York, also showed that the apparently opposing forces of global warming and global dimming can occur at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Global dimming interacts with global warming by blocking sunlight that would otherwise cause evaporation and the particulates bind to water droplets. Water vapor is the major greenhouse gas. On the other hand, global dimming is affected by evaporation and rain. Rain has the effect of clearing out polluted skies.</p>
<p>Brown clouds have been found to amplify global warming according to Veerabhadran Ramanathan, an atmospheric chemist at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography in La Jolla, CA. "The conventional thinking is that brown clouds have masked as much as 50 percent of global warming by greenhouse gases through so-called global dimming... While this is true globally, this study reveals that over southern and eastern Asia, the soot particles in the brown clouds are in fact amplifying the atmospheric warming trend caused by greenhouse gases by as much as 50 percent."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_use_to_mitigate_global_warming">Possible use to mitigate global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_dimming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Possible use to mitigate global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">Mitigation of global warming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">Albedo</a></div>
<p>Some scientists have suggested using aerosols to stave off the effects of global warming as an emergency <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a> measure.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Budyko" title="Mikhail Budyko">Mikhail Budyko</a> suggested that if global warming became a problem, the planet could be cooled by burning sulfur in the stratosphere, which would create a haze.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> An increase in planetary <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> of just 0.5 percent is sufficient to halve the effect of a CO<sub>2</sub> doubling.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>The simplest solution would be to simply emit more sulfates, which would end up in <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> - the lowest part of the atmosphere. If this were done, Earth would still face many problems, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Using sulfates causes environmental problems such as <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a><sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_V._2006_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_V._2006-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a> causes human health problems<sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_V._2006_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_V._2006-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dimming causes ecological problems such as changes in evaporation and rainfall patterns<sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_V._2006_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_V._2006-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Droughts and/or increased rainfall cause problems for agriculture<sup id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_V._2006_51-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_V._2006-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Aerosol has a relatively short lifetime</li>
</ul>
<p>The solution advocated is transporting sulfates into the next higher layer of the atmosphere - <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Aerosols in the stratosphere last years instead of weeks - so only a relatively smaller (though still large) amount of sulfate emissions would be necessary, and side effects would be less. This would require developing an efficient way to transport large amounts of gases into stratosphere, many of which have been proposed though none are known to be effective or economically viable.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a blog post, <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Schmidt" title="Gavin Schmidt">Gavin Schmidt</a> stated that "Ideas that we should increase aerosol emissions to counteract global warming have been described as a '<a href="/wiki/Deal_with_the_Devil" title="Deal with the Devil">Faustian bargain</a>' because that would imply an ever increasing amount of emissions in order to match the accumulated greenhouse gas in the atmosphere, with ever increasing monetary and health costs."<sup id="cite_ref-gbd1_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gbd1-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
