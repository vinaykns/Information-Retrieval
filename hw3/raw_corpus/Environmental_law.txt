<p><b>Environmental law</b> - or "environmental and natural resources law" - is a collective term describing the network of <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Custom_(law)" title="Custom (law)">customary</a> laws addressing the effects of human activity on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_subjects">Regulatory subjects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regulatory subjects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The broad category of "environmental law" may be broken down into a number of more specific <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_economics" title="Regulatory economics">regulatory</a> subjects. While there is no single agreed-upon taxonomy, the core environmental law regimes address environmental <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. A related but distinct set of regulatory regimes, now strongly influenced by environmental legal principles, focus on the management of specific <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, such as forests, minerals, or fisheries. Other areas, such as environmental impact assessment, may not fit neatly into either category, but are nonetheless important components of environmental law.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_assessment">Impact assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">Environmental impact assessment</a></div>
<p>Environmental impact assessment (EA) is the assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental consequences</a> (positive and negative) of a plan, policy, program, or concrete projects prior to the decision to move forward with the proposed action. In this context, the term "<b>environmental impact assessment</b>"(EIA) is usually used when applied to concrete projects by individuals or companies and the term "<a href="/wiki/Strategic_environmental_assessment" title="Strategic environmental assessment">strategic environmental assessment</a>" (SEA) applies to policies, plans and programmes most often proposed by organs of state.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Environmental assessments may be governed by rules of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_law" title="Administrative law">administrative procedure</a> regarding public participation and documentation of decision making, and may be subject to judicial review.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_quality">Air quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Air quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_quality_law" title="Air quality law">Air quality law</a></div>
<p>Air quality laws govern the emission of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollutants</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. A specialized subset of air quality laws regulate <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">the quality of air inside buildings</a>. Air quality laws are often designed specifically to protect human health by limiting or eliminating airborne pollutant concentrations. Other initiatives are designed to address broader ecological problems, such as limitations on chemicals that affect the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">emissions trading</a> programs to address <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a>. Regulatory efforts include identifying and categorizing air pollutants, setting limits on acceptable emissions levels, and dictating necessary or appropriate mitigation technologies.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_quality_law" title="Water quality law">Water quality law</a></div>
<p>Water quality laws govern the <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">release of pollutants into water resources</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground water">ground water</a>, and stored <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>. Some water quality laws, such as drinking water regulations, may be designed solely with reference to human health. Many others, including restrictions on the alteration of the <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">chemical, physical, radiological, and biological characteristics of water resources</a>, may also reflect efforts to protect <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystems</a> more broadly. Regulatory efforts may include identifying and categorizing water pollutants, dictating acceptable pollutant concentrations in water resources, and limiting pollutant discharges from effluent sources. Regulatory areas include <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment and disposal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">industrial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_wastewater_treatment" title="Agricultural wastewater treatment">agricultural</a> waste water management, and control of <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> from construction sites and urban environments.</p>

<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Waste_management_law" title="Waste management law">Waste management law</a></div>
<p>Waste management laws govern the transport, treatment, storage, and disposal of all manner of <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">nuclear waste</a>, among <a href="/wiki/List_of_waste_types" title="List of waste types">many other types</a>. Waste laws are generally designed to minimize or eliminate the uncontrolled dispersal of waste materials into the environment in a manner that may cause ecological or biological harm, and include laws designed to reduce the generation of waste and promote or mandate waste recycling. Regulatory efforts include identifying and categorizing waste types and mandating transport, treatment, storage, and disposal practices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contaminant_cleanup">Contaminant cleanup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contaminant cleanup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_cleanup_law" title="Environmental cleanup law">Environmental cleanup law</a></div>
<p>Environmental cleanup laws govern the removal of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Contaminant" class="mw-redirect" title="Contaminant">contaminants</a> from environmental media such as <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ground_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground water">ground water</a>. Unlike pollution control laws, cleanup laws are designed to respond after-the-fact to environmental contamination, and consequently must often define not only the necessary response actions, but also the parties who may be responsible for undertaking (or paying for) such actions. Regulatory requirements may include rules for emergency response, liability allocation, site assessment, remedial investigation, feasibility studies, remedial action, post-remedial monitoring, and site reuse.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_safety">Chemical safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemical safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chemical safety laws govern the use of <a href="/wiki/Chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemicals">chemicals</a> in human activities, particularly man-made chemicals in modern industrial applications. As contrasted with media-oriented environmental laws (e.g., air or water quality laws), chemical control laws seek to manage the (potential) pollutants themselves. Regulatory efforts include banning specific chemical constituents in consumer products (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">Bisphenol A</a> in plastic bottles), and regulating <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a>.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Water law">Water law</a></div>
<p>Water resources laws govern the ownership and use of <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ground_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground water">ground water</a>. Regulatory areas may include water conservation, use restrictions, and ownership regimes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_resources">Mineral resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mineral resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mining_law" title="Mining law">Mining law</a></div>
<p>Mineral resource laws cover several basic topics, including the ownership of the mineral resource and who can work them. Mining is also affected by various regulations regarding the health and safety of miners, as well as the environmental impact of mining.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_resources">Forest resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forest resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forestry_law" title="Forestry law">Forestry law</a></div>
<p><b>Forestry laws</b> govern activities in designated <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest lands</a>, most commonly with respect to <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forest management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">timber harvesting</a>. Ancillary laws may regulate forest land acquisition and <a href="/wiki/Prescribed_burn" class="mw-redirect" title="Prescribed burn">prescribed burn</a> practices. Forest management laws generally adopt management policies, such as <a href="/wiki/Multiple_use" title="Multiple use">multiple use</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustained_yield" title="Sustained yield">sustained yield</a>, by which public forest resources are to be managed. Governmental agencies are generally responsible for planning and implementing forestry laws on public forest lands, and may be involved in forest inventory, planning, and conservation, and oversight of timber sales. Broader initiatives may seek to slow or reverse <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_and_plants">Wildlife and plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wildlife and plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wildlife laws govern the potential impact of human activity on wild animals, whether directly on individuals or populations, or indirectly via habitat degradation. Similar laws may operate to protect plant species. Such laws may be enacted entirely to protect <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, or as a means for protecting species deemed important for other reasons. Regulatory efforts may including the creation of special <a href="/wiki/Conservation_status" title="Conservation status">conservation statuses</a>, prohibitions on killing, harming, or disturbing protected species, efforts to induce and support species recovery, establishment of wildlife refuges to support conservation, and prohibitions on trafficking in species or animal parts to combat <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish_and_game">Fish and game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fish and game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Game_law" title="Game law">Game law</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_law" title="Fisheries law">Fisheries law</a></div>
<p>Fish and game laws regulate the right to pursue and take or kill certain kinds of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wild_animal" title="Wild animal">wild animal</a> (<a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (food)">game</a>). Such laws may restrict the days to harvest fish or game, the number of animals caught per person, the species harvested, or the weapons or fishing gear used. Such laws may seek to balance dueling needs for preservation and harvest and to manage both <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_biology" title="Population biology">populations</a> of fish and game. Game laws can provide a legal structure to collect <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">license</a> fees and other <a href="/wiki/Federal_Duck_Stamp" title="Federal Duck Stamp">money</a> which is used to fund <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_conservation" title="Wildlife conservation">conservation</a> efforts as well as to obtain harvest information used in <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_management" title="Wildlife management">wildlife management</a> practice.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_principles">Important principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Important principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental law has developed in response to emerging awareness of and concern over issues impacting the entire world. While laws have developed piecemeal and for a variety of reasons, some effort has gone into identifying key concepts and guiding principles common to environmental law as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The principles discussed below are not an exhaustive list and are not universally recognized or accepted. Nonetheless, they represent important principles for the understanding of environmental law around the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_Development">Sustainable Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sustainable Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a></div>
<p>Defined by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> as "development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs," <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> may be considered together with the concepts of "integration" (development cannot be considered in isolation from sustainability) and "interdependence" (social and economic development, and environmental protection, are interdependent).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Laws mandating <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">environmental impact assessment</a> and requiring or encouraging development to minimize environmental impacts may be assessed against this principle.</p>
<p>The modern concept of sustainable development was a topic of discussion at the 1972 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a> (Stockholm Conference), and the driving force behind the 1983 <a href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Environment_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Commission on Environment and Development">World Commission on Environment and Development</a> (WCED, or Bruntland Commission). In 1992, the first UN <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a> resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a>, Principle 3 of which reads: "The right to development must be fulfilled so as to equitably meet developmental and environmental needs of present and future generations." Sustainable development has been a core concept of international environmental discussion ever since, including at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_2002" title="Earth Summit 2002">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> (Earth Summit 2002), and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Sustainable_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development">United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development</a> (Earth Summit 2012, or Rio+20).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equity">Equity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Equity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Intergenerational_equity" title="Intergenerational equity">Intergenerational equity</a></div>
<p>Defined by UNEP to include intergenerational equity - "the right of future generations to enjoy a fair level of the common patrimony" - and intragenerational equity - "the right of all people within the current generation to fair access to the current generation's entitlement to the Earth's natural resources" - environmental equity considers the present generation under an obligation to account for long-term impacts of activities, and to act to sustain the global environment and resource base for future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Pollution control and resource management laws may be assessed against this principle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transboundary_responsibility">Transboundary responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Transboundary responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Defined in the international law context as an obligation to protect one's own environment, and to prevent damage to neighboring environments, UNEP considers transboundary responsibility at the international level as a potential limitation on the rights of the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Laws that act to limit <a href="/wiki/Externalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Externalities">externalities</a> imposed upon human health and the environment may be assessed against this principle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_participation_and_transparency">Public participation and transparency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Public participation and transparency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Identified as essential conditions for "accountable governments . . ., industrial concerns," and organizations generally, public participation and transparency are presented by UNEP as requiring "effective protection of the human right to hold and express opinions and to seek, receive and impart ideas," "a right of access to appropriate, comprehensible and timely information held by governments and industrial concerns on economic and social policies regarding the sustainable use of natural resources and the protection of the environment, without imposing undue financial burdens upon the applicants and with adequate protection of privacy and business confidentiality," and "effective judicial and administrative proceedings." These principles are present in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">environmental impact assessment</a>, laws requiring publication and access to relevant environmental data, and <a href="/wiki/Administrative_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative procedure">administrative procedure</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautionary_principle">Precautionary principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Precautionary principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">Precautionary principle</a></div>
<p>One of the most commonly encountered and controversial principles of environmental law, the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a> formulated the precautionary principle as follows:</p>
<dl>
<dd>In order to protect the environment, the precautionary approach shall be widely applied by States according to their capabilities. Where there are threats of serious or irreversible damage, lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent environmental degradation.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The principle may play a role in any debate over the need for environmental regulation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl>
<dd>The concept of prevention . . . can perhaps better be considered an overarching aim that gives rise to a multitude of legal mechanisms, including prior assessment of environmental harm, licensing or authorization that set out the conditions for operation and the consequences for violation of the conditions, as well as the adoption of strategies and policies. Emission limits and other product or process standards, the use of best available techniques and similar techniques can all be seen as applications of the concept of prevention.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polluter_pays_principle">Polluter pays principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Polluter pays principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polluter_pays_principle" title="Polluter pays principle">Polluter pays principle</a></div>
<p>The polluter pays principle stands for the idea that "the environmental costs of economic activities, including the cost of preventing potential harm, should be internalized rather than imposed upon society at large."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> All issues related to responsibility for cost for <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">environmental remediation</a> and compliance with pollution control regulations involve this principle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early examples of legal enactments designed to consciously preserve the environment, for its own sake or human enjoyment, are found throughout history. In the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, the primary protection was found in the law of <a href="/wiki/Nuisance_in_English_law" title="Nuisance in English law">nuisance</a>, but this only allowed for private actions for damages or injunctions if there was harm to land. Thus smells emanating from <a href="/wiki/Pig_sty" class="mw-redirect" title="Pig sty">pig stys</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a> against dumping rubbish,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or damage from exploding dams.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Private enforcement, however, was limited and found to be woefully inadequate to deal with major environmental threats, particularly threats to common resources. During the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Stink" title="Great Stink">Great Stink</a>" of 1858, the dumping of sewerage into the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> began to smell so ghastly in the summer heat that Parliament had to be evacuated. Ironically, the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Commission_of_Sewers_Act_1848" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Commission of Sewers Act 1848">Metropolitan Commission of Sewers Act 1848</a> had allowed the Metropolitan Commission for Sewers to close <a href="/wiki/Cesspit" title="Cesspit">cesspits</a> around the city in an attempt to "clean up" but this simply led people to pollute the river. In 19 days, Parliament passed a further Act to build the <a href="/wiki/London_sewerage_system" title="London sewerage system">London sewerage system</a>. London also suffered from terrible <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and this culminated in the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Smog" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Smog">Great Smog</a>" of 1952, which in turn triggered its on legislative response: the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_1956" title="Clean Air Act 1956">Clean Air Act 1956</a>. The basic regulatory structure was to set limits on emissions for households and business (particularly burning <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>) while an inspectorate would enforce compliance.</p>
<p>Notwithstanding early analogues, the concept of "environmental law" as a separate and distinct body of law is a twentieth-century development.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The recognition that the natural environment was fragile and in need of special legal protections, the translation of that recognition into legal structures, the development of those structures into a larger body of "environmental law," and the strong influence of environmental law on natural resource laws, did not occur until about the 1960s. At that time, numerous influences - including a growing awareness of the unity and fragility of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>; increased public concern over the impact of industrial activity on natural resources and human health; the increasing strength of the regulatory state; and more broadly the advent and success of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmentalism as a political movement</a> - coalesced to produce a huge new body of law in a relatively short period of time. While the modern history of environmental law is one of continuing controversy, by the end of the twentieth century environmental law had been established as a component of the legal landscape in all <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed nations</a> of the world, many <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing ones</a>, and the larger project of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental law is a continuing source of controversy. Debates over the necessity, fairness, and cost of environmental regulation are ongoing, as well as regarding the appropriateness of regulations vs. market solutions to achieve even agreed-upon ends.</p>
<p>Allegations of scientific uncertainty fuel the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">ongoing debate</a> over greenhouse gas regulation, and are a major factor in debates over whether to ban particular pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In cases where the science is well-settled, it is not unusual to find that corporations intentionally hide or distort the facts, or sow confusion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is very common for regulated industry to argue against environmental regulation on the basis of cost.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Difficulties arise in performing <a href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a> of environmental issues. It is <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">difficult to quantify</a> the value of an environmental value such as a healthy ecosystem, clean air, or species diversity. Many environmentalists' response to pitting economy vs. ecology is summed up by former Senator and founder of <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a> <a href="/wiki/Gaylord_Nelson" title="Gaylord Nelson">Gaylord Nelson</a>, "The economy is a wholly owned subsidiary of the environment, not the other way around."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Furthermore, environmental issues are seen by many as having an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">ethical or moral</a> dimension, which would transcend financial cost. Even so, there are some efforts underway to systemically recognize environmental costs and assets, and account for them properly in economic terms.</p>
<p>While affected industries spark controversy in fighting regulation, there are also many environmentalists and public interest groups who believe that current regulations are inadequate, and advocate for stronger protection.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Environmental law conferences - such as the annual <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon_School_of_Law#Public_Interest_Environmental_Law_Conference" title="University of Oregon School of Law">Public Interest Environmental Law Conference</a> in Eugene, Oregon - typically have this focus, also connecting environmental law with class, race, and other issues.</p>
<p>An additional debate is to what extent environmental laws are fair to all regulated parties. For instance, researchers Preston Teeter and Jorgen Sandberg highlight how smaller organizations can often incur disproportionately larger costs as a result of environmental regulations, which can ultimately create an additional barrier to entry for new firms, thus stifling competition and innovation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_world">Around the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Around the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_laws_by_country" title="List of environmental laws by country">List of environmental laws by country</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_environmental_agreements" title="List of international environmental agreements">List of international environmental agreements</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_law">International law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: International law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protocol" title="Environmental protocol">Environmental protocol</a></div>
<p>Global and regional environmental issues are increasingly the subject of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. Debates over environmental concerns implicate core principles of international law and have been the subject of numerous international agreements and declarations.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">Customary international law</a> is an important source of international environmental law. These are the norms and rules that countries follow as a matter of custom and they are so prevalent that they bind all states in the world. When a principle becomes customary law is not clear cut and many arguments are put forward by states not wishing to be bound. Examples of customary international law relevant to the environment include the duty to warn other states promptly about icons of an environmental nature and environmental damages to which another state or states may be exposed, and Principle 21 of the Stockholm Declaration ('good neighbourliness' or sic utere).</p>
<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_environmental_agreements" title="List of international environmental agreements">legally binding international agreements</a> encompass a wide variety of issue-areas, from terrestrial, marine and atmospheric pollution through to wildlife and biodiversity protection. International environmental agreements are generally <a href="/wiki/Multilateral_treaty" title="Multilateral treaty">multilateral</a> (or sometimes <a href="/wiki/Bilateral_treaty" title="Bilateral treaty">bilateral</a>) <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a> (a.k.a. convention, agreement, protocol, etc.). <a href="/wiki/Treaty#Protocols" title="Treaty">Protocols</a> are subsidiary agreements built from a primary treaty. They exist in many areas of international law but are especially useful in the environmental field, where they may be used to regularly incorporate recent scientific knowledge. They also permit countries to reach agreement on a framework that would be contentious if every detail were to be agreed upon in advance. The most widely known protocol in international environmental law is the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, which followed from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a>.</p>
<p>While the bodies that proposed, argued, agreed upon and ultimately adopted existing international agreements vary according to each agreement, certain conferences, including 1972's <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a>, 1983's <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">World Commission on Environment and Development</a>, 1992's <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_(1992)" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth Summit (1992)">United Nations Conference on Environment and Development</a> and 2002's <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_2002" title="Earth Summit 2002">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> have been particularly important. <a href="/wiki/Multilateral_environmental_agreement" title="Multilateral environmental agreement">Multilateral environmental agreements</a> sometimes create an International Organization, Institution or Body responsible for implementing the agreement. Major examples are the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Fauna_and_Flora" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora</a> (CITES) and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN).</p>
<p>International environmental law also includes the opinions of international courts and tribunals. While there are few and they have limited authority, the decisions carry much weight with legal commentators and are quite influential on the development of international environmental law. One of the biggest challenges in international decisions is to determine an adequate compensation for environmental damages.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The courts include the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a> (ICJ); the international Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS); the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a>; <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and other regional treaty tribunals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Network_for_Environmental_Compliance_and_Enforcement" title="International Network for Environmental Compliance and Enforcement">International Network for Environmental Compliance and Enforcement</a> (INECE), the major environmental issues in Africa are “drought and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, air pollution, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>, freshwater availability, degradation of soil and vegetation, and widespread poverty.” <sup id="cite_ref-INECE_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INECE-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) is focused on the “growing urban and industrial pollution, water quality, electronic waste and indoor air from cookstoves.” <sup id="cite_ref-EPA_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-23">[23]</a></sup> They hope to provide enough aid on concerns regarding pollution before their impacts contaminate the African environment as well as the global environment. By doing so, they intend to “protect human health, particularly vulnerable populations such as children and the poor.” <sup id="cite_ref-EPA_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-23">[23]</a></sup> In order to accomplish these goals in Africa, EPA programs are focused on strengthening the ability to enforce environmental laws as well as public compliance to them. Other programs work on developing stronger environmental laws, regulations, and standards.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Asian Environmental Compliance and Enforcement Network (AECEN) is an agreement between 16 Asian countries dedicated to improving cooperation with environmental laws in Asia. These countries include Cambodia, China, Indonesia, India, Maldives, Japan, Korea, Malaysia, Nepal, Philippines, Pakistan, Singapore, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Vietnam, and Lao PDR.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> issues secondary legislation on environmental issues that are valid throughout the EU (so called regulations) and many directives that must be implemented into national legislation from the 28 member states (national states). Examples are the Regulation (EC) No. 338/97 on the implementation of CITES; or the Natura 2000 network the centerpiece for nature &amp; biodiversity policy, encompassing the bird Directive (79/409/EEC/ changed to 2009/147/EC)and the habitats directive (92/43/EEC). Which are made up of multiple SACs (Special Areas of Conservation, linked to the habitats directive) &amp; SPAs (Special Protected Areas, linked to the bird directive), throughout Europe.</p>
<p>EU legislation is ruled in Article 249 Treaty for the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU). Topics for common EU legislation are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Climate change</li>
<li>Air pollution</li>
<li>Water protection and management</li>
<li>Waste management</li>
<li>Soil protection</li>
<li>Protection of nature, species and biodiversity</li>
<li>Noise pollution</li>
<li>Cooperation for the environment with third countries (other than EU member states)</li>
<li>Civil protection</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> is working with countries in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> to improve “environmental governance, water pollution and water security, clean fuels and vehicles, public participation, and pollution prevention.”<sup id="cite_ref-Middle_East_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Middle_East-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main concerns on environmental issues in the Oceanic Region are “illegal releases of air and water <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a>/timber trade, illegal shipment of <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous wastes</a>, including <a href="/wiki/E-waste" class="mw-redirect" title="E-waste">e-waste</a> and ships slated for destruction, and insufficient institutional structure/lack of enforcement capacity”.<sup id="cite_ref-INECE_Asia_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INECE_Asia-26">[26]</a></sup> The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.sprep.org/">Secretariat of the Pacific Regional Environmental Programme</a> (SPREP) is an international organization between Australia, the Cook Islands, FMS, Fiji, France, Kiribati, Marshall Islands, Nauru, New Zealand, Niue, Palau, PNG, Samoa, Solomon Island, Tonga, Tuvalu, USA, and Vanuatu. The SPREP was established in order to provide assistance in improving and protecting the environment as well as assure sustainable development for future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-SPREP_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPREP-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Environment_Protection_and_Biodiversity_Conservation_Act_1999" title="Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999">Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999</a> is the center piece of environmental legislation in the Australian Government. It sets up the “legal framework to protect and manage nationally and internationally important flora, fauna, ecological communities and heritage places”.<sup id="cite_ref-Australia_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Australia-29">[29]</a></sup> It also focuses on protecting world heritage properties, national heritage properties, wetlands of international importance, nationally threatened species and ecological communities, migratory species, Commonwealth marine areas, <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef_Marine_Park" title="Great Barrier Reef Marine Park">Great Barrier Reef Marine Park</a>, and the environment surrounding nuclear activities.<sup id="cite_ref-Australia_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Australia-29">[29]</a></sup> <i>Commonwealth v Tasmania</i> (1983), also known as the "Tasmanian Dam Case", is the most influential case for Australian environmental law.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Brazilian government created the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Environment_(Brazil)" title="Ministry of the Environment (Brazil)">Ministry of Environment</a> in 1992 in order to develop better strategies of protecting the environment, use natural resources sustainably, and enforce public environmental policies. The Ministry of Environment has authority over policies involving environment, water resources, preservation, and environmental programs involving the Amazon.<sup id="cite_ref-Brazil_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Environment_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of the Environment Act">Department of the Environment Act</a> establishes the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Environment_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of the Environment (Canada)">Department of the Environment</a> in the Canadian government as well as the position <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Environment_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of the Environment (Canada)">Minister of the Environment</a>. Their duties include “the preservation and enhancement of the quality of the natural environment, including water, air and soil quality; renewable resources, including migratory birds and other non-domestic flora and fauna; water; meteorology;"<sup id="cite_ref-Department_of_the_Environment_Act_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Department_of_the_Environment_Act-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Environmental_Protection_Act,_1999" title="Canadian Environmental Protection Act, 1999">Environmental Protection Act</a> is the main piece of Canadian environmental legislation that was put into place March 31, 2000. The Act focuses on “respecting pollution prevention and the protection of the environment and human health in order to contribute to sustainable development."<sup id="cite_ref-CEPA_Canada_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEPA_Canada-33">[33]</a></sup> Other principle federal statutes include the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Environmental_Assessment_Act" title="Canadian Environmental Assessment Act">Canadian Environmental Assessment Act</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Species_at_Risk_Act" title="Species at Risk Act">Species at Risk Act</a>. When provincial and federal legislation are in conflict federal legislation takes precedence, that being said individual provinces can have their own legislation such as Ontario's <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Bill_of_Rights" title="Environmental Bill of Rights">Environmental Bill of Rights</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act_(Ontario)" title="Clean Water Act (Ontario)">Clean Water Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Environmental_Protection_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Environmental Protection of the People's Republic of China">Ministry of Environmental Protection of the People's Republic of China</a></div>
<p>According to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>, "China has been working with great determination in recent years to develop, implement, and enforce a solid environmental law framework. Chinese officials face critical challenges in effectively implementing the laws, clarifying the roles of their national and provincial governments, and strengthening the operation of their legal system."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Explosive economic and industrial growth in China has led to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_China" title="Environmental issues in China">significant environmental degradation</a>, and China is currently in the process of developing more stringent legal controls.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The harmonization of Chinese society and the natural environment is billed as a rising policy priority.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Legitimacy_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Legitimacy-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecuador">Ecuador</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Ecuador">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/2008_Constitution_of_Ecuador" title="2008 Constitution of Ecuador">2008 Constitution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> became the first country in the world to codify the <a href="/wiki/Rights_of_Nature" title="Rights of Nature">Rights of Nature</a>. The Constitution, specifically Articles 10 and 71-74, recognizes the inalienable rights of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> to exist and flourish, gives people the authority to petition on the behalf of ecosystems, and requires the government to remedy violations of these rights. The rights approach is a break away from traditional environmental regulatory systems, which regard nature as property and legalize and manage degradation of the environment rather than prevent it.<sup id="cite_ref-Celdf_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Celdf-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Rights of Nature articles in Ecuador's constitution are part of a reaction to a combination of political, economic, and social phenomena. Ecuador's abusive past with the <a href="/wiki/Oil_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil industry">oil industry</a>, most famously the <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation#Environmental_damage_in_Ecuador" title="Chevron Corporation">class-action litigation</a> against <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>, and the failure of an extraction-based economy and <a href="/wiki/Neoliberal" class="mw-redirect" title="Neoliberal">neoliberal</a> reforms to bring economic prosperity to the region has resulted in the election of a New Leftist regime, led by President <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Correa" title="Rafael Correa">Rafael Correa</a>, and sparked a demand for new approaches to development. In conjunction with this need, the principle of "Buen Vivir," or good living—focused on social, environmental and spiritual wealth versus material wealth—gained popularity among citizens and was incorporated into the new constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-Gudynas_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gudynas-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>The influence of indigenous groups, from whom the concept of "Buen Vivir" originates, in the forming of the constitutional ideals also facilitated the incorporation of the Rights of Nature as a basic tenet of their culture and conceptualization of "Buen Vivir." <sup id="cite_ref-Becker_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Becker-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_Protection_Law_(Egypt)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Environmental Protection Law (Egypt) (page does not exist)">Environmental Protection Law</a> outlines the responsibilities of the Egyptian government to “preparation of draft legislation and decrees pertinent to environmental management, collection of data both nationally and internationally on the state of the environment, preparation of periodical reports and studies on the state of the environment, formulation of the national plan and its projects, preparation of environmental profiles for new and urban areas, and setting of standards to be used in planning for their development, and preparation of an annual report on the state of the environment to be prepared to the President."<sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In India, Environmental law is governed by the Environment Protection Act, 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This act is enforced by the Central Pollution Control Board and the numerous State Pollution Control Boards. Apart from this, there are also individual legislations specifically enacted for the protection of Water, Air, Wildlife, etc. Such legislations include&#160;:-</p>
<ul>
<li>The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974</li>
<li>The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Cess Act, 1977</li>
<li>The Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980</li>
<li>The Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981</li>
<li>Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) (Union Territories) Rules, 1983</li>
<li>The Biological Diversity Act, 2002 and the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972.</li>
<li>Batteries (Management and Handling) Rules, 2001</li>
<li>Recycled Plastics, Plastics Manufacture and Usage Rules, 1999</li>
<li>The National Green Tribunal established under the National Green Tribunal Act of 2010<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> has jurisdiction over all environmental cases dealing with a substantial environmental question and acts covered under the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974;</li>
<li>Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Cess Rules, 1978</li>
<li>Ganga Action Plan, 1986</li>
<li>The Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980</li>
<li>The Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991 and the Biological Diversity Act, 2002. The acts covered under Indian Wild Life Protection Act 1972 do not fall within the jurisdiction of the National Green Tribunal.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Appeals can be filed in the Hon'ble Supreme Court of India.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Basel Convention on Control of TransboundaryMovements on Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal, 1989 and Its Protocols</li>
<li>Hazardous Wastes (Management and Handling) Amendment Rules, 2003<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Basic_Environmental_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Basic Environmental Law (page does not exist)">Basic Environmental Law</a> is the basic structure of Japan’s environmental policies replacing the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Basic_Law_for_Environmental_Pollution_Control&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Basic Law for Environmental Pollution Control (page does not exist)">Basic Law for Environmental Pollution Control</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nature_Conservation_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nature Conservation Law (page does not exist)">Nature Conservation Law</a>. The updated law aims to address “global environmental problems, urban pollution by everyday life, loss of accessible natural environment in urban areas and degrading environmental protection capacity in forests and farmlands.”<sup id="cite_ref-Japan_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Japan-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The three basic environmental principles that the Basic Environmental Law follows are “the blessings of the environment should be enjoyed by the present generation and succeeded to the future generations, a sustainable society should be created where environmental loads by human activities are minimized, and Japan should contribute actively to global environmental conservation through international cooperation.”<sup id="cite_ref-Japan_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Japan-49">[49]</a></sup> From these principles, the Japanese government have established policies such as “environmental consideration in policy formulation, establishment of the Basic Environment Plan which describes the directions of long-term environmental policy, environmental impact assessment for development projects, economic measures to encourage activities for reducing environmental load, improvement of social infrastructure such as sewerage system, transport facilities etc., promotion of environmental activities by corporations, citizens and NGOs, environmental education, and provision of information, promotion of science and technology."<sup id="cite_ref-Japan_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Japan-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_environmental_law" title="New Zealand environmental law">New Zealand environmental law</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_for_the_Environment_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry for the Environment (New Zealand)">Ministry for the Environment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Commissioner_for_the_Environment" title="Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment">Office of the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment</a> were established by the <a href="/wiki/Environment_Act_1986" title="Environment Act 1986">Environment Act 1986</a>. These positions are responsible for advising the Minister on all areas of environmental legislation. A common theme of New Zealand’s environmental legislation is sustainably managing natural and physical resources, fisheries, and forests. The <a href="/wiki/Resource_Management_Act_1991" title="Resource Management Act 1991">Resource Management Act 1991</a> is the main piece of environmental legislation that outlines the government’s strategy to managing the “environment, including air, water soil, biodiversity, the coastal environment, noise, subdivision, and land use planning in general.”<sup id="cite_ref-New_Zealand_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Zealand-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Natural_Resources_and_Environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment">Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment</a> of the Russian Federation makes regulation regarding “conservation of natural resources, including the subsoil, water bodies, forests located in designated conservation areas, fauna and their habitat, in the field of hunting, hydrometeorology and related areas, environmental monitoring and pollution control, including radiation monitoring and control, and functions of public environmental policy making and implementation and statutory regulation."<sup id="cite_ref-Russia_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Russia-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
