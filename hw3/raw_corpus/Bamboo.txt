<p>The <b>bamboos</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="noexcerpt"><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/En-us-bamboo.ogg" title="Listen"><img alt="Listen" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="11" data-file-height="11" /></a><sup><span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-bamboo.ogg" title="File:En-us-bamboo.ogg">i</a></span></sup></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span> are <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Subfamily_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Subfamily (biology)">subfamily</a> Bambusoideae of the grass family <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>. In bamboo, as in other grasses, the internodal regions of the <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stem</a> are usually hollow and the <a href="/wiki/Vascular_bundle" title="Vascular bundle">vascular bundles</a> in the cross section are scattered throughout the stem instead of in a cylindrical arrangement. The <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledonous</a> <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">woody</a> <a href="/wiki/Xylem" title="Xylem">xylem</a> is also absent. The absence of <a href="/wiki/Secondary_growth" title="Secondary growth">secondary growth</a> wood causes the stems of <a href="/wiki/Monocotyledon" title="Monocotyledon">monocots</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a> and large bamboos, to be columnar rather than tapering.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bamboos include some of the fastest-growing plants in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-Farrelly_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrelly-4">[4]</a></sup> due to a unique <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizome</a>-dependent system. Certain species of bamboo can grow 91&#160;cm (3&#160;ft) within a 24-hour period, at a rate of almost 4&#160;cm (1.5&#160;in) an hour (a growth around 1&#160;mm every 90 seconds, or one inch every 40 minutes).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Giant_bamboo" title="Giant bamboo">Giant bamboos</a> are the largest members of the grass family. Bamboos are of notable economic and cultural significance in South Asia, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and East Asia, being used for <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building materials</a>, as a food source, and as a versatile raw product. Bamboo has a higher specific <a href="/wiki/Compressive_strength" title="Compressive strength">compressive strength</a> than wood, brick, or concrete and a specific <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> that rivals steel.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word bamboo comes from the <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a> term <i>bambu</i>, which was introduced to English through <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bamboos have long been considered the most primitive grasses, mostly because of the presence of <a href="/wiki/Bracteate" title="Bracteate">bracteate</a>, indeterminate inflorescences, "pseudospikelets", and flowers with three <a href="/wiki/Lodicule" title="Lodicule">lodicules</a>, six <a href="/wiki/Stamens" class="mw-redirect" title="Stamens">stamens</a>, and three <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigmata</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark.2C_LG_1995_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark.2C_LG_1995-10">[10]</a></sup> Following more recent <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular phylogenetic">molecular phylogenetic</a> research, many <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribes</a> and genera of grasses formerly included in the Bambusoideae are now classified in other subfamilies, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Anomochlooideae" title="Anomochlooideae">Anomochlooideae</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Puelioideae" title="Puelioideae">Puelioideae</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ehrhartoideae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ehrhartoideae">Ehrhartoideae</a>. The subfamily in its current sense belongs to the <a href="/wiki/BOP_clade" title="BOP clade">BOP clade</a> of grasses, where it is sister to the <a href="/wiki/Pooideae" title="Pooideae">Pooideae</a> (bluegrasses and relatives).<sup id="cite_ref-GPWGII_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPWGII-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bamboos contains three clades classified as tribes, and these strongly correspond with geographic divisions representing the New World herbaceous species (<a href="/wiki/Olyreae" title="Olyreae">Olyreae</a>), tropical woody bamboos (<a href="/wiki/Bambuseae" title="Bambuseae">Bambuseae</a>), and temperate woody bamboos (<a href="/wiki/Arundinarieae" title="Arundinarieae">Arundinarieae</a>). The woody bamboos do not form a <a href="/wiki/Monophyletic_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophyletic group">monophyletic group</a>; instead, the tropical woody and herbaceous bamboos are sister to the temperate woody bamboos.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelchner2013_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelchner2013-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GPWGII_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPWGII-9">[9]</a></sup> Altogether, more than 1,400 species are placed in 115 genera.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelchner2013_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelchner2013-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bambusoideae_World_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Bambusoideae_World_map.png/220px-Bambusoideae_World_map.png" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Bambusoideae_World_map.png/330px-Bambusoideae_World_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Bambusoideae_World_map.png/440px-Bambusoideae_World_map.png 2x" data-file-width="1033" data-file-height="517" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bambusoideae_World_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Worldwide distribution of bamboos</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most bamboo are native to warm and moist tropical and warm temperate climates. However, many species are found in diverse climates, from hot tropical regions to cool mountainous regions and highland cloud forests. In the Asia-Pacific region they occur across East Asia, from 50°N latitude in <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> south to Northern Australia, and west to India and the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. China, Japan, Korea, India, and Australia, all have several endemic populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Bystriakova_et_al_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bystriakova_et_al-12">[12]</a></sup> They also occur in small numbers in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>, confined to tropical areas, from southern Senegal in the north to southern Mozambique and Madagascar in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-gorilla_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gorilla-13">[13]</a></sup> In the Americas bamboo has a native range from 47 °S in southern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and the beech forests of central <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, through the South American tropical rainforests, to the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> in Ecuador near 14,000 feet. Bamboo is also native through <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, northward into the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeastern United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Canada and continental Europe is not known to have any native species of bamboo.<sup id="cite_ref-rhs_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, some attempts have been made to grow bamboo on a commercial basis in the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">Great Lakes region</a> of east-central Africa, especially in Rwanda.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the United States, several companies are growing, harvesting, and distributing species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostachys_nigra" title="Phyllostachys nigra">Phyllostachys nigra</a></i> (Henon) and <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostachys_edulis" title="Phyllostachys edulis">Phyllostachys edulis</a></i> (Moso).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bamboos include some of the fastest-growing plants on Earth, with reported growth rates up to 91&#160;cm (36&#160;in) in 24 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-Farrelly_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrelly-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the growth rate is dependent on local soil and climatic conditions, as well as species, and a more typical growth rate for many commonly cultivated bamboos in temperate climates is in the range of 3–10&#160;cm (1.2–3.9&#160;in) per day during the growing period. Primarily growing in regions of warmer climates during the late <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Cretaceous period">Cretaceous period</a>, vast fields existed in what is now Asia. Some of the largest timber bamboo can grow over 30&#160;m (98&#160;ft) tall, and be as large as 25–30&#160;cm (9.8–11.8&#160;in) in diameter. However, the size range for mature bamboo is species-dependent, with the smallest bamboos reaching only several inches high at maturity. A typical height range that would cover many of the common bamboos grown in the United States is 4.6–12&#160;m (15–39&#160;ft), depending on species. <a href="/wiki/Anji_County" title="Anji County">Anji County</a> of China, known as the "Town of Bamboo", provides the optimal climate and soil conditions to grow, harvest, and process some of the most valued bamboo poles available worldwide.</p>
<p>Unlike all trees, individual bamboo culms emerge from the ground at their full diameter and grow to their full height in a single <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> of three to four months. During this time, each new shoot grows vertically into a culm with no branching out until the majority of the mature height is reached. Then, the branches extend from the nodes and leafing out occurs. In the next year, the pulpy wall of each culm slowly hardens. During the third year, the culm hardens further. The shoot is now a fully mature culm. Over the next 2–5 years (depending on species), fungus begins to form on the outside of the culm, which eventually penetrates and overcomes the culm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Around 5–8 years later (species- and climate-dependent), the fungal growths cause the culm to collapse and decay. This brief life means culms are ready for harvest and suitable for use in construction within about three to seven years. Individual bamboo culms do not get any taller or larger in diameter in subsequent years than they do in their first year, and they do not replace any growth lost from pruning or natural breakage. Bamboo has a wide range of hardiness depending on species and locale. Small or young specimens of an individual species produce small culms initially. As the clump and its rhizome system mature, taller and larger culms are produced each year until the plant approaches its particular species limits of height and diameter.</p>
<p>Many tropical bamboo species die at or near freezing temperatures, while some of the hardier temperate bamboos can survive temperatures as low as −29&#160;°C (−20&#160;°F). Some of the hardiest bamboo species can be grown in <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_zone" title="Hardiness zone">USDA plant hardiness zones</a> 5, although they typically defoliate and may even lose all above-ground growth, yet the rhizomes survive and send up shoots again the next spring. In milder climates, such as USDA zone 7 and above, most bamboo remain fully leafed out and green year-round.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_flowering">Mass flowering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mass flowering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Most bamboo species flower infrequently. In fact, many only flower at intervals as long as 65 or 120 years. These taxa exhibit <a href="/wiki/Mass_flowering" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass flowering">mass flowering</a> (or gregarious flowering), with all plants in a particular 'cohort' flowering over a several-year period. Any plant derived through clonal propagation from this cohort will also flower regardless of whether it has been planted in a different location. The longest mass flowering interval known is 130 years, and it is for the species <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostachys_bambusoides" title="Phyllostachys bambusoides">Phyllostachys bambusoides</a></i> (Sieb. &amp; Zucc.). In this species, all plants of the same stock flower at the same time, regardless of differences in geographic locations or climatic conditions, and then the bamboo dies. The lack of environmental impact on the time of flowering indicates the presence of some sort of "alarm clock" in each cell of the plant which signals the diversion of all energy to flower production and the cessation of vegetative growth.<sup id="cite_ref-Soderstrom.2C_TR_1979_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soderstrom.2C_TR_1979-18">[18]</a></sup> This mechanism, as well as the evolutionary cause behind it, is still largely a mystery.</p>
<p>One hypothesis to explain the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Semelparous" class="mw-redirect" title="Semelparous">semelparous</a> mass flowering is the predator satiation hypothesis, which argues that by fruiting at the same time, a population increases the survival rate of its seeds by flooding the area with fruit, so even if predators eat their fill, seeds will still be left over. By having a flowering cycle longer than the lifespan of the rodent predators, bamboos can regulate animal populations by causing starvation during the period between flowering events. Thus, the death of the adult clone is due to resource exhaustion, as it would be more effective for parent plants to devote all resources to creating a large seed crop than to hold back energy for their own regeneration.<sup id="cite_ref-Janzen_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janzen-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another, the fire cycle hypothesis, states that periodic flowering followed by death of the adult plants has evolved as a mechanism to create disturbance in the habitat, thus providing the seedlings with a gap in which to grow. This argues that the dead culms create a large fuel load, and also a large target for lightning strikes, increasing the likelihood of wildfire.<sup id="cite_ref-Keely.26Bond_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keely.26Bond-20">[20]</a></sup> Because bamboos can be aggressive as early successional plants, the seedlings would be able to outstrip other plants and take over the space left by their parents.</p>
<p>However, both have been disputed for different reasons. The predator satiation hypothesis does not explain why the flowering cycle is 10 times longer than the lifespan of the local rodents, something not predicted. The bamboo fire cycle hypothesis is considered by a few scientists to be unreasonable; they argue<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> that fires only result from humans and there is no natural fire in India. This notion is considered wrong based on distribution of lightning strike data during the dry season throughout India. However, another argument against this is the lack of precedent for any living organism to harness something as unpredictable as lightning strikes to increase its chance of survival as part of natural evolutionary progress.<sup id="cite_ref-Keely.26Bond2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keely.26Bond2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, a mathematical explanation for the extreme length of the flowering cycles has been offered, involving both the <a href="/wiki/Stabilizing_selection" title="Stabilizing selection">stabilizing selection</a> implied by the predator satiation hypothesis and others, and the fact that plants that flower at longer intervals tend to release more seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The hypothesis claims that bamboo flowering intervals grew by <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> multiplication. A mutant bamboo plant flowering at a noninteger multiple of its population's flowering interval would release its seeds alone, and would not enjoy the benefits of collective flowering (such as protection from predators). However, a mutant bamboo plant flowering at an integer multiple of its population's flowering interval would release its seeds only during collective flowering events, and would release more seeds than the average plant in the population. It could, therefore, take over the population, establishing a flowering interval that is an integer multiple of the previous flowering interval. The hypothesis predicts that observed bamboo flowering intervals should factorize into small <a href="/wiki/Prime_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime numbers">prime numbers</a>.</p>
<p>The mass fruiting also has direct economic and ecological consequences, however. The huge increase in available fruit in the forests often causes a boom in rodent populations, leading to increases in disease and famine in nearby human populations. For example, devastating consequences occur when the <i><a href="/wiki/Melocanna" title="Melocanna">Melocanna</a> bambusoides</i> population flowers and fruits once every 30–35 years<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> around the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>. The death of the bamboo plants following their fruiting means the local people lose their building material, and the large increase in bamboo fruit leads to a rapid increase in rodent populations. As the number of rodents increases, they consume all available food, including grain fields and stored food, sometimes leading to <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a>. These rats can also carry dangerous diseases, such as <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typhoid" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>, which can reach epidemic proportions as the rodents increase in number.<sup id="cite_ref-Soderstrom.2C_TR_1979_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soderstrom.2C_TR_1979-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Janzen_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janzen-19">[19]</a></sup> The relationship between rat populations and bamboo flowering was examined in a 2009 <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_Series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nova (TV Series)">Nova</a></i> documentary <a href="/wiki/List_of_NOVA_episodes#Season_36:_2008.E2.80.932009" class="mw-redirect" title="List of NOVA episodes">"Rat Attack"</a>.</p>
<p>In any case, flowering produces masses of seeds, typically suspended from the ends of the branches. These seeds give rise to a new generation of plants that may be identical in appearance to those that preceded the flowering, or they may produce new cultivars with different characteristics, such as the presence or absence of striping or other changes in coloration of the culms.</p>
<p>Several bamboo species are never known to set seed even when sporadically flowering has been reported. <i><a href="/wiki/Bambusa_vulgaris" title="Bambusa vulgaris">Bambusa vulgaris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bambusa_balcooa" title="Bambusa balcooa">Bambusa balcooa</a></i>, and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dendrocalamus_stocksii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dendrocalamus stocksii (page does not exist)">Dendrocalamus stocksii</a></i> are common examples of such bamboo.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_diet">Animal diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Animal diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Soft bamboo shoots, stems, and leaves are the major food source of the giant panda of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Red_panda" title="Red panda">red panda</a> of Nepal, and the <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_lemur" title="Bamboo lemur">bamboo lemurs</a> of Madagascar. Rats eat the fruits as described above. <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">Mountain gorillas</a> of Africa also feed on bamboo, and have been documented consuming bamboo sap which was fermented and alcoholic;<sup id="cite_ref-gorilla_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gorilla-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzees" class="mw-redirect" title="Chimpanzees">chimpanzees</a> and elephants of the region also eat the stalks.</p>
<p>The larvae of the bamboo borer (the <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Omphisa_fuscidentalis" title="Omphisa fuscidentalis">Omphisa fuscidentalis</a></i>) of Laos, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, Thailand, and <a href="/wiki/Yunnan_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Yunnan Province">Yunnan Province</a>, China, feeds off the pulp of live bamboo. In turn, these <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a> are considered a <a href="/wiki/Entomophagy" title="Entomophagy">local delicacy</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_timber">Commercial timber</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial timber">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Timber is harvested from both cultivated and wild stands, and some of the larger bamboos, particularly species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllostachys" title="Phyllostachys">Phyllostachys</a></i>, are known as "timber bamboos".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvesting">Harvesting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div>

<p>Bamboo used for construction purposes must be harvested when the culms reach their greatest strength and when sugar levels in the <a href="/wiki/Sap" title="Sap">sap</a> are at their lowest, as high sugar content increases the ease and rate of <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a> infestation.</p>
<p>Harvesting of bamboo is typically undertaken according to these cycles:</p>
<p><b>1) Lifecycle of the culm</b>: As each individual <a href="/wiki/Culm_(botany)" title="Culm (botany)">culm</a> goes through a 5– to 7-year lifecycle, culms are ideally allowed to reach this level of maturity prior to full capacity harvesting. The clearing out or thinning of culms, particularly older decaying culms, helps to ensure adequate light and resources for new growth. Well-maintained clumps may have a productivity three to four times that of an unharvested wild clump. Consistent with the lifecycle described above, bamboo is harvested from two to three years through to five to seven years, depending on the species.</p>
<p><b>2) Annual cycle</b>: As all growth of new bamboo occurs during the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>, disturbing the clump during this phase will potentially damage the upcoming crop. Also during this high-rainfall period, sap levels are at their highest, and then diminish towards the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. Picking immediately prior to the wet/growth season may also damage new shoots. Hence, harvesting is best a few months prior to the start of the wet season.</p>
<p><b>3) Daily cycle</b>: During the height of the day, <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> is at its peak, producing the highest levels of sugar in sap, making this the least ideal time of day to harvest. Many traditional practitioners believe the best time to harvest is at dawn or dusk on a waning moon.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaching">Leaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Leaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leaching is the removal of sap after harvest. In many areas of the world, the sap levels in harvested bamboo are reduced either through leaching or postharvest photosynthesis. Examples of this practice include:</p>
<ol>
<li>Cut bamboo is raised clear of the ground and leaned against the rest of the clump for one to two weeks until leaves turn yellow to allow full consumption of sugars by the plant.</li>
<li>A similar method is undertaken, but with the base of the culm standing in fresh water, either in a large drum or stream to leach out sap.</li>
<li>Cut culms are immersed in a running stream and weighted down for three to four weeks.</li>
<li>Water is pumped through the freshly cut culms, forcing out the sap (this method is often used in conjunction with the injection of some form of treatment).</li>
</ol>
<p>In the process of water leaching, the bamboo is dried slowly and evenly in the shade to avoid cracking in the outer skin of the bamboo, thereby reducing opportunities for pest infestation.</p>
<p>Durability of bamboo in construction is directly related to how well it is handled from the moment of planting through harvesting, transportation, storage, design, construction, and maintenance. Bamboo harvested at the correct time of year and then exposed to ground contact or rain will break down just as quickly as incorrectly harvested material.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_2">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two general patterns for the growth of bamboo are "clumping" (sympodial) and "<a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">running</a>" (monopodial). Clumping bamboo species tend to spread slowly, as the growth pattern of the rhizomes is to simply expand the root mass gradually, similar to ornamental grasses. "Running" bamboos, though, need to be controlled during cultivation because of their potential for aggressive behavior. They spread mainly through their <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a>, which can spread widely underground and send up new culms to break through the surface. Running bamboo species are highly variable in their tendency to spread; this is related to both the species and the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> conditions. Some can send out runners of several metres a year, while others can stay in the same general area for long periods. If neglected, over time, they can cause problems by moving into adjacent areas.</p>
<p>Bamboos seldom and unpredictably flower, and the frequency of flowering varies greatly from species to species. Once flowering takes place, a plant declines and often dies entirely. Although a few species of bamboo are always in flower at any given time, collectors desiring to grow specific bamboo typically obtain their plants as divisions of already-growing plants, rather than waiting for seeds to be produced.</p>
<p>Regular observations indicate major growth directions and locations. Once the rhizomes are cut, they are typically removed; however, rhizomes take a number of months to mature, and an immature, severed rhizome usually ceases growing if left in-ground. If any bamboo shoots come up outside of the bamboo area afterwards, their presence indicates the precise location of the removed rhizome. The fibrous roots that radiate from the rhizomes do not produce more bamboo.</p>
<p>Bamboo growth is also controlled by surrounding the plant or grove with a physical barrier. Typically, concrete and specially rolled <a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a> plastic are the materials used to create the barrier, which is placed in a 60– to 90-cm-deep ditch around the planting, and angled out at the top to direct the rhizomes to the surface. (This is only possible if the barrier is installed in a straight line.) If the containment area is small, this method can be detrimental to ornamental bamboo, as the bamboo within can become rootbound and start to display the signs of any unhealthy containerized plant. In addition, rhizomes can escape over the top, or beneath the barrier if it is not deep enough. Strong rhizomes and tools can penetrate plastic barriers, so care must be taken. In small areas, regular maintenance may be the best method for controlling the running bamboos. Barriers and edging are unnecessary for clump-forming bamboos, although these may eventually need to have portions removed if they become too large.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ornamental_bamboos">Ornamental bamboos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ornamental bamboos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental plant</a> sold in containers and marketed as "lucky bamboo" is actually an entirely unrelated plant, <i><a href="/wiki/Dracaena_sanderiana" class="mw-redirect" title="Dracaena sanderiana">Dracaena sanderiana</a></i>. It is a resilient member of the lily family that grows in the dark, tropical rainforests of Southeast Asia and Africa. Lucky bamboo has long been associated with the Eastern practice of <i><a href="/wiki/Feng_shui" title="Feng shui">feng shui</a></i> and images of the plant widely available on the Web are often used to depict bamboo. On a similar note, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_knotweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese knotweed">Japanese knotweed</a> is also sometimes mistaken for a bamboo, but it grows wild and is considered an invasive species. <i>Phyllostachys</i> species of bamboo are also considered invasive and illegal to sell or propagate in some areas of the US.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<i>Khao lam</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th" xml:lang="th">ข้าวหลาม</span>) is <a href="/wiki/Glutinous_rice" title="Glutinous rice">glutinous rice</a> with sugar and coconut cream cooked in specially prepared bamboo sections of different diameters and lengths</div>

<p>Although the shoots (new culms that come out of the ground) of bamboo contain a toxin taxiphyllin (a cyanogenic glycoside) that produces cyanide in the gut, proper processing renders them edible. They are used in numerous Asian dishes and broths, and are available in supermarkets in various sliced forms, in both fresh and canned versions. The <a href="/wiki/Golden_bamboo_lemur" title="Golden bamboo lemur">golden bamboo lemur</a> ingests many times the quantity of the taxiphyllin-containing bamboo that would kill a human.</p>
<p>The bamboo shoot in its fermented state forms an important ingredient in cuisines across the Himalayas. In Assam, India, for example, it is called <i>khorisa</i>. In <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, a delicacy popular across ethnic boundaries consists of bamboo shoots fermented with turmeric and oil, and cooked with potatoes into a dish that usually accompanies rice (<i>alu tama (आलु तामा)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">Nepali</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, they are sliced thin and then boiled with <i>santan</i> (thick coconut milk) and spices to make a dish called <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulai_rebung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gulai rebung (page does not exist)">gulai rebung</a></i>. Other recipes using bamboo shoots are <i><a href="/wiki/Sayur_lodeh" title="Sayur lodeh">sayur lodeh</a></i> (mixed vegetables in coconut milk) and <i>lun pia</i> (sometimes written <i><a href="/wiki/Lumpia" title="Lumpia">lumpia</a></i>: fried wrapped bamboo shoots with vegetables). The shoots of some species contain toxins that need to be leached or boiled out before they can be eaten safely.</p>
<p>Pickled bamboo, used as a condiment, may also be made from the <a href="/wiki/Pith" title="Pith">pith</a> of the young shoots.</p>
<p>The sap of young stalks tapped during the rainy season may be <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermented</a> to make <i>ulanzi</i> (a sweet wine) or simply made into a soft drink. Bamboo leaves are also used as wrappers for steamed <a href="/wiki/Dumplings" class="mw-redirect" title="Dumplings">dumplings</a> which usually contains glutinous rice and other ingredients.</p>
<p>Pickled bamboo shoots (<a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">Nepali</a>: तामा <i>tama</i>) are cooked with black-eyed beans as a delicacy food in Nepal. Many Nepalese restaurant around the world serve this dish as <i>aloo bodi tama</i>. Fresh bamboo shoots are sliced and pickled with mustard seeds and turmeric and kept in glass jar in direct sunlight for the best taste. It is used alongside many dried beans in cooking during winters. Baby shoots (<a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">Nepali</a>: <i>tusa</i>) of a very different variety of bamboo (Nepali: निगालो Nigalo) native to Nepal is cooked as a curry in hilly regions.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sambalpur" title="Sambalpur">Sambalpur</a>, India, the tender shoots are grated into <a href="/wiki/Julienning" title="Julienning">juliennes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (food)">fermented</a> to prepare <i>kardi</i>. The name is derived from the Sanskrit word for bamboo shoot, <i>karira</i>. This fermented bamboo shoot is used in various culinary preparations, notably <i>amil</i>, a sour vegetable soup. It is also made into pancakes using <a href="/wiki/Rice_flour" title="Rice flour">rice flour</a> as a binding agent. The shoots that have turned a little fibrous are fermented, dried, and ground to sand-sized particles to prepare a garnish known as <i>hendua</i>. It is also cooked with tender pumpkin leaves to make sag green leaves.</p>
<p>In Konkani cuisine, the tender shoots (<i>kirlu</i>) are grated and cooked with crushed jackfruit seeds to prepare <i>kirla sukke</i>.</p>
<p>The empty hollow in the stalks of larger bamboo is often used to cook food in many Asian cultures. Soups are boiled and rice is cooked in the hollows of fresh stalks of bamboo directly over a flame. Similarly, steamed tea is sometimes rammed into bamboo hollows to produce <a href="/wiki/Compressed_tea" class="mw-redirect" title="Compressed tea">compressed forms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pu-erh_tea" title="Pu-erh tea">Pu-erh tea</a>. Cooking food in bamboo is said to give the food a subtle but distinctive taste.</p>
<p>In addition, bamboo is frequently used for cooking utensils within many cultures, and is used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Chopsticks" title="Chopsticks">chopsticks</a>. In modern times, some see bamboo tools as an ecofriendly alternative to other manufactured utensils.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_construction" title="Bamboo construction">Bamboo construction</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Bamboo, like true <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, is a natural <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> with a high strength-to-weight ratio useful for structures.<sup id="cite_ref-Lakkad_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lakkad-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In its natural form, bamboo as a construction material is traditionally associated with the cultures of South Asia, East Asia, and the South Pacific, to some extent in Central and South America, and by extension in the aesthetic of <a href="/wiki/Tiki_culture" title="Tiki culture">Tiki culture</a>. In China and India, bamboo was used to hold up <a href="/wiki/Simple_suspension_bridge" title="Simple suspension bridge">simple suspension bridges</a>, either by making cables of split bamboo or twisting whole culms of sufficiently pliable bamboo together. One such bridge in the area of Qian-Xian is referenced in writings dating back to 960 AD and may have stood since as far back as the third century BC, due largely to continuous maintenance.</p>
<p>Bamboo has also long been used as scaffolding; the practice has been banned in China for buildings over six stories, but is still in continuous use for skyscrapers in Hong Kong.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-30">[30]</a></sup> In the Philippines, the <a href="/wiki/Nipa_hut" title="Nipa hut">nipa hut</a> is a fairly typical example of the most basic sort of <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">housing</a> where bamboo is used; the walls are split and woven bamboo, and bamboo slats and poles may be used as its support. In <a href="/wiki/Japanese_architecture" title="Japanese architecture">Japanese architecture</a>, bamboo is used primarily as a supplemental and/or decorative element in buildings such as fencing, fountains, grates, and gutters, largely due to the ready abundance of quality timber.<sup id="cite_ref-BambooJapan_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BambooJapan-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Various structural shapes may be made by training the bamboo to assume them as it grows. Squared sections of bamboo are created by compressing the growing stalk within a square form. Arches may similarly be created by forcing the bamboo's growth into the desired form, costing much less than it would to obtain the same shape with regular wood timber. More traditional forming methods, such as the application of heat and pressure, may also be used to curve or flatten the cut stalks.<sup id="cite_ref-Hidalgo_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hidalgo-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bamboo can be cut and laminated into sheets and planks. This process involves cutting stalks into thin strips, planing them flat, and boiling and drying the strips; they are then glued, pressed, and finished.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-33">[33]</a></sup> Long used in China and Japan, entrepreneurs started developing and selling laminated <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_floor" title="Bamboo floor">bamboo flooring</a> in the West during the mid-1990s;<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-33">[33]</a></sup> products made from bamboo laminate, including flooring, cabinetry, furniture, and even decorations, are currently surging in popularity, transitioning from the boutique market to mainstream providers such as <a href="/wiki/Home_Depot" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Depot">Home Depot</a>. The bamboo goods industry (which also includes small goods, fabric, etc.) is expected to be worth $25 billion by 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-GreenBiz_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBiz-34">[34]</a></sup> The quality of bamboo laminate varies among manufacturers and varies according to the maturity of the plant from which it was harvested (six years being considered the optimum); the sturdiest products fulfill their claims of being up to three times harder than <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> hardwood while others may be softer than standard hardwood.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bamboo intended for use in construction should be treated to resist insects and rot. The most common solution for this purpose is a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">borax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a>. Another process involves boiling cut bamboo to remove the starches that attract insects.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bamboo has been used as reinforcement for concrete in those areas where it is plentiful, though dispute exists over its effectiveness in the various studies done on the subject. Bamboo does have the necessary strength to fulfil this function, but untreated bamboo will swell with water absorbed from the concrete, causing it to crack. Several procedures must be followed to overcome this shortcoming.<sup id="cite_ref-US-Ag_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US-Ag-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several institutes, businesses, and universities are researching the use of bamboo as an ecological construction material. In the United States and France, it is possible to get houses made entirely of bamboo,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which are earthquake- and cyclone-resistant and internationally certified. Three <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> standards are given for bamboo as a construction material.</p>
<p>In parts of India, bamboo is used for drying clothes indoors, both as a rod high up near the ceiling to hang clothes on, and as a stick wielded with acquired expert skill to hoist, spread, and to take down the clothes when dry. It is also commonly used to make ladders, which apart from their normal function, are also used for carrying bodies in funerals. In <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, the bamboo groves and forests are called <i>Veluvana</i>, the name <i>velu</i> for bamboo is most likely from Sanskrit, while <i>vana</i> means forest.</p>
<p>Furthermore, bamboo is also used to create flagpoles for <a href="/wiki/Saffron" title="Saffron">saffron</a>-coloured, <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> religious flags, which can be seen fluttering across India, especially in <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, as well as in Guyana and Suriname in South America.</p>
<p>Bamboo was used for the structural members of the <a href="/wiki/Expo_2010_pavilions#India" title="Expo 2010 pavilions">India pavilion</a> at <a href="/wiki/Expo_2010" title="Expo 2010">Expo 2010</a> in Shanghai. The pavilion is the world’s largest bamboo dome, about 34&#160;m (112&#160;ft) in diameter, with bamboo beams/members overlaid with a ferro-concrete slab, waterproofing, copper plate, solar PV panels, a small windmill, and live plants. A total of 30&#160;km (19&#160;mi) of bamboo was used. The dome is supported on 18-m-long steel piles and a series of steel ring beams. The bamboo was treated with borax and boric acid as a fire retardant and insecticide and bent in the required shape. The bamboo sections were joined with reinforcement bars and concrete mortar to achieve the necessary lengths.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textiles">Textiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Textiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_textiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Bamboo textiles">Bamboo textiles</a></div>
<p>Since the fibers of bamboo are very short (less than 3&#160;mm (0.12&#160;in)), they are not usually transformed into yarn by a natural process. The usual process by which textiles labeled as being made of bamboo are produced uses only <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a> made from the fibers with heavy employment of chemicals. To accomplish this, the fibers are broken down with chemicals and extruded through mechanical spinnerets; the chemicals include <a href="/wiki/Lye" title="Lye">lye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a>, and strong acids.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-33">[33]</a></sup> Retailers have sold both end products as "bamboo fabric" to cash in on bamboo's current ecofriendly cachet; however, the Canadian <a href="/wiki/Competition_Bureau" title="Competition Bureau">Competition Bureau</a><sup id="cite_ref-ccb_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccb-37">[37]</a></sup> and the US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FTC_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTC-38">[38]</a></sup> as of mid-2009, are cracking down on the practice of labeling bamboo rayon as natural bamboo fabric. Under the guidelines of both agencies, these products must be labeled as rayon with the optional qualifier "from bamboo".<sup id="cite_ref-FTC_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTC-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_writing_surface">As a writing surface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: As a writing surface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_and_wooden_slips" title="Bamboo and wooden slips">Bamboo and wooden slips</a></div>
<p>Bamboo was in widespread use in early China as a medium for written documents. The earliest surviving examples of such documents, written in ink on string-bound bundles of bamboo strips (or "slips"), date from the fifth century BC during the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a>. However, references in earlier texts surviving on other media make it clear that some precursor of these Warring States period bamboo slips was in use as early as the late <a href="/wiki/Shang" class="mw-redirect" title="Shang">Shang</a> period (from about 1250 BC).</p>
<p>Bamboo or wooden strips were the standard writing material during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>, and excavated examples have been found in abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Subsequently, <a href="/wiki/History_of_paper" title="History of paper">paper</a> began to displace bamboo and wooden strips from mainstream uses, and by the fourth century AD, bamboo slips had been largely abandoned as a medium for writing in China. Several paper industries are surviving on bamboo forests. Ballarpur (Chandrapur, Maharstra) paper mills use bamboo for paper production.</p>
<p>Bamboo fiber has been used to make paper in China since early times. A high-quality, handmade paper is still produced in small quantities. Coarse bamboo paper is still used to make <a href="/wiki/Joss_paper" title="Joss paper">spirit money</a> in many Chinese communities.<sup id="cite_ref-spiritpaper_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spiritpaper-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bamboo <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)" title="Pulp (paper)">pulps</a> are mainly produced in China, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, Thailand, and India, and are used in <a href="/wiki/Printing_and_writing_paper" title="Printing and writing paper">printing and writing papers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Market_pulp_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Market_pulp-41">[41]</a></sup> The most common bamboo species used for paper are <i><a href="/wiki/Dendrocalamus_asper" title="Dendrocalamus asper">Dendrocalamus asper</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bambusa_blumeana" title="Bambusa blumeana">Bambusa blumeana</a></i>. It is also possible to make <a href="/wiki/Dissolving_pulp" title="Dissolving pulp">dissolving pulp</a> from bamboo. The average fiber length is similar to <a href="/wiki/Hardwoods" class="mw-redirect" title="Hardwoods">hardwoods</a>, but the properties of bamboo pulp are closer to <a href="/wiki/Softwood" title="Softwood">softwood</a> pulps due to it having a very broad fiber length distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-Market_pulp_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Market_pulp-41">[41]</a></sup> With the help of molecular tools, it is now possible to distinguish the superior fiber-yielding species/varieties even at juvenile stages of their growth, which can help in unadulterated merchandise production.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bamboo has often been used to construct weapons and is still incorporated in several Asian martial arts.</p>
<ul>
<li>A bamboo staff, sometimes with one end sharpened, is used in the Tamil martial art of <i><a href="/wiki/Silambam" title="Silambam">silambam</a></i>, a word derived from a term meaning "hill bamboo".</li>
<li>Staves used in the Indian martial art of <i><a href="/wiki/Gatka" title="Gatka">gatka</a></i> are commonly made from bamboo, a material favoured for its light weight.</li>
<li>A bamboo sword called a <i><a href="/wiki/Shinai" title="Shinai">shinai</a></i> is used in the Japanese martial art of <i><a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a></i>.</li>
<li>Bamboo is used for crafting the bows, called <i><a href="/wiki/Yumi" title="Yumi">yumi</a></i>, and arrows used in the Japanese martial art <i><a href="/wiki/Kyudo" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyudo">kyudo</a></i>.</li>
<li>Bamboo is sometimes used to craft the limbs of the longbow and recurve bow used in traditional archery, and to make superior weapons for bowhunting and target archery.</li>
<li>The first gunpowder-based weapons, such as the <a href="/wiki/Fire_lance" title="Fire lance">fire lance</a>, were made of bamboo.</li>
<li>Bamboo was apparently used in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_torture" title="Bamboo torture">means of torture</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>Bamboo has traditionally been used to make a wide range of everyday utensils, particularly in Japan,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> where archaeological excavations have uncovered bamboo baskets dating to the Late Jomon period (2000–1000 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>Bamboo has a long history of use in <a href="/wiki/Asian_furniture" title="Asian furniture">Asian furniture</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chinese_furniture" title="Chinese furniture">Chinese bamboo furniture</a> is a distinct style based on a millennia-long tradition.<br />
Several manufacturers offer <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_bicycle" title="Bamboo bicycle">bamboo bicycles</a>, surfboards, snowboards, and skateboards.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><br />
Due to its flexibility, bamboo is also used to make <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">fishing rods</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Split_cane_rod" class="mw-redirect" title="Split cane rod">split cane rod</a> is especially prized for <a href="/wiki/Fly_fishing" title="Fly fishing">fly fishing</a>. Bamboo has been traditionally used in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> as a firecracker called a <i>meriam buluh</i> or <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_cannon" title="Bamboo cannon">bamboo cannon</a>. Four-foot-long sections of bamboo are cut, and a mixture of water and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbide" title="Calcium carbide">calcium carbide</a> are introduced. The resulting acetylene gas is ignited with a stick, producing a loud bang. Bamboo can be used in <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">water desalination</a>. A bamboo filter is used to remove the salt from seawater<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Bamboo#Talk:Bamboo.23Desalination" title="Talk:Bamboo">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Food is cooked in <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a> in bamboo in fire. This is called <i>tukir</i>.</p>
<p>Many minority groups in remote areas that have water access in Asia use bamboo that is 3–5 years old to make rafts. They use 8 to 12 poles, 6–7 m (20 to 24&#160;ft) long, laid together side by side to a width of about 1&#160;m (3.3&#160;ft). Once the poles are lined up together, they cut a hole crosswise through the poles at each end and use a small bamboo pole pushed through that hole like a screw to hold all the long bamboo poles together. Floating houses use whole bamboo stalks tied together in a big bunch to support the house floating in the water. Bamboo is also used to make eating utensils such as chopsticks, trays, and tea scoops.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> (960–1279 AD) Chinese scientist and <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a> <a href="/wiki/Shen_Kuo" title="Shen Kuo">Shen Kuo</a> (1031–1095) used the evidence of underground <a href="/wiki/Petrified" class="mw-redirect" title="Petrified">petrified</a> bamboo found in the dry northern climate of <a href="/wiki/Yan%27an" title="Yan'an">Yan'an</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanbei" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanbei">Shanbei</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a> province to support his geological theory of gradual <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chan_15_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chan_15-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_614_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_614-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism_and_culture">Symbolism and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Symbolism and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg/220px-Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg/330px-Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg/440px-Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2024" data-file-height="1557" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hs%C3%BC_Wei_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Bamboo</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Xu_Wei" title="Xu Wei">Xu Wei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>.</div>

<p>Bamboo's long life makes it a Chinese symbol of uprightness, while in India it is a symbol of friendship. The rarity of its <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_blossom" title="Bamboo blossom">blossoming</a> has led to the flowers' being regarded as a sign of impending famine. This may be due to rats feeding upon the profusion of flowers, then multiplying and destroying a large part of the local food supply. The most recent flowering began in May 2006 (see <a href="/wiki/Mautam" title="Mautam">Mautam</a>). Bamboo is said to bloom in this manner only about every 50 years (see 28–60 year examples in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fao.org/documents/show_cdr.asp?url_file=/docrep/x5390e/x5390e05.htm">FAO: 'gregarious' species table</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Culture of China">Chinese culture</a>, the bamboo, <a href="/wiki/Prunus_mume" title="Prunus mume">plum</a> blossom, <a href="/wiki/Orchid" class="mw-redirect" title="Orchid">orchid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chrysanthemum" title="Chrysanthemum">chrysanthemum</a> (often known as <i>méi lán zhú jú</i> 梅兰竹菊) are collectively referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Four_Gentlemen" title="Four Gentlemen">Four Gentlemen</a>. These four plants also represent the four seasons and, in <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> ideology, four aspects of the <i><a href="/wiki/Junzi" title="Junzi">junzi</a></i> ("prince" or "noble one"). The <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> (<i>sōng</i> 松), the bamboo (<i>zhú</i> 竹), and the plum blossom (<i>méi</i> 梅) are also admired for their perseverance under harsh conditions, and are together known as the "<a href="/wiki/Three_Friends_of_Winter" title="Three Friends of Winter">Three Friends of Winter</a>" (岁寒三友 <i>suìhán sānyǒu</i>) in Chinese culture. The "Three Friends of Winter" is traditionally used as a system of ranking in Japan, for example in <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">sushi</a> sets or accommodations at a traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Ryokan_(inn)" title="Ryokan (inn)">ryokan</a></i>. Pine (<i>matsu</i> 松) is of the first rank, bamboo (<i>take</i> 竹) is of second rank, and plum (<i>ume</i> 梅) is of the third.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bozo_people" title="Bozo people">Bozo</a> ethnic group of West Africa take their name from the <a href="/wiki/Bambara_language" title="Bambara language">Bambara</a> phrase <i>bo-so</i>, which means "bamboo house". Bamboo is also the national plant of <a href="/wiki/St._Lucia" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Lucia">St. Lucia</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bamboo.2C_noble_and_useful">Bamboo, noble and useful</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Bamboo, noble and useful">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg/170px-S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg" width="170" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg/255px-S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg/340px-S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg 2x" data-file-width="909" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:S03_03_02_016_image_63.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Photo of carved Chinese bamboo wall vase. 1918. Brooklyn Museum Archives, Goodyear Archival Collection.</div>

<p>Bamboo, one of the "<a href="/wiki/Four_Gentlemen" title="Four Gentlemen">Four Gentlemen</a>" (bamboo, orchid, plum blossom and chrysanthemum), plays such an important role in traditional Chinese culture that it is even regarded as a behavior model of the gentleman. As bamboo has features such as uprightness, tenacity, and hollow heart, people endow bamboo with integrity, elegance, and plainness, though it is not physically strong. Countless poems praising bamboo written by ancient Chinese poets are actually metaphorically about people who exhibited these characteristics. According to laws, an ancient poet, <a href="/wiki/Bai_Juyi" title="Bai Juyi">Bai Juyi</a> (772–846), thought that to be a gentleman, a man does not need to be physically strong, but he must be mentally strong, upright, and perseverant. Just as a bamboo is hollow-hearted, he should open his heart to accept anything of benefit and never have arrogance or prejudice. Bamboo is not only a symbol of a gentleman, but also plays an important role in Buddhism, which was introduced into China in the first century. As canons of Buddhism forbids cruelty to animals, flesh and egg were not allowed in the diet. The tender bamboo shoot (<i>sǔn</i>筍 in Chinese) thus became a nutritious alternative. Preparation methods developed over thousands of years have come to be incorporated into Asian cuisines, especially for monks. A Buddhist monk, Zan Ning, wrote a manual of the bamboo shoot called "<i>Sǔn Pǔ</i>筍譜" offering descriptions and recipes for many kinds of bamboo shoots.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Bamboo shoot has always been a traditional dish on the Chinese dinner table, especially in southern China. In ancient times, those who could afford a big house with a yard would plant bamboo in their garden.</p>
<p>In Japan, a bamboo forest sometimes surrounds a Shinto <a href="/wiki/Jinja_(Shinto)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jinja (Shinto)">shrine</a> as part of a sacred barrier against <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">evil</a>. Many Buddhist temples also have bamboo groves.</p>

<p>Bamboo plays an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Vietnam" title="Culture of Vietnam">culture of Vietnam</a>. Bamboo symbolizes the spirit of <a href="/wiki/Vovinam" title="Vovinam">Vovinam</a> (a Vietnamese martial arts): <i>cương nhu phối triển</i> (coordination between <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_(martial_arts)" title="Hard and soft (martial arts)">hard and soft (martial arts)</a>). Bamboo also symbolizes the Vietnamese hometown and Vietnamese soul: the gentlemanlike, straightforwardness, hard working, optimism, unity, and adaptability. A Vietnamese proverb says, "When the bamboo is old, the bamboo sprouts appear", the meaning being Vietnam will never be annihilated; if the previous generation dies, the children take their place. Therefore, the Vietnam nation and Vietnamese value will be maintained and developed eternally. Traditional Vietnamese villages are surrounded by thick bamboo hedges (<i>lũy tre</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mythology">In mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several Asian cultures, including that of the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a>, believe humanity emerged from a bamboo stem.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Philippine_mythology" title="Philippine mythology">Philippine mythology</a>, one of the more famous <a href="/wiki/Creation_account" class="mw-redirect" title="Creation account">creation accounts</a> tells of the first man, <i>Malakás</i> ("Strong"), and the first woman, <i>Maganda</i> ("Beautiful"), each emerged from one half of a split bamboo stem on an island formed after the battle between Sky and Ocean. In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, a similar story includes a man who dreams of a beautiful woman while sleeping under a bamboo plant; he wakes up and breaks the bamboo stem, discovering the woman inside. The <a href="/wiki/Japanese_folktales" title="Japanese folktales">Japanese folktale</a> "<a href="/wiki/Tale_of_the_Bamboo_Cutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Tale of the Bamboo Cutter">Tale of the Bamboo Cutter</a>" (<i>Taketori Monogatari</i>) tells of a princess from the Moon emerging from a shining bamboo section. <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian</a> bamboo <i>('ohe)</i> is a <i>kinolau</i> or body form of the <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Polynesian mythology">Polynesian</a> creator god <a href="/wiki/K%C4%81ne" title="Kāne">Kāne</a>.</p>
<p>A bamboo cane is also the weapon of <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese</a> legendary hero, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_Giong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Saint Giong (page does not exist)">Saint Giong</a>, who had grown up immediately and magically since the age of three because of his wish to liberate his land from Ân invaders. An ancient Vietnamese legend (<a href="/wiki/The_Hundred-knot_Bamboo_Tree" title="The Hundred-knot Bamboo Tree">The Hundred-knot Bamboo Tree</a>) tells of a poor, young farmer who fell in love with his landlord's beautiful daughter. The farmer asked the landlord for his daughter's hand in marriage, but the proud landlord would not allow her to be bound in marriage to a poor farmer. The landlord decided to foil the marriage with an impossible deal; the farmer must bring him a "bamboo tree of 100 <a href="/wiki/Plant_stems" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant stems">nodes</a>". But <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a> (<i>Bụt</i>) appeared to the farmer and told him that such a tree could be made from 100 nodes from several different trees. <i>Bụt</i> gave to him four magic words to attach the many nodes of bamboo: <i>Khắc nhập, khắc xuất</i>, which means "joined together immediately, fell apart immediately". The triumphant farmer returned to the landlord and demanded his daughter. Curious to see such a long bamboo, the landlord was magically joined to the bamboo when he touched it, as the young farmer said the first two magic words. The story ends with the happy marriage of the farmer and the landlord's daughter after the landlord agreed to the marriage and asked to be separated from the bamboo.</p>
<p>In a Chinese legend, the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yao" title="Emperor Yao">Emperor Yao</a> gave two of his daughters to the future <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Shun" title="Emperor Shun">Emperor Shun</a> as a test for his potential to rule. Shun passed the test of being able to run his household with the two emperor's daughters as wives, and thus Yao made Shun his successor, bypassing his unworthy son. After Shun's death, the tears of his two bereaved wives fell upon the bamboos growing there explains the origin of <a href="/wiki/Spotted_bamboo" title="Spotted bamboo">spotted bamboo</a>. The two women later became goddesses <a href="/wiki/Xiangshuishen" title="Xiangshuishen">Xiangshuishen</a> after drowning themselves in the <a href="/wiki/Xiang_River" title="Xiang River">Xiang River</a>.</p>
