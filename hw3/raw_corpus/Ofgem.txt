<p>The <b>Office of Gas and Electricity Markets</b> (<b>Ofgem</b>), supporting the <b>Gas and Electricity Markets Authority</b> (<b>GEMA</b>), is the <a href="/wiki/Government_regulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Government regulation">government regulator</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and downstream <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> markets in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. It was formed by the merger of the <b>Office of Electricity Regulation</b> (<b>OFFER</b>) and <b>Office of Gas Supply</b> (<b>Ofgas</b>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_and_duties">Powers and duties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Gas_and_Electricity_Markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Powers and duties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Authority's powers and duties are largely provided for in statute (such as the <a href="/wiki/Gas_Act_1986" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas Act 1986">Gas Act 1986</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_Act_1989" title="Electricity Act 1989">Electricity Act 1989</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Utilities_Act_2000" title="Utilities Act 2000">Utilities Act 2000</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Competition_Act_1998" title="Competition Act 1998">Competition Act 1998</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_Act_2002" title="Enterprise Act 2002">Enterprise Act 2002</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Act_2004" title="Energy Act 2004">Energy Act 2004</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the Energy Act 2008<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Act_2010" title="Energy Act 2010">Energy Act 2010</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) as well as arising from directly effective European Community legislation. Duties and functions concerning gas are set out in the Gas Act and those relating to electricity are set out in the Electricity Act.</p>
<p>Its primary duty is to protect the interests of consumers, where possible by promoting competition.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Authority‘s main objective is to protect existing and future consumers' interests in relation to gas conveyed through pipes and electricity conveyed by distribution or transmission systems. Consumers' interests are their interests taken as a whole, including their interests in the reduction of greenhouse gases and in the security of the supply of gas and electricity to them.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since 2010 the Authority has imposed nearly £100&#160;million in fines and redress levies against energy suppliers, including a £12&#160;million redress levy on <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a> in May 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-OfgemPenalty_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OfgemPenalty-8">[8]</a></sup> and a £1 million redress levy on <a href="/wiki/British_Gas" title="British Gas">British Gas</a> in July 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-BritishGas_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritishGas-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Gas_and_Electricity_Markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gas and Electricity Markets Authority is governed by the Chairman David Gray, executive members as well as non-executive members.<sup id="cite_ref-ofgem1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ofgem1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dermot Nolan was appointed Chief Executive of Ofgem in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-ofgem1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ofgem1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ofgem is divided into Ofgem (Smarter Grids &amp; Governance, Markets, Sustainable Development and Group Finance Director) and Ofgem E-Serve containing Group Functions: Environmental Programmes, Operations/HR, Information Management and Technology, Finance and Risk Management, and Commercial: Offshore, Legal, Smart Metering Delivery, New Schemes Development.<sup id="cite_ref-ofgem1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ofgem1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_competition_in_the_UK_market">Development of competition in the UK market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Gas_and_Electricity_Markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development of competition in the UK market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The liberalisation and <a href="/wiki/Privatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Privatisation">privatisation</a> of the energy markets in the United Kingdom began with the <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> Government in the 1980s (often called the Thatcher-Lawson agenda, due to the key role of <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Lawson" title="Nigel Lawson">Nigel Lawson</a> in the Thatcher government cabinet). Aspects of the Ofgem model have been adopted by <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in the 1990s, the supply of electricity and gas to end consumers in the UK has been unbundled from the rest of the industry. At the time of privatisation, British Gas and one regional <a href="/wiki/Public_electricity_supplier" title="Public electricity supplier">public electricity supplier</a> (PES) held a <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> on supplying all domestic gas and electricity consumers respectively in Great Britain. Between 1996 and 1999, domestic energy consumers gradually got the freedom to choose their supplier, and finally in May 1998, the domestic gas market was fully opened to competition, closely followed by the domestic <a href="/wiki/Electricity_market" title="Electricity market">electricity market</a> in May 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before there was competition on domestic markets, Ofgem set <a href="/wiki/Price_controls" title="Price controls">price controls</a> fixing maximum price that the monopoly suppliers could charge domestic customers. These price controls remained in place when markets started to get liberalised, and were then gradually removed between 2000 and 2002. Ofgem’s decision to remove price controls was based on the assessment that competition was developing well at that time and that the Competition Act 1998, being effective since March 2000, would deter companies from the abuse of market power, and provide Ofgem with sufficient power to tackle any abuse. Moreover, consumer surveys showed good awareness of the ability to switch, high and rising switching rates away from the former monopoly supplies, and substantial and continuing falls in their market shares.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, the Social Action Strategy review Group was established and the Competition Act came into force. In 2003 the Wholesale Gas Probe was published. Two years after the removal of the last price controls, in April 2004, Ofgem published a major review of the state of competition in the domestic energy supply markets, concluding that supply competition had delivered substantial benefits for all consumers and that the markets were competitive, though not yet mature. In 2005 there was the EU Energy Sector Enquiry, as well as the Supply Licence Review. The Energy Supply Ombudsman was then established in 2006 and in 2008 the Energy Supply Probe was published.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Against the background of unprecedented increases in world <a href="/wiki/Price_of_petroleum" class="mw-redirect" title="Price of petroleum">fuel prices</a> leading to record increases in wholesale and retail gas and electricity prices so that a typical household's energy bills more than doubled since early 2004 Ofgem undertook the Energy Supply Probe. The numbers of consumers in debt to their energy suppliers, average debt levels and disconnection rates were all rising. These energy price rises came at a time when household budgets were under pressure from the rising cost of food, petrol, mortgages and other essentials. Vulnerable consumers and those in fuel poverty were particularly affected. The Energy Supply Probe published the findings on the operation of the UK retail energy markets and set out a package of measures to tackle the issues raised.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMA_investigation">CMA investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Gas_and_Electricity_Markets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CMA investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2014 Ofgem announced a <a href="/wiki/Competition_and_Markets_Authority" title="Competition and Markets Authority">Competition and Markets Authority</a> (CMA) investigation into the trading practices and competitiveness of the country's major energy companies: <a href="/wiki/Centrica" title="Centrica">Centrica</a>, <a href="/wiki/SSE_plc" title="SSE plc">SSE plc</a>, <a href="/wiki/RWE_npower" class="mw-redirect" title="RWE npower">RWE npower</a>, <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Power" title="Scottish Power">Scottish Power</a> and <a href="/wiki/EDF_Energy" title="EDF Energy">EDF Energy</a>. The investigation, which took two years, followed a referral by Ofgem to the competition regulator. "There is near-unanimous support for a referral and the CMA investigation offers an important opportunity to clear the air. This will help rebuild consumer trust and confidence in the energy market as well as provide the certainty investors have called for," Ofgem CEO Dermot Nolan in announcing the investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-CMAprobe_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMAprobe-16">[16]</a></sup> In August 2016 Ofgem said that it would implement the CMA's recommendation that suppliers should be required to provide the details of customers who have been on expensive tariffs for three years or more to rival suppliers. Ofgem also said that it would impose an interim price cap on customers using pre-payment meters.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
