			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Biofuel in the United States</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> produces mainly <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>, which uses <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> as the main <a href="/wiki/Feedstock" class="mw-redirect" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a>. In 2005 the US overtook <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> as the world's largest ethanol producer.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2006 the US produced 4.855&#160;billion US gallons (18.38<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-RFA1E_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFA1E-2">[2]</a></sup> The United States, together with Brazil accounted for 70 percent of all ethanol production, with total world production of 13.5&#160;billion US gallons (51<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) (40 million metric tons). When accounting just for fuel ethanol production in 2007, the U.S. and Brazil are responsible for 88% of the 13.1&#160;billion US gallons (50<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) total world production. Biodiesel is commercially available in most <a href="/wiki/Oilseed" class="mw-redirect" title="Oilseed">oilseed</a>-producing states. As of 2005<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it was somewhat more expensive than fossil diesel, though it is still commonly produced in relatively small quantities (in comparison to petroleum products and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>). Due to increasing pollution control and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> requirements and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tax_relief&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tax relief (page does not exist)">tax relief</a>, the U.S. market is expected to grow to 1 to 2&#160;billion US gallons (3.8<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup> to 7.6<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) by 2010.</p>
<p>Biofuels are mainly used mixed with <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. They are also used as additives. The largest biodiesel consumer is the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most light vehicles on the road today in the US can run on blends of up to 10% <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol</a>, and motor vehicle manufacturers already produce vehicles designed to run on much higher ethanol blends. The demand for bioethanol fuel in the United States was stimulated by the discovery in the late 90s that <a href="/wiki/Methyl_tert-butyl_ether" title="Methyl tert-butyl ether">methyl tertiary butyl ether (MTBE)</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Oxygenate" title="Oxygenate">oxygenate</a> additive in gasoline, was contaminating groundwater.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MTBE_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTBE-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_biofuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellulosic biofuel">Cellulosic biofuels</a> are under development, to avoid upward pressure on food prices and land use changes that would be expected to result from a major increase in use of food biofuels.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biofuels are not just limited to liquid fuels. One of the often overlooked uses of biomass in the United States is in the <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. There is a small, but growing number of people using <a href="/wiki/Woodgas" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodgas">woodgas</a> to fuel cars and trucks all across America.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The challenge is to expand the market for biofuels beyond the farm states where they have been most popular to date.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">Flex-fuel vehicles</a> are assisting in this transition because they allow drivers to choose different fuels based on price and availability.</p>
<p>It should also be noted that the growing ethanol and biodiesel industries are providing jobs in plant construction, operations, and maintenance, mostly in rural communities. According to the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Fuels_Association" title="Renewable Fuels Association">Renewable Fuels Association</a>, the ethanol industry created almost 154,000 U.S. jobs in 2005 alone, boosting household income by $5.7 billion. It also contributed about $3.5 billion in <a href="/wiki/Tax_revenue" title="Tax revenue">tax revenues</a> at the local, state, and federal levels.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-7">[7]</a></sup> On the other hand, in 2010, the industry received $6.646 billion in federal support (not counting state and local support).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based upon average U.S. corn yields for the years 2007 through 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> conversion of the entire US corn crop would yield 34.4 billion gallons of ethanol which is approximately 25% of 2012 finished motor fuel demand.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The United States used <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> in the beginning of the 20th century. For example, models of <a href="/wiki/Ford_T" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford T">Ford T</a> ran with <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>. Then the interest in biofuels declined until the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">first</a> and <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">second</a> oil crisis (1973 and 1979).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Department of Energy established the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> in 1974 and started to work in 1977. The NREL publish papers on biofuels. Congress also voted the Energy Policy Act in 1994 and a newer in 2005 to promote renewable fuels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Congress established regulars under the Energy Policy Act of 2005 intended to encourage the mixing of renewable fuels into our nation's motor vehicle fuel supply. Congress supported the renewable fuels program under the Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007 to contain particular annual volume standards for complete renewable fuel and also for the fixed renewable fuel groups of biomass-based diesel, biofuel, and advanced biofuel. The reassessed statutory requirements also incorporate new criteria for both renewable fuels and for the feedstocks used to produce them, including lifecycle greenhouse gas emission thresholds.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/2007_U.S._Farm_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="2007 U.S. Farm Bill">2007 U.S. Farm Bill</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_Fuel_Standard_.28RFS1.29">Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS1)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS1)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">Low-carbon fuel standard</a></div>
<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Fuel_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable Fuel Standard">National Renewable Fuel Standard</a> program (RFS1) was established under the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act of 2005</a>, which amended the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Clean_Air_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Clean Air Act">Clean Air Act</a> by establishing the first national renewable fuel standard. The U.S. Congress gave the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) the responsibility to coordinate with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a>, and stakeholders to design and implement this new program.<sup id="cite_ref-epa.gov_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Renewable Fuel Standard called for 7.5&#160;billion US gallons (28<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of biofuels to be used annually by 2012, expanding the market for biofuels.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The EPA announced that the 2009 Renewable Fuel Standard will require most refiners, importers, and non-oxygenate blenders of gasoline to displace 10.21% of their gasoline with renewable fuels such as ethanol. That requirement aims to ensure that at least 11.1&#160;billion US gallons (42<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of renewable fuels will be sold in 2009, in keeping with the targets established by the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Independence_and_Security_Act_of_2007" title="Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007">Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007</a> (EISA). While the RFS requirement is increasing by about 23%—from 9&#160;billion US gallons (34<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) in 2008 to 11.1&#160;billion US gallons (42<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) in 2009—the percentage requirement is increasing by nearly one third, from 7.76% in 2008 to 10.21% in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-apps1.eere.energy.gov_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apps1.eere.energy.gov-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The EPA determines that fuel blenders are meeting renewable fuel requirements by examining <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Identification_Number" title="Renewable Identification Number">Renewable Identification Numbers</a> that they submit to the agency.</p>
<p>The 2009 RFS is also pushing up against what is known as the "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Blend_wall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blend wall (page does not exist)">blend wall</a>". To address the blend wall issue, DOE and others are studying the use of mid-range blends, such as <a href="/wiki/E15_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="E15 fuel">E15</a> and <a href="/wiki/E20_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="E20 fuel">E20</a>, for use in standard gasoline-burning vehicles. Allowing all gasoline blends to contain up to 20% ethanol would double the potential market for ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-apps1.eere.energy.gov_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apps1.eere.energy.gov-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_Fuel_Standard_.28RFS2.29">Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2009, the EPA released proposed revisions to the National Renewable Fuel Standard program. These revisions addressed changes to the Renewable Fuel Standard program as required by EISA. The revised statutory requirements establish new specific volume standards for <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_biofuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellulosic biofuel">cellulosic biofuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biomass-based diesel</a>, advanced biofuel, and total renewable fuel that must be used in transportation fuel each year. The revised statutory requirements also include new definitions and criteria for both renewable fuels and the feedstocks used to produce them, including new <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emission" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas emission">greenhouse gas emission</a> (GHG) thresholds for renewable fuels. The regulatory requirements for RFS will apply to domestic and foreign producers and importers of renewable fuel.</p>
<p>Of these modifications, several are significantly notable. First, the volume standard under RFS2 was increased beginning in 2008 from 5.4 to 9.0&#160;billion US gallons (20,000,000 to 34,000,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>). Thereafter, the required volume continues to increase under RFS2, eventually reaching 36&#160;billion US gallons (140<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) by 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-epa.gov_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_reduction_thresholds">Greenhouse gas reduction thresholds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas reduction thresholds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>EISA established new renewable fuel categories and eligibility requirements, including setting the first ever mandatory greenhouse gas reduction thresholds for the various categories of fuels. For each renewable fuel pathway, greenhouse gas emissions are evaluated over the full <a href="/wiki/Life_Cycle_Analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Life Cycle Analysis">life cycle</a>, including production and transport of the feedstock; land use change; production, distribution, and blending of the renewable fuel; and end use of the renewable fuel. The GHG emissions are then compared to the life cycle emissions of 2005 <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> baseline fuels (base year established as 2005 by EISA) displaced by the renewable fuel, such as <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>. The life cycle GHG emissions performance reduction thresholds as established by EISA range from 20 to 60 percent reduction depending on the renewable fuel category.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_fuels">Renewable fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Renewable fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethanol_fuel">Ethanol fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ethanol fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel_in_the_United_States" title="Ethanol fuel in the United States">Ethanol fuel in the United States</a></div>
<p>The demand for ethanol fuel in the United States was stimulated by the discovery in the late 1990s that <a href="/wiki/Methyl_tert-butyl_ether" title="Methyl tert-butyl ether">methyl tertiary butyl ether (MTBE)</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Oxygenate" title="Oxygenate">oxygenate</a> additive in gasoline, was contaminating groundwater.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MTBE_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTBE-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to the risks of widespread and costly litigation, and because MTBE use in gasoline was banned in almost 20 states by 2006, the substitution of MTBE opened a new market for ethanol fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup> This demand shift for ethanol as an oxygenate additive took place at a time when <a href="/wiki/Oil_price_increases_since_2003" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil price increases since 2003">oil prices</a> were already significantly rising.<sup id="cite_ref-RFA1E_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFA1E-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RFA08_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFA08-15">[15]</a></sup> This shift also contributed to an expansion in the use of gasohol <a href="/wiki/Common_ethanol_fuel_mixtures#E10" title="Common ethanol fuel mixtures">E10</a> and to a sharp increase in the production and sale of E85 <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">flex vehicles</a> since 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-EEREAP08_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEREAP08-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_ethanol_blends">Low ethanol blends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Low ethanol blends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Common_ethanol_fuel_mixtures" title="Common ethanol fuel mixtures">Common ethanol fuel mixtures</a></div>
<p>Most cars on the road today in the U.S. can run on blends of up to 10% <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol</a> (<a href="/wiki/Common_ethanol_fuel_mixtures#E10" title="Common ethanol fuel mixtures">E10</a>), and motor vehicle manufacturers already produce vehicles designed to run on much higher ethanol blends. Though E10 is mandatory only in 10 states, ethanol blends in the US are available in other states as optional or added on lower percentages as a substitute to <a href="/wiki/MTBE" class="mw-redirect" title="MTBE">MTBE</a> (used to <a href="/wiki/Oxygenate" title="Oxygenate">oxygenate</a> gasoline) without any labeling, making E blends present in two-thirds of the US gas supply.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTE10_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTE10-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flex-fuel_vehicles">Flex-fuel vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flex-fuel vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/E85_in_the_United_States" title="E85 in the United States">E85 in the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">Flexible-fuel vehicle</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/DaimlerChrysler" class="mw-redirect" title="DaimlerChrysler">DaimlerChrysler</a>, and <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="General Motors Corporation">GM</a> are among the automobile companies that sell "<a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Flexible-fuel">flexible-fuel</a>" cars, trucks, and minivans that can use gasoline and ethanol blends ranging from pure gasoline up to 85% ethanol (<a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a>). By mid-2008, there were approximately seven million E85-compatible vehicles on U.S. roads.<sup id="cite_ref-EEREAP08_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEREAP08-16">[16]</a></sup> However, a 2005 survey found that 68% of American flex-fuel car owners were not aware they owned an E85 flex.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup> This is due to the fact that the exterior of flex and non-flex vehicles look exactly the same; there is no sale price difference between them; the lack of consumer's awareness about E85s; and also the decision of American automakers of not putting any kind of exterior labeling, so buyers can be aware they are getting an E85 vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Apollo_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Apollo-19">[19]</a></sup> Since 2006 many new FFV models in the US feature a bright yellow gas cap to remind drivers of the E85 capabilities,<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-YellowCap_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YellowCap-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">GM</a> is also using badging with the text "Flexfuel/E85 Ethanol" to clearly mark the car as an E85 FFV.<sup id="cite_ref-Aboutcom1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aboutcom1-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Aboutcom2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aboutcom2-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A major restriction hampering sales of E85 flex vehicles or fuelling with E85, is the limited infrastructure available to sell E85 to the public, as by October 2008 there were only 1,802 gasoline filling stations selling E85 to the public in the entire US,<sup id="cite_ref-E85stat_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E85stat-26">[26]</a></sup> with a great concentration of E85 stations in the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Belt" title="Corn Belt">Corn Belt</a> states, led by <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> with 357 stations, more than any other state, followed by <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> with 189, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> with 118, and <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> with 112.<sup id="cite_ref-E85stat_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E85stat-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Finde85_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finde85-27">[27]</a></sup> Only seven states do not have E85 available to the public, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-E85stat_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E85stat-26">[26]</a></sup> The main constraint for a more rapid expansion of E85 availability is that it requires dedicated storage tanks at filling stations,<sup id="cite_ref-SusEthanol_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusEthanol-3">[3]</a></sup> at an estimated cost of <a href="/wiki/USD" class="mw-redirect" title="USD">USD</a> 60,000 for each dedicated ethanol tank.<sup id="cite_ref-C.26E0907_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C.26E0907-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chrysler, General Motors, and Ford have each pledged to manufacture 50 percent of their entire vehicle line as flexible fuel in model year 2012, if enough fueling infrastructure develops.<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Regarding energy policy, <a href="/wiki/President-elect" class="mw-redirect" title="President-elect">President-elect</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> pledged during his electoral campaign to significantly reduce oil consumption, with measures that among others include mandating all new vehicles to have FFV capability by the end of 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biodiesel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel_in_the_United_States" title="Biodiesel in the United States">Biodiesel in the United States</a></div>
<p>GreenHunter Energy, Inc. has begun commercial operations at its biodiesel refinery in <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, that can produce 105&#160;million US gallons per year (400<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a) of biodiesel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> That production capacity makes it the largest biodiesel refinery in the United States, barely beating out the 100&#160;million US gallons per year (380<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a) biodiesel refinery built by <a href="/wiki/Imperium_Renewables" title="Imperium Renewables">Imperium Renewables</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>.</p>
<p>For comparison, the total U.S. production capacity for biodiesel reached 2,240&#160;million US gallons per year (8.5<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a) in 2007, although poor market conditions held 2007 production to about 450&#160;million US gallons (1.7<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>), according to the <a href="/wiki/National_Biodiesel_Board" title="National Biodiesel Board">National Biodiesel Board</a> (NBB).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_Bio" title="Fuel Bio">Fuel Bio</a> Opened the largest <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> manufacturing plant on the east coast of the United States in <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth,_New_Jersey" title="Elizabeth, New Jersey">Elizabeth</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_Bio" title="Fuel Bio">Fuel Bio</a>'s operation is capable of producing a name plate capacity of 50&#160;million US gallons (190<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> per year.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methanol_fuel">Methanol fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Methanol fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Methanol_fuel" title="Methanol fuel">Methanol fuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_cars1_005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Wiki_cars1_005.jpg/200px-Wiki_cars1_005.jpg" width="200" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Wiki_cars1_005.jpg/300px-Wiki_cars1_005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Wiki_cars1_005.jpg/400px-Wiki_cars1_005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="614" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_cars1_005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 1996 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Ford Taurus</a> was the first flexible-fuel vehicle produced with versions capable of running with either <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol (E85)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methanol_fuel" title="Methanol fuel">methanol (M85)</a> blended with gasoline.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol</a> was first produced from <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of wood, resulting in its common English name of <a href="/wiki/Wood_alcohol" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood alcohol">wood alcohol</a>. Presently, methanol is usually produced using <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (the chief constituent of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) as a raw material. It may also be produced by <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of many organic materials or by <a href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer Tropsch</a> from <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic gas">synthetic gas</a>, so be called biomethanol. Production of methanol from <a href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a> using <a href="/wiki/Biomass-to-liquid" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomass-to-liquid">Biomass-To-Liquid</a> can offer methanol production from biomass at efficiencies up to 75%. Widespread production by this route has a postulated potential (see Olah reference above) to offer methanol fuel at a low cost and with benefits to the environment. These production methods, however, are not suitable for <a href="/wiki/Small_scale_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Small scale production">small scale production</a>.</p>
<p>Successful test programs in Europe and the US, mainly in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, were conducted with methanol <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">flex-fuel vehicles</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Methanol_fuel#Methanol_fuel_programs_in_the_U.S._and_other_nations" title="Methanol fuel">M85</a> flex-fuel vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Methanolstory_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methanolstory-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SusTransp_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SusTransp-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> began development of a flexible-fuel vehicle in 1982, and between 1985 and 1992, 705 experimental FFVs were built and delivered to California and Canada, including the 1.6L <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(North_America)" title="Ford Escort (North America)">Ford Escort</a>, the 3.0L <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Taurus</a>, and the 5.0L <a href="/wiki/Ford_LTD_Crown_Victoria" title="Ford LTD Crown Victoria">LTD Crown Victoria</a>. These vehicles could operate on either gasoline or methanol with only one fuel system. Legislation was passed to encourage the US auto industry to begin production, which started in 1993 for the M85 FFVs at Ford. In 1996, a new FFV Ford Taurus was developed, with models fully capable of running on either methanol or ethanol blended with gasoline.<sup id="cite_ref-Methanolstory_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methanolstory-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This ethanol version of the Taurus became the first commercial production of an E85 FFV.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The momentum of the FFV production programs at the American car companies continued, although by the end of the 1990s, the emphasis shifted to the E85 version, as it is today.<sup id="cite_ref-Methanolstory_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Methanolstory-34">[34]</a></sup> Ethanol was preferred over methanol because there is a large support from the farming community, and thanks to the government's incentive programs and corn-based ethanol subsidies.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, California's Governor, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a>, terminated the use of methanol after 25 years and 200 million miles of successful operation, to join the expanding use of ethanol driven by producers of corn. In spite of this, he was optimistic about the future of the program, claiming "it will be back." Ethanol is currently (as of 2009) priced at 2 to 3 dollars per gallon, while methanol made from natural gas remains at 47 cents per gallon. Presently there are over 60 operating gas stations in California supplying methanol in their pumps.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Butanol_fuel">Butanol fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Butanol fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A team of chemical engineers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a> have developed a method for converting common algae into <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a>, which can be used as fuel in existing gasoline internal combustible engines. The algae are grown on "raceway" troughs and the algaes growth is enhanced by delivering high concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> through hollow fiber membranes.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Questions have been raised about the amount of energy needed to produce fuel-grade ethanol compared to the amount of energy it releases upon combustion. Depending on who you ask, you may get a different answer as to whether or not ethanol from corn produces more energy than it consumes.<sup id="cite_ref-pubs.acs.org_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.acs.org-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Therefore, companies like BP and <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> have been looking at the next generation of biofuel, specifically investigating butanol.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Specific advantages to using butanol compared to ethanol include, higher energy content per kg, the ability to be blended at higher concentrations without further adaptation of vehicles, and potential to use existing storage/transport infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, using corn as a feedstock to produce either ethanol or butanol seems infeasible without significant technology improvements regarding yields. Currently, about 2.5 US gallons (9.5&#160;L) of butanol can be produced per bushel of corn (373&#160;l/t).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Meanwhile, about 2.75 US gallons (10.4&#160;L) of ethanol can be produced per bushel of corn (410&#160;l/t).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Our current production of ethanol is about 5&#160;billion US gallons per year (19<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a), but it requires 20% of the United States' corn crop and only replaces 1% of its petroleum use.<sup id="cite_ref-pubs.acs.org_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.acs.org-40">[40]</a></sup> Reaching the 36&#160;billion US gallons (140<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) biofuel mandate by 2022, would be a difficult task if only using a corn grain feedstock.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_Natural_Gas_.28RNG.29">Renewable Natural Gas (RNG)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Renewable Natural Gas (RNG)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many cellulosic biomass materials produce methane naturally. Methane is the main component of natural gas. Naturally occurring methane is often considered a source of pollution from landfills, dairy farms, and human waste sources. The EPA routinely issues rules to reduce the emission of methane from landfills<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and other sources.</p>
<p>Naturally occurring methane emissions, typically called landfill gas or biogas, are not clean enough to be introduced directly in the US National Natural Gas Pipeline system, but technology to do so has been developed.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The pipeline quality gas is called Renewable Natural Gas (RNG).</p>
<p>In July 2014 the EPA issued a ruling that RNG produced from landfills would qualify as D3 RIN qualifying fuel under RFS2.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In one month’s time, D3 RIN generation soared from the 4,000 recorded for July to 3.49 million for August 2014. As can be seen by the 4,000 D3 RIN credits issued in July 2014, industry had made little progress at developing an economically viable D3 fuel prior to the EPAs ruling that RNG from landfills would qualify as a D3 fuel.</p>
<p>D3 RIN generation has continued to increase and 2 years later in August 2016 it was 15.6 million EGE (ethanol equivalent gallons).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In 2015, over 98% of D3 RIN credits issued were issued for RNG.</p>
<p>The American Biogas Council believes 17.9 billion diesel gallon equivalents of RNG can be produced each year from the existing US waste stream<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_state">By state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: By state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Louisiana">Louisiana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Louisiana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University_Agricultural_Center" title="Louisiana State University Agricultural Center">Louisiana State University Agricultural Center</a> researchers in the Louisiana Institute for Biofuels and Bioprocessing are studying the regular production of biomass for economically viable conversion to biofuels and bioenergy using existing refinery infrastructure. This project, called the LSU AgCenter Sustainable Bioproducts Initiative,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> involves a team of university and industry partners who are studying how to use energy cane and sweet sorghum to help reinvigorate the Louisiana sugar and chemical industries. The project will expand the United States’ southern regional agricultural sector by utilizing sweet sorghum and energy cane to produce butanol, gasoline, isoprene and byproduct chemicals. Developers believe the project eventually will contribute significantly to improving rural prosperity and job creation in the southern region of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a> researcher Bruce Dale says that 30% of USA's energy can be achieved by 2030. The greenhouse emissions are reduced by 86% for cellulose compared to corn's 29% reduction. A plant is being built now in Georgia to make up to 100&#160;million US gallons (380,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) per year.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minnesota">Minnesota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Minnesota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Minnesota Governor <a href="/wiki/Tim_Pawlenty" title="Tim Pawlenty">Tim Pawlenty</a> signed a bill on May 12, 2008, that will require all diesel fuel sold in the state for use in internal combustion engines to contain at least 20% <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> by May 1, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oregon">Oregon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Oregon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kulongoski" title="Ted Kulongoski">Ted Kulongoski</a> signed <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> in July 2007 that will require all gasoline sold in the state to be blended with 10% <a href="/wiki/Bioethanol" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioethanol">bioethanol</a> (a blend known as BE10) and all diesel fuel sold in the state to be blended with 2% <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> (a blend known as BD2).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Oregon currently has the only biofuels station in the country that can be used by any type of vehicle.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Department_of_Energy_projects">United States Department of Energy projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States Department of Energy projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> has announced<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> that it has selected six university-led advanced biofuels projects to receive up to $4.4 million, subject to annual appropriations. The awardees—<a href="/wiki/Georgia_Tech_Research_Corporation" title="Georgia Tech Research Corporation">Georgia Tech Research Corporation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine" title="University of Maine">University of Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana_State_University_-_Bozeman" class="mw-redirect" title="Montana State University - Bozeman">Montana State University</a>, Steven's <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Technology">Institute of Technology</a> in New Jersey, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toledo" title="University of Toledo">University of Toledo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>—will all receive funding to conduct research and development of cost-effective, environmentally friendly biomass conversion technologies for turning non-food feedstocks into advanced biofuels. Combined with a university cost share of 20%, more than $5.7 million is slated for investment in these projects.</p>
<p>Most of the projects will involve <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a>, including the University of Georgia and Montana State University projects, which are both focused on producing oils from algae. The University of Georgia will investigate the use of poultry litter to produce low-cost nutrients for algae, while Montana State, in partnership with Utah State University, will research the oil content, growth, and oil production of algae cultures in open ponds. Applying microbiology to biomass conversion, the University of Maine will study the use of bacteria to create biofuels from regionally available feedstocks, such as seaweed sludge and paper mill waste streams, while the University of Toledo will attempt to use pellets containing enzymes to efficiently convert cellulosic biomass into ethanol.</p>
<p>In contrast, Georgia Tech Research Corporation and Steven's Institute of Technology are both investigating the <a href="/wiki/Gasification_of_biomass" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasification of biomass">gasification of biomass</a>. Georgia Tech will evaluate two experimental gasifiers run on <a href="/wiki/Forest_residue" class="mw-redirect" title="Forest residue">forest residues</a>, while Steven's Institute will test a novel <a href="/wiki/Microchannel_reactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Microchannel reactor">microchannel reactor</a> that gasifies <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis_oil" title="Pyrolysis oil">pyrolysis oil</a>, a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum-like_oil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Petroleum-like oil (page does not exist)">petroleum-like oil</a> produced by exposing biomass sources such as <a href="/wiki/Woodchips" title="Woodchips">wood chips</a> to high temperatures in the absence of oxygen. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gasified_biomass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gasified biomass (page does not exist)">Gasified biomass</a> can be used as a gaseous fuel or passed through a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> to produce a wide range of liquid fuels and chemicals.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuel_companies">Biofuel companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biofuel_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Biofuel companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unfortunately, costs of producing ethanol from cellulosic feedstock such as wood chips are still about 70% higher than production from corn, because of an extra step in the production process, when compared to production of corn-derived ethanol. Until recently, the idea of extracting ethanol from farm waste and other sources was barely clinging to life in the recesses of university campuses and federal labs, because production problems, as well as the need to bring together a vast team of specialists. Consider: Finding a bacterium from a cow's intestinal tract or from elephant dung that has the correct enzyme to degrade cellulose, and then bringing in <a href="/wiki/Geneticists" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneticists">geneticists</a> to modify that enzyme kept this discouraging feat from ever growing beyond its embryonic state. Now, that is all changing with a race by approximately thirty companies attempting to accomplish this alchemical feat, and in the process working directly or coordinating with: environmental groups, biotechnology firms, some major oil companies, chemical giants, auto makers, defense hawks, and venture capitalists. The winner will be whoever can make cellulosic ethanol in mass quantities for as little money per gallon as possible.</p>
<p>With the majority of such biofuel companies (<a href="/wiki/Iogen_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="Iogen Corp.">Iogen</a> Corporation, <a href="/wiki/SunOpta" title="SunOpta">SunOpta</a>'s BioProcess Group, <a href="/wiki/Genencor_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Genencor International">Genencor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novozymes" title="Novozymes">Novozymes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.dyadic.com/">Dyadic International, Inc. (DYAI)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Energy_Sources" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative Energy Sources">Alternative Energy Sources</a>, Inc. [Nasdaq:AENS], <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flex_Fuels_USA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Flex Fuels USA (page does not exist)">Flex Fuels USA</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a> (now owned by Alternative Energy Sources),<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=BRI_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="BRI Energy (page does not exist)">BRI Energy</a>, LLC,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abengoa_Bioenergy" class="mw-redirect" title="Abengoa Bioenergy">Abengoa Bioenergy</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> located in North America, the United States is in a unique position to lead the way in the development, production, and sale of a new source of energy.</p>
<p>One notable company that deserves special mention is <a href="/wiki/Archer-Daniels-Midland_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Archer-Daniels-Midland Company">Archer-Daniels-Midland Company</a> (ADM) which has already invested heavily into building approximately 100 corn-ethanol production plants, known as bio-refineries, and churns out about one-fifth of the country's ethanol supply. This occurred due to seasonal overcapacity in its <a href="/wiki/Corn_syrup" title="Corn syrup">corn syrup</a> plants when surplus was available to produce ethanol. Moreover, ADM is in a unique position to utilize unused parts of the corn crop, and convert previously discarded waste into a viable product.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The hull surrounding corn contains fiber that the <a href="/wiki/Decatur,_Illinois" title="Decatur, Illinois">Decatur, Illinois</a>, grain-processing giant's ethanol-making microorganisms can not use. Figuring out how to convert the fiber into more sugar could increase the output of an existing corn-ethanol plant by 15%. Consequently, ADM wouldn't have to figure out how to collect a new source of biomass but merely use the existing infrastructure for gathering corn—resulting in an advantage over its competitors. ADM executives want government help to build a plant that could cost between $50 million and $100 million. Prescient in their position in the quest for success, ADM recently hired the head of petroleum refining at <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>, Patricia A. Woertz, to metamorphasize ADM into the <a href="/wiki/Exxon-Mobil" class="mw-redirect" title="Exxon-Mobil">Exxon-Mobil</a> of the ethanol industry.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> If ADM succeeds, it will catapult beyond the ethanol industry to compete with the larger, global energy industry. In essence, the old paradigm of processing a barrel of crude oil into gasoline will be replaced with processing a bushel of corn into ethanol.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, the chemical giant, is attempting to figure out how to construct a bio-refinery fueled by <a href="/wiki/Corn_stover" title="Corn stover">corn stover</a>—the stalk and leaves that are left in the field after farmers harvest their crop. The company's goal is to make ethanol from cellulose as cheaply as from corn kernels by 2009. If it works, the technology could double the amount of ethanol produced by a field of corn.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diversa" class="mw-redirect" title="Diversa">Diversa</a> Corporation, a biotech company based in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, examined how biomass is converted into energy in the natural environment. They have found that the enzymes inherent in the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> that inhabit the digestive tracts of the household termite efficiently convert 95% of cellulose into fermentable sugars. Using proprietary <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> extraction and <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">cloning</a> technologies, they were able to isolate the cellulose-degrading enzymes. By reenacting this natural process, the company created a cocktail of high-performance enzymes for industrial ethanol production enablers. Although still in the early stages of this work, the initial results are promising. Currently, these expensive enzymes cost about 25 cents per gallon of ethanol, although this price is very likely to decline by half in the coming years.</p>
<p>Construction of the first U.S. commercial plant producing cellulosic ethanol will commence in the State of Iowa in February 2007. The Voyager Ethanol plant in <a href="/wiki/Emmetsburg,_Iowa" title="Emmetsburg, Iowa">Emmetsburg</a>, owned by <a href="/wiki/POET" title="POET">POET, LLC</a>, will be converted from a 50-million-US-gallon-per-year (190<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a) conventional corn dry mill facility into a 125-million-US-gallon-per-year (470<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup>&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/a) commercial-scale biorefinery producing ethanol from not only corn but also the stalk, leaves, and cobs of the corn plant.</p>
<p>Most ethanol plants rely on <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> to power their processing equipment. The process to be used at the Emmetsburg plant will enable the plant to make 11% more ethanol by weight of corn and 27% more by area of corn. The process cuts the need for fossil fuel power at the plant by 83% by using some of its own byproduct for power. The $200 million plant is scheduled to begin in February and take about 30 months to complete. Project completion is contingent upon partial funding from a USDOE grant, which is likely as the U.S. Government views the renewable energy project as a full-blown national security issue.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Green_Plains_Renewable_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Plains Renewable Energy">Green Plains Renewable Energy</a> claims to be the fourth largest ethanol fuel producer in North America (as of February 2012).<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
