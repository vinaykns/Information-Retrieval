<p><b>Carbon-neutral fuels</b> can refer to a variety of energy fuels or energy systems which have no net greenhouse gas emissions or carbon footprint. One class is <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic fuel</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ammonia#As_a_fuel" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) produced from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenate</a> waste <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> recycled from <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_emissions_from_fossil-fuel_combustion" title="Flue-gas emissions from fossil-fuel combustion">power plant flue</a> <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gas</a> or derived from <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>. Other types can be produced from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources such as wind turbines, solar panels, and hydroelectric power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeman2008_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeman2008-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wang2012_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang2012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MacDowell2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDowell2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eisaman2012_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eisaman2012-5">[5]</a></sup> Such fuels are potentially <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon-neutral">carbon-neutral</a> because they do not result in a net increase in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Graves2011rev_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graves2011rev-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Socolow2011_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Socolow2011-7">[7]</a></sup> Until <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">captured carbon</a> is used for <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a> <a href="/wiki/Feedstock" class="mw-redirect" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a>, carbon neutral fuel synthesis is the primary means of <b>carbon capture and utilization</b> or <b>recycling</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>To the extent that carbon-neutral fuels displace <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, or if they are produced from waste carbon or seawater carbonic acid, and their combustion is subject to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> at the flue or exhaust pipe, they result in <a href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative carbon dioxide emission</a> and net <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> from the atmosphere, and thus constitute a form of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_remediation" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas remediation">greenhouse gas remediation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goeppert2012_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goeppert2012-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-House2011_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-House2011-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lackner2012_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lackner2012-11">[11]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">power to gas</a> carbon-neutral and <b>carbon-negative fuels</b> can be produced by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis of water</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a> to produce methane which may then be <a href="/wiki/Power_storage#Synthetic_hydrocarbon_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Power storage">stored</a> to be burned later in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural gas power plant">power plants</a> as synthetic <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, transported by pipeline, truck, or tanker ship, or be used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">gas to liquids</a> processes such as the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> to make traditional fuels for transportation or heating.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pennline2010_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennline2010-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Graves2011cells_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graves2011cells-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carbon-neutral fuels are used in Germany and Iceland for <a href="/wiki/Power_storage#Synthetic_hydrocarbon_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Power storage">distributed storage of renewable energy</a>, minimizing problems of wind and solar intermittency, and enabling transmission of wind, water, and solar power through existing natural gas pipelines. Such renewable fuels could alleviate the costs and dependency issues of imported fossil fuels without requiring either electrification of the vehicle fleet or conversion to hydrogen or other fuels, enabling continued compatible and affordable vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup> A 250 kilowatt synthetic methane plant has been built in Germany and it is being scaled up to 10 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon-neutral fuels are synthetic hydrocarbons. They can be produced in chemical reactions between carbon dioxide, which can be captured from power plants or the air, and hydrogen, which is created by the electrolysis of water using renewable energy. The fuel, often referred to as electrofuel, stores the energy that was used in the production of the hydrogen.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson-16">[16]</a></sup> Coal can also be used to produce the hydrogen, but that would not be a carbon-neutral source. Carbon dioxide can be captured and buried, making fossil fuels carbon-neutral, although not renewable. Carbon capture from exhaust gas can make carbon-neutral fuels carbon negative. Other hydrocarbons can be broken down to produce hydrogen and carbon dioxide which could then be stored while the hydrogen is used for energy or fuel, which would also be carbon-neutral.<sup id="cite_ref-Kleiner_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kleiner-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most energy-efficient fuel to produce is <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which is made from a chemical reaction of a carbon-dioxide molecule with three hydrogen molecules to produce methanol and water. The stored energy can be recovered by burning the methanol in a combustion engine, releasing carbon dioxide, water, and heat. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a> can be produced in a similar reaction. More energy can be used to combine methanol or methane into larger hydrocarbon fuel molecules.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Researchers have also suggested using methanol to produce <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a>. This fuel could be used as a substitute for diesel fuel due to its ability to self ignite under high pressure and temperature. It is already being used in some areas for heating and energy generation. It is nontoxic, but must be stored under pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Olah_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olah-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Octane" title="Octane">Octane</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> can also be produced from carbon dioxide and hydrogen.</p>
<p>All synthetic hydrocarbons are generally produced at temperatures of 200–300&#160;°C, and at pressures of 20 to 50 bar. <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">Catalysts</a> are usually used to improve the efficiency of the reaction and create the desired type of hydrocarbon fuel. Such reactions are exothermic and use about 3&#160;mol of hydrogen per mole of carbon dioxide involved. They also produce large amounts of water as a byproduct.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeman2008_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeman2008-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_carbon_for_recycling">Sources of carbon for recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources of carbon for recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most economical source of carbon for recycling into fuel is <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_emissions_from_fossil-fuel_combustion" title="Flue-gas emissions from fossil-fuel combustion">flue-gas emissions from fossil-fuel combustion</a> where it can be obtained for about USD $7.50 per ton.<sup id="cite_ref-MacDowell2010_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDowell2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Socolow2011_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Socolow2011-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pennline2010_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pennline2010-13">[13]</a></sup> Automobile exhaust gas capture has also been seen as economical but would require extensive design changes or retrofitting.<sup id="cite_ref-Musadi2011_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Musadi2011-19">[19]</a></sup> Since carbonic acid in seawater is in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibrium</a> with atmospheric carbon dioxide, extraction of carbon from seawater has been studied.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Willauer2011_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willauer2011-21">[21]</a></sup> Researchers have estimated that carbon extraction from seawater would cost about $50 per ton.<sup id="cite_ref-Eisaman2012_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eisaman2012-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_air_capture" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon dioxide air capture">Carbon capture from ambient air</a> is more costly, at between $600 and $1000 per ton and is considered impractical for fuel synthesis or carbon sequestration.<sup id="cite_ref-Socolow2011_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Socolow2011-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Goeppert2012_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goeppert2012-9">[9]</a></sup> Direct air capture is less developed than other methods. Proposals for this method involve using a caustic chemical to react with carbon dioxide in the air to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>. These can then be broken down and hydrated to release pure CO<sub>2</sub> gas and regenerate the caustic chemical. This process requires more energy than other methods because carbon dioxide is at much lower concentrations in the atmosphere than in other sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Researchers have also suggested using biomass as a carbon source for fuel production. Adding hydrogen to the biomass would reduce its carbon to produce fuel. This method has the advantage of using plant matter to cheaply capture carbon dioxide. The plants also add some chemical energy to the fuel from biological molecules. This may be a more efficient use of biomass than conventional <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> because it uses most of the carbon and chemical energy from the biomass instead of releasing as much energy and carbon. Its main disadvantage is, as with conventional ethanol production, it competes with food production.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeman2008_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeman2008-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_and_nuclear_energy_costs">Renewable and nuclear energy costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renewable and nuclear energy costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nighttime <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> is considered the most economical form of electrical power with which to synthesize fuel, because the <a href="/wiki/Load_curve" class="mw-redirect" title="Load curve">load curve</a> for electricity peaks sharply during the warmest hours of the day, but wind tends to blow slightly more at night than during the day. Therefore, the price of nighttime wind power is often much less expensive than any alternative. Off-peak wind power prices in high wind penetration areas of the U.S. averaged 1.64 cents per <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt-hour">kilowatt-hour</a> in 2009, but only 0.71 cents/kWh during the least expensive six hours of the day.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup> Typically, <a href="/wiki/Wholesale" class="mw-redirect" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> electricity costs 2 to 5 cents/kWh during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Commercial fuel synthesis companies suggest they can produce gasoline for less than <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> fuels when oil costs more than $55 per barrel.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Navy estimates that 100 megawatts of electricity can produce 41,000 gallons of <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> per day and shipboard production from nuclear power would cost about $6 per gallon. While that was about twice the petroleum fuel cost in 2010, it is expected to be much less than the market price in less than five years if recent trends continue. Moreover, since the delivery of fuel to a <a href="/wiki/Carrier_battle_group" title="Carrier battle group">carrier battle group</a> costs about $8 per gallon, shipboard production is already much less expensive.<sup id="cite_ref-jetsea_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jetsea-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstration_projects_and_commercial_development">Demonstration projects and commercial development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demonstration projects and commercial development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 250 kilowatt methane synthesis plant was constructed by the Center for Solar Energy and Hydrogen Research (ZSW) at <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> in Germany and began operating in 2010. It is being upgraded to 10 megawatts, scheduled for completion in autumn, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/George_Olah" class="mw-redirect" title="George Olah">George Olah</a> carbon dioxide recycling plant operated by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a> in <a href="/wiki/Grindav%C3%ADk" title="Grindavík">Grindavík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> has been producing 2 million liters of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> transportation fuel per year from flue exhaust of the <a href="/wiki/Svartsengi_Power_Station" title="Svartsengi Power Station">Svartsengi Power Station</a> since 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It has the capacity to produce 5 million liters per year.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> has constructed a carbon-neutral <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (LNG) plant in <a href="/wiki/Werlte,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Werlte, Germany">Werlte, Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The plant is intended to produce transportation fuel to offset LNG used in their <a href="/wiki/Audi_A3#A3_Sportback_g-tron_.282013-.29" title="Audi A3">A3 Sportback g-tron</a> automobiles, and can keep 2,800 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the environment per year at its initial capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Commercial developments are taking place in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia, South Carolina</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Camarillo,_California" title="Camarillo, California">Camarillo, California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington, England">Darlington, England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A demonstration project in <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a> proposes synthesizing both fuels and <a href="/wiki/Food_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Food oil">food oils</a> from recovered flue gases.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_remediation">Greenhouse gas remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon-neutral fuels might lead to greenhouse gas remediation because carbon dioxide gas would be reused to produce fuel instead of being released into the atmosphere. Capturing the carbon dioxide in flue gas emissions from power plants would eliminate their greenhouse gas emissions, although burning the fuel in vehicles would release that carbon because there is no economical way to capture those emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup> This approach would reduce net carbon dioxide emission by about 50% if it were used on all fossil fuel power plants. Most <a href="/wiki/Coal_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal power plant">coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural gas power plant">natural gas power plants</a> have been predicted to be economically retrofittable with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon dioxide scrubbers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> to recycle flue exhaust or for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Socolow2011_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Socolow2011-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-House2011_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-House2011-10">[10]</a></sup> Such recycling is expected to not only cost less than the excess <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Economic impacts of climate change">economic impacts of climate change</a> if it were not done, but also to pay for itself as global fuel <a href="/wiki/Economic_demand" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic demand">demand</a> growth and <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> shortages increase the price of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fungibility" title="Fungibility">fungible</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goeppert2012_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goeppert2012-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lackner2012_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lackner2012-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Capturing CO<sub>2</sub> directly from the air or extracting carbonic acid from seawater would also reduce the amount of carbon dioxide in the environment, and create a closed cycle of carbon to eliminate new carbon dioxide emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeman2008_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeman2008-2">[2]</a></sup> Use of these methods would eliminate the need for fossil fuels entirely, assuming that enough renewable energy could be generated to produce the fuel. Using synthetic hydrocarbons to produce synthetic materials such as plastics could result in permanent sequestration of carbon from the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_fuels.2C_methanol_or_ethanol">Traditional fuels, methanol or ethanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Traditional fuels, methanol or ethanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Methanol_economy" title="Methanol economy">Methanol economy</a></div>
<p>Some authorities have recommended producing <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> instead of traditional transportation fuels. It is a liquid at normal temperatures and can be toxic if ingested. Methanol has a higher <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane rating</a> than gasoline but a lower <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>, and can be mixed with other fuels or used on its own. It may also be used in the production of more complex hydrocarbons and polymers. Direct methanol fuel cells have been developed by Caltech's <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> to convert methanol and oxygen into electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-Olah_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olah-18">[18]</a></sup> It is possible to convert methanol into gasoline, jet fuel or other hydrocarbons, but that requires additional energy and more complex production facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-12">[12]</a></sup> Methanol is slightly more corrosive than traditional fuels, requiring automobile modifications on the order of USD $100 each to use it.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeman2008_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeman2008-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Steinberg1995_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steinberg1995-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, a method using carbon spikes, copper <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanoparticles</a> and nitrogen that converts carbon dioxide to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> was developed.<sup id="cite_ref-CO2_to_ethanol_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CO2_to_ethanol-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon-neutral_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Investigation of carbon-neutral fuels has been ongoing for decades. A 1965 report suggested synthesizing methanol from carbon dioxide in air using nuclear power for a mobile fuel depot.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Shipboard production of synthetic fuel using <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> was studied in 1977 and 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> A 1984 report studied the recovery of carbon dioxide from fossil fuel plants.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> A 1995 report compared converting <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle fleet">vehicle fleets</a> for the use of carbon-neutral methanol with the further <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids#Mobil_process" title="Gas to liquids">synthesis of gasoline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Steinberg1995_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steinberg1995-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
