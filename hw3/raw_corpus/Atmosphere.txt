<p>An <b>atmosphere</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="gre" xml:lang="gre">ἀτμός <i>(atmos)</i></span>, meaning 'vapour', and <span lang="" xml:lang="">σφαῖρα <i>(sphaira)</i></span>, meaning 'sphere'<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a layer of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> surrounding a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">material body</a>, that is held in place by the <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> of that body. An atmosphere is more likely to be retained if the gravity it is subject to is high and the temperature of the atmosphere is low.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a> is mostly composed of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (about 78%), <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (about 21%), <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> (about 0.9%) with carbon dioxide and other gases in trace amounts. Oxygen is used by most <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> for <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a>, nitrogen is <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">fixed</a> by bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> used in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nucleotides">nucleotides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is used by <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> for <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. The atmosphere helps protect living organisms from genetic damage by <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">solar</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. Its current composition is the product of billions of years of biochemical modification of the <a href="/wiki/Paleoatmosphere" title="Paleoatmosphere">paleoatmosphere</a> by living organisms.</p>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere" title="Stellar atmosphere">stellar atmosphere</a> describes the outer region of a star, and typically includes the portion starting from the <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a> <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> outwards. Stars with sufficiently low temperatures may form compound <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in their outer atmosphere.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure">Pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric pressure</a></div>
<p>Atmospheric pressure is the <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> per unit area that is applied perpendicularly to a surface by the surrounding gas. It is determined by a planet's gravitational force in combination with the total mass of a column of gas above a location. On Earth, units of air pressure are based on the internationally recognized <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere</a> (atm), which is defined as 101.325 k<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> (760&#160;<a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">Torr</a> or 14.696 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>). It is measured with a barometer.</p>
<p>The pressure of an atmospheric gas decreases with altitude due to the diminishing mass of gas above. The height at which the pressure from an atmosphere declines by a factor of <i><a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)">e</a></i> (an <a href="/wiki/Irrational_number" title="Irrational number">irrational number</a> with a value of 2.71828..) is called the <a href="/wiki/Scale_height" title="Scale height">scale height</a> and is denoted by <i>H</i>. For an atmosphere with a uniform temperature, the scale height is proportional to the temperature and inversely proportional to the product of the mean <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> of dry air and the local acceleration of gravity at that location. For such a model atmosphere, the pressure declines exponentially with increasing altitude. However, atmospheres are not uniform in temperature, so the exact determination of the atmospheric pressure at any particular altitude is more complex.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_escape">Atmospheric escape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atmospheric escape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_escape" title="Atmospheric escape">Atmospheric escape</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Surface_gravity" title="Surface gravity">Surface gravity</a>, the force that holds down an atmosphere, differs significantly among the planets. For example, the large gravitational force of the giant planet <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> is able to retain light gases such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> that escape from objects with lower gravity. Secondly, the distance from the Sun determines the energy available to heat atmospheric gas to the point where some fraction of its molecules' <a href="/wiki/Thermal_motion" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal motion">thermal motion</a> exceed the planet's <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a>, allowing those to escape a planet's gravitational grasp. Thus, the distant and cold <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> are able to retain their atmospheres despite their relatively low gravities. <a href="/wiki/Rogue_planet" title="Rogue planet">Rogue planets</a>, theoretically, may also retain thick atmospheres.</p>
<p>Since a collection of gas molecules be moving at a wide range of velocities, there will always be some fast enough to produce a slow leakage of gas into space. Lighter molecules move faster than heavier ones with the same thermal <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>, and so gases of low <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular weight</a> are lost more rapidly than those of high molecular weight. It is thought that <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> may have lost much of their water when, after being <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation" title="Photodissociation">photo dissociated</a> into hydrogen and oxygen by solar <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, the hydrogen escaped. <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> helps to prevent this, as, normally, the solar wind would greatly enhance the escape of hydrogen. However, over the past 3 billion years Earth may have lost gases through the magnetic polar regions due to auroral activity, including a net 2% of its atmospheric oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other mechanisms that can cause <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_escape" title="Atmospheric escape">atmosphere depletion</a> are <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>-induced sputtering, <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact</a> erosion, <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, and sequestration—sometimes referred to as "freezing out"—into the <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_ice_cap" title="Polar ice cap">polar caps</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrain">Terrain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terrain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Atmospheres have dramatic effects on the surfaces of rocky bodies. Objects that have no atmosphere, or that have only an exosphere, have terrain that is covered in <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">craters</a>. Without an atmosphere, the planet has no protection from <a href="/wiki/Meteors" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteors">meteors</a>, and all of them collide with the surface and create craters.</p>
<p>A rocky body with a thick atmosphere does not have significant craters on its surface. The friction generated when a meteor enters an atmosphere causes the vast majority of it to burn up before hitting the surface. When meteors do impact, the effects are often erased by the action of wind. As a result, craters are rare on objects with atmospheres.</p>
<p>All objects with atmospheres have <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wind_erosion" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind erosion">Wind erosion</a> is a significant factor in shaping the terrain of rocky planets with atmospheres, and over time can erase the effects of both craters and <a href="/wiki/Volcanoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanoes">volcanoes</a>. In addition, since <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>s can not exist without pressure, an atmosphere allows liquid to be present at the surface, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>. <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> are known to have liquids at their surface and terrain on the planet suggests that <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> had liquid on its surface in the past.</p>

<p>Initial atmospheric composition is generally related to the chemistry and temperature of the local <a href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a> during planetary formation and the subsequent escape of interior gases. The original atmospheres started with the radially local rotating gases that collapsed to the spaced rings that formed the planets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They were then modified over time by various complex factors, resulting in quite different outcomes.</p>
<p>The atmospheres of the planets <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> are primarily composed of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, with small quantities of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and traces of other gases.</p>
<p>The atmospheric composition on Earth is largely governed by the by-products of the life that it sustains. Dry air from <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a> contains 78.08% nitrogen, 20.95% oxygen, 0.93% argon, 0.04% carbon dioxide, and traces of hydrogen, helium, and other "noble" gases (by volume), but generally a variable amount of water vapour is also present, on average about 1% at sea level.</p>
<p>The low temperatures and higher gravity of the Solar System's <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a>—<a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>—allow them more readily to retain gases with low <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular masses</a>. These planets have hydrogen–helium atmospheres, with trace amounts of more complex compounds.</p>
<p>Two satellites of the outer planets possess significant atmospheres. <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, a moon of Saturn, and <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>, a moon of Neptune, have atmospheres mainly of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. When in the part of its orbit closest to the Sun, <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> has an atmosphere of nitrogen and methane similar to Triton's, but these gases are frozen when it is farther from the Sun.</p>
<p>Other bodies within the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> have extremely thin atmospheres not in equilibrium. These include the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> gas), <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> (sodium gas), <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> (oxygen), <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enceladus (moon)">Enceladus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>).</p>
<p>The first exoplanet whose atmospheric composition was determined is <a href="/wiki/HD_209458" title="HD 209458">HD 209458b</a>, a gas giant with a close orbit around a star in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Pegasus" title="Pegasus">Pegasus</a>. Its atmosphere is heated to temperatures over 1,000&#160;K, and is steadily escaping into space. Hydrogen, oxygen, carbon and sulfur have been detected in the planet's inflated atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Atmosphere of Earth</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a> consists of a number of layers, summarised in the diagram above which explains what the layers are , that differ in properties such as composition, temperature and pressure. The lowest layer is the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, which extends from the surface to the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Three quarters of the atmosphere's mass resides within the troposphere, and is the layer within which the Earth's weather develops. The depth of this layer varies between 17&#160;km at the equator to 7&#160;km at the poles. The stratosphere, extending from the top of the troposphere to the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>, contains the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. The ozone layer ranges in altitude between 15 and 35&#160;km, and is where most of the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation from the Sun is absorbed. The top of the mesosphere, ranges from 50 to 85&#160;km, and is the layer wherein most <a href="/wiki/Meteors" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteors">meteors</a> burn up. The <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> extends from 85&#160;km to the base of the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> at 690&#160;km and contains the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, a region where the atmosphere is ionised by incoming solar radiation. The ionosphere increases in thickness and moves closer to the Earth during daylight and rises at night allowing certain frequencies of radio communication a greater range. The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="Kármán line">Kármán line</a>, located within the thermosphere at an altitude of 100&#160;km, is commonly used to define the boundary between Earth's atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> begins variously from about 690 to 1,000&#160;km above the surface, where it interacts with the planet's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>, to space. Each of the layers has a different <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>, defining the rate of change in temperature with height.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">Atmospheric circulation</a></div>
<p>The circulation of the atmosphere occurs due to thermal differences when <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> becomes a more efficient transporter of heat than <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a>. On planets where the primary heat source is solar radiation, excess heat in the tropics is transported to higher latitudes. When a planet generates a significant amount of heat internally, such as is the case for <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, convection in the atmosphere can transport thermal energy from the higher temperature interior up to the surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the perspective of a planetary <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a>, the atmosphere is an evolutionary agent essential to the shaping of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> picks up <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> and other particles which when they collide with the terrain erodes the <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">relief</a> and leaves <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposits</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">eolian</a> processes). <a href="/wiki/Frost_line" title="Frost line">Frost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitations</a>, which depend on the atmospheric composition, also influence the relief. Climate changes can influence a planet's geological history. Conversely, studying surface of Earth leads to an understanding of the atmosphere and climate of a planet&#160;— both its present state and its past.</p>
<p>For a <a href="/wiki/Meteorologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorologist">meteorologist</a>, the composition of the atmosphere determines the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and its variations.</p>
<p>For a <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paleontologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleontologist">paleontologist</a>, the atmospheric composition is closely dependent on the appearance of the life and its <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.</p>
