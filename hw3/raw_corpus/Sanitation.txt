<p><b>Sanitation</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygienic</a> means of <a href="/wiki/Health_promotion" title="Health promotion">promoting health</a> through <a href="/wiki/Hazard_prevention" class="mw-redirect" title="Hazard prevention">prevention</a> of human contact with the <a href="/wiki/Hazard" title="Hazard">hazards</a> of <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">wastes</a> as well as the treatment and proper disposal of <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>. Hazards can be either physical, <a href="/wiki/Microbiological" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbiological">microbiological</a>, biological or chemical agents of disease. Wastes that can cause health problems include human and animal excreta, solid wastes, domestic wastewater (sewage or <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a>) industrial wastes and agricultural wastes. Hygienic means of prevention can be by using engineering solutions (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary sewers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> management, solid waste management, excreta management), simple technologies (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrines" class="mw-redirect" title="Pit latrines">pit latrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilets" class="mw-redirect" title="Dry toilets">dry toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilet" title="Urine-diverting dry toilet">urine-diverting dry toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tank</a>s), or even simply by <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_(public_health)" title="Behavior change (public health)">behavior changes</a> in personal hygiene practices, such as <a href="/wiki/Hand_washing" title="Hand washing">hand washing</a> with soap.</p>
<p>Providing sanitation to people requires a systems approach, rather than only focusing on the <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> plant itself.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> The experience of the user, excreta and wastewater collection methods, transportation or conveyance of waste, treatment, and <a href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse</a> or disposal all need to be thoroughly considered.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main objective of a sanitation system is to protect and promote human health by providing a clean environment and breaking the cycle of disease.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> defines the term "sanitation" as follows:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"Sanitation generally refers to the provision of facilities and services for the safe disposal of human urine and feces. The word 'sanitation' also refers to the maintenance of hygienic conditions, through services such as garbage collection and wastewater disposal."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Sanitation includes all four of these engineering infrastructure items (even though often only the first one is strongly associated with the term "sanitation"): Excreta management systems, wastewater management systems (included here are <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Wastewater treatment plants">wastewater treatment plants</a>), solid waste management systems, drainage systems for rainwater, also called stormwater drainage.</p>
<p>There are some slight variations on the definition of sanitation in use. For example, for many organisations, <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a> promotion is seen as an integral part of sanitation. For this reason, the <a href="/wiki/Water_Supply_and_Sanitation_Collaborative_Council" title="Water Supply and Sanitation Collaborative Council">Water Supply and Sanitation Collaborative Council</a> defines sanitation as "The collection, transport, treatment and disposal or <a href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse of human excreta</a>, domestic wastewater and solid waste, and associated hygiene promotion."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the fact that sanitation includes wastewater treatment, the two terms are often use side by side as "sanitation and wastewater management". The term sanitation has been connected to several descriptors so that the terms sustainable sanitation, improved sanitation, unimproved sanitation, environmental sanitation, on-site sanitation, ecological sanitation, dry sanitation are all in use today. Sanitation should be regarded with a systems approach in mind which includes collection/containment, conveyance/transport, treatment, disposal or reuse.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The overall purposes of sanitation are to provide a healthy living environment for everyone, to protect the natural resources (such as <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>), and to provide safety, security and <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a> for people when they <a href="/wiki/Defecate" class="mw-redirect" title="Defecate">defecate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Urinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Urinate">urinate</a>.</p>
<p>Effective sanitation systems provide barriers between excreta and humans in such a way as to break the <a href="/wiki/Disease_transmission" class="mw-redirect" title="Disease transmission">disease transmission</a> cycle (for example in the case of fecal-borne diseases).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This aspect is visualised with the F-diagram where all major routes of <a href="/wiki/Fecal-oral_route" class="mw-redirect" title="Fecal-oral route">fecal-oral disease transmission</a> begin with the letter F: feces, fingers, flies, fields, fluids, food.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term sanitation is connected with various descriptors or adjectives to signify certain types of sanitation systems (which may deal only with <a href="/wiki/Human_excreta" class="mw-redirect" title="Human excreta">human excreta</a> management or with the entire sanitation system, i.e. also greywater, stormwater and solid waste management) - in alphabetical order:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community-led_total_sanitation">Community-led total sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Community-led total sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Community-led_total_sanitation" title="Community-led total sanitation">Community-led total sanitation</a></div>
<p>Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS) is an approach to achieve <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_(public_health)" title="Behavior change (public health)">behavior change</a> in mainly rural people by a process of "triggering", leading to spontaneous and long-term abandonment of <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> practices. CLTS takes an approach to rural sanitation that works without hardware subsidies and that facilitates communities to recognize the problem of open defecation and take <a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action</a> to clean up and become "open defecation free".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_sanitation">Dry sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dry sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">Dry toilet</a></div>
<p>The term "dry sanitation" is somewhat misleading as sanitation includes hand-washing and can never be "dry". A more precise term would be "dry excreta management". When people speak of "dry sanitation" they usually mean sanitation systems with <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">dry toilets</a> with <a href="/wiki/Urine_diversion" title="Urine diversion">urine diversion</a>, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Urine-diverting_dry_toilet" title="Urine-diverting dry toilet">urine-diverting dry toilet</a> (UDDT).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_sanitation">Ecological sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecological sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_sanitation" title="Ecological sanitation">Ecological sanitation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_sanitation" title="Ecological sanitation">Ecological sanitation</a>, which is commonly abbreviated to ecosan, is an approach, rather than a technology or a device which is characterized by a desire to "close the loop" (mainly for the nutrients and organic matter) between sanitation and agriculture in a safe manner. Put in other words: "Ecosan systems safely recycle excreta resources (plant nutrients and organic matter) to crop production in such a way that the use of non-renewable resources is minimised". When properly designed and operated, ecosan systems provide a hygienically safe, economical, and closed-loop system to convert human excreta into nutrients to be returned to the soil, and water to be returned to the land. Ecosan is also called resource-oriented sanitation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_sanitation">Environmental sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental sanitation encompasses the control of environmental factors that are connected to <a href="/wiki/Disease_transmission" class="mw-redirect" title="Disease transmission">disease transmission</a>. Subsets of this category are solid waste management, water and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> treatment, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a> treatment and noise and pollution control.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improved_and_unimproved_sanitation">Improved and unimproved sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Improved and unimproved sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">Improved sanitation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">Improved sanitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unimproved_sanitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Unimproved sanitation">unimproved sanitation</a> refers to the management of human feces at the household level. This terminology is the indicator used to describe the target of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goal" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennium Development Goal">Millennium Development Goal</a> on sanitation, by the <a href="/wiki/WHO" class="mw-redirect" title="WHO">WHO</a>/<a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_sanitation">Lack of sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lack of sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lack of sanitation refers to the absence of sanitation. In practical terms it usually means lack of toilets or lack of hygienic toilets that anybody would want to use voluntarily. The result of lack of sanitation is usually <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> (and open urination but this is of less concern) with the associated serious public health issues.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On-site_sanitation">On-site sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: On-site sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Onsite_sewage_facility" title="Onsite sewage facility">Onsite sewage facility</a></div>
<p>On-site sanitation, also called decentralised sanitation, is a system where the treatment of excreta or <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> takes place at the same location where it is generated. Examples are pit <a href="/wiki/Latrine" title="Latrine">latrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imhoff_tank" title="Imhoff tank">Imhoff tanks</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Septic_drain_field" title="Septic drain field">drainfield</a> combination is the oldest and most common type of on-site sewage facility in the U.S., although newer <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biofilter" title="Biofilter">biofilter</a> units exist which represent scaled down versions of municipal <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> plants.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sustainable sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</a> considers the entire "sanitation value chain", from the experience of the user, <a href="/wiki/Human_excreta" class="mw-redirect" title="Human excreta">excreta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> collection methods, transportation or conveyance of waste, treatment, and reuse or disposal.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> The term is widely used since about 2009. In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Sanitation_Alliance" title="Sustainable Sanitation Alliance">Sustainable Sanitation Alliance</a> had defined five sustainability criteria to compare the sustainability of sanitation systems. In order to be sustainable, a sanitation system has to be not only (i) economically viable, (ii) socially acceptable, and (iii) technically and (iv) institutionally appropriate, it should also (v) protect the environment and the natural resources.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other terms used to describe certain types of sanitation include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Community-based sanitation, abbreviated as CBS (often related to decentralised wastewater treatment or DEWATS)</li>
<li>Container-based sanitation, also abbreviated as CBS (this is related to mobile sanitation and <a href="/wiki/Portable_toilet" title="Portable toilet">portable toilets</a>)</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_aspects">Health aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Health aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Child_mortality" title="Child mortality">Child mortality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">Infant mortality</a></div>
<p>For any social and economic development, adequate sanitation in conjunction with good hygiene and safe water are essential to good health. Lack of proper sanitation causes diseases. Most of the diseases resulting from sanitation have a direct relation to poverty. The lack of clean water and poor sanitation causes many diseases and the spread of diseases. It is estimated that inadequate sanitation is responsible for 4.0 percent of deaths and 5.7 percent of disease burden worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lack of sanitation is a serious issue that is affecting most developing countries and countries in transition. The importance of the isolation of excreta and waste lies in an effort to prevent diseases which can be <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(medicine)" title="Transmission (medicine)">transmitted</a> through human waste, which afflict both <a href="/wiki/Developed_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> to differing degrees.</p>
<p>It is estimated that up to 5 million people die each year from preventable <a href="/wiki/Water-borne_diseases" class="mw-redirect" title="Water-borne diseases">waterborne diseases</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> as a result of inadequate sanitation and hygiene practices. The effects of sanitation has impacted the society of people throughout history.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sanitation is a necessity for a healthy life.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diarrhea">Diarrhea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diarrhea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">Diarrhea</a></div>
<p>Diarrhea plays a significant role: Deaths resulting from diarrhea are estimated to be between 1.6 and 2.5 million deaths every year.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Most of the affected are young children below the ages of five.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Children suffering from diarrhea are more vulnerable to become underweight (due to <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunted growth</a>) which makes them more vulnerable to other diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Acute_respiratory_infection" class="mw-redirect" title="Acute respiratory infection">acute respiratory infections</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>.</p>
<p>Numerous studies have shown that improvements in drinking water and sanitation (<a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a>) lead to decreased risks of diarrhoea.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> Such improvements might include for example use of water filters, provision of high-quality <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">piped water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewer</a> connections.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">Open defecation</a> - or lack of sanitation - is a major factor in causing various diseases, most notably <a href="/wiki/Infectious_diarrhea" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious diarrhea">diarrhea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helminthiasis" title="Helminthiasis">intestinal worm infections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UN2015_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN2015-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For example, infectious diarrhea resulted in about 0.7 million deaths in children under five years old in 2011 and 250 million lost school days.<sup id="cite_ref-UN2015_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN2015-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It can also lead to <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunted growth</a> in children. Open defecation is a leading cause of diarrheal death; 2,000 children under the age of five die every day, one every 40 seconds, from <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malnutrition_and_stunting">Malnutrition and stunting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Malnutrition and stunting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition_in_children" title="Malnutrition in children">Malnutrition in children</a></div>
<p>The combination of direct and indirect deaths from <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a> caused by unsafe water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) practices is estimated by the World Health Organisation to lead to 860,000 deaths per year in children under five years of age.<sup>[6]</sup> The multiple interdependencies between malnutrition and infectious diseases make it very difficult to quantify the portion of malnutrition that is caused by infectious diseases which are in turn caused by unsafe WASH practices. Based on expert opinions and a literature survey, researchers at WHO arrived at the conclusion that approximately half of all cases of malnutrition (which often leads to stunting) in children under five is associated with repeated diarrhoea or intestinal worm infections as a result of unsafe water, inadequate sanitation or insufficient hygiene.<sup>[6]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_diseases_caused_by_lack_of_sanitation">List of diseases caused by lack of sanitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: List of diseases caused by lack of sanitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Relevant diseases and conditions caused by lack of sanitation and hygiene include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">Waterborne diseases</a>, which can contaminate drinking water</li>
<li>Diseases transmitted by the <a href="/wiki/Fecal-oral_route" class="mw-redirect" title="Fecal-oral route">fecal-oral route</a>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helminthiasis" title="Helminthiasis">Infections with intestinal helminths</a> (worms) - approximately two billion people are infected with <a href="/wiki/Soil-transmitted_helminths" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil-transmitted helminths">soil-transmitted helminths</a> worldwide; they are transmitted by eggs present in human faeces which in turn contaminate soil in areas where sanitation is poor.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">Stunted growth</a> in children</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a>, particularly in children</li>
</ul>
<p>The list of diseases that could be reduced with proper access to sanitation and hygiene practices is very long. For example, in India, 15 diseases have been listed which could be stamped out by improving sanitation:<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anaemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaemia">Anaemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ascariasis" title="Ascariasis">Ascariasis</a> (a type of intestinal worm infection)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Campylobacteriosis" title="Campylobacteriosis">Campylobacteriosis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">Cholera</a></li>
<li>Cyanobacteria toxins</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dengue" class="mw-redirect" title="Dengue">Dengue</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">Hepatitis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Japanese_encephalitis" title="Japanese encephalitis">Japanese encephalitis</a> (JE)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leptospirosis" title="Leptospirosis">Leptospirosis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">Malaria</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ringworm" class="mw-redirect" title="Ringworm">Ringworm</a> or Tinea (a type of intestinal worm infection)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scabies" title="Scabies">Scabies</a></li>

<p><a href="/wiki/Polio" class="mw-redirect" title="Polio">Polio</a> is another disease which is related to improper sanitation and hygiene.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indicator_organisms">Indicator organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Indicator organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Waste_water_.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Waste water .jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Waste_water_.jpg/220px-Waste_water_.jpg" width="220" height="391" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Waste_water_.jpg/330px-Waste_water_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Waste_water_.jpg/440px-Waste_water_.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1836" data-file-height="3264" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When analysing environmental samples, various types of <a href="/wiki/Indicator_organism" title="Indicator organism">indicator organisms</a> are used to check for fecal pollution of the sample. Commonly used indicators for <a href="/wiki/Bacteriological_water_analysis" title="Bacteriological water analysis">bacteriological water analysis</a> include the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> (abbreviated as <i>E. coli)</i> and non-specific fecal <a href="/wiki/Coliforms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coliforms">coliforms</a><i>.</i> With regards to samples of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">sewage sludge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biosolids" title="Biosolids">biosolids</a> or fecal matter from <a href="/wiki/Dry_toilet" title="Dry toilet">dry toilets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helminth" class="mw-redirect" title="Helminth">helminth</a> eggs are a commonly used indicator. With helminth egg analysis, eggs are extracted from the sample after which a viability test is done to distinguish between viable and non viable eggs. The viable fraction of the helminth eggs in the sample is then counted.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wastewater_and_stormwater_management">Wastewater and stormwater management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Wastewater and stormwater management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">Wastewater</a></div>

<p>Wastewater management consists of collection, <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> (be it municipal or <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial wastewater">industrial wastewater</a>), disposal or reuse of treated wastewater. The latter is also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">water reclamation</a>.</p>
<p>The standard sanitation technology in urban areas is the collection of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> in gravity driven sewers, its treatment in <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">wastewater treatment plants</a> for <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a> or disposal in rivers, lakes or the sea. Sewers are either combined with <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a> or separated from them as <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary sewers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer" title="Combined sewer">Combined sewers</a> are usually found in the central, older parts or urban areas. Heavy <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">rainfall</a> and inadequate maintenance can lead to combined sewer overflows or <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">sanitary sewer overflows</a>, i.e., more or less diluted raw <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> being discharged into the environment. Industries often discharge wastewater into municipal sewers, which can complicate wastewater treatment unless industries pre-treat their discharges.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The high investment cost of conventional wastewater collection systems are difficult to afford for many <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. Some countries therefore promote alternative wastewater collection systems such as condominial sewerage, which uses pipes with smaller diameters at lower trench depth with different network layouts from conventional sewerage.</p>
<p>In developed countries centralised treatment of municipal wastewater is now quite widespread. In <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> most wastewater is still discharged untreated into the environment. For example, in Latin America only about 15% of collected sewage is being treated (see <a href="/wiki/Water_and_sanitation_in_Latin_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Water and sanitation in Latin America">water and sanitation in Latin America</a>).</p>
<p>In many suburban and rural areas households are not connected to sewers, and they rather use <a href="/wiki/Onsite_sewage_facility" title="Onsite sewage facility">on-site sewage facilities</a> or decentralised treatment if they are connected to piped water and use flush toilets. They discharge their wastewater into <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a> or other types of on-site sanitation systems (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sequencing_batch_reactor" title="Sequencing batch reactor">Sequencing batch reactors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">constructed wetlands</a>). On-site systems include <a href="/wiki/Drain_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Drain field">drain fields</a>, which require significant area of land. This makes septic systems unsuitable for most cities.</p>
<p>The reuse of untreated or partially treated wastewater in <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigated agriculture</a> is common in developing countries. The reuse of treated wastewater in landscaping, especially on golf courses, irrigated agriculture and for industrial use is becoming increasingly widespread.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_waste_disposal">Solid waste disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Solid waste disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Disposal of <a href="/wiki/Solid_waste" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid waste">solid waste</a> is most commonly conducted in <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>, but incineration, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">composting</a> and conversion to <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> are also avenues. In the case of landfills, <a href="/wiki/Advanced_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced countries">advanced countries</a> typically have rigid protocols for <a href="/wiki/Daily_cover" title="Daily cover">daily cover</a> with topsoil, where <a href="/wiki/Underdeveloped_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Underdeveloped countries">underdeveloped countries</a> customarily rely upon less stringent protocols.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The importance of daily cover lies in the reduction of vector contact and spreading of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Daily cover also minimises odor emissions and reduces windblown litter. Likewise, developed countries typically have requirements for perimeter sealing of the landfill with clay-type soils to minimize migration of <a href="/wiki/Leachate" title="Leachate">leachate</a> that could contaminate <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> (and hence jeopardize some <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> supplies).</p>
<p>For incineration options, the release of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" class="mw-redirect" title="Air pollutant">air pollutants</a>, including certain <a href="/wiki/Toxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic">toxic</a> components is an attendant adverse outcome. Recycling and biofuel conversion are the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> options that generally have superior lifecycle costs, particularly when total <a href="/wiki/Ecological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological">ecological</a> consequences are considered.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Composting value will ultimately be limited by the market demand for compost product.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_industry">Food industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Food industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Canteen_kitchen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Canteen_kitchen.jpg/220px-Canteen_kitchen.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Canteen_kitchen.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="260" data-file-height="180" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Canteen_kitchen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Modern <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> food preparation area.</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">food safety</a>.</div>
<p>Sanitation within the food industry means the adequate treatment of food-contact surfaces by a process that is effective in destroying vegetative cells of <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> significance, and in substantially reducing numbers of other undesirable microorganisms, but without adversely affecting the food or its safety for the consumer (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>, 21CFR110, USA). <a href="/wiki/Sanitation_Standard_Operating_Procedures" title="Sanitation Standard Operating Procedures">Sanitation Standard Operating Procedures</a> are mandatory for food industries in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, which are regulated by 9 CFR part 416 in conjunction with 21 CFR part 178.1010. Similarly, in Japan, food hygiene has to be achieved through compliance with food sanitation law.<sup id="cite_ref-fslj_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fslj-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the food and <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceutical</a> industries, the term "sanitary equipment" means equipment that is fully cleanable using <a href="/wiki/Clean-in-place" title="Clean-in-place">clean-in-place</a> (CIP) and sterilization-in-place (SIP) procedures: that is fully drainable from cleaning solutions and other <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>. The design should have a minimum amount of deadleg, or areas where the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> during cleaning is insufficient to remove product deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In general, to improve cleanability, this equipment is made from <a href="/wiki/Stainless_Steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Stainless Steel">Stainless Steel</a> 316L, (an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> containing small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>). The surface is usually <a href="/wiki/Electropolish" class="mw-redirect" title="Electropolish">electropolished</a> to an effective surface roughness of less than 0.5 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a> to reduce the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> adhesion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_countries">Developing countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Developing countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Globally 2.6 billion people remain without access to any kind of improved sanitation, and sanitation-related diseases and poor hygienic conditions cause approximately 2.2 million annual deaths, mostly of children under the age of five years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Millennium_Development_Goals_and_Sustainable_Development_Goals">Millennium Development Goals and Sustainable Development Goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Millennium Development Goals and Sustainable Development Goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, during the Millennium Summit in New York in 2000 and the 2002 <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_Sustainable_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Summit on Sustainable Development">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> in Johannesburg, developed the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs) aimed at poverty eradication and sustainable development. The specific goal for the year 2015 is to reduce by half the number of people who had no access to <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable water</a> and sanitation in the baseline year of 1990. In the year 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> replaced the Millennium Development Goals.</p>
<p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a> (JMP) of the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a> (WHO) and of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Children%27s_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Children's Fund">United Nations Children's Fund</a> (UNICEF) reported that the number of people lacking basic sanitation services rose from 2.1 billion in 2001 to 2.6 billion by 2004. As the JMP and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> (UNDP) Human Development Report in 2006 has shown, progress meeting the MDG sanitation target is too slow, with a large gap between the target coverage and the current reality.</p>
<p>There are numerous reasons for this gap. A major one is that sanitation is rarely given political attention received by other topics despite its key importance. Sanitation is not high on the international development agenda, and projects such as those relating to <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> projects are emphasised.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> declared 2008 "The <a href="/wiki/International_Year_of_Sanitation" title="International Year of Sanitation">International Year of Sanitation</a>", in recognition of the slow progress being made towards the MDGs sanitation target.<sup id="cite_ref-Kurian_and_McCarney_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kurian_and_McCarney-27">[27]</a></sup> The year aimed to develop awareness and action to meet the target. Particular concerns are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Removing the stigma around sanitation, so that the importance of sanitation can be more easily and publicly discussed.</li>
<li>Highlighting the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">poverty reduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> and other benefits that flow from better hygiene, household sanitation arrangements and wastewater treatment.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/WHO" class="mw-redirect" title="WHO">WHO</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> has defined <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">improved sanitation</a> as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flush_toilet" title="Flush toilet">Flush toilet</a> or flush / pour-flush to a <a href="/wiki/Pit_toilet" class="mw-redirect" title="Pit toilet">pit latrine</a> with are connected to either a <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewer</a> system or a <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic system</a></li>
<li>Ventilated improved pit latrine (VIP)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">Pit latrine</a> with slab</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Composting_toilet" title="Composting toilet">Composting toilet</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The JMP publishes a report of updated estimates every two years on the use of various types of drinking-water sources and sanitation facilities at the national, regional and global levels. For example, in March 2012, the JMP released an update.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> According to the definition above, 1.8 billion more people used improved sanitation in 2010 than in 1990, bringing the percentage of people using improved sanitation to 63% globally. However, the world did not achieve the sanitation target of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goal" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennium Development Goal">Millennium Development Goals</a> in 2015. 2.5 billion lack improved sanitation.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> According to the JMP, if current trends continue, in 2015 2.4 billion people will lack access to improved sanitation facilities. 15 per cent of the population still practise <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a>, defined as defecation in fields, forests, bushes, bodies of water or other open spaces. This represents 1.1 billion people. Though the proportion of people practising <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> is decreasing, the absolute number has remained at over one billion for several years, due to population growth.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>This outcome presents substantial public health risks as the waste could contaminate <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> and cause life-threatening forms of <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> to infants. Most cities can neither afford a sewage collection system, nor a sewage treatment system.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Improved sanitation, including <a href="/wiki/Hand_washing" title="Hand washing">hand washing</a> and water purification, could save the lives of 1.5 million children who die from diarrheal diseases each year.<sup id="cite_ref-who.int_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Various_initiatives">Various initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Various initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/Bill_%26_Melinda_Gates_Foundation" title="Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation">Bill &amp; Melinda Gates Foundation</a> launched the Reinvent the Toilet Challenge to promote safer, more effective ways to treat human waste. The program is aimed at developing technologies that might help bridge the global sanitation gap.</p>

<p>Research from the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> suggests that sanitation and hygiene promotion needs to be better "mainstreamed" in <a href="/wiki/Development_cooperation" class="mw-redirect" title="Development cooperation">development cooperation</a>, if the MDG on sanitation is to be met. At present, promotion of sanitation and hygiene is mainly carried out through water institutions. The research argues that many institutions that should carry out activities to develop better sanitation and hygiene in developing countries. For example, educational institutions can teach on <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a>, and health institutions can dedicate resources to preventative works (to avoid, for example, outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-odisanitation_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odisanitation-34">[34]</a></sup> There are also civil society organisations providing some of the necessary infrastructure where national governments cannot do that on their own.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sanitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Toilet#History" title="Toilet">Toilet Â§&#160;History</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_water_supply_and_sanitation" title="History of water supply and sanitation">History of water supply and sanitation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_waste_management" title="History of waste management">History of waste management</a></div>
<p>The earliest evidence of urban sanitation was seen in <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harappa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a>, and the recently discovered <a href="/wiki/Rakhigarhi" title="Rakhigarhi">Rakhigarhi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley civilization">Indus Valley civilization</a>. This urban plan included the world's first urban sanitation systems. Within the city, individual homes or groups of homes obtained water from <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a>. From a room that appears to have been set aside for bathing, waste water was directed to covered drains, which lined the major streets.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> cities and <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villas</a> had elements of sanitation systems, delivering water in the streets of towns such as <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, and building stone and wooden drains to collect and remove <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> from populated areasâ€”see for instance the <a href="/wiki/Cloaca_Maxima" title="Cloaca Maxima">Cloaca Maxima</a> into the <a href="/wiki/River_Tiber" class="mw-redirect" title="River Tiber">River Tiber</a> in Rome. But there is little record of other sanitation in most of Europe until the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>. Unsanitary conditions and overcrowding were widespread throughout <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, resulting periodically in cataclysmic <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemics</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Plague_of_Justinian" title="Plague of Justinian">Plague of Justinian</a> (541-42) and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> (1347â€“1351), which killed tens of millions of people and radically altered societies.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Very high infant and child mortality prevailed in Europe throughout <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times, due not only to deficiencies in sanitation but to an insufficient food supply for a population which had expanded faster than <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This was further complicated by frequent <a href="/wiki/Warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Warfare">warfare</a> and exploitation of civilians by autocratic rulers.</p>
