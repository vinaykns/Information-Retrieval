			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Food chain</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Food_chain_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Food chain (disambiguation)">Food chain (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Food_chain.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Food_chain.png/160px-Food_chain.png" width="160" height="455" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Food_chain.png/240px-Food_chain.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Food_chain.png/320px-Food_chain.png 2x" data-file-width="786" data-file-height="2237" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Food_chain.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Food chain in a Swedish lake. <a href="/wiki/Osprey" title="Osprey">Osprey</a> feed on <a href="/wiki/Northern_pike" title="Northern pike">northern pike</a>, which in turn feed on <a href="/wiki/Perch" title="Perch">perch</a> which eat <a href="/wiki/Common_Bleak" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Bleak">bleak</a> that feed on freshwater <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>food chain</b> is a linear network of links in a <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food web</a> starting from <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">producer organisms</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> which use radiation from the sun to make their food) and ending at <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predator</a> species (like <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bears</a> or <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whales</a>), <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">detritivores</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Woodlice" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodlice">woodlice</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposer</a> species (such as <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>). A food chain also shows how the organisms are related with each other by the food they eat. Each level of a food chain represents a different <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>. A food chain differs from a food web, because the complex network of <a href="/wiki/Polyphagous" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyphagous">different animals</a>' feeding relations are aggregated and the chain only follows a direct, linear pathway of <a href="/wiki/Monophagous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monophagous">one animal at a time</a>. A common metric used to quantify food web trophic structure is food chain length. In its simplest form, the length of a chain is the number of links between a trophic consumer and the base of the web and the mean chain length of an entire web is the arithmetic average of the lengths of all chains in a food web.<sup id="cite_ref-Briand87_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Briand87-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PostPace_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PostPace-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Food chains were first introduced by the African-Arab scientist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Al-Jahiz" title="Al-Jahiz">Al-Jahiz</a> in the 9th century and later popularized in a book published in 1927 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton</a>, which also introduced the food web concept.<sup id="cite_ref-Elton27_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elton27-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Allesina08_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allesina08-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Egerton07_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egerton07-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_chain_length">Food chain length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_chain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Food chain length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg/220px-Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg/330px-Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg/440px-Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg 2x" data-file-width="565" data-file-height="440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chesapeake_Waterbird_Food_Web.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food web</a> of waterbirds from <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> is a network of food chains</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:215px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Food chains are directional paths of trophic energy or, equivalently, sequences of links that start with basal species, such as producers or fine organic matter, and end with consumer organisms. <sup id="cite_ref-Martinez91_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez91-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:370</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The food chain's length is a <a href="/wiki/Continuous_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous variable">continuous variable</a> that provides a measure of the passage of energy and an index of <a href="/wiki/Biological_organisation" title="Biological organisation">ecological structure</a> that increases in value counting progressively through the linkages in a linear fashion from the lowest to the highest trophic (feeding) levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Vander99_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vander99-7">[7]</a></sup> Food chains are often used in ecological modeling (such as a three species food chain). They are simplified abstractions of real food webs, but complex in their dynamics and mathematical implications.<sup id="cite_ref-PostConners_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PostConners-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ecologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecologist">Ecologists</a> have formulated and tested hypotheses regarding the nature of ecological patterns associated with food chain length, such as increasing length increasing with ecosystem size, reduction of energy at each successive level, or the proposition that long food chain lengths are unstable.<sup id="cite_ref-Vander99_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vander99-7">[7]</a></sup> Food chain studies have an important role in <a href="/wiki/Ecotoxicology" title="Ecotoxicology">ecotoxicology studies</a> tracing the pathways and <a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">biomagnification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_contaminant" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental contaminant">environmental contaminants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Odum05_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Odum05-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">Producers</a>, such as plants, are organisms that utilize solar or chemical energy to synthesize starch. All food chains must start with a producer. In the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep sea</a>, food chains centered on <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cold_seep" title="Cold seep">cold seeps</a> exist in the absence of sunlight. <a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">Chemosynthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> use <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from hydrothermal vents and cold seeps as an energy source (just as plants use sunlight) to produce carbohydrates; they form the base of the food chain. <a href="/wiki/Consumers_(food_chain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumers (food chain)">Consumers</a> are organisms that eat other organisms. All organisms in a food chain, except the first organism, are consumers.</p>
