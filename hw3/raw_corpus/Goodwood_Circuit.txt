<p><b>Goodwood Circuit</b> is a historic venue for both two- and four-wheeled motorsport in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The 3.8 kilometres (2.4&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> circuit is situated near <a href="/wiki/Chichester" title="Chichester">Chichester</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>, close to the south coast of England, on the estate of <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_House" title="Goodwood House">Goodwood House</a>, and completely encircles <a href="/wiki/Chichester/Goodwood_Airport" title="Chichester/Goodwood Airport">Chichester/Goodwood Airport</a>. This is the racing circuit dating from 1948, not to be confused with the separate <a href="/wiki/Hillclimbing" title="Hillclimbing">hillclimb</a> course located at Goodwood House and first used in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_1948.E2.80.931966">History 1948–1966</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History 1948–1966">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The racing circuit began life as the perimeter track of <a href="/wiki/RAF_Westhampnett" title="RAF Westhampnett">RAF Westhampnett</a> airfield, which was constructed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as a relief airfield for <a href="/wiki/RAF_Tangmere" title="RAF Tangmere">RAF Tangmere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">[3]</a></sup> The first race meeting took place on 18 September 1948, organised by the <a href="/wiki/British_Automobile_Racing_Club" title="British Automobile Racing Club">Junior Car Club</a> and sanctioned by the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Gordon-Lennox,_9th_Duke_of_Richmond" title="Frederick Gordon-Lennox, 9th Duke of Richmond">Duke of Richmond and Gordon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The winner of the first race was P. de F. C. Pycroft, in his 2,664 c.c. Pycroft-Jaguar, at 66.42 m.p.h.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Moss" title="Stirling Moss">Stirling Moss</a> won the 500cc race (later to become <a href="/wiki/Formula_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Formula 3">Formula 3</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">[3]</a></sup> followed by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Brandon" title="Eric Brandon">Eric Brandon</a> and "Curly" Dryden, all in <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">Coopers</a>.</p>

<p>Goodwood became famous for its <a href="/wiki/Glover_Trophy" title="Glover Trophy">Glover Trophy</a> non-championship <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> race, Goodwood Nine Hours sports car endurance races run in 1952, 1953 and 1955, and the <a href="/wiki/RAC_Tourist_Trophy" title="RAC Tourist Trophy">Tourist Trophy</a> sports car race, run here 1958-1964. The cars that raced in those events can be seen recreating (in shorter form) the endurance races at the Goodwood Revival each year in the Sussex trophy and the Royal Automobile Club Tourist Trophy (RAC TT).</p>

<p>Goodwood has, over the years, played host to many famous drivers: <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hawthorn" title="Mike Hawthorn">Mike Hawthorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Hill" title="Graham Hill">Graham Hill</a> had their first single seat races there, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penske" title="Roger Penske">Roger Penske</a> visited in 1963, and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Clark" title="Jim Clark">Jim Clark</a> and Jack Sears competed in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">[3]</a></sup> The accident that ended <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Moss" title="Stirling Moss">Stirling Moss</a>'s International career happened at St. Mary's Corner in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Donald_Campbell" title="Donald Campbell">Donald Campbell</a> demonstrated his <a href="/wiki/Bluebird_CN7" class="mw-redirect" title="Bluebird CN7">Bluebird CN7</a> <a href="/wiki/Land_Speed_Record" class="mw-redirect" title="Land Speed Record">Land Speed Record</a> car at Goodwood in July 1960 at its initial public launch, and again in July 1962, before being shipped to Australia—where it finally broke the record in 1964. The car was a 30-foot-long (9.1&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Siddeley" title="Bristol Siddeley">Bristol Siddeley</a> turbine-powered 4,500&#160;hp (3,400&#160;kW) streamliner, with a theoretical top speed of 450 to 500 miles per hour (720 to 800&#160;km/h). The laps of Goodwood were effectively at "tick-over" speed, because the car had only four degrees of steering lock, with a maximum of 100&#160;mph (160&#160;km/h) on the straight on one lap.</p>
<p>Goodwood saw its last race meeting for over 30 years in 1966, because the owners did not want to modify the track with <a href="/wiki/Chicane" title="Chicane">chicanes</a> to control the increased speeds of modern racing cars. The last event was a club meeting organised by the <a href="/wiki/British_Automobile_Racing_Club" title="British Automobile Racing Club">British Automobile Racing Club</a> on 2 July 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The circuit claimed the life of <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a>-founder <a href="/wiki/Bruce_McLaren" title="Bruce McLaren">Bruce McLaren</a> in a testing accident on 2 June 1970.</p>
<p>The accident happened on Lavant Straight, when a rear bodywork failure on McLaren's M8D car caused it to spin and leave the track, hitting a structure on the infield at over 100&#160;mph while travelling sideways.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Goodwood is noted for its annual <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_Festival_of_Speed" title="Goodwood Festival of Speed">Festival of Speed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_Revival" title="Goodwood Revival">Goodwood Revival</a> events.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goodwood_Festival_of_Speed">Goodwood Festival of Speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Goodwood Festival of Speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_Festival_of_Speed" title="Goodwood Festival of Speed">Goodwood Festival of Speed</a></div>
<p>The Goodwood Festival of Speed is an annual hill climb, held in late June or early July not on the circuit, but in the nearby grounds of Goodwood House. It features historic and modern motor-racing vehicles. In 2010, the event had over 176,000 visitors over four days.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goodwood_Revival">Goodwood Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Goodwood Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Goodwood_Revival" title="Goodwood Revival">Goodwood Revival</a></div>
<p>Following the success of the Festival of Speed hill climb, racing returned to the Goodwood circuit in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">[3]</a></sup> The Goodwood Revival is a three-day festival held each September for the types of cars and motorcycles that would have competed during the circuit's original period, 1948–1966. Historic aircraft help to complete the vintage feel. In 2008, a crowd of 68,000 people attended the event on the main Sunday - 9,000 more than in 2007.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The track is now used for classic races, track days, and try-out days. Nearly everyone dresses up in vintage outfit from mods and rockers to racing drivers and just smart period clothes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_events">Other events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodwood_Circuit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Rally" title="Mongol Rally">Mongol Rally</a>, a charity fundraising car rally to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, moved its starting point from <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park, London</a> to Goodwood. Entrants are on show to the public in the paddock before beginning the rally with a parade lap of the circuit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The National Finals of the <a href="/wiki/Greenpower" title="Greenpower">Greenpower</a> schools electric car racing challenge takes place at Goodwood each year. The Greenpower challenge is a nationwide series of electric vehicle endurance races for schools, who build their own 24 volt single-seater racing cars.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There is also a corporate version of the race, featuring teams like Lola, <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Land_Rover" title="Jaguar Land Rover">Jaguar Land Rover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Motors" class="mw-redirect" title="Bentley Motors">Bentley Motors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prodrive" title="Prodrive">Prodrive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 'Breakfast Club' was introduced in March 2006. This is a semi regular free to enter, and open-to-all monthly gathering of drivers and riders who come to view each other's cars, bikes etc. Each meeting is themed with striking examples of the days theme paraded on the start finish straight.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The circuit also hosted the <a href="/wiki/1982_UCI_Road_World_Championships" title="1982 UCI Road World Championships">1982 UCI Road World Championships</a> for cycle racing, notable for the men's professional race, which saw a late breakaway by the American rider <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Boyer" title="Jacques Boyer">Jacques Boyer</a> being closed down by a pack led by Boyer's team-mate (and future triple <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> winner and double Road World Champion) <a href="/wiki/Greg_LeMond" title="Greg LeMond">Greg LeMond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The circuit was a filming location in the series <i><a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
