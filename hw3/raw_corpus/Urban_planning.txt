			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Urban planning</h1>

<p><b>Urban planning</b> is a technical and political process concerned with the development and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">use of land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planning_permission" title="Planning permission">planning permission</a>, protection and use of the environment, <a href="/wiki/Public_welfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Public welfare">public welfare</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Urban_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban environment">urban environment</a>, including air, water, and the <a href="/wiki/Urban_infrastructure" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban infrastructure">infrastructure</a> passing into and out of <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communications</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Distribution_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Distribution network">distribution networks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Urban planning is also referred to as <b>urban and regional planning</b>, <b>regional planning</b>, <b>town planning</b>, <b>city planning</b>, <b>rural planning</b> or some combination in various areas worldwide. It takes many forms and it can share perspectives and practices with <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urban planning guides orderly development in urban, <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a>. Although predominantly concerned with the planning of <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">settlements</a> and communities, urban planning is also responsible for the planning and development of <a href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">water use</a> and resources, rural and agricultural land, parks and conserving areas of natural environmental significance. <a href="/wiki/Urban_planner" title="Urban planner">Practitioners of urban planning</a> are concerned with research and analysis, strategic thinking, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, urban design, <a href="/wiki/Public_consultation" title="Public consultation">public consultation</a>, policy recommendations, implementation and management.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urban planners work with the cognate fields of architecture, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architecture" title="Landscape architecture">landscape architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a> to achieve strategic, policy and sustainability goals. Early urban planners were often members of these cognate fields. Today urban planning is a separate, independent professional discipline. The discipline is the broader category that includes different sub-fields such as <a href="/wiki/Land-use_planning" title="Land-use planning">land-use planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_planning" title="Environmental planning">environmental planning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transportation_planning" title="Transportation planning">transportation planning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_urban_planning" title="History of urban planning">History of urban planning</a></div>
<p>There is evidence of urban planning and designed communities dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley Civilization">Indus Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minoan_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Minoan Civilization">Minoan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> civilizations in the third millennium BCE. Archeologists studying the ruins of cities in these areas find paved streets that were laid out at right angles in a grid pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-Davreu1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davreu1-5">[5]</a></sup> The idea of a planned out urban area evolved as different civilizations adopted it. Beginning in the 8th century BCE, Greek city states were primarily centered on orthogonal (or grid-like) plans.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a>, inspired by the Greeks, also used orthogonal plans for their cities. City planning in the Roman world was developed for military defense and public convenience. The spread of the Roman Empire subsequently spread the ideas of urban planning. As the Roman Empire declined, these ideas slowly disappeared. However, many cities in Europe still held onto the planned Roman city center. Cities in Europe from the 9th to 14th centuries, often grew organically and sometimes chaotically. But many hundreds of new towns were newly built according to preconceived plans, and many others were enlarged with newly planned extensions. Most of these were realized from the 12th to 14th centuries, with a peak-period at the end of the 13th.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> From the 15th century on, much more is recorded of urban design and the people that were involved. In this period, theoretical treatises on architecture and urban planning start to appear in which theoretical questions are addressed and designs of towns and cities are described and depicted. During the Enlightenment period, several European rulers ambitiously attempted to redesign capital cities. During the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Second French Republic">Second French Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baron_Georges-Eug%C3%A8ne_Haussmann" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron Georges-Eugène Haussmann">Baron Georges-Eugène Haussmann</a>, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>, redesigned the city of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> into a more modern capital, with long, straight, wide boulevards.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Planning and architecture went through a paradigm shift at the turn of the 20th century. The industrialized cities of the 19th century grew at a tremendous rate. The pace and style of this industrial construction was largely dictated by the concerns of private business. The evils of urban life for the <a href="/wiki/Working_poor" title="Working poor">working poor</a> were becoming increasingly evident as a matter for public concern. The <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a> style of government management of the economy, in fashion for most of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, was starting to give way to a <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">New Liberalism</a> that championed intervention on the part of the poor and disadvantaged. Around 1900, theorists began developing urban planning models to mitigate the consequences of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial age">industrial age</a>, by providing citizens, especially factory workers, with healthier environments.</p>
<p>Urban planning started to become professionalized during this time. The <a href="/wiki/Town_and_Country_Planning_Association" title="Town and Country Planning Association">Town and Country Planning Association</a> was founded in 1899 and the first academic course on urban planning was offered by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Liverpool" title="University of Liverpool">University of Liverpool</a> in 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 1920s, the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a> and uniformity began to surface in urban planning, and lasted until the 1970s. Many planners started to believe that the ideas of modernism in urban planning led to higher crime rates and social problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_Morris_1997_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Morris_1997-10">[10]</a></sup> Urban planners now focus more on individualism and diversity in urban centers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Theories_of_urban_planning" title="Theories of urban planning">Theories of urban planning</a></div>
<p>Planning theory is the body of scientific concepts, definitions, behavioral relationships, and assumptions that define the body of knowledge of urban planning. There are eight procedural theories of planning that remain the principal theories of planning procedure today: the rational-comprehensive approach, the incremental approach, the transactive approach, the communicative approach, the advocacy approach, the equity approach, the radical approach, and the humanist or phenomenological approach.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_aspects">Technical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technical aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Technical_aspects_of_urban_planning" title="Technical aspects of urban planning">Technical aspects of urban planning</a></div>
<p>Technical aspects of urban planning involve the applying scientific, technical processes, considerations and features that are involved in planning for <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. Urban planning includes techniques such as: predicting population growth, zoning, geographic mapping and analysis, analyzing park space, surveying the water supply, identifying transportation patterns, recognizing food supply demands, allocating healthcare and social services, and analyzing the impact of land use.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_planners">Urban planners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Urban planners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Urban_planner" title="Urban planner">Urban planner</a></div>
<p>An urban planner is a professional who works in the field of urban planning for the purpose of optimizing the effectiveness of a community's land use and infrastructure. They formulate plans for the development and management of urban and suburban areas, typically analyzing land use compatibility as well as economic, environmental and social trends. In developing the plan for a community (whether commercial, residential, agricultural, natural or recreational), urban planners must also consider a wide array of issues such as sustainability, air pollution, traffic congestion, crime, land values, legislation and <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> codes.</p>
<p>The importance of the urban planner is increasing throughout the 21st century, as modern society begins to face issues of increased population growth, climate change and unsustainable development. An urban planner could be considered a <a href="/wiki/Green-collar_worker" title="Green-collar worker">green collar</a> professional.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
