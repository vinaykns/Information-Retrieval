			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Land ethic</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>A <b>land ethic</b> is a philosophy or theoretical framework about how, ethically, humans should regard the land. The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> (1887–1948) in his <i><a href="/wiki/A_Sand_County_Almanac" title="A Sand County Almanac">A Sand County Almanac</a></i> (1949), a classic text of the environmental movement. There he argues that there is a critical need for a "new ethic," an "ethic dealing with human's relation to land and to the animals and plants which grow upon it".<sup id="cite_ref-Leopold1949_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leopold1949-1">[1]</a></sup> Leopold offers an ecologically-based land ethic that rejects strictly human-centered views of the environment and focuses on the preservation of healthy, self-renewing ecosystems. <i>A Sand County Almanac</i> was the first systematic presentation of a holistic or ecocentric approach to the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although Leopold is credited with coining the term "land ethic," there are many philosophical theories that speak to how humans should treat the land. Some of the most prominent land ethics include those rooted in economics, utilitarianism, libertarianism, egalitarianism, and ecology.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics_based_land_ethic">Economics based land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Economics based land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a land ethic based wholly upon economic self-interest.<sup id="cite_ref-Leopold1949_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leopold1949-1">[1]</a></sup> Leopold sees two flaws in this type of ethic. First, he argues that most members of an ecosystem have no economic worth. For this reason, such an ethic can ignore or even eliminate these members when they are actually necessary for the health of the biotic community of the land. And second, it tends to relegate conservation necessary for healthy ecosystems to the Government and these tasks are too large and dispersed to be adequately addressed by such an institution. This ties directly into the context within which Leopold wrote <i>Sand County Almanac.</i></p>
<p>For example, when the <a href="/wiki/US_Forest_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="US Forest Service">US Forest Service</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>, the prevailing ethos was economic and <a href="/wiki/Utilitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a>. Leopold argued for an <a href="/wiki/Ecological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological">ecological</a> approach, becoming one of the first to popularize this term created by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chandler_Cowles" title="Henry Chandler Cowles">Henry Chandler Cowles</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> during his early 1900s research at the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Dunes" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiana Dunes">Indiana Dunes</a>. Conservation became the preferred term for the more anthropocentric model of <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">resource management</a>, while the writing of Leopold and his inspiration, <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a>, led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Utilitarian_based_land_ethic">Utilitarian based land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Utilitarian based land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Utilitarianism was first put forth by <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Bentham" title="Jeremy Bentham">Jeremy Bentham</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>. Though there are many varieties of <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a>, generally it is the view that a morally right action is an action that produces the maximum good for people.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Utilitarianism has often been used when deciding how to use land and it is closely connected with an economic based ethic. For example, it forms the foundation for industrial farming; as an increase in yield, which would increase the number of people able to receive goods from farmed land, is judged from this view to be a good action or approach. In fact, a common argument in favor of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a> is this it is a good practice because it increases the benefits for humans; benefits such as food abundance and a drop in food prices. However, a utilitarian based land ethic is different from a purely economic one as it could be used to justify the limiting of a person's rights to make profit. For example, in the case of the farmer planting crops on a slope, if the runoff of soil into the community creek led to the damage of several neighbor's properties, then the good of the individual farmer would be overridden by the damage caused to his neighbors. Thus, while a utilitarian based land ethic can be used to support economic activity, it can also be used to challenge this activity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Libertarian_based_land_ethic">Libertarian based land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Libertarian based land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another philosophical approach often used to guide actions when making (or not making) changes to the land is <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarianism</a>. Roughly, libertarianism is the ethical view that agents own themselves and have particular moral rights including the right to acquire property.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In a looser sense, libertarianism is commonly identified with the belief that each individual person has a right to a maximum amount of freedom or liberty when this freedom does not interfere with other people's freedom. A well known libertarian theorist is <a href="/wiki/John_Hospers" title="John Hospers">John Hospers</a>. For libertarians, property rights are natural rights. Thus, it would be acceptable for the above farmer to plant on a slope as long as this action does not limit the freedom of his or her neighbors.</p>
<p>This view is closely connected to utilitarianism. Libertarians often use utilitarian arguments to support their own arguments. For example, in 1968, <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Hardin" title="Garrett Hardin">Garrett Hardin</a> applied this philosophy to land issues when he argued that the only solution to the "<a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>" was to place soil and water resources into the hands of private citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Hardin then supplied utilitarian justifications to support his argument. However, you could argue that this possibly leaves a libertarian based land ethics open to the above critique lodged against economic based approaches. Even excepting this, the libertarian view has been challenged by the critique that people making self-interested decisions often cause large ecological disasters such as the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010-6">[6]</a></sup> Even so, libertarianism is a philosophical view commonly held within the United States and, especially, held by U.S. ranchers and farmers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Land_ethic#Libertarian_based" title="Talk:Land ethic">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Egalitarian_based_land_ethic">Egalitarian based land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Egalitarian based land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Egalitarian based land ethics are often developed as a response to libertarianism. This is because, while libertarianism ensures the maximum amount of human liberty, it does not require that people help others. In addition, it also leads to the uneven distribution of wealth. A well known egalitarian philosopher is <a href="/wiki/John_Rawls" title="John Rawls">John Rawls</a>. When focusing on land use, what this translates into is its uneven distribution and the uneven distribution of the fruits of that land.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010-6">[6]</a></sup> While both a utilitarian and libertarian based land ethic could conceivably rationalize this mal-distribution, an egalitarian approach typically favors equality whether that be equal entitlement to land and/or access to food.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, there is also the question of negative rights when holding to an egalitarian based ethic. In other words, if you recognize that a person has a right to something, then someone has the responsibility to supply this opportunity or item; whether that be an individual person or the government. Thus, an egalitarian based land ethic could provide a strong argument for the preservation of soil fertility and water because it links land and water with the right to food, with the growth of human populations, and the decline of soil and water resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_Paul_2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecologically_based_land_ethic">Ecologically based land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecologically based land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to economic, utilitarian, libertarian, and egalitarian based land ethics, there are also land ethics based upon the principle that the land (and the organisms that live off the land) has intrinsic value. These ethics are, roughly, coming out of an ecological or systems view. This position was first put forth by Ayers Brinser in Our Use of the Land, published in 1939. Brinser argued that white settlers brought with them "the seeds of a civilization which has grown by consuming the land, that is, a civilization which has used up the land in much the same way that a furnace burns coal.” Later, <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a>'s posthumously published <a href="/wiki/Sand_County_Almanac" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand County Almanac">Sand County Almanac</a> popularized this idea. Another examples is the <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> view which argues that human communities are built upon a foundation of the surrounding ecosystems or the biotic communities and that all life is of inherent worth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Similar to egalitarian based land ethics, the above land ethics were also developed as alternatives to utilitarian and libertarian based approaches. Leopold's ethic is currently one of the most popular ecological approaches. Other writers and theorists who hold this view include <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Berry" title="Wendell Berry">Wendell Berry</a> (b. 1934), <a href="/wiki/N._Scott_Momaday" title="N. Scott Momaday">N. Scott Momaday</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Baird_Callicott" title="J. Baird Callicott">J. Baird Callicott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_B._Thompson_(philosopher)" title="Paul B. Thompson (philosopher)">Paul B. Thompson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Kingsolver" title="Barbara Kingsolver">Barbara Kingsolver</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aldo_Leopold.27s_land_ethic">Aldo Leopold's land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aldo Leopold's land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his classic essay, "The Land Ethic," published posthumously in <a href="/wiki/A_Sand_County_Almanac" title="A Sand County Almanac">A Sand County Almanac</a>, Leopold proposes that the next step in the evolution of ethics is the expansion of ethics to include nonhuman members of the <a href="/wiki/Biotic_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Biotic community">biotic community</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Leopold1949_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leopold1949-1">[1]</a></sup> collectively referred to as "the land." Leopold states the basic principle of his land ethic as: "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. It is wrong when it tends otherwise."</p>
<p>He also describes it in this way: "The land ethic simply enlarges the boundaries of the community to include soils, waters, plants, and animals, or collectively: the land . . . [A] land ethic changes the role of <i>Homo sapiens</i> from conqueror of the land-community to plain member and citizen of it. It implies respect for his fellow-members, and also respect for the community as such."</p>
<p>Leopold was a naturalist, not a philosopher, and there is much debate about what exactly Leopold's land ethic asserts and how he argues for it. At its core, the land ethics claims (1) that humans should view themselves as plain members and citizens of biotic communities, not as "conquerors" of the land; (2) that we should extend ethical consideration to ecological wholes ("soils, waters, plants, and animals"), (3) that our primary ethical concern should not be with individual plants or animals, but with the healthy functioning of whole biotic communities, and (4) that the "summary moral maxim" of ecological ethics is that we should seek to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. Beyond this, scholars disagree about the extent to which Leopold rejected traditional human-centered approaches to the environment and how literally he intended his basic moral maxim to be applied. They also debate whether Leopold based his land ethic primarily on human-centered interests, as many passages in <i>A Sand County Almanac</i> suggest, or whether he placed significant weight on the intrinsic value of nature. One prominent student of Leopold, <a href="/wiki/J._Baird_Callicott" title="J. Baird Callicott">J. Baird Callicott</a>, has suggested that Leopold grounded his land ethics on various scientific claims, including a Darwinian view of ethics as rooted in special affections for kith and kin, a Copernican view of humans as plain members of nature and the cosmos, and the finding of modern ecology that ecosystems are complex, interrelated wholes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, this interpretation has recently been challenged by <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Millstein" title="Roberta Millstein">Roberta Millstein</a>, who has offered evidence that Darwin's influence on Leopold was not related to Darwin's views about moral sentiments, but rather to Darwin's views about interdependence in the struggle for existence.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions_of_Leopold.27s_land_ethic">Attractions of Leopold's land ethic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Attractions of Leopold's land ethic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leopold's ecocentric land ethic is popular today with mainstream environmentalists for a number of reasons. Unlike more radical environmental approaches, such as <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biocentrism_(ethics)" title="Biocentrism (ethics)">biocentrism</a>, it does not require huge sacrifices of human interests. Leopold does not, for example, believe that humans should stop eating or hunting or experimenting on animals. Nor does he call for a massive reduction in human population, or for permitting humans to interfere with nature only to satisfy vital human needs (regardless of economic or other human costs). As an environmental ethic, Leopold's land ethic is a comparatively moderate view that seeks to strike a balance between human interests and a healthy and biotically diverse natural environment. Many of the things mainstream environmentalists favor--preference for native plants and animals over invasive species, hunting or selective culling to control overpopulated species that are damaging to the environment, and a focus on preserving healthy, self-regenerating natural ecosystems both for human benefit and for their own intrinsic value--jibe with Leopold's ecocentric land ethic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some critics fault Leopold for lack of clarity in spelling out exactly what the land ethic is and its specific implications for how humans should think about the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is clear that Leopold did not intend his basic normative principle ("A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community") to be regarded as an ethical absolute. Thus construed, it would prohibit clearing land to build homes, schools, or farms, and generally require a "hands-off" approach to nature that Leopold plainly did not favor. Presumably, therefore, his maxim should be seen as a general guideline for valuing natural ecosystems and striving to achieve what he terms a sustainable state of "harmony between men and land." But this is vague and, according to some critics, not terribly helpful.</p>
<p>A second common criticism of Leopold is that he fails to state clearly why we should adopt the land ethic.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He often cites examples of environmental damage (e.g., soil erosion, pollution, and deforestation) that result from traditional human-centered, "conqueror" attitudes towards nature. But it is unclear why such examples support the land ethic specifically, as opposed to biocentricism or some other nature-friendly environmental ethic. Leopold also frequently appeals to modern ecology, evolutionary theory, and other scientific discoveries to support his land ethic. Some critics have suggested that such appeals may involve an illicit move from facts to values.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At a minimum, such critics claim, more should be said about the normative basis of Leopold's land ethic.</p>
<p>Other critics object to Leopold's ecological holism. According to animal rights advocate, Tom Regan, Leopold's land ethic condones sacrificing the good of individual animals to the good of the whole, and is thus a form of "environmental fascism."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to these critics, we rightly reject such holistic approaches in human affairs. Why, they ask, should we adopt them in our treatment of non-human animals?</p>
<p>Finally, some critics have questioned whether Leopold's land ethic might require unacceptable interferences with nature in order to protect current, but transient, ecological balances.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> If the fundamental environmental imperative is to <i>preserve</i> the integrity and stability of natural ecosystems, wouldn't this require frequent and costly human interventions to prevent naturally occurring changes to natural environments? In nature, the "stability and integrity" of ecosystems are disrupted or destroyed all the time by drought, fire, storms, pests, newly invasive predators, etc. Must humans act to prevent such ecological changes, and if so, at what cost? Why should we place such high value on current ecological balances? Why think it is our role to be nature's steward or policeman? According to these critics, Leopold's stress on preserving existing ecological balances is overly human-centered and fails to treat nature with the respect it deserves.</p>
