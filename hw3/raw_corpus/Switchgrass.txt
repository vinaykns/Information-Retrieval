<p><i><b>Panicum virgatum</b></i>, commonly known as <b>switchgrass</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Perennial" class="mw-redirect" title="Perennial">perennial</a> warm season <a href="/wiki/Bunchgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunchgrass">bunchgrass</a> native to North America, where it occurs naturally from <a href="/wiki/55th_parallel_north" title="55th parallel north">55°N latitude</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> southwards into the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Switchgrass is one of the dominant <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of the central <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Tallgrass_prairie" title="Tallgrass prairie">tallgrass prairie</a> and can be found in remnant <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a>, in native grass <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a>, and naturalized along roadsides. It is used primarily for <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> production, game cover, as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_grass" title="Ornamental grass">ornamental grass</a>, and more recently as a <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> crop for <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">phytoremediation</a> projects, fiber, electricity, and heat production and for <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">biosequestration</a> of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.</p>
<p>Other common names for switchgrass include tall panic grass, Wobsqua grass, blackbent, tall prairiegrass, wild <a href="/wiki/Agrostis" title="Agrostis">redtop</a>, thatchgrass, and Virginia switchgrass.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Switchgrass_roots.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Switchgrass_roots.jpg/500px-Switchgrass_roots.jpg" width="500" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Switchgrass_roots.jpg/750px-Switchgrass_roots.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Switchgrass_roots.jpg/1000px-Switchgrass_roots.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2828" data-file-height="681" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Switchgrass_roots.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Root system of switchgrass grown at the <a href="/wiki/Land_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Land Institute">Land Institute</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Switchgrass is a hardy, deep-rooted, <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomatous</a> grass that begins growth in late <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a>. It can grow up to 2.7&#160;m high, but is typically shorter than <a href="/wiki/Big_bluestem" class="mw-redirect" title="Big bluestem">big bluestem</a> grass or <a href="/wiki/Indiangrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiangrass">indiangrass</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are 30–90&#160;cm long, with a prominent midrib. Switchgrass uses <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> carbon fixation</a>, giving it an advantage in conditions of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> and high temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-Silzer_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silzer-1">[1]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> have a well-developed <a href="/wiki/Panicle" title="Panicle">panicle</a>, often up to 60&#160;cm long, and it bears a good crop of <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>. The seeds are 3–6&#160;mm long and up to 1.5&#160;mm wide, and are developed from a single-flowered <a href="/wiki/Spikelet" class="mw-redirect" title="Spikelet">spikelet</a>. Both <a href="/wiki/Glume" title="Glume">glumes</a> are present and well developed. When ripe, the seeds sometimes take on a pink or dull-purple tinge, and turn golden brown with the foliage of the plant in the fall. Switchgrass is both a perennial and self-seeding crop, which means farmers do not have to plant and reseed after annual harvesting. Once established, a switchgrass stand can survive for ten years or longer.<sup id="cite_ref-plentiful_switchgrass_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plentiful_switchgrass-2">[2]</a></sup> Unlike corn, switchgrass can grow on marginal lands and requires relatively modest levels of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical fertilizer">chemical fertilizers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-plentiful_switchgrass_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plentiful_switchgrass-2">[2]</a></sup> Overall, it is considered a resource-efficient, low-input crop for producing bioenergy from farmland.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of North America, especially the prairies of the Midwestern United States, was once prime habitat to vast swaths of native grasses, including switchgrass, <a href="/wiki/Indiangrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiangrass">indiangrass</a> (<i>Sorghastrum nutans</i>), eastern gamagrass (<i><a href="/wiki/Tripsacum_dactyloides" title="Tripsacum dactyloides">Tripsacum dactyloides</a></i>), big bluestem (<i><a href="/wiki/Andropogon_gerardi" title="Andropogon gerardi">Andropogon gerardi</a></i>), little bluestem (<i><a href="/wiki/Schizachyrium_scoparium" title="Schizachyrium scoparium">Schizachyrium scoparium</a></i>), bluestem prairie (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andropogon_panicum_sorghastrum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Andropogon panicum sorghastrum (page does not exist)">Andropogon panicum sorghastrum</a></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-ESI_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESI-3">[3]</a></sup> and others. As <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">European settlers</a> began spreading west across the continent, the native grasses were plowed under and the land converted to crops such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a>. Introduced grasses such as <a href="/wiki/Fescue" class="mw-redirect" title="Fescue">fescue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poa" title="Poa">bluegrass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orchardgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Orchardgrass">orchardgrass</a><sup id="cite_ref-Ernst_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst-4">[4]</a></sup> also replaced the native grasses for use as hay and pasture for cattle.<sup id="cite_ref-ESI_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESI-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Switchgrass is a versatile and adaptable plant. It can grow and even thrive in many weather conditions, lengths of growing seasons, soil types, and land conditions. Its distribution spans south of latitude 55°N from <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, south over most of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, and further south into <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PAVI2_profile_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAVI2_profile-5">[5]</a></sup> As a warm-season <a href="/wiki/Perennial" class="mw-redirect" title="Perennial">perennial</a> grass, most of its growth occurs from late spring through early fall; it becomes dormant and unproductive during colder months. Thus, the productive season in its northern habitat can be as short as three months, but in the southern reaches of its habitat the growing season may be as long as eight months, around the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a> area.<sup id="cite_ref-ball_2002_southern_forages_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball_2002_southern_forages-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Switchgrass is a diverse species, with striking differences between plants. This diversity, which presumably reflects evolution and adaptation to new environments as the species spread across the continent, provides a range of valuable traits for breeding programs. Switchgrass has two distinct forms, or "cytotypes": the lowland <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>, which tend to produce more biomass, and the upland cultivars, which are generally of more northern origin, more cold-tolerant, and therefore usually preferred in northern areas. Upland switchgrass types are generally shorter (≤ 2.4&#160;m, tall) and less coarse than lowland types. Lowland cultivars may grow to ≥ 2.7&#160;m, in favorable environments. Both upland and lowland cultivars are deeply rooted (&gt; 1.8&#160;m, in favorable soils) and have short <a href="/wiki/Rhizome" title="Rhizome">rhizomes</a>. The upland types tend to have more vigorous rhizomes, so the lowland cultivars may appear to have a <a href="/wiki/Bunchgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunchgrass">bunchgrass</a> habit, while the upland types tend to be more sod-forming. Lowland cultivars appear more plastic in their morphology, produce larger plants if stands become thin or when planted in wide rows, and they seem to be more sensitive to moisture stress than upland cultivars.<sup id="cite_ref-Parrish_et_al._7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parrish_et_al.-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In native prairies, switchgrass is historically found in association with several other important native <a href="/wiki/Tallgrass_prairie" title="Tallgrass prairie">tallgrass prairie</a> plants, such as big bluestem, indiangrass, little bluestem, <a href="/wiki/Sideoats_grama" class="mw-redirect" title="Sideoats grama">sideoats grama</a>, eastern gamagrass, and various <a href="/wiki/Forbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Forbs">forbs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sunflowers" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunflowers">sunflowers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gayfeather" class="mw-redirect" title="Gayfeather">gayfeather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prairie_clover" class="mw-redirect" title="Prairie clover">prairie clover</a>, and prairie coneflower). These widely adapted tallgrass species once occupied millions of hectares.<sup id="cite_ref-Rinehart_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rinehart-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Switchgrass’ suitability for cultivation in the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Gran Chaco</a> is being studied by Argentina’s <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Tecnolog%C3%ADa_Agropecuaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Instituto Nacional de Tecnología Agropecuaria">Instituto Nacional de Tecnología Agropecuaria</a> (INTA).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment_and_management">Establishment and management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Establishment and management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Switchgrass can be grown on land considered unsuitable for <a href="/wiki/Row_crop" title="Row crop">row crop</a> production, including land that is too <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erodible</a> for <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> production, as well as <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sandy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravelly</a> soils in <a href="/wiki/Humid" class="mw-redirect" title="Humid">humid</a> regions that typically produce low <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yields</a> of other <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> crops. No single method of establishing switchgrass can be suggested for all situations. The crop can be established both by <a href="/wiki/No-till" class="mw-redirect" title="No-till">no-till</a> and conventional <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a>. When seeded as part of a diverse mixture, planting guidelines for warm-season grass mixtures for conservation plantings should be followed. Regional guidelines for growing and managing switchgrass for bioenergy or conservation plantings are available. Several key factors can increase the likelihood of success for establishing switchgrass. These include:<sup id="cite_ref-Parrish_et_al._2008_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parrish_et_al._2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Planting switchgrass after the soil is well warmed during the spring.</li>
<li>Using seeds that are highly <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinable</a> and planting 0.6 - 1.2&#160;cm deep, or up to 2&#160;cm deep in sandy soils.</li>
<li>Packing or firming the soil both before and after seeding.</li>
<li>Providing no <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilization</a> at planting to minimize competition.</li>
<li>Controlling <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> with chemical and/or cultural control methods.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Mowing">Mowing</a> and properly labeled <a href="/wiki/Herbicides" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a> are recommended for <a href="/wiki/Weed_control" title="Weed control">weed control</a>. Chemical weed control can be used in the <a href="/wiki/Fall" class="mw-redirect" title="Fall">fall</a> prior to establishment, or before or after planting. Weeds should be mowed just above the height of the growing switchgrass. <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">Hormone</a> herbicides, such as <a href="/wiki/2,4-D" class="mw-redirect" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a>, should be avoided as they are known to reduce development of switchgrass when applied early in the establishing year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Plantings that appear to have failed due to weed infestations are often wrongly assessed, as the failure is often more apparent than real. Switchgrass stands that are initially weedy commonly become well established with appropriate management in subsequent years.<sup id="cite_ref-Parrish_et_al._2008_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parrish_et_al._2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Once established, switchgrass can take up to three years to reach its full production potential.<sup id="cite_ref-samson_2007_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samson_2007-12">[12]</a></sup> Depending on the region, it can typically produce 1/4 to 1/3 of its yield potential in its first year and 2/3 of its potential in the year after seeding.<sup id="cite_ref-bransby_2005_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bransby_2005-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>After establishment, switchgrass management will depend on the goal of the seeding. Historically, most switchgrass seedings have been managed for the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_Reserve_Program" title="Conservation Reserve Program">Conservation Reserve Program</a> in the US. Disturbance such as periodic mowing, burning, or disking is required to optimize the stand’s utility for encouraging <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. Increased attention is being placed on switchgrass management as an <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crop</a>. Generally, the crop requires modest application of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen fertilizer">nitrogen fertilizer</a>, as it is not a heavy feeder. Typical nitrogen (N) content of <a href="/wiki/Senescence" title="Senescence">senescent</a> material in the fall is 0.5% N. Fertilizer nitrogen applications of about 5&#160;kg N/<a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare (ha)</a> applied for each <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonne</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> removed is a general guideline. More specific recommendations for fertilization are available regionally in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Herbicides are not often used on switchgrass after the seeding year, as the crop is generally quite competitive with weeds. Most bioenergy conversion processes for switchgrass, including those for cellulosic ethanol and pellet fuel production, can generally accept some alternative <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvested</a> <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. Stands of switchgrass should be harvested no more than twice per year, and one cutting often provides as much biomass as two. Switchgrass can be harvested with the same <a href="/wiki/Farm_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Farm equipment">field equipment</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Hay" title="Hay">hay</a> production, and it is well-suited to <a href="/wiki/Baler" title="Baler">baling</a> or bulk field harvesting. If its <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> is properly taken into consideration, switchgrass can offer great potential as an energy crop.<sup id="cite_ref-Parrish_et_al._2008_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parrish_et_al._2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Samson_et_al..2C_2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson_et_al..2C_2008-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Switchgrass can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Feedstock" class="mw-redirect" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a> for <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> energy production, as <a href="/wiki/Ground_cover" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground cover">ground cover</a> for <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a>, and to control <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a>, as game cover, and as feedstock for biodegradable plastics. It can be used by cattle farmers for <a href="/wiki/Hay" title="Hay">hay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> and as a substitute for wheat <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a> in many applications, including <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> bedding, straw bale housing, and as a substrate for growing mushrooms.</p>

<p>Additionally, switchgrass is grown as a drought-resistant <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_grass" title="Ornamental grass">ornamental grass</a> in average to wet soils and in full sun to part shade.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioenergy">Bioenergy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bioenergy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Switchgrass has been researched as a renewable <a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">bioenergy</a> crop since the mid-1980s, because it is a native <a href="/wiki/Perennial" class="mw-redirect" title="Perennial">perennial</a> warm season <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grass</a> with the ability to produce moderate to high <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yields</a> on marginal farmlands. It is now being considered for use in several bioenergy conversion processes, including <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> production, <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, and direct combustion for <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> applications. The main <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomic</a> advantages of switchgrass as a bioenergy crop are its stand longevity, drought and flooding tolerance, relatively low <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> input requirements, ease of management, hardiness in poor soil and climate conditions, and widespread adaptability in <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> climates. In some warm humid southern zones, such as <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, it has the ability to produce up to 25 oven-dry tonnes per hectare (ODT/ha). A summary of switchgrass yields across 13 research trial sites in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> found the top two cultivars in each trial to yield 9.4 to 22.9 t/ha, with an average yield of 14.6 ODT/ha.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_and_Kzos_2005_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin_and_Kzos_2005-15">[15]</a></sup> However, these yields were recorded on small plot trials, and commercial field sites could be expected to be at least 20% lower than these results. In the United States, switchgrass yields appear to be highest in warm humid regions with long growing seasons such as the US Southeast and lowest in the dry short season areas of the Northern <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_and_Kzos_2005_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin_and_Kzos_2005-15">[15]</a></sup> The energy inputs required to grow switchgrass are favorable when compared with <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annual seed bearing crops</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Canola" title="Canola">canola</a>, which can require relatively high energy inputs for field operations, crop drying, and fertilization. Whole plant <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> perennial <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4</a> grass feedstocks are desirable biomass energy feedstocks, as they require fewer fossil energy inputs to grow and effectively capture solar energy because of their C4 <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a> system and perennial nature. One study cites it takes from 0.97 to 1.34 GJ to produce 1 tonne of switchgrass, compared with 1.99 to 2.66 GJ to produce 1 tonne of corn.<sup id="cite_ref-Dale_and_Kim_2004_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dale_and_Kim_2004-16">[16]</a></sup> Another study found that switchgrass uses 0.8 GJ/ODT of fossil energy compared to grain corn's 2.9 GJ/ODT.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2005_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2005-17">[17]</a></sup> Given that switchgrass contains approximately 18.8 GJ/ODT of biomass, the energy output-to-input ratio for the crop can be up to 20:1.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2008_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2008-18">[18]</a></sup> This highly favorable ratio is attributable to its relatively high energy output per hectare and low energy inputs for production.</p>
<p>Considerable effort is being expended in developing switchgrass as a <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> crop in the USA. In <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush%27s_2006_State_of_the_Union_Address" class="mw-redirect" title="George W. Bush's 2006 State of the Union Address">George W. Bush's 2006 State of the Union Address</a>, he proposed using switchgrass for ethanol;<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> since then, over <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>100 million has been invested into researching switchgrass as a potential biofuel source.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Switchgrass has the potential to produce up to 380&#160;liters of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> per tonne harvested.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, current technology for herbaceous biomass conversion to ethanol is about 340&#160;liters per tonne.<sup id="cite_ref-Iogen_website_2009_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iogen_website_2009-24">[24]</a></sup> In contrast, corn ethanol yields about 400&#160;liters per tonne.<sup id="cite_ref-Farrell_et_al._2006_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrell_et_al._2006-25">[25]</a></sup><br />
There are substantial efforts being made to increase the yield of corn derived ethanol:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>(Corn-) Ethanol yield has already improved from 2.4 gallons per bushel in the 1980s to 2.8 gallons in modern plants. Corn hybrids developed specifically for ethanol production have demonstrated ethanol yield increases of 2.7 percent—and using the cellulose (fiber) in the corn kernel, in addition to the starch, could increase yield by another 10 to 13 percent. With this combination of hybrid and process optimization, theoretical yields of 3.51 gallons of ethanol per bushel are within reason—with no negative impact on protein or oil content for animal feed uses of the distillers grains.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Process improvements for the conventional corn-based industry are based on new technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Cavitation" title="Cavitation">cavitation</a>, new <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> and new <a href="/wiki/Enzymes" class="mw-redirect" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><br /></p>
<p>The main advantage of using switchgrass over corn as an ethanol feedstock is its cost of production is generally about 1/2 that of grain corn, and more biomass energy per hectare can be captured in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2008_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2008-18">[18]</a></sup> Thus, switchgrass cellulosic ethanol should give a higher <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yield</a> of ethanol per hectare at lower cost. However, this will depend on whether the cost of constructing and operating cellulosic ethanol plants can be reduced considerably. The switchgrass ethanol industry <a href="/wiki/Energy_returned_on_energy_invested" title="Energy returned on energy invested">energy balance</a> is also considered to be substantially better than that of <a href="/wiki/Corn_ethanol" title="Corn ethanol">corn ethanol</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Bioconversion" title="Bioconversion">bioconversion</a> process, the <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a> fraction of switchgrass can be burned to provide sufficient <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> to operate the <a href="/wiki/Biorefinery" title="Biorefinery">biorefinery</a>. Studies have found that for every unit of energy input needed to create a biofuel from switchgrass, four <a href="/wiki/Units_of_energy" title="Units of energy">units of energy</a> are yielded.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In contrast, corn ethanol yields about 1.28 units of energy per unit of energy input.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang_et_al._2007_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang_et_al._2007-32">[32]</a></sup> A recent study from the Great Plains <sup id="cite_ref-Schmer_et_al._2008_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmer_et_al._2008-33">[33]</a></sup> indicated that for ethanol production from switchgrass, this figure is 6.4, or alternatively, that 540% more energy was contained in the ethanol produced than was used in growing the switchgrass and converting it to <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">liquid fuel</a>. However, there remain <a href="/wiki/Commercialization" title="Commercialization">commercialization</a> barriers to the development of cellulosic ethanol technology. Projections in the early 1990s for commercialization of cellulosic ethanol by the year 2000<sup id="cite_ref-Lynd_et_al._1991_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lynd_et_al._1991-34">[34]</a></sup> have not been met. The commercialization of cellulosic ethanol is thus proving to be a significant challenge, despite noteworthy research efforts.</p>

<p>Thermal energy applications for switchgrass appear to be closer to near-term scale-up than cellulosic ethanol for industrial or small-scale applications. For example, switchgrass can be <a href="/wiki/Pellet_mill" title="Pellet mill">pressed into fuel pellets</a> that are subsequently burned in <a href="/wiki/Pellet_stoves" class="mw-redirect" title="Pellet stoves">pellet stoves</a> used to heat homes (which typically burn corn or <a href="/wiki/Wood_pellet" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood pellet">wood pellets</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-samson_2007_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samson_2007-12">[12]</a></sup> Switchgrass has been widely tested as a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Power_generation" class="mw-redirect" title="Power generation">power generation</a>. The most widely studied project to date has been the Chariton Valley Project in <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The Show-Me-Energy Cooperative (SMEC) in <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> is using switchgrass and other warm-season grasses, along with <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> residues, as feedstocks for pellets used for the firing of a coal-fired power plant. In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">Eastern Canada</a>, switchgrass is being used on a pilot scale as a feedstock for commercial heating applications. <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">Combustion</a> studies have been undertaken and it appears to be well-suited as a commercial boiler fuel. Research is also being undertaken to develop switchgrass as a pellet fuel because of lack of surplus wood residues in eastern Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-Bailey_Stamler_et_al._2006_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey_Stamler_et_al._2006-37">[37]</a></sup> as a slowdown in the forest products industry in 2009 is now resulting in <a href="/wiki/Wood_pellet" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood pellet">wood pellet</a> shortages throughout Eastern North America. Generally speaking, the direct firing of switchgrass for thermal applications can provide the highest <a href="/wiki/Net_energy_gain" title="Net energy gain">net energy gain</a> and energy output-to-input ratio of all switchgrass bioconversion processes.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson_and_Bailey_Stamler_2009_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson_and_Bailey_Stamler_2009-38">[38]</a></sup> Research has found switchgrass, when pelletized and used as a solid biofuel, is a good candidate for displacing fossil fuels. Switchgrass pellets were identified to have a 14.6:1 energy output-to-input ratio, which is substantially better than that for liquid biofuel options from farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2005_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson.2C_R._et_al._2005-17">[17]</a></sup> As a greenhouse gas mitigation strategy, switchgrass pellets were found to be an effective means to use farmland to mitigate greenhouse gases on the order of 7.6-13 tonnes per hectare of CO<sub>2</sub>. In contrast, switchgrass cellulosic ethanol and corn ethanol were found to mitigate 5.2 and 1.5 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per hectare, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Samson_et_al..2C_2008_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samson_et_al..2C_2008-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, the major constraint to the development of grasses for thermal energy applications has been the difficulty associated with burning grasses in conventional <a href="/wiki/Boilers" class="mw-redirect" title="Boilers">boilers</a>, as biomass quality problems can be of particular concern in combustion applications. These technical problems now appear to have been largely resolved through crop management practices such as fall <a href="/wiki/Mowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Mowing">mowing</a> and spring <a href="/wiki/Harvesting" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvesting">harvesting</a> that allow for <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> to occur, which leads to fewer <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a>-forming compounds (such as K and Cl) and N in the grass. This reduces <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(waste)" title="Clinker (waste)">clinker</a> formation and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, and enables switchgrass to be a clean combustion fuel source for use in smaller combustion appliances. Fall harvested grasses likely have more application for larger commercial and industrial boilers.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Switchgrass is also being used to heat small <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> and farm buildings in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> through a process used to make a low quality <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> substitute.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bai et al. (2010) conducted a study to analyze the environmental sustainability of using switchgrass plant material as a feedstock for <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> production.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle analysis">Life cycle analysis</a> was used to make this assessment. They compared efficiency of <a href="/wiki/E10_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="E10 fuel">E10</a>, <a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a>, and ethanol with <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. They took into account air and water emissions associated with growing, managing, processing and storing the switchgrass crop. They also factored in the transportation of the stored switchgrass to the ethanol plant where they assumed the distance was 20&#160;km. The reductions in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> potential by using E10 and E85 were 5 and 65%, respectively. Their models also suggested that the “human toxicity potential” and “eco-toxicity potential” were substantially greater for the high ethanol fuels (i.e., E85 and ethanol) than for gasoline and E10.</p>
<p>In 2014, a genetically altered form of the bacterium Caldicellulosiruptor bescii was created which can cheaply and efficiently turn switchgrass into ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradable_plastics_production">Biodegradable plastics production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biodegradable plastics production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a novel application, US scientists have genetically modified switchgrass to enable it to produce <a href="/wiki/Polyhydroxybutyrate" title="Polyhydroxybutyrate">polyhydroxybutyrate</a>, which accumulates in beadlike granules within the plant's cells.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In preliminary tests, the dry weight of a plants leaves were shown to comprise up to 3.7% of the polymer.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Such low accumulation rates do not, as of 2009, allow for commercial use of switchgrass as a biosource.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_conservation">Soil conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Soil conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Mine_reclamation" title="Mine reclamation">Mine reclamation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restoration_ecology" title="Restoration ecology">Restoration ecology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Revegetation" title="Revegetation">Revegetation</a></div>
<p>Switchgrass is useful for <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_amendment" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil amendment">amendment</a>, particularly in the United States and Canada, where switchgrass is endemic. Switchgrass has a deep fibrous root system – nearly as deep as the plant is tall. Since it, along with other native grasses and <a href="/wiki/Forb" title="Forb">forbs</a>, once covered the plains of the United States that are now the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Belt" title="Corn Belt">Corn Belt</a>, the effects of the past switchgrass habitat have been beneficial, lending to the fertile farmland that exists today. The deep fibrous root systems of switchgrass left a deep rich layer of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> in the soils of the Midwest, making those <a href="/wiki/Mollisol" title="Mollisol">mollisol</a> soils some of the most productive in the world. By returning switchgrass and other perennial prairie grasses as an agricultural crop, many marginal soils may benefit from increased levels of organic material, permeability, and fertility, due to the grass's deep root system.</p>
<p>Soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, both from wind and water, is of great concern in regions where switchgrass grows. Due to its height, switchgrass can form an effective wind erosion barrier.<sup id="cite_ref-plant_fact_sheet_NRCS_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plant_fact_sheet_NRCS-48">[48]</a></sup> Its root system, also, is excellent for holding soil in place, which helps prevent erosion from flooding and runoff. Some <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Transportation">highway departments</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Department_of_Transportation" title="Kansas Department of Transportation">KDOT</a>) have used switchgrass in their seed mixes when re-establishing growth along roadways.<sup id="cite_ref-kdot_bids_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kdot_bids-49">[49]</a></sup> It can also be used on <a href="/wiki/Strip_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Strip mine">strip mine</a> sites, dikes,<sup id="cite_ref-plant_fact_sheet_NRCS_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plant_fact_sheet_NRCS-48">[48]</a></sup> and pond dams. <a href="/wiki/Conservation_district" title="Conservation district">Conservation districts</a> in many parts of the United States use it to control erosion in grass waterways because of its ability to anchor soils while providing habitat for wildlife.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forages_and_grazing">Forages and grazing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Forages and grazing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Switchgrass is an excellent <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> for cattle; however, it has shown toxicity in horses, sheep, and goats<sup id="cite_ref-lee_2001_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lee_2001-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-johnson_2006_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson_2006-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stegelmeier_2007_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stegelmeier_2007-52">[52]</a></sup> through chemical compounds known as <a href="/wiki/Saponins" class="mw-redirect" title="Saponins">saponins</a>, which cause <a href="/wiki/Photosensitivity" title="Photosensitivity">photosensitivity</a> and liver damage in these animals. Researchers are continuing to learn more about the specific conditions under which switchgrass causes harm to these species, but until more is discovered, it is recommended switchgrass not be fed to them. For cattle, however, it can be fed as hay, or grazed.</p>
<p>Grazing switchgrass calls for watchful management practices to ensure survival of the stand. It is recommended that grazing begin when the plants are about 50&#160;cm tall, and that grazing be discontinued when the plants have been eaten down to about 25&#160;cm, and to rest the pasture 30 – 45 days between grazing periods.<sup id="cite_ref-ball_2006_table_28_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball_2006_table_28-53">[53]</a></sup> Switchgrass becomes stemmy and unpalatable as it matures, but during the target grazing period, it is a favorable forage with a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_feed_value&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Relative feed value (page does not exist)">relative feed value</a> (RFV) of 90-104.<sup id="cite_ref-ball_2006_table_33b_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball_2006_table_33b-54">[54]</a></sup> The grass's upright growth pattern places its growing point off the soil surface onto its stem, so leaving 25&#160;cm of stubble is important for regrowth. When harvesting switchgrass for hay, the first cutting occurs at the late boot stage – around mid-June. This should allow for a second cutting in mid-August, leaving enough regrowth to survive the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-wolf_2008_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wolf_2008-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_cover">Game cover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panicum_virgatum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Game cover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Switchgrass is well-known among wildlife conservationists as good forage and habitat for upland <a href="/wiki/Game_bird" class="mw-redirect" title="Game bird">game bird</a> species, such as <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">grouse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wild_turkey" title="Wild turkey">wild turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">song birds</a>, with its plentiful small seeds and tall cover. A study published in 2015 has shown that switchgrass, when grown in a traditional monoculture, has an adverse impact on some wildlife.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Depending on how thickly switchgrass is planted, and what it is partnered with, it also offers excellent forage and cover for other wildlife across the country. For those producers who have switchgrass stands on their farm, it is considered an environmental and aesthetic benefit due to the abundance of wildlife attracted by the switchgrass stands. Some members of Prairie Lands Bio-Products, Inc. in Iowa have even turned this benefit into a profitable business by leasing their switchgrass land for hunting during the proper seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-hipple_2002_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hipple_2002-57">[57]</a></sup> The benefits to wildlife can be extended even in large-scale agriculture through the process of strip harvesting, as recommended by <a href="/wiki/The_Wildlife_Society" title="The Wildlife Society">The Wildlife Society</a>, which suggests that rather than harvesting an entire field at once, strip harvesting could be practiced so that the entire habitat is not removed, thereby protecting the wildlife inhabiting the switchgrass.<sup id="cite_ref-bies_2006_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bies_2006-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
