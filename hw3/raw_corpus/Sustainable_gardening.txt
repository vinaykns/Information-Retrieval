			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sustainable gardening</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Sustainable gardening</b> includes the more specific sustainable landscapes, sustainable landscape design, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_landscaping" title="Sustainable landscaping">sustainable landscaping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_landscape_architecture" title="Sustainable landscape architecture">sustainable landscape architecture</a>, resulting in sustainable sites. It comprises a disparate group of <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticultural</a> interests that can share the aims and objectives associated with the international post-1980s <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> programs developed to address the fact that humans are now using natural biophysical resources faster than they can be replenished by nature.<sup id="cite_ref-MEA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MEA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Included within this compass are those home gardeners, and members of the landscape and nursery industries, and municipal authorities, that integrate environmental, social, and economic factors to create a more sustainable future.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Organic_gardening" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic gardening">Organic gardening</a> and the use of <a href="/wiki/Native_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Native plants">native plants</a> are integral to sustainable gardening.<sup id="cite_ref-cornucopia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornucopia-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">Sustainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a></div>
<p>After the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> in the early 1980s it was some time before the emergence of Sustainable Horticulture (as sustainable <i>production</i> horticulture) at the International Society of Horticultural Science's First International Symposium on Sustainability in Horticulture held at the International Horticultural Congress in Toronto in 2002. This symposium produced "conclusions ... on Sustainability in Horticulture and a Declaration for the 21st Century".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The principles and objectives outlined at this conference were discussed in more practical terms at the following conference at Seoul in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the eco-friendly principles and ideas espoused by sustainable gardens, landscapes and sites perpetuate sustainable practices established as a reaction to resource-intensive <a href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a>. These practices were established as movements for self-sufficiency and small-scale farming based on a holistic systems approach and ecological principles. Included here would be: <a href="/wiki/Biodynamic_agriculture" title="Biodynamic agriculture">biodynamic agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">no-till farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agroecology" title="Agroecology">agroecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fukuoka_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Fukuoka farming">Fukuoka farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">forest gardening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_gardening" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic gardening">organic gardening</a> and others. On a larger scale there is the more recent "whole farm planning"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which was established in 1995, and <a href="/wiki/Ecoagriculture" title="Ecoagriculture">ecoagriculture</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> established in 2000, and other variants of sustainable agricultural systems. Perhaps the most influential of these approaches is <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, established by Australians <a href="/wiki/Bill_Mollison" title="Bill Mollison">Bill Mollison</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Holmgren" title="David Holmgren">David Holmgren</a> as both a design system and a loosely defined philosophy or lifestyle ethic.<sup id="cite_ref-Holmgren.2C_D._2002_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmgren.2C_D._2002-9">[9]</a></sup> Permaculture shares many principles and practices of the above but not the broad philosophical base as indicated by the title of the 2002 publication <i>Permaculture, principles and pathways beyond sustainability</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holmgren.2C_D._2002_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmgren.2C_D._2002-9">[9]</a></sup> The application of sustainability principles to the horticultural sphere has now becoming broadly accepted in commerce and academia.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American Sustainable Sites Initiative<sup id="cite_ref-SS_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-10">[10]</a></sup> is an interdisciplinary approach used by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Landscape_Architects" title="American Society of Landscape Architects">American Society of Landscape Architects</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lady_Bird_Johnson_Wildflower_Center" title="Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center">Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Botanic_Garden" title="United States Botanic Garden">United States Botanic Garden</a> to create voluntary national guidelines and performance benchmarks for sustainable land design, construction and maintenance practices: it was founded in 2005. Using the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Report</a>’s definition of sustainable development as a model, it defines sustainability within its own sphere of reference as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>… <i>design, construction, operations and maintenance practices that meet the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>by attempting to:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>...<i>protect, restore and enhance the ability of landscapes to provide <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> that benefit humans and other organisms</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_and_concepts">Principles and concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Principles and concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Managing global biophysical cycles and ecosystem services for the benefit of humans, other organisms and future generations has now become a global human responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The method of applying sustainability to gardens, landscapes and sites is still under development and varies somewhat according to the context under consideration. However, there are a number of basic and common underlying biological and operational principles and practices in the sustainable sites literature.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_principles">Biological principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biological principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sustainable management of man-made landscapes emulates the natural processes that sustain the biosphere and its ecosystems. First and foremost is the harnessing the energy of the Sun and the cycling of materials thereby minimising waste and energy use.</p>
<p>Running within, and dependent on, the natural economy there is the production and consumption of goods and services in the “human economy” which has now significantly altered, in a detrimental way, natural biogeochemical cycles (notable here are the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a> so sustainable practices maximise support for ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-MEA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MEA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_plants">Native plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Native plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Native_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Native plants">native plants</a> in a garden or landscape can both preserve and protect natural ecosystems, and reduce the amount of care and energy required to maintain a healthy garden or landscape. Native plants are adapted to the local climate and geology, and often require less maintenance than exotic species. Native plants also support populations of native birds, insects, and other animals that they <a href="/wiki/Coevolved" class="mw-redirect" title="Coevolved">coevolved</a> with, thus promoting a healthy <a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">community</a> of organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-cornucopia_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornucopia-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plants in a garden or maintained landscape often form a <a href="/wiki/Source-sink_dynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Source-sink dynamics">source population</a> from which plants can colonize new areas. Avoiding the use of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> helps to prevent such plants from establishing new populations. Similarly, the use of native species can provide a valuable source to help these plants colonise new areas.</p>
<p>Some non-native species can form an <a href="/wiki/Ecological_trap" title="Ecological trap">ecological trap</a> in which native species are lured into an environment that appears attractive but is poorly suited to them.</p>
<p>However, in Britain research by the University of Sheffield as part of the BUGS project (Biodiversity in Urban Gardens in Sheffield) <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.bugs.group.shef.ac.uk">[6]</a> has revealed that for many invertebrates - the majority of wild animals in most gardens - it is not just native plants which can sustain them. The findings were published in popular form in Ken Thompson's book 'No Nettles Required: The truth about wildlife gardening'<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.rbooks.co.uk/product.aspx?id=1905811144">[7]</a>. He confirms the approach which Chris Baines had promoted in 'How to Make a Wildlife Garden' <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.franceslincoln.co.uk/en/C/0/Book/213/How_to_Make_a_Wildlife_Garden.html">[8]</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_principles">Operational principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operational principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Enhancement of ecosystem services is encouraged throughout the lifecycle of any site by providing clear design, construction, (operations), and management criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-SS_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-10">[10]</a></sup> To be sustainable over the long term requires environmental, social and economic demands are integrated to provide intergenerational equity by providing regenerative sustainable systems. Operational guidelines will link to and supplement existing guidelines for the built environment (supplementing existing green building and landscape guidelines),<sup id="cite_ref-SS_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-10">[10]</a></sup> the wider environment, and they will include metrics (benchmarks, audits, criteria, indexes etc.) that give some <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_measurement" title="Sustainability measurement">measure of sustainability</a> (a rating system) by clarifying what is sustainable or not sustainable or, more likely, what is more or less sustainable.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale">Scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Impacts of a site can be assessed and measured over any spatio-temporal scale or context.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_and_indirect_environmental_impact">Direct and indirect environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Direct and indirect environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Impacts of a site may be <i>direct</i> by having direct measurable impacts on biodiversity and ecology at the site itself or <i>indirect</i> when impacts occur away from the site.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Site_principles">Site principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Site principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The following are some site principles for sustainable gardening:<sup id="cite_ref-SS_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Do no harm</li>
<li>Use the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">Precautionary principle</a></li>
<li>Design with nature and culture</li>
<li>Use a decision-making hierarchy of preservation, conservation, and regeneration</li>
<li>Provide regenerative systems as <a href="/wiki/Intergenerational_equity" title="Intergenerational equity">intergenerational equity</a></li>
<li>Support a living process</li>
<li>Use a system thinking approach</li>
<li>Use a collaborative and ethical approach</li>
<li>Maintain integrity in leadership and research</li>
<li>Foster environmental stewardship</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_site_sustainability">Measuring site sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Measuring site sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One major feature distinguishing the approach of sustainable gardens, landscapes and sites from other similar enterprises is the quantification of site sustainability by establishing performance benchmarks. Because sustainability is such a broad and inclusive concept the environmental impacts of sites can be categorised in numerous ways depending on the purpose for which the figures are required. The process can include minimising negative environmental impacts and maximising positive impacts. As currently applied the environment is usually given priority over social and economic factors which may be added in or regarded as an inevitable and integral part of the management process. A home gardener is likely to use simpler metrics than a professional landscaper or ecologist.</p>
<p>Three methodologies for measuring site sustainability include <a href="/wiki/BREEAM" title="BREEAM">BREEAM</a> developed by the BRE organisation in the UK, Leed, developed in America and the Oxford 360 degree sustainability Index used in Oxford Park and developed by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Sustainable_Group" title="Oxford Sustainable Group">Oxford Sustainable Group</a> in Scandinavia.</p>
<p>The <i>Sustainable Sites Initiative</i><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> is producing recommendations for the American Landscape Industry. The standards and guidelines finally adopted will lead to a uniform national standard, which does not currently exist. Sustainable Sites is currently in the pilot program stage, and will formally introduce its first rating system by 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The U.S. Green Building Council supports the project and plans to adopt the Sustainable Sites metrics into future versions of its Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design Green Building Rating System. Sites are rated according to their impact on ecosystem services:<sup id="cite_ref-SS_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-10">[10]</a></sup> The following ecosystem services have been identified by the study group:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul>
<li>Local climate regulation</li>
<li>Air and water cleansing</li>
<li>Water supply and regulation</li>
<li>Erosion and sediment control</li>
<li>Hazard mitigation</li>
<li>Pollination</li>
<li>Habitat functions</li>
<li>Waste decomposition and treatment</li>
<li>Global climate regulation</li>
<li>Human health and well-being benefits</li>
<li>Food and renewable non-food products</li>
<li>Cultural benefits</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constraints">Constraints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Constraints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any kind of auditing or benchmarking will depend on the selection and weighting of the metrics chosen; the depth and detail of analysis required; the purpose for which the figures are required; and the environmental circumstances of the particular site.</p>
