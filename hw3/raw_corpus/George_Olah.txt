<p><b>George Andrew Olah</b> (born <b>Oláh György</b>; May 22, 1927 – March 8, 2017) was a Hungarian and American chemist. His research involved the generation and reactivity of <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a> via <a href="/wiki/Superacids" class="mw-redirect" title="Superacids">superacids</a>. For this research, Olah was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 1994 "for his contribution to carbocation chemistry."<sup id="cite_ref-nobelprize_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize-3">[3]</a></sup> He was also awarded the <a href="/wiki/Priestley_Medal" title="Priestley Medal">Priestley Medal</a>, the highest honor granted by the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/F._Albert_Cotton#F.A._Cotton_Medal_for_Excellence_in_Chemical_Research" title="F. Albert Cotton">F.A. Cotton Medal for Excellence in Chemical Research</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Andrew_Olah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Olah was born in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, Hungary, on May 22, 1927, to Magda (Krasznai) and Gyula Oláh, a lawyer.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After the high school of Budapesti Piarista Gimnazium (<a href="/wiki/Piarists" title="Piarists">Scolopi fathers</a>), he studied under organic chemist <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_Zempl%C3%A9n" title="Géza Zemplén">Géza Zemplén</a> at the Technical University of Budapest, now the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_University_of_Technology_and_Economics" title="Budapest University of Technology and Economics">Budapest University of Technology and Economics</a>, where he earned M.S. and Ph.D degrees in Chemical Engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-10">[10]</a></sup> From 1949 through 1954, he taught at the school as a professor of organic chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-scalacs_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scalacs-11">[11]</a></sup> In the subsequent two years, from 1954–1956, he worked at the Research Institute of the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Hungarian Academy of Sciences">Hungarian Academy of Sciences</a>, where he was Associate Scientific Director and Head of the Department of Organic Chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-scalacs_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scalacs-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Andrew_Olah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="1956 Hungarian Revolution">1956 Hungarian Revolution</a>, he and his family moved briefly to England and then to Canada, where he joined <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Dow Chemical">Dow Chemical</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sarnia" title="Sarnia">Sarnia</a>, Ontario, with another Hungarian chemist, Stephen J. Kuhn. Olah's pioneering work on carbocations started during his eight years with Dow.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1965, he returned to academia at <a href="/wiki/Case_Western_Reserve_University" title="Case Western Reserve University">Case Western Reserve University</a> in Cleveland, Ohio, chairing the Department of Chemistry from 1965 to 1969, and from 1967 through 1977 he was the C. F. Maybery Distinguished Professor of Research in Chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-scalacs_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scalacs-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1971, Olah became a <a href="/wiki/Naturalized_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalized citizen">naturalized citizen</a> of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-10">[10]</a></sup> He then moved to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At USC, Olah was a distinguished professor and the director of the <a href="/wiki/Loker_Hydrocarbon_Research_Institute" title="Loker Hydrocarbon Research Institute">Loker Hydrocarbon Research Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Starting in 1980, he served as the Distinguished Donald P. and Katherine B. Loker Professor of Chemistry and later became a distinguished professor in USC's School of Engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1994, Olah was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> "for his contribution to <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocation</a> chemistry".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In particular, Olah's search for stable nonclassical <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a> led to the discovery of protonated <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> stabilized by <a href="/wiki/Superacids" class="mw-redirect" title="Superacids">superacids</a>, like FSO<sub>3</sub>H-SbF<sub>5</sub> ("<a href="/wiki/Magic_acid" title="Magic acid">Magic Acid</a>").</p>
<dl>
<dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + H<sup>+</sup> → CH<sub>5</sub><sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Because these cations were able to be stabilized, scientists could now use <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy</a> to study them in greater depth, as well as use them as catalysts in organic synthesis reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Olah, with Canadian chemist <a href="/wiki/Saul_Winstein" title="Saul Winstein">Saul Winstein</a>, was also involved in a career-long battle with <a href="/wiki/Herbert_C._Brown" title="Herbert C. Brown">Herbert C. Brown</a> of <a href="/wiki/Purdue" class="mw-redirect" title="Purdue">Purdue</a> over the existence of so-called "<a href="/wiki/Non-classical_ion" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-classical ion">nonclassical</a>" carbocations – such as the <a href="/wiki/Norbornane" title="Norbornane">norbornyl</a> cation, which can be depicted as cationic character delocalized over several bonds.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Olah's studies of the cation with NMR spectroscopy provided more evidence suggesting that Winstein's model of the non-classical cation, "featuring a pair of [delocalized] electrons smeared between three carbon atoms," was correct.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, the Olah family formed an <a href="/wiki/Financial_endowment" title="Financial endowment">endowment fund</a> (the George A. Olah Endowment) which grants annual awards to outstanding chemists, including the George A. Olah Award in Hydrocarbon or Petroleum Chemistry, formerly known as the ACS Award in Petroleum Chemistry. The awards are selected and administered by the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later in his career, his research shifted from <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> and their transformation into fuel to the <a href="/wiki/Methanol_economy" title="Methanol economy">methanol economy</a>, namely generating methanol from methane.<sup id="cite_ref-CE_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-19">[19]</a></sup> He joined with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Zubrin" title="Robert Zubrin">Robert Zubrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Korin" title="Anne Korin">Anne Korin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_Woolsey" class="mw-redirect" title="James Woolsey">James Woolsey</a> in promoting a flexible-fuel mandate initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2005, Olah wrote an essay promoting the methanol economy in which he suggested that methanol could be produced from <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas (H<sub>2</sub>) and industrially derived or atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), using energy from renewable sources to power the production process.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Andrew_Olah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He married Judith Agnes Lengyel in 1949, and they had two children, George, born in Hungary in 1954, and Ronald, born in the U.S. in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-10">[10]</a></sup> Olah died on March 8, 2017, at his home in <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills" class="mw-redirect" title="Beverly Hills">Beverly Hills</a>, California.<sup id="cite_ref-abc1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> After his death, the Hungarian government said that the "country has lost a great patriot and one of the most outstanding figures of Hungarian scientific life."<sup id="cite_ref-abc1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc1-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
