			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Alternative energy</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Alternative energy</b> is any energy source that is an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>. These alternatives are intended to address concerns about such fossil fuels, such as its high carbon dioxide emissions, an important factor in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">Marine energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> are all alternative sources of energy.</p>
<p>The nature of what constitutes an alternative energy source has changed considerably over time, as have controversies regarding energy use. Because of the variety of energy choices and differing goals of their advocates, defining some energy types as "alternative" is considered very controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-Zehner_2012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zehner_2012-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historians of economies have examined the key transitions to alternative energies and regard the transitions as pivotal in bringing about significant economic change.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Prior to the shift to an alternative energy, supplies of the dominant energy type became erratic, accompanied by rapid increases in energy prices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_as_an_alternative_to_wood">Coal as an alternative to wood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coal as an alternative to wood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late medieval period, coal was the new alternative fuel to save the society from overuse of the dominant fuel, wood. The deforestation had resulted in shortage of wood, at that time soft coal appeared as a savior. Historian <a href="/wiki/Norman_F._Cantor" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman F. Cantor">Norman F. Cantor</a> describes how:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Europeans had lived in the midst of vast forests throughout the earlier medieval centuries. After 1250 they became so skilled at deforestation that by 1500 AD they were running short of wood for heating and cooking... By 1500 Europe was on the edge of a fuel and nutritional disaster, [from] which it was saved in the sixteenth century only by the burning of soft coal and the cultivation of potatoes and maize. "<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum_as_an_alternative_to_whale_oil">Petroleum as an alternative to whale oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Petroleum as an alternative to whale oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">Whale oil</a> was the dominant form of lubrication and fuel for lamps in the early 19th century, but the <a href="/wiki/History_of_whaling" title="History of whaling">depletion of the whale stocks</a> by mid century caused whale oil prices to skyrocket setting the stage for the adoption of petroleum which was first commercialized in <a href="/wiki/Titusville,_Pennsylvania" title="Titusville, Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in 1859.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethanol_as_an_alternative_to_fossil_fuels">Ethanol as an alternative to fossil fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ethanol as an alternative to fossil fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">Ethanol fuel</a></div>
<p>In 1917, Alexander Graham Bell advocated ethanol from corn, wheat and other foods as an alternative to coal and oil, stating that the world was in measurable distance of depleting these fuels. For Bell, the problem requiring an alternative was lack of renewability of orthodox energy sources.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Since the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel_in_Brazil" title="Ethanol fuel in Brazil">Brazil has had an ethanol fuel program</a> which has allowed the country to become the world's second largest producer of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (after the United States) and the world's largest exporter.<sup id="cite_ref-RFA1E_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RFA1E-14">[14]</a></sup> Brazil’s ethanol fuel program uses modern equipment and cheap <a href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a> as feedstock, and the residual cane-waste (<a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a>) is used to process heat and power.<sup id="cite_ref-MLA_2004_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MLA_2004-15">[15]</a></sup> There are no longer light vehicles in Brazil running on pure gasoline. By the end of 2008 there were 35,000 filling stations throughout Brazil with at least one ethanol pump.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">Cellulosic ethanol</a> can be produced from a diverse array of feedstocks, and involves the use of the whole crop. This new approach should increase yields and reduce the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> because the amount of energy-intensive fertilizers and fungicides will remain the same, for a higher output of usable material.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cellu_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cellu-18">[18]</a></sup> As of 2008, there are nine <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol_commercialization" title="Cellulosic ethanol commercialization">commercial cellulosic ethanol plants</a> which are either operating, or under construction, in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Second-generation_biofuels" title="Second-generation biofuels">Second-generation biofuels</a> technologies are able to manufacture biofuels from inedible biomass and could hence prevent conversion of food into fuel."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As of July 2010, there is one commercial second-generation (2G) ethanol plant <a href="/wiki/Inbicon" title="Inbicon">Inbicon Biomass Refinery</a>, which is operating in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_gasification_as_an_alternative_to_petroleum">Coal gasification as an alternative to petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Coal gasification as an alternative to petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>'s administration advocated <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a> as an alternative to expensive imported oil. The program, including the <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Fuels_Corporation" title="Synthetic Fuels Corporation">Synthetic Fuels Corporation</a> was scrapped when petroleum prices plummeted in the 1980s. The carbon footprint and environmental impact of coal gasification are both very high.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_types_of_alternative_energy">Common types of alternative energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Common types of alternative energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydro_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydro electricity">Hydro electricity</a> captures energy from falling water.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">Nuclear energy</a> uses nuclear fission to release energy stored in the atomic bonds of heavy elements.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wind_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind energy">Wind energy</a> is the generation of electricity from wind, commonly by using propeller-like turbines.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> is the use of sunlight. Light can be changed into thermal (heat) energy or directly into electricity via photovoltaic devices.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">Geothermal energy</a> is the use of the earth's internal heat to boil water for heating buildings or generating electricity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">Ethanol</a> are plant-derived gasoline substitutes for powering vehicles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">Hydrogen</a> can be used as a carrier of energy, produced by various technologies such as cracking of hydrocarbons or water electrolysis.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enabling_technologies">Enabling technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Enabling technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ice_storage_air_conditioning" title="Ice storage air conditioning">Ice storage air conditioning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Storage_heater" title="Storage heater">thermal storage heaters</a> are methods of shifting consumption to use low cost off-peak electricity. When compared to resistance heating, <a href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat pumps">heat pumps</a> conserve electrical power (or in rare cases mechanical or thermal power) by collecting heat from a cool source such as a body of water, the ground or the air.</p>
<p>Thermal storage technologies allow heat or cold to be stored for periods of time ranging from diurnal to <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">interseasonal</a>, and can involve storage of <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible energy</a> (i.e. by changing the temperature of a medium) or <a href="/wiki/Latent_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Latent energy">latent energy</a> (e.g. through phase changes of a medium (i.e. changes from solid to liquid or vice versa), such as between water and slush or ice). Energy sources can be natural (via solar-thermal collectors, or dry cooling towers used to collect winter's cold), waste energy (such as from HVAC equipment, industrial processes or power plants), or surplus energy (such as seasonally from hydropower projects or intermittently from wind farms). The <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> (Alberta, Canada) is illustrative. <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">Borehole thermal energy storage</a> allows the community to get 97% of its year-round heat from solar collectors on the garage roofs.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The storages can be insulated tanks, borehole clusters in substrates ranging from gravel to bedrock, deep aquifers, or shallow pits that are lined and insulated. Some applications require inclusion of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat pumps">heat pump</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy_vs_non-renewable_energy">Renewable energy vs non-renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renewable energy vs non-renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a></div>
<p>Renewable energy is generated from <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>—such as <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal energy">tides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal heat</a>—which are renewable (naturally replenished). When comparing the processes for producing energy, there remain several fundamental differences between renewable energy and fossil fuels. The process of producing oil, coal, or natural gas fuel is a difficult and demanding process that requires a great deal of complex equipment, physical and chemical processes. On the other hand, alternative energy can be widely produced with basic equipment and natural processes. Wood, the most renewable and available alternative fuel, emits the same amount of carbon when burned as would be emittted if it degraded naturally.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Nuclear power is an alternative to fossil fuels that is non-renewable, like fossil fuels, nuclear ones are a finite resource.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecologically_friendly_alternatives">Ecologically friendly alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecologically friendly alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A renewable energy source such as <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> is sometimes regarded as a good alternative to providing heat and electricity with fossil fuels. Biofuels are not inherently ecologically friendly for this purpose, while burning biomass is carbon-neutral, air pollution is still produced. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, once leader in use of <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> as a biofuel, has suspended all subsidies for palm oil due to the scientific evidence that their use "may sometimes create more environmental harm than fossil fuels".<sup id="cite_ref-pal_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pal-26">[26]</a></sup> The Netherlands government and environmental groups are trying to trace the origins of imported palm oil, to certify which operations produce the oil in a responsible manner.<sup id="cite_ref-pal_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pal-26">[26]</a></sup> Regarding biofuels from foodstuffs, the realization that converting the entire grain harvest of the US would only produce 16% of its auto fuel needs, and the decimation of Brazil's CO<sub>2</sub> absorbing tropical rain forests to make way for biofuel production has made it clear that placing energy markets in competition with food markets results in higher food prices and insignificant or negative impact on energy issues such as global warming or dependence on foreign energy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Recently, alternatives to such undesirable sustainable fuels are being sought, such as commercially viable sources of cellulosic ethanol.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relatively_new_concepts_for_alternative_energy">Relatively new concepts for alternative energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Relatively new concepts for alternative energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon-neutral_and_negative_fuels">Carbon-neutral and negative fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Carbon-neutral and negative fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral_fuel" title="Carbon-neutral fuel">Carbon-neutral fuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol_economy" title="Methanol economy">Methanol economy</a></div>
<p>Carbon-neutral fuels are <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic fuels</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ammonia#As_a_fuel" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>) produced by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenating</a> waste <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> recycled from <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_emissions_from_fossil-fuel_combustion" title="Flue-gas emissions from fossil-fuel combustion">power plant flue-gas emissions</a>, recovered from automotive <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gas</a>, or derived from <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Graves2011rev_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graves2011rev-29">[29]</a></sup> Commercial fuel synthesis companies suggest they can produce synthetic fuels for less than <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> fuels when oil costs more than $55 per barrel.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Renewable methanol (RM) is a fuel produced from <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and carbon dioxide by catalytic <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> where the hydrogen has been obtained from <a href="/wiki/Water_electrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Water electrolysis">water electrolysis</a>. It can be blended into transportation fuel or processed as a chemical feedstock.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/George_Olah" class="mw-redirect" title="George Olah">George Olah</a> carbon dioxide recycling plant operated by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a> in <a href="/wiki/Grindav%C3%ADk" title="Grindavík">Grindavík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> has been producing 2 million liters of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> transportation fuel per year from flue exhaust of the <a href="/wiki/Svartsengi_Power_Station" title="Svartsengi Power Station">Svartsengi Power Station</a> since 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It has the capacity to produce 5 million liters per year.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A 250 kilowatt methane synthesis plant was constructed by the Center for Solar Energy and Hydrogen Research (ZSW) at <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> in Germany and began operating in 2010. It is being upgraded to 10 megawatts, scheduled for completion in autumn, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-zsw-bw1_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zsw-bw1-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zsw-bw2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zsw-bw2-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> has constructed a carbon-neutral <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (LNG) plant in <a href="/wiki/Werlte" title="Werlte">Werlte</a>, Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The plant is intended to produce transportation fuel to offset LNG used in their <a href="/wiki/Audi_A3#A3_Sportback_g-tron_.282013-.29" title="Audi A3">A3 Sportback g-tron</a> automobiles, and can keep 2,800 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the environment per year at its initial capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Other commercial developments are taking place in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia, South Carolina</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Camarillo,_California" title="Camarillo, California">Camarillo, California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Darlington" title="Darlington">Darlington</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Such fuels are considered <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon-neutral">carbon-neutral</a> because they do not result in a net increase in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lackner2012_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lackner2012-41">[41]</a></sup> To the extent that synthetic fuels displace <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, or if they are produced from waste carbon or seawater carbonic acid,<sup id="cite_ref-Eisaman2012_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eisaman2012-42">[42]</a></sup> and their combustion is subject to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> at the flue or exhaust pipe, they result in <a href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative carbon dioxide emission</a> and net <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> from the atmosphere, and thus constitute a form of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_remediation" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas remediation">greenhouse gas remediation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goeppert2012_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goeppert2012-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Such renewable fuels alleviate the costs and dependency issues of imported fossil fuels without requiring either electrification of the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle fleet">vehicle fleet</a> or conversion to hydrogen or other fuels, enabling continued compatible and affordable vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-44">[44]</a></sup> Carbon-neutral fuels offer relatively low cost energy <a href="/wiki/Power_storage#Synthetic_hydrocarbon_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Power storage">storage</a>, alleviating the problems of wind and solar <a href="/wiki/Intermittency" title="Intermittency">intermittency</a>, and they enable distribution of wind, water, and solar power through existing natural gas pipelines.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nighttime <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> is considered the most economical form of electrical power with which to synthesize fuel, because the <a href="/wiki/Load_curve" class="mw-redirect" title="Load curve">load curve</a> for electricity peaks sharply during the day, but wind tends to blow slightly more at night than during the day, so, the price of nighttime wind power is often much less expensive than any alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-44">[44]</a></sup> Germany has built a 250 kilowatt synthetic methane plant which they are scaling up to 10 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-zsw-bw1_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zsw-bw1-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zsw-bw2_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zsw-bw2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algae_fuel">Algae fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Algae fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Algae_fuel" title="Algae fuel">Algae fuel</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> which is derived from algae. During photosynthesis, algae and other photosynthetic organisms capture carbon dioxide and sunlight and convert it into oxygen and biomass. This is usually done by placing the algae between two panes of glass. The algae creates three forms of energy fuel: heat (from its growth cycle), biofuel (the natural "oil" derived from the algae), and biomass (from the algae itself, as it is harvested upon maturity).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The heat can be used to power building systems (such as heat process water) or to produce energy. Biofuel is oil extracted from the algae upon maturity, and used to create energy similar to the use of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. The biomass is the matter left over after extracting the oil and water, and can be harvested to produce combustible methane for energy production, similar to the warmth felt in a compost pile or the methane collected from biodegradable materials in a landfill. Additionally, the benefits of algae biofuel are that it can be produced industrially, as well as vertically (i.e. as a building facade), thereby obviating the use of arable land and food crops (such as soy, palm, and canola).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass_briquettes">Biomass briquettes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Biomass briquettes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biomass_briquettes" title="Biomass briquettes">Biomass briquettes</a> are being developed in the developing world as an alternative to charcoal. The technique involves the conversion of almost any plant matter into compressed briquettes that typically have about 70% the calorific value of charcoal. There are relatively few examples of large scale briquette production. One exception is in North Kivu, in eastern <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a>, where forest clearance for charcoal production is considered to be the biggest threat to Mountain Gorilla habitat. The staff of <a href="/wiki/Virunga_National_Park" title="Virunga National Park">Virunga National Park</a> have successfully trained and equipped over 3500 people to produce biomass briquettes, thereby replacing charcoal produced illegally inside the national park, and creating significant employment for people living in extreme poverty in conflict affected areas. <sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas_digestion">Biogas digestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Biogas digestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">Biogas</a> digestion deals with harnessing the methane gas that is released when waste breaks down. This gas can be retrieved from garbage or sewage systems. Biogas digesters are used to process methane gas by having bacteria break down <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> in an anaerobic environment. <sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The methane gas that is collected and refined can be used as an energy source for various products.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_hydrogen_production">Biological hydrogen production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biological hydrogen production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen gas is a completely clean burning fuel; its only by-product is water.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It also contains relatively high amount of energy compared with other fuels due to its chemical structure.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>2H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2H<sub>2</sub>O + High Energy</p>
<p>High Energy + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → 2H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></p>
<p>This requires a high-energy input, making commercial hydrogen very inefficient.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Use of a biological vector as a means to split water, and therefore produce hydrogen gas, would allow for the only energy input to be solar radiation. Biological vectors can include bacteria or more commonly algae. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Biological_hydrogen_production_(Algae)" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological hydrogen production (Algae)">biological hydrogen production</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> It requires the use of single celled organisms to create hydrogen gas through fermentation. Without the presence of oxygen, also known as an anaerobic environment, regular cellular respiration cannot take place and a process known as fermentation takes over. A major by-product of this process is hydrogen gas. If this could be implemented on a large scale, then sunlight, nutrients and water could create hydrogen gas to be used as a dense source of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Large-scale production has proven difficult. Not until 1999, was it even possible to induce these anaerobic conditions by sulfur deprivation.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Since the fermentation process is an evolutionary back up, turned on during stress, the cells would die after a few days. In 2000, a two-stage process was developed to take the cells in and out of anaerobic conditions and therefore keep them alive.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> For the last ten years, finding a way to do this on a large-scale has been the main goal of research. Careful work is being done to ensure an efficient process before large-scale production, however once a mechanism is developed, this type of production could solve our energy needs.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a> provided 75% of the worlds renewable electricity in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Much of the electricity used today is a result of the heyday of conventional hydroelectric development between 1960 and 1980, which has virtually ceased in Europe and North America due to environmental concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Globally there is a trend towards more hydroelectricity. From 2004 to 2014 the installed capacity rose from 715 to 1,055 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> A popular alternative to the large dams of the past is <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">run-of-the-river</a> where there is no water stored behind a dam and generation usually varies with seasonal rainfall. Using run-of-the-river in wet seasons and solar in dry seasons can balance seasonal variations for both. Another move away from large dams is <a href="/wiki/Small_hydro" title="Small hydro">small hydro</a>, these tend to be situated high up on tributaries, rather than on main rivers in valley bottoms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Offshore_wind">Offshore wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Offshore wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Offshore <a href="/wiki/Wind_farms" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind farms">wind farms</a> are similar to land-based wind farms, but are located on the ocean. Offshore wind farms can be placed in water up to 40 metres (130&#160;ft) deep, whereas floating wind turbines can float in water up to 700 metres (2,300&#160;ft) deep.<sup id="cite_ref-works_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-works-60">[60]</a></sup> The advantage of having a floating wind farm is to be able to harness the winds from the open ocean. Without any obstructions such as hills, trees and buildings, winds from the open ocean can reach up to speeds twice as fast as coastal areas.<sup id="cite_ref-works_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-works-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Significant generation of <a href="/wiki/Offshore_wind_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Offshore wind energy">offshore wind energy</a> already contributes to electricity needs in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and now the first offshore wind farms are under development in U.S. waters. While the offshore wind industry has grown dramatically over the last several decades, especially in Europe, there is still uncertainty associated with how the construction and operation of these wind farms affect marine animals and the <a href="/wiki/Marine_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine environment">marine environment</a>.</p>
<p>Traditional offshore <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbines" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbines">wind turbines</a> are attached to the seabed in shallower waters within the nearshore marine environment. As offshore wind technologies become more advanced, floating structures have begun to be used in deeper waters where more wind resources exist.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_and_hydrokinetic_energy">Marine and hydrokinetic energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Marine and hydrokinetic energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marine and Hydrokinetic (MHK) or <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a> development includes projects using the following devices:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wave power is the transport of energy by <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">wind waves</a>, and the capture of that energy to do useful work – for example, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pumping</a> water into reservoirs. A machine able to exploit significant waves in open coastal areas is generally known as a <a href="/wiki/Wave_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Wave energy">wave energy converter</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">Tidal power</a> turbines are placed in coastal and estuarine areas and daily flows are quite predictable.</li>
<li>In-stream turbines in fast-moving rivers;</li>
<li>Ocean current turbines in areas of strong marine currents;</li>
<li>Ocean Thermal Energy Converters in deep tropical waters.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_power">Nuclear power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Nuclear power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the year 2015 ten new reactors came online and 67 more were under construction including the first eight new Generation III+ <a href="/wiki/AP1000" title="AP1000">AP1000</a> reactors in the US and China and the first four new Generation III <a href="/wiki/EPR_(nuclear_reactor)" title="EPR (nuclear reactor)">EPR</a> reactors in Finland, France and China.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Reactors are also under construction in Belarus, Brazil, India, Iran, Japan, Pakistan, Russia, Slovakia, South Korea, Turkey, Ukraine and United Arab Emirates.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Thorium</a> is a fissionable material for possible future use in a <a href="/wiki/Thorium-based_nuclear_power" title="Thorium-based nuclear power">Thorium-based reactor</a>. Proponents of thorium reactors claims several potential advantages over a <a href="/wiki/Uranium_fuel_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium fuel cycle">uranium fuel cycle</a>, such as thorium's <a href="/wiki/Thorium#Occurrence" title="Thorium">greater abundance</a>, better resistance to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear weapons proliferation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hargraves-Moir_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hargraves-Moir-66">[66]</a></sup> and reduced <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> production.<sup id="cite_ref-Hargraves-Moir_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hargraves-Moir-66">[66]</a></sup> Thorium reactors can be modified to produce <a href="/wiki/Uranium-233" title="Uranium-233">Uranium-233</a>, which can then be processed into highly enriched uranium, which has been tested in low yield weapons,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and is unproven on a commercial scale.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investing_in_alternative_energy">Investing in alternative energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Investing in alternative energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As an emerging economic sector, there are limited stock market investment opportunities in alternative energy available to the general public. The public can buy shares of alternative energy companies from various stock markets, with wildly volatile returns. The recent IPO of SolarCity demonstrates the nascent nature of this sector- within a few weeks, it already had achieved the second highest market cap within the alternative energy sector.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Investors can also choose to invest in ETFs (exchange-traded funds) that track an alternative energy index, such as the WilderHill New Energy Index.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Additionally, there are a number of mutual funds, such as Calvert's Global Alternative Energy Mutual Fund<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> that are a bit more proactive in choosing the selected investments.</p>
<p>Recently, <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_Inc." title="Mosaic Inc.">Mosaic Inc.</a> launched an online platform allowing residents of California and New York to invest directly in solar.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Investing in solar projects had previously been limited to accredited investors,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> or a small number of willing banks.</p>
<p>Over the last three years publicly traded alternative energy companies have been very volatile, with some 2007 returns in excess of 100%, some 2008 returns down 90% or more, and peak-to-trough returns in 2009 again over 100%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In general there are three sub-segments of “alternative” energy investment: solar energy, wind energy and hybrid electric vehicles. Alternative energy sources which are renewable and have lower carbon emissions than fossil fuels are hydropower, wind energy, solar energy, geothermal energy, and bio fuels. Each of these four segments involve very different technologies and investment concerns.</p>
<p>For example, photovoltaic solar energy is based on semiconductor processing and accordingly, benefits from steep cost reductions similar to those realized in the microprocessor industry (i.e., driven by larger scale, higher module efficiency, and improving processing technologies). PV solar energy is perhaps the only energy technology whose electricity generation cost could be reduced by half or more over the next 5 years. Better and more efficient manufacturing process and new technology such as advanced thin film solar cell is a good example of that helps to reduce industry cost.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>The economics of solar PV electricity are highly dependent on silicon pricing and even companies whose technologies are based on other materials (e.g., First Solar) are impacted by the balance of supply and demand in the silicon market.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, because some companies sell completed solar cells on the open market (e.g., Q-Cells), this creates a low barrier to entry for companies that want to manufacture solar modules, which in turn can create an irrational pricing environment.</p>
<p>In contrast, because wind power has been harnessed for over 100 years, its underlying technology is relatively stable. Its economics are largely determined by siting (e.g., how hard the wind blows and the grid investment requirements) and the prices of steel (the largest component of a wind turbine) and select composites (used for the blades). Because current wind turbines are often in excess of 100 meters high, logistics and a global manufacturing platform are major sources of competitive advantage. These issues and others were explored in a research report by Sanford Bernstein.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_energy_in_transportation">Alternative energy in transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Alternative energy in transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to steadily rising gas prices in 2008 with the US national average price per gallon of regular unleaded gas rising above $4.00 at one point,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> there has been a steady movement towards developing higher fuel efficiency and more <a href="/wiki/Alternative_fuel_vehicle" title="Alternative fuel vehicle">alternative fuel vehicles</a> for consumers. In response, many smaller companies have rapidly increased research and development into radically different ways of powering consumer vehicles. <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">Hybrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery electric vehicles</a> are commercially available and are gaining wider industry and consumer acceptance worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>For example, Nissan USA introduced the world's first mass-production Electric Vehicle "<a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> A plug-in hybrid car, the "<a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Volt" title="Chevrolet Volt">Chevrolet Volt</a>" also has been produced, using an electric motor to drive the wheels, and a small four-cylinder engine to generate additional electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Making_alternative_energy_mainstream">Making alternative energy mainstream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Making alternative energy mainstream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before alternative energy becomes mainstream there are a few crucial obstacles that it must overcome: First there must be increased understanding of how alternative energies are beneficial; secondly the availability components for these systems must increase; and lastly the pay-back period must be decreased.</p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> (EV) and <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_Hybrid_Electric_Vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle">Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles</a> (PHEV) are on the rise. These vehicles depend on investment in home and public charging infrastructure, as well as implementing much more alternative energy for future transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous organizations within the academic, federal, and commercial sectors conducting large scale advanced research in the field of alternative energy. This research spans several areas of focus across the alternative energy spectrum. Most of the research is targeted at improving efficiency and increasing overall energy yields.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the US, multiple federally supported research organizations have focused on alternative energy in recent years. Two of the most prominent of these labs are <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> (NREL), both of which are funded by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> and supported by various corporate partners.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Sandia has a total budget of $2.4 billion<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> while NREL has a budget of $375 million.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_Energy">Mechanical Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Mechanical Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mechanical energy associated with human activities such as blood circulation, respiration, walking, typing and running etc. is ubiquitous but usually wasted. It has attracted tremendous attention from researchers around the globe to find methods to scavenge such mechanical energies. The best solution currently is to use piezoelecric materials, which can generate flow of electrons when deformed. Various devices using piezoelectric materials have been built to scavenge mechanical energy. Considering that the piezoelectric constant of the material plays a critical role in the overall performance of a piezoelectric device, one critical research direction to improve device efficiency is to find new material of large piezoelectric response. Lead Magnesium Niobate-Lead Titanate (PMN-PT) is a next-generation piezoelectric material with super high piezoelectric constant when ideal composition and orientation are obtained. In 2012, PMN-PT Nanowires with a very high piezoelectric constant were fabricated by a hydro-thermal approach<sup><a href="/wiki/Nanogenerator" title="Nanogenerator">[14]</a></sup> and then assembled into an energy-harvesting device<sup><a href="/wiki/Nanogenerator" title="Nanogenerator">[15]</a></sup>. The record-high piezoelectric constant was further improved by the fabrication of a single-crystal PMN-PT nanobelt,<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> which was then used as the essential building block for a piezoelectric nanogenerator.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> can be used for <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_conditioning" title="Solar air conditioning">cooling</a> or electrical power generation using the sun.</p>
<p>Solar heat has long been employed in <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passively</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_solar_heating" class="mw-redirect" title="Active solar heating">actively</a> heated buildings, as well as district heating systems. Examples of the latter are the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> is Alberta, Canada and numerous district systems in Denmark<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> In Europe, there are two programs for the application of solar heat: the Solar District Heating (SDH)<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>'s Solar Heating and Cooling (SHC) program.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>The obstacles preventing the large scale implementation of solar powered energy generation is the inefficiency of current solar technology, and the cost. Currently, photovoltaic (PV) panels only have the ability to convert around 16% of the sunlight that hits them into electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> (NREL), have heavily funded solar research programs. The NREL solar program has a budget of around $75 million<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and develops research projects in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) technology, solar thermal energy, and solar radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> The budget for Sandia’s solar division is unknown, however it accounts for a significant percentage of the laboratory’s $2.4 billion budget.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several academic programs have focused on solar research in recent years. The Solar Energy Research Center (SERC) at <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> (UNC) has the sole purpose of developing cost effective solar technology. In 2008, researchers at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) developed a method to store solar energy by using it to produce hydrogen fuel from water.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Such research is targeted at addressing the obstacle that solar development faces of storing energy for use during nighttime hours when the sun is not shining.</p>
<p>In February 2012, North Carolina-based Semprius Inc., a solar development company backed by German corporation <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, announced that they had developed the world’s most efficient solar panel. The company claims that the prototype converts 33.9% of the sunlight that hits it to electricity, more than double the previous high-end conversion rate.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind Energy">Wind Energy</a></div>
<p>Wind energy research dates back several decades to the 1970s when <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> developed an analytical model to predict wind turbine power generation during high winds.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Today, both <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> have programs dedicated to wind research. Sandia’s laboratory focuses on the advancement of materials, aerodynamics, and sensors.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The NREL wind projects are centered on improving wind plant power production, reducing their capital costs, and making wind energy more cost effective overall.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Field Laboratory for Optimized Wind Energy (FLOWE) at <a href="/wiki/Caltech" class="mw-redirect" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> was established to research alternative approaches to wind energy farming technology practices that have the potential to reduce the cost, size, and environmental impact of wind energy production.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>Renewable energies such as wind, solar, biomass and geothermal combined, supplied 1.3% of global final energy consumption in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">Biomass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Biomass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">Biogas</a></div>

<p>Biomass can be regarded as "<a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biological material</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> derived from living, or recently living organisms. It most often refers to plants or plant-derived materials which are specifically called <a href="/wiki/Lignocellulosic_biomass" title="Lignocellulosic biomass">lignocellulosic biomass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Biomass_Energy_Center_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biomass_Energy_Center-103">[103]</a></sup> As an energy source, biomass can either be used directly via combustion to produce heat, or indirectly after converting it to various forms of <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>. Conversion of biomass to biofuel can be achieved by different methods which are broadly classified into: <i>thermal</i>, <i>chemical</i>, and <i>biochemical</i> methods. Wood remains the largest biomass energy source today;<sup id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-104">[104]</a></sup> examples include forest residues (such as dead trees, branches and <a href="/wiki/Tree_stump" title="Tree stump">tree stumps</a>), yard clippings, wood chips and even <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a>. In the second sense, biomass includes plant or animal matter that can be converted into fibers or other industrial <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemicals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>. Industrial biomass can be grown from numerous types of plants, including <a href="/wiki/Miscanthus" title="Miscanthus">miscanthus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switchgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> and a variety of tree species, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oil_palm" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil palm">oil palm</a> (<a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a>).</p>
<p>Biomass, biogas and biofuels are burned to produce heat/power and in doing so harm the environment. Pollutants such as sulphurous oxides (SO<sub>x</sub>), nitrous oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>), and particulate matter (PM) are produced from this combustion. The World Health Organisation estimates that 7 million premature deaths are caused each year by air pollution,<sup id="cite_ref-who.int_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-106">[106]</a></sup> and biomass combustion is a major contributor of it.<sup id="cite_ref-who.int_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-106">[106]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> The use of biomas is carbon neutral over time, but is otherwise similar to burning fossil fuels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethanol_biofuels">Ethanol biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Ethanol biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_biofuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethanol biofuels">Ethanol biofuels</a></div>
<p>As the primary source of biofuels in North America, many organizations are conducting research in the area of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> production. On the Federal level, the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a> conducts a large amount of research regarding ethanol production in the United States. Much of this research is targeted toward the effect of ethanol production on domestic food markets.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> has conducted various ethanol research projects, mainly in the area of <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">Cellulosic ethanol</a> has many benefits over traditional corn based-ethanol. It does not take away or directly conflict with the food supply because it is produced from wood, grasses, or non-edible parts of plants.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Moreover, some studies have shown cellulosic ethanol to be more cost effective and economically sustainable than corn-based ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> conducts in-house cellulosic ethanol research<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> and is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Joint_BioEnergy_Institute" title="Joint BioEnergy Institute">Joint BioEnergy Institute</a> (JBEI), a research institute founded by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> with the goal of developing cellulosic biofuels.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_biofuels">Other biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Other biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a></div>
<p>From 1978 to 1996, the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> experimented with using algae as a biofuels source in the "<a href="/wiki/Aquatic_Species_Program" title="Aquatic Species Program">Aquatic Species Program</a>.”<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A self-published article by Michael Briggs, at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Hampshire" title="University of New Hampshire">University of New Hampshire</a> Biofuels Group, offers estimates for the realistic replacement of all motor vehicle fuel with biofuels by utilizing algae that have a natural oil content greater than 50%, which Briggs suggests can be grown on algae ponds at wastewater treatment plants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This oil-rich algae can then be extracted from the system and processed into biofuels, with the dried remainder further reprocessed to create ethanol.</p>
<p>The production of algae to harvest oil for biofuels has not yet been undertaken on a commercial scale, but feasibility studies have been conducted to arrive at the above yield estimate. In addition to its projected high yield, algaculture— unlike food crop-based biofuels — does not entail a decrease in food production, since it requires neither farmland nor fresh water. Many companies are pursuing algae bio-reactors for various purposes, including scaling up biofuels production to commercial levels.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several groups in various sectors are conducting research on Jatropha curcas, a poisonous shrub-like tree that produces seeds considered by many to be a viable source of biofuels feedstock oil.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> Much of this research focuses on improving the overall per acre oil yield of Jatropha through advancements in genetics, soil science, and horticultural practices. <a href="/wiki/SG_Biofuels" title="SG Biofuels">SG Biofuels</a>, a San Diego-based Jatropha developer, has used molecular breeding and biotechnology to produce elite hybrid seeds of Jatropha that show significant yield improvements over first generation varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> The Center for Sustainable Energy Farming (CfSEF) is a Los Angeles-based non-profit research organization dedicated to Jatropha research in the areas of plant science, agronomy, and horticulture. Successful exploration of these disciplines is projected to increase Jatropha farm production yields by 200-300% in the next ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal">Geothermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Geothermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal electricity">Geothermal electricity</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">Geothermal energy</a> is produced by tapping into the heat within the earths crust. It is considered sustainable because that thermal energy is constantly replenished.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> However, the science of geothermal energy generation is still young and developing economic viability. Several entities, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a><sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a><sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> are conducting research toward the goal of establishing a proven science around geothermal energy. The International Centre for Geothermal Research (IGC), a German geosciences research organization, is largely focused on geothermal energy development research.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel" title="Hydrogen fuel">Hydrogen fuel</a></div>
<p>Over $1 billion has been spent on the research and development of hydrogen fuel in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> Both the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a><sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a><sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> have departments dedicated to hydrogen research. Much of this work centers on hydrogen storage and fuel cell technologies</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_debate" title="Renewable energy debate">Renewable energy debate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disadvantages_of_hydroelectricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Disadvantages of hydroelectricity">Disadvantages of hydroelectricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power#Tidal_power_issues" title="Tidal power">Tidal power §&#160;Tidal power issues</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Issues_relating_to_biofuels" title="Issues relating to biofuels">Issues relating to biofuels</a></div>
<p>The generation of alternative energy on the scale needed to replace fossil energy, in an effort to reverse global climate change, is likely to have significant negative environmental impacts. For example, <a href="/wiki/Biomass_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomass energy">biomass energy</a> generation would have to increase 7-fold to supply current primary energy demand, and up to 40-fold by 2100 given economic and energy growth projections.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> Humans already appropriate 30 to 40% of all photosynthetically fixed carbon worldwide, indicating that expansion of additional biomass harvesting is likely to stress ecosystems, in some cases precipitating collapse and extinction of animal species that have been deprived of vital food sources.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> The total amount of energy capture by vegetation in the United States each year is around 58 quads (61.5 EJ), about half of which is already harvested as agricultural crops and forest products. The remaining biomass is needed to maintain ecosystem functions and diversity.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> Since annual energy use in the United States is ca. 100 quads, biomass energy could supply only a very small fraction. To supply the current worldwide energy demand solely with biomass would require more than 10% of the Earth’s land surface, which is comparable to the area use for all of world agriculture (i.e., ca. 1500 million hectares), indicating that further expansion of biomass energy generation will be difficult without precipitating an ethical conflict, given current world hunger statistics, over growing plants for biofuel versus food.<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given environmental concerns (e.g., fish migration, destruction of sensitive aquatic ecosystems, etc.) about building new dams to capture hydroelectric energy, further expansion of conventional <a href="/wiki/Hydropower_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydropower in the United States">hydropower in the United States</a> is unlikely. Windpower, if deployed on the large scale necessary to substitute fossil energy, is likely to face public resistance. If 100% of U.S. energy demand were to be supplied by windmills, about 80 million hectares (i.e., more than 40% of all available farmland in the United States) would have to be covered with large windmills (50m hub height and 250 to 500 m apart).<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> It is therefore not surprising that the major <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_wind_power" title="Environmental impact of wind power">environmental impact of wind power</a> is related to land use and less to wildlife (birds, bats, etc.) mortality. Unless only a relatively small fraction of electricity is generated by windmills in remote locations, it is unlikely that the public will tolerate large windfarms given concerns about blade noise and aesthetics.<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biofuels are different from fossil fuels in regard to net greenhouse gases but are similar to fossil fuels in that biofuels contribute to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>. Burning produces airborne carbon <a href="/wiki/Particulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Particulate">particulates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setbacks">Setbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Setbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Renewable alternative forms of energy have faced opposition from multiple groups, including conservatives and liberals. Around twelve states have passed proposals written to inhibit the alternative energy movement. Kansas lawmakers struck down a bill to phase out renewable energy mandates but face the possibility of the bill reappearing.<sup id="cite_ref-A_Pushback_on_Green_Power_137-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Pushback_on_Green_Power-137">[137]</a></sup></p>
<p>The opposition cites the potentially high cost of branching out to these alternatives in order to support the continuation and reliance on fossil fuels. Ohio's mandate to phase in alternative energy faces opposition who believe higher electricity prices will result, while supporters fear the loss of economic development and jobs that alternative energy could bring.<sup id="cite_ref-A_Pushback_on_Green_Power_137-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Pushback_on_Green_Power-137">[137]</a></sup></p>
<p>With nuclear meltdowns in Chernobyl and Fukushima, nuclear power presents a constant danger and is more unlikely to be a useful alternative source. The costs of maintaining nuclear facilities, the potential risk of meltdowns, and the cost of cleaning up meltdowns are cited as reasons behind the movement away from the use of nuclear energy. Nuclear power plants cannot compete with fossil fuels currently due to the latter's lower price and availability. Nuclear power plants also face competition from the increasing renewable energy market.<sup id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_138-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-138">[138]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="nuclear not in reference (March 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Renewable energy does have some setbacks when relying on them completely. Spikes in electricity bills occurred in Southern Australia when wind was not powering the turbines responsible for a large portion of the regions power. This led to the government to reintegrate a gas-powered station to supply power while the wind turbines were unable to.<sup id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_138-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-138">[138]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solar energy is the fastest growing and one of the most renowned form of alternative energy today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="wind is fastest growing (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Solar panels are an icon of the 'green power' movement, however the process of manufacturing the quartz based panels can be detrimental to the environment. Raw quartz (silica) is used to create solar cells must be mined using harsh chemicals that harm the surrounding environment, as well as those working in the mines. Silicosis is a form of lung disease that is caused by the inhalation of crystalline silica dust resulting in nodule lesions in the lungs. The silica must be cultivated into metallurgical-grade silicon, the process requiring a massive amount of energy as the quartz is placed into furnaces heated with fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> The metallurgical grade silica must be processed into polysilicon. This process also produces tetrachloride, a toxic substance that, if not disposed of correctly, can be harmful to the surrounding environment. Hydrochloric acid is formed when tetrachloride interacts with water, raising water and soil pH. Incidents of tetrachloride spills are common in China, as the production of solar panels has shifted from Europe and the United States to Asian countries within the early 2000's. Because of such, the villagers of Gaolong are unable to leave their homes due to air and soil becoming toxic. This was due to Luoyang Zhongui High-Technology Co. repeatedly dumped tetrachloride in a nearby field for almost a year.<sup id="cite_ref-140" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup></p>
