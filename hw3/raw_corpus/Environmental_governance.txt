<p><b>Environmental governance</b> is a concept in <a href="/wiki/Political_ecology" title="Political ecology">political ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental policy</a> that advocates <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>) as the supreme consideration for managing all human activities—political, social and economic.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Governance includes government, business and civil society, and emphasizes whole system management. To capture this diverse range of elements, environmental governance often employs alternative systems of governance, for example watershed-based management.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It views <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> and the environment as global <a href="/wiki/Public_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Public goods">public goods</a>, belonging to the category of goods that are not diminished when they are shared.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This means that everyone benefits from for example, a breathable atmosphere, stable climate and stable biodiversity.</p>
<p>Public goods are non-rivalrous—a natural resource enjoyed by one person can still be enjoyed by others—and non-excludable—it is impossible to prevent someone consuming the good (breathing). Nevertheless, public goods are recognized as beneficial and therefore have value. The notion of a global public good thus emerges, with a slight distinction: it covers necessities that must not be destroyed by one person or state.</p>
<p>The non-rivalrous character of such goods calls for a management approach that restricts public and private actors from damaging them. One approach is to attribute an <a href="/wiki/Economic_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic value">economic value</a> to the resource. Water is possibly the best example of this type of good.</p>
<p>As of 2013 environmental governance is far from meeting these imperatives. “Despite a great awareness of environmental questions from developed and developing countries, there is <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> and the appearance of new environmental problems. This situation is caused by the parlous state of global environmental governance, wherein current global environmental governance is unable to address environmental issues due to many factors. These include fragmented governance within the United Nations, lack of involvement from financial institutions, proliferation of environmental agreements often in conflict with <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> measures; all these various problems disturb the proper functioning of global environmental governance. Moreover, divisions among northern countries and the persistent gap between developed and developing countries also have to be taken into account to comprehend the institutional failures of the current global environmental governance."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>What is Environmental Governance?</b></p>
<p>Environmental governance refers to the processes of decision-making involved in the control and management of the environment and natural resources. <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN), define Environmental Governance as the 'Multi-level interactions (i.e., local, national, international/global) among, but not limited to, three main actors, i.e., state, <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a>, and civil society, which interact with one another, whether in formal and informal ways; in formulating and implementing policies in response to environment-related demands and inputs from the society; bound by rules, procedures, processes, and widely accepted behavior; possessing characteristics of “good governance”; for the purpose of attaining environmentally-<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>' (ICUN 2014)</p>
<p>Key principles of environmental governance include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Embedding the environment in all levels of decision-making and action</li>
<li>Conceptualizing cities and communities, economic and political life as a subset of the environment</li>
<li>Emphasizing the connection of people to the ecosystems in which they live</li>
<li>Promoting the transition from open-loop/cradle-to-grave systems (like garbage disposal with no recycling) to closed-loop/<a href="/wiki/Cradle-to-cradle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cradle-to-cradle">cradle-to-cradle</a> systems (like <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste" title="Zero waste">zero waste</a> strategies).</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Neoliberal Environmental Governance</b> – is an approach to the theory of environmental governance framed by a perspective on <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a> as an ideology, policy and practice in relation to the biophysical world. There are many definitions and applications of neoliberalism, e.g. in economic, international relations, etc. However, the traditional understanding of neoliberalism is often simplified to the notion of the primacy of market-led economics through the rolling back of the state, <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Privatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Privatisation">privatisation</a>. Neoliberalism has evolved particularly over the last 40 years with many scholars leaving their ideological footprint on the neoliberal map. <a href="/wiki/Hayek" title="Hayek">Hayek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedman" title="Friedman">Friedman</a> believed in the superiority of the free market over state intervention. As long as the market was allowed to act freely, the supply/demand law would ensure the ‘optimal’ price and reward. In <a href="/wiki/Karl_Polanyi" title="Karl Polanyi">Karl Polanyi</a>’s opposing view this would also create a state of tension in which self-regulating <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free markets</a> disrupt and alter social interactions and “displace other valued means of living and working”.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in contrast to the notion of an unregulated market economy there has also been a “paradoxical increase in [state] intervention”<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in the choice of economic, legislative and social policy reforms, which are pursued by the state to preserve the neoliberal order. This contradictory process is described by Peck and Tickell as roll back/roll out neoliberalism in which on one hand the state willingly gives up the control over resources and responsibility for social provision while on the other, it engages in “purposeful construction and consolidation of neoliberalised state forms, modes of governance, and regulatory relations".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been a growing interest in the effects of neoliberalism on the politics of the non-human world of environmental governance. Neoliberalism is seen to be more than a homogenous and monolithic ‘thing’ with a clear end point.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is a series of path-dependent, spatially and temporally “connected neoliberalisation” processes which affect and are affected by nature and environment that “cover a remarkable array of places, regions and countries”.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Co-opting neoliberal ideas of the importance of private property and the protection of individual (investor) rights, into environmental governance can be seen in the example of recent multilateral trade agreements (see in particular the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement</a>). Such neoliberal structures further reinforce a process of nature enclosure and primitive accumulation or “accumulation by dispossession” that serves to privatise increasing areas of nature.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The ownership-transfer of resources traditionally not privately owned to free market mechanisms is believed to deliver greater efficiency and optimal return on investment.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Other similar examples of neo-liberal inspired projects include the enclosure of minerals, the fisheries quota system in the North Pacific<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Water_privatization" title="Water privatization">privatisation of water supply</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> All three examples share neoliberal characteristics to “deploy markets as the solution to environmental problems” in which scarce natural resources are commercialized and turned into commodities.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The approach to frame the ecosystem in the context of a price-able commodity is also present in the work of neoliberal geographers who subject nature to price and supply/demand mechanisms where the earth is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Quantifiable" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantifiable">quantifiable</a> resource (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Costanza</a>, for example, estimates the earth <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem's service</a> value to be between 16 and <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(currency)#1_trillion_to_100_trillion" title="Orders of magnitude (currency)">54 trillion dollars</a> per year<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_drivers_of_environmental_degradation">Main drivers of environmental degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main drivers of environmental degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">Economic growth</a></i> – The development-centric vision that prevails in most countries and international institutions advocates a headlong rush towards more economic growth. Environmental economists on the other hand, point to a close correlation between economic growth and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>, arguing for qualitative development as an alternative to growth. As a result, the past couple of decades has seen a big shift towards sustainable development as an alternative to neo-liberal economics. There are those, particularly within the alternative globalization movement, who maintain that it is feasible to change to a <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a> phase without losing social efficiency or lowering the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">Consumption</a></i> – The growth of consumption and the cult of consumption, or <a href="/wiki/Consumerist" title="Consumerist">consumerist</a> ideology, is the major cause of economic growth. Overdevelopment, seen as the only alternative to poverty, has become an end in itself. The means for curbing this growth are not equal to the task, since the phenomenon is not confined to a growing middle class in developing countries, but also concerns the development of irresponsible lifestyles, particularly in northern countries, such as the increase in the size and number of homes and cars per person.</p>
<p><i>Destruction of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a></i> – The complexity of the planet’s ecosystems means that the loss of any species has unexpected consequences. The stronger the impact on biodiversity, the stronger the likelihood of a chain reaction with unpredictable negative effects. Another important factor of environmental degradation that falls under this destruction of biodiversity, and must not be ignored is <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. Despite all the damage inflicted, a number of ecosystems have proved to be <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilient</a>. Environmentalists are endorsing a precautionary principle whereby all potentially damaging activities would have to be analyzed for their <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">Population growth</a></i> – Forecasts predict 8.9 billion people on the planet in 2050. This is a subject which primarily affects developing countries, but also concerns northern countries; although their demographic growth is lower, the environmental impact per person is far higher in these countries. Demographic growth needs to be countered by developing education and family planning programs and generally improving women’s status.</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">Pollution</a>" - Pollution caused by the use of fossil fuels is another driver of environmental destruction. The burning of carbon-based fossil fuels such as coal and oil, releases carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. One of the major impacts of this is the climate change that is currently taking place on the planet, where the earth's temperature is gradually rising. Given that fuels such as coal and oil are the most heavily used fuels, this a great concern to many environmentalists.</p>
<p>"Agricultural practices" - Destructive agricultural practices such as overuse of fertilizers and <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a> lead to land degradation. The soil gets <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">eroded</a>, and leads to silting in rivers and reservoirs. Soil erosion is a continuous cycle and ultimately results in <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> of the land. Apart from <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> is also a possibility; chemicals used in farming can <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">run-off</a> into rivers and <a href="/wiki/Water_contamination" class="mw-redirect" title="Water contamination">contaminate the water</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The crisis by the impact of human activities on nature calls for governance. Which includes responses by international institutions, governments and citizens, who should meet this crisis by pooling the experience and knowledge of each of the agents and institutions concerned.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a> measures taken remain insufficient. The necessary reforms require time, energy, money and diplomatic negotiations. The situation has not generated a unanimous response. Persistent divisions slow progress towards global environmental governance.</p>
<p>The global nature of the crisis limits the effects of national or sectoral measures. Cooperation is necessary between actors and institutions in international trade, sustainable development and <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">peace</a>.</p>
<p>Global, continental, national and local governments have employed a variety of approaches to environmental governance. Substantial positive and negative spillovers limit the ability of any single jurisdiction to resolve issues.</p>
<p>Challenges facing environmental governance include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inadequate continental and global agreements</li>
<li>Unresolved tensions between maximum development, sustainable development and maximum protection, limiting funding, damaging links with the economy and limiting application of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Multilateral_Environment_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Multilateral Environment Agreement (page does not exist)">Multilateral Environment Agreements</a> (MEAs).</li>
<li>Environmental funding is not self-sustaining, diverting resources from problem-solving into funding battles.</li>
<li>Lack of integration of sector policies</li>
<li>Inadequate institutional capacities</li>
<li>Ill-defined priorities</li>
<li>Unclear objectives</li>
<li>Lack of coordination within the UN, governments, the private sector and civil society</li>
<li>Lack of shared vision</li>
<li>Interdependencies among development/sustainable economic growth, trade, agriculture, health, peace and security.</li>
<li>International imbalance between environmental governance and trade and finance programs, e.g., <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> (WTO).</li>
<li>Limited credit for organizations running projects within the Global Environment Facility (GEF)</li>
<li>Linking <a href="/wiki/UNEP" class="mw-redirect" title="UNEP">UNEP</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> (UNDP) and the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> with MEAs</li>
<li>Lack of government capacity to satisfy MEA obligations</li>
<li>Absence of the gender perspective and equity in environmental governance</li>
<li>Inability to influence public opinion<sup id="cite_ref-inforesources2005_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inforesources2005-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unep_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Time lag between human action and environmental effect, sometimes as long as a generation<sup id="cite_ref-Underdal.2C_A_2010.2C_pp.386-393_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Underdal.2C_A_2010.2C_pp.386-393-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Environmental problems being embedded in very complex systems, of which our understanding is still quite weak<sup id="cite_ref-Underdal.2C_A_2010.2C_pp.386-393_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Underdal.2C_A_2010.2C_pp.386-393-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>All of these challenges have implications on governance, however international environmental governance is necessary. The IDDRI claims that rejection of <a href="/wiki/Multilateralism" title="Multilateralism">multilateralism</a> in the name of efficiency and protection of national interests conflicts with the promotion of international law and the concept of global public goods. Others cite the complex nature of environmental problems.</p>
<p>On the other hand, The <a href="/wiki/Local_Agenda_21" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> program has been implemented in over 7,000 communities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Environmental problems, including global-scale problems, may not always require global solutions. For example, marine pollution can be tackled regionally, and ecosystem deterioration can be addressed locally. Other global problems such as climate change benefit from local and regional action.</p>
<p>Bäckstrand and Saward wrote, “sustainability and environmental protection is an arena in which innovative experiments with new hybrid, plurilateral forms of governance, along with the incorporation of a transnational civil society spanning the public-private divide, are taking place.”<sup id="cite_ref-Saward_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saward-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_governance">Local governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Local governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 1997 report observed a global consensus that sustainable development implementation should be based on local level solutions and initiatives designed with and by the local communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Leach.2C_M._1997_pp_1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leach.2C_M._1997_pp_1-21">[21]</a></sup> Community participation and partnership along with the decentralisation of government power to local communities are important aspects of environmental governance at the local level. Initiatives such as these are integral divergence from earlier environmental governance approaches which was “driven by state agendas and resource control”<sup id="cite_ref-Leach.2C_M._1997_pp_1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leach.2C_M._1997_pp_1-21">[21]</a></sup> and followed a top-down or trickle down approach rather than the bottom up approach that local level governance encompasses. Local level governance shifts decision making power away from the state and/or governments to the grassroots. Local level governance is extremely important even on a global scale. Environmental governance at the global level is defined as international and as such has resulted in the marginalisation of local voices. Local level governance is important to bring back power to local communities in the global fight against environmental degridation.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57-22">[22]</a></sup> Pulgar Vidal observed a “new institutional framework, [wherein] decision-making regarding access to and use of natural resources has become increasingly decentralized.”<sup id="cite_ref-pv_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pv-23">[23]</a></sup> He noted four techniques that can be used to develop these processes:</p>
<ul>
<li>formal and informal regulations, procedures and processes, such as consultations and participative democracy;</li>
<li>social interaction that can arise from participation in development programs or from the reaction to perceived injustice;</li>
<li>regulating social behaviours to reclassify an individual question as a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Public matter (page does not exist)">public matter</a>;</li>
<li>within-group <a href="/wiki/Participation_(decision_making)" title="Participation (decision making)">participation</a> in decision-making and relations with external actors.</li>
</ul>
<p>He found that the key conditions for developing decentralized environmental governance are:</p>
<ul>
<li>access to <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">social capital</a>, including local knowledge, leaders and local shared vision;</li>
<li>democratic access to information and decision-making;</li>
<li>local government activity in environmental governance: as facilitator of access to natural resources, or as policy maker;</li>
<li>an institutional framework that favours decentralized environmental governance and creates forums for social interaction and making widely accepted agreements acceptable.<sup id="cite_ref-pv_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pv-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The legitimacy of decisions depends on the local population's participation rate and on how well participants represent that population.<sup id="cite_ref-poats_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poats-24">[24]</a></sup> With regard to public authorities, questions linked to biodiversity can be faced by adopting appropriate policies and strategies, through exchange of knowledge and experience, the forming of partnerships, correct management of land use, monitoring of biodiversity and optimal use of resources, or reducing consumption, and promoting environmental certifications, such as EMAS and/or ISO 14001. Local authorities undoubtedly have a central role to play in the protection of biodiversity and this strategy is successful above all when the authorities show strength by involving stakeholders in a credible environmental improvement project and activating a transparent and effective communication policy (Ioppolo et al., 2013).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_governance">State governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: State governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>States play a crucial role in environmental governance, because "however far and fast international economic integration proceeds, political authority remains vested in national governments".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It is for this reason that governments should respect and support the commitment to implementation of international agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the state level, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental management">environmental management</a> has been found to be conducive to the creation of roundtables and committees. In France, the <i>Grenelle de l’environnement</i><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> process:</p>
<ul>
<li>included a variety of actors (e.g. the state, political leaders, unions, businesses, not-for-profit organizations and environmental protection foundations);</li>
<li>allowed stakeholders to interact with the legislative and executive powers in office as indispensable advisors;</li>
<li>worked to integrate other institutions, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Social_Council" class="mw-disambig" title="Economic and Social Council">Economic and Social Council</a>, to form a pressure group that participated in the process for creating an environmental governance model;</li>
<li>attempted to link with environmental management at regional and local levels.</li>
</ul>
<p>If environmental issues are excluded from e.g., the economic agenda, this may delegitimize those institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>“In southern countries, the main obstacle to the integration of intermediate levels in the process of territorial environmental governance development is often the dominance of developmentalist inertia in states’ political mindset. The question of the environment has not been effectively integrated in national development planning and programs. Instead, the most common idea is that environmental protection curbs economic and social development, an idea encouraged by the frenzy for exporting raw materials extracted using destructive methods that consume resources and fail to generate any added value.”<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Of course they are justified in this thinking, as their main concerns are social injustices such as poverty alleviation. Citizens in some of these states have responded by developing empowerment strategies to ease poverty through sustainable development. In addition to this, policymakers must be more aware of these concerns of the global south, and must make sure to integrate a strong focus on social justice in their policies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Governance">Global Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the global level there are numerous important actors involved in environmental governance and "a range of institutions contribute to and help define the practice of global environmental governance.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57-22">[22]</a></sup> The idea of global environmental governance is to govern the environment at a global level through a range of nation states and non state actors such as national governments, NGOs and other international organisations such as UNEP (United Nations Environment Programme). Global environmental governance is the answer to calls for new forms of governance because of the increasing complexity of the international agenda. It is perceived to be an effective form of multilateral management and essential to the international community in meeting goals of mitigation and the possible reversal of the impacts on the global environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58-31">[31]</a></sup> However, a precise definition of global environmental governance is still vague and there are many issues surrounding global governance.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58-31">[31]</a></sup> Elliot argues that “the congested institutional terrain still provides more of an appearance than a reality of comprehensive global governance.”<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58-31">[31]</a></sup> This meant that there are too many institutions within the global governance of the environment for it to be completely inclusive and coherent leaving it merely portraying the image of this to the global public. Global environmental governance is about more than simply expanding networks of institutions and decision makers. “It is a political practice which simultaneously reflects, constitutes and masks global relations of power and powerlessness.”<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58-31">[31]</a></sup> State agendas exploit the use of global environmental governance to enhance their oven agendas or wishes even if this is at the detriment of the vital element behind global environmental governance which is the environment. Elliot states that global environmental governance “is neither normatively neutral nor materially benign.”<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._58-31">[31]</a></sup> As explored by Newell, report notes by The Global Environmental Outlook noted that the systems of global environmental governance are becoming increasingly irrelevant or impotent due to patterns of globalisation such as; imbalances in productivity and the distribution of goods and services, unsustainable progression of extremes of wealth and poverty and population and economic growth overtaking environmental gains.<sup id="cite_ref-Newell.2C_P._2008_pp_508_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newell.2C_P._2008_pp_508-32">[32]</a></sup> Newell states that, despite such acknowledgements, the “managing of global environmental change within International Relations continues to look to international regimes for the answers.”<sup id="cite_ref-Newell.2C_P._2008_pp_508_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newell.2C_P._2008_pp_508-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_of_scale">Issues of scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Issues of scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-tier_governance">Multi-tier governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Multi-tier governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The literature on governance scale shows how changes in the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental issues">environmental issues</a> have led to the movement from a local view to recognising their larger and more complicated scale. This move brought an increase in the diversity, specificity and complexity of initiatives. Meadowcroft pointed out innovations that were layered on top of existing structures and processes, instead of replacing them.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lafferty and Meadowcroft give three examples of multi-tiered governance: <a href="/wiki/Internationalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Internationalisation">internationalisation</a>, increasingly comprehensive approaches, and involvement of multiple governmental entities.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Lafferty and Meadowcroft described the resulting multi-tiered system as addressing issues on both smaller and wider scales.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_fit">Institutional fit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Institutional fit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hans_Bruyninckx" title="Hans Bruyninckx">Hans Bruyninckx</a> claimed that a mismatch between the scale of the environmental problem and the level of the policy intervention was problematic.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Young claimed that such mismatches reduced the effectiveness of interventions.<sup id="cite_ref-young_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-young-36">[36]</a></sup> Most of the literature addresses the level of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> rather than ecological scale.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elinor_Ostrom" title="Elinor Ostrom">Elinor Ostrom</a>, amongst others, claimed that the mismatch is often the cause of unsustainable management practices and that simple solutions to the mismatch have not been identified.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Considerable debate has addressed the question of which level(s) should take responsibility for fresh water management. Development workers tend to address the problem at the local level. National governments focus on policy issues.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This can create conflicts among states because rivers cross borders, leading to efforts to evolve governance of <a href="/wiki/River_basins" class="mw-redirect" title="River basins">river basins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot.2C_L._1956_pp._57-22">[22]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_governance_issues">Environmental governance issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental governance issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Soil_degradation" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil degradation">Soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land deterioration</a> reduces its <a href="/wiki/Field_capacity" title="Field capacity">capacity</a> for capturing, <a href="/wiki/Water_storage" title="Water storage">storing</a> and recycling water, energy and food. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alliance_21&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alliance 21 (page does not exist)">Alliance 21</a> proposed solutions in the following domains:<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The scientific consensus on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> is expressed in the reports of <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) and also in the statements by all major scientific bodies in the United States such as <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The drivers of climate change can include - Changes in <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">solar irradiance</a> - Changes in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">trace gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> concentrations Evidence of climate change can be identified by examining - Atmospheric concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Green_House_Gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Green House Gas">Green House Gases</a> (GHGs) such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO2) - Land and sea surface temperatures - Atmospheric water vapor - <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">Precipitation</a> - The occurrence or strength of extreme weather and climate events - <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">Glaciers</a> - Rapid sea <a href="/wiki/Ice_loss" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice loss">ice loss</a> - Sea level<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is suggested by climate models that the changes in temperature and sea level can be the causal effects of human activities such as <a href="/wiki/Fuel_consumption" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel consumption">consumption</a> of fossil fuels, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, increased <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural production">agricultural production</a> and production of <a href="/wiki/Xenobiotic" title="Xenobiotic">xenobiotic</a> gases.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been increasing actions in order to mitigate climate change and reduce its impact at national, regional and international levels. <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) plays the most important role in addressing climate change at an international level.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The goal of combating climate change led to the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> by 191 states, an agreement encouraging the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>, mainly CO<sub>2</sub>. Since developed economies produce more emissions per capita, limiting emissions in all countries inhibits opportunities for emerging economies, the only major success in efforts to produce a global response to the phenomenon.</p>
<p>Two decades following the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Report</a>, however, there has been no improvement in the key indicators highlighted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental governance for protecting the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> has to act in many levels. Biodiversity is fragile because it is threatened by almost all human actions. To promote conservation of biodiversity, agreements and laws have to be created to regulate agricultural activities, <a href="/wiki/Urban_growth" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban growth">urban growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> of countries, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_use&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Resource use (page does not exist)">use of natural resources</a>, control of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, the correct use of water and protection of <a href="/wiki/Air_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Air quality">air quality</a>. Before making any decision for a region or country decision makers, politicians and community have to take into account what are the potential impacts for biodiversity, that any project can have.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">Population growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> have been a great contributor for deforestation. Also, population growth requires more intense agricultural areas use, which also results in necessity of new areas to be deforested. This causes <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat loss">habitat loss</a>, which is one of the major threats for biodiversity. Habitat loss and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a> affects all species, because they all rely on limited resources, to feed on and to breed.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>‘<i>Species are genetically unique and irreplaceable their loss is irreversible. Ecosystems vary across a vast range of parameters, and similar ecosystems (whether wetlands, forests, coastal reserves etc) cannot be presumed to be interchangeable, such that the loss of one can be compensated by protection or restoration of another</i>’.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>To avoid habitat loss, and consequently biodiversity loss, politicians and lawmakers should be aware of the precautionary principle, which means that before approving a project or law all the pros and cons should be carefully analysed. Sometimes the impacts are not explicit, or not even proved to exist. However, if there is any chance of an irreversible impact happen, it should be taken into consideration.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>To promote environmental governance for biodiversity protection there has to be a clear articulation between values and interests while negotiating environmental management plans.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> International agreements are good way to have it done right.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (CBD) was signed in Rio de Janeiro in 1992 human activities. The CBD’s objectives are: “to conserve biological diversity, to use biological diversity in a sustainable fashion, to share the benefits of biological diversity fairly and equitably.” The Convention is the first global agreement to address all aspects of biodiversity: genetic resources, species and ecosystems. It recognizes, for the first time, that the conservation of biological diversity is “a common concern for all humanity”. The Convention encourages joint efforts on measures for scientific and technological cooperation, access to genetic resources and the transfer of clean environmental technologies.</p>
<p>The Convention on Biological Diversity most important edition happened in 2010 when the Strategic Plan for Biodiversity 2011-2020 and the Aichi Targets, were launched. These two projects together make the United Nations decade on Biodiversity. It was held in Japan and has the targets of ‘<i>halting and eventually reversing the loss of biodiversity of the planet</i>’.<sup id="cite_ref-cbd.int_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbd.int-50">[50]</a></sup> The Strategic Plan for Biodiversity has the goal to ‘<i>promote its overall vision of living in harmony with nature</i>’ As result (...) ‘<i>mainstream biodiversity at different levels. Throughout the United Nations Decade on Biodiversity, governments are encouraged to develop, implement and communicate the results of national strategies for implementation of the Strategic Plan for Biodiversity</i>’.<sup id="cite_ref-cbd.int_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbd.int-50">[50]</a></sup> According to the CBD <sup id="cite_ref-cbd.int_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbd.int-50">[50]</a></sup> the five Aichi targets are:</p>
<ol>
<li>‘<i>Address the underlying causes of biodiversity loss by mainstreaming biodiversity across government and society</i>;</li>
<li><i>Reduce the direct pressures on biodiversity and promote sustainable use</i>;</li>
<li><i>Improve the status of biodiversity by safeguarding ecosystems, species and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a></i>;</li>
<li><i>Enhance the benefits to all from biodiversity and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a></i>;</li>
<li><i>Enhance implementation through <a href="/wiki/Participatory_planning" title="Participatory planning">participatory planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">capacity building</a></i>.’</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2003 <a href="/wiki/UN_World_Water_Development_Report" title="UN World Water Development Report">UN World Water Development Report</a> claimed that the amount of water available over the next twenty years would drop by 30%.</p>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" width="42" height="34" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="290" /></div>

<p>At that time, 40% of the planet’s inhabitants did not have access to the minimum necessary for basic hygiene. Over 2.2 million people died in 2000 from diseases linked to contaminated water, or from drowning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="drowning? (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2004, the UK’s <a href="/wiki/WaterAid" title="WaterAid">WaterAid</a> charity reported that one child died every 15 seconds from water-linked diseases.</p>
<p>According to Alliance 21<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> “All levels of water supply management are necessary and independent. The integrated approach to the catchment areas must take into account the needs of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and those of towns, jointly and not separately as is often seen to be the case....The governance of a water supply must be guided by the principles of sustainable development.”</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a> have always been variable but they are becoming increasingly so with changing climate conditions. Because of how limited water resources are in Australia, there needs to be an effective implementation of environmental governance conducted within the country. Water restrictions are an important policy device used in Australian environmental governance to limit the amount of water used in urban and agricultural environments (Beeton et al. 2006). There is increased pressure on surface water resources in Australia because of the uncontrolled growth in <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> use and the constant threat of drought. These increased pressures not only affect the quantity and quality of the waterways but they also negatively affect biodiversity. The government needs to create policies that preserve, protect and monitor Australia’s inland water. The most significant environmental governance policy imposed by the Australian government is environmental flow allocations that allocate water to the natural environment. The proper implementation of water trading systems could help to conserve water resources in Australia. Over the years there has been an increase in demand for water, making Australia the third largest per capita user of water in the world (Beeton et al. 2006). If this trend continues, the gap between supply and demand will need to be addressed. The government needs to implement more efficient water allocations and raise water rates (UNEP, 2014). By changing public perception to promote the action of reusing and recycling water some of the stress of water shortages can be alleviated. More extensive solutions like desalination plants, building more dams and using aquifer storage are all options that could be taken to conserve water levels but all these methods are controversial. With caps on surface water use, both urban and rural consumers are turning to groundwater use; this has caused groundwater levels to decline significantly. Groundwater use is very hard to monitor and regulate. There is not enough research currently being conducted to accurately determine sustainable yields. Some regions are seeing improvement in groundwater levels by applying caps on bores and the amount of water that consumers are allowed to extract. There have been projects in environmental governance aimed at restoring vegetation in the riparian zone. Restoring riparian vegetation helps increase biodiversity, reduce salinity, prevent soil erosion and prevent riverbank collapse. Many rivers and waterways are controlled by weirs and locks that control the flow of rivers and also prevent the movement of fish. The government has funded fish-ways on some weirs and locks to allow for native fish to move upstream. Wetlands have significantly suffered under restricted water resources with water bird numbers dropping and a decrease in species diversity. The allocation of water for bird breeding through environmental flows in <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Marshes" title="Macquarie Marshes">Macquarie Marshes</a> has led to an increase in breeding (Beeton et al. 2006). Because of dry land salinity throughout Australia there has been an increase in the levels of salt in Australian waterways. There has been funding in salt interception schemes which help to improve in-stream salinity levels but whether river salinity has improved or not is still unclear because there is not enough data available yet. High salinity levels are dangerous because they can negatively affect larval and juvenile stages of certain fish. The introduction of invasive species into waterways has negatively affected native aquatic species because invasive species compete with native species and alter natural habitats. There has been research in producing <a href="/wiki/Daughterless_carp" class="mw-redirect" title="Daughterless carp">daughterless carp</a> to help eradicate carp. Government funding has also gone into building in-stream barriers that trap the carp and prevent them from moving into floodplains and wetlands. Investment in national and regional programmes like the Living Murray (MDBC), Healthy Waterways Partnership and the Clean Up the Swan Programme are leading to important environmental governance. The Healthy Rivers programme promotes restoration and recovery of environmental flows, riparian re-vegetation and aquatic pest control. The Living Murray programme has been crucial for the allocation of water to the environment by creating an agreement to recover 500 billion litres of water to the Murray River environment. Environmental governance and water resource management in Australia must be constantly monitored and adapted to suit the changing environmental conditions within the country (Beeton et al. 2006). If environmental programmes are governed with transparency there can be a reduction in policy fragmentation and an increase in policy efficiency (Mclntyre, 2010). <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozone_layer">Ozone layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ozone layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 16 September 1987 the United Nations General Assembly signed the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> to address the declining <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. Since that time, the use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (industrial refrigerants and aerosols) and farming fungicides such as <a href="/wiki/Methyl_bromide" class="mw-redirect" title="Methyl bromide">methyl bromide</a> has mostly been eliminated, although other damaging gases are still in use.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_risk">Nuclear risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nuclear risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_non-proliferation_treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear non-proliferation treaty">Nuclear non-proliferation treaty</a> is the primary multilateral agreement governing nuclear activity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transgenic_organisms">Transgenic organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transgenic organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified organisms">Genetically modified organisms</a> are not the subject of any major multilateral agreements. They are the subject of various restrictions at other levels of governance. GMOs are in widespread use in the US, but are heavily restricted in many other jurisdictions.</p>
<p>Controversies have ensued over <a href="/wiki/Golden_rice" title="Golden rice">golden rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/AquAdvantage_salmon" title="AquAdvantage salmon">genetically modified salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monsanto#Seeds" title="Monsanto">genetically modified seeds</a>, disclosure and other topics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautionary_principle">Precautionary principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Precautionary principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a> or precautionary approach states that if an action or policy has a suspected risk of causing harm to the public or to the environment, in the absence of scientific consensus that the action or policy is harmful, the burden of proof that it is not harmful falls on those taking an action. As of 2013 it was not the basis of major multilateral agreements. The Precautionary Principle is put into effect if there is a chance that proposed action may cause harm to the society or the environment. Therefore, those involved in the proposed action must provide evidence that it will not be harmful, even if scientists do not believe that it will cause harm. It falls upon the policymakers to make the optimal decision, if there is any risk, even without any credible scientific evidence. However, taking precautionary action also means that there is an element of cost involved, either social or economic. So if the cost was seen as insignificant the action would be taken without the implementation of the precautionary principle.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> But often the cost is ignored, which can lead to harmful repercussions. This is often the case with industry and scientists who are primarily concerned with protecting their own interests.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agreements">Agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventions">Conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main multilateral conventions, also known as Rio Conventions, are as follows:</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (CBD) (1992–1993): aims to conserve biodiversity. Related agreements include the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cbd.int/doc/legal/cartagena-protocol-en.pdf">Cartagena Protocol</a> on biosafety.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCC) (1992–1994): aims to stabilize concentrations of greenhouse gases at a level that would <a href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">stabilize the climate system</a> without threatening food production, and enabling the pursuit of sustainable economic development; it incorporates the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_to_Combat_Desertification" title="United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification">United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification</a> (UNCCD) (1994–1996): aims to combat desertification and mitigate the effects of drought and desertification, particularly in Africa.</p>
<p>Further conventions:</p>
<ul>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ramsar.org">Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance</a> (1971–1975)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://whc.unesco.org">UNESCO World Heritage Convention</a> (1972–1975)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cites.org">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Flora and Fauna (CITES)</a> (1973–1975)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cms.int">Bonn Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species</a> (1979–1983)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.unece.org/env/water">Convention on the Protection and Use of Transboundary Watercourses and International Lakes (Water Convention)</a> (1992–1996)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.basel.int">Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal</a> (1989–1992)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pic.int">Rotterdam Convention on the Prior Informed Consent Procedures for Certain Hazardous Chemicals and Pesticides in International Trade</a></li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pops.int">Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants (COP)</a> (2001–2004)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Rio Conventions are characterized by:</p>
<ul>
<li>obligatory execution by signatory states</li>
<li>involvement in a sector of global environmental governance</li>
<li>focus on the fighting poverty and the development of sustainable living conditions;</li>
<li>funding from the Global Environment Facility (GEF) for countries with few financial resources;</li>
<li>inclusion of a for assessing ecosystem status<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Environmental conventions are regularly criticized for their:</p>
<ul>
<li>rigidity and verticality: they are too descriptive, homogenous and top down, not reflecting the diversity and complexity of environmental issues. Signatory countries struggle to translate objectives into concrete form and incorporate them consistently;</li>
<li>duplicate structures and aid: the sector-specific format of the conventions produced duplicate structures and procedures. Inadequate cooperation between government ministries;</li>
<li>contradictions and incompatibility: e.g., “if reforestation projects to reduce CO<sub>2</sub> give preference to monocultures of exotic species, this can have a negative impact on biodiversity (whereas natural regeneration can strengthen both biodiversity and the conditions needed for life).”<sup id="cite_ref-inforesources2005_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inforesources2005-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Until now, the formulation of environmental policies at the international level has been divided by theme, sector or territory, resulting in treaties that overlap or clash. International attempts to coordinate environment institutions, include the Inter-Agency Coordination Committee and the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Sustainable_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Commission for Sustainable Development">Commission for Sustainable Development</a>, but these institutions are not powerful enough to effectively incorporate the three aspects of sustainable development.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKanieHaas2004_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKanieHaas2004-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multilateral_Environmental_Agreements_.28MEAs.29">Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MEAs are agreements between several countries that apply internationally or regionally and concern a variety of environmental questions. As of 2013 over 500 Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs), including 45 of global scope involve at least 72 signatory countries.<sup id="cite_ref-inomata_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inomata-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Further agreements cover regional environmental problems, such as deforestation in Borneo or pollution in the Mediterranean. Each agreement has a specific mission and objectives ratified by multiple states.</p>
<p>Many Multilateral Environmental Agreements have been negotiated with the support from the United Nations Environmental Programme and work towards the achievement of the United Nations Millennium Development Goals as a means to instil sustainable practices for the environment and its people.<sup id="cite_ref-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136-62">[62]</a></sup> Multilateral Environmental Agreements are considered to present enormous opportunities for greener societies and economies which can deliver numerous benefits in addressing food, energy and water security and in achieving sustainable development.<sup id="cite_ref-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136-62">[62]</a></sup> These agreements can be implemented on a global or regional scale, for example the issues surrounding the disposal of hazardous waste can be implemented on a regional level as per the Bamako Convention on the Ban of the Import into Africa and the Control of Transboundary Movement and Management of Hazardous Waste within Africa which applies specifically to Africa, or the global approach to hazardous waste such as the Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal which is monitored throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steiner.2C_A_2010_pp._1-136-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>“The environmental governance structure defined by the Rio and Johannesburg Summits is sustained by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://en.coredem.info/wiki/Environmental_governance#International_institutions">UNEP</a>, MEAs and developmental organizations and consists of assessment and policy development, as well as project implementation at the country level.</p>
<p>"The governance structure consists of a chain of phases:</p>
<ul>
<li>a) assessment of environment status;</li>
<li>b) international policy development;</li>
<li>c) formulation of MEAs;</li>
<li>d) policy implementation;</li>
<li>e) policy assessment;</li>
<li>f) enforcement;</li>
<li>g) sustainable development.</li>
</ul>
<p>"Traditionally, UNEP has focused on the normative role of engagement in the first three phases. Phases (d) to (f) are covered by MEAs and the sustainable development phase involves developmental organizations such as UNDP and the World Bank.”<sup id="cite_ref-inomata_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inomata-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lack of coordination affects the development of coherent governance. The report shows that donor states support development organizations, according to their individual interests. They do not follow a joint plan, resulting in overlaps and duplication. MEAs tend not to become a joint frame of reference and therefore receive little financial support. States and organizations emphasize existing regulations rather than improving and adapting them.<sup id="cite_ref-inomata_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inomata-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The risks associated with nuclear fission raised global awareness of environmental threats. The 1963 <a href="/wiki/Partial_Nuclear_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty">Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty</a> prohibiting atmospheric nuclear testing was the beginning of the globalization of environmental issues. Environmental law began to be modernized and coordinated with the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockholm Conference">Stockholm Conference</a> (1972), backed up in 1980 by the Vienna Convention on the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_treaties" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of treaties">Law of Treaties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer</a> was signed and ratified in 1985. In 1987, 24 countries signed the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> which imposed the gradual withdrawal of CFCs.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Report</a>, published in 1987 by the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">UN Commission on Environment and Development</a>, stipulated the need for economic development that “meets the needs of the present without compromising the capacity of future generations to meet their needs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rio_Conference_.281992.29_and_reactions">Rio Conference (1992) and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rio Conference (1992) and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED), better known as the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a>, was the first major international meeting since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and was attended by delegations from 175 countries. Since then the biggest international conferences that take place every 10 years guided the global governance process with a series of MEAs. Environmental treaties are applied with the help of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Secretariats&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Secretariats (page does not exist)">secretariats</a>.</p>
<p>Governments created international treaties in the 1990s to check global threats to the environment. These treaties are far more restrictive than global protocols and set out to change non-sustainable production and consumption models.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agenda_21">Agenda 21</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Agenda 21">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> is a detailed plan of actions to be implemented at the global, national and local levels by UN organizations, member states and key individual groups in all regions. Agenda 21 advocates making sustainable development a legal principle law. At the local level, local Agenda 21 advocates an inclusive, territory-based strategic plan, incorporating sustainable environmental and social policies.</p>
<p>The Agenda has been accused of using <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> principles, including free trade to achieve environmental goals. For example, chapter two, entitled “International Cooperation to Accelerate Sustainable Development in Developing Countries and Related Domestic Policies” states, “The international economy should provide a supportive international climate for achieving environment and development goals by: promoting sustainable development through trade liberalization.”</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Actors">Actors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Actors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_institutions">International institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: International institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Environment_Program">United Nations Environment Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: United Nations Environment Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Environment Program">United Nations Environment Program</a></div>
<p>The UNEP has had its biggest impact as a monitoring and advisory body, and in developing environmental agreements. It has also contributed to strengthening the institutional capacity of environment ministries.</p>
<p>In 2002 UNEP held a conference to focus on product lifecycle impacts, emphasizing the fashion, advertising, financial and retail industries, seen as key agents in promoting sustainable consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Ivanova, UNEP adds value in environmental monitoring, scientific assessment and information sharing, but cannot lead all environmental management processes. She proposed the following tasks for UNEP:</p>
<ul>
<li>initiate a strategic independent overhaul of its mission;</li>
<li>consolidate the financial information and transparency process;</li>
<li>restructure organizing governance by creating an operative executive council that balances the omnipresence of the overly imposing and fairly ineffectual Governing Council/Global Ministerial Environment Forum (GMEF).</li>
</ul>
<p>Other proposals offer a new mandate to “produce greater unity amongst social and environmental agencies, so that the concept of ‘environment for development’ becomes a reality. It needs to act as a platform for establishing standards and for other types of interaction with national and international organizations and the United Nations. The principles of cooperation and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.amisdelaterre.org/Des-responsabilites-communes-mais.html">common but differentiated responsibilities</a> should be reflected in the application of this revised mandate.”<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sherman proposed principles to strengthen UNEP:</p>
<ul>
<li>obtain a social consensus on a long-term vision;</li>
<li>analyze the current situation and future scenarios;</li>
<li>produce a comprehensive plan covering all aspects of sustainable development;</li>
<li>build on existing strategies and processes;</li>
<li>multiply links between national and local strategies;</li>
<li>include all these points in the financial and budget plan;</li>
<li>adopt fast controls to improve process piloting and identification of progress made;</li>
<li>implement effective participation mechanisms.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Another group stated, “Consider the specific needs of developing countries and respect of the fundamental principle of 'common but differentiated responsibilities'. Developed countries should promote technology transfer, new and additional financial resources, and capacity building for meaningful participation of developing countries in international environmental governance. Strengthening of international environmental governance should occur in the context of sustainable development and should involve civil society as an important stakeholder and agent of transformation.”<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Environment_Facility_.28GEF.29">Global Environment Facility (GEF)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Global Environment Facility (GEF)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_Environment_Facility" title="Global Environment Facility">Global Environment Facility</a></div>
<p>Created in 1991, the Global Environment Facility is an independent financial organization initiated by donor governments including Germany and France. It was the first financial organization dedicated to the environment at the global level. As of 2013 it had 179 members. Donations are used for projects covering biodiversity, climate change, international waters, destruction of the ozone layer, soil degradation and <a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">persistent organic pollutants</a>.</p>
<p>GEF's institutional structure includes UNEP, UNDP and the World Bank. It is the funding mechanism for the four environmental conventions: climate change, biodiversity, persistent organic pollutants and desertification. GEF transfers resources from developed countries to developing countries to fund UNDP, UNEP and World Bank projects. The World Bank manages the annual budget of US$561.10 million.<sup id="cite_ref-young2002_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-young2002-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The GEF has been criticized for its historic links with the World Bank, at least during its first phase during the 1990s,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> and for having favoured certain regions to the detriment of others.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Another view sees it as contributing to the emergence of a global "green market". It represents “an adaptation (of the World Bank) to this emerging world order, as a response to the emergence of environmental movements that are becoming a geopolitical force.”<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.AB20023_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.AB20023-73">[73]</a></sup> Developing countries demanded financial transfers to help them protect their environment.</p>
<p>GEF is subject to economic profitability criteria, as is the case for all the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://en.coredem.info/wiki/Environmental_governance#Conventions">conventions</a>. It received more funds in its first three years than the UNEP has since its creation in 1972. GEF funding represents less than 1% of development aid between 1992 and 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.AB20023_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZo.C3.AB20023-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Commission_on_Sustainable_Development_.28CSD.29">United Nations Commission on Sustainable Development (CSD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: United Nations Commission on Sustainable Development (CSD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Sustainable_Development" title="Commission on Sustainable Development">Commission on Sustainable Development</a></div>
<p>This intergovernmental institution meets twice a year to assess follow-up on Rio Summit goals. The CSD is made up of 53 member states, elected every three years and was reformed in 2004 to help improve implementation of Agenda 21. It meets twice a year, focusing on a specific theme during each two-year period: 2004-2005 was dedicated to water and 2006-2007 to climate change. The CSD has been criticized for its low impact, general lack of presence and the absence of Agenda 21 at the state level specifically, according to a report by the <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Its mission focuses on sequencing actions and establishing agreements puts it in conflict with institutions such as UNEP and OECD.<sup id="cite_ref-Najam_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Najam-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Environment_Organization_.28WEO.29">World Environment Organization (WEO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: World Environment Organization (WEO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Organization" title="United Nations Environment Organization">United Nations Environment Organization</a></div>
<p>A proposed World Environment Organization, analogous to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> could be capable of adapting treaties and enforcing international standards.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>The European Union, particularly France and Germany, and a number of NGOs favour creating a WEO. The United Kingdom, the US and most developing countries prefer to focus on voluntary initiatives.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> WEO partisans maintain that it could offer better political leadership, improved legitimacy and more efficient coordination. Its detractors argue that existing institutions and missions already provide appropriate environmental governance; however the lack of coherence and coordination between them and the absence of clear division of responsibilities prevents them from greater effectiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKanieHaas2004_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKanieHaas2004-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unep_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Bank">World Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: World Bank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a></div>
<p>The World Bank influences environmental governance through other actors, particularly the GEF. The World Bank’s mandate is not sufficiently defined in terms of environmental governance despite the fact that it is included in its mission. However, it allocates 5 to 10% of its annual funds to environmental projects. The institution's capitalist vocation means that its investment is concentrated solely in areas which are profitable in terms of cost benefits, such as climate change action and ozone layer protection, whilst neglecting other such as adapting to climate change and desertification. Its financial autonomy means that it can make its influence felt indirectly on the creation of standards, and on international and regional negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following intense criticism in the 1980s for its support for destructive projects which, amongst other consequences, caused deforestation of tropical forests, the World Bank drew up its own environment-related standards in the 1990s so it could correct its actions. These standards differ from UNEP’s standards, meant to be the benchmark, thus discrediting the institution and sowing disorder and conflict in the world of environmental governance. Other financial institutions, regional development banks and the private sector also drew up their own standards. Criticism is not directed at the World Bank’s standards in themselves, which Najam considered as “robust”,<sup id="cite_ref-Najam_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Najam-75">[75]</a></sup> but at their legitimacy and efficacy.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="GEF">GEF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: GEF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Global_Environment_Facility" title="Global Environment Facility">GEF</a>'s account of itself as of 2012 <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.thegef.org/gef/whatisgef">[11]</a> is as "the largest public funder of projects to improve the global environment", period, which "provides grants for projects related to biodiversity, climate change, international waters, land degradation, the ozone layer, and persistent organic pollutants." It claims to have provided "$10.5 billion in grants and leveraging $51 billion in co-financing for over 2,700 projects in over 165 countries [and] made more than 14,000 small grants directly to civil society and community-based organizations, totaling $634 million." It serves as mechanism for the:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (CBD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention_on_Persistent_Organic_Pollutants" title="Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants">Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants</a> (POPs)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convention_to_Combat_Desertification" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention to Combat Desertification">Convention to Combat Desertification</a> (UNCCD)</li>
<li>implementation of <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol_on_Substances_That_Deplete_the_Ozone_Layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Montreal Protocol on Substances That Deplete the Ozone Layer">Montreal Protocol on Substances That Deplete the Ozone Layer</a> in some countries with "economies in transition" <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.thegef.org/gef/whatisgef">[12]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>This mandate reflects the restructured GEF as of October 2011 <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.thegef.org/gef/instrument">[13]</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Trade_Organization_.28WTO.29">World Trade Organization (WTO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: World Trade Organization (WTO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a></div>
<p>The WTO’s mandate does not include a specific principle on the environment. All the problems linked to the environment are treated in such a way as to give priority to trade requirements and the principles of the WTO’s own trade system. This produces conflictual situations. Even if the WTO recognizes the existence of MEAs, it denounces the fact that around 20 MEAs are in conflict with the WTO’s trade regulations. Furthermore, certain MEAs can allow a country to ban or limit trade in certain products if they do not satisfy established environmental protection requirements. In these circumstances, if one country’s ban relating to another country concerns two signatories of the same MEA, the principles of the treaty can be used to resolve the disagreement, whereas if the country affected by the trade ban with another country has not signed the agreement, the WTO demands that the dispute be resolved using the WTO’s trade principles, in other words, without taking into account the environmental consequences.</p>

In a recently dispute over labelling of <a href="/wiki/Dolphin_safe_label" title="Dolphin safe label">dolphin safe labels</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> between the US and Mexico, the ruling was relatively narrow and did not, as some critics claimed,</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Monetary_Fund_.28IMF.29">International Monetary Fund (IMF)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: International Monetary Fund (IMF)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a></div>
<p>The IMF’s mission is to encourage growth and development. The IMF advocates reduced public expenditure, increased exports and foreign investment. The environment is not a priority for the IMF, leading it to favor projects that may have negative environmental effects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The IMF Green Fund proposal of <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Strauss-Kahn" title="Dominique Strauss-Kahn">Dominique Strauss-Kahn</a><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> specifically to address "climate-related shocks in Africa",<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> despite receiving serious attention<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> was rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-82">[82]</a></sup> Strauss-Kahn's proposal, backed by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, was that "developed countries would make an initial capital injection into the fund using some of the $176 billion worth of SDR allocations from last year in exchange for a stake in the green fund." However, "most of the 24 directors ... told Strauss-Kahn that climate was not part of the IMF's mandate and that SDR allocations are a reserve asset never intended for development issues."<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="UN_ICLEI">UN ICLEI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: UN ICLEI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_Local_Environmental_Initiatives" class="mw-redirect" title="International Council for Local Environmental Initiatives">International Council for Local Environmental Initiatives</a></div>
<p>The UN's main body for coordinating municipal and urban decision-making<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> is named the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_Local_Environmental_Initiatives" class="mw-redirect" title="International Council for Local Environmental Initiatives">International Council for Local Environmental Initiatives</a>. Its slogan is "Local Governments for Sustainability". This body sponsored the concept of <a href="/wiki/Full_cost_accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="Full cost accounting">full cost accounting</a> that makes environmental governance the foundation of other governance.</p>

<ul>

</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Global trade regimes, credits and banking reserve rules be reformed to advance debt relief and incentives to implement polices and practices that reduce and mitigate climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Starting national councils to implement this and other key agreements, e.g., <a href="/wiki/ICLEI_Local_Governments_for_Sustainability_USA" class="mw-redirect" title="ICLEI Local Governments for Sustainability USA">ICLEI Local Governments for Sustainability USA</a></li>
<li>Spreading <a href="/w/index.php?title=EcoBudget&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EcoBudget (page does not exist)">ecoBudget</a> (2008)<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Triple_Bottom_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Triple Bottom Line">Triple Bottom Line</a> (2007) "tools for embedding <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> into council operations",<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> e.g. <a href="/wiki/Guntur" title="Guntur">Guntur</a>'s Municipal Corporation, one of the first four to ipmlement the entire framework.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_Planning_Toolkit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sustainability Planning Toolkit (page does not exist)">Sustainability Planning Toolkit</a> (launched 2009)<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> integrating these and other tools</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cities_Climate_Registry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cities Climate Registry (page does not exist)">Cities Climate Registry</a> (launched 2010)<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> - part of UNEP <a href="/w/index.php?title=Campaign_on_Cities_and_Climate_Change&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Campaign on Cities and Climate Change (page does not exist)">Campaign on Cities and Climate Change</a><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_secretariats">Other secretariats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Other secretariats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other international institutions incorporate environmental governance in their action plans, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> (UNDP), promoting development;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) which works on the climate and atmosphere;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a> (FAO) working on the protection of agriculture, forests and fishing;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) which focuses on nuclear security.</li>
</ul>
<p>Over 30 UN agencies and programmes support environmental management, according to Najam.<sup id="cite_ref-Najam_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Najam-75">[75]</a></sup> This produces a lack of coordination, insufficient exchange of information and dispersion of responsibilities. It also results in proliferation of initiatives and rivalry between them.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to Bauer, Busch and Siebenhüner,<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> the different conventions and multilateral agreements of global environmental regulation is increasing their secretariats' influence. Influence varies according to bureaucratic and leadership efficiency, choice of technical or client-centered.</p>
<p>The United Nations is often the target of criticism, including from within over the multiplication of secretariats due to the chaos it produces. Using a separate secretariat for each MEA creates enormous overhead given the 45 international-scale and over 500 other agreements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="States">States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_governance_at_the_state_level">Environmental governance at the state level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Environmental governance at the state level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Environmental protection has created opportunities for mutual and collective monitoring among neighbouring states. The European Union provides an example of the institutionalization of joint regional and state environmental governance. Key areas include information, led by the <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> (EEA), and the production and monitoring of norms by states or local institutions. See also the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_of_the_European_Union" title="Environmental policy of the European Union">Environmental policy of the European Union</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="State_participation_in_global_environmental_governance">State participation in global environmental governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: State participation in global environmental governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>US refusal to ratify major environment agreements produced tensions with ratifiers in Europe and Japan.</p>
<p>The World Bank, IMF and other institutions are dominated by the developed countries and do not always properly consider the requirements of developing countries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental governance applies to business as well as government. Considerations are typical of those in other domains:</p>
<ul>
<li>values (vision, mission, principles);</li>
<li>policy (strategy, objectives, targets);</li>
<li>oversight (responsibility, direction, training, communication);</li>
<li>process (management systems, initiatives, internal control, monitoring and review, stakeholder dialogue, transparency, environmental accounting, reporting and verification);</li>
<li>performance (performance indicators, benchmarking, eco-efficiency, reputation, compliance, liabilities, business development).<sup id="cite_ref-white_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-91">[91]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>White and Klernan among others discuss the correlation between environmental governance and financial performance. This correlation is higher in sectors where environmental impacts are greater.<sup id="cite_ref-white_91-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Business environmental issues include emissions, biodiversity, historical liabilities, product and material waste/recycling, energy use/supply and many others.<sup id="cite_ref-white_91-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-white-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Environmental governance has become linked to traditional corporate governance as an increasing number of shareholders are corporate environmental impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Corporate governance is the set of <a href="/wiki/Business_process" title="Business process">processes</a>, customs, <a href="/wiki/Policies" class="mw-redirect" title="Policies">policies</a>, laws, and institutions affecting the way a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a>) is managed. Corporate <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> is affected by the relationships among <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>. These stakeholders research and quantify performance to compare and contrast the environmental performance of thousands of companies.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large corporations with global supply chains evaluate the environmental performance of business partners and suppliers for marketing and ethical reasons. Some consumers seek environmentally friendly and sustainable products and companies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-governmental_organizations">Non-governmental organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Non-governmental organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-governmental organizations">Non-governmental organizations</a></div>
<p>According to Bäckstrand and Saward,<sup id="cite_ref-Saward_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saward-20">[20]</a></sup> “broader participation by non-state actors in multilateral environmental decisions (in varied roles such as agenda setting, campaigning, lobbying, consultation, monitoring, and implementation) enhances the democratic legitimacy of environmental governance.”</p>
<p>Local activism is capable of gaining the support of the people and authorities to combat environmental degradatation. In Cotacachi, Ecuador, a social movement used a combination of education, direct action, the influence of local public authorities and denunciation of the mining company’s plans in its own country, Canada, and the support of international environmental groups to influence mining activity.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fisher cites cases in which multiple strategies were used to effect change.<sup id="cite_ref-fisher_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-95">[95]</a></sup> She describes civil society groups that pressure international institutions and also organize local events. Local groups can take responsibility for environmental governance in place of governments.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Bengoa,<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> “social movements have contributed decisively to the creation of an institutional platform wherein the fight against poverty and exclusion has become an inescapable benchmark.” But despite successes in this area, “these institutional changes have not produced the processes for transformation that could have made substantial changes to the opportunities available to rural inhabitants, particularly the poorest and those excluded from society.” He cites several reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li>conflict between in-group cohesion and openness to outside influence;</li>
<li>limited trust between individuals;</li>
<li>contradiction between social participation and innovation;</li>
<li>criticisms without credible alternatives to environmentally damaging activities</li>
</ul>
<p>A successful initiative in Ecuador involved the establishment of stakeholder federations and management committees (NGOs, communities, municipalities and the ministry) for the management of a protected forest.<sup id="cite_ref-poats_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poats-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposals">Proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Institute_for_Sustainable_Development" title="International Institute for Sustainable Development">International Institute for Sustainable Development</a> proposed an agenda for global governance. These objectives are:<sup id="cite_ref-Najam_75-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Najam-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>expert leadership;</li>
<li>positioning science as the authoritative basis of sound environmental policy;</li>
<li>coherence and reasonable coordination;</li>
<li>well-managed institutions;</li>
<li>incorporate environmental concerns and actions within other areas of international policy and action</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coherence_and_coordination">Coherence and coordination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Coherence and coordination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the increase in efforts, actors, agreements and treaties, the global environment continue to degrade at a rapid rate. From the big hole in Earth’s ozone layer to over-fishing to the uncertainties of climate change, the world is confronted by several intrinsically global challenges.<sup id="cite_ref-unep.org_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep.org-99">[99]</a></sup> However, as the environmental agenda becomes more complicated and extensive, the current system has proven ineffective in addressing and tackling problems related to trans-boundary externalities and the environment is still experiencing degradation at unprecedented levels.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inforesources<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> identifies four major obstacles to global environmental governance, and describes measures in response. The four obstacles are:</p>
<ul>
<li>parallel structures and competition, without a coherent strategy</li>
<li>contradictions and incompatibilities, without appropriate compromise</li>
<li>competition between multiple agreements with incompatible objectives, regulations and processes</li>
<li>integrating policy from macro- to micro- scales.</li>
</ul>
<p>Recommended measures:</p>
<ul>
<li>MDGs (Milenium Development Goals)<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and conventions, combining sustainability and reduction of poverty and equity;</li>
<li>country-level approach linking global and local scales</li>
<li>coordination and division of tasks in a multilateral approach that supports developing countries and improves coordination between donor countries and institutions</li>
<li>use of Poverty Reduction Strategy Papers (PRSPs)<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> in development planning</li>
<li>transform conflicts into tradeoffs, synergies and win-win options</li>
</ul>
<p>Contemporary debates surrounding global environmental governance have converged on the idea of developing a stronger and more effective institutional framework. The views on how to achieve this, however, still hotly debated. Currently, rather than teaming up with the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), international environmental responsibilities have been spread across many different agencies including: a) specialised agencies within the UN system such as the World Meteorological Organisation, the International Maritime Organisation and others; b) the programs in the UN system such as the UN Development Program; c) the UN regional economic and social commission; d) the Bretton Woods institutions; e) the World Trade Organisation and; f) the environmentally focused mechanisms such as the Global Environment Facility and close to 500 international environmental agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-unep.org_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unep.org-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some analysts also argue that multiple institutions and some degree of overlap and duplication in policies is necessary to ensure maximum output from the system.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Others, however, claim that institutions have become too dispersed and lacking in coordination which can be damaging to their effectiveness in global environmental governance.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Whilst there are various arguments for and against a WEO, the key challenge, however, remains the same: how to develop a rational and effective framework that will protect the global environment efficiently.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Democratization">Democratization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Democratization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in 2002, Saward<sup id="cite_ref-Saward_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saward-20">[20]</a></sup> and others began to view the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a> process as capable opening up the possibility of stakeholder democracy. The summits were deliberative rather than simply participative, with NGOs, women, men, indigenous peoples and businesses joining the decision-making process alongside states and international organizations, characterized by:</p>
<ul>
<li>the importance given to scientific and technical considerations</li>
<li>the official and unofficial participation of many actors with heterogeneous activity scopes</li>
<li>growing uncertainty</li>
<li>a new interpretation of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> and social organization models<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>As of 2013, the absence of joint rules for composing such fora leads to the development of non-transparent relations that favour the more powerful stakeholders. Criticisms<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> assert that they act more as a lobbying platform, wherein specific interest groups attempt to influence governments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_reform">Institutional reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Institutional reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Actors inside and outside the United Nations are discussing possibilities for global environmental governance that provides a solution to current problems of fragility, coordination and coherence.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> Deliberation is focusing on the goal of making UNEP more efficient. A 2005 resolution recognizes “the need for more efficient environmental activities in the United Nations system, with enhanced coordination, improved policy advice and guidance, strengthened scientific knowledge, assessment and cooperation, better treaty compliance, while respecting the legal autonomy of the treaties, and better integration of environmental activities in the broader sustainable development framework.”</p>
<p>Proposals include:<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>greater and better coordination between agencies;</li>
<li>strengthen and acknowledge UNEP’s scientific role;</li>
<li>identify MEA areas to strengthen coordination, cooperation and teamwork between different agreements;</li>
<li>increase regional presence;</li>
<li>implement the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bali_Strategic_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bali Strategic Plan (page does not exist)">Bali Strategic Plan</a> on improving technology training and support for the application of environmental measures in poor countries;</li>
<li>demand that UNEP and MEAs participate formally in all relevant WTO committees as observers.</li>
<li>strengthen its financial situation;</li>
<li>improve secretariats’ efficiency and effectiveness.</li>
</ul>
<p>One of the main studies addressing this issue proposes:</p>
<ul>
<li>clearly divide tasks between development organizations, UNEP and the MEAs</li>
<li>adopt a political direction<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for environmental protection and sustainable development</li>
<li>authorize the UNEP Governing Council/Global Ministerial Environment Forum to adopt the UNEP medium-term strategy</li>
<li>allow Member States to formulate and administer MEAs an independent secretariat for each convention</li>
<li>support UNEP in periodically assessing MEAs and ensure coordination and coherence</li>
<li>establish directives for setting up national/regional platforms capable of incorporating MEAs in the Common Country Assessment (CCA) process and <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Assistance_Framework_(UNDAF)" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Development Assistance Framework (UNDAF)">United Nations Development Assistance Framework (UNDAF)</a><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></li>
<li>establish a global joint planning framework</li>
<li>study the aptitude and efficiency of environmental activities’ funding, focusing on differential costs</li>
<li>examine and redefine the concept of funding differential costs as applicable to existing financial mechanisms</li>
<li>reconsider remits, division of tasks and responsibilities between entities that provide services to the multipartite conferences. Clearly define the services that UN offices provide to MEA secretariats</li>
<li>propose measures aiming to improve personnel provision and geographic distribution for MEA secretariats</li>
<li>improve transparency resource use for supporting programmes and in providing services to MEAs. Draw up a joint budget for services supplied to MEAs.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2001 Alliance 21 report proposes six fields of action:<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>strengthen citizens' critical faculties to ensure greater democratic control of political orientations</li>
<li>develop a global and critical approach</li>
<li>develop civic education training for teachers</li>
<li>develop training for certain socio-professional groups</li>
<li>develop environmental education for the entire population;</li>
<li>assess the resulting experiences of civil society</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transform_daily_life">Transform daily life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: Transform daily life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Individuals can modify consumption, based on <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_simplicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Voluntary simplicity">voluntary simplicity</a>: changes in purchasing habits, simplified lifestyles (less work, less consumption, more socialization and constructive leisure time). But individual actions must not replace vigilance and pressure on policies.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup> Notions of responsible consumption developed over decades, revealing the political nature of individual purchases, according to the principle that consumption should satisfy the population’s basic needs. These needs comprise the physical wellbeing of individuals and society, a healthy diet, access to drinking water and plumbing, education, healthcare and physical safety.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup> The general attitude centres on the need to reduce consumption and reuse and recycle materials. In the case of food consumption, local, organic and fair trade products which avoid ill treatment of animals has become a major trend.</p>
<p>Alternatives to the personal automobile are increasing, including public transport, car sharing and bicycles and alternative propulsion systems.</p>
<p>Alternative energy sources are becoming less costly.</p>
<p>Ecological industrial processes turn the waste from one industry into raw materials for another.</p>
<p>Governments can reduce subsidies/increase taxes/tighten regulation on unsustainable activities.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_Environmental_governance_Global_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Community Environmental governance Global Alliance (page does not exist)">Community Environmental governance Global Alliance</a> encourages holistic approaches to environmental and economic challenges, incorporating indigenous knowledge. <a href="/wiki/Okotoks" title="Okotoks">Okotoks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> capped population growth based on the carrying capacity of the Sheep River.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> The Fraser Basin Council Watershed Governance<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> in British Columbia, Canada, manages issues that span municipal jurisdictions. <a href="/wiki/Smart_Growth" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart Growth">Smart Growth</a> is an international movement that employs key tenets of Environmental governance in <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies_and_regulations">Policies and regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Policies and regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Establish policies and regulations that promote “infrastructures for well being” whilst addressing the political, physical and cultural levels.</p>
<p>Eliminate subsidies that have a negative environmental impact and tax pollution</p>
<p>Promoting workers’ personal and family development.<sup id="cite_ref-boug_66-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boug-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination">Coordination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_governance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: Coordination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A programme of national workshops on synergies between the three Rio Conventions launched in late 2000, in collaboration with the relevant secretariats. The goal was to strengthen coordination at the local level by:</p>
<ul>
<li>sharing information</li>
<li>promoting political dialogue to obtain financial support and implement programmes</li>
<li>enabling the secretariats to update their joint work programmes.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>According to Campbell,<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> “In the context of globalization, the question of linking up environmental themes with other subjects, such as trade, investment and conflict resolution mechanisms, as well as the economic incentives to participate in and apply agreements would seem to provide an important lesson for the effective development of environmental governance structures.” Environmental concerns would become part of the global economic system. “These problems also contain the seeds of a new generation of international conflicts that could affect both the stability of international relations and collective security. Which is why the concept of ‘collective security’ has arisen.”</p>
<p>Moving local decisions to the global level is as important as the way in which local initiatives and best practices are part of a global system. Kanie<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> points out that NGOs, scientists, international institutions and stakeholder partnerships can reduce the distance that separates the local and international levels.</p>
