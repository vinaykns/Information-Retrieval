			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Urbanization</h1>

<p><b>Urbanization</b> refers to the population shift from <a href="/wiki/Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural">rural</a> to <a href="/wiki/Urban_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban areas">urban areas</a>, "the gradual increase in the proportion of people living in urban areas", and the ways in which each society adapts to the change.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is predominantly the process by which towns and cities are formed and become larger as more people begin living and working in central areas.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> projected that half of the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world's population</a> would live in urban areas at the end of 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is predicted that by 2050 about 64% of the <a href="/wiki/Developing_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing world">developing world</a> and 86% of the <a href="/wiki/Developed_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed world">developed world</a> will be urbanized.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> That is equivalent to approximately 3 billion urbanites by 2050, much of which will occur in Africa and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-5">[5]</a></sup> Notably, the United Nations has also recently projected that nearly all global population growth from 2017 to 2030 will be absorbed by cities, about 1.1 billion new urbanites over the next 13 years.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urbanization is relevant to a range of disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>. The phenomenon has been closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Modernization" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernization">modernization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, and the sociological process of <a href="/wiki/Rationalization_(sociology)" title="Rationalization (sociology)">rationalization</a>. Urbanization can be seen as a specific condition at a set time (e.g. the proportion of total population or area in cities or towns) or as an increase in that condition over time. So urbanization can be quantified either in terms of, say, the level of urban development relative to the overall population, or as the <a href="/wiki/Rate_(mathematics)" title="Rate (mathematics)">rate</a> at which the urban proportion of the population is increasing. Urbanization creates enormous social, economic and environmental changes, which provide an opportunity for sustainability with the “potential to use resources more efficiently, to create more sustainable land use and to protect the biodiversity of natural ecosystems.” <sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urbanization is not merely a modern phenomenon, but a rapid and historic transformation of human <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social roots</a> on a global scale, whereby predominantly <a href="/wiki/Rural_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural culture">rural culture</a> is being rapidly replaced by predominantly <a href="/wiki/Urban_culture" title="Urban culture">urban culture</a>. The first major change in settlement patterns was the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a> into villages many thousand years ago. Village culture is characterized by common bloodlines, intimate relationships, and communal behavior whereas urban culture is characterized by distant bloodlines, unfamiliar relations, and competitive behavior. This unprecedented movement of people is forecast to continue and intensify during the next few decades, mushrooming cities to sizes unthinkable only a century ago.</p>
<p>Today, in Asia the urban agglomerations of <a href="/wiki/Keihanshin" title="Keihanshin">Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> are each already home to over 20 million people, while <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> are forecast to approach or exceed 40 million people each within the coming decade. Outside Asia, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> are, or soon will be, home to over 20 million people.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the development of the earliest cities in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> until the 18th century, an equilibrium existed between the vast majority of the population who engaged in <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence agriculture</a> in a rural context, and small centres of populations in the towns where economic activity consisted primarily of trade at <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" title="Market (place)">markets</a> and manufactures on a small scale. Due to the primitive and relatively stagnant state of agriculture throughout this period the ratio of rural to urban population remained at a fixed equilibrium, though a significant increase in the percentage of the global urban population can still be traced in the 1st millennium BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the onset of the <a href="/wiki/British_Agricultural_Revolution" title="British Agricultural Revolution">agricultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial</a> revolution in the late 18th century this relationship was finally broken and an unprecedented growth in urban population took place over the course of the 19th century, both through continued migration from the countryside and due to the tremendous <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic expansion</a> that occurred at that time. In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> the proportion of the population living in cities jumped from 17% in 1801 to 72% in 1891 (for other countries the figure was: 37% in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, 41% in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> and 28% in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-urbanization_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urbanization-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>As labourers were freed up from working the land due to higher agricultural productivity they converged on the new industrial cities like <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> which were experiencing a boom in commerce, trade and industry. Growing trade around the world also allowed cereals to be imported from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunedin_(ship)" title="Dunedin (ship)">refrigerated meat</a> from <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Spatially, <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">cities also expanded</a> due to the development of <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport systems</a>, which facilitated commutes of longer distances to the city centre for the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>.</p>
<p>Urbanization rapidly spread across the Western world and, since the 1950s, it has begun to take hold in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing world">developing world</a> as well. At the turn of the 20th century, just 15% of the world population lived in cities.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> the year 2007 witnessed the turning point when more than 50% of the world population were living in cities, for the first time in human history.<sup id="cite_ref-urbanization_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urbanization-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in June 2016 published urbanization data from the time period 3700 BC to 2000 AD, the data was used to make a <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> showing the development of cities on the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a> during the time period.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Urbanization occurs as individual, commercial flight<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, social<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and government action reduce the time and expense of commuting and transportation and improve opportunities for jobs, education, housing, and transportation. Living in a city can provide opportunities of proximity, diversity, and marketplace competition. As against this, there may be alienation issues<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, stress, increased cost of living, and negative social aspects that result from mass marginalization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Suburbanization" title="Suburbanization">Suburbanization</a>, which is happening in the cities of the largest developing countries, may be regarded as an attempt to balance these negative aspects of urban life while still allowing access to the large extent of shared resources.</p>
<p>In cities, money, services, wealth and opportunities are centralized. Many rural inhabitants come to the city to seek their fortune and alter their social position. Businesses, which provide jobs and exchange capital, are more concentrated in urban areas. Whether the source is trade or tourism, it is also through the ports or banking systems, commonly located in cities, that foreign money flows into a country.</p>
<p>Many people move into cities for the economic opportunities, but this does not fully explain the very high recent urbanization rates in places like China and India. <a href="/wiki/Rural_flight" title="Rural flight">Rural flight</a> is a contributing factor to urbanization. In rural areas, often on small family farms or collective farms in villages, it has historically been difficult to access manufactured goods, though the relative overall <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> is very subjective, and may certainly surpass that of the city. Farm living has always been susceptible to unpredictable environmental conditions, and in times of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> or pestilence, survival may become extremely problematic.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #B0C4DE;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<div style="text-align:left;">Thai farmers are seen as poor, stupid, and unhealthy. As young people flee the farms, the values and knowledge of rice farming and the countryside are fading, including the tradition of long kek, helping neighbors plant, harvest, or build a house. We are losing what we call Thai-ness, the values of being kind, helping each other, having mercy and gratefulness.— Iam Thongdee, Professor of Humanities, Mahidol University in Bangkok<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Thai_Farm_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Thai_Farm-15">[15]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In a New York Times article concerning the acute migration away from farming in Thailand, life as a farmer was described as "hot and exhausting". "Everyone says the farmer works the hardest but gets the least amount of money". In an effort to counter this impression, the Agriculture Department of Thailand is seeking to promote the impression that farming is "honorable and secure".<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Thai_Farm_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Thai_Farm-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, in Thailand, urbanization has also resulted in massive increases in problems such as obesity. City life, especially in modern urban slums of the developing world, is certainly hardly immune to pestilence or climatic disturbances such as floods, yet continues to strongly attract migrants. Examples of this were the <a href="/wiki/2011_Thailand_floods" title="2011 Thailand floods">2011 Thailand floods</a> and <a href="/wiki/2007_Jakarta_flood" title="2007 Jakarta flood">2007 Jakarta flood</a>. Urban areas are also far more prone to <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Drugs">drugs</a>, and other urban social problems. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Factory_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Factory farming">industrialization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> has negatively affected the economy of small and middle-sized farms and strongly reduced the size of the rural labour market.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #B0C4DE;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: right;">
<div>
<div style="text-align:left;">These are the costs of participating in the urban economy. Your increased income is canceled out by increased expenditure. In the end, you have even less left for food. —Madhura Swaminathan, economist at Kolkata’s Indian Statistical Institute<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_900_Million_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_900_Million-16">[16]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Particularly in the developing world, conflict over land rights due to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> has led to less politically powerful groups, such as farmers, losing or forfeiting their land, resulting in obligatory migration into cities. In China, where land acquisition measures are forceful, there has been far more extensive and rapid urbanization (54%) than in India (36%), where peasants form militant groups (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Naxalites" class="mw-redirect" title="Naxalites">Naxalites</a>) to oppose such efforts. Obligatory and unplanned migration often results in rapid growth of slums. This is also similar to areas of violent conflict, where people are driven off their land due to violence. <a href="/wiki/Bogota" class="mw-redirect" title="Bogota">Bogota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> is one example of this.</p>
<p>Cities offer a larger variety of services, including specialist services not found in rural areas. These services requires workers, resulting in more numerous and varied job opportunities. Elderly people may be forced to move to cities where there are doctors and hospitals that can cater for their health needs. Varied and high quality educational opportunities are another factor in urban migration, as well as the opportunity to join, develop, and seek out social communities.</p>
<p>Urbanization also creates opportunities for women that are not available in rural areas. This creates a gender-related transformation where women are engaged in paid employment and have access to education. This may cause fertility to decline. However, women are sometimes still at a disadvantage due to their unequal position in the labour market, their inability to secure assets independently from male relatives and exposure to violence.<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>People in cities are more productive than in rural areas. An important question is whether this is due to <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_agglomeration" title="Economies of agglomeration">agglomeration effects</a> or whether cities simply attract those who are more productive. Economists have recently shown that there exists a large productivity gain due to locating in dense agglomerations.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It is thus possible that agents<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> locate in cities in order to benefit from these agglomeration effects.</p>

<p>The dominant <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbation</a>(s) of a country can benefit to a greater extent from the same things cities offer, making them magnets for not just the non-urban population, but also urban and suburban population from other cities. Dominant conurbations are quite often <a href="/wiki/Primate_city" title="Primate city">primate cities</a>, but do not have to be. For instance <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manila" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Manila">Greater Manila</a> is rather a conurbation than a city: its 20 million overall population (over 20% national population) make it very much a primate city, but Quezon City (2.7 million), the largest municipality in Greater Manila, and Manila (1.6 million), the capital, are not. A conurbation's dominance can be measured by output, wealth, and especially population, each expressed as a percentage of an entire country. Greater Seoul is one conurbation with massive dominance over South Korea, it is home to 50% of the entire national population.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though Greater Busan-Ulsan (15%, 8 million) and Greater Osaka (14%, 18 million) exhibit strong dominance in their respective countries, yet they are losing population to their even more dominant rivals, Seoul and Tokyo respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_effect">Economic effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As cities develop, effects can include a dramatic increase and change in costs, often pricing the local <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> out of the market, including such functionaries as employees of the local municipalities. For example, Eric Hobsbawm's book <i>The age of revolution: 1789–1848</i> (published 1962 and 2005) chapter 11, stated "Urban development in our period [1789–1848] was a gigantic process of class segregation, which pushed the new labouring poor into great morasses of misery outside the centres of government and business and the newly specialized residential areas of the bourgeoisie. The almost universal European division into a 'good' west end and a 'poor' east end of large cities developed in this period." This is likely due the prevailing south-west wind which carries coal smoke and other airborne pollutants downwind, making the western edges of towns preferable to the eastern ones. Similar problems now affect the developing world, rising inequality resulting from rapid urbanization trends. The drive for rapid urban growth and often efficiency can lead to less equitable urban development. Think tanks such as the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> have proposed policies that encourage labor-intensive growth as a means of absorbing the influx of low-skilled and unskilled labor.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> One problem these migrant workers are involved with is the growth of <a href="/wiki/Slums" class="mw-redirect" title="Slums">slums</a>. In many cases, the rural-urban low skilled or unskilled migrant workers, attracted by economic opportunities in urban areas, cannot find a job and afford housing in cities and have to dwell in slums.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Urban problems, along with infrastructure developments, are also fueling suburbanization trends in developing nations, though the trend for core cities in said nations tends to continue to become ever denser. Urbanization is often viewed as a negative trend, but there are positives in the reduction of expenses in commuting and transportation while improving opportunities for jobs, education, housing, and transportation. Living in cities permits individuals and families to take advantage of the opportunities of proximity and diversity.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brand_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brand-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> While cities have a greater variety of markets and goods than rural areas, infrastructure congestion, monopolization, high overhead costs, and the inconvenience of cross-town trips frequently combine to make marketplace competition harsher in cities than in rural areas.</p>
<p>In many developing countries where economies are growing, the growth is often erratic and based on a small number of industries. For young people in these countries barriers exist such as, lack of access to financial services and business advisory services, difficulty in obtaining credit to start a business, and lack of entrepreneurial skills, in order for them to access opportunities in these industries. Investment in human capital so that young people have access to quality education and infrastructure to enable access to educational facilities is imperative to overcoming economic barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The existence of <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">Urban heat islands</a> has become a growing concern over the years. An urban heat island is formed when industrial and urban areas produce and retain heat. Much of the solar energy that reaches rural areas is consumed by evaporation of water from vegetation and soil. In cities, where there is less vegetation and exposed soil, most of the sun's energy is instead absorbed by buildings and asphalt; leading to higher surface temperatures. Vehicles, factories and industrial and domestic heating and cooling units release even more heat.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As a result, cities are often 1 to 3&#160;°C (1.8 to 5.4&#160;°F) warmer than surrounding landscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Impacts also include reducing soil moisture and a reduction in reabsorption of carbon dioxide emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The occurrence of eutrophication in bodies of water is another effect large urban populations have on the environment. When rain occurs in these large cities, the rain filters down the pollutants such as CO<sub>2</sub> and other green house gases in the air onto the ground below. Then, those chemicals are washed directly into rivers, streams and oceans, causing a decline in water quality and damaging marine ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Whole_Earth_Discipline" title="Whole Earth Discipline">Whole Earth Discipline</a></i>, Stewart Brand argues that the effects of urbanization are primarily positive for the environment. First, the birth rate of new urban dwellers falls immediately to replacement rate, and keeps falling, reducing environmental stresses caused by population growth. Secondly, emigration from rural areas reduces destructive subsistence farming techniques, such as improperly implemented <a href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" class="mw-redirect" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> agriculture.</p>
<p>In July 2013 a report issued by the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> warned that with 2.4 billion more people by 2050, the amount of food produced will have to increase by 70%, straining food resources, especially in countries already facing food insecurity due to changing environmental conditions. The mix of changing environmental conditions and the growing population of urban regions, according to UN experts, will strain basic sanitation systems and health care, and potentially cause a humanitarian and environmental disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_social_effects">Health and social effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health and social effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #00C4DE;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<div style="text-align:left;">When cities don’t plan for increases in population it drives up house and land prices, creating rich (ghettos) and poor ghettos. You get a very unequal society and that inequality is manifested where people live, in our neighbourhoods, and it means there can be less capacity for empathy and less development for all society"— Jack Finegan, Urban Programme Specialist at <a href="/wiki/UN-Habitat" class="mw-redirect" title="UN-Habitat">UN-Habitat</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the developing world, urbanization does not translate into a significant increase in <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-34">[34]</a></sup> Rapid urbanization has led to increased mortality from <a href="/wiki/Non-communicable_disease" title="Non-communicable disease">non-communicable diseases</a> associated with lifestyle, including <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-35">[35]</a></sup> Differences in mortality from <a href="/wiki/Contagious_disease" title="Contagious disease">contagious diseases</a> vary depending on the particular disease and location.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urban health levels are on average better in comparison to rural areas. However, residents in poor urban areas such as slums and <a href="/wiki/Informal_settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Informal settlement">informal settlements</a> suffer "disproportionately from disease, injury, premature death, and the combination of ill-health and poverty entrenches disadvantage over time."<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-17">[17]</a></sup> Many of the urban poor have difficulty accessing health services due to their inability to pay for them; so they resort to less qualified and unregulated providers.</p>
<p>While urbanization is associated with improvements in <a href="/wiki/Public_hygiene" class="mw-redirect" title="Public hygiene">public hygiene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> and access to <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, it also entails changes in occupational, <a href="/wiki/Dietary" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary">dietary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exercise" class="mw-redirect" title="Exercise">exercise</a> patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-35">[35]</a></sup> It can have mixed effects on health patterns, alleviating some problems and accentuating others.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-34">[34]</a></sup> For instance, in children urbanization is associated with a lower risk of <a href="/wiki/Undernutrition" class="mw-redirect" title="Undernutrition">under-nutrition</a> but a higher risk of <a href="/wiki/Overweight" title="Overweight">overweight</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-34">[34]</a></sup> Overall, <a href="/wiki/Body_mass_index" title="Body mass index">body mass index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> levels increase sharply with national income and the degree of urbanization.<sup id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-35">[35]</a></sup> Agriculturists have studied the effects on health of urbanization and globalization. Fast food is often food of choice, which is causing a decline in health.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Easier access to non-traditional foods may lead to less healthy dietary patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-35">[35]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> the prevalence of <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a> in urban areas appears to be more than twice as high as in rural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_35-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-35">[35]</a></sup> In general, major <a href="/wiki/Risk_factor" title="Risk factor">risk factors</a> for <a href="/wiki/Chronic_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronic disease">chronic diseases</a> are more prevalent in urban environments.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_forms">Changing forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Changing forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different forms of urbanization can be classified depending on the style of architecture and planning methods as well as historic growth of areas.</p>
<p>In cities of the <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed world</a> urbanization traditionally exhibited a concentration of human activities and settlements around the downtown area, the so-called <i>in-migration</i>. In-migration refers to migration from former colonies and similar places. The fact that many immigrants settle in impoverished city centres led to the notion of the "peripheralization of the core", which simply describes that people who used to be at the periphery of the former empires now live right in the centre.</p>
<p>Recent developments, such as <a href="/wiki/Inner-city" class="mw-redirect" title="Inner-city">inner-city</a> redevelopment schemes, mean that new arrivals in cities no longer necessarily settle in the centre. In some developed regions, the reverse effect, originally called <a href="/wiki/Counter_urbanization" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter urbanization">counter urbanization</a> has occurred, with cities losing population to rural areas, and is particularly common for richer families. This has been possible because of improved communications, and has been caused by factors such as the fear of crime and poor urban environments. It has contributed to the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Shrinking_cities" title="Shrinking cities">shrinking cities</a> experienced by some parts of the industrialized world.</p>
<p>When the residential area shifts outward, this is called suburbanization. A number of researchers and writers suggest that suburbanization has gone so far to form new points of concentration outside the downtown both in developed and developing countries such as India.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This networked, poly-centric form of concentration is considered by some emerging pattern of urbanization. It is called variously exurbia, edge city (Garreau, 1991), network city (Batten, 1995), or postmodern city (Dear, 2000). Los Angeles is the best-known example of this type of urbanization. Interestingly, in the United States, this process has reversed as of 2011, with "re-urbanization" occurring as <i>suburban flight</i> due to chronically high transport costs.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rural migrants are attracted by the possibilities that cities can offer, but often settle in <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">shanty towns</a> and experience extreme poverty. The inability of countries to provide adequate housing for these rural migrants is related to <a href="/wiki/Overurbanization" title="Overurbanization">overurbanization</a>, a phenomenon in which the rate of urbanization grows more rapidly that the rate of economic development, leading to high unemployment and high demand for resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-39">[39]</a></sup> In the 1980s, this was attempted to be tackled with the <b>urban bias theory</b> which was promoted by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lipton" title="Michael Lipton">Michael Lipton</a>.</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:25em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #B0C4DE;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<div style="text-align:left;">...the most important class conflict in the poor countries of the world today is not between labour and capital. Nor is it between foreign and national interests. It is between rural classes and urban classes. The rural sector contains most of the poverty and most of the low-cost sources of potential advance; but the urban sector contains most of the articulateness, organization and power. So the urban classes have been able to win most of the rounds of the struggle with the countryside...".— Michael Lipton, author of urban bias theory<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most of the urban poor in developing countries unable to find work, can spend their lives in insecure, poorly paid jobs. According to research by the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> pro-poor urbanization will require labour-intensive growth, supported by labour protection, flexible land use regulation and investments in basic services.'<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Urbanization can be planned urbanization or organic. Planned urbanization, i.e.: <a href="/wiki/Planned_community" title="Planned community">planned community</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Garden_city_movement" title="Garden city movement">garden city movement</a>, is based on an advance plan, which can be prepared for military, aesthetic, economic or <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a> reasons. Examples can be seen in many ancient cities; although with exploration came the collision of nations, which meant that many invaded cities took on the desired planned characteristics of their occupiers. Many ancient organic cities experienced redevelopment for military and economic purposes, new roads carved through the cities, and new parcels of land were cordoned off serving various planned purposes giving cities distinctive geometric designs. UN agencies prefer to see <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">urban infrastructure</a> installed before urbanization occurs. <a href="/wiki/Landscape_planning" title="Landscape planning">Landscape planners</a> are responsible for landscape infrastructure (<a href="/wiki/Public_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Public park">public parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_urban_drainage_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable urban drainage systems">sustainable urban drainage systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">greenways</a> etc.) which can be planned before urbanization takes place, or afterward to revitalize an area and create greater <a href="/wiki/Livability" class="mw-redirect" title="Livability">livability</a> within a region. Concepts of control of the urban expansion are considered in the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Planners" class="mw-redirect" title="American Institute of Planners">American Institute of Planners</a>.</p>
<p>As the population continues to grow and urbanize at unprecedented rates, <a href="/wiki/New_urbanism" class="mw-redirect" title="New urbanism">new urbanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smart_growth" title="Smart growth">smart growth</a> techniques will create a successful transition into developing environmentally, economically, and socially sustainable cities. Smart Growth and New Urbanism’s principles include walkability, mixed-use development, comfortable high-density design, land conservation, social equity, and economic diversity. Mixed-use communities work to fight gentrification with affordable housing to promote social equity, decrease <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependency</a> to lower use of fossil fuels, and promote a localized economy. Walkable communities have a 38% higher average GDP per capita than less walkable urban metros (Leinberger, Lynch). By combining economic, environmental, and social sustainability, cities will become equitable, resilient, and more appealing than urban sprawl that overuses land, promotes automobile use, and segregates the population economically.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-control_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-control-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
