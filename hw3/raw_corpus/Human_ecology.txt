<p><b>Human Ecology</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Transdisciplinary">transdisciplinary</a> study of the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_environment" title="Social environment">social</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">built environments</a>. The philosophy and study of human ecology has a diffuse history with advancements in <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a>, and natural <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, among others.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_development">Historical development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The roots of ecology as a broader discipline can be traced to the Greeks and a lengthy list of developments in <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history science</a>. Ecology also has notably developed in other cultures. Traditional knowledge, as it is called, includes the human propensity for intuitive knowledge, intelligent relations, understanding, and for passing on information about the natural world and the human experience.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Huntignton00_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huntignton00-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Turner00_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner00-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Davis03_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis03-4">[4]</a></sup> The term <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel" title="Ernst Haeckel">Ernst Haeckel</a> in 1866 and defined by direct reference to <i>the economy of nature</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Odum05_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Odum05-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like other contemporary researchers of his time, Haeckel adopted his terminology from <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> where human ecological connections were more evident. In his 1749 publication, <i>Specimen academicum de oeconomia naturae</i>, Linnaeus developed a science that included the <i>economy and polis of nature</i>. Polis stems from its Greek roots for a political community (originally based on the city-states), sharing its roots with the word police in reference to the promotion of growth and maintenance of good social order in a community.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pearce10_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce10-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kricher09_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kricher09-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Egerton07_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egerton07-8">[8]</a></sup> Linnaeus was also the first to write about the close affinity between humans and primates.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid09_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid09-9">[9]</a></sup> Linnaeus presented early ideas found in modern aspects to human ecology, including the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_nature" title="Balance of nature">balance of nature</a> while highlighting the importance of ecological functions (<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> in modern terms): "In exchange for performing its function satisfactorily, nature provided a species with the necessaries of life"<sup id="cite_ref-Foster03_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foster03-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:66</sup> The work of Linnaeus influenced <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and other scientists of his time who used Linnaeus' terminology (i.e., the <i>economy and polis of nature</i>) with direct implications on matters of human affairs, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haecek66_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haecek66-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stauffer57_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stauffer57-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kormandy78_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kormandy78-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecology is not just biological, but a human science as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Odum05_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Odum05-5">[5]</a></sup> An early and influential social scientist in the history of human ecology was <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>. Spencer was influenced by and reciprocated his influence onto the works of Charles Darwin. Herbert Spencer coined the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest" title="Survival of the fittest">survival of the fittest</a>", he was an early founder of sociology where he developed the idea of society as an organism, and he created an early precedent for the socio-ecological approach that was the subsequent aim and link between sociology and human ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Catton94_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catton94-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-claeys00_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-claeys00-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Human ecology is the discipline that inquires into the patterns and process of interaction of humans with their environments. Human values, wealth, life-styles, resource use, and waste, etc. must affect and be affected by the physical and biotic environments along urban-rural gradients. The nature of these interactions is a legitimate ecological research topic and one of increasing importance.<sup id="cite_ref-McDonnell90_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDonnell90-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1233</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The history of human ecology has strong roots in geography and sociology departments of the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gross04_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross04-17">[17]</a></sup> In this context a major historical development or landmark that stimulated research into the ecological relations between humans and their urban environments was founded in <a href="/wiki/George_Perkins_Marsh" title="George Perkins Marsh">George Perkins Marsh</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Man_and_Nature" title="Man and Nature">Man and Nature; or, physical geography as modified by human action</a></i>, which was published in 1864. Marsh was interested in the active agency of human-nature interactions (an early precursor to <a href="/wiki/Urban_ecology" title="Urban ecology">urban ecology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Niche_construction" title="Niche construction">human niche construction</a>) in frequent reference to the <i>economy of nature</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelinski05_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelinski05-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stallin07_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stallin07-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Liu07_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu07-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1894, an influential <a href="/wiki/Chicago_school_(sociology)" title="Chicago school (sociology)">sociologist at the University of Chicago</a> named <a href="/wiki/Albion_W._Small" class="mw-redirect" title="Albion W. Small">Albion W. Small</a>, collaborated with sociologist <a href="/wiki/George_E._Vincent" class="mw-redirect" title="George E. Vincent">George E. Vincent</a> and published a "‘‘laboratory guide’’ to studying people in their ‘‘every-day occupations.’’"<sup id="cite_ref-Gross04_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross04-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:578</sup> This was a guidebook that trained students of sociology how they could study <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> in a way that a <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural historian</a> would study birds. Their publication "explicitly included the relation of the social world to the material environment."<sup id="cite_ref-Gross04_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross04-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:578</sup></p>
<p>The first English-language use of the term "ecology" is credited to American chemist and founder of the field of <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Swallow_Richards" title="Ellen Swallow Richards">Ellen Swallow Richards</a>. Richards first introduced the term as "<a href="/wiki/Oekology" class="mw-redirect" title="Oekology">oekology</a>" in 1892, and subsequently developed the term "human ecology".<sup id="cite_ref-Merchant2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merchant2-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "human ecology" was published in 1907 in <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Swallow_Richards" title="Ellen Swallow Richards">Ellen Swallow Richards</a> work "Sanitation in Daily Life", defined there as "the study of the surroundings of human beings in the effects they produce on the lives of men".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Richard's use of the term recognized humans as part of rather than separate from nature.<sup id="cite_ref-Merchant2_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merchant2-21">[21]</a></sup> The term made its first formal appearance in the field of sociology in the 1921 book "Introduction to the Science of Sociology",<sup id="cite_ref-Park21_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park21-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schnore58_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schnore58-24">[24]</a></sup> published by <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Park" title="Robert E. Park">Robert E. Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_W._Burgess" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernest W. Burgess">Ernest W. Burgess</a> (also from the sociology department at the University of Chicago). Their student, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roderick_D._McKenzie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roderick D. McKenzie (page does not exist)">Roderick D. McKenzie</a> helped solidify human ecology as a sub-discipline within the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_school_(sociology)" title="Chicago school (sociology)">Chicago school</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald-25">[25]</a></sup> These authors emphasized the difference between human ecology and ecology in general by highlighting <a href="/wiki/Cultural_evolution" title="Cultural evolution">cultural evolution</a> in human societies.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Human ecology has a fragmented academic history with developments spread throughout a range of disciplines, including: <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>. Some authors have argued that geography is human ecology. Much historical debate has hinged on the placement of humanity as part or as separate from nature.<sup id="cite_ref-Gross04_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross04-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Barrows23_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barrows23-26">[26]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-Bruhn74_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruhn74-27">[27]</a></sup> In light of the branching debate of what constitutes human ecology, recent interdisciplinary researchers have sought a unifying scientific field they have titled <a href="/wiki/Coupled_human-environment_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Coupled human-environment system">coupled human and natural systems</a> that "builds on but moves beyond previous work (e.g., human ecology, ecological anthropology, environmental geography)."<sup id="cite_ref-Liu09_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu09-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:639</sup> Other fields or branches related to the historical development of human ecology as a discipline include <a href="/wiki/Cultural_ecology" title="Cultural ecology">cultural ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_ecology" title="Urban ecology">urban ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sociology" title="Environmental sociology">environmental sociology</a>, and anthropological ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-Orlove80_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orlove80-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nettle09_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nettle09-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zimmerer94_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerer94-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biological ecologists have traditionally been reluctant to study human ecology gravitating instead to the <a href="/wiki/Biophilia_hypothesis" title="Biophilia hypothesis">allure of wild nature</a>. Human ecology has a history of focusing attention on humans’ impact on the biotic world.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McDonnell97_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDonnell97-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sears" title="Paul Sears">Paul Sears</a> was an early proponent of applying human ecology, addressing topics aimed at the population explosion of humanity, global resource limits, pollution, and published a comprehensive account on human ecology as a discipline in 1954. He saw the vast “explosion” of problems humans were creating for the environment and reminded us that “what is important is the work to be done rather than the label."<sup id="cite_ref-Sears54_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sears54-33">[33]</a></sup> "When we as a profession learn to diagnose the total landscape, not only as the basis of our culture, but as an expression of it, and to share our special knowledge as widely as we can, we need not fear that our work will be ignored or that our efforts will be unappreciated."<sup id="cite_ref-Sears54_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sears54-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:963</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Human ecology has been defined as a type of analysis applied to the relations in human beings that was traditionally applied to plants and animals in <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Park36_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park36-34">[34]</a></sup> Toward this aim, human ecologists (which can include <a href="/wiki/Sociologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociologists">sociologists</a>) integrate diverse perspectives from a broad spectrum of disciplines covering "wider points of view".<sup id="cite_ref-Borden08_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Borden08-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:107</sup> In its 1972 premier edition, the editors of <i><a href="/wiki/Human_Ecology_(journal)" title="Human Ecology (journal)">Human Ecology: An Interdisciplinary Journal</a></i> gave an introductory statement on the scope of topics in human ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-HEJ_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HEJ-36">[36]</a></sup> Their statement provides a broad overview on the interdisciplinary nature of the topic:</p>
<ul>
<li>Genetic, physiological, and social adaptation to the environment and to environmental change;</li>
<li>The role of social, cultural, and psychological factors in the maintenance or disruption of ecosystems;</li>
<li>Effects of population density on health, social organization, or environmental quality;</li>
<li>New adaptive problems in urban environments;</li>
<li>Interrelations of technological and environmental changes;</li>
<li>The development of unifying principles in the study of biological and cultural adaptation;</li>
<li>The genesis of maladaptions in human biological and cultural evolution;</li>
<li>The relation of food quality and quantity to physical and intellectual performance and to demographic change;</li>
<li>The application of computers, remote sensing devices, and other new tools and techniques<sup id="cite_ref-HEJ_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HEJ-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Forty years later in the same journal, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_G._Bates&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Daniel G. Bates (page does not exist)">Daniel G. Bates</a> (2012)<sup id="cite_ref-Bates12_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates12-37">[37]</a></sup> notes lines of continuity in the discipline and the way it has changed:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Today there is greater emphasis on the problems facing individuals and how actors deal with them with the consequence that there is much more attention to decision-making at the individual level as people strategize and optimize risk, costs and benefits within specific contexts. Rather than attempting to formulate a cultural ecology or even a specifically “human ecology” model, researchers more often draw on demographic, economic and evolutionary theory as well as upon models derived from field ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates12_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates12-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While theoretical discussions continue, research published in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Ecology_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Human Ecology Review (page does not exist)">Human Ecology Review</a></i> suggests that recent discourse has shifted toward applying principles of human ecology. Some of these applications focus instead on addressing problems that cross disciplinary boundaries or transcend those boundaries altogether. Scholarship has increasingly tended away from <a href="/wiki/Gerald_L._Young" title="Gerald L. Young">Gerald L. Young</a>'s idea of a "unified theory" of human ecological knowledge—that human ecology may emerge as its own discipline—and more toward the pluralism best espoused by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Shepard" title="Paul Shepard">Paul Shepard</a>: that human ecology is healthiest when "running out in all directions.".<sup id="cite_ref-Shepard67_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shepard67-38">[38]</a></sup> But human ecology is neither anti-discipline nor anti-theory, rather it is the ongoing attempt to formulate, synthesize, and apply theory to bridge the widening schism between man and nature. This new human ecology emphasizes complexity over <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductionism</a>, focuses on changes over stable states, and expands ecological concepts beyond plants and animals to include people.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_to_epidemiology_and_public_health">Application to epidemiology and public health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Application to epidemiology and public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The application of ecological concepts to epidemiology has similar roots to those of other disciplinary applications, with Carl Linnaeus having played a seminal role. However, the term appears to have come into common use in the medical and public health literature in the mid-twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This was strengthened in 1971 by the publication of Epidemiology as Medical Ecology,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and again in 1987 by the publication of a textbook on Public Health and Human Ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> An “ecosystem health” perspective has emerged as a thematic movement, integrating research and practice from such fields as environmental management, public health, biodiversity, and economic development.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Drawing in turn from the application of concepts such as the social-ecological model of health, human ecology has converged with the mainstream of global public health literature.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_home_economics">Connection to home economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Connection to home economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to its links to other disciplines, human ecology has a strong historical linkage to the field of home economics through the work of <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Swallow_Richards" title="Ellen Swallow Richards">Ellen Swallow Richards</a>, among others. However, as early as the 1960s, a number of universities began to rename "home economics" departments, schools, and colleges as "human ecology" programs. In part, this name change was a response to perceived difficulties with the term "home economics" in a modernizing society, and reflects a recognition of "human ecology" as one of the initial choices for the discipline which was to become "home economics".<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-45">[45]</a></sup> Current Human Ecology programs include <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University_College_of_Human_Ecology" title="Cornell University College of Human Ecology">Cornell University College of Human Ecology</a> and the University of Alberta's Department of Human Ecology,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_of_the_Anthropocene">Niche of the Anthropocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Niche of the Anthropocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Novel_ecosystem" title="Novel ecosystem">Novel ecosystem</a></div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Perhaps the most important implication involves our view of human society. <i>Homo sapiens</i> is not an external disturbance, it is a keystone species within the system. In the long term, it may not be the magnitude of extracted goods and services that will determine sustainability. It may well be our disruption of ecological recovery and stability mechanisms that determines system collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-O.27Neil01_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Neil01-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:3282</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Changes to the Earth by human activities have been so great that a new geological epoch named the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a> has been proposed.<sup id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz11_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz11-48">[48]</a></sup> The human niche or ecological <i>polis</i> of human society, as it was known historically, has created entirely new arrangements of ecosystems as we convert matter into technology. Human ecology has created anthropogenic biomes (called <a href="/wiki/Anthrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthrome">anthromes</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ellis11_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis11-49">[49]</a></sup> The habitats within these anthromes reach out through our road networks to create what has been called <a href="/wiki/Technoecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Technoecosystems">technoecosystems</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Technosols" class="mw-redirect" title="Technosols">technosols</a>. <a href="/wiki/Technodiversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Technodiversity">Technodiversity</a> exists within these technoecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Odum05_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Odum05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rossiter07_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossiter07-50">[50]</a></sup> In direct parallel to the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_spheres" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's spheres">ecosphere</a>, human civilization has also created a <a href="/wiki/Novel_ecosystem#Technosphere" title="Novel ecosystem">technosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stairs97_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stairs97-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Adams09_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adams09-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lugoa00_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lugoa00-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zabel99_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zabel99-54">[54]</a></sup> The way that the human species engineers or constructs technodiversity into the environment, threads back into the processes of cultural and biological evolution, including the human economy.<sup id="cite_ref-Rowley-Conwy11_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rowley-Conwy11-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jablonka11_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonka11-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_services">Ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG/220px-Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG/330px-Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG/440px-Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1662" data-file-height="1662" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bee_pollinating_Aquilegia_vulgaris.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">bumblebee</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollinating</a> a flower, one example of an ecosystem service</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The ecosystems of planet Earth are coupled to human environments. Ecosystems regulate the global <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysical cycle">geophysical cycles</a> of energy, climate, soil nutrients, and water that in turn support and grow <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> (including the environmental, physiological, cognitive, cultural, and spiritual dimensions of life). Ultimately, every manufactured product in human environments comes from natural systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Liu09_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu09-28">[28]</a></sup> Ecosystems are considered <a href="/wiki/Common-pool_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Common-pool resources">common-pool resources</a> because ecosystems do not exclude beneficiaries and they can be depleted or degraded.<sup id="cite_ref-Becker95_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Becker95-57">[57]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">green space</a> within communities provides sustainable health services that reduces mortality and regulates the spread of vector borne disease.<sup id="cite_ref-McMichael99_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McMichael99-58">[58]</a></sup> Research shows that people who are more engaged with regular access to natural areas have lower rates of diabetes, heart disease and psychological disorders.<sup id="cite_ref-Hartig08_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hartig08-59">[59]</a></sup> These ecological health services are regularly depleted through urban development projects that do not factor in the common-pool value of ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickett07_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickett07-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Termorshuizen07_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Termorshuizen07-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ecological commons delivers a diverse supply of community services that sustains the well-being of human society.<sup id="cite_ref-Diaz06_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz06-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ostrom99_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostrom99-63">[63]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a>, an international UN initiative involving more than 1,360 experts worldwide, identifies four main <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystem service">ecosystem service</a> types having 30 sub-categories stemming from natural capital. The ecological commons includes provisioning (e.g., food, raw materials, medicine, water supplies), regulating (e.g., climate, water, soil retention, flood retention), cultural (e.g., science and education, artistic, spiritual), and supporting (e.g., soil formation, nutrient cycling, water cycling) services.<sup id="cite_ref-MEA05_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MEA05-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-de_Groot02_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de_Groot02-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Policy and human institutions should rarely assume that human enterprise is benign. A safer assumption holds that human enterprise almost always exacts an ecological toll - a debit taken from the ecological commons.<sup id="cite_ref-Sienkiewicz06_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sienkiewicz06-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:95</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sixth_mass_extinction">Sixth mass extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sixth mass extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a></div>
<p>Global assessments of biodiversity indicate that the current epoch, the Holocene (or Anthropocene)<sup id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz08_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz08-67">[67]</a></sup> is a sixth mass extinction. Species loss is accelerating at 100–1000 times faster than average background rates in the fossil record.<sup id="cite_ref-Wake08_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wake08-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-May10_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-May10-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McCallum07_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCallum07-70">[70]</a></sup> The field of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation biology</a> involves ecologists that are researching, confronting, and searching for solutions to sustain the planet's ecosystems for future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-Ehrlich08_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich08-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Human activities are associated directly or indirectly with nearly every aspect of the current extinction spasm."<sup id="cite_ref-Wake08_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wake08-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:11472</sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Nature is a <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilient</a> system. Ecosystems regenerate, withstand, and are forever adapting to fluctuating environments. Ecological resilience is an important conceptual framework in conservation management and it is defined as the preservation of biological relations in ecosystems that persevere and regenerate in response to disturbance over time. However, persistent, systematic, large and nonrandom disturbance caused by the niche constructing behavior of human beings, habitat conversion and land development, has pushed many of the Earth's ecosystems to the extent of their resilient thresholds. Three planetary thresholds have already been crossed, including 1) <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a>, 2) <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and 3) <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycles</a>. These biophysical systems are ecologically interrelated and naturally resilient, but human civilization has transitioned the planet to an <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a> epoch, where the threshold for planetary scale resilience has been crossed and the ecological state of the Earth is deteriorating rapidly to the detriment of humanity.<sup id="cite_ref-Rockstr.C3.B6m09_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rockstr.C3.B6m09-72">[72]</a></sup> The world's fisheries and oceans, for example, are facing dire challenges as the threat of global collapse appears imminent, with serious ramifications for the well-being of humanity;<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-73">[73]</a></sup> while the Anthropocene is yet to be classified as an official epoch, current evidence suggest that "an epoch-scale boundary has been crossed within the last two centuries."<sup id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz11_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz11-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:835</sup> The ecology of the planet is further threatened by global warming, but investments in nature conservation can provide a regulatory feedback to store and regulate carbon and other greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-Mooney09_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mooney09-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chapin00_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin00-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_footprint">Ecological footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ecological footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a></div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>While we are used to thinking of cities as geographically discrete places, most of the land "occupied" by their residents lies far beyond their borders. The total area of land required to sustain an urban region (its "ecological footprint") is typically at least an order of magnitude greater than that contained within municipal boundaries or the associated built-up area.<sup id="cite_ref-Rees92_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees92-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:121</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1992, <a href="/wiki/William_E._Rees_(academic)" class="mw-redirect" title="William E. Rees (academic)">William Rees</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> concept. The ecological footprint and its close analog the <a href="/wiki/Water_footprint" class="mw-redirect" title="Water footprint">water footprint</a> has become a popular way of accounting for the level of impact that human society is imparting on the Earth's ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Rees92_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees92-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hoekstra09_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoekstra09-77">[77]</a></sup> All indications are that the human enterprise is unsustainable as the footprint of society is placing too much stress on the ecology of the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-Moran08_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moran08-78">[78]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">WWF</a> 2008 living planet report and other researchers report that human civilization has exceeded the bio-regenerative capacity of the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-Moran08_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moran08-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> This means that the footprint of human consumption is extracting more natural resources than can be replenished by ecosystems around the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_economics">Ecological economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ecological economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">Natural capital</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic science</a> that extends its methods of valuation onto nature in an effort to address the inequity between market growth and biodiversity loss.<sup id="cite_ref-de_Groot02_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de_Groot02-65">[65]</a></sup> Natural capital is the stock of materials or information stored in biodiversity that generates services that can enhance the welfare of communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Costanza97_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costanza97-80">[80]</a></sup> Population losses are the more sensitive indicator of natural capital than are species extinction in the accounting of ecosystem services. The prospect for recovery in the economic crisis of nature is grim. Populations, such as local ponds and patches of forest are being cleared away and lost at rates that exceed species extinctions.<sup id="cite_ref-Ceballos02_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos02-81">[81]</a></sup> The mainstream growth-based economic system adopted by governments worldwide does not include a price or markets for natural capital. This type of economic system places further ecological debt onto future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel97_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel97-82">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pastor98_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pastor98-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Many human-nature interactions occur indirectly due to the production and use of human-made (manufactured and synthesized) products, such as electronic appliances, furniture, plastics, airplanes, and automobiles. These products insulate humans from the natural environment, leading them to perceive less dependence on natural systems than is the case, but all manufactured products ultimately come from natural systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Liu09_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu09-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:640</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Human societies are increasingly being placed under stress as the <a href="/wiki/Common-pool_resource" title="Common-pool resource">ecological commons</a> is diminished through an accounting system that has incorrectly assumed "... that nature is a fixed, indestructible capital asset."<sup id="cite_ref-Dasgupta08_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dasgupta08-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:44</sup> The current wave of threats, including massive extinction rates and concurrent loss of natural capital to the detriment of human society, is happening rapidly. This is called a biodiversity crisis, because 50% of the worlds species are predicted to go extinct within the next 50&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-Koh04_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koh04-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Western92_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western92-86">[86]</a></sup> Conventional monetary analyses are unable to detect or deal with these sorts of ecological problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Rees02_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees02-87">[87]</a></sup> Multiple global ecological economic initiatives are being promoted to solve this problem. For example, governments of the <a href="/wiki/G8" class="mw-redirect" title="G8">G8</a> met in 2007 and set forth <a href="/wiki/The_Economics_of_Ecosystems_and_Biodiversity" title="The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity">The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity</a> (TEEB) initiative:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In a global study we will initiate the process of analyzing the global economic benefit of biological diversity, the costs of the loss of biodiversity and the failure to take protective measures versus the costs of effective conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The work of <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_E._Boulding" title="Kenneth E. Boulding">Kenneth E. Boulding</a> is notable for building on the integration between ecology and its economic origins. Boulding drew parallels between ecology and economics, most generally in that they are both studies of individuals as members of a system, and indicated that the “household of man” and the “household of nature” could somehow be integrated to create a perspective of greater value.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interdisciplinary_approaches">Interdisciplinary approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interdisciplinary approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Human ecology may be defined: (1) from a bio-ecological standpoint as the study of man as the ecological dominant in plant and animal communities and systems; (2) from a bio-ecological standpoint as simply another animal affecting and being affected by his physical environment; and (3) as a human being, somehow different from animal life in general, interacting with physical and modified environments in a distinctive and creative way. A truly interdisciplinary human ecology will most likely address itself to all three.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8–9</sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Human ecology expands functionalism from ecology to the human mind. People's perception of a complex world is a function of their ability to be able to comprehend beyond the immediate, both in time and in space. This concept manifested in the popular slogan promoting sustainability: "think global, act local." Moreover, people's conception of community stems from not only their physical location but their mental and emotional connections and varies from "community as place, community as way of life, or community of collective action."<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In these early years, human ecology was still deeply enmeshed in its respective disciplines: <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. Scholars through the 1970s until present have called for a greater integration between all of the scattered disciplines that has each established formal ecological research.<sup id="cite_ref-Young74_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Liu07_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu07-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While some of the early writers considered how art fit into a human ecology, it was Sears who posed the idea that in the long run human ecology will in fact look more like art. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Carpenter_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bill Carpenter (writer) (page does not exist)">Bill Carpenter</a> (1986) calls human ecology the "possibility of an aesthetic science," renewing dialogue about how art fits into a human ecological perspective. According to Carpenter, human ecology as an aesthetic science counters the disciplinary fragmentation of knowledge by examining human consciousness.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_education">In education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the reputation of human ecology in institutions of higher learning is growing, there is no human ecology at the primary and secondary education levels. Educational theorist Sir <a href="/wiki/Ken_Robinson_(educationalist)" title="Ken Robinson (educationalist)">Kenneth Robinson</a> has called for diversification of education to promote creativity in academic and non-academic (i.e.- educate their “whole being”) activities to implement a “new conception of human ecology”.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioregionalism_and_urban_ecology">Bioregionalism and urban ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bioregionalism and urban ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">Bioregionalism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Urban_ecology" title="Urban ecology">Urban ecology</a></div>
<p>In the late 1960s, ecological concepts started to become integrated into the applied fields, namely <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architecture" title="Landscape architecture">landscape architecture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ian_McHarg" title="Ian McHarg">Ian McHarg</a> called for a future when all planning would be “human ecological planning” by default, always bound up in humans’ relationships with their environments. He emphasized local, place-based planning that takes into consideration all the “layers” of information from <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> to <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">cultural history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Proponents of the <a href="/wiki/New_urbanism" class="mw-redirect" title="New urbanism">new urbanism</a> movement, like <a href="/wiki/James_Howard_Kunstler" title="James Howard Kunstler">James Howard Kunstler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andres_Duany" class="mw-redirect" title="Andres Duany">Andres Duany</a>, have embraced the term human ecology as way to describe the problem of—and prescribe the solutions for—the landscapes and lifestyles of an automobile oriented society. Duany has called the human ecology movement to be "the agenda for the years ahead."<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> While McHargian planning is still widely respected, the <a href="/wiki/Landscape_urbanism" title="Landscape urbanism">landscape urbanism</a> movement seeks a new understanding between human and environment relations. Among these theorists is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederich_Steiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Frederich Steiner (page does not exist)">Frederich Steiner</a>, who published <i>Human Ecology: Following Nature's Lead</i> in 2002 which focuses on the relationships among landscape, culture, and planning. The work highlights the beauty of scientific inquiry by revealing those purely human dimensions which underlie our concepts of ecology. While Steiner discusses specific ecological settings, such as cityscapes and waterscapes, and the relationships between socio-cultural and environmental regions, he also takes a diverse approach to ecology—considering even the unique synthesis between ecology and political geography. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deiter_Steiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deiter Steiner (page does not exist)">Deiter Steiner</a>'s 2003 <i>Human Ecology: Fragments of Anti-fragmentary</i> view of the world is an important expose of recent trends in human ecology. Part literature review, the book is divided into four sections: "human ecology", "the implicit and the explicit", "structuration", and "the regional dimension".<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Much of the work stresses the need for transciplinarity, antidualism, and wholeness of perspective.</p>
