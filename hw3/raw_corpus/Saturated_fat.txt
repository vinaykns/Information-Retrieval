<p>A <b>saturated fat</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> in which the fatty acids all have <a href="/wiki/Single_bond" title="Single bond">single bonds</a>.</p>
<p>A fat is made of two kinds of smaller molecules: <a href="/wiki/Monoglyceride" title="Monoglyceride">monoglyceride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>. Fats are made of long chains of carbon (C) atoms. Some carbon atoms are linked by single bonds (-C-C-) and others are linked by <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a> (-C=C-).<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> Double bonds can react with hydrogen to form single bonds. They are called <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a>, because the second bond is broken up and each half of the bond is attached to (saturated with) a hydrogen atom. Most animal fats are saturated. The fats of plants and fish are generally unsaturated.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-1">[1]</a></sup> Saturated fats tend to have higher <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> than their corresponding unsaturated fats, leading to the popular understanding that saturated fats tend to be solids at room temperatures, while unsaturated fats tend to be liquid at room temperature with varying degrees of viscosity (meaning both saturated and unsaturated fats are found to be liquid at body temperature).</p>
<p>Various fats contain different proportions of saturated and <a href="/wiki/Unsaturated_fat" title="Unsaturated fat">unsaturated fat</a>. Examples of foods containing a high proportion of saturated fat include animal fat products such as <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Whole_milk" class="mw-redirect" title="Whole milk">whole milk</a> <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a> and fatty <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meats</a> which also contain <a href="/wiki/Dietary_cholesterol" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary cholesterol">dietary cholesterol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aha_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aha-2">[2]</a></sup> Certain vegetable products have high saturated fat content, such as <a href="/wiki/Coconut_oil" title="Coconut oil">coconut oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palm_kernel_oil" title="Palm kernel oil">palm kernel oil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Many prepared foods are high in saturated fat content, such as <a href="/wiki/Pizza" title="Pizza">pizza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a> <a href="/wiki/Dessert" title="Dessert">desserts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aha_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aha-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effect of saturated fat on risk of disease is controversial. Many reviews recommend a diet low in saturated fat and argue it will lower risks of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular diseases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, or death.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, other reviews have rejected those arguments or advocated for examining the proportion of saturated to unsaturated fat in the diet.<sup id="cite_ref-bmj351_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj351-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bmj346_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj346-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bmj353_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj353-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fat_profiles">Fat profiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fat profiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While nutrition labels regularly combine them, the saturated fatty acids appear in different proportions among food groups. <a href="/wiki/Lauric_acid" title="Lauric acid">Lauric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myristic_acid" title="Myristic acid">myristic</a> acids are most commonly found in "tropical" oils (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Palm_kernel_oil" title="Palm kernel oil">palm kernel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coconut_oil" title="Coconut oil">coconut</a>) and dairy products. The saturated fat in meat, <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, cacao, and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a> is primarily the triglycerides of <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">stearic</a> acids.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_saturated_fatty_acids">Examples of saturated fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples of saturated fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_saturated_fatty_acids" title="List of saturated fatty acids">List of saturated fatty acids</a></div>
<p>Some common examples of fatty acids:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">Butyric acid</a> with 4 carbon atoms (contained in <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lauric_acid" title="Lauric acid">Lauric acid</a> with 12 carbon atoms (contained in <a href="/wiki/Coconut_oil" title="Coconut oil">coconut oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_kernel_oil" title="Palm kernel oil">palm kernel oil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Breast_milk" title="Breast milk">breast milk</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Myristic_acid" title="Myristic acid">Myristic acid</a> with 14 carbon atoms (contained in cow's <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> and dairy products)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">Palmitic acid</a> with 16 carbon atoms (contained in <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">Stearic acid</a> with 18 carbon atoms (also contained in <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_butter" title="Cocoa butter">cocoa butter</a>)</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_with_diseases">Association with diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Association with diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the 1950s, it has been demonstrated that consumption of foods containing high amounts of saturated fatty acids (including meat fats, milk fat, butter, lard, coconut oil, palm oil, and palm kernel oil) is potentially less healthy than consuming fats with a lower proportion of saturated fatty acids. Sources of lower saturated fat but higher proportions of unsaturated fatty acids include olive oil, peanut oil, canola oil, avocados, safflower, corn, sunflower, soy, and cottonseed oils.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_disease">Cardiovascular disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat_and_cardiovascular_disease_controversy" title="Saturated fat and cardiovascular disease controversy">Saturated fat and cardiovascular disease controversy</a></div>
<p>The effect of saturated fat on <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a> is controversial.</p>
<p>Many health authorities such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Dietetic_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="American Dietetic Association">American Dietetic Association</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ADA.2FDOC_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADA.2FDOC-23">[23]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/British_Dietetic_Association" title="British Dietetic Association">British Dietetic Association</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BDA_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDA-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AHA_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AHA-25">[25]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/World_Heart_Federation" title="World Heart Federation">World Heart Federation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-WorldHeartFederation_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldHeartFederation-26">[26]</a></sup> the British <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NHS_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS-27">[27]</a></sup> among others,<sup id="cite_ref-FDA_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FDA-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> advise that saturated fat is a <a href="/wiki/Risk_factor" title="Risk factor">risk factor</a> for cardiovascular disease. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> in May 2015 recommends switching from saturated to unsaturated fats.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Systematic_reviews" class="mw-redirect" title="Systematic reviews">systematic reviews</a> have examined the relationship between saturated fat and cardiovascular disease and have come to different conclusions:</p>
<p>A 2015 systematic review found no association between consumption and risk of heart disease, stroke, diabetes, or death.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2014 systematic review looking at observational studies of dietary intake of fatty acids, observational studies of measured fatty acid levels in the blood, and intervention studies of polyunsaturated fat supplementation concludes that the findings ″do not support cardiovascular guidelines that promote high consumption of long-chain omega-3 and omega-6 and polyunsaturated fatty acids and suggest reduced consumption of total saturated fatty acids.″<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Researchers acknowledged that despite their results, further research is necessary, especially in people who are initially healthy. Until the picture becomes clearer, experts recommend people stick to the current guidelines on fat consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyslipidemia">Dyslipidemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dyslipidemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lipid_hypothesis" title="Lipid hypothesis">Lipid hypothesis</a></div>
<p>The consumption of saturated fat is generally considered a risk factor for <a href="/wiki/Dyslipidemia" title="Dyslipidemia">dyslipidemia</a>, which in turn is a risk factor for some types of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are strong, consistent, and graded relationships between saturated fat intake, <a href="/wiki/Blood_cholesterol" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood cholesterol">blood cholesterol</a> levels, and the mass occurrence of cardiovascular disease. The relationships are accepted as causal.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Labarthe2011_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Labarthe2011-53">[53]</a></sup> Abnormal blood lipid levels, that is high total cholesterol, high levels of triglycerides, high levels of <a href="/wiki/Low-density_lipoprotein" title="Low-density lipoprotein">low-density lipoprotein</a> (LDL, "bad" cholesterol) or low levels of <a href="/wiki/High-density_lipoprotein" title="High-density lipoprotein">high-density lipoprotein</a> (HDL, "good" cholesterol) cholesterol are all associated with increased risk of heart disease and stroke.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldHeartFederation_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldHeartFederation-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Meta-analyses have found a significant relationship between saturated fat and serum cholesterol levels.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> High total cholesterol levels, which may be caused by many factors, are associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease.<sup id="cite_ref-Bucher1999_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bucher1999-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lewington2007_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewington2007-56">[56]</a></sup> However, other indicators measuring cholesterol such as high total/HDL cholesterol ratio are more predictive than total <a href="/wiki/Serum_cholesterol" class="mw-redirect" title="Serum cholesterol">serum cholesterol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewington2007_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewington2007-56">[56]</a></sup> In a study of <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> in 52 countries, the <a href="/wiki/Apolipoprotein_B" title="Apolipoprotein B">ApoB</a>/<a href="/wiki/Apolipoprotein_A1" title="Apolipoprotein A1">ApoA1</a> (related to LDL and HDL, respectively) ratio was the strongest predictor of CVD among all risk factors.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> There are other pathways involving <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglyceride</a> levels, <a href="/wiki/Insulin_resistance" title="Insulin resistance">insulin sensitivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial function</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thrombogenicity" title="Thrombogenicity">thrombogenicity</a>, among others, that play a role in CVD, although it seems, in the absence of an adverse blood lipid profile, the other known risk factors have only a weak atherogenic effect.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Different saturated fatty acids have differing effects on various lipid levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Thijssen_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thijssen-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer">Cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breast_cancer">Breast cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breast cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology_and_etiology_of_breast_cancer#Specific_dietary_fatty_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Epidemiology and etiology of breast cancer">Epidemiology and etiology of breast cancer §&#160;Specific dietary fatty acids</a></div>
<p>A meta-analysis published in 2003 found a significant positive relationship in both control and cohort studies between saturated fat and breast cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However two subsequent reviews have found weak or insignificant associations of saturated fat intake and breast cancer risk,<sup id="cite_ref-Hanf_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanf-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and note the prevalence of confounding factors.<sup id="cite_ref-Hanf_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hanf-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colorectal_cancer">Colorectal cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Colorectal cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One review found limited evidence for a positive relationship between consuming animal fat and incidence of colorectal cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ovarian_cancer">Ovarian cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ovarian cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A meta-analysis of eight observational studies published in 2001 found a statistically significant positive relationship between saturated fat and ovarian cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> However, a 2013 study found that a pooled analysis of 12 cohort studies observed no association between total fat intake and ovarian cancer risk. Further analysis revealed that omega-3 fatty acids were protective against ovarian cancer and that trans fats were a risk factor.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> This study revealed that histological subtypes should be examined in determining the impact of dietary fat on ovarian cancer, rather than an oversimplified focus on total fat intake.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prostate_cancer">Prostate cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prostate cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer#Oils_and_fatty_acids" title="Prostate cancer">Prostate cancer §&#160;Oils and fatty acids</a></div>
<p>Some researchers have indicated that serum <a href="/wiki/Myristic_acid" title="Myristic acid">myristic acid</a><sup id="cite_ref-pmid14693732_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14693732-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-saturated_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saturated-68">[68]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic acid</a><sup id="cite_ref-saturated_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saturated-68">[68]</a></sup> and dietary myristic<sup id="cite_ref-palmiticmyristic_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-palmiticmyristic-69">[69]</a></sup> and palmitic<sup id="cite_ref-palmiticmyristic_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-palmiticmyristic-69">[69]</a></sup> saturated fatty acids and serum palmitic combined with <a href="/wiki/Tocopherol#Alpha-tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">alpha-tocopherol</a> supplementation<sup id="cite_ref-pmid14693732_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14693732-67">[67]</a></sup> are associated with increased risk of prostate cancer in a dose-dependent manner. These associations may, however, reflect differences in intake or metabolism of these fatty acids between the precancer cases and controls, rather than being an actual cause.<sup id="cite_ref-saturated_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saturated-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bones">Bones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mounting evidence indicates that the amount and type of fat in the diet can have important effects on bone health. Most of this evidence is derived from animal studies. The data from one study indicated that bone mineral density is negatively associated with saturated fat intake, and that men may be particularly vulnerable.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_recommendations">Dietary recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dietary recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recommendations to reduce or limit dietary intake of saturated fats are made by the World Health Organization,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Health Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> the US Department of Health and Human Services,<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> the UK Food Standards Agency,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> the Australian Department of Health and Aging,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> the Singapore Government Health Promotion Board,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> the Indian Government Citizens Health Portal,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> the New Zealand Ministry of Health,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> the Food and Drugs Board Ghana,<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> the Republic of Guyana Ministry of Health,<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and Hong Kong's Centre for Food Safety.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, the World Health Organization (WHO) and Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) expert consultation report concluded that "intake of saturated fatty acids is directly related to cardiovascular risk. The traditional target is to restrict the intake of saturated fatty acids to less than 10% of daily energy intake and less than 7% for high-risk groups. If populations are consuming less than 10%, they should not increase that level of intake. Within these limits, intake of foods rich in myristic and palmitic acids should be replaced by fats with a lower content of these particular fatty acids. In developing countries, however, where energy intake for some population groups may be inadequate, energy expenditure is high and body fat stores are low (BMI &lt;18.5 kg/m<sup>2</sup>). The amount and quality of fat supply has to be considered keeping in mind the need to meet energy requirements. Specific sources of saturated fat, such as coconut and palm oil, provide low-cost energy and may be an important source of energy for the poor."<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2004 statement released by the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Centers for Disease Control">Centers for Disease Control</a> (CDC) determined that "Americans need to continue working to reduce saturated fat intake…"<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In addition, reviews by the <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a> led the Association to recommend reducing saturated fat intake to less than 7% of total calories according to its 2006 recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> This concurs with similar conclusions made by the US <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a>, which determined that reduction in saturated fat consumption would positively affect health and reduce the prevalence of heart disease.<sup id="cite_ref-DGfA_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DGfA-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> claims the majority of UK people eat too much saturated fat. The <a href="/wiki/British_Heart_Foundation" title="British Heart Foundation">British Heart Foundation</a> also advises people to cut down on saturated fat. People are advised to cut down on saturated fat and read labels on food they buy.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dr. German and Dr. Dillard of University of California and Nestle Research Center in Switzerland, in their 2004 review, pointed out that "no lower safe limit of specific saturated fatty acid intakes has been identified" and recommended that the influence of varying saturated fatty acid intakes against a background of different individual lifestyles and genetic backgrounds should be the focus in future studies.<sup id="cite_ref-German_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-German-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>Blanket recommendations to lower saturated fat were criticized at a 2010 conference debate of the <a href="/wiki/American_Dietetic_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="American Dietetic Association">American Dietetic Association</a> for focusing too narrowly on reducing saturated fats rather than emphasizing increased consumption of healthy fats and unrefined carbohydrates. Concern was expressed over the health risks of replacing saturated fats in the diet with refined carbohydrates, which carry a high risk of obesity and heart disease, particularly at the expense of <a href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fat" title="Polyunsaturated fat">polyunsaturated fats</a> which may have health benefits. None of the panelists recommended heavy consumption of saturated fats, emphasizing instead the importance of overall dietary quality to cardiovascular health.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_description">Molecular description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Molecular description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It should be noted, as this is the defining factor of <b>saturated</b> fats, that the two-dimensional illustration has implicit hydrogen atoms bonded to each of the carbon atoms in the polycarbon tail of the <a href="/wiki/Myristic_acid" title="Myristic acid">myristic acid</a> molecule (there are 13 carbon atoms in the tail; 14 carbon atoms in the entire molecule).</p>
<p>Carbon atoms are also implicitly drawn, as they are portrayed as <a href="/wiki/Line-line_intersection" class="mw-redirect" title="Line-line intersection">intersections</a> between two straight lines. "Saturated," in general, refers to a maximum number of hydrogen atoms bonded to each carbon of the polycarbon tail as allowed by the <a href="/wiki/Octet_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Octet Rule">Octet Rule</a>. This also means that only <a href="/wiki/Single_bond" title="Single bond">single bonds</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sigma_bonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Sigma bonds">sigma bonds</a>) will be present between adjacent carbon atoms of the tail.</p>
