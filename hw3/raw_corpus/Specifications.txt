			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Specification (technical standard)</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Specifications&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Specifications">Specifications</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Specification" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Specification_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Specification (disambiguation)">Specification (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>There are different types of specifications, which generally are mostly types of documents, forms or orders or relates to information in databases. The word <i>specification</i> is defined as "to state explicitly or in detail" or "to be specific". A specification may refer to a type of <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standard</a> (the main topic of this page).</p>
<p>Using a word "specification" without additional information to what kind of specification you refer to is confusing and considered bad practice within <a href="/wiki/Systems_engineering" title="Systems engineering">systems engineering</a>.</p>
<p>A <b>requirement specification</b> is a set of documented <a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">requirements</a> to be satisfied by a material, design, product, or service.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <b>functional specification</b> is closely related to the requirement specification and may show functional block diagrams.</p>
<p>A <b>design or product specification</b> describes the features of the solutions for the Requirement Specification, referring to the designed solution <b>or</b> final produced solution. Sometimes the term <i>specification</i> is here used in connection with a <a href="/wiki/Data_sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Data sheet">data sheet</a> (or <i>spec sheet</i>). This may be confusing. A data sheet describes the technical characteristics of an item or product as designed and/or produced. It can be published by a manufacturer to help people choose products or to help use the products. A data sheet is not a technical specification as described in this article.</p>
<p>A "<b>in-service</b>" or "<b>maintained as</b>" <b>specification</b>, specifies the conditions of a system or object after years of operation, including the effects of wear and maintenance (configuration changes).</p>
<p>Specifications may also refer to technical standards, which may be developed by any of various kinds of organizations, both <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private</a>. Example organization types include a <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Consortium" title="Consortium">consortium</a> (a small group of corporations), a <a href="/wiki/Trade_association" title="Trade association">trade association</a> (an industry-wide group of corporations), a national government (including its <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_agency" title="Regulatory agency">regulatory agencies</a>, and national laboratories and institutes), a <a href="/wiki/Professional_association" title="Professional association">professional association</a> (society), a purpose-made <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organization</a> such as <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>, or vendor-neutral developed generic requirements. It is common for one organization to <i>refer to</i> (<i>reference</i>, <i>call out</i>, <i>cite</i>) the standards of another. Voluntary standards may become mandatory if adopted by a government or business contract.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, it is vital for <a href="/wiki/Suppliers" class="mw-redirect" title="Suppliers">suppliers</a>, purchasers, and users of materials, products, or services to understand and agree upon all requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A specification is a type of a <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standard</a> which is often referenced by a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> or procurement document. It provides the necessary details about the specific requirements.</p>
<p>Specifications may be written by government agencies, standards organizations (<a href="/wiki/ASTM" class="mw-redirect" title="ASTM">ASTM</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Committee_for_Standardization" title="European Committee for Standardization">CEN</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Standard" title="United States Military Standard">DoD</a>, etc.), trade associations, corporations, and others.</p>
<p>A product specification does not necessarily prove a product to be correct or useful. An item might be <a href="/wiki/Verification_and_validation" title="Verification and validation">verified</a> to comply with a specification or stamped with a specification number: This does not, by itself, indicate that the item is fit for any particular use. The people who use the item (engineers, trade unions, etc.) or specify the item (building codes, government, industry, etc.) have the responsibility to consider the choice of available specifications, specify the correct one, enforce compliance, and use the item correctly. <a href="/wiki/Verification_and_Validation" class="mw-redirect" title="Verification and Validation">Validation</a> of suitability is necessary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidance_and_content">Guidance and content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Guidance and content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sometimes a guide or a <a href="/wiki/Standard_operating_procedure" title="Standard operating procedure">standard operating procedure</a> is available to help write and format a good specification.<sup id="cite_ref-eswg_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eswg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-agws_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agws-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MilSpec_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilSpec-5">[5]</a></sup> A specification might include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Descriptive title, number, <a href="/wiki/Identifier" title="Identifier">identifier</a>, etc. of the specification</li>
<li>Date of last effective revision and revision designation</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> to indicate the document <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a> and origin<sup id="cite_ref-gsg_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gsg-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Table of Contents (TOC), if the document is long</li>
<li>Person, office, or agency responsible for questions on the specification, updates, and deviations.</li>
<li>The significance, scope or importance of the specification and its intended use.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terminology" title="Terminology">Terminology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Definitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Definitions">definitions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abbreviations" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbreviations">abbreviations</a> to clarify the meanings of the specification<sup id="cite_ref-iso1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rabt_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rabt-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Test_method" title="Test method">Test methods</a> for measuring all specified characteristics</li>
<li>Material requirements: physical, mechanical, electrical, chemical, etc. Targets and <a href="/wiki/Tolerance_(engineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tolerance (engineering)">tolerances</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acceptance_testing" title="Acceptance testing">Acceptance testing</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Performance_test_(assessment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Performance test (assessment)">Performance testing</a> requirements. Targets and <a href="/wiki/Tolerance_(engineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tolerance (engineering)">tolerances</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drawings" class="mw-redirect" title="Drawings">Drawings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">photographs</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Technical_illustration" title="Technical illustration">technical illustrations</a></li>
<li>Workmanship</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Certification" title="Certification">Certifications</a> required.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">Safety</a> considerations and requirements</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">Environmental</a> considerations and requirements</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">Quality control</a> requirements, <a href="/wiki/Acceptance_sampling" title="Acceptance sampling">acceptance sampling</a>, inspections, acceptance criteria</li>
<li>Person, office, or agency responsible for enforcement of the specification.</li>
<li>Completion and delivery.</li>
<li>Provisions for rejection, reinspection, rehearing, corrective measures</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/References" class="mw-redirect" title="References">References</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citation" title="Citation">citations</a> for which any instructions in the content maybe required to fulfill the <a href="/wiki/Traceability" title="Traceability">traceability</a> and clarity of the document<sup id="cite_ref-rabt_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rabt-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dbrc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dbrc-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibrs_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibrs-10">[10]</a></sup></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Change_control" title="Change control">Change record</a> to summarize the chronological development, revision and completion if the document is to be circulated internally<sup id="cite_ref-pdfs_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pdfs-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Addendum" title="Addendum">Annexes and Appendices</a> that are expand details, add clarification, or offer options.<sup id="cite_ref-pdfs_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pdfs-12">[12]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_specifications_in_North_America">Construction specifications in North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction specifications in North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specifications in North America form part of the contract documents that accompany and govern the construction of building] and infrastructure projects. Specifications describe the quality and performance of building materials, using code citations and published standards, whereas the drawings or Building Information Model (BIM) illustrates quantity and location of materials. The guiding master document of names and numbers is the latest edition of <a href="/wiki/MasterFormat" title="MasterFormat">MasterFormat</a>. This is a consensus document that is jointly sponsored by two professional organizations: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Construction_Specifications_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Construction Specifications Canada (page does not exist)">Construction Specifications Canada</a> and Construction Specifications Institute based in the United States and updated every two years.</p>
<p>While there is a tendency to believe that "Specifications overrule Drawings" in the event of discrepancies between the text document and the drawings, the actual intent must be made explicit in the contract between the Owner and the Contractor. The standard AIA (American Institute of Architects) and EJCDC (Engineering Joint Contract Documents Committee) states that the drawings and specifications are complementary, together providing the information required for a complete facility. Many public agencies, such as the Naval Facilities Command (NAVFAC) state that the specifications overrule the drawings. This is based on the idea that words are easier for a jury (or mediator) to interpret than drawings in case of a dispute.</p>
<p>The standard listing of construction specifications falls into <a href="/wiki/50_Divisions" title="50 Divisions">50 Divisions</a>, or broad categories of work types and work results involved in construction. The divisions are subdivided into sections, each one addressing a specific material type (concrete) or a work product (steel door) of the construction work. A specific material may be covered in several locations, depending on the work result: stainless steel (for example) can be covered as a sheet material used in Flashing and Sheet Metal in Division 07; it can be part of a finished product, such as a handrail, covered in Division 05; or it can be a component of building hardware, covered in Division 08. The original listing of specification divisions was based on the time sequence of construction, working from exterior to interior, and this logic is still somewhat followed as new materials and systems make their way into the construction process.</p>
<p>Each Section is subdivided into three distinct Parts: "General", "Products" and "Execution". The MasterFormat and Section Format <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>system can be successfully applied to residential, commercial, civil, and industrial construction. Although many Architects find the rather voluminous commercial style of specifications too lengthy for most residential projects and therefore either produce more abbreviated specifications of their own or use ArCHspec (which was specifically created for residential projects). Master specification systems are available from multiple vendors such as Arcom, Visispec, BSD, and Spectext. These systems were created to standardize language across the United States and are usually subscription based.</p>
<p>Specifications can be either "performance-based", whereby the specifier restricts the text to stating the performance that must be achieved by the completed work, "prescriptive" where the specifier states the specific criteria such as fabrication standards applicable to the item, or "proprietary" whereby the specifier indicates specific products, vendors and even contractors that are acceptable for each workscope. In addition, specifications can be "closed" with a specific list of products, or "open" allowing for substitutions made by the Contractor. Most construction specifications are a combination of performance-based and proprietrary types, naming acceptable manufacturers and products while also specifying certain standards and design criteria that must be met.</p>
<p>While North American specifications are usually restricted to broad descriptions of the work, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> ones and Civil work can include actual work quantities, including such things as <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> of <a href="/wiki/Drywall" title="Drywall">drywall</a> to be built in square meters, like a bill of materials. This type of specification is a collaborative effort between a specwriter and a <a href="/wiki/Quantity_surveyor" title="Quantity surveyor">quantity surveyor</a>. This approach is unusual in North America, where each bidder performs a quantity survey on the basis of both drawings and specifications. In many countries on the European continent, content that might be described as "specifications" in the United States are covered under the building code or municipal code. Civil and infrastructure work in the United States often includes a quantity breakdown of the work to be performed as well.</p>
<p>Although specifications are usually issued by the <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a>'s office, specification writing itself is undertaken by the architect and the various <a href="/wiki/Architectural_engineering" title="Architectural engineering">engineers</a> or by specialist specification writers. Specification writing is often a distinct professional trade, with professional certifications such as "Certified Construction Specifier" (CCS) available through the Construction Specifications Institute and the Registered Specification Writer (RSW)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> through Construction Specifications Canada. Specification writers are either employees of or sub-contractors to architects, engineers, or construction management companies. Specification writers frequently meet with manufacturers of <a href="/wiki/Building_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Building materials">building materials</a> who seek to have their products specified on upcoming construction projects so that contractors can include their products in the estimates leading to their proposals.</p>
<p>In February 2015, ArCHspec went live, from ArCH (Architects Creating Homes), a nationwide American professional society of Architects whose purpose is to improve residential architecture. ArCHspec was created specifically for use by Licensed Architects while designing SFR (Single Family Residential) architectural projects. Unlike the more commercial CSI (50+ division commercial specifications), ArCHspec utilizes the more recognizable 16 traditional Divisions, plus a Division 0 (Scope &amp; Bid Forms) and Division 17 (low voltage). Many Architects, up to this point, did not provide specifications for residential designs, which is one of the reasons ArCHspec was created: to fill a void in the industry with more compact specifications for residential use. Shorter form specifications documents suitable for residential use are also available through Arcom, and follow the 50 division format, which was adopted in both the United States and Canada starting in 2004. The 16 division format is no longer considered standard, and is not supported by either CSI or CSC, or any of the subscription master specification services, data repositories, product lead systems, and the bulk of governmental agencies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_specifications_in_the_UK">Construction specifications in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Construction specifications in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specifications in the UK are part of the contract documents that accompany and govern the construction of a building. They are prepared by construction professionals such as <a href="/wiki/Architects" class="mw-redirect" title="Architects">architects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architectural_technologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Architectural technologists">architectural technologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineer" title="Structural engineer">structural engineers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">landscape architects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Building_services_engineering" title="Building services engineering">building services engineers</a>. They are created from previous project specifications, in-house documents or master specifications such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Specification" title="National Building Specification">National Building Specification</a> (NBS). The National Building Specification is owned by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">Royal Institute of British Architects</a> (RIBA) through their commercial group RIBA Enterprises (RIBAe). NBS master specifications provide content that is broad and comprehensive, and delivered using software functionality that enables specifiers to customize the content to suit the needs of the project and to keep up to date. UK project specification types fall into two main categories prescriptive and performance. Prescriptive specifications define the requirements using generic or proprietary descriptions of what is required, whereas performance specifications focus on the outcomes rather than the characteristics of the components. Specifications are an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Building_Information_Modeling" class="mw-redirect" title="Building Information Modeling">Building Information Modeling</a> and cover the non-geometric requirements.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_specifications_in_other_countries">Construction specifications in other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Construction specifications in other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Egypt</b>&#160;: Specifications are part of contract documents. The Housing and Building National Research Center "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.hbrc.edu.eg/en/Home.html">HBRC</a>" is responsible for developing construction specifications and codes. The HBRC had published more than 15 books which cover building activities like (Earthworks - Plastering - etc.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_drug_specifications">Food and drug specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food and drug specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pharmaceutical products can usually be tested and qualified by various <a href="/wiki/Pharmacopoeia" title="Pharmacopoeia">Pharmacopoeia</a>. Current existing pronounced standards include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Pharmacopoeia" title="British Pharmacopoeia">British Pharmacopoeia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Pharmacopoeia" title="European Pharmacopoeia">European Pharmacopoeia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Japanese_Pharmacopoeia" title="Japanese Pharmacopoeia">Japanese Pharmacopoeia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_International_Pharmacopoeia" title="The International Pharmacopoeia">The International Pharmacopoeia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia" title="United States Pharmacopeia">United States Pharmacopeia</a></li>
</ul>
<p>If any pharmaceutical product is not covered by the above <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standards</a>, it can be evaluated by the additional source of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacopoeia" title="Pharmacopoeia">Pharmacopoeia</a> from other nations, from industrial specifications, or from a standardized <a href="/wiki/Formulary_(pharmacy)" title="Formulary (pharmacy)">formulary</a> such as</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_National_Formulary_for_Children" title="British National Formulary for Children">British National Formulary for Children</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_National_Formulary" title="British National Formulary">British National Formulary</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Formulary" class="mw-redirect" title="National Formulary">National Formulary</a></li>
</ul>
<p>A similar approach is adopted by the food manufacturing, of which <a href="/wiki/Codex_Alimentarius" title="Codex Alimentarius">Codex Alimentarius</a> ranks the highest standards, followed by regional and national standards.<sup id="cite_ref-anzfsc_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anzfsc-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The coverage of food and drug <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standards</a> by <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> is currently less fruitful and not yet put forward as an urgent agenda due to the tight restrictions of regional or national constitution<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Specifications and other standards can be externally imposed as discussed above, but also internal manufacturing and quality specifications. These exist not only for the <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> product but also for the processing <a href="/wiki/Machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> processes, <a href="/wiki/Packaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Packaging">packaging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cold_chain" title="Cold chain">cold chain</a>), etc. and are exemplified by <i>ISO 14134</i> and <i>ISO 15609</i><sup id="cite_ref-oois_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oois-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sqwp_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sqwp-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The converse of explicit statement of specifications is a process for dealing with observations that are out-of-specification. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">United States Food and Drug Administration</a> has published a non-binding recommendation that addresses just this point.<sup id="cite_ref-CDER2006_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDER2006-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the present time, much of the information and regulations concerning food and food products remain in a form which makes it difficult to apply automated information processing, storage and transmission methods and techniques.</p>
<p>Data systems that can process, store and transfer information about food and food products need formal specifications for the representations of data about food and food products in order to operate effectively and efficiently.</p>
<p>Development of formal specifications for food and drug data with the necessary and sufficient clarity and precision for use specifically by digital computing systems have begun to emerge from government agencies and standards organizations.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">United States Food and Drug Administration</a> has published specifications for a "Structured Product Label" which drug manufacturers must by mandate use to submit electronically the information on a drug label.<sup id="cite_ref-fdaspl_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fdaspl-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> has made some progress in the area of food and drug <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standards</a> and formal specifications for data about regulated substances through the publication of <i>ISO 11238</i><sup id="cite_ref-iso11238_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso11238-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_technology">Information technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Information technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specification_need">Specification need</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Specification need">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Specifications are needed to avoid errors due to lack of compatibility, for instance, in interoperability issues.</p>
<p>For instance, when two applications share Unicode data, but use different normal forms or use them incorrectly, in an incompatible way or without sharing a minimum set of interoperability specification, errors and data loss can result. For example, Mac OS X has many components that prefer or require only decomposed characters (thus decomposed-only Unicode encoded with UTF-8 is also known as "UTF8-MAC"). In one specific instance, the combination of OS X errors handling composed characters, and the <a href="/wiki/Samba_(software)" title="Samba (software)">samba</a> file- and printer-sharing software (which replaces decomposed letters with composed ones when copying file names), has led to confusing and data-destroying interoperability problems.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Applications may avoid such errors by preserving input code points, and only normalizing them to the application's preferred normal form for internal use.</p>
<p>Such errors may also be avoided with algorithms normalizing both strings before any binary comparison.</p>
<p>However errors due to file name encoding incompatibilities have always existed, due to a lack of minimum set of common specification between software hoped to be inter-operable between various file system drivers, operating systems, network protocols, and thousands of software packages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_specification">Formal specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Formal specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Formal_specification" title="Formal specification">Formal specification</a></div>
<p>A <b>formal specification</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> description of <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> or <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">hardware</a> that may be used to develop an <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">implementation</a>. It describes <i>what</i> the system should do, not (necessarily) <i>how</i> the system should do it. Given such a specification, it is possible to use <a href="/wiki/Formal_verification" title="Formal verification">formal verification</a> techniques to demonstrate that a candidate system design is correct with respect to that specification. This has the advantage that incorrect candidate system designs can be revised before a major investment has been made in actually implementing the design. An alternative approach is to use provably correct <a href="/wiki/Program_refinement" class="mw-redirect" title="Program refinement">refinement</a> steps to transform a specification into a design, and ultimately into an actual implementation, that is correct by construction.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_specification">Architectural specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Architectural specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In (hardware, software, or enterprise) systems development, an <b>architectural specification</b> is the set of <a href="/wiki/Documentation" title="Documentation">documentation</a> that describes the <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a>, and more <a href="/wiki/View_model" title="View model">views</a> of that <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Program_specification">Program specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Program specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>program specification</b> is the definition of what a <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> is expected to do. It can be <i>informal</i>, in which case it can be considered as a user manual from a developer point of view, or <i><a href="/wiki/Formal_specification" title="Formal specification">formal</a></i>, in which case it has a definite meaning defined in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> or programmatic terms. In practice, many successful specifications are written to understand and fine-tune applications that were already well-developed, although <a href="/wiki/Safety-critical" class="mw-redirect" title="Safety-critical">safety-critical</a> <a href="/wiki/Software_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Software systems">software systems</a> are often carefully specified prior to application development. Specifications are most important for external interfaces that must remain stable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functional_specification">Functional specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Functional specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Functional_specification" title="Functional specification">Functional specification</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a>, a <b>functional specification</b> (also, <b>functional spec</b> or <b>specs</b> or <b>functional specifications document (FSD)</b>) is the set of <a href="/wiki/Documentation" title="Documentation">documentation</a> that describes the behavior of a computer program or larger <a href="/wiki/Software_system" title="Software system">software system</a>. The documentation typically describes various inputs that can be provided to the <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> system and how the <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> responds to those inputs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Web_service_specification">Web service specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Web service specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Web_service_specifications" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Web service specifications">List of Web service specifications</a></div>
<p>Web services specifications are often under the umbrella of a <a href="/wiki/Quality_management_system" title="Quality management system">quality management system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mdsw_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdsw-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Document_specification">Document specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Specification_(technical_standard)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Document specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These types of documents define how a specific document should be written, which may include, but is not limited to, the systems of a document naming, version, layout, referencing, structuring, appearance, language, copyright, hierarchy or format, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-tsds_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tsds-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iect_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iect-27">[27]</a></sup> Very often, this kind of specifications is complemented by a designated template.<sup id="cite_ref-dtsp_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dtsp-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lsso_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lsso-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ssop_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ssop-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
