			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Lake</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>A <b>lake</b> is an area of variable size filled with water, localized in a <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">basin</a>, that is surrounded by land, apart from any <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">river</a> or other outlet that serves to feed or drain the lake.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Lakes lie on land and are not part of the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">ocean</a>, and therefore are distinct from <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, and are also larger and deeper than <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, though there are no official or scientific definitions.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lakes can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, which are usually flowing. Most lakes are fed and drained by rivers and streams.</p>
<p>Natural lakes are generally found in mountainous areas, <a href="/wiki/Rift_zone" title="Rift zone">rift zones</a>, and areas with ongoing <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. Other lakes are found in <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a> or along the courses of mature rivers. In some parts of the world there are many lakes because of chaotic drainage patterns left over from the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. All lakes are temporary over geologic time scales, as they will slowly fill in with sediments or spill out of the basin containing them.</p>
<p>Many lakes are artificial and are constructed for industrial or agricultural use, for <a href="/wiki/Hydro-electric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydro-electric power">hydro-electric power</a> generation or domestic water supply, or for aesthetic or recreational purposes or even for other activities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology.2C_meaning.2C_and_usage_of_.22lake.22">Etymology, meaning, and usage of "lake"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology, meaning, and usage of &quot;lake&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The word <i>lake</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>lake</i> ("lake, pond, waterway"), from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>lacu</i> ("pond, pool, stream"), from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>lakō</i> ("pond, ditch, slow moving stream"), from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root *<i>leǵ-</i> ("to leak, drain"). Cognates include <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>laak</i> ("lake, pond, ditch"), <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> <i>lāke</i> ("water pooled in a riverbed, puddle") as in: <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moorlake" class="extiw" title="de:Moorlake">de:Moorlake</a>, <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfslake" class="extiw" title="de:Wolfslake">de:Wolfslake</a>, <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterlake" class="extiw" title="de:Butterlake">de:Butterlake</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Lache</i> ("pool, puddle"), and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> <i>lækur</i> ("slow flowing stream"). Also related are the English words <i>leak</i> and <i>leach</i>.</p>
<p>There is considerable uncertainty about defining the difference between lakes and <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, and no current internationally accepted definition of either term across scientific disciplines or political boundaries exists.<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Limnologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Limnologist">limnologists</a> have defined lakes as water bodies which are simply a larger version of a pond, which can have wave action on the shoreline or where wind-induced turbulence plays a major role in mixing the water column. None of these definitions completely excludes ponds and all are difficult to measure. For this reason, simple size-based definitions are increasingly used to separate ponds and lakes. One definition of <i>lake</i> is a body of water of 2 hectares (5 acres) or more in area;<sup id="cite_ref-BiologicalConservation1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BiologicalConservation1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:331</sup><sup id="cite_ref-BiologicalReviews1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BiologicalReviews1-6">[6]</a></sup> however, others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have defined lakes as waterbodies of 5 hectares (12 acres) and above,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or 8 hectares (20 acres) and above <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (see also the <a href="/wiki/Pond#Technical_definitions" title="Pond">definition of "pond"</a>). <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton</a>, one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, regarded lakes as waterbodies of 40 hectares (99 acres) or more.<sup id="cite_ref-JournalOfEcology1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JournalOfEcology1-8">[8]</a></sup> The term <i>lake</i> is also used to describe a feature such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a>, which is a dry basin most of the time but may become filled under seasonal conditions of heavy rainfall. In common usage, many lakes bear names ending with the word <i>pond</i>, and a lesser number of names ending with <i>lake</i> are in quasi-technical fact, ponds. One textbook illustrates this point with the following: "In Newfoundland, for example, almost every lake is called a pond, whereas in Wisconsin, almost every pond is called a lake."<sup id="cite_ref-Cech2009_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cech2009-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>One <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> book proposes to define the term "lake" as a body of water with the following five characteristics:<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>it partially or totally fills one or several basins connected by straits<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>has essentially the same water level in all parts (except for relatively short-lived variations caused by wind, varying ice cover, large inflows, etc.)<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>it does not have regular intrusion of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a><sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>a considerable portion of the sediment suspended in the water is captured by the basins (for this to happen they need to have a sufficiently small inflow-to-volume ratio)<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>the area measured at the mean water level exceeds an arbitrarily chosen threshold (for instance, one <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>With the exception of the seawater intrusion criterion, the others have been accepted or elaborated upon by other hydrology publications.<sup id="cite_ref-Shahin2002_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shahin2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Copernicus2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Copernicus2004-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_of_lakes">Distribution of lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution of lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The majority of lakes on Earth are <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, and most lie in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> at higher <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(geomorphology)#Deranged_drainage_pattern" title="Drainage system (geomorphology)">deranged drainage system</a> has an estimated 31,752 lakes larger than 3 square kilometres (1.2&#160;sq&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and an unknown total number of lakes, but is estimated to be at least 2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> has 187,888 lakes 500 square metres (5,400&#160;sq&#160;ft) or larger, of which 56,000 are large (10,000 square metres (110,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) or larger).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most lakes have at least one natural outflow in the form of a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a>, which maintain a lake's average level by allowing the drainage of excess water.<sup id="cite_ref-fenh_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-15">[15]</a></sup> Some lakes do not have a natural outflow and lose water solely by evaporation or underground seepage or both. They are termed <a href="/wiki/Endorheic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a> lakes.</p>
<p>Many lakes are artificial and are constructed for <a href="/wiki/Hydro-electric" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydro-electric">hydro-electric</a> power generation, aesthetic purposes, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreational</a> purposes, <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> use, <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> use or domestic water supply.</p>
<p>Evidence of extraterrestrial lakes exists; "definitive evidence of lakes filled with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>" was announced by NASA<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as returned by the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini Probe</a> observing the moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, which orbits the planet <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.</p>
<p>Globally, lakes are greatly outnumbered by ponds: of an estimated 304 million standing water bodies worldwide, 91% are 1 hectare (2.5 acres) or less in area (see definition of <a href="/wiki/Pond#Technical_definitions" title="Pond">ponds</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-DowningPrairie2006_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DowningPrairie2006-16">[16]</a></sup> Small lakes are also much more numerous than large lakes: in terms of area, one-third of the world's standing water is represented by lakes and ponds of 10 hectares (25 acres) or less.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, large lakes account for much of the area of standing water with 122 large lakes of 1,000 square kilometres (390 sq mi, 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>, 247,000 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>) or more representing about 29% of the total global area of standing inland water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Since they progressively become filled by sediment, lakes are considered ephemeral over geological time scales, and long-living lakes imply that active processes keep forming the basins in which they form.<sup id="cite_ref-GSA2006_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSA2006-17">[17]</a></sup> There are a number of natural processes that can form lakes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_lakes">Tectonic lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tectonic lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The longest-living lakes on Earth are related to tectonic processes which is created due to a <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> uplift of a mountain range which create depressions that accumulate water and form lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-GSA2006_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSA2006-17">[17]</a></sup> The forming process of tectonic lake is: the earth surface, perhaps the high mountain and plateau, or the hills or plain - there appeared rupture on the ground, and depression along rupture direction - water begins to be stored gradually, and form the lake. Examples of tectonic lakes include Lake Balaton (Central Europe), Holxil Lake, Zonag Lake, and Kusai Lake (In the depression belt between Aerdaijin Mountains and Holxil Mountains in Qinghai Province, China), Xijir Ulan Lake, Ulan Ul Lake, and Duogecuoren Lake (Holxil Mountains and Tanggula Mountains, China), Ngoring Lake and Gyaring Lake (In the upper reaches of the Yellow River, China).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landslide_and_ice-dam_lakes">Landslide and ice-dam lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Landslide and ice-dam lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lakes can also form by means of landslides or by glacial blockages. An example of the latter occurred during the last ice age in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>, when a huge lake formed behind a glacial flow; when the ice retreated, the result was an immense flood that created the <a href="/wiki/Dry_Falls" title="Dry Falls">Dry Falls</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Lakes_State_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Sun Lakes State Park">Sun Lakes</a>, Washington.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salt_lakes">Salt lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Salt lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salt_lake_(geography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt lake (geography)">Salt lakes</a> (also called <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> lakes) can form where there is no natural outlet or where the water evaporates rapidly and the drainage surface of the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> has a higher-than-normal <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> content. Examples of salt lakes include <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxbow_lakes">Oxbow lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oxbow lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Small, crescent-shaped lakes called <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lakes</a> can form in river valleys as a result of meandering. The slow-moving river forms a sinuous shape as the outer side of bends are eroded away more rapidly than the inner side. Eventually a horseshoe bend is formed and the river cuts through the narrow neck. This new passage then forms the main passage for the river and the ends of the bend become silted up, thus forming a bow-shaped lake.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crater_lakes">Crater lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Crater lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">Crater lakes</a> are formed in <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">volcanic craters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">calderas</a> which fill up with precipitation more rapidly than they empty via evaporation. Sometimes the latter are called caldera lakes, although often no distinction is made. An example is <a href="/wiki/Crater_Lake" title="Crater Lake">Crater Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, in the caldera of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Mazama" title="Mount Mazama">Mount Mazama</a>. The caldera was created in a massive volcanic eruption that led to the <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> of Mount Mazama around 4860 BC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_lakes">Glacial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Glacial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The advance and retreat of glaciers can scrape depressions in the surface where water accumulates; such lakes are common in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and Canada. The most notable examples are probably the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> of North America. As a particular case, <a href="/wiki/Gloe_lakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloe lakes">gloe lakes</a> are basins that have emerged from the sea as a consequence of post-glacial rebound, and are now filled with freshwater.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_lakes">Other lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some lakes, such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Jackson_(Tallahassee,_Florida)" title="Lake Jackson (Tallahassee, Florida)">Lake Jackson</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, USA, come into existence as a result of <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a> activity.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Subglacial_lake" title="Subglacial lake">subglacial lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, possibly the largest in the world. The pressure from the ice atop it and its internal chemical composition mean that, if the lake were drilled into, a fissure could result that would spray somewhat like a <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geyser</a>.</p>
<p>Most lakes are geologically young and shrinking since the natural results of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> will tend to wear away the sides and fill the basin.<sup id="cite_ref-GSA2006_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSA2006-17">[17]</a></sup> Exceptions are those such as Lake Baikal and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a> that lie along continental <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift zones</a> and are created by the crust's <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> as two plates are pulled apart. These lakes are the oldest and deepest in the world. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, which is 25–30 million years old, is deepening at a faster rate than it is being filled by erosion and may be destined over millions of years to become attached to the <a href="/wiki/Global_ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Global ocean">global ocean</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, for example, is thought to have originated as a rift-valley lake.</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tectonic_lake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tectonic lake (page does not exist)">Tectonic lake</a>: Owing to the internal force action of the earth's crust, there produce the tectonic lake basins, which store water and transform into tectonic lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Periglacial_lake" title="Periglacial lake">Periglacial lake</a>: Part of the lake's margin is formed by an <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice cap</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a>, the ice having obstructed the natural <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> of the land.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Subglacial_lake" title="Subglacial lake">Subglacial lake</a>: A lake which is permanently covered by ice. They can occur under glaciers, ice caps or ice sheets. There are many such lakes, but <a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is by far the largest. They are kept liquid because the overlying ice acts as a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulator" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal insulator">thermal insulator</a> retaining energy introduced to its underside by <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, by water percolating through crevasses, by the pressure from the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of the ice sheet above or by <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a> below.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">Glacial lake</a>: a lake with origins in a melted glacier, such as a <a href="/wiki/Kettle_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Kettle lake">kettle lake</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Artificial_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial lake">Artificial lake</a>: A lake created by flooding land behind a <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dam</a>, called an impoundment or <a href="/wiki/Reservoir_(water)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reservoir (water)">reservoir</a>, by deliberate human excavation, or by the flooding of an excavation incident to a mineral-extraction operation such as an <a href="/wiki/Open-pit_mining" title="Open-pit mining">open pit mine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarry</a>. Some of the world's largest lakes are reservoirs like <a href="/wiki/Hirakud_Dam" title="Hirakud Dam">Hirakud Dam</a> in India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">Endorheic lake</a>, terminal or closed: A lake which has no significant outflow, either through rivers or underground diffusion. Any water within an endorheic basin leaves the system only through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Seepage#Permeability_and_seepage" class="mw-redirect" title="Seepage">seepage</a>. These lakes, such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> in central Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> in central Asia, or the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a> in the Western United States, are most common in deserts.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meromictic_lake" title="Meromictic lake">Meromictic lake</a>: A lake which has layers of water which do not intermix. The deepest layer of water in such a lake does not contain any dissolved oxygen. The layers of sediment at the bottom of a meromictic lake remain relatively undisturbed because there are no living <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerobic organisms">aerobic organisms</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">Fjord lake</a>: A lake in a glacially eroded valley that has been eroded below sea level.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">Oxbow lake</a>: A lake which is formed when a wide meander from a stream or a river is cut off to form a lake. They are called "oxbow" lakes due to the distinctive curved shape that results from this process.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rift_lake" title="Rift lake">Rift lake</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sag_pond" title="Sag pond">sag pond</a>: A lake which forms as a result of subsidence along a geological fault in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a>. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Rift_Valley_lakes" title="Rift Valley lakes">Rift Valley lakes</a> of eastern Africa and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Underground_lake" title="Underground lake">Underground lake</a>: A lake which is formed under the surface of the Earth's crust. Such a lake may be associated with <a href="/wiki/Caves" class="mw-redirect" title="Caves">caves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">springs</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">Crater lake</a>: A lake which forms in a volcanic <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> or crater after the volcano has been inactive for some time. Water in this type of lake may be <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh</a> or highly <a href="/wiki/Acidic" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidic">acidic</a>, and may contain various dissolved <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">minerals</a>. Some also have <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal (geology)">geothermal</a> activity, especially if the volcano is merely dormant rather than extinct.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lava_lake" title="Lava lake">Lava lake</a>: A pool of molten lava contained in a volcanic crater or other depression. Lava lakes that have partly or completely solidified are also referred to as lava lakes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Former_lakes" title="Category:Former lakes">Former</a>: A lake which is no longer in existence. Such lakes include <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> lakes and lakes which have permanently dried up through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> or human intervention. <a href="/wiki/Owens_Lake" title="Owens Lake">Owens Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, USA, is an example of a former lake. Former lakes are a common feature of the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range</a> area of southwestern North America.</li>
<li>Ephemeral lake, intermittent lake, or seasonal lake: A seasonal lake that exists as a <a href="/wiki/Bodies_of_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodies of water">body of water</a> during part of the year.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Category:Shrunken_lakes" title="Category:Shrunken lakes">Shrunken</a>: Closely related to <i>former</i> lakes, a shrunken lake is one which has drastically decreased in size over geological time. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">Lake Agassiz</a>, which once covered much of central North America, is a good example of a shrunken lake. Two notable remnants of this lake are <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipegosis" title="Lake Winnipegosis">Lake Winnipegosis</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eolic_lake&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eolic lake (page does not exist)">Eolic lake</a>: A lake which forms in a depression created by the activity of the winds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vlei" title="Vlei">Vlei</a>, in South Africa, shallow lakes which vary considerably with seasons.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Epishelf_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Epishelf lake">Epishelf lakes</a>, unique lakes which exist on top of a dense saltwater body and are surrounded by ice. These are mostly found in the Antarctica.</li>
</ul>

<p>Lakes have numerous features in addition to lake type, such as <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> (also known as catchment area), inflow and outflow, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> content, <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissolved oxygen">dissolved oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollutants</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sedimentation</a>.</p>
<p>Changes in the level of a lake are controlled by the difference between the input and output compared to the total volume of the lake. Significant input sources are precipitation onto the lake, runoff carried by streams and channels from the lake's <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">catchment</a> area, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> channels and aquifers, and artificial sources from outside the catchment area. Output sources are evaporation from the lake, surface and groundwater flows, and any extraction of lake water by humans. As climate conditions and human water requirements vary, these will create fluctuations in the lake level.</p>
<p>Lakes can be also <a href="/wiki/Trophic_state_index" title="Trophic state index">categorized</a> on the basis of their richness in nutrients, which typically affect plant growth. Nutrient-poor lakes are said to be <i><a href="/wiki/Oligotroph" title="Oligotroph">oligotrophic</a></i> and are generally clear, having a low concentration of plant life. <i><a href="/wiki/Mesotrophic_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesotrophic lake">Mesotrophic lakes</a></i> have good clarity and an average level of nutrients. <i><a href="/wiki/Eutrophic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutrophic">Eutrophic</a></i> lakes are enriched with nutrients, resulting in good plant growth and possible <a href="/wiki/Algal_blooms" class="mw-redirect" title="Algal blooms">algal blooms</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">Hypertrophic</a></i> lakes are bodies of water that have been excessively enriched with nutrients. These lakes typically have poor clarity and are subject to devastating algal blooms. Lakes typically reach this condition due to human activities, such as heavy use of fertilizers in the lake catchment area. Such lakes are of little use to humans and have a poor ecosystem due to decreased dissolved oxygen.</p>
<p>Due to the unusual relationship between water's <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, lakes form layers called <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermoclines</a>, layers of drastically varying temperature relative to depth. Fresh water is most dense at about 4 degrees Celsius (39.2&#160;°F) at sea level. When the temperature of the water at the surface of a lake reaches the same temperature as deeper water, as it does during the cooler months in <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> climates, the water in the lake can mix, bringing oxygen-starved water up from the depths and bringing oxygen down to decomposing sediments. Deep temperate lakes can maintain a reservoir of cold water year-round, which allows some cities to tap that reservoir for <a href="/wiki/Deep_lake_water_cooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep lake water cooling">deep lake water cooling</a>.</p>

<p>Since the surface water of deep <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> lakes never reaches the temperature of maximum density, there is no process that makes the water mix. The deeper layer becomes oxygen starved and can become saturated with carbon dioxide, or other gases such as sulfur dioxide if there is even a trace of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a>. Exceptional events, such as earthquakes or landslides, can cause mixing which rapidly brings the deep layers up to the surface and release a vast cloud of gas which lay trapped in solution in the colder water at the bottom of the lake. This is called a <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">limnic eruption</a>. An example is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos#The_1986_disaster" title="Lake Nyos">the disaster at Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>. The amount of gas that can be dissolved in water is directly related to pressure. As deep water surfaces, the pressure drops and a vast amount of gas comes out of solution. Under these circumstances carbon dioxide is hazardous because it is heavier than air and displaces it, so it may flow down a river valley to human settlements and cause mass <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>.</p>
<p>The material at the bottom of a lake, or <i>lake bed</i>, may be composed of a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Inorganic" class="mw-redirect" title="Inorganic">inorganics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic material">organic material</a>, such as decaying plant or animal matter. The composition of the lake bed has a significant impact on the flora and fauna found within the lake's environs by contributing to the amounts and the types of nutrients available.</p>
<p>A paired (black and white) layer of the varved lake sediments correspond to a year. During winter, when organisms die, carbon is deposited down, resulting to a black layer. At the same year, during summer, only few organic materials are deposited, resulting to a white layer at the lake bed. These are commonly used to track past paleontological events.</p>
<p>Natural lakes provide a <a href="/wiki/Macrocosm_and_microcosm" title="Macrocosm and microcosm">microcosm</a> of living and nonliving elements that are relatively independent of their surrounding environments. Therefore, lake organisms can often be studied in isolation from the lake’s surroundings.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">Limnology</a> is the study of inland bodies of water and related ecosystems. Limnology divides lakes into three zones: the <i><a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">littoral zone</a></i>, a sloped area close to land; the <i><a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">photic</a></i> or <i>open-water zone</i>, where sunlight is abundant; and the deep-water <i><a href="/wiki/Profundal_zone" title="Profundal zone">profundal</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic zone</a></i>, where little sunlight can reach. The depth to which light can reach in lakes depends on <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>, determined by the density and size of suspended <a href="/wiki/Particle_(ecology)" title="Particle (ecology)">particles</a>. A particle is in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> if its weight is less than the random turbidity <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> acting upon it. These particles can be sedimentary or <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biological</a> in origin and are responsible for the color of the water. Decaying plant matter, for instance, may be responsible for a yellow or brown color, while algae may cause greenish water. In very shallow water bodies, iron oxides make water reddish brown. Biological particles include <a href="/wiki/Alga" class="mw-redirect" title="Alga">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. Bottom-dwelling detritivorous <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> can be responsible for turbid waters, because they stir the mud in search of food. <a href="/wiki/Piscivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Piscivorous">Piscivorous</a> fish contribute to turbidity by eating plant-eating (<a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonivorous</a>) fish, thus increasing the amount of algae (see aquatic <a href="/wiki/Trophic_cascade" title="Trophic cascade">trophic cascade</a>). The light depth or transparency is measured by using a <i><a href="/wiki/Secchi_disk" title="Secchi disk">Secchi disk</a></i>, a 20-cm (8&#160;in) disk with alternating white and black <a href="/wiki/Circular_sector" title="Circular sector">quadrants</a>. The depth at which the disk is no longer visible is the <i>Secchi depth</i>, a measure of transparency. The Secchi disk is commonly used to test for eutrophication. For a detailed look at these processes, see <a href="/wiki/Lentic_ecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Lentic ecosystems">lentic ecosystems</a>.</p>
<p>A lake moderates the surrounding region's temperature and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> because water has a very high <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> (4,186 J·kg<sup>−1</sup>·K<sup>−1</sup>). In the daytime a lake can cool the land beside it with local winds, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a>; in the night it can warm it with a <a href="/wiki/Land_breeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Land breeze">land breeze</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_lakes_disappear">How lakes disappear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: How lakes disappear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The lake may be infilled with deposited sediment and gradually become a <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marsh</a>. Large water plants, typically <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reeds</a>, accelerate this closing process significantly because they partially decompose to form peat soils that fill the shallows. Conversely, peat soils in a marsh can naturally burn and reverse this process to recreate a shallow lake resulting in a dynamic equilibrium between marsh and lake.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This is significant since wildfire has been largely suppressed in the developed world over the past century. This has artificially converted many shallow lakes into emergent marshes. Turbid lakes and lakes with many plant-eating fish tend to disappear more slowly. A "disappearing" lake (barely noticeable on a human timescale) typically has extensive plant mats at the water's edge. These become a new habitat for other plants, like <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">peat moss</a> when conditions are right, and animals, many of which are very rare. Gradually the lake closes and young <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> may form, forming a <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fen</a>. In lowland river valleys where a river can <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meander</a>, the presence of peat is explained by the infilling of historical <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lakes</a>. In the very last stages of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>, trees can grow in, eventually turning the wetland into a forest.</p>
<p>Some lakes can disappear seasonally. These are called <b>intermittent lakes</b>, <b>ephemeral lakes</b>, or <b>seasonal lakes</b> and can be found in <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karstic terrain</a>. A prime example of an intermittent lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Cerknica" title="Lake Cerknica">Lake Cerknica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lag_Prau_Pulte" class="mw-redirect" title="Lag Prau Pulte">Lag Prau Pulte</a> in <a href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" class="mw-redirect" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>. Other intermittent lakes are only the result of above-average precipitation in a closed, or <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basin</a>, usually filling dry lake beds. This can occur in some of the driest places on earth, like <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a>. This occurred in the spring of 2005, after unusually heavy rains.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The lake did not last into the summer, and was quickly evaporated (see photos to right). A more commonly filled lake of this type is <a href="/wiki/Sevier_Lake" title="Sevier Lake">Sevier Lake</a> of west-central <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>.</p>
<p>Sometimes a lake will disappear quickly. On 3 June 2005, in <a href="/wiki/Nizhny_Novgorod_Oblast" title="Nizhny Novgorod Oblast">Nizhny Novgorod Oblast</a>, Russia, a lake called <a href="/wiki/Lake_Beloye_(Nizhny_Novgorod_Oblast)" title="Lake Beloye (Nizhny Novgorod Oblast)">Lake Beloye</a> vanished in a matter of minutes. News sources reported that government officials theorized that this strange phenomenon may have been caused by a shift in the soil underneath the lake that allowed its water to drain through channels leading to the <a href="/wiki/Oka_River" title="Oka River">Oka River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The presence of ground permafrost is important to the persistence of some lakes. According to research published in the journal <i>Science</i> ("Disappearing Arctic Lakes", June 2005), thawing permafrost may explain the shrinking or disappearance of hundreds of large Arctic lakes across western Siberia. The idea here is that rising air and soil temperatures thaw permafrost, allowing the lakes to drain away into the ground.</p>
<p>Some lakes disappear because of human development factors. The shrinking <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> is described as being "murdered" by the diversion for irrigation of the rivers feeding it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_lakes">Extraterrestrial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Only one world other than Earth is known to harbor large lakes, Saturn's largest moon, <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. Photographs and spectroscopic analysis by the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> show liquid <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> on the surface, which is thought to be mixed with liquid <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. The largest Titanean lake, <a href="/wiki/Kraken_Mare" title="Kraken Mare">Kraken Mare</a> at 400,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>, is three-times the size of any lake on Earth, and even the second, <a href="/wiki/Ligeia_Mare" title="Ligeia Mare">Ligeia Mare</a>, is estimated to be slightly larger than Earth's Lake Michigan–Huron.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>'s large moon <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a> is volcanically active, and as a result <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">Sulphur</a> deposits have accumulated on the surface. Some photographs taken during the <a href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo spacecraft">Galileo mission</a> appear to show lakes of liquid sulfur in volcanic caldera, though these are more analogous to lake of lava than of water on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> is too cold and has too little <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> to permit the pooling of liquid water. Geologic evidence appears to confirm, however, that ancient lakes once formed on the surface. It is also possible that volcanic activity on Mars will occasionally melt subsurface ice, creating large temporary lakes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This water would quickly freeze and then sublimate, unless insulated in some manner, such as by a coating of volcanic ash.</p>
<p>There are dark basaltic plains on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">lunar maria</a> but smaller, that are called <i>lacus</i> (singular <i>lacus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "lake") because they were thought by early astronomers to be lakes of water.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_lakes_on_Earth">Notable lakes on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Notable lakes on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li>The <b>largest lake by surface area</b> is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan-Huron" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Michigan-Huron">Lake Michigan-Huron</a>, which is hydrologically a single lake. Its surface area is 45,300 sq. mi./117,400&#160;km<sup>2</sup>. For those who consider Lake Michigan-Huron to be separate lakes, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> would be the largest at 31,700 sq. mi./82,100&#160;km<sup>2</sup>.</li>
<li>The <b>deepest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, with a bottom at 1,637 metres (5,371&#160;ft). Its <b>mean depth</b> is also the greatest in the world (749 metres (2,457&#160;ft)).<br />
It is also the world's <b>largest lake by volume</b> (23,600 cubic kilometres (5,700&#160;cu&#160;mi), though smaller than the Caspian Sea at 78,200 cubic kilometres (18,800&#160;cu&#160;mi)), and the second longest (about 630 kilometres (390&#160;mi) from tip to tip).</li>
<li>The <b>longest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>, with a length of about 660 kilometres (410&#160;mi) (measured along the lake's center line).<br />
It is also the second largest by volume and second deepest (1,470 metres (4,820&#160;ft)) in the world, after lake Baikal.</li>
<li>The world's <b>oldest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>). <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maracaibo" title="Lake Maracaibo">Lake Maracaibo</a> is considered by some to be the second-oldest lake on Earth, but since it lies at sea level and nowadays is a contiguous body of water with the sea, others consider that it has turned into a small bay.</li>
<li>The world's <b>highest</b> lake, if size is not a criterion, may be the crater lake of <a href="/wiki/Ojos_del_Salado" title="Ojos del Salado">Ojos del Salado</a>, at 6,390 metres (20,965&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>The highest large (greater than 250 square kilometres (97&#160;sq&#160;mi)) lake in the world is the 290 square kilometres (110&#160;sq&#160;mi) <a href="/wiki/Lake_Puma_Yumco" title="Lake Puma Yumco">Pumoyong Tso</a> (Pumuoyong Tso), in the <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">Tibet Autonomous Region</a> of China, at 28-34N 90-24E, 5,018 metres (16,463&#160;ft) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>The world's <b>highest</b> commercially navigable lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> at 3,812&#160;m (12,507&#160;ft). It is also the largest lake in South America.</li>
<li>The world's <b>lowest</b> lake is the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, bordering <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> at 418 metres (1,371&#160;ft) below sea level. It is also one of the lakes with highest <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> concentration.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan%E2%80%93Huron" title="Lake Michigan–Huron">Lake Michigan–Huron</a> has the <b>longest lake coastline</b> in the world: about 5,250 kilometres (3,260&#160;mi), excluding the coastline of its many inner islands. Even if it is considered two lakes, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a> alone would still have the longest coastline in the world at 2,980 kilometres (1,850&#160;mi).</li>
<li>The largest island in a lake is <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, with a surface area of 2,766 square kilometres (1,068&#160;sq&#160;mi). <a href="/wiki/Lake_Manitou" title="Lake Manitou">Lake Manitou</a>, on Manitoulin Island, is the largest lake on an island in a lake.</li>
<li>The largest lake on an island is <a href="/wiki/Nettilling_Lake" title="Nettilling Lake">Nettilling Lake</a> on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, with an area of 5,542 square kilometres (2,140&#160;sq&#160;mi) and a maximum length of 123 kilometres (76&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>The largest lake in the world that drains naturally in two directions is <a href="/wiki/Wollaston_Lake" title="Wollaston Lake">Wollaston Lake</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">Lake Toba</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> is in what is probably the largest resurgent <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> on Earth.</li>
<li>The largest lake completely within the boundaries of a single city is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Wanapitei" title="Lake Wanapitei">Lake Wanapitei</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada. Before the current city boundaries came into effect in 2001, this status was held by <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ramsey" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Ramsey">Lake Ramsey</a>, also in Sudbury.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Enriquillo" title="Lake Enriquillo">Lake Enriquillo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> is the only saltwater lake in the world inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>.</li>
<li>Lake Bernard, Ontario, Canada, claims to be the largest lake in the world with no islands.</li>
<li>The largest lake in one country is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.A</a>. However, it is sometimes considered part of Lake Michigan-Huron, making the record go to <a href="/wiki/Great_Bear_Lake" title="Great Bear Lake">Great Bear Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the largest lake within one jurisdiction.</li>
<li>The <b>largest lake on an island in a lake on an island</b> is Crater Lake on Vulcano Island in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Taal" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Taal">Lake Taal</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</li>
<li>The <b>northernmost named lake</b> on Earth is <a href="/wiki/Upper_Dumbell_Lake" title="Upper Dumbell Lake">Upper Dumbell Lake</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Qikiqtaaluk_Region" title="Qikiqtaaluk Region">Qikiqtaaluk Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> at a latitude of 82°28'N. It is 5.2 kilometres (3.2&#160;mi) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Alert,_Nunavut" title="Alert, Nunavut">Alert</a>, the northernmost settlement in the world. There are also several small lakes north of Upper Dumbell Lake, but they are all unnamed and only appear on very detailed maps.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_by_continent">Largest by continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Largest by continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest lakes (surface area) by <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> are:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Australia</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> (salt lake)</li>
<li><b>Africa</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a>, also the third-largest freshwater lake on Earth. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_of_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Lakes of Africa">Great Lakes of Africa</a>.</li>
<li><b>Antarctica</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a> (subglacial)</li>
<li><b>Asia</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> (if the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> is considered a lake, it is the largest in Eurasia, but is divided between the two geographic continents)</li>
<li><b>Oceania</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> when filled; the largest permanent (and freshwater) lake in Oceania is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Taupo" title="Lake Taupo">Lake Taupo</a>.</li>
<li><b>Europe</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Lake_Onega" title="Lake Onega">Lake Onega</a>, both in northwestern Russia.</li>
<li><b>North America</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan-Huron" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Michigan-Huron">Lake Michigan-Huron</a>, which is hydrologically a single lake. However, lakes <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Huron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Michigan</a> are usually considered separate lakes, in which case <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> would be the largest.<sup id="cite_ref-Manivanan2008_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manivanan2008-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>South America</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a>, which is also the highest navigable body of water on Earth at 3,812 metres (12,507&#160;ft) above sea level. The much larger <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maracaibo" title="Lake Maracaibo">Lake Maracaibo</a> is much older, but perceived by some to no longer be genuinely a lake for multiple reasons.</li>
</ul>
