<p>The <b>Welsh Government</b> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <span lang="cy"><i>Llywodraeth Cymru</i></span>) is the executive of the <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolved</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>. The government was established in 1999 as the <b>Welsh Assembly Government</b> by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a>, which created a devolved administration for Wales in line with the result of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_1997" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 1997">1997 referendum on Welsh devolution</a>. The government consists of cabinet secretaries, who attend cabinet meetings, and <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">ministers</a> who do not, and also of a <a href="/wiki/Counsel_General_for_Wales" title="Counsel General for Wales">counsel general</a>. It is led by the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">first minister</a>, usually the leader of the largest party in the National Assembly, who selects cabinet secretaries, ministers and deputy ministers with the approval of the assembly. The government is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Table_(parliamentary_procedure)" title="Table (parliamentary procedure)">tabling</a> policy in <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">devolved areas</a> for consideration by the assembly and implementing policy that has been approved by it.<sup id="cite_ref-WG335_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WG335-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WG336_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WG336-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>As initially established, the Welsh Government had no independent executive powers in law (unlike, for instance, the Scottish ministers and British government ministers). The National Assembly was established as a <i><a href="/wiki/Body_corporate" class="mw-redirect" title="Body corporate">body corporate</a></i> by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a> and the executive, as a <a href="/wiki/Committee" title="Committee">committee</a> of the assembly, only had those powers that the assembly as a whole voted to delegate to ministers.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> formally separated the National Assembly for Wales and the Welsh Government, giving Welsh ministers independent executive authority, this taking effect after the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2007" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2007">May 2007 elections</a>. Following separation, the Welsh ministers exercise functions in their own right. Further transfers of executive functions from the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British government</a> can be made directly to the Welsh ministers (with their consent) by an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a> approved by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliament</a>.</p>
<p>Separation was designed to clarify the respective roles of the assembly and the government. Under the structures established by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a>, the role of Welsh ministers is to make decisions; develop and implement policy; exercise executive functions and make statutory instruments. The 60 assembly members in the National Assembly scrutinise the government's decisions and policies; hold ministers to account; approve budgets for the Welsh Government's programmes; and enact acts of assembly on subjects that have been <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">devolved</a> to the Welsh administration.</p>
<p>The result mirrored much more closely the relationship between the British government and British parliament and that between the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_2007_election_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales">After the 2007 election of the National Assembly for Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After the 2007 election of the National Assembly for Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_separation">Legal separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The new arrangements provided for in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> created a formal legal separation between the National Assembly for Wales, comprising 60 assembly members, and the Welsh Government, comprising the First Minister, Welsh ministers, deputy ministers and the counsel general. This separation between the two bodies took effect on the appointment of the First Minister by <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> following the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2007" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2007">assembly election</a> on 3 May 2007.</p>
<p>Separation was meant to clarify the respective roles of the assembly and the government. The role of the government is to make decisions; develop and implement policy; exercise executive functions and make statutory instruments. The 60 assembly members in the National Assembly scrutinise the Welsh Government's decisions and policies; hold ministers to account; approve budgets for the Welsh Government's programmes; and have the power to enact <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Measure of the National Assembly for Wales">assembly measures</a> on certain matters. Assembly measures can now go further than the subordinate legislation which the assembly had the power to make prior to 2007.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_of_functions">Transfer of functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transfer of functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The assembly's functions, including that of making subordinate legislation, in the main, transferred to the Welsh ministers upon separation. A third body was also established under the 2006 Act from May 2007, called the National Assembly for Wales Commission. It employs the staff supporting the new National Assembly for Wales, and holds property, enters into contracts and provides support services on its behalf.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_ministers">Welsh ministers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Welsh ministers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 2006 Act made new provision for the appointment of Welsh ministers. The <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">First Minister</a> is nominated by the Assembly and then appointed by Her Majesty the Queen. The First Minister then appoints the Welsh Ministers and the <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Welsh_Ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Welsh Ministers">Deputy Welsh Ministers</a>, with the approval of Her Majesty. The Act created a new post of <a href="/wiki/Counsel_General_for_Wales" title="Counsel General for Wales">Counsel General for Wales</a>, the principal source of legal advice to the Welsh Government. The Counsel General is appointed by the Queen, on the nomination of the First Minister, whose recommendation must be agreed by the National Assembly. The Counsel General may be, but does not have to be, an Assembly Member. The Act permits a maximum of 12 Welsh Ministers, which includes Deputy Welsh Ministers, but excludes the First Minister and the Counsel General. Accordingly, the maximum size of the Welsh Government is 14.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_referendum_on_law-making_powers">2011 referendum on law-making powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011 referendum on law-making powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions_and_areas_of_competence">Functions and areas of competence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Functions and areas of competence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following the "yes" vote in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">referendum on further law-making powers for the assembly</a> on 3 March 2011, the Welsh Government is now entitled to propose bills to the National Assembly for Wales on subjects within 20 fields of policy. Subject to limitations prescribed by the Government of Wales Act 2006, <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of the National Assembly for Wales">Acts of the National Assembly</a> may make any provision that could be made by Act of Parliament. The 20 areas of responsibility devolved to the National Assembly for Wales (and within which Welsh ministers exercise executive functions) are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Agriculture, fisheries, forestry and rural development</li>
<li>Ancient monuments and historical buildings</li>
<li>Culture</li>
<li>Economic development</li>
<li>Education and training</li>
<li>Environment</li>
<li>Fire and rescue services and promotion of fire safety</li>
<li>Food</li>
<li>Health and health services</li>
<li>Highways and transport</li>
<li>Housing</li>
<li>Local government</li>
<li>National Assembly for Wales</li>
<li>Public administration</li>
<li>Social welfare</li>
<li>Sport and recreation</li>
<li>Tourism</li>
<li>Town and country planning</li>
<li>Water and flood defences</li>
<li>Welsh language</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming">Renaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Welsh Assembly Government was renamed Welsh Government (<i><span lang="cy">Llywodraeth Cymru</span></i>) under the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2014" title="Wales Act 2014">Wales Act 2014</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabinet_Secretaries.2C_Ministers_and_Counsel_General">Cabinet Secretaries, Ministers and Counsel General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cabinet Secretaries, Ministers and Counsel General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The government is composed of cabinet secretaries and ministers. The <a href="/wiki/Counsel_General_for_Wales" title="Counsel General for Wales">counsel general</a> attends cabinet meetings. The current government is formed by <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Labour" title="Welsh Labour">Welsh Labour</a> and also the sole <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats" title="Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrats</a> Assembly Member, <a href="/wiki/Kirsty_Williams" title="Kirsty Williams">Kirsty Williams</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_service">Civil service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Civil service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Welsh Government also includes a civil service that supports the Welsh ministers. According to a report from 2014, there are over 5,000 civil servants working across Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The civil service is a matter <a href="/wiki/Reserved_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserved matter">reserved</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliament</a> at Westminster: Welsh Government civil servants work within the rules and customs of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty's Civil Service">Her Majesty's Civil Service</a>, but serve the devolved administration rather than the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ere_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ere-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_secretary">Permanent secretary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Permanent secretary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Permanent_secretary" class="mw-redirect" title="Permanent secretary">permanent secretary</a> heads the civil service of the Welsh Government and chairs the Strategic Delivery and Performance Board.</p>
<p>The permanent secretary is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty's Civil Service">Her Majesty's Civil Service</a>, and therefore takes part in the permanent secretaries management group of the Civil Service<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and is answerable to the most senior civil servant in Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_secretary" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet secretary">cabinet secretary</a>, for his or her professional conduct. He or she remains, however, at the direction of the Welsh ministers.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_Delivery_and_Performance_Board">Strategic Delivery and Performance Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Strategic Delivery and Performance Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Strategic Delivery and Performance Board translates the strategic direction set by the Welsh cabinet and its committees into work that is joined up across Welsh Government departments and makes the best use of its resources. The board is made up of two deputy permanent secretaries, the director-general for health and social services/NHS Wales chief executive, four directors and 3 non-executive directors, and is chaired by the permanent secretary.</p>
<p>Board members are appointed at the discretion of and by the permanent secretary. Membership is not wholly dependent on functional responsibilities; it is designed to provide balanced advice and support to the permanent secretary, and collective leadership to the organisation as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Government_sponsored_bodies">Welsh Government sponsored bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Welsh Government sponsored bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government_sponsored_bodies" title="Welsh Government sponsored bodies">Welsh Government sponsored bodies</a></div>
<p>The Welsh Government is responsible for a number of Welsh Government sponsored bodies (WGSBs). These are, respectively,</p>
<ul>
<li>executive WGSBs, which are <a href="/wiki/Non-departmental_public_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-departmental public bodies">non-departmental public bodies</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Council_of_Wales" title="Arts Council of Wales">Arts Council of Wales</a>;</li>
<li>advisory WGSBs, which are non-departmental public bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Buildings_Council_for_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Buildings Council for Wales">Historic Buildings Council for Wales</a>; and</li>
<li>tribunals such as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mental_Health_Review_Tribunal_for_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mental Health Review Tribunal for Wales (page does not exist)">Mental Health Review Tribunal for Wales</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>WGSBs are staffed by public servants rather than civil servants.</p>
<p>The Welsh Government is also responsible for some public bodies that are not classed as WGSBs, such as <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">NHS Wales</a>, and the Welsh Offices of England and Wales legal offices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Offices">Offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Welsh Government has a total of 35 offices across Wales,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with a number in London and overseas.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Historically, most <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> staff were based in Cardiff, especially in <a href="/wiki/Crown_Buildings,_Cathays_Park" title="Crown Buildings, Cathays Park">Cathays Park</a>. However, in 2002, the Fullerton Review concluded that "the Assembly could no longer sustain having the majority of its operational functions located in and around Cardiff".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Since 2004, Welsh Government civil servants have been relocated across Wales as part of the Location Strategy, which involves the creation of new offices at <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llandudno_Junction" title="Llandudno Junction">Llandudno Junction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2006, the mergers of <a href="/wiki/ELWa" class="mw-redirect" title="ELWa">ELWa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Tourist_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Tourist Board">Wales Tourist Board</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Development_Agency" title="Welsh Development Agency">Welsh Development Agency</a> into the Welsh Government brought these agencies' offices into the Welsh Government estate.</p>
<p>The office of the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_for_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="First Minister for Wales">First Minister</a> is in <a href="/wiki/T%C5%B7_Hywel" title="TÅ· Hywel">TÅ· Hywel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Senedd" title="Senedd">Senedd</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Bay" title="Cardiff Bay">Cardiff Bay</a>; an office is also kept at the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Building,_Cathays_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Building, Cathays Park">Welsh Government building</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cathays_Park" title="Cathays Park">Cathays Park</a> where the majority of Cardiff-based Welsh Government civil servants are located.</p>
