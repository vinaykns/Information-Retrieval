<p><b>Intergenerational equity</b> in economic, psychological, and sociological contexts, is the concept or idea of fairness or <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a> in relationships between <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>, <a href="/wiki/Youth" title="Youth">youth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adults" class="mw-redirect" title="Adults">adults</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_age" title="Old age">seniors</a>, particularly in terms of treatment and interactions. It has been studied in environmental and sociological settings.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics_usage">Economics usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intergenerational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Economics usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intergenerational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the first recorded debt issuance in Sumaria in 1796 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> one of the penalties for failure to repay a loan has been <a href="/wiki/Debt_bondage" title="Debt bondage">debt bondage</a>. In some instances, this repayment of financial debt with labor included the debtor's children, essentially condemning the debtor family to perpetual slavery. About one millennium after written debt contracts were created, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Debt_relief" title="Debt relief">debt forgiveness</a> appears in the Old Testament, called <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_(Christianity)" title="Jubilee (Christianity)">Jubilee</a> (<a href="/wiki/Leviticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Leviticus">Leviticus</a> 25), and in Greek law when Solon introduces <a href="/wiki/Seisachtheia" title="Seisachtheia">Seisachtheia</a>. Both of these historical examples of debt forgiveness involved freeing children from slavery caused by their parents' debt.</p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> is illegal in all countries today, North Korea has a policy called, "Three Generations of Punishment"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which has been documented by <a href="/wiki/Shin_Dong-hyuk" title="Shin Dong-hyuk">Shin Dong-hyuk</a> and used as a moral paragon of punishing children for parents' mistakes. Stanley Druckenmiller and Geoffrey Canada have applied this concept (calling it "Generational Theft"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) to the large increase in government debt being left by the Baby Boomers to their children.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_management">Investment management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intergenerational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Investment management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the context of institutional <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a>, intergenerational equity is the principle that an endowed institution's spending rate must not exceed its after-inflation rate of compound return, so that investment gains are spent equally on current and future constituents of the endowed assets. This concept was originally set out in 1974 by <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Tobin" title="James Tobin">James Tobin</a>, who wrote that, "The trustees of endowed institutions are the guardians of the future against the claims of the present. Their task in managing the endowment is to preserve equity among generations."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in terms of an economical context. <i>Intergenerational equity</i> refers to relationship that a particular family has on resources. An example is the forest-dwelling civilians in Papua New Guinea, who for generations have lived in a certain part of the forest and thus becomes their land. The adult population sell the trees for palm oil to make money. If they cannot make a sustainable development on managing their resources, their next or future generations will lose this resources.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._national_debt">U.S. national debt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intergenerational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: U.S. national debt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/National_debt_of_the_United_States" title="National debt of the United States">National debt of the United States</a></div>
<p>One debate about the national debt relates to intergenerational equity. For example, if one generation is receiving the benefit of government programs or employment enabled by deficit spending and debt accumulation, to what extent does the resulting higher debt impose risks and costs on future generations? There are several factors to consider:</p>
<ul>
<li>For every dollar of debt held by the public, there is a government obligation (generally marketable Treasury securities) counted as an asset by investors. Future generations benefit to the extent these assets are passed on to them, which by definition must correspond to the level of debt passed on.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>As of 2010, approximately 72% of the financial assets were held by the wealthiest 5% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This presents a wealth and income distribution question, as only a fraction of the people in future generations will receive principal or interest from investments related to the debt incurred today.</li>
<li>To the extent the U.S. debt is owed to foreign investors (approximately half the "debt held by the public" during 2012), principal and interest are not directly received by U.S. heirs.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Higher debt levels imply higher interest payments, which create costs for future taxpayers (e.g., higher taxes, lower government benefits, higher inflation, or increased risk of fiscal crisis).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>To the extent the borrowed funds are invested today to improve the long-term productivity of the economy and its workers, such as via useful infrastructure projects, future generations may benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>For every dollar of intragovernmental debt, there is an obligation to specific program recipients, generally non-marketable securities such as those held in the Social Security Trust Fund. Adjustments that reduce future deficits in these programs may also apply costs to future generations, via higher taxes or lower program spending.</li>
</ul>
<p>Economist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">Paul Krugman</a> wrote in March 2013 that by neglecting public investment and failing to create jobs, we are doing far more harm to future generations than merely passing along debt: "Fiscal policy is, indeed, a moral issue, and we should be ashamed of what we’re doing to the next generation's economic prospects. But our sin involves investing too little, not borrowing too much." Young workers face high unemployment and studies have shown their income may lag throughout their careers as a result. Teacher jobs have been cut, which could affect the quality of education and competitiveness of younger Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Australian politician <a href="/wiki/Christine_Milne" title="Christine Milne">Christine Milne</a> made similar statements in the lead up to the 2014 Carbon Price Repeal Bill, naming the Liberal National Party (elected to parliament in 2013) and inherently its ministers, as intergenerational thieves; her statement was based on the party's attempts to roll back progressive carbon tax policy and the impact this would have on the intergenerational equity of future generations.</p>

<p>Conversations about intergenerational equity occur across several fields.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They include <a href="/wiki/Transition_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Transition economics">transition economics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">social policy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Government_budget" title="Government budget">government budget-making</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Intergenerational equity is also explored in <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> concerns,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_justice_usage">Social justice usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intergenerational_equity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Social justice usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conversations about intergenerational equity are also relevant to <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> arenas as well, where issues such as <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> are equal in importance to <a href="/wiki/Youth_rights" title="Youth rights">youth rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Youth_voice" title="Youth voice">youth voice</a> are pressing and urgent. There is a strong interest within the legal community towards the application of intergenerational equity in law.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
