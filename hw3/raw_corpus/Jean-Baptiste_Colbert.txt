<p><b>Jean-Baptiste Colbert</b> (<small>French:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ʒɑ̃.ba.tist kɔl.bɛʁ]</a></span>; 29 August 1619 – 6 September 1683) was a French politician who served as the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Economy,_Industry_and_Employment_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of the Economy, Industry and Employment (France)">Minister of Finances</a> of France from 1665 to 1683 under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">King Louis XIV</a>. His relentless hard work and thrift made him an esteemed minister. He achieved a reputation for his work of improving the state of French <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> and bringing the <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economy</a> back from the brink of <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a>. Historians note that, despite Colbert's efforts, France actually became increasingly <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">impoverished</a> because of the King's excessive spending on wars.<sup id="cite_ref-Letters_to_John_Law_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letters_to_John_Law-1">[1]</a></sup> Colbert worked to create a favourable <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_trade" title="Balance of trade">balance of trade</a> and increase France's <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonial</a> holdings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Colbert's <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> reforms included the foundation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Manufacture_royale_de_glaces_de_miroirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Manufacture royale de glaces de miroirs">Manufacture royale de glaces de miroirs</a></i> in 1665 to supplant the importation of <a href="/wiki/Venetian_glass" title="Venetian glass">Venetian glass</a> (forbidden in 1672, as soon as the French glass manufacturing industry was on sound footing) and to encourage the technical expertise of <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Cloth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth">cloth</a> <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> in France. He also founded royal <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestry</a> works at <a href="/wiki/Gobelins_Manufactory" title="Gobelins Manufactory">Gobelins</a> and supported those at <a href="/wiki/Beauvais_tapestry" class="mw-redirect" title="Beauvais tapestry">Beauvais</a>. Colbert worked to develop the domestic <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economy</a> by raising <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> and by encouraging major <a href="/wiki/Public_works" title="Public works">public works</a> projects. Colbert also worked to ensure that the <a href="/wiki/French_East_India_Company" title="French East India Company">French East India Company</a> had access to foreign markets, so that they could always obtain <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dyewoods" title="Dyewoods">dyewoods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">pepper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>. In addition, Colbert founded the French <a href="/wiki/Merchant_marine" class="mw-redirect" title="Merchant marine">merchant marine</a>.</p>
<p>Colbert issued more than 150 <a href="/wiki/Edict" title="Edict">edicts</a> to <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulate</a> the <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a>. One such <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> had the intention of improving the quality of <a href="/wiki/Cloth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth">cloth</a>. The edict declared that if the authorities found a <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a>'s cloth unsatisfactory on three separate occasions, they were to tie him to a post with the cloth attached to him. He created the seigneurial system.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Colbert's father and grandfather operated as merchants in his birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. He claimed to have Scottish ancestry. A general (but unconfirmed) belief exists that he spent his early youth at a <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> college, working for a Parisian banker; as well as working for the father of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chapelain" title="Jean Chapelain">Jean Chapelain</a>.</p>
<p>Before the age of 20, Colbert had a post in the war office, a position generally attributed to the marriage of an uncle to the sister of Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Michel_le_Tellier" class="mw-redirect" title="Michel le Tellier">Michel le Tellier</a>. Colbert spent some time as an inspector of troops, eventually becoming the personal secretary of Le Tellier. In 1647, through unknown means, Colbert acquired the confiscated goods of an uncle, Pussort. In 1648, he and his wife Marie Charron, received 40,000 crowns from an unknown source; and in 1649 Colbert became the councillor of state (Political minister).</p>
<p>In 1657, he purchased the <a href="/wiki/Barony" title="Barony">Barony</a> of <a href="/wiki/Seignelay" title="Seignelay">Seignelay</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_power">Rise to power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise to power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Colbert was recommended to <a href="/wiki/King_Louis_XIV" class="mw-redirect" title="King Louis XIV">King Louis XIV</a> by Mazarin. While <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Mazarin" title="Cardinal Mazarin">Cardinal Mazarin</a> was in exile, Louis' trust in Colbert grew. In 1652 Colbert was asked to manage the affairs of the Cardinal while he was away. This new responsibility would detach Colbert from his other responsibility as <i>commissaire des guerres</i>. Although Colbert was not a supporter of Mazarin in principle, he would defend the cardinal's interests with unflagging devotion.</p>
<p>Colbert's earliest recorded attempt at tax reform came in the form of a <i>mémoire</i> to Mazarin, showing that of the taxes paid by the people, not one-half reached the King. The paper also contained an attack upon the <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Fouquet" title="Nicolas Fouquet">Superintendent Fouquet</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Postmaster" title="Postmaster">postmaster</a> of Paris, a spy of Fouquet's, read the letter, leading to a dispute which Mazarin attempted to suppress.</p>
<p>In 1661, Mazarin died and Colbert "made sure of the King's favour" by revealing the location of some of Mazarin's hidden wealth. In January 1664 Colbert became the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A2timents_du_Roi" title="Bâtiments du Roi">Superintendent of buildings</a>; in 1665 he became <a href="/wiki/Controller-General_of_Finances" title="Controller-General of Finances">Controller-General of Finances</a>; in 1669, he became <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_the_Navy_(France)" title="Secretary of State of the Navy (France)">Secretary of State of the Navy</a>; he also gained appointments as minister of <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonies</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palace</a>. In short, Colbert acquired power in every department except that of war.</p>
<p>A great financial and fiscal reform at once claimed all his energies. Not only the nobility, but many others who had no legal claim to exemption, paid no taxes; the bulk of the burden fell on the wretched country-folk. Supported by the young king <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, Colbert aimed the first blow at the man accused of being the greatest of the royal embezzlers, the superintendent <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Fouquet" title="Nicolas Fouquet">Nicolas Fouquet</a>. Fouquet's fall simultaneously secured Colbert's own advancement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_reform">Economic reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>With the abolition of the office of superintendent and of many other offices dependent upon it, the supreme control of the finances became vested in a royal council. The sovereign functioned as its president; but Colbert, though for four years he possessed the title only of <i><a href="/wiki/Intendant_des_finances" title="Intendant des finances">intendant</a></i>, operated as its ruling spirit, having had great personal authority conferred upon him by the king. One must not judge the career on which Colbert now entered without remembering the corruption of the previous financial administration.</p>
<p>His ruthlessness in this case, dangerous precedent though it gave, seemed perhaps necessary; the council could not respect individual interests. When he had severely punished guilty officials, he turned his attention to the fraudulent creditors of the government. Colbert had a simple method of operation. He repudiated some of the public loans and cut off from others a percentage, which varied, at first according to his own decision, and afterwards according to that of the council that he established to examine all claims against the state.</p>
<p>Much more serious difficulties met his attempts to introduce equality in the pressure of the taxes on the various classes. To diminish the number of the privileged proved impossible, but Colbert firmly resisted false claims for exemption, and lightened the unjust direct taxation by increasing the indirect taxes, from which the privileged could not escape. At the same time he immensely improved the mode of collection on his own.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_theory">Economic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Colbertism" title="Colbertism">Colbertism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">Protectionism</a></div>
<p>Having thus introduced a measure of order and economy into the workings of the government, Colbert now called for the enrichment of the country by commerce. The state, through Colbert's <i><a href="/wiki/Dirigiste" class="mw-redirect" title="Dirigiste">dirigiste</a></i> policies, fostered manufacturing enterprises in a wide variety of fields. The authorities established new industries, protected inventors, invited in workmen from foreign countries, and prohibited French workmen from emigrating.</p>
<p>To maintain the character of French goods in foreign markets, as well as to afford a guarantee to the home consumer, Colbert had the quality and measure of each article fixed by law, punishing breaches of the regulations by public exposure of the delinquent and by destruction of the goods concerned, and, on the third offense, by the <a href="/wiki/Pillory" title="Pillory">pillory</a>. Colbert prohibited the production of qualities which would have suited many purposes of consumption, and the odious supervision which became necessary involved great waste of time and a stereotyped regularity which resisted all improvements. Other parts of Colbert's schemes have met with less equivocal condemnation.</p>
<p>By his firm maintenance of the <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> system, each industry remained in the hands of certain privileged <i><a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a></i>; while the lower classes found opportunities of advancement closed. He did, however, wisely consult the interests of internal commerce.</p>
<p>Unable to abolish the duties on the passage of goods from <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_France" title="Provinces of France">province to province</a>, he did what he could to induce the provinces to equalise them. Currency exchange rates still remained between these provinces despite a policy focusing on the unification of French trade. His régime improved roads and canals. <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Paul_Riquet" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Paul Riquet">Pierre Paul Riquet</a> (1604–1680) planned and constructed the <a href="/wiki/Canal_du_Midi" title="Canal du Midi">Canal du Midi</a> under Colbert's patronage.</p>
<p>To encourage overseas trade with the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a> and other places, Colbert granted privileges to companies, but, like the noted <a href="/wiki/French_East_India_Company" title="French East India Company">French East India Company</a>, all proved unsuccessful.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Promoter_of_culture">Promoter of culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Promoter of culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Colbert took much interest in art and literature. He possessed a remarkably fine private library, which he delighted to fill with valuable manuscripts from every part of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a> where France had placed a consul. He employed <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Carcavi" title="Pierre de Carcavi">Pierre de Carcavi</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Baluze" title="Étienne Baluze">Étienne Baluze</a> as librarians. Colbert's grandson sold the manuscript collection in 1732 to the <a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Nationale" class="mw-redirect" title="Bibliothèque Nationale">Bibliothèque Royale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colbert founded a number of institutions:</p>
<ul>
<li>in 1663 the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Inscriptions_et_M%C3%A9dailles" class="mw-redirect" title="Académie des Inscriptions et Médailles">Academy of Inscriptions and Medals</a>,</li>
<li>in 1666 the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">Academy of Sciences</a> (now part of the <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_France" title="Institut de France">Institut de France</a>),</li>
<li>in 1667 the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">Paris Observatory</a>, which he employed <a href="/wiki/Claude_Perrault" title="Claude Perrault">Claude Perrault</a> to build and brought <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">Giovanni Domenico Cassini</a> (1625–1712) from Italy to superintend,</li>
<li>in 1671 the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_royale_d%27architecture" title="Académie royale d'architecture">Academy of Architecture</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_de_musique" class="mw-redirect" title="Académie de musique">Academy of Music</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_at_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="French Academy at Rome">French Academy at Rome</a>, and Academies at <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a>, <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a> and many other towns.</li>
</ul>
<p>He reorganised the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_de_peinture_et_de_sculpture" class="mw-redirect" title="Académie de peinture et de sculpture">Academy of Painting and Sculpture</a> which Mazarin had established. Wishing to increase the prestige of the image of France and the French royal family, Colbert played an active role in bringing the great Italian architect-sculptor, Gian Lorenzo Bernini, to Paris (June–October 1665), in order to design the new East Facade of the Louvre and sculpt a marble portrait bust of Louis XIV. This was quite an international diplomatic-artistic coup because Bernini, who had never left Rome or its vicinity, was personal architect to the pope, whom Louis was attempting to humiliate. However the relations between the two strong-willed men, Colbert and Bernini, proved melodramatically stormy and Colbert ultimately rejected Bernini's Louvre design, though the Italian artist's bust of the king (housed today in the Versailles palace) won Louis's appreciation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Colbert himself became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">Académie française</a>; and proposed one very characteristic rule with the intention of expediting the great <i><a href="/wiki/Le_dictionnaire_de_l%27Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" class="mw-redirect" title="Le dictionnaire de l'Académie française">Dictionary</a></i>, in which he had a great interest: no one could count as present at any meeting unless he arrived before the hour of commencement and remained till the hour for leaving. In 1673 Colbert presided over the first exhibition of the works of living painters; and he enriched the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> with hundreds of pictures and statues.</p>
<p>He gave many pensions to men of letters, among whom we find <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">Molière</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Corneille" title="Pierre Corneille">Corneille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Racine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Boileau-Despr%C3%A9aux" title="Nicolas Boileau-Despréaux">Boileau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Daniel_Huet" title="Pierre Daniel Huet">P D Huet</a> (1630–1721) and <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Varillas" title="Antoine Varillas">Antoine Varillas</a> (1626–1696); and even foreigners, as <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Huygens</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carlo_Dati&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carlo Dati (page does not exist)">Carlo Dati</a> the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_della_Crusca" title="Accademia della Crusca">Dellacruscan</a>. Evidence exists to show that by this munificence he hoped to draw out praises of his sovereign and himself; but this motive certainly does not account for all the splendid, if in some cases specious, services that he rendered to literature, science and art.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Colbert worked incessantly hard until his final hours. Work was his religion; he once pondered whether it was better to rise early and work or retire very late and work. He concluded that rising early and retiring late would be the ideal combination. Towards the end of his life he suffered from stomach aches, which caused him much distress. He was reduced to eating moist bread dipped in chicken broth for his meals.</p>
<p>By 64 he was bedridden and died shortly after his birthday. The surgeons who examined him found that he had been suffering from <a href="/wiki/Kidney_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney stone">kidney stones</a>. A huge stone was found in his urinary tract, which would explain his pain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Colbert played a subordinate role in the struggle between the king and the <a href="/wiki/Papacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Papacy">papacy</a> as to the royal rights over vacant <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">bishoprics</a>, and he seems to have sympathised with the proposal that suggested seizing part of the wealth of the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>. In his hatred of idleness he ventured to suppress no less than seventeen <i>fêtes</i>, and he had a project for reducing the number of persons devoted to clerical and monastic life, by increasing the age for taking the vows.</p>
<p>He showed himself at first unwilling to interfere with <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>, for he realised the commercial value of the <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> (French Protestants), who were well represented among the merchant classes; but when the king resolved to make all France <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, he followed him and urged his subordinates to do all that they could to promote <a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion" title="Religious conversion">conversions</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatleft"><a href="/wiki/File:Blason_Colbert.svg" class="image" title="Coat of arms of the Colbert family"><img alt="Coat of arms of the Colbert family" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Blason_Colbert.svg/180px-Blason_Colbert.svg.png" width="180" height="200" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Blason_Colbert.svg/270px-Blason_Colbert.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Blason_Colbert.svg/360px-Blason_Colbert.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="733" data-file-height="813" /></a></div>
<p>Of his children, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert,_Marquis_de_Seignelay" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Marquis de Seignelay">Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Marquis de Seignelay</a>, followed his father as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_the_Navy_(France)" title="Secretary of State of the Navy (France)">Secretary of State of the Navy</a> while <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Nicolas_Colbert" title="Jacques-Nicolas Colbert">Jacques-Nicolas Colbert</a> was <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Rouen" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Rouen">Archbishop of Rouen</a>.</p>
<p>His policies inspired those of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a>, the first treasury secretary of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Six ships of the <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a> bore his name:</p>
<ul>
<li>A steam corvette in 1848</li>
<li>A battleship in 1875</li>
<li>A cargo ship in 1914</li>
<li>A dundee in 1916</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/French_cruiser_Colbert_(1928)" title="French cruiser Colbert (1928)">French heavy cruiser</a>, launched 1928</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/French_cruiser_Colbert_(C611)" title="French cruiser Colbert (C611)">French missile cruiser</a>, launched 1956</li>
</ul>
<p>In literature, the power struggle between Colbert and Fouquet is one of the main plotlines of <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">Alexandre Dumas</a> Père's novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vicomte_of_Bragelonne:_Ten_Years_Later" title="The Vicomte of Bragelonne: Ten Years Later">The Vicomte of Bragelonne</a></i>, the second sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Musketeers" title="The Three Musketeers">The Three Musketeers</a></i>. Dumas paints Colbert as an uncouth and ruthless schemer who stops at little, in contrast to the more refined Fouquet, counselled by <a href="/wiki/Aramis" title="Aramis">Aramis</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Baptiste_Colbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>"It is simply, and solely, the abundance of money within a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> [which] makes the difference in its grandeur and power."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
