<p><b>Solar Energy Generating Systems (SEGS)</b> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, with the combined capacity from three separate locations at 354 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatts">megawatts</a> (MW, 474,700&#160;hp), is now the world's <a href="/wiki/List_of_solar_thermal_power_stations#Operational" title="List of solar thermal power stations">second largest</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">solar thermal energy</a> generating facility, after the commissioning of the even larger <a href="/wiki/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility" title="Ivanpah Solar Power Facility">Ivanpah facility</a> in 2014. It consists of nine <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar power plant">solar power plants</a> in California's <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Insolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Insolation">insolation</a> is among the best available in the United States. SEGS I–II (44&#160;MW) are located at <a href="/wiki/Daggett,_California" title="Daggett, California">Daggett</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=34_51_45_N_116_49_45_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">34°51′45″N</span> <span class="longitude">116°49′45″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">34.86250°N 116.82917°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">34.86250; -116.82917</span></span></span></a></span>), SEGS&#160;III–VII (150&#160;MW) are installed at <a href="/wiki/Kramer_Junction,_California" title="Kramer Junction, California">Kramer Junction</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=35_00_43_N_117_33_32_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°00′43″N</span> <span class="longitude">117°33′32″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.01194°N 117.55889°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.01194; -117.55889</span></span></span></a></span>), and SEGS VIII–IX (160&#160;MW) are placed at <a href="/wiki/Harper_Lake" title="Harper Lake">Harper Lake</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=35_01_55_N_117_20_50_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°01′55″N</span> <span class="longitude">117°20′50″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.03194°N 117.34722°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.03194; -117.34722</span></span></span></a></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-energy_blog_2005_09_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy_blog_2005_09-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NextEra_Energy_Resources" title="NextEra Energy Resources">NextEra Energy Resources</a> operates and partially owns the plants located at Kramer Junction and Harper Lake. A tenth plant (SEGS X, 80&#160;MW) had been in construction and SEGS XI and SEGS XII had been planned by <a href="/wiki/BrightSource_Energy" title="BrightSource Energy">Luz Industries</a>, but the developer filed for bankruptcy in 1992, because it was unable to secure construction financing.<sup id="cite_ref-CEC_filing_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEC_filing-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants.27_scale_and_operations">Plants' scale and operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plants' scale and operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The plants have a 354 MW net (394 MW gross) installed capacity. The nameplate capacity, which operating continuously, would dеliver the samе net power output, coming only from the solar source is around <span class="nowrap">75 <a href="/wiki/MWe" class="mw-redirect" title="MWe">MWe</a> —</span>, representing a 21% <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a>. In addition, the turbines can be utilized at night by burning <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.</p>
<p>NextEra claims that the solar plants power 232,500 homеs (during the day, at peak power) and displace 3,800&#160;tons of pollution pеr year that would have been produced if the electricity had bееn providеd by fossil fuels, such as oil.<sup id="cite_ref-FPL_Energy_Solar_Factsheet_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FPL_Energy_Solar_Factsheet-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The facilities have a total of 936,384 mirrors and cover more than 1,600 acres (647.5&#160;ha). Lined up, the parabolic mirrors would extend over 229 miles (369&#160;km).</p>
<p>As an example of cost, in 2002, one of the 30 MW Kramer Junction sites required $90 million to construct, and its operation and maintenance cost was about $3 million per year (4.6 cents per kilowatt hour).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With a considered lifetime of 20 years, the operation, maintenance and investments interest and depreciation triples the price, to approximately 14 cents per kilowatt hour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle_of_operation">Principle of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principle of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The installation uses <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic trough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar thermal">solar thermal</a> technology along with <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> to generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. About 90% of the electricity is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Natural gas is only used when the solar power is insufficient to meet the demand from <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Edison" title="Southern California Edison">Southern California Edison</a>, the distributor of power in southern California.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mirrors">Mirrors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mirrors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The parabolic mirrors are shaped like quarter-pipes. The sun shines onto the panels made of glass, which are 94%&#160;reflective, unlike a typical mirror, which is only 70%&#160;reflective. The mirrors automatically track the sun throughout the day. The greatest source of mirror breakage is wind, with 3,000 mirrors typically replaced each year. Operators can turn the mirrors to protect them during intense wind storms. An automated washing mechanism is used to periodically clean the parabolic reflective panels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_transfer">Heat transfer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heat transfer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sunlight bounces off the mirrors and is directed to a central tube filled with <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_oil" title="Synthetic oil">synthetic oil</a>, which heats to over 400&#160;°C (750&#160;°F). The reflected light focused at the central tube is 71 to 80&#160;times more intense than the ordinary sunlight. The synthetic oil transfers its heat to water, which boils and drives the <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> steam turbine,<sup id="cite_ref-solel_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solel-5">[5]</a></sup> thereby generating electricity. Synthetic oil is used to carry the heat (instead of water) to keep the pressure within manageable parameters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_locations">Individual locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Individual locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SEGS power plants were built by <a href="/wiki/BrightSource_Energy" title="BrightSource Energy">Luz Industries</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-solel_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solel-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-madrigal1109_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-madrigal1109-6">[6]</a></sup> and commissioned between December 20, 1984 and October 1, 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After Luz Industries' <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> in 1991 plants were sold to various investor groups as individual projects, and expansion including three more plants was halted.<sup id="cite_ref-CEC_filing_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEC_filing-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kramer Junction employs about 95&#160;people and 45&#160;people work at Harper Lake.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harper_Lake">Harper Lake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Harper Lake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SEGS VIII and SEGS IX, located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=35.031815_N_-117.347270_E_region:US-CA_type:landmark&amp;title=SEGS+VIII+and+IX"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°01′55″N</span> <span class="longitude">117°20′50″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.031815°N 117.347270°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.031815; -117.347270</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">SEGS VIII and IX</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, until <a href="/wiki/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility" title="Ivanpah Solar Power Facility">Ivanpah Solar Power Facility</a> commissioning in 2014, were the largest solar thermal power plants individually and collectively in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> They were the last, the largest, and the most advanced of the nine plants at SEGS, designed to take advantage of the economies of scale. Construction of the tenth plant in the same locality was halted because of the bankruptcy of Luz Industries. Construction of the approved eleventh and twelfth plants never started. Each of the three planned plants had 80 MW of installed capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abengoa_Solar" title="Abengoa Solar">Abengoa Solar</a> recently constructed the 280MW <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Solar_Project" title="Mojave Solar Project">Mojave Solar Project</a> (MSP) adjacent to the SEGS VIII and SEGS IX plants.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The MSP also uses concentrating solar thermal trough technology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kramer_Junction">Kramer Junction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kramer Junction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg/220px-Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg/330px-Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg/440px-Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems_at_Kramer_Junction.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2191" data-file-height="1385" /></a>

<p>This location (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=35.013218_N_-117.560531_E_region:US-CA_type:landmark&amp;title=SEGS+III%E2%80%93VII"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°00′48″N</span> <span class="longitude">117°33′38″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.013218°N 117.560531°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">35.013218; -117.560531</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">SEGS III–VII</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>) receives an average of 340&#160;days of sunshine per year, which makes it an ideal place for solar power generation. The average <a href="/wiki/Direct_insolation" title="Direct insolation">direct normal radiation</a> (DNR) is 7.44&#160;<a href="/wiki/KWh" class="mw-redirect" title="KWh">kWh</a>/m²/day (310&#160;W/m²),<sup id="cite_ref-NREL99_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL99-9">[9]</a></sup> one of the best in the nation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daggett">Daggett</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Daggett">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SEGS I and II are located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;params=34.8631_N_-116.827_E_region:US-CA_type:landmark&amp;title=SEGS+I+and+II"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">34°51′47″N</span> <span class="longitude">116°49′37″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">34.8631°N 116.827°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">34.8631; -116.827</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">SEGS I and II</span>)</span></span></span></a></span> and are owned by Cogentrix Energy (<a href="/wiki/Carlyle_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlyle Group">Carlyle Group</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> SEGS II was shut down in 2014 and will be replaced with 44 MW of photovoltaics, Sunray Energy 2.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Energy_Generating_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 1999, a 900,000-US-gallon (3,400&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) <a href="/wiki/Mineral_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Mineral Oil">Mineral Oil</a> storage tank exploded at the SEGS I (Daggett) solar power plant, sending flames and smoke into the sky. Authorities were trying to keep flames away from two adjacent containers that held <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. The immediate area of 0.5 square miles (1.3&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) was evacuated.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
