			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Zero-carbon city</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>A <b>zero-carbon city</b> runs entirely on <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>; it has no <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> and will in this respect not cause harm to the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-World.27s_First_Zero_Carbon_City_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-World.27s_First_Zero_Carbon_City-1">[1]</a></sup> Most cities throughout the world produce energy by burning coal, oil and gas, unintentionally emitting carbon. Almost every activity humans do involves burning one of these <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. To become a zero carbon city, an established modern city must collectively reduce emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> to zero and all practices that <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Direct_greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">emit</a> greenhouse gases must cease. Also, renewable energy must supersede other <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-renewable energy">non-renewable energy</a> sources and become the sole source of energy, so a zero-carbon city is a renewable-energy-economy city. This transition which includes decarbonising electricity (increasing the importance of the sources of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable electricity">renewable electricity</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Zero-emission_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero-emission transport">zero-emission transport</a>, is undertaken as a response to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Zero-carbon cities maintain optimal living conditions while eliminating <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a>. Instead of using established cities, many developers are starting from scratch in order to create a zero-carbon city. This way they can make sure every aspect of a city contributes to it being carbon free.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cities_aspiring_to_be_Carbon-free">Cities aspiring to be Carbon-free</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cities aspiring to be Carbon-free">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two places that are prototyped to become zero-carbon cities: Masdar City, United Arab Emirates and Dongtan, China.<sup id="cite_ref-Eco-cities:_Masdar_city.27s_new_eco-model_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eco-cities:_Masdar_city.27s_new_eco-model-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> also has a stated ambition to become a carbon-free city, with a Low Carbon Cities programme being piloted in Malaysia as of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Once created these cities will become living examples of sustainable development, which demonstrate optimal resource utilization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Masdar_City">Masdar City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Masdar City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the world's first zero-carbon cities, called <a href="/wiki/Masdar_City" title="Masdar City">Masdar City</a>, is being constructed in the Gulf emirate of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>, it is set to house 50,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-World.27s_First_Zero_Carbon_City_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-World.27s_First_Zero_Carbon_City-1">[1]</a></sup> Construction began in February 2008. The desert city is designed to be powered entirely with renewable energy, including <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>. The Masdar Initiative is an environmental urban ambition by <a href="/wiki/Foster_%2B_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="Foster + Partners">Foster + Partners</a> to develop a sustainable city where residents would enjoy a carbon and <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>-free environment. The 6 million square metre project is based on the principles of an ancient walled city, combined with modern alternative energy technologies.</p>
<p>Critics say that the city concept appears cold and uninviting, however, this project “promises to question conventional urban wisdom at a fundamental level”. Masdar promises to set new benchmarks for the sustainable city of the future.” The city will be entirely self-sustaining Masdar CEO, <a href="/wiki/Sultan_Ahmed_Al_Jaber" title="Sultan Ahmed Al Jaber">Sultan Ahmed Al Jaber</a> explained, “... We are creating a synergetic environment; it is a true alternative energy cluster. Here you will find researchers, students, scientists, business investment professionals, and policy makers all within the same community. It will be a living example of sustainable development that will position Abu Dhabi and Masdar at the forefront of intelligent resource utilization. It will combine the talent, expertise and resources to enable the technological breakthroughs necessary for truly sustainable development.”</p>
<p>Construction of this large-scale project will occur in two phases. In phase one, a solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic power">photovoltaic power</a> plant will be built as a central energy source. In phase two, urban growth will occur. The site is located in close proximity to Abu Dhabi’s transportation infrastructure, which will allow for easy access to and from surrounding communities. An efficient network of rail, road and public transit will link the city to central Abu Dhabi and the international airport.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dongtan">Dongtan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dongtan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dongtan" title="Dongtan">Dongtan</a>, China is located at the east end of Chongming and is adjacent to the Chongming Dongtan National Nature Reserve. Seeing as this area is near wetlands, some question its effects on the surrounding environment. The director of the project, Peter Head, insists it will not affect the wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The developers plan on a fully built city, with 80,000 residents by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_for_sustainability_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning_for_sustainability-6">[6]</a></sup> Dongtan development plans include use of the ten principles of eco-cities.</p>
<p>Adjacent to booming Shanghai, plans are coming together for the prototypical city of Dongtan, which designers argue would be the world’s first truly sustainable new urban development. London-based Arup and the Shanghai Industrial Investment Corporation (SIIC), the city’s investment branch, have partnered to create a master plan for Dongtan, an area three quarters the size of Manhattan. The brief calls for integrated sustainable urban planning and design to create a city as close to carbon-neutral as possible within economic constraints. Located in sensitive wetlands on Chongming Island at the mouth of the Yangtze River just north of Shanghai, Dongtan’s first phase, a marina village of 20,000 inhabitants, will be unveiled at the 2010 World Expo in Shanghai.</p>
<p>By 2020, nearly 80,000 people are expected to inhabit the city’s environmentally sustainable neighborhoods. As a strategic partner, Arup is responsible for a range of services, including urban design, sustainable energy management, waste management, renewable energy process implementation, architecture, infrastructure, and even the planning of communities and social structures. Peter Head, director of Arup’s sustainable urban design, leads the project for the firm from its London’s office (during design, Arup is offsetting the emissions of its team’s travel to and from the site in cooperation with emissions brokerage firm CO2e). “Renewable energy will be used to reduce particulate CO2 emissions. Transport vehicles will run on batteries or hydrogen-fuel cells and not use any diesel or petrol, creating a relatively quiet city,” he explains. Other priorities include recycling organic waste to reduce landfills and generate clean energy.</p>
<p>Head insists development won’t affect the wetlands. “First of all, water usually discharged into the river will be collected, treated, and recycled within the city boundaries,” he says. “There will be a 2-mile buffer zone of eco-farm between city development and the wetlands.” While farming is water intensive, relatively small amounts of water reach the plants themselves. Head says Dongtan “will capture and recycle water in the city and use recycled water to grow green vegetables hydroponically. This makes the whole water cycle much more efficient". Dongtan is expected to eventually be home to more than 500,000 residents, which is an insignificant number for a country of 1.3 billion people. But the ambitious carbon-neutral experiment of Dongtan is meant to be a model for development worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiatives">Initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cars will be banned within the city, instead battery-powered and auto piloted <a href="/wiki/Personal_rapid_transit" title="Personal rapid transit">personal rapid transit</a> systems (PRT) will be used<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Visitors to the city must park their cars outside and use public transit.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_for_sustainability_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning_for_sustainability-6">[6]</a></sup> In both prototypes there will be ideal conditions to encourage walking or cycling. Additionally, both Masdar City and Dongtan will be connected to their larger counterparts via rail or road.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eco-cities" title="Eco-cities">Eco-cities</a> are self-sufficient in energy. They meet energy need through solar, wind and biomass sources. About 80%<sup id="cite_ref-New_scientist_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_scientist-9">[9]</a></sup> of energy will be solar energy, it will be generated through <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_panels" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic panels">photovoltaic panels</a>, concentrated solar collectors, and solar thermal tubes.<sup id="cite_ref-New_scientist_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_scientist-9">[9]</a></sup> In order to keep cooling costs down wind cooling towers and narrow streets will be put in, these help to maximize shaded areas. Planners in Dongtan are putting meters in each house to display energy use.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_for_sustainability_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning_for_sustainability-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guiding_principles">Guiding principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero-carbon_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Guiding principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
