<div class="" style="background-color: #F9F9F9; color: black; text-align: center; font-size: larger; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">IUPAC</a> definition</div>
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>Generic term used in the case of polymeric <i>material</i> that may contain other substances<br />
to improve performance and/or reduce costs.</p>
<p><i>Note 1</i>: The use of this term instead of <i><a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a></i> is a source of confusion and thus is<br />
not recommended.</p>
<p><i>Note 2</i>: This term is used in polymer engineering for materials often compounded that<br />
can be processed by flow.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Plastic</b> is a material consisting of any of a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_polymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic polymers">synthetic</a> or semi-synthetic <a href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a> that are <a href="/wiki/Malleability" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleability">malleable</a> and can be <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">molded</a> into solid objects. Plastics are typically <a href="/wiki/Organic_polymer" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic polymer">organic polymers</a> of high <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a>, but they often contain other substances. They are usually synthetic, most commonly derived from <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemicals</a>, but many are made from renewable materials such as polylactic acid from corn or cellulosics from cotton linters.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">Plasticity</a> is the general property of all materials that are able to irreversibly deform without breaking, but this occurs to such a degree with this class of moldable <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> that their name is an emphasis on this ability.</p>
<p>Due to their relatively low cost, ease of manufacture, versatility, and imperviousness to water, plastics are used in an enormous and expanding range of products, from paper clips to spaceships. They have already displaced many traditional materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)" title="Horn (anatomy)">horn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>, in most of their former uses. In developed countries, about a third of plastic is used in packaging and another third in buildings such as <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_siding" title="Vinyl siding">vinyl siding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> Other uses include automobiles (up to 20% plastic<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup>), furniture, and toys.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> In the developing world, the ratios may be different - for example, reportedly 42% of India's consumption is used in packaging.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> Plastics have many uses in the medical field as well, to include polymer implants, however the field of <a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">plastic surgery</a> is not named for use of plastic material, but rather the more generic meaning of the word plasticity in regard to the reshaping of flesh.</p>
<p>The world's first fully synthetic plastic was <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">bakelite</a>, invented in New York in 1907 by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Baekeland" title="Leo Baekeland">Leo Baekeland</a><sup id="cite_ref-Landmark_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landmark-4">[4]</a></sup> who coined the term 'plastics'.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Many chemists contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> of plastics, including <a href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Staudinger" title="Hermann Staudinger">Hermann Staudinger</a> who has been called "the father of <a href="/wiki/Polymer_chemistry" title="Polymer chemistry">polymer chemistry</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Francis_Mark" title="Herman Francis Mark">Herman Mark</a>, known as "the father of <a href="/wiki/Polymer_physics" title="Polymer physics">polymer physics</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The success and dominance of plastics starting in the early 20th century led to environmental concerns regarding its slow decomposition rate after being discarded as trash due to its composition of very large molecules. Toward the end of the century, one approach to this problem was met with wide efforts toward <a href="/wiki/Plastic_recycling" title="Plastic recycling">recycling</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plastic" class="extiw" title="wikt:plastic">plastic</a></i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> πλαστικός (<i>plastikos</i>) meaning "capable of being shaped or molded", from πλαστός (<i>plastos</i>) meaning "molded".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It refers to their malleability, or <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plasticity</a> during manufacture, that allows them to be <a href="/wiki/Casting" title="Casting">cast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_isostatic_pressing" title="Hot isostatic pressing">pressed</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Extruded" class="mw-redirect" title="Extruded">extruded</a> into a variety of shapes—such as <a href="/wiki/Artificial_membrane" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial membrane">films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">fibers</a>, plates, tubes, bottles, boxes, and much more.</p>
<p>The common word <i>plastic</i> should not be confused with the technical adjective <i>plastic</i>, which is applied to any material which undergoes a permanent change of shape (<a href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Plastic deformation">plastic deformation</a>) when strained beyond a certain point. <a href="/wiki/Aluminum" class="mw-redirect" title="Aluminum">Aluminum</a> which is stamped or forged, for instance, exhibits plasticity in this sense, but is not plastic in the common sense; in contrast, in their finished forms, some plastics will break before deforming and therefore are not plastic in the technical sense.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_plastic_development" title="Timeline of plastic development">Timeline of plastic development</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The development of plastics has evolved from the use of natural plastic materials (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Chewing_gum" title="Chewing gum">chewing gum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shellac" title="Shellac">shellac</a>) to the use of chemically modified, natural materials (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">natural rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galalith" title="Galalith">galalite</a>) and finally to completely synthetic molecules (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">bakelite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a>). Early plastics were bio-derived materials such as egg and blood proteins, which are <a href="/wiki/Organic_polymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic polymers">organic polymers</a>. In 1600 BC, <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesoamerican">Mesoamericans</a> used natural rubber for balls, bands, and figurines.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> Treated cattle horns were used as windows for lanterns in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Materials that mimicked the properties of horns were developed by treating milk-proteins (<a href="/wiki/Casein" title="Casein">casein</a>) with lye.</p>
<p>In the 1800s, as <a href="/wiki/Industrial_chemistry" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial chemistry">industrial chemistry</a> developed during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, many materials were reported. The development of plastics also accelerated with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Goodyear" title="Charles Goodyear">Charles Goodyear</a>'s discovery of <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanization</a> to thermoset materials derived from natural rubber.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parkesine" title="Parkesine">Parkesine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>) is considered the first man-made plastic. The plastic material was patented by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Parkes" title="Alexander Parkes">Alexander Parkes</a>, In <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> in 1856.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was unveiled at <a href="/wiki/1862_International_Exhibition" title="1862 International Exhibition">the 1862 Great International Exhibition</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Parkesine</i> won a bronze medal at the 1862 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_fair" title="World's fair">World's fair</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Parkesine was made from <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> (the major component of plant cell walls) treated with <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> as a solvent. The output of the process (commonly known as cellulose nitrate or pyroxilin) could be dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> and hardened into a transparent and elastic material that could be molded when heated.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By incorporating pigments into the product, it could be made to resemble <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>.</p>
<p>In 1897, the Hanover, Germany mass printing press owner Wilhelm Krische was commissioned to develop an alternative to blackboards.<sup id="cite_ref-ganoksin_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganoksin-12">[12]</a></sup> The resultant horn-like plastic made from the milk protein casein was developed in cooperation with the Austrian chemist (Friedrich) Adolph Spitteler (1846–1940). The final result was unsuitable for the original purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-Christel_Trimborn_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christel_Trimborn-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1893, French chemist Auguste Trillat discovered the means to insolubilize casein by immersion in formaldehyde, producing material marketed as <a href="/wiki/Galalith" title="Galalith">galalith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ganoksin_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganoksin-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1900s, <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">Bakelite</a>, the first fully synthetic thermoset, was reported by Belgian chemist <a href="/wiki/Leo_Baekeland" title="Leo Baekeland">Leo Baekeland</a> by using phenol and formaldehyde.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, improvements in chemical technology led to an explosion in new forms of plastics, with mass production beginning in the 1940s and 1950s (around <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-PlasticAge_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlasticAge-14">[14]</a></sup> Among the earliest examples in the wave of new polymers were <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> (PS), first produced by <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> in the 1930s,<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> (PVC), first created in 1872 but commercially produced in the late 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1923, Durite Plastics Inc. was the first manufacturer of phenol-furfural resins.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1933, <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemical Industries</a> (ICI) researchers Reginald Gibson and Eric Fawcett.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Natta" title="Giulio Natta">Giulio Natta</a> and began to be manufactured in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, expanded polystyrene (used for building insulation, packaging, and cups) was invented by <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Dow Chemical">Dow Chemical</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">Polyethylene terephthalate</a> (PET)'s discovery is credited to employees of the <a href="/wiki/Calico_Printers%27_Association" title="Calico Printers' Association">Calico Printers' Association</a> in the UK in 1941; it was licensed to <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> for the USA and ICI otherwise, and as one of the few plastics appropriate as a replacement for glass in many circumstances, resulting in widespread use for bottles in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span></h2>
<p>Most plastics contain <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> polymers. The vast majority of these polymers are based on chains of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atoms alone or with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> as well. The backbone is that part of the chain on the main "path" linking a large number of repeat units together. To customize the properties of a plastic, different molecular groups "hang" from the backbone (usually they are "hung" as part of the monomers before the monomers are linked together to form the polymer chain). The structure of these "side chains" influence the properties of the polymer. This fine tuning of the repeating unit's molecular structure influences the properties of the polymer.</p>
<p>Most plastics contain other organic or <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compounds</a> blended in. The amount of additives ranges from zero percentage (for example in polymers used to wrap foods) to more than 50% for certain electronic applications. The average content of additives is 20% by weight of the polymer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many of the controversies associated with plastics are associated with the additives.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Organotin_chemistry" title="Organotin chemistry">Organotin compounds</a> are particularly toxic.<sup id="cite_ref-Transportandrelease_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transportandrelease-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fillers">Fillers</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Filler_(materials)" title="Filler (materials)">Fillers</a> improve performance and/or reduce production costs. <a href="/wiki/Stabilizer_(chemistry)" title="Stabilizer (chemistry)">Stabilizing additives</a> include <a href="/wiki/Fire_retardant" title="Fire retardant">fire retardants</a> to lower the flammability of the material. Many plastics contain fillers, relatively inert and inexpensive materials that make the product cheaper by weight.</p>
<p>Typically fillers are mineral in origin, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>. Some fillers are more chemically active and are called reinforcing agents. Other fillers include zinc oxide, wood flour, ivory dust, cellulose and starch.<sup id="cite_ref-Seymour_1987_374_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seymour_1987_374-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasticizers">Plasticizers</span></h3>
<p>Since many organic polymers are too rigid for particular applications, they are blended with <a href="/wiki/Plasticizer" title="Plasticizer">plasticizers</a> (the largest group of additives<sup id="cite_ref-Transportandrelease_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transportandrelease-17">[17]</a></sup>), oily compounds that confer improved <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheology</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colorants">Colorants</span></h3>
<p>Colorants are common additives, although their weight contribution is small.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span></h2>
<p>Plastics are usually classified by their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">chemical structure</a> of the polymer's backbone and <a href="/wiki/Side_chain" title="Side chain">side chains</a>. Some important groups in these classifications are the <a href="/wiki/Acryl_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Acryl group">acrylics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyesters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicones" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicones">silicones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyurethanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyurethanes">polyurethanes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halocarbon" title="Halocarbon">halogenated plastics</a>. Plastics can also be classified by the chemical process used in their synthesis, such as <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyaddition" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyaddition">polyaddition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">cross-linking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoplastics_and_thermosetting_polymers">Thermoplastics and thermosetting polymers</span></h3>
<p>There are two types of plastics: <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermosetting_polymer" title="Thermosetting polymer">thermosetting polymers</a>. Thermoplastics are the plastics that do not undergo chemical change in their composition when heated and can be molded again and again. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Common thermoplastics range from 20,000 to 500,000 <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic mass unit">amu</a>, while thermosets are assumed to have infinite molecular weight. These chains are made up of many repeating molecular units, known as <i>repeat units</i>, derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a></i>; each polymer chain will have several thousand repeating units.</p>
<p>Thermosets can melt and take shape once; after they have solidified, they stay solid. In the thermosetting process, a chemical reaction occurs that is irreversible. The vulcanization of rubber is a thermosetting process. Before heating with sulfur, the polyisoprene is a tacky, slightly runny material, but after vulcanization the product is rigid and non-tacky.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_classifications">Other classifications</span></h3>
<p>Other classifications are based on qualities that are relevant for manufacturing or <a href="/wiki/Product_design" title="Product design">product design</a>. Examples of such classes are the thermoplastic and thermoset, <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_plastic" title="Engineering plastic">structural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical resistance">electrically conductive</a>. Plastics can also be classified by various <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical properties</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass transition temperature">glass transition temperature</a>, and resistance to various chemical products.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradability">Biodegradability</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">Biodegradable plastic</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biodegradable" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodegradable">Biodegradable</a> plastics break down (degrade) upon exposure to sunlight (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Ultra-violet_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultra-violet radiation">ultra-violet radiation</a>), water or dampness, bacteria, enzymes, wind abrasion, and in some instances, rodent, pest, or insect attack are also included as forms of <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>. Some modes of degradation require that the plastic be exposed at the surface, whereas other modes will only be effective if certain conditions exist in landfill or composting systems. <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">Starch</a> powder has been mixed with plastic as a filler to allow it to degrade more easily, but it still does not lead to complete breakdown of the plastic. Some researchers have actually <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically engineered</a> bacteria that synthesize a completely biodegradable plastic, but this material, such as <a href="/wiki/Biopol" class="mw-redirect" title="Biopol">Biopol</a>, is expensive at present.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Companies have made <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_additives" title="Biodegradable additives">biodegradable additives</a> to enhance the biodegradation of plastics.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_vs_synthetic">Natural vs synthetic</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioplastic" title="Bioplastic">Bioplastic</a></div>
<p>Most plastics are produced from <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemicals</a>. Motivated by the finiteness of petrochemical reserves and threat of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, bioplastics are being developed. Bioplastics are made substantially from renewable plant materials such as cellulose and starch.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In comparison to the global consumption of all flexible packaging, estimated at 12.3 million tonnes/year, estimates put global production capacity at 327,000 tonnes/year for related bio-derived materials.<sup id="cite_ref-NNFCC_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NNFCC-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystalline_vs_amorphous">Crystalline vs amorphous</span></h4>
<p>Some plastics are partially <a href="/wiki/Crystalline" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> and partially <a href="/wiki/Amorphous" class="mw-redirect" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> in <a href="/wiki/Molecular" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular">molecular</a> structure, giving them both a <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> (the temperature at which the attractive <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> are overcome) and one or more glass transitions (temperatures above which the extent of localized molecular flexibility is substantially increased). The so-called <a href="/wiki/Crystallization_of_polymers" title="Crystallization of polymers">semi-crystalline</a> plastics include polyethylene, polypropylene, poly (vinyl chloride), polyamides (nylons), polyesters and some polyurethanes. Many plastics are completely amorphous, such as polystyrene and its copolymers, poly (<a href="/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate" title="Methyl methacrylate">methyl methacrylate</a>), and all thermosets.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bakelite">Bakelite</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">Bakelite</a></div>
<p>The first plastic based on a synthetic polymer was made from <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, with the first viable and cheap synthesis methods invented in 1907, by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Hendrik_Baekeland" class="mw-redirect" title="Leo Hendrik Baekeland">Leo Hendrik Baekeland</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Belgian-American" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian-American">Belgian-born American</a> living in <a href="/wiki/New_York_state" class="mw-redirect" title="New York state">New York state</a>. Baekeland was looking for an insulating shellac to coat wires in electric motors and generators. He found that combining phenol (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH) and formaldehyde (HCOH) formed a sticky mass and later found that the material could be mixed with wood flour, asbestos, or slate dust to create strong and fire resistant "composite" materials. The new material tended to foam during synthesis, requiring that Baekeland build pressure vessels to force out the bubbles and provide a smooth, uniform product, as he announced in 1909, in a meeting of the American Chemical Society.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Bakelite was originally used for electrical and mechanical parts, coming into widespread use in consumer goods and jewelry in the 1920s. Bakelite was a purely synthetic material, not derived from living matter. It was also an early thermosetting plastic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polystyrene">Polystyrene</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">Polystyrene</a> and <a href="/wiki/PVC" class="mw-redirect" title="PVC">PVC</a></div>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Styrene polymerization</div>

<p>Unplasticised polystyrene is a rigid, brittle, inexpensive plastic that has been used to make <a href="/wiki/Plastic_model" title="Plastic model">plastic model</a> kits and similar knick-knacks. It also is the basis for some of the most popular "foamed" plastics, under the name <i>styrene foam</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Styrofoam" title="Styrofoam">Styrofoam</a></i>. Like most other foam plastics, foamed polystyrene can be manufactured in an "open cell" form, in which the foam bubbles are interconnected, as in an absorbent sponge, and "closed cell", in which all the bubbles are distinct, like tiny balloons, as in gas-filled foam insulation and flotation devices. In the late 1950s, <i>high impact</i> styrene was introduced, which was not brittle. It finds much current use as the substance of toy figurines and novelties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyvinyl_chloride">Polyvinyl chloride</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vinylchloride_polymerization.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Vinylchloride_polymerization.png/220px-Vinylchloride_polymerization.png" width="220" height="51" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Vinylchloride_polymerization.png/330px-Vinylchloride_polymerization.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Vinylchloride_polymerization.png/440px-Vinylchloride_polymerization.png 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="157" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vinylchloride_polymerization.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vinylchloride polymerization</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Polyvinyl chloride (PVC, commonly called "vinyl")<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> incorporates chlorine atoms. The C-Cl bonds in the backbone are hydrophobic and resist oxidation (and burning). PVC is stiff, strong, heat and weather resistant, properties that recommend its use in devices for <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a>, gutters, house siding, enclosures for computers and other electronics gear. PVC can also be softened with chemical processing, and in this form it is now used for <a href="/wiki/Shrinkwrap" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrinkwrap">shrink-wrap</a>, food packaging, and rain gear.</p>
<p>All PVC polymers are degraded by heat and light. When this happens, hydrogen chloride is released into the atmosphere and oxidation of the compound occurs.<sup id="cite_ref-PUSA_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PUSA-27">[27]</a></sup> Because hydrogen chloride readily combines with water vapor in the air to form hydrochloric acid,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> polyvinyl chloride is not recommended for long-term archival storage of silver, photographic film or paper (<a href="/wiki/Mylar" class="mw-redirect" title="Mylar">mylar</a> is preferable).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nylon">Nylon</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">Nylon</a></div>
<p>The plastics industry was revolutionized in the 1930s with the announcement of <a href="/wiki/Polyamide" title="Polyamide">polyamide</a> (PA), far better known by its trade name <b>nylon</b>. Nylon was the first purely synthetic fiber, introduced by <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont Corporation</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1939_World%27s_Fair" class="mw-redirect" title="1939 World's Fair">1939 World's Fair</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>
<p>In 1927, DuPont had begun a secret development project designated Fiber66, under the direction of Harvard chemist <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Carothers" title="Wallace Carothers">Wallace Carothers</a> and chemistry department director <a href="/wiki/Elmer_Keiser_Bolton" title="Elmer Keiser Bolton">Elmer Keiser Bolton</a>. Carothers had been hired to perform pure research, and he worked to understand the new materials' molecular structure and physical properties. He took some of the first steps in the molecular design of the materials.</p>
<p>His work led to the discovery of synthetic nylon fiber, which was very strong but also very flexible. The first application was for bristles for <a href="/wiki/Toothbrushes" class="mw-redirect" title="Toothbrushes">toothbrushes</a>. However, Du Pont's real target was <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, particularly silk <a href="/wiki/Stocking" title="Stocking">stockings</a>. Carothers and his team synthesized a number of different polyamides including polyamide 6.6 and 4.6, as well as polyesters.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="center">
<div class="thumb tnone">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg/500px-Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg.png" width="500" height="63" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg/750px-Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg/1000px-Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="3063" data-file-height="385" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Condensation_polymerization_diacid_diamine.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
General condensation polymerization reaction for nylon</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It took DuPont twelve years and US$27 million to refine nylon, and to synthesize and develop the industrial processes for bulk manufacture. With such a major investment, it was no surprise that Du Pont spared little expense to promote nylon after its introduction, creating a public sensation, or "nylon mania".</p>
<p>Nylon mania came to an abrupt stop at the end of 1941 when the USA entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The production capacity that had been built up to produce <a href="/wiki/Pantyhose" title="Pantyhose">nylon stockings</a>, or just <i>nylons</i>, for American women was taken over to manufacture vast numbers of parachutes for fliers and paratroopers. After the war ended, DuPont went back to selling nylon to the public, engaging in another promotional campaign in 1946 that resulted in an even bigger craze, triggering the so-called <a href="/wiki/Nylon_riots" title="Nylon riots">nylon riots</a>.</p>
<p>Subsequently, polyamides 6, 10, 11, and 12 have been developed based on monomers which are ring compounds; e.g. <a href="/wiki/Caprolactam" title="Caprolactam">caprolactam</a>. Nylon 66 is a material manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Step-growth_polymerization" title="Step-growth polymerization">condensation polymerization</a>.</p>
<p>Nylons still remain important plastics, and not just for use in fabrics. In its bulk form it is very wear resistant, particularly if oil-impregnated, and so is used to build gears, <a href="/wiki/Plain_bearing" title="Plain bearing">plain bearings</a>, valve seats, seals and because of good heat-resistance, increasingly for under-the-hood applications in cars, and other mechanical parts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poly.28methyl_methacrylate.29">Poly(methyl methacrylate)</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Poly(methyl_methacrylate)" title="Poly(methyl methacrylate)">Poly(methyl methacrylate)</a></div>
<p><b>Poly(methyl methacrylate)</b> (<b>PMMA</b>), also known as <b>acrylic</b> or <b>acrylic glass</b> as well as by the trade names <b>Plexiglas</b>, <b>Acrylite</b>, <b>Lucite</b>, and <b>Perspex</b> among several others (see below), is a <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Transparency (optics)">transparent</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> often used in sheet form as a lightweight or shatter-resistant alternative to <a href="/wiki/Soda-lime_glass" title="Soda-lime glass">glass</a>. The same material can be utilised as a casting resin, in inks and coatings, and has many other uses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rubber">Rubber</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">Natural rubber</a> is an elastomer (an elastic hydrocarbon polymer) that originally was derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a></i>, a milky <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloidal suspension</a> found in specialised vessels in some plants. It is useful directly in this form (indeed, the first appearance of rubber in Europe was cloth waterproofed with unvulcanized latex from Brazil). However, in 1839, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Goodyear" title="Charles Goodyear">Charles Goodyear</a> invented vulcanized rubber; a form of natural rubber heated with sulfur (and a few other chemicals), forming cross-links between polymer chains (<a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanization</a>), improving elasticity and durability.</p>
<p>In 1851, Nelson Goodyear added fillers to natural rubber materials to form <a href="/wiki/Ebonite" title="Ebonite">ebonite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Seymour_1987_374_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seymour_1987_374-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_rubber">Synthetic rubber</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">Synthetic rubber</a></div>
<p>The first fully synthetic rubber was synthesized by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Vasiljevich_Lebedev" class="mw-redirect" title="Sergei Vasiljevich Lebedev">Sergei Lebedev</a> in 1910. In World War II, supply blockades of natural rubber from <a href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a> caused a boom in development of synthetic rubber, notably <a href="/wiki/Styrene-butadiene" title="Styrene-butadiene">styrene-butadiene rubber</a>. In 1941, annual production of synthetic rubber in the U.S. was only 231 tonnes which increased to 840,000 tonnes in 1945. In the <a href="/wiki/Space_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Space race">space race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_arms_race" title="Nuclear arms race">nuclear arms race</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caltech" class="mw-redirect" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> researchers experimented with using synthetic rubbers for solid fuel for rockets. Ultimately, all large military rockets and missiles would use synthetic rubber based solid fuels, and they would also play a significant part in the civilian space effort.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_plastics">Properties of plastics</span></h2>
<p>The properties of plastics are defined chiefly by the organic chemistry of the polymer such as hardness, density, and resistance to heat, organic solvents, <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>. In particular, most plastics will melt upon heating to a few hundred degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">celsius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> While plastics can be made electrically conductive, with the conductivity of up to 80 kS/cm in stretch-oriented <a href="/wiki/Polyacetylene" title="Polyacetylene">polyacetylene</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> they are still no match for most metals like <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> which have conductivities of several hundreds kS/cm.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h2>
<p>Pure plastics have low toxicity due to their insolubility in water and because they are biochemically inert, due to a large molecular weight. Plastic products contain a variety of additives, some of which can be toxic. For example, <a href="/wiki/Plasticizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasticizers">plasticizers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Adipate" title="Adipate">adipates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phthalate" title="Phthalate">phthalates</a> are often added to brittle plastics like polyvinyl chloride to make them pliable enough for use in food packaging, <a href="/wiki/Toys" class="mw-redirect" title="Toys">toys</a>, and many other items. Traces of these compounds can leach out of the product. Owing to concerns over the effects of such leachates, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has restricted the use of <a href="/wiki/Bis(2-ethylhexyl)_phthalate" title="Bis(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate">DEHP</a> (di-2-ethylhexyl phthalate) and other phthalates in some applications, and the United States has limited the use of DEHP, <a href="/wiki/Dibutyl_phthalate" title="Dibutyl phthalate">DPB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzyl_butyl_phthalate" title="Benzyl butyl phthalate">BBP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diisononyl_phthalate" title="Diisononyl phthalate">DINP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diisodecyl_phthalate" title="Diisodecyl phthalate">DIDP</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Di(n-octyl)_phthalate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Di(n-octyl) phthalate (page does not exist)">DnOP</a> in children's toys and child care articles with the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Product_Safety_Improvement_Act" title="Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act">Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act</a>. Some compounds leaching from polystyrene food containers have been proposed to interfere with hormone functions and are suspected human carcinogens.<sup id="cite_ref-NationalGeographic_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeographic-33">[33]</a></sup> Other chemicals of potential concern include <a href="/wiki/Alkylphenol" title="Alkylphenol">alkylphenols</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Transportandrelease_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transportandrelease-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whereas the finished plastic may be non-toxic, the monomers used in the manufacture of the parent polymers may be toxic. In some cases, small amounts of those chemicals can remain trapped in the product unless suitable processing is employed. For example, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>'s <a href="/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="International Agency for Research on Cancer">International Agency for Research on Cancer</a> (IARC) has recognized <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>, the precursor to PVC, as a human <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NationalGeographic_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeographic-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bisphenol_A">Bisphenol A</span></h3>
<p>Some polymers may also decompose into the monomers or other toxic substances when heated. In 2011, it was reported that "almost all plastic products" sampled released chemicals with estrogenic activity, although the researchers identified plastics which did not leach chemicals with estrogenic activity.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The primary building block of <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">bisphenol A</a> (BPA), is an <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptor</a> that may leach into food.<sup id="cite_ref-NationalGeographic_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NationalGeographic-33">[33]</a></sup> Research in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Health_Perspectives" title="Environmental Health Perspectives">Environmental Health Perspectives</a> finds that BPA leached from the lining of tin cans, <a href="/wiki/Dental_sealant" title="Dental sealant">dental sealants</a> and polycarbonate bottles can increase body weight of lab animals' offspring.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> A more recent animal study suggests that even low-level exposure to BPA results in insulin resistance, which can lead to inflammation and heart disease.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of January 2010, the LA Times newspaper reports that the United States FDA is spending $30 million to investigate indications of BPA being linked to cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bis(2-ethylhexyl)_adipate" title="Bis(2-ethylhexyl) adipate">Bis(2-ethylhexyl) adipate</a>, present in <a href="/wiki/Plastic_wrap" title="Plastic wrap">plastic wrap</a> based on PVC, is also of concern, as are the <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> present in <a href="/wiki/New_car_smell" title="New car smell">new car smell</a>.</p>
<p>The European Union has a permanent ban on the use of <a href="/wiki/Phthalates" class="mw-redirect" title="Phthalates">phthalates</a> in toys. In 2009, the United States government banned certain types of phthalates commonly used in plastic.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution" title="Plastic pollution">Plastic pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">Marine debris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a></div>
<p>Most plastics are durable and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">degrade</a> very slowly; the very chemical bonds that make them so durable tend to make them resistant to most natural processes of degradation. However, microbial species and communities capable of degrading plastics are discovered from time to time, and some show promise as being useful for bioremediating certain classes of plastic waste.</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1975 a team of Japanese scientists studying ponds containing <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">waste water</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> factory, discovered a strain of <i><a href="/wiki/Flavobacterium" title="Flavobacterium">Flavobacterium</a></i> that digested certain byproducts of <a href="/wiki/Nylon_6" title="Nylon 6">nylon 6</a> manufacture, such as the linear dimer of <a href="/wiki/Aminocaproic_acid" title="Aminocaproic acid">6-aminohexanoate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Nylon 4 or polybutyrolactam can be degraded by the (ND-10 and ND-11) strands of Pseudomonas sp. found in sludge. This produced γ-aminobutyric acid (GABA) as a byproduct.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid19865515_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19865515-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Several species of soil fungi can consume <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This includes two species of the Ecuadorian fungus <a href="/wiki/Pestalotiopsis" title="Pestalotiopsis">Pestalotiopsis</a> that can consume polyurethane aerobically and also in anaerobic conditions such as those at the bottom of landfills.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenic_consortia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Methanogenic consortia (page does not exist)">Methanogenic consortia</a> degrade <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a>, using it as a carbon source.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_putida" title="Pseudomonas putida">Pseudomonas putida</a> can convert <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a> oil into various <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable</a> <a href="/wiki/Polyhydroxyalkanoates" title="Polyhydroxyalkanoates">polyhydroxyalkanoates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>Microbial communities isolated from soil samples mixed with starch have been shown to be capable of degrading <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fungus <a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_fumigatus" title="Aspergillus fumigatus">Aspergillus fumigatus</a> effectively degrades plasticized PVC.<sup id="cite_ref-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_45-46_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_45-46-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phanerochaete_chrysosporium" class="mw-redirect" title="Phanerochaete chrysosporium">Phanerochaete chrysosporium</a> has been grown on PVC in a mineral salt agar.<sup id="cite_ref-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_76_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_76-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phanerochaete_chrysosporium" class="mw-redirect" title="Phanerochaete chrysosporium">Phanerochaete chrysosporium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lentinus_tigrinus" title="Lentinus tigrinus">Lentinus tigrinus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_niger" title="Aspergillus niger">Aspergillus niger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_sydowii" title="Aspergillus sydowii">Aspergillus sydowii</a> can also effectively degrade PVC.<sup id="cite_ref-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_122_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_122-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phanerochaete_chrysosporium" class="mw-redirect" title="Phanerochaete chrysosporium">Phanerochaete chrysosporium</a> was grown on PVC in a mineral salt agar.<sup id="cite_ref-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_76_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ishtiaq_Ali_2011_76-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acinetobacter" title="Acinetobacter">Acinetobacter</a> has been found to partially degrade low molecular weight <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> <a href="/wiki/Oligomers" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligomers">oligomers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid19865515_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19865515-40">[40]</a></sup> When used in combination, <a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_fluorescens" title="Pseudomonas fluorescens">Pseudomonas fluorescens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sphingomonas" title="Sphingomonas">Sphingomonas</a> can degrade over 40% of the weight of plastic bags in less than three months.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The thermophilic bacterium <a href="/wiki/Brevibacillus_borstelensis" title="Brevibacillus borstelensis">Brevibacillus borstelensis</a> (strain 707) was isolated from a soil sample and found capable of using low-density <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> as a sole carbon source when incubated at 50 degrees Celsius. Pre-exposure of the plastic to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation broke chemical bonds and aided biodegradation; the longer the period of UV exposure, the greater the promotion of the degradation.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Less desirably, hazardous molds have been found aboard space stations, molds that degrade rubber into a digestible form.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>Several species of yeasts, bacteria, algae and lichens have been found growing on synthetic polymer artifacts in museums and at archaeological sites.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the plastic-polluted waters of the <a href="/wiki/Sargasso_Sea" title="Sargasso Sea">Sargasso Sea</a>, bacteria have been found that consume various types of plastic; however it is unknown to what extent these bacteria effectively clean up poisons rather than simply releasing them into the marine microbial ecosystem.</li>
<li>Plastic eating microbes also have been found in landfills.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nocardia" title="Nocardia">Nocardia</a> can degrade PET with an esterase enzyme.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>The fungus <a href="/wiki/Geotrichum_candidum" title="Geotrichum candidum">Geotrichum candidum</a>, found in Belize, has been found to consume the <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> plastic found in CDs.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenol-formaldehyde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phenol-formaldehyde (page does not exist)">Phenol-formaldehyde</a>, commonly known as bakelite, is degraded by the white rot fungus <a href="/wiki/Phanerochaete_chrysosporium" class="mw-redirect" title="Phanerochaete chrysosporium">Phanerochaete chrysosporium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Futuro" title="Futuro">futuro</a> house was made of fibreglass-reinforced polyesters, polyester-polyurethane, and poly(methylmethacrylate.) One such house was found to be harmfully degraded by Cyanobacteria and Archaea.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Since the 1950s, one billion tons of plastic have been discarded and some of that material might persist for centuries or much longer, as is demonstrated by the persistence of natural materials such as amber.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Serious environmental threats from plastic have been suggested in the light of the increasing presence of <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastics</a> in the marine food chain along with many highly toxic chemical pollutants that accumulate in plastics. They also accumulate in larger fragmented pieces of plastic called <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">nurdles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In the 1960s the latter were observed in the guts of seabirds, and since then have been found in increasing concentration.<sup id="cite_ref-Accumulation_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Accumulation-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2009, it was estimated that 10% of modern waste was plastics,<sup id="cite_ref-PlasticAge_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PlasticAge-14">[14]</a></sup> although estimates vary according to region.<sup id="cite_ref-Accumulation_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Accumulation-63">[63]</a></sup> Meanwhile, 50-80% of debris in marine areas is plastic.<sup id="cite_ref-Accumulation_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Accumulation-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the ban on the use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFCs</a> in extrusion of polystyrene (and in general use, except in life-critical fire suppression systems; see <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>), the production of polystyrene contributed to the depletion of the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>, but current extrusion processes use non-CFCs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span></h3>
<p>The effect of plastics on global warming is mixed. Plastics are generally made from petroleum. If the plastic is incinerated, it increases carbon emissions; if it is placed in a landfill, it becomes a carbon sink<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> although biodegradable plastics have caused methane emissions. <sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Due to the lightness of plastic versus glass or metal, plastic may reduce energy consumption. For example, packaging beverages in PET plastic rather than glass or metal is estimated to save 52% in transportation energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Applications_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applications-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_of_plastics">Production of plastics</span></h3>
<p>Production of plastics from crude oil requires 62 to 108 MJ/Kg (taking into account the average efficiency of US utility stations of 35%). Producing silicon and semiconductors for modern electronic equipment is even more energy consuming: 230 to 235 MJ/Kg of silicon, and about 3,000 MJ/Kg of semiconductors.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> This is much higher than the energy needed to produce many other materials, e.g. iron (from iron ore) requires 20-25 MJ/Kg of energy, glass (from sand, etc.) 18-35 MJ/Kg, steel (from iron) 20-50 MJ/Kg, paper (from timber) 25-50 MJ/Kg.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incineration_of_plastics">Incineration of plastics</span></h3>
<p>Controlled high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incineration</a>, above 850 degrees Celsius for two seconds,<sup id="cite_ref-incineration_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-incineration-68">[68]</a></sup> performed with selective additional heating, breaks down toxic dioxins and furans from burning plastic, and is widely used in municipal solid waste incineration.<sup id="cite_ref-incineration_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-incineration-68">[68]</a></sup> Municipal solid waste incinerators also normally include flue gas treatments to reduce pollutants further.<sup id="cite_ref-incineration_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-incineration-68">[68]</a></sup> This is needed because uncontrolled incineration of plastic produces polychlorinated dibenzo-p-dioxins, a carcinogen (cancer causing chemical). The problem occurs because the heat content of the waste stream varies.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Open-air burning of plastic occurs at lower temperatures, and normally releases such <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> fumes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrolytic_disposal">Pyrolytic disposal</span></h3>
<p>Plastics can be <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis#Plastic_waste_disposal" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolyzed</a> into <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">hydrocarbon</a> fuels, since plastics include hydrogen and carbon. One kilogram of waste plastic produces roughly a liter of hydrocarbon.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling">Recycling</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plastic_recycling" title="Plastic recycling">Plastic recycling</a></div>
<p>Thermoplastics can be remelted and reused, and thermoset plastics can be ground up and used as filler, although the purity of the material tends to degrade with each reuse cycle. There are methods by which plastics can be broken down to a feedstock state.</p>
<p>The greatest challenge to the recycling of plastics is the difficulty of automating the sorting of plastic wastes, making it <a href="/wiki/Labor_intensity" title="Labor intensity">labor-intensive</a>. Typically, workers sort the plastic by looking at the resin identification code, although common containers like soda bottles can be sorted from memory. Typically, the caps for PETE bottles are made from a different kind of plastic which is not recyclable, which presents additional problems for the sorting process. Other recyclable materials such as metals are easier to process mechanically. However, new processes of mechanical sorting are being developed to increase the capacity and efficiency of plastic recycling.</p>
<p>While containers are usually made from a single type and color of plastic, making them relatively easy to sort, a consumer product like a cellular phone may have many small parts consisting of over a dozen different types and colors of plastics. In such cases, the resources it would take to separate the plastics far exceed their value and the item is discarded. However, developments are taking place in the field of <a href="/wiki/Active_Disassembly" title="Active Disassembly">active disassembly</a>, which may result in more product components being reused or recycled. Recycling certain types of plastics can be unprofitable as well. For example, polystyrene is rarely recycled because the process is usually not cost effective. These unrecycled wastes are typically disposed of in <a href="/wiki/Landfills" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfills">landfills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incinerated</a> or used to produce electricity at <a href="/wiki/Trash-to-energy_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Trash-to-energy plant">waste-to-energy</a> plants.</p>
<p>An early success in the recycling of plastics is <a href="/wiki/Vinyloop" title="Vinyloop">Vinyloop</a>, an industrial process to separate PVC from other materials through dissolution, filtration and separation of contaminants. A solvent is used in a closed loop to elute PVC from the waste. This makes it possible to recycle composite PVC waste, which is normally incinerated or put in a landfill. Vinyloop-based recycled PVC's primary energy demand is 46 percent lower than conventionally produced PVC. The global warming potential is 39 percent lower. This is why the use of recycled material leads to a significantly better ecological outcome.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> This process was used after the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> in London 2012. Parts of temporary Buildings like the <a href="/wiki/Water_Polo_Arena" title="Water Polo Arena">Water Polo Arena</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Artillery_Barracks" title="Royal Artillery Barracks">Royal Artillery Barracks</a> were recycled. In this way, the PVC Policy could be fulfilled, which says that no PVC waste should be left after the games had ended.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988, to assist recycling of disposable items, the Plastic Bottle Institute of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Plastics_Industry" title="Society of the Plastics Industry">Society of the Plastics Industry</a> devised a now-familiar scheme to mark plastic bottles by plastic type. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">Chemical industry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plastics_industry" title="Plastics industry">Plastics industry</a></div>
<p>Plastic manufacturing is a major part of the chemical industry, and some of <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_chemical_producers" title="List of largest chemical producers">largest chemical companies internationally</a> became involved early in the industry, such as industry leaders <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical</a>.</p>
<p>In 2014, sales of the top fifty companies amounted to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>961,300,000,000.<sup id="cite_ref-c.26en20142_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c.26en20142-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> was the world's largest chemical producer for the ninth year in a row.<sup id="cite_ref-c.26en20142_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c.26en20142-74">[74]</a></sup> More than half of the companies on the list were headquartered in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (twelve), <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (eight) or <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (six); eighteen countries in total were represented.</p>
<p>Trade associations which represent the industry include the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemistry_Council" title="American Chemistry Council">American Chemistry Council</a>.</p>
