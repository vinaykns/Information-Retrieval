			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Payment for ecosystem services</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Payments for ecosystem services</b> (PES), also known as <b>payments for environmental services (or benefits)</b>, are incentives offered to farmers or landowners in exchange for managing their land to provide some sort of ecological service. They have been defined as "a transparent system for the additional provision of environmental services through conditional payments to voluntary providers."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These programmes promote the conservation of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Marketplace" class="mw-redirect" title="Marketplace">marketplace</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a> have no standardized definition but might broadly be called "the benefits of nature to households, communities, and economies"<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or, more simply, "the good things nature does."<sup id="cite_ref-jenk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenk-3">[3]</a></sup> Twenty-four specific ecosystem services were identified and assessed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a></i>, a 2005 UN-sponsored report designed to assess the state of the world's ecosystems. The report defined the broad categories of ecosystem services as food production (in the form of <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, capture <a href="/wiki/Fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wildness" title="Wildness">wild</a> foods), <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> (in the form of <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>), <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Resources">resources</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biochemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Biochemicals">biochemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural">natural</a> <a href="/wiki/Medicines" class="mw-redirect" title="Medicines">medicines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>), fresh <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Air quality">air quality</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Purified_water" title="Purified water">water purification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waste_treatment" title="Waste treatment">waste treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_hazard" title="Natural hazard">natural hazard</a> <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural">cultural</a> services (including <a href="/wiki/Spirtuality" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirtuality">spiritual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Values" class="mw-redirect" title="Values">values</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Notably, however, there is a "big three" among these 24 services which are currently receiving the most money and interest worldwide. These are climate change mitigation, watershed services and biodiversity conservation, and demand for these services in particular is predicted to continue to grow as time goes on.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> One seminal 1997 <i>Nature</i> magazine article estimated the annual value of global ecological benefits at $33 trillion, a number nearly twice the gross global product at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2014, the author of this 1997 research (Robert Costanza) and a qualified group of co-authors re-took this assessment - using only a slightly modified methodology but with more detailed 2011 data - and increased the aggregate global ecosystem services provisioning estimate to between $125–145 trillion a year. The same research project also estimated between $4.3 to 20.2 trillion a year of losses to ecosystem services, due to land use change.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>PES has also been touted as a tool for rural development. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> released a document outlining the place of PES in development.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> But the link between the environment and development had been officially recognized long before with the 1972 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">Stockholm Conference on the Human Environment</a> and later reaffirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Rio Conference on Environment and Development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, it is important to note PES programs are usually not designed to be primarily poverty alleviation schemes, although they may incorporate development mechanisms.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some PES programs involve contracts between consumers of ecosystem services and the suppliers of these services. However, the majority of the PES programs are funded by governments and involve intermediaries, such as non-government organisations. The party supplying the environmental services normally holds the <a href="/wiki/Property_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Property rights">property rights</a> over an environmental good that provides a flow of benefits to the demanding party in return for compensation. In the case of private contracts, the beneficiaries of the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> services are willing to pay a price that can be expected to be lower than their welfare gain due to the services. The providers of the ecosystem services can be expected to be willing to accept a payment that is greater than the cost of providing the services.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_perspectives_on_payment_for_ecosystem_services">Theoretical perspectives on payment for ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theoretical perspectives on payment for ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three main theoretical perspectives concerning PES. The first is that of environmental economics, the second of ecological economics, and the third of those who reject the very idea of ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_economics">Environmental economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Environmental economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basic conceptualization of nature from the perspective of environmental economics is that manufactured capital can be used as a substitute for natural capital.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-13">[13]</a></sup> The definition of PES provided by environmental economics is the most popular: a voluntary transaction between a service buyer and service seller that takes place on the condition that either a specific ecosystem service is provided or land is used in a way to secure that service.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This definition is directly related to the Coase theorem, upon which PES is strongly based from the environmental economics perspective, which states that in a competitive market, in the absence of transaction costs and in the presence of clear property rights, direct negotiation between private parties can lead to efficient outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, in reality, transaction costs are virtually always present and private parties cannot always reach agreements on their own. One of the main reasons is lack of sustained financing, which often leads governments to provide some type of funding assistance. The environmental economics theorists acknowledge that PES systems can resemble an environmental subsidy, complicating the strict Coasian backing.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_economics">Ecological economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecological economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The conceptualization of nature as understood by ecological economics is that manufactured capital and natural capital are not exclusive or substitutable, but rather complementary.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-13">[13]</a></sup> PES as understood by ecological economics comprises three schematic components.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">[17]</a></sup> The first surrounds the importance of the economic incentive. This idea concerns the relative weight an economic incentive may carry when understood in relation to social, moral, or other non-economic incentives.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">[17]</a></sup> The second component is directness of the transfer, referring to the extent of interaction between ultimate buyers and sellers.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">[17]</a></sup> The most direct program would occur between one buyer and one seller, with no intermediaries. A relatively indirect program would remove the buyers and sellers from each other, placing intermediaries between them, commonly in the form of NGOs and governments. The third and final component is the degree of commodification. This addresses the extent to which the environmental service (ES) being provided can be specifically and clearly assessed and measured. Some ES may be relatively easy to assess, such as tons of carbon sequestered, while others may prove difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rejection_of_ecosystem_services">Rejection of ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rejection of ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Those who reject the idea of valuation of ecosystem services argue that nature should be conserved and valued for nature's sake, and that nature's value is impossible to quantify because its value is inherently infinite.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup> They posit that the attempt to force the idea of ecosystem services into the market system leads to conservation only when it is deemed useful for human life, abandoning ideals environmental conservation when nature conflicts with human interest or simply does not affect human activity.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">[18]</a></sup> There are also those who support the valuation of nature from a purely practical standpoint, expressed in the idea that "something is better than nothing." They realize and acknowledge the problematic nature of the quantitative valuation of nature but at the same time argue that practically, in a highly commodified society, it is a necessary measure.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">Commodification</a> of natural capital results in undervaluing ecological systems by not accounting for the innumerable wide-range services provided. PES may decrease in utility as 1) wealth becomes concentrated to the point that natural resource scarcity results in higher short-term value for unsustainable resource extraction, and 2) the long-term cost to engineer limited-range replacement services is <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externalized</a> onto citizens. This occurs either through increased expense to the existing systems or as justification to privatize services for further profit. For example, a parent corporation can profit both from the exploitation of an ecosystem, and by engineering and operating the services formerly provided.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations_and_motives_for_incentivizing_production_of_ecosystem_services">Organizations and motives for incentivizing production of ecosystem services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organizations and motives for incentivizing production of ecosystem services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the goal of all PES programs is the procurement of some sort of ecosystem service, the reasons why organizations or governments would incentivize the production of these services are diverse. For example, the world's largest and longest running PES program is the United States' <a href="/wiki/Conservation_Reserve_Program" title="Conservation Reserve Program">Conservation Reserve Program</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-jenk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenk-3">[3]</a></sup> which pays about $1.8 billion a year under 766,000 contracts with farmers and landowners to "rent" a total 34,700,000 acres (140,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of what it considers "environmentally-sensitive land."<sup id="cite_ref-programe_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-programe-20">[20]</a></sup> These farmers agree to plant "long-term, resource-conserving covers to improve water quality, control soil erosion and enhance habitats for waterfowl and wildlife."<sup id="cite_ref-programe_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-programe-20">[20]</a></sup> This program has existed in some form or another since the wake of the American <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a>, when the federal government began paying farmers to avoid farming on poor quality, erodible land.<sup id="cite_ref-jenk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1999, the Chinese central government announced an even more expensive project under its $43 billion <a href="/wiki/Grain_for_Green" title="Grain for Green">Grain for Green</a> program, by which it offers farmers grain in exchange for not clearing forested slopes for farming, thereby reducing erosion and saving the streams and rivers below from the associated deluge of sedimentation.<sup id="cite_ref-ecosystemmarketplace1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecosystemmarketplace1-21">[21]</a></sup> Notably, some sources cite the cost of the entire program at $95 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-jenk_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenk-3">[3]</a></sup> Many less extensive nationally funded PES projects which bear resemblances to the American and Chinese land set-aside programs exist around the world, including programs in Canada, the EU, Japan and Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-ecosystemmarketplace1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecosystemmarketplace1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Rhode_Island" title="Jamestown, Rhode Island">Jamestown, Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, farmers usually harvest the hay in their fields twice a year. However, this practice destroys the habitats of many local grassland birds. Economists from the University of Rhode Island and EcoAssets Markets Inc. raised money from residents of Jamestown who were willing to help the birds. The range of investments was between $5 and $200 per person for a total of $9,800. This money was enough to compensate three Jamestown farms for the cost of reducing their yearly harvests and getting their hay from another source. In this way, the birds have sufficient time to nest and leave the grounds without being subject to a hay harvest. In this example, the farmers benefit because they only have to harvest their fields once a year instead of twice, and the contributors benefit because they value the lives of the birds more than the money they contributed to the project.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City, Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> has managed the majority of its watershed since the 1850s through multi-jurisdictional regulatory mechanisms such as specifying allowable uses (and restricting them), and purchasing land or conservation easements. This long-standing, legally-defensible, yet often-overlooked strategy preserves ecosystem services, while still allowing widely utilized recreation including skiing, snowboarding, hiking, mountain biking, and fishing.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Existing uses of the land are generally unaffected, and commercial enterprises are restricted to no- or low-impact tourism-related activities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_and_South_America">Central and South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Central and South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">Costa Rica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png/220px-Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png/330px-Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png/440px-Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png 2x" data-file-width="982" data-file-height="1226" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Costa_Rica_map_shaded_relief.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Costa Rica</div>

<p>Costa Rica's PES program, <i>Pagos por servicios ambientales</i> (PSA) was established in 1997, and was the first PES program to be implemented on a national scale.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup> It came on the back of Forestry Law 7575 of 1996 which prioritized environmental services over other forest activities such as timber production, and which established the national fund for forest financing (<i>Fondo Nacional de Financiamento Forestal),</i> FONAFIFO.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-24">[24]</a></sup> The PSA follows several years of different environmental programs in Costa Rica including the Forest Credit Certificate (<i>Certificado de Abono Forestal</i>, CAF) of 1986 and the Forest Protection Certificate (<i>Certificado para la Protección del Bosque,</i> CPB) of 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-24">[24]</a></sup> One of the main reasons for establishing the PSA program was to reframe conservation subsidies as payments for services.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-25">[25]</a></sup> It explicitly recognized four environmental services: mitigation of greenhouse gas emissions, hydrological services, biodiversity protection, and provision of scenic beauty.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup> During the early years of the PSA program from 2001 to 2006, it was funded by a World Bank loan and a grant from the Global Environment Facility (GEF) under the project name "Ecomarkets."<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup> From 2007 to 2014, the World Bank renewed its support for the program through a new project called "Mainstreaming Market-Based Instruments for Environmental Management."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This support also generated FONAFIFO's Sustainable Biodiversity Fund (FBS), designed to target PES programs at owners of small pieces of land, indigenous communities, and communities with low development rates.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Financing of PSA activities was initially accomplished in part through a fuel tax established by Forestry Law 7575. The tax was used to flexibly target ecologically important areas. In 2006 a water tariff was introduced to provide additional funding. The water tariff has a relatively narrow application when compared to the fuel tax. Under the water tariff, holders of water concessions pay fees, a portion of which is transferred for use in the PSA exclusively within the watershed in which the revenues were generated. This removes the potential for revenues to be distributed as needed and has been criticized for concentrating funding in select areas, despite their relatively low ecological importance.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>FONAFIFO acts as a semi-autonomous intermediary organization between service buyers an service sellers. As of 2004, FONAFIFO had contracted 11 different companies in agribusiness, hydropower, municipal water supply, and tourism to pay for the water services they receive. Since then, FONAFIFO has reached agreements with several more companies.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 2005, 95% of land enrolled in Costa Rica's PSA was under forest conservation contracts, covering 10% of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-24">[24]</a></sup> It is estimated that from the time the program began to 2005, forest cover area increased form 394,000 hectares to 2.45 million hectares.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It is also estimated that the PSA prevented 11 million tons of carbon emissions between 1999 and 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Despite these successes, the PSA has been criticized for critical shortcomings. As it stands, the PSA payment system employs a flat rate cash payment to all participating landowners. This has resulted in large swaths of ecologically high value areas being left unenrolled in the program due to associated higher opportunity costs for land-use change not being adequately compensated for by the flat rate payment scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Los_Negros.2C_Bolivia">Los Negros, Bolivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Los Negros, Bolivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The program in Los Negros, Bolivia is a small user-financed program of combined payments for watershed and biodiversity services started by local NGO Fundación Natura Bolivia in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup> The target area of the program is the watershed in Los Negros valley servicing the town of Santa Rosa and other downhill towns. By August 2007, 2774 hectares of native vegetation were enrolled in the program under 46 landowners.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Funding for the program was initially provided by the US Fish and Wildlife Service, before the Municipality of Pampagrande began making payments for the services<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup> One of the program's most unique aspects is the landowners' specific request that they be paid in-kind with beehives. They claimed that they wanted their compensation to last beyond a simple cash transfer. Along with the beehives, payment recipients are able to receive training in apiculture. It also allowed for those who prefer cash to sell their hives.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>An organizational obstacle to the program is that some farmers fear that the scheme is just a way to dispossess them of their land. This was a major factor in deciding to be paid in-kind as it is perceived as less of an attempt at land appropriation. Natura is addressing this issue by maintaing a constant presence in the community and leveraging social networks to convince farmers of the program's benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another issue regards the service buyer of the program. The Municipality of Pampagrande has received some limited support from irrigators in contributing to the program payments. This structure essentially provides the environmental services to downstream users essentially free of charge. Natura is working to implement a strategy through which beneficiaries of environmental services will directly contribute to their maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Program evaluation has been impeded by two factors, namely a lack of baseline data and insufficient data as the program develops. These are important in order to establish the additionality of the program. This issue is not unique to Los Negros, however, as many programs suffer from a lack of sufficient monitoring and evaluation mechanisms.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_30-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Honduras">Honduras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Honduras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_de_Otoro" title="Jesús de Otoro">Jesús de Otoro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, the Cumes River is the town's main source of clean water. Coffee producers were dumping their waste into the river upstream, polluting the source and directly affecting the consumers downstream.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> To solve this problem, the local Council for Administration of Water and Sewage Disposal (JAPOE) created a payment program to benefit coffee producers upstream and the town's inhabitants who lived downstream. The villagers downstream paid around $0.06 per household per month to JAPOE, who redirected the money toward the upstream farmers. The farmers complied with guidelines, such as construction of irrigation ditches, proper management of waste, and use of organic fertilizers.</p>
<p>Pico Bonito Forests, near <a href="/wiki/La_Ceiba" title="La Ceiba">La Ceiba</a>, Honduras, is a mission-driven, for-profit venture between the <a href="/wiki/Pico_Bonito" class="mw-redirect" title="Pico Bonito">Pico Bonito</a> National Park Foundation and the <a href="/wiki/EcoLogic_Development_Fund" title="EcoLogic Development Fund">EcoLogic Development Fund</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credits" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon credits">Carbon credits</a> are generated by planting native trees to capture, or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequester</a>, carbon dioxide. The credits are then sold though the World Bank's BioCarbon Fund to countries aiming to meet their <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a> reduction targets. The project offers a unique business model because it is owned jointly by investors and the communities near the park. Community members earn income and share profits from implementing the sustainable forestry practices that capture carbon. By 2017, the project is expected to sequester from .45-.55 Mt of carbon through <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a> and up to an additional .5 Mt of carbon through avoided <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> as destructive practices are replaced with sustainable practices.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Scolel Té program in <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, aims to create a market for positive externalities of <a href="/wiki/Shade-grown_coffee" title="Shade-grown coffee">shade-grown coffee</a> plantations. Designed by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>'s Institute of Ecology and Resource Management along with the Edinburgh Centre for Carbon Management, using the Plan Vivo System, Scolel Té is a PES program under which farmers agree to responsible farming and reforestation practices in exchange for payment for carbon offsets. The NGO Ambio manages Scolel Té. Farmers submit their reforestation plans to Ambio, which judges their financial benefits and the amount of carbon sequestration associated with each plan. The farmers then receive payments from the Fondo BioClimatico, managed by Ambio. Funding for the Fondo BioClimatico comes from the sale of <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Emissions_Reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Voluntary Emissions Reduction">Voluntary Emissions Reduction</a> (VERs) to private groups at a price of $13 per ton of Carbon sequestered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoima_and_Kibaale.2C_Uganda">Hoima and Kibaale, Uganda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Payment_for_ecosystem_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hoima and Kibaale, Uganda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ug-map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ug-map.png/220px-Ug-map.png" width="220" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Ug-map.png 1.5x" data-file-width="330" data-file-height="354" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ug-map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Uganda</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Hoima and Kibaale PES intervention took place from 2010 to 2013 and was especially unique because it was the first PES program set up specifically for a randomized control trial to empirically determine its impact on deforestation<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:12_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-34">[34]</a></sup> In the treatment villages, owners of forested land were paid $28 per year over the course of two years for every hectare of forest land that was left intact, with the possibility of additional payment for planting new trees. The payment scheme amounted to 5% of average annual income for the typical participating landowner.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The program evaluation found there to be significantly less deforestation in participating villages (2%-5%) than in control villages (7%-10%).<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is important to note that the program did not carry on beyond the evaluation period, and it is assumed that previous forest practice will resume once landowners stop receiving program payments.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:12_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
