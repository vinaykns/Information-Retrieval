			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmentally friendly</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Environment_friendly&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment friendly">Environment friendly</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Environmentally friendly</b> or <b>environment-friendly</b>, (also referred to as <b>eco-friendly</b>, <b>nature-friendly</b>, and <b>green</b>) are <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> terms referring to <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">laws</a>, guidelines and policies that claim reduced, minimal, or no harm upon <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-definition_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition-1">[1]</a></sup> Companies use these ambiguous terms to promote goods and services, sometimes with additional, more specific certifications, such as <a href="/wiki/Ecolabel" title="Ecolabel">ecolabels</a>. Their overuse can be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gfs_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gfs-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> has developed ISO 14020 and ISO 14024 to establish principles and procedures for environmental labels and declarations that certifiers and eco-labellers should follow. In particular, these standards relate to the avoidance of financial <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflicts of interest</a>, the use of sound <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific methods</a> and accepted test procedures, and <a href="/wiki/Openness" title="Openness">openness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(behavior)" title="Transparency (behavior)">transparency</a> in the setting of standards.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<p>Products located in members of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> can use the EU's <a href="/wiki/Eco-label" class="mw-redirect" title="Eco-label">Eco-label</a> pending the EU's approval.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eco-Management_and_Audit_Scheme" title="Eco-Management and Audit Scheme">EMAS</a> is another EU label<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> that signifies whether an organization management is green as opposed to the product.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> also uses the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angel_(certification)" title="Blue Angel (certification)">Blue Angel</a>, based on Germany's standard.<sup id="cite_ref-Type_1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Type_1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmentally_friendly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_marketing" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental marketing">environmental marketing</a> claims require caution. Ambiguous titles such as <i>environmentally friendly</i> can be confusing without a specific definition; some regulators are providing guidance.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> has deemed some ecolabels misleading in determining whether a product is truly "green".<sup id="cite_ref-EPAICOU_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPAICOU-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, one label is that of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Choice_Program" title="Environmental Choice Program">Environmental Choice Program</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Type_1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Type_1-9">[9]</a></sup> Created in 1988,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> only products approved by the program are allowed to display the label.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmentally_friendly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Energy Rating Label is a Type III label<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Type_III_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Type_III-15">[15]</a></sup> that provides information on "energy service per unit of energy consumption".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was first created in 1986, but negotiations led to a redesign in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmentally_friendly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The environmentally friendly trends are marketed with a different color association, using the color blue for clean air and clean water, as opposed to green in western cultures. Japanese and Korean built hybrid vehicles use the color blue instead of green all throughout the vehicle, and use the word "blue" indiscriminately.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmentally_friendly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> is a program with a primary goal of increasing <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> and indirectly decreasing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Energy Star has different sections for different nations or areas, including the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> the European Union <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The program, which was founded in the United States, also exists in Canada, Japan, New Zealand, and Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
