<p>A <b>revolution</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Latin</a> <i>revolutio</i>, "a turn around") is a fundamental <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">change</a> in political power or organizational structures that takes place in a relatively short period of time when the population rises up in revolt against the current authorities. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> described two types of political revolution:</p>
<ol>
<li>Complete change from one constitution to another</li>
<li>Modification of an existing constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Revolutions have occurred through <a href="/wiki/Human_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Human history">human history</a> and vary widely in terms of methods, duration, and motivating <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a>. Their results include major changes in <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Social institution">socio</a>-<a href="/wiki/Political_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Political institution">political institutions</a>.</p>
<p>Scholarly debates about what does and does not constitute a revolution center on several issues. Early studies of revolutions primarily analyzed events in <a href="/wiki/European_history" class="mw-redirect" title="European history">European history</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Psychological" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychological">psychological</a> perspective, but more modern examinations include global events and incorporate perspectives from several <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>. Several generations of scholarly thought on revolutions have generated many competing theories and contributed much to the current understanding of this complex phenomenon.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The word "revolucion" is known in French from the 13th century, and "revolution" in English by the late fourteenth century, with regards to the revolving motion of celestial bodies. "Revolution" in the sense of representing abrupt change in a <a href="/wiki/Social_order" title="Social order">social order</a> is attested by at least 1450.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Political usage of the term had been well established by 1688 in the description of the replacement of <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II</a> with <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_Orange" class="mw-redirect" title="William III of Orange">William III</a>. The process was termed <i>"<a href="/wiki/The_Glorious_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="The Glorious Revolution">The Glorious Revolution</a>"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>

<p>There are many different typologies of revolutions in social science and literature. For example, classical scholar <a href="/wiki/Alexis_de_Tocqueville" title="Alexis de Tocqueville">Alexis de Tocqueville</a> differentiated<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> between <a href="/wiki/Political_revolution" title="Political revolution">political revolutions</a>, sudden and violent revolutions that seek not only to establish a new political system but to transform an entire society, and slow but sweeping transformations of the entire society that take several generations to bring about (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>). One of several different <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> typologies divides revolutions into pre-capitalist, early bourgeois, bourgeois, bourgeois-democratic, early proletarian, and socialist revolutions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Tilly" title="Charles Tilly">Charles Tilly</a>, a modern scholar of revolutions, differentiated<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> between a <a href="/wiki/Coup" class="mw-redirect" title="Coup">coup</a>, a top-down seizure of power, a <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil war</a>, a revolt and a "great revolution" (revolutions that transform economic and social structures as well as political institutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> of 1789, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution of 1917</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Revolution_of_Iran" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic Revolution of Iran">Islamic Revolution of Iran</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other types of revolution, created for other typologies, include the <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolutions</a>; <a href="/wiki/Proletarian_revolution" title="Proletarian revolution">proletarian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Communist_revolution" title="Communist revolution">communist revolutions</a> (inspired by the ideas of Marxism that aims to replace <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a>); failed or abortive revolutions (revolutions that fail to secure power after temporary victories or large-scale mobilization); or violent vs. <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_revolution" title="Nonviolent revolution">nonviolent revolutions</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>revolution</i> has also been used to denote great changes outside the political sphere. Such revolutions are usually recognized as having transformed in <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> much more than <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political systems</a>; they are often known as <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolutions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some can be global, while others are limited to single countries. One of the classic examples of the usage of the word <i>revolution</i> in such context is the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Revolution" title="Commercial Revolution">Commercial Revolution</a>. Note that such revolutions also fit the "slow revolution" definition of Tocqueville.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A similar example is the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Revolution" title="Digital Revolution">Digital Revolution</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_socioeconomic_revolutions">Political and socioeconomic revolutions</span></h2>
<p>Perhaps most often, the word "revolution" is employed to denote a change in <a href="/wiki/Social_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Social institution">socio</a>-<a href="/wiki/Political_institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Political institution">political institutions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Forantorr_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forantorr-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kroeber_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kroeber-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goodwin" title="Jeff Goodwin">Jeff Goodwin</a> gives two definitions of a revolution. A broad one, where revolution is</p>
<blockquote>
<p>any and all instances in which a state or a political <a href="/wiki/Regime" title="Regime">regime</a> is overthrown and thereby transformed by a popular <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">movement</a> in an irregular, extraconstitutional and/or violent fashion</p>
</blockquote>
<p>and a narrow one, in which</p>
<blockquote>
<p>revolutions entail not only <a href="/wiki/Mass_mobilization" title="Mass mobilization">mass mobilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regime_change" title="Regime change">regime change</a>, but also more or less rapid and fundamental social, economic and/or cultural change, during or soon after the struggle for <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Power (sociology)">power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NOWO:9_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOWO:9-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Goldstone" title="Jack Goldstone">Jack Goldstone</a> defines them as</p>
<blockquote>
<p>an effort to transform the political institutions and the justifications for political authority in society, accompanied by formal or informal mass mobilization and noninstitutionalized actions that undermine authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Political and socioeconomic revolutions have been studied in many <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political sciences</a> and <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>. Among the leading scholars in that area have been or are <a href="/wiki/Crane_Brinton" title="Crane Brinton">Crane Brinton</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Brockett&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles Brockett (page does not exist)">Charles Brockett</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Farideh_Farhi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Farideh Farhi (page does not exist)">Farideh Farhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Foran_(sociologist)" title="John Foran (sociologist)">John Foran</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mason_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Mason Hart (page does not exist)">John Mason Hart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel Huntington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Goldstone" title="Jack Goldstone">Jack Goldstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goodwin" title="Jeff Goodwin">Jeff Goodwin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ted_Roberts_Gurr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ted Roberts Gurr (page does not exist)">Ted Roberts Gurr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Halliday" title="Fred Halliday">Fred Halliday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalmers_Johnson" title="Chalmers Johnson">Chalmers Johnson</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim_McDaniel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tim McDaniel (page does not exist)">Tim McDaniel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barrington_Moore" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrington Moore">Barrington Moore</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeffery_Paige&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jeffery Paige (page does not exist)">Jeffery Paige</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vilfredo_Pareto" title="Vilfredo Pareto">Vilfredo Pareto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terence_Ranger" title="Terence Ranger">Terence Ranger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Rosenstock-Huessy" title="Eugen Rosenstock-Huessy">Eugen Rosenstock-Huessy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theda_Skocpol" title="Theda Skocpol">Theda Skocpol</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_C._Scott" title="James C. Scott">James Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Selbin" title="Eric Selbin">Eric Selbin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tilly" title="Charles Tilly">Charles Tilly</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Kay_Trimberger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ellen Kay Trimberger (page does not exist)">Ellen Kay Trimberger</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carlos_Vistas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carlos Vistas (page does not exist)">Carlos Vistas</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Walton_(sociologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Walton (sociologist) (page does not exist)">John Walton</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_Wickham-Crowley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Timothy Wickham-Crowley (page does not exist)">Timothy Wickham-Crowley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Wolf" title="Eric Wolf">Eric Wolf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NOWO:5_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOWO:5-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scholars of revolutions, like <a href="/wiki/Jack_Goldstone" title="Jack Goldstone">Jack Goldstone</a>, differentiate four current 'generations' of scholarly research dealing with revolutions.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup> The scholars of the first generation such as <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Le_Bon" title="Gustave Le Bon">Gustave Le Bon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Ellwood" title="Charles A. Ellwood">Charles A. Ellwood</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pitirim_Sorokin" title="Pitirim Sorokin">Pitirim Sorokin</a>, were mainly descriptive in their approach, and their explanations of the phenomena of revolutions was usually related to <a href="/wiki/Social_psychology" title="Social psychology">social psychology</a>, such as Le Bon's <a href="/wiki/Crowd_psychology" title="Crowd psychology">crowd psychology</a> theory.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Second generation theorists sought to develop detailed <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theories</a> of why and when revolutions arise, grounded in more complex <a href="/wiki/Social_behavior" title="Social behavior">social behavior</a> theories. They can be divided into three major approaches: psychological, sociological and political.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The works of <a href="/wiki/Ted_Robert_Gurr" title="Ted Robert Gurr">Ted Robert Gurr</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ivo_K._Feierbrand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ivo K. Feierbrand (page does not exist)">Ivo K. Feierbrand</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosalind_L._Feierbrand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rosalind L. Feierbrand (page does not exist)">Rosalind L. Feierbrand</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_A._Geschwender&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James A. Geschwender (page does not exist)">James A. Geschwender</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_C._Schwartz" title="David C. Schwartz">David C. Schwartz</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Denton_E._Morrison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Denton E. Morrison (page does not exist)">Denton E. Morrison</a> fall into the first category. They followed theories of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frustration-aggression_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Frustration-aggression theory">frustration-aggression theory</a> and saw the cause of revolution in the state of mind of the masses, and while they varied in their approach as to what exactly caused the people to revolt (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Modernization" class="mw-redirect" title="Modernization">modernization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>), they agreed that the primary cause for revolution was the widespread frustration with socio-political situation.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second group, composed of academics such as <a href="/wiki/Chalmers_Johnson" title="Chalmers Johnson">Chalmers Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Smelser" title="Neil Smelser">Neil Smelser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Jessop" title="Bob Jessop">Bob Jessop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hart" title="Mark Hart">Mark Hart</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_A._Tiryakian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edward A. Tiryakian (page does not exist)">Edward A. Tiryakian</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Hagopian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark Hagopian (page does not exist)">Mark Hagopian</a>, followed in the footsteps of <a href="/wiki/Talcott_Parsons" title="Talcott Parsons">Talcott Parsons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Structural-functionalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural-functionalist">structural-functionalist</a> theory in sociology; they saw society as a system in equilibrium between various resources, demands and subsystems (political, cultural, etc.). As in the psychological school, they differed in their definitions of what causes disequilibrium, but agreed that it is a state of a severe disequilibrium that is responsible for revolutions.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, the third group, which included writers such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Tilly" title="Charles Tilly">Charles Tilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_P._Huntington" title="Samuel P. Huntington">Samuel P. Huntington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ammann" title="Peter Ammann">Peter Ammann</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_L._Stinchcombe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arthur L. Stinchcombe (page does not exist)">Arthur L. Stinchcombe</a> followed the path of <a href="/wiki/Political_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Political sciences">political sciences</a> and looked at <a href="/wiki/Pluralist_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Pluralist theory">pluralist theory</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interest_group_conflict_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Interest group conflict theory (page does not exist)">interest group conflict theory</a>. Those theories see events as outcomes of a <a href="/wiki/Power_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Power struggle">power struggle</a> between competing <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">interest groups</a>. In such a model, revolutions happen when two or more groups cannot come to terms within a normal <a href="/wiki/Decision_making" class="mw-redirect" title="Decision making">decision making</a> process traditional for a given <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political system</a>, and simultaneously have enough resources to employ <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> in pursuing their goals.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet3_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet3-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second generation theorists saw the development of the revolutions as a two-step process; first, some <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">change</a> results in the present situation being different from the past; second, the new situation creates an opportunity for a revolution to occur. In that situation, an event that in the past would not be sufficient to cause a revolution (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Riot" title="Riot">riot</a>, a bad <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a>), now is sufficient; however, if authorities are aware of the danger, they can still prevent a revolution through <a href="/wiki/Reform" title="Reform">reform</a> or <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">repression</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many such early studies of revolutions tended to concentrate on four classic cases—famous and uncontroversial examples that fit virtually all definitions of revolutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> (1688), the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> (1789–1799), the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution of 1917</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War" title="Chinese Civil War">Chinese Revolution (also known as the Chinese Civil War)</a> (1927–1949).<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup> In his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Anatomy_of_Revolution" title="The Anatomy of Revolution">The Anatomy of Revolution</a></i>, however, the Harvard historian <a href="/wiki/Crane_Brinton" title="Crane Brinton">Crane Brinton</a> focused on the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the French Revolution, and the Russian Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In time, scholars began to analyze hundreds of other events as revolutions (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_revolutions_and_rebellions" title="List of revolutions and rebellions">list of revolutions and rebellions</a>), and differences in definitions and approaches gave rise to new definitions and explanations. The theories of the second generation have been criticized for their limited geographical scope, difficulty in <a href="/wiki/Empirical" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical">empirical</a> verification, as well as that while they may explain some particular revolutions, they did not explain why revolutions did not occur in other societies in very similar situations.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The criticism of the second generation led to the rise of a third generation of theories, with writers such as <a href="/wiki/Theda_Skocpol" title="Theda Skocpol">Theda Skocpol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barrington_Moore" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrington Moore">Barrington Moore</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeffrey_Paige&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jeffrey Paige (page does not exist)">Jeffrey Paige</a> and others expanding on the old <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> <a href="/wiki/Class_conflict" title="Class conflict">class conflict</a> approach, turning their attention to rural agrarian-state conflicts, state conflicts with autonomous <a href="/wiki/Elites" class="mw-redirect" title="Elites">elites</a>, and the impact of interstate <a href="/wiki/Economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> competition on domestic <a href="/wiki/Political_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Political change">political change</a>. Particularly Skocpol's <i><a href="/wiki/States_and_Social_Revolutions" title="States and Social Revolutions">States and Social Revolutions</a></i> became one of the most widely recognized works of the third generation; Skocpol defined revolution as "rapid, basic transformations of society's state and class structures [...] accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below", attributing revolutions to a conjunction of multiple conflicts involving state, elites and the lower classes.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>From the late 1980s a new body of scholarly work began questioning the dominance of the third generation's theories. The old theories were also dealt a significant blow by new revolutionary events that could not be easily explain by them. The <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_Revolution" title="Nicaraguan Revolution">Nicaraguan Revolutions</a> of 1979, the 1986 <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">People Power Revolution</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and the 1989 <a href="/wiki/Autumn_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Autumn of Nations">Autumn of Nations</a> in Europe saw multi-class coalitions topple seemingly powerful regimes amidst popular demonstrations and <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">mass strikes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nonviolent_revolution" title="Nonviolent revolution">nonviolent revolutions</a>.</p>
<p>Defining revolutions as mostly European violent state versus people and <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Class struggle">class struggles</a> conflicts was no longer sufficient. The study of revolutions thus evolved in three directions, firstly, some researchers were applying previous or updated <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">structuralist</a> theories of revolutions to events beyond the previously analyzed, mostly European conflicts. Secondly, scholars called for greater attention to conscious <a href="/wiki/Agency_(philosophy)" title="Agency (philosophy)">agency</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> in shaping revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Mobilization" title="Mobilization">mobilization</a> and objectives. Third, analysts of both revolutions and <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movements</a> realized that those phenomena have much in common, and a new 'fourth generation' literature on <a href="/wiki/Contentious_politics" title="Contentious politics">contentious politics</a> has developed that attempts to combine insights from the study of social movements and revolutions in hopes of understanding both phenomena.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further, social science research on revolution, primarily work in political science, has begun to move beyond individual or comparative case studies towards large-N empirical studies assessing the causes and implications of revolution. Initial studies generally rely on the Polity Project’s data on <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">democratization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Such analyses, like those by Enterline,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zeev_Maoz" title="Zeev Maoz">Maoz</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and Mansfield and Snyder,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> identify revolutions based on regime changes indicated by a change in the country’s score on Polity’s autocracy to democracy scale. More recently, scholars like Jeff Colgan have argued that Polity, which measures the degree of democratic or autocratic authority in a state’s governing institutions based on the openness of executive recruitment, constraints on executive authority, and political competition, is inadequate because it measures democratization, not revolution, and fails to account for regimes which come to power by revolution but fail to change the structure of the state and society sufficiently to yield a notable difference in Polity score.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Instead, Colgan offers a new data set<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> on revolutionary leaders which identifies governments that “transform the existing social, political, and economic relationships of the state by overthrowing or rejecting the principal existing institutions of society.” This most recent data set has been employed to make empirically-based contributions to the literature on revolution by identifying links between revolution and the likelihood of international disputes.</p>
<p>Revolutions have also been approached from anthropological perspectives. Drawing on Victor Turner’s writings on ritual and performance, <a href="/wiki/Bjorn_Thomassen" title="Bjorn Thomassen">Bjorn Thomassen</a><sup id="cite_ref-Thomassen_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomassen-24">[24]</a></sup> has argued that revolutions can be understood as “liminal” moments: Modern political revolutions very much resemble rituals and can therefore be studied within a process approach. This would imply not only a focus on political behaviour “from below”, but also to recognize moments where “high and low” are relativized, made irrelevant or subverted, and where the micro and macro levels fuse together in critical conjunctions.</p>
<p>Economist <a href="/wiki/Douglass_North" title="Douglass North">Douglass North</a> argued that it is much easier for revolutionaries to alter formal political <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> such as laws and constitutions than to alter informal social conventions. According to North, inconsistencies between rapidly changing formal institutions and slow-changing informal ones can inhibit effective sociopolitical change. Because of this, the long-term effect of revolutionary political restructuring is often more moderate than the ostensible short-term effect.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>While revolutions encompass events ranging from <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the relatively peaceful revolutions that overthrew communist regimes</a> to the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(1978%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (1978–present)">violent Islamic revolution in Afghanistan</a>, they exclude <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coups d'états</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rebellion" title="Rebellion">revolts and rebellions</a> that make no effort to transform institutions or the justification for authority (such as <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zef_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Józef Piłsudski">Józef Piłsudski</a>'s <a href="/wiki/May_Coup_(Poland)" title="May Coup (Poland)">May Coup</a> of 1926 or the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>), as well as peaceful transitions to <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> through institutional arrangements such as <a href="/wiki/Plebiscite" class="mw-redirect" title="Plebiscite">plebiscites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Election#Difficulties_with_elections" title="Election">free elections</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> after the death of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstonet4_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstonet4-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
