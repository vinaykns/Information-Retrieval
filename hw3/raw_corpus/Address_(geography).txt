			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Address (geography)</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>An <b>address</b> is a collection of information, presented in a mostly fixed format, used to give the location of a building, apartment, or other structure or a plot of land, generally using <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">political boundaries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Street_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Street name">street names</a> as references, along with other identifiers such as <a href="/wiki/House_number" class="mw-redirect" title="House number">house</a> or <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a> numbers. Some addresses also contain special codes, such as a <a href="/wiki/Postal_code" title="Postal code">postal code</a>, to make identification easier and aid in the routing of <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">mail</a>.</p>

<p>Addresses have several functions:</p>
<ul>
<li>providing a means of physically locating a building, especially in a city where there are many buildings and streets;</li>
<li>identifying buildings as the end points of a <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">postal system</a>;</li>
<li>signalling social status;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>as parameters in statistics collection, especially in <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>-taking and the <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> industry;</li>
<li>addresses are the most accepted representation of a location.</li>
</ul>
<p>Addresses may have drawbacks:</p>
<ul>
<li>They are a non-standardised means of representing a location;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>They do not contain the coordinates of the location they represent.</li>
</ul>

<p>Until the advent of modern postal systems around the 18th-19th century, most houses and buildings were not numbered. <a href="/wiki/Street" title="Street">Streets</a> may have been named for landmarks, such as a city gate or market, or for the professions of their inhabitants. In many cities in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, most minor streets were never named. This is still the case today in much of Japan. A local postman would be familiar with the area and thus able to deliver mail, but as postal systems developed, it became necessary to number buildings to aid in mail delivery.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_numbering_or_naming">House numbering or naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: House numbering or naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">House numbering</a></div>
<p>In most English-speaking countries, the usual method of house numbering is an alternating numbering scheme progressing in one direction along a street, with odd numbers on one side (often west or south or the left-hand side leading away from a main road) and even numbers on the other side, although there is significant variation on this basic pattern. Many older towns and cities in the UK have "up and down" numbering where the numbers progress sequentially along one side of the road, and then sequentially back down the other side. Cities in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, particularly those planned on a <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">grid plan</a>, often incorporate <a href="/wiki/City_block" title="City block">block</a> numbers, quadrants (explained below), and <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal directions">cardinal directions</a> into their street numbers, so that in many such cities, addresses roughly follow a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian coordinate system</a>. Some other cities around the world have their own schemes.</p>
<p>Although house numbering is the principal identification scheme in many parts of the world, it is also common for houses in the United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> to be identified by name, rather than number, especially in villages. In these cases, the street name will usually follow the house name. Such an address might read: "Smith Cottage, Frog Lane, Barchester, Barsetshire, BZ9 9BA" or "Dunroamin, Emo, Co. Laois, Ireland" (fictional examples).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadrants"><span id="cartesian"></span>Quadrants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quadrants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cities with Cartesian-coordinate-based addressing systems, the streets that form the north-south and east-west dividing lines constitute the <i>x</i> and <i>y</i> axes of a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Cartesian coordinate plane">Cartesian coordinate plane</a> and thus divide the city into <b>quadrants.</b> The quadrants are typically identified in the street names, although the manner of doing so varies from city to city. For example, in one city, all streets in the northeast quadrant may have "NE" prefixed or suffixed to their street names, while in another, the intersection of <i>North</i> Calvert Street and <i>East</i> 27th Street can be only in the northeast quadrant.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Street-naming_conventions">Street-naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Street-naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Street_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Street name">Street names</a> may follow a variety of themes. In many <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> cities, such as, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> USA, and <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton, Alberta</a>, Canada, streets are simply <a href="/wiki/Numbered_street" title="Numbered street">numbered sequentially</a> across the street grid. <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> has its <a href="/wiki/Numbered_street" title="Numbered street">numbered streets</a> running north-south and lettered or alphabetically named streets running east-west, while diagonal avenues are typically named after states. In <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, and many other <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> cities, streets are in a large grid and are numbered in increments of 100 based on their location relative to the center of the city in blocks. A similar system is in use in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Mile_Road_System_(Detroit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mile Road System (Detroit)">Mile Road System</a>. In some housing developments in North America and elsewhere, street names may all follow the same theme (for example, bird species), or start with the same letter. Streets in <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> are often named after famous people or significant dates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postal_codes">Postal codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Postal codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Postal_code" title="Postal code">Postal codes</a> are a relatively recent development in addressing, designed to speed the sorting and processing of mail by assigning unique numeric or alphanumeric codes to each geographical locality.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postal_alternatives_to_physical_addresses">Postal alternatives to physical addresses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Postal alternatives to physical addresses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For privacy and other purposes, postal services have made it possible to receive mail without revealing one's physical address or even having a fixed physical address. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Post_office_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Post office box">post office boxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Service_address" title="Service address">service addresses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poste_restante" title="Poste restante">poste restante</a> (general delivery).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Address_format">Address format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Address format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In English-speaking countries, the <a href="/wiki/Postal_code" title="Postal code">postal code</a> usually comes last. In much of Europe, the code precedes the town name, thus: "1010 <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>". Often, the country code is still placed in front of the postal code: "<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">CH</a>-1010 <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>". However, this is no longer demanded by postal authorities and has even been forbidden in certain countries.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>If a house number is provided, it is written on the same line as the street name; a house name is written on the previous line. When addresses are written inline, line breaks are replaced by commas. Conventions on the placing of house numbers differ: either before or after the street name. Similarly, there are differences in the placement of postal codes: in the UK, they are written on a separate line at the end of the address; in Australia, Canada and the United States, they usually appear immediately after the state or province, on the same line; in Austria, Belgium, France, Germany and The Netherlands they appear before the city, on the same line.</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Asian_addressing_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="East Asian addressing system (page does not exist)">East Asian addressing systems</a>, including Chinese, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_addressing_system" title="Japanese addressing system">Japanese</a> and Korean addressing systems, when written in their native scripts, use the opposite ordering, starting with the province/prefecture, ending with the addressee. However both have the same order as western countries when written in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>. The Hungarian system also goes from large to small units, except the name of the addressee is put into the first line.</p>
<p>The Universal Postal Convention strongly recommends the following:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"The addressee’s address shall be worded in a precise and complete manner. It shall be written very legibly in roman letters and arabic numerals. If other letters and numerals are used in the country of destination, it shall be recommended that the address be given also in these letters and numerals. The name of the place of destination and the name of the country of destination shall be written in capital letters together with the correct postcode number or delivery zone number or post office box number, if any. The name of the country of destination shall be written preferably in the language of the country of origin. To avoid any difficulty in the countries of transit, it is desirable for the name of the country of destination to be added in an internationally known language. Designated operators may recommend that, on items addressed to countries where the recommended position of the postcode is in front of the name of the location of destination, the postcode should be preceded by the <a href="/wiki/ISO_3166-1_alpha-2" title="ISO 3166-1 alpha-2">EN ISO 3166–1 Alpha 2</a> country code followed by a hyphen. This shall in no way detract from the requirement for the name of the destination country to be printed in full."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In common with the rest of the English-speaking world, addresses in Australia put the street number—which may be a range—before the street name, and the placename before the postcode. Unlike addresses in most other comparable places, the city is not included in the address, but rather a much more fine-grained locality is used, usually referred to in Australia as a <a href="/wiki/Suburbs_and_localities_(Australia)" title="Suburbs and localities (Australia)">suburb or town</a> – although these words are understood in a different way than in other countries. Because the suburb or town serves to locate the street or delivery type, the <a href="/wiki/Postcodes_in_Australia" title="Postcodes in Australia">postcode</a> serves only as routing information rather than to distinguish previous other parts of an address. As an example, there are around 8000 localities in Victoria (cf. <a href="/wiki/List_of_localities_in_Victoria_(Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="List of localities in Victoria (Australia)">List of localities in Victoria (Australia)</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Melbourne_suburbs" title="List of Melbourne suburbs">List of Melbourne suburbs</a>), yet around 700 unique geographic postcodes.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For certain large volume receivers or post offices, the "locality" may be an institution or street name. It is always considered incorrect to include the city or metropolis name in an address (unless this happens to be the name of the suburb), and doing so may delay delivery.</p>
<p>Australia Post recommends<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that the last line of the address should be set in capital letters. In Australia, subunits are essential and should be separated from the street by two spaces; apartments, flats and units are typically separated with a forward slash instead. As in the US, the state/territory is crucial information as many placenames are reused in different states/territories; it is usually separated from the suburb with two spaces and abbreviated. In printed matter, the postcode follows after two spaces; in handwritten matter, the postcode should be written in the boxes provided.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarus">Belarus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Belarus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some neighbourhoods may be planned in such a way that some, or most, apartment buildings don't face a named street. In this case, a number of expedients can be used. In older neighbourhoods, a "main" building may have the same number as one or more "subsidiary" buildings accessible via driveways behind the main building. They will be addressed as <i>vul. Lenina, d. 123</i> (<i>123 Lenin St</i>) An address may also cover one or more <i>subsidiary buildings</i> behind the main building, addressed as <i>vul. Lenina, d. 123, bud. 2</i> (<i>123 Lenin St, unit 2</i>, where <i>bud.</i> (abbreviation for будынак, <i>budynak</i>) means a '(subsidiary) building'). In newer areas with more regular street plans, apartment buildings that don't face a named street may be designated with Cyrillic letters appended to the building number, e.g. <i>123-а</i>, <i>123-б</i>, etc., in <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic alphabetical</a> order.</p>
<p>In some <i><a href="/wiki/Microraion" class="mw-redirect" title="Microraion">microraion</a></i> neighbourhoods, with few, if any, buildings facing named streets, the name (or more likely number of the <i>microraion</i> (planned housing development)) would be used instead of the street name; thus someone may live at <i>4-th microrayon, d. 123, kv. 56</i>, i.e. <i>123 - 4th Microraion, apt. 56</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The address starts with the most specific information (addressee individual identification) and ends with the most general information (postcode and town for domestic mail or country for cross border mail.) Spatial information of a physical address (including building, wing, stairwell, floor and door) may be useful for internal path of delivery, but is not allowed in the delivery point location line (i.e. the line containing street, number and box number). If needed, this information will appear on a line above the delivery point location line.</p>
<p>The street number is placed after the thoroughfare name (unlike in France), separated by a space. Separators such as punctuation (point, comma or other signs) or ‘n°’, or ‘nr’ are not allowed. Extension designation (box numbers), if present, appears in the delivery point location line, preceded by the word for "box" (<i>bus</i> in Dutch, <i>bte</i> in French). Symbols such as <i>b</i>, <i>Bt</i>, <i>#</i>, <i>-</i>, <i>/</i> are not allowed as separators between the street number element and the box number element.</p>

<p>The Belgian addressing guidelines are registered with the Universal Postal Union (<a href="/wiki/UPU" class="mw-redirect" title="UPU">UPU</a> and see the link <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.upu.int/en/activities/addressing/postal-addressing-systems-in-member-countries.html">http://www.upu.int/en/activities/addressing/postal-addressing-systems-in-member-countries.html</a>). These guidelines indicate exactly how to combine the various address components in order to obtain a correctly formatted postal address.</p>
<p>The complete set of addressing guidelines can be found on the website of the Belgian postal operator (bpost). The correct representation of an address is not limited to the correct structure of address components but also relates to the content of addresses and their position on envelopes (see <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.bpost.be/site/fr/residential/letters-cards/send/best_practices.html">http://www.bpost.be/site/fr/residential/letters-cards/send/best_practices.html</a> (in French)).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Addressing guidelines can differ between English- and French-speaking populations in Canada. Here are some formatting rules that are used in common:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cardinal directions like <i>North</i>, <i>North West</i>, etc. can be abbreviated in either English or French, and appear after the street name. Ordinal numbered streets (e.g. 6th, 2nd) can be written in either English or French.</li>
<li>Postal codes come in a letter-number-letter-space-number-letter-number format, for example: A1A 1A1. There should be two spaces between the province abbreviation and the postal code.</li>
<li>If sending a parcel from outside Canada, the word "CANADA" must be placed at the very bottom.</li>
<li>If there is an apartment number it should be written before the house number and separated by a hyphen. The postal code is then separated by two spaces and should be written on the same line as the city and province.</li>
</ul>
<p>See the example below for a comparison of the English and French address formats.</p>
<p>English (from <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a>):</p>
<pre>
NICOLE MARTIN
123 SHERBROOKE ST

</pre>
<p>French (from <a href="/wiki/OQLF" class="mw-redirect" title="OQLF">OQLF</a>):</p>
<pre>
Monsieur Jean-Pierre Lamarre
101-3485, rue de la Montagne
Montréal (Québec)  H3G 2A6
</pre>

<li>See <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.canadapost.ca/tools/pg/manual/PGaddress-e.asp">Addressing Guidelines</a> for accurate, up-to-date information on the addressing guidelines most commonly used in Canada.</li>
<li>See the <a href="/wiki/Office_qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_de_la_langue_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Office québécois de la langue française">Office québécois de la langue française</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bdl.oqlf.gouv.qc.ca/bdl/gabarit_bdl.asp?id=3415"><i>Adressage</i></a> webpage (in French only) for more information about how to write an address according to guidelines used in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and other French-speaking areas.</li>
</ul>

<p>Postal codes are in the format "###&#160;##" (i.e. 158 00 = Prague 58) or "CZ-#####" (especially for international sendings). At pre-printed Czech postcards and envelopes, the postal code is filled into frames at separate last row. If the envelope doesn't have pre-printed rows and frames, the postal code should be before the town (or post office) name.</p>
<p>In private mails, the first line is usually constituted by a courtesy title (pan, paní, slečna, žák…) For private mails addressed to the workplace, the order is inverse (name + company) as in official mails (company + name).</p>
<p>The basic system of <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">house numbering</a> are conscription house numbers (čísla popisná, čp. or č. p.). In case of temporary or recreational house, an evidentional house number (číslo evidenční, ev. č. or če., or distinguished by initial 0 or E prefix) is used instead. In most of bigger cities and also in some towns and big villages with street names, there is double system of house numbering. As the first number is used the conscription or evidentional number (which corresponds to the chronologic order of cadastral registration of the house), as the second number (past a slash) is used the orientational number (orientační číslo, č. or., č. o.) which expresses position on the street. Sometimes, only one of the two numbers is used or the numbers are used in inversed order and it can be difficult to distinguish which number is which. Generally, orientation numbers (if exists) are preferred for mail services.</p>

<p>The postal code always consists of five digits.</p>
<p>Organisations, government agencies, and companies which receive large amounts of mail often have a special "<a href="/wiki/Postal_codes_in_France#CEDEX" title="Postal codes in France">CEDEX</a>" address which goes after the last line (for instance, "75001 PARIS CEDEX").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Germany, the address is generally formatted as follows:</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Format</th>
<th>Example</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><i>Addressee (Natural person/Organization)</i><br />
<i>More detailed description of addressee (optional)</i><br />
<i>Streetname + number</i><br />
<i><a href="/wiki/Postal_codes_in_Germany" title="Postal codes in Germany">Postal code</a> + town</i><br />
<i>Country (if other than Germany)</i></td>
<td>Firma ABC<br />
Kundendienst<br />
Hauptstr. 5<br />
01234&#160;Musterstadt<br />
<br /></td>
</tr>
</table>
<ul>
<li>The postal code always consists of five digits.</li>
<li>Organizations that receive large amounts of mail may be assigned a bulk customer postal code. These are different from regular postal codes in that they do not have a street name line. Some bulk customer postal codes are shared between several organizations.</li>
<li>There are a few places that have house numbers but no street names (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Baltrum" title="Baltrum">Baltrum</a>) as well as addresses that have a street name but no house number.</li>
<li>Some (but not all) private post companies are also able to deliver to <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post" title="Deutsche Post">Deutsche Post</a>-operated P.O. boxes.</li>
<li>Post codes follow the structure of DPAG's mail routing, not administrative boundaries.</li>
<li>Each post code is used exclusively for street addresses, P.O. boxes or bulk recipients.</li>
<li>Sub-building information, such as apartment numbers, is rarely used - a name on the post box is usually the only method of identification of an addressee within a building.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official languages</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> are English and <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantonese Chinese">Cantonese Chinese</a>. For domestic mail within Hong Kong, the address may be written entirely in either English or Chinese. For overseas mail going out from Hong Kong, the address may be written in the language of the destination country, provided that the city name and the country name are in English.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, for an overseas mail from Hong Kong to China, <a href="/wiki/Macao" class="mw-redirect" title="Macao">Macao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, the address may be written entirely in Chinese. While <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional Chinese character">traditional Chinese characters</a> are commonly used in Hong Kong, <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Simplified Chinese character">simplified Chinese characters</a> are also understood by Hong Kong's postmen. Note that Hong Kong does not use any postal codes, though many rural properties have a property identification code, e.g. HKT-12345.</p>
<p>An address written in English should begin with the smallest unit and end with the largest unit, as in the following example for a domestic mail within Hong Kong.</p>

<p>A Japanese postal address, when written in Japanese phonetic and Chinese characters, starts with the largest geographical division, continues with progressively smallers subdivisions before ending with the addressee, i.e. country, prefecture, town, chōme, banchi, building number, building name, floor number, company name, addressee. This is the most common addressing format used when mailing within <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is common practice to add the appropriate honorific to the addressee's name, e.g. 様 for a private individual or 御中 for a company or institution.</p>
<p>When written in the Latin alphabet, the address begins with the smallest geographical area and ends with the largest one as in the following example. Macrons (as on ō and ū) may be omitted.</p>
<p>Japanese-style envelopes are vertically aligned and the address is written from top to bottom, then right to left. Western-style envelopes are horizontally aligned and the address is written from left to right, top to bottom.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macau">Macau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Macau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official languages</a> of <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> are <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a>. For domestic mail within Macau, the address may be written entirely in either Portuguese or Chinese. For overseas mail going out from Macau, the address may be written in the language of the destination country, provided that the city name and the country name are in English. However, for an overseas mail from Macau to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, the address may be written entirely in Chinese. While <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese characters</a> are commonly used in Macau, <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese characters</a> are also understood by Macau's postmen. Note that Macau does not use any postal codes.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Netherlands, the address is generally formatted as follows:</p>
<table class="wikitable">

<th>Example</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><i>Name<br />
(Businesspark name etc.)</i><br />

<p>The postal code is a unique street identifier, and always consists of four numbers followed by a space and then two capital letters. <a href="/wiki/PostNL" title="PostNL">PostNL</a>, which is appointed by the Dutch government to carry out the UPD (Dutch for <i>Universal Postal Service</i>), recommends putting two spaces between postal code and town. Also, the name of the town should be written in capitals.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because the Dutch postal code uniquely identifies a street, a shortened format may also be used. This method only needs the postal code and the number. The ideal format for this method is the number after the postal code, meaning that this: '5631 AV 1092' will still get the letter delivered to the correct location.</p>
<p>It is also possible to replace the street name line with a PO box (e.g. "postbus 1200") or <a href="/wiki/Freepost" title="Freepost">freepost</a> number (e.g. "antwoordnummer 150"), which have their own postal code.</p>

<b>Lower Hutt</b> 5011</td>

<p>Some neighbourhoods may be planned in such a way that some, or most, apartment buildings face no named street. In this case, a number of expedients can be used. In older neighbourhoods, such as the historical center of Moscow, a "main" building may have the same number as one or more "subsidiary" buildings accessible via driveways behind the main building. They will be addressed as, for example, ul. Lenina, d. 123 (that is, 123 Lenin St). An address may also cover one or more subsidiary buildings behind the main building, addressed as ul. Lenina, d. 123, str. 2 (123 Lenin St, Unit 2, where str. (abbreviation for строение, stroenie) means a '(subsidiary) building'. (November 2010)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> areas with more regular street plans, apartment buildings that face no named street may be designated with Cyrillic letters appended to the building number, such as 123-а, 123-б, etc., in alphabetic order.</p>

<p>The simpler the code, the bigger the locality: 1000 Ljubljana, 2000 Maribor (big cities); 1310 <a href="/wiki/Ribnica,_Slovenia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ribnica, Slovenia">Ribnica</a>, 9250 <a href="/wiki/Gornja_Radgona" title="Gornja Radgona">Gornja Radgona</a> (mid-sized towns); 4263 <a href="/wiki/Bohinjska_Bela" title="Bohinjska Bela">Bohinjska Bela</a>, 8262 <a href="/wiki/Kr%C5%A1ka_Vas,_Bre%C5%BEice" title="Krška Vas, Brežice">Krška vas</a> (smaller settlements, including villages).</p>
<p>Some cities have more than one post office, thus having multiple postcodes (usually in the x1xx format). For example, Ljubljana which has a "general" postcode 1000, also has additional ones, ranging from 1101 to 1133 (for some reason, however, omitting 1103 and 1105), <a href="/wiki/Kamnik" title="Kamnik">Kamnik</a> has 1240 and 1241, etc. Albeit they exist, it is not necessary to use them - usually the "general" postcodes are used.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=69" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some neighbourhoods may be planned in such a way that some, or most, apartment buildings don't face a named street. In this case, a number of expedients can be used. In older neighbourhoods, a "main" building may have the same number as one or more "subsidiary" buildings accessible via driveways behind the main building. They will be addressed as <i>vul. Bandery, d. 123</i> (<i>123 Bandera St</i>) An address may also cover one or more <i>subsidiary buildings</i> behind the main building, addressed as <i>vul. Bandery, d. 123, bud. 2</i> (<i>123 Bandera St, unit 2</i>, where <i>bud.</i> (abbreviation for будинок, <i>budynоk</i>) means a '(subsidiary) building'). In newer areas with more regular street plans, apartment buildings that don't face a named street may be designated with Cyrillic letters appended to the building number, e.g. <i>123-а</i>, <i>123-б</i>, etc., in <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic alphabetical</a> order.</p>
<p>In some <i><a href="/wiki/Microraion" class="mw-redirect" title="Microraion">microraion</a></i> neighbourhoods, with few, if any, buildings facing named streets, the name (or more likely number of the <i>microraion</i> (planned housing development)) would be used instead of the street name; thus someone may live at <i>4-th microrayon, bud. 123, kv. 56</i>, i.e. <i>123 - 4th Microraion, apt. 56</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Address_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=70" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The format specified by the postal operator <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a> is as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Mail_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Mail-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tr>
<th>Format</th>
<th>Example</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><i>Addressee's name</i><br />
<i>Number and street name</i><br />
<i>Locality (only if required)</i><br />
<i><a href="/wiki/Post_town" title="Post town">POST TOWN</a></i><br />
<i><a href="/wiki/Postcodes_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postcodes in the United Kingdom">POSTCODE</a></i></td>
<td>Mr A Smith<br />
3 High Street<br />
Hedge End<br />
SOUTHAMPTON<br />
SO31 4NG</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The locality is required only where its absence would cause ambiguity, for example where a post town or postcode district includes two streets with the same name. Royal Mail specifies that post towns should be written in block capitals. Until 1996 a <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">postal county</a> (or permitted abbreviation) was required after the post town, unless it was a <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom#Special_post_towns" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">special post town</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/London_postal_district" title="London postal district">London</a>. The post town and postcode should each be on a separate line. Historically, each line of an address ended with a comma and was indented from the previous line. Royal Mail discourage this usage and specify that all lines should start from the same point and not be staggered or aligned to the centre.<sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Mail_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Mail-25">[25]</a></sup> The postcode identifies, from left to right, increasingly smaller units of the postal delivery system. The first half of the postcode, known as the outward code, contains the <a href="/wiki/List_of_postcode_areas_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of postcode areas in the United Kingdom">postcode area</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_postcode_districts_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of postcode districts in the United Kingdom">postcode district</a>. The second half, known as the inward code, contains the postcode sector and postcode unit.<sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Mail_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Mail-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Notes</b>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Traditionally, only the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> (USPS) has been permitted to deliver to a <a href="/wiki/P.O._Box" class="mw-redirect" title="P.O. Box">P.O. Box</a>. For this reason the recipient may choose to insert their physical (aka street) address as line two, expanding the complete address to four lines. Providing both allows a sender to ship via the USPS or via a private carrier. Some USPS facilities allow a user of a P.O. box to use the street address of the postal facility with the P.O. box number in the place of a suite number, in which case the user may receive packages from private carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mail will be delivered to the line immediately above the city, state, zip code line.</li>
<li>The state and type of street, e.g. <i>Lane</i>, is often abbreviated as shown in the PO standard.</li>
<li>The USPS discourages the use of all punctuation except the hyphen in ZIP+4 codes,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> slashes in fractional addresses, hyphenated street numbers, and periods in decimal addresses (e.g. the street name contains a decimal point).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Hyphenated street numbers are common in the New York City borough of <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>, Hawaii, and Southern California;<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> see <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">house numbering</a>.</li>
<li>Sometimes the name of the town required by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> does not necessarily mean that address is within that city. See also <a href="/wiki/ZIP_code#ZIP_codes_and_previous_zoning_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="ZIP code">ZIP codes and previous zoning lines</a>.</li>
<li>Standards for variations on this format (apartment, rural route, general delivery, grid, P.O. Box and highway addresses) are covered in USPS Publication 28.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> addresses often include an urbanization or condominium name.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>The USPS allows for Spanish conventions in addresses in Puerto Rico. The USPS prefers that territories be addressed in the standard domestic format (e.g. "San Juan PR 00907")<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> but in practice territory names are sometimes written as if they are a country (e.g. "San Juan 00907 Puerto Rico").</li>
<li>International <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a> mail will use "DPO" as the city; <a href="/wiki/Military_mail" title="Military mail">military mail</a> will use "APO" or "FPO". Both use "AE", "AP", or "AA" in place of the state code, depending on the continent.</li>
<li>Three independent countries with a <a href="/wiki/Compact_of_Free_Association" title="Compact of Free Association">Compact of Free Association</a> with the U.S. (<a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Federated States of Micronesia">Federated States of Micronesia</a>) have their own domestic government-run mail services, but are integrated into the USPS addressing and ZIP code system. (See <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service#International_services" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service#International services</a>.)</li>
</ul>
