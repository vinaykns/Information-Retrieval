<p>The "bourgeoisie", in its original sense, is intimately linked to the existence of cities recognized as such by their urban charters (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Municipal_charter" title="Municipal charter">municipal charter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">town privileges</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">German town law</a>) so there was no bourgeoisie "outside the walls of the city" beyond which the people were "<a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasants</a>" submitted to the stately courts and <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorialism</a> (except for the traveling "<a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">Fair</a> bourgeoisie" living outside urban territories, who retained their city rights and <a href="/wiki/Domicile_(law)" title="Domicile (law)">domicile</a>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist philosophy</a> the bourgeoisie is the social class that came to own the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> during modern <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> and whose societal concerns are the value of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> and the preservation of capital, to ensure the perpetuation of their economic supremacy in society.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter" title="Joseph Schumpeter">Joseph Schumpeter</a> saw the creation of new bourgeoisie as the driving force behind the capitalist engine, particularly entrepreneurs who took risks to bring innovation to industries and the economy through the process of <a href="/wiki/Creative_destruction" title="Creative destruction">creative destruction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Modern French word <i>bourgeois</i> derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>burgeis</i> (walled city), which derived from <i>bourg</i> (<a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a>), from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Frankish" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Frankish">Old Frankish</a> <i>burg</i> (town); in other European languages, the etymologic derivations are the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>burgeis</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> <i>burgher</i>, the German <i>Bürger</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Burgess_(title)" title="Burgess (title)">burgess</a></i>, and the Polish <i>burżuazja</i>, which occasionally is synonymous with the <a href="/wiki/Intelligentsia" title="Intelligentsia">intelligentsia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In English, "bourgeoisie" (a French citizen-class) identified a social class oriented to <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">economic materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">hedonism</a>, and to upholding the extreme political and economic interests of the capitalist ruling class.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the 18th century, before the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> (1789–99), in the French <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal order</a>, the masculine and feminine terms <i>bourgeois</i> and <i>bourgeoise</i> identified the rich men and women who were members of the urban and rural <a href="/wiki/Estates_General_(France)" title="Estates General (France)">Third Estate</a> – the common people of the French realm, who violently deposed the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a> King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a> (r. 1774–91), his clergy, and his <a href="/wiki/French_nobility" title="French nobility">aristocrats</a>. Hence, since the 19th century, the term "bourgeoisie" usually is politically and sociologically synonymous with the ruling upper class of a capitalist society.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, the medieval French word <i>bourgeois</i> denoted the inhabitants of the <i>bourgs</i> (walled market-towns), the <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craftsmen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a>, and others, who constituted "the bourgeoisie", they were the socio-economic class between the peasants and the landlords, between the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">workers</a> and the owners of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>. As the economic managers of the (raw) materials, the goods, and the services, and thus the <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> (money) produced by the feudal economy, the term "bourgeoisie" evolved to also denote the middle class – the businessmen and businesswomen who accumulated, administered, and controlled the capital that made possible the development of the bourgs into cities.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Columbia_Encyclopedia_1994_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Columbia_Encyclopedia_1994-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contemporarily, the terms "bourgeoisie" and "bourgeois" (noun) identify the ruling class in capitalist societies, as a social stratum; while "bourgeois" (adjective / noun modifier) describes the <i>Weltanschauung</i> (<a href="/wiki/Worldview" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldview">worldview</a>) of men and women whose way of thinking is socially and culturally determined by their economic materialism and <a href="/wiki/Philistinism" title="Philistinism">philistinism</a>, a social identity famously mocked in <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">Molière</a>'s comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Bourgeois_gentilhomme" title="Le Bourgeois gentilhomme">Le Bourgeois gentilhomme</a></i> (1670), which satirises buying the trappings of a noble-birth identity as the means of climbing the social ladder.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moli.C3.A8re.2C_ed._Warren_1899_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moli.C3.A8re.2C_ed._Warren_1899-10">[10]</a></sup> The 18th century saw a partial rehabilitation of bourgeois values in genres such as the <i>drame bourgeois</i> (bourgeois drama) and "<a href="/wiki/Bourgeois_tragedy" title="Bourgeois tragedy">bourgeois tragedy</a>".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_rise">Origins and rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins and rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_capitalism#Origins_of_capitalism" title="History of capitalism">History of capitalism §&#160;Origins of capitalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trade#History" title="Trade">Trade §&#160;History</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fuggerkontor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Fuggerkontor.jpg/170px-Fuggerkontor.jpg" width="170" height="266" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Fuggerkontor.jpg/255px-Fuggerkontor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Fuggerkontor.jpg/340px-Fuggerkontor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1358" data-file-height="2121" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fuggerkontor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 16th-century German banker <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Fugger" title="Jakob Fugger">Jakob Fugger</a> and his principal accountant, M. Schwarz, registering an entry to a ledger. The background shows a file cabinet indicating the European cities where the Fugger Bank conducts business. (1517)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bourgeoisie emerged as a historical and political phenomenon in the 11th century when the <i>bourgs</i> of Central and Western Europe developed into cities dedicated to commerce. This urban expansion was possible thanks to economic concentration due to the appearance of protective self-organisation into <a href="/wiki/Guilds" class="mw-redirect" title="Guilds">guilds</a>. Guilds arose when individual businessmen (such as craftsmen, artisans and merchants) conflicted with their <a href="/wiki/Rent-seeking" title="Rent-seeking">rent-seeking</a> feudal <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal land tenure">landlords</a> who demanded greater <a href="/wiki/Economic_rent" title="Economic rent">rents</a> than previously agreed.</p>
<p>In the event, by the end of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (ca. AD 1500), under régimes of the early national monarchies of Western Europe, the bourgeoisie acted in self-interest, and politically supported the king or queen against <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">legal</a> and financial disorder caused by the greed of the feudal lords.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the late-16th and early 17th centuries, the bourgeoisies of England and the Netherlands had become the financial – thus political – forces that deposed the feudal order; economic power had vanquished military power in the realm of politics.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Columbia_Encyclopedia_1994_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Columbia_Encyclopedia_1994-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_progress_to_reaction">From progress to reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From progress to reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 17th and 18th centuries, the bourgeoisie were the politically <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a> social class who supported the principles of <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitutional government</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Natural_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural right">natural right</a>, against the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Privilege" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of Privilege">Law of Privilege</a> and the claims of <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">rule by divine right</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelates</a> had autonomously exercised during the feudal order.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (1642–51), the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a> (1775–83), and <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> (1789–99) were partly motivated by the desire by the bourgeoisie to rid themselves of the feudal and royal encroachments on their personal liberty, commercial prospects, and the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>. In the 19th century, the bourgeoisie propounded liberalism, and gained political rights, religious rights, and civil liberties for themselves and the lower social classes; thus the bourgeoisie was a progressive philosophic and political force in Western societies.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> (1750–1850), by the mid-19th century the great expansion of the bourgeoisie social class caused its <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">stratification</a> – by business activity and by economic function – into the <i>haute bourgeoisie</i> (bankers and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrialists</a>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Petite_bourgeoisie" title="Petite bourgeoisie">petite bourgeoisie</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Tradesman" title="Tradesman">tradesmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/White-collar_worker" title="White-collar worker">white-collar workers</a>). Moreover, by the end of the 19th century, the capitalists (the original bourgeoisie) had ascended to the upper class, while the developments of technology and <a href="/wiki/Trade_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade (occupation)">technical occupations</a> allowed the rise of working-class men and women to the lower strata of the bourgeoisie; yet the social progress was incidental.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denotations">Denotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Denotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marxist_theory">Marxist theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marxist theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>, the bourgeois during Middle Ages usually was a self-employed businessman – such as a merchant, banker, or entrepreneur – whose economic role in society was being the financial intermediary to the feudal landlord and the peasant who worked the fief, the land of the lord. Yet, by the 18th century, the time of the Industrial Revolution (1750–1850) and of industrial capitalism, the bourgeoisie had become the economic ruling class who owned the means of production (capital and land), and who controlled the means of coercion (armed forces and legal system, police forces and prison system).</p>
<p>In such a society, the bourgeoisie's ownership of the means of production allowed them to employ and exploit the wage-earning working class (urban and rural), people whose only economic means is labour; and the bourgeois control of the means of coercion suppressed the sociopolitical challenges by the lower classes, and so preserved the economic status quo; workers remained workers, and employers remained employers.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, Marx distinguished two types of bourgeois capitalist: (i) the functional capitalists, who are business administrators of the means of production; and (ii) rentier capitalists whose livelihoods derive either from the <a href="/wiki/Economic_rent" title="Economic rent">rent</a> of property or from the <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>-income produced by finance capital, or both.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the course of economic relations, the working class and the bourgeoisie continually engage in <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a>, where the capitalists <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">exploit</a> the workers, while the workers resist their economic exploitation, which occurs because the worker owns no means of production, and, to earn a living, he or she seeks employment from the bourgeois capitalist; the worker produces goods and services that are property of the employer, who sells them for a price.</p>
<p>Besides describing the social class who owns the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>, the Marxist use of the term "bourgeois" also describes the <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerist</a> style of life derived from the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>. Marx acknowledged the bourgeois industriousness that created wealth, but criticised the moral hypocrisy of the bourgeoisie when they ignored the alleged origins of their wealth: the exploitation of the proletariat, the urban and rural workers. Further sense denotations of "bourgeois" describe ideological concepts such as "bourgeois freedom", which is thought to be opposed to substantive forms of freedom; "bourgeois independence"; "bourgeois personal individuality"; the "bourgeois family"; et cetera, all derived from owning capital and property. (See: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Communist_Manifesto" title="The Communist Manifesto">The Communist Manifesto</a></i>, 1848.)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France_and_French-speaking_countries">France and French-speaking countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: France and French-speaking countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In English, the term <i>bourgeoisie</i> is often used to denote the middle classes. In fact, the French term encompasses both the upper and middle classes,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a misunderstanding which has occurred in other languages as well. The bourgeoisie in France and many French-speaking countries consists of four evolving social layers: <i>petite bourgeoisie</i>, <i>moyenne bourgeoisie</i>, <i>grande bourgeoisie</i>, and <i>haute bourgeoisie</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Petite_Bourgeoisie"><i>Petite Bourgeoisie</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Petite Bourgeoisie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>petite bourgeoisie</i> consists of people who have experienced a brief ascension in <a href="/wiki/Social_mobility" title="Social mobility">social mobility</a> for one or two generations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It usually starts with a trade or craft, and by the second and third generation, a family may rise another level. The <i>petite bourgeois</i> would belong to the British lower middle class and would be American middle income. They are distinguished mainly by their mentality, and would differentiate themselves from the <i>proletariat</i> or working class. This class would include artisans, small traders, shopkeepers, and small farm owners. They are not employed, but may not be able to afford employees themselves.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Moyenne_Bourgeoisie"><i>Moyenne Bourgeoisie</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Moyenne Bourgeoisie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>moyenne bourgeoisie</i> or middle bourgeoisie contains people who have solid incomes and assets, but not the aura of those who have become established at a higher level. They tend to belong to a family that has been bourgeois for three or more generations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some members of this class may have relatives from similar backgrounds, or may even have aristocratic connections. The <i>moyenne bourgeoisie</i> is the equivalent of the British and American upper-middle classes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Grande_Bourgeoisie"><i>Grande Bourgeoisie</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grande Bourgeoisie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>grande bourgeoisie</i> are families that have been bourgeois since the 19th century, or for at least four or five generations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Members of these families tend to marry with the aristocracy or make other advantageous marriages. This bourgeoisie family has acquired an established historical and cultural heritage over the decades. The names of these families are generally known in the city where they reside, and their ancestors have often contributed to the region's history. These families are respected and revered. They belong to the upper class, and in the British class system are considered part of the gentry. <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">In the French-speaking countries, they are sometimes referred <i>la petite haute bourgeoisie</i></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Haute_Bourgeoisie"><i>Haute Bourgeoisie</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Haute Bourgeoisie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>haute bourgeoisie</i> is a social rank in the bourgeoisie that can only be acquired through time. In France, it is composed of bourgeois families that have existed since the French Revolution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They hold only honourable professions and have experienced many illustrious marriages in their family's history. They have rich cultural and historical heritages, and their financial means are more than secure.</p>
<p>These families exude an aura of nobility, which prevents them from certain marriages or occupations. They only differ from nobility in that due to circumstances, the lack of opportunity, and/or political regime, they have not been ennobled. These people nevertheless live a lavish lifestyle, enjoying the company of the great artists of the time. In France, the families of the <i>haute bourgeoisie</i> are also referred to as <i>les 200 familles</i>, a term coined in the first half of the 20th century. Michel Pinçon and Monique Pinçon-Charlot studied the lifestyle of the French bourgeoisie, and how they boldly guard their world from the <i>nouveau riche</i>, or newly rich.</p>
<p>In the French language, the term <i>bourgeoisie</i> almost designates a caste by itself, even though social mobility into this socio-economic group is possible. Nevertheless, the <i>bourgeoisie</i> is differentiated from <i>la classe moyenne</i>, or the middle class, which consists mostly of white-collar employees, by holding a profession referred to as a <i>profession libérale</i>, which <i>la classe moyenne</i>, in its definition does not hold.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Yet, in English the definition of a white-collar job encompasses the <i>profession libérale</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of their ascribed cultural excellence as a social class, the <a href="/wiki/Italian_fascism" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian fascism">Italian fascist</a> régime (1922–45) of Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> regarded the bourgeoisie as an obstacle to <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a> in aid to transforming Italian society.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellassai05_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellassai05-14">[14]</a></sup> Nonetheless, despite such intellectual and social hostility, the Fascist State ideologically exploited the Italian bourgeoisie and their materialistic, middle-class spirit, for the more efficient cultural manipulation of the upper (aristocratic) and the lower (working) classes of Italy.</p>
<p>In 1938, Prime Minister Mussolini gave a speech wherein he established a clear ideological distinction between capitalism (the social function of the bourgeoisie) and the bourgeoisie (as a social class), whom he dehumanised by reducing them into high-level abstractions: a moral category and a state of mind.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellassai05_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellassai05-14">[14]</a></sup> Culturally and philosophically, Mussolini isolated the bourgeoisie from Italian society by portraying them as social parasites upon the fascist Italian state and "The People"; as a social class who drained the human potential of Italian society, in general, and of the working class, in particular; as exploiters who victimised the Italian nation with an approach to life characterised by <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">hedonism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">materialism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bellassai05_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bellassai05-14">[14]</a></sup> Nevertheless, despite the slogan <i>The Fascist Man Disdains the ″Comfortable″ Life</i>, which epitomised the anti-bourgeois principle, in its final years of power, for mutual benefit and profit, the Mussolini fascist régime transcended ideology to merge the political and financial interests of Prime Minister Benito Mussolini with the political and financial interests of the bourgeoisie, the Catholic social circles who constituted the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a> of Italy.</p>
<p>Philosophically, as a <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialist</a> creature, the bourgeois man was stereotyped as irreligious; thus, to establish an <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existential</a> distinction between the supernatural faith of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> and the materialist faith of temporal religion; in <i>The Autarchy of Culture: Intellectuals and Fascism in the 1930s</i>, the priest Giuseppe Marino said that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Christianity is essentially anti-bourgeois. ... A Christian, a true Christian, and thus a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_(Christian_terminology)#Use_by_the_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic (Christian terminology)">Catholic</a>, is the opposite of a bourgeois.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Culturally, the bourgeois man is unmanly, effeminate, and infantile; describing his <a href="/wiki/Philistinism" title="Philistinism">philistinism</a> in <i>Bonifica antiborghese</i> (1939), Roberto Paravese said that the:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Middle class, middle man, incapable of great virtue or great vice: and there would be nothing wrong with that, if only he would be willing to remain as such; but, when his child-like or feminine tendency to camouflage pushes him to dream of grandeur, honours, and thus riches, which he cannot achieve honestly with his own "second-rate" powers, then the average man compensates with cunning, schemes, and mischief; he kicks out ethics, and becomes a bourgeois.</p>
<p>The bourgeois is the average man who does not accept to remain such, and who, lacking the strength sufficient for the conquest of essential values—those of the spirit—opts for material ones, for appearances.<sup id="cite_ref-paravese39_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paravese39-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The economic security, <a href="/wiki/Discretionary_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Discretionary income">financial freedom</a>, and social mobility of the bourgeoisie threatened the philosophic integrity of <a href="/wiki/Italian_fascism" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian fascism">Italian fascism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideological monolith</a> that was the <a href="/wiki/Fascist_regime" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist regime">régime</a> of Prime Minister Benito Mussolini. Any assumption of <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">legitimate</a> political power (government and rule) by the bourgeoisie represented a fascist loss of <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> state power for social control through political unity—one people, one nation, one leader. Sociologically, to the fascist man, to become a bourgeois was a character flaw inherent to the masculine mystique; therefore, the ideology of Italian fascism scornfully defined the bourgeois man as "spiritually castrated".<sup id="cite_ref-paravese39_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paravese39-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bourgeois_culture">Bourgeois culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bourgeois culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_hegemony">Cultural hegemony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural hegemony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Karl Marx said that the culture of a society is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">mores of the ruling-class</a>, wherein their superimposed <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">value system</a> is abided by each social class (the upper, the middle, the lower) regardless of the socio-economic results it yields to them. In that sense, contemporary societies are bourgeois to the degree that they practice the <a href="/wiki/Mores" title="Mores">mores</a> of the small-business "shop culture" of early modern France; which the writer <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Zola" title="Émile Zola">Émile Zola</a> (1840–1902) <a href="/wiki/Naturalism_(literature)" title="Naturalism (literature)">naturalistically</a> presented, analysed, and ridiculed in the twenty-two-novel series (1871–1893) about <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Rougon-Macquart" title="Les Rougon-Macquart">Les Rougon-Macquart</a></i> family; the thematic thrust is the necessity for social progress, by subordinating the economic sphere to the social sphere of life.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspicuous_consumption">Conspicuous consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Conspicuous consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The critical analyses of the bourgeois mentality by the German intellectual <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">Walter Benjamin</a> (1892–1940) indicated that the shop culture of the <a href="/wiki/Petite_bourgeoisie" title="Petite bourgeoisie">petite bourgeoisie</a> established the sitting room as the centre of personal and family life; as such, the English bourgeois culture is, he alleges, a sitting-room culture of <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">prestige</a> through <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">conspicuous consumption</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">material culture</a> of the bourgeoisie concentrated on mass-produced <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">luxury goods</a> of high quality; between generations, the only variance was the materials with which the goods were manufactured.</p>
<p>In the early part of the 19th century, the bourgeois house contained a home that first was stocked and decorated with hand-painted <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>, machine-printed cotton fabrics, machine-printed <a href="/wiki/Wallpaper" title="Wallpaper">wallpaper</a>, and Sheffield steel (<a href="/wiki/Crucible_steel" title="Crucible steel">crucible</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a> of these things was inherent to their practical functions. By the latter part of the 19th century, the bourgeois house contained a home that had been remodelled by conspicuous consumption. Here, Benjamin argues, the goods were bought to display wealth (<a href="/wiki/Discretionary_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Discretionary income">discretionary income</a>), rather than for their practical utility. The bourgeoisie had transposed the wares of the shop window to the sitting room, where the clutter of display signalled bourgeois success.<sup id="cite_ref-Benjamin_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin-18">[18]</a></sup> (See: <i><a href="/wiki/Culture_and_Anarchy" title="Culture and Anarchy">Culture and Anarchy</a></i>, 1869.)</p>
<p>Two spatial constructs manifest the bourgeois mentality: (i) the shop-window display, and (ii) the sitting room. In English, the term "sitting-room culture" is synonymous for "bourgeois mentality", a "<a href="/wiki/Philistinism" title="Philistinism">philistine</a>" cultural perspective from the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Era">Victorian Era</a> (1837–1901), especially characterised by the repression of emotion and of sexual desire; and by the construction of a regulated social-space where "<a href="/wiki/Victorian_morality" title="Victorian morality">propriety</a>" is the key personality trait desired in men and women.<sup id="cite_ref-Benjamin_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, from such a psychologically constricted <a href="/wiki/Worldview" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldview">worldview</a>, regarding the rearing of children, contemporary sociologists claim to have identified "progressive" middle-class values, such as respect for non-conformity, self-direction, <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> and the encouragement of innovation; as in the Victorian Era, the transposition to the US of the bourgeois system of social values has been identified as a requisite for employment success in the professions.<sup id="cite_ref-The_American_Class_Structure_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_American_Class_Structure-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marriages.2C_Families_.26_Intimate_Relationships_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriages.2C_Families_.26_Intimate_Relationships-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representations">Representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beyond the <a href="/wiki/Intellectualism" title="Intellectualism">intellectual</a> realms of <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>, history, and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> that discuss, describe, and analyse the <i>bourgeoisie</i> as a social class, the colloquial usage of the <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> terms <i>bourgeois</i> and <i>bourgeoise</i> describe the social <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotypes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_money" title="Old money">old money</a> and of the <i><a href="/wiki/Nouveau_riche" title="Nouveau riche">nouveau riche</a></i>, who is a politically timid conformist satisfied with a wealthy, <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerist</a> style of life characterised by <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">conspicuous consumption</a> and the continual striving for <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">prestige</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This being the case, the cultures of the world describe the <a href="/wiki/Philistinism" title="Philistinism">philistinism</a> of the middle-class personality, produced by the excessively rich life of the bourgeoisie, is examined and analysed in comedic and dramatic plays, novels, and films. (See: <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">Authenticity</a>.)</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_(1658).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg/220px-Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg" width="220" height="281" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg/330px-Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg/440px-Moli%C3%A8re_-_Nicolas_Mignard_%281658%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="601" data-file-height="768" /></a>

The 17th-century French playwright Molière (1622–73) catalogued the social-climbing essence of the bourgeoisie in <i>Le Bourgeois gentilhomme</i> (1670).</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Le_Bourgeois_gentilhomme" title="Le Bourgeois gentilhomme">Le Bourgeois gentilhomme</a></i> (The Would-be Gentleman, 1670) by <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">Molière</a> (Jean-Baptiste Poquelin), is a comedy-ballet that <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirises</a> Monsieur Jourdain, the prototypical <a href="/wiki/Nouveau_riche" title="Nouveau riche">nouveau riche</a> man who buys his way up the social-class scale, to realise his aspirations of becoming a gentleman, to which end he studies dancing, fencing, and philosophy, the trappings and accomplishments of a gentleman, to be able to pose as a man of <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble birth</a>, someone who, in 17th-century France, was a man to the manner born; Jourdain's self-transformation also requires managing the private life of his daughter, so that her marriage can also assist his social ascent.<sup id="cite_ref-Moli.C3.A8re.2C_ed._Warren_1899_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moli.C3.A8re.2C_ed._Warren_1899-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Mann_in_1926.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Thomas_Mann_in_1926.jpg/170px-Thomas_Mann_in_1926.jpg" width="170" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Thomas_Mann_in_1926.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="233" data-file-height="300" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Mann_in_1926.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> (1875–1955) portrayed the moral, intellectual, and physical decadence of the German upper bourgeoisie in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Buddenbrooks" title="Buddenbrooks">Buddenbrooks</a></i> (1926)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Buddenbrooks" title="Buddenbrooks">Buddenbrooks</a></i> (1901), by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a> (1875–1955), chronicles the <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">moral</a>, intellectual, and <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding#Humans" title="Inbreeding">physical</a> decay of a rich family through its declines, material and spiritual, in the course of four generations, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarch</a> Johann Buddenbrook Sr. and his son, Johann Buddenbrook Jr., who are typically successful German businessmen; each is a reasonable man of solid character.</p>
<p>Yet, in the children of Buddenbrook Jr., the materially comfortable style of life provided by the dedication to solid, middle-class <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">values</a> elicits decadence: The fickle daughter, Toni, lacks and does not seek a purpose in life; son Christian is honestly decadent, and lives the life of a ne’er-do-well; and the businessman son, Thomas, who assumes command of the Buddenbrook family fortune, occasionally falters from middle-class solidity by being interested in art and philosophy, the impractical <a href="/wiki/Intellectualism" title="Intellectualism">life of the mind</a>, which, to the bourgeoisie, is the epitome of social, moral, and material decadence.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Babbitt_(novel)" title="Babbitt (novel)">Babbitt</a></i> (1922), by <a href="/wiki/Sinclair_Lewis" title="Sinclair Lewis">Sinclair Lewis</a> (1885–1951), satirizes the American bourgeois George Follansbee Babbitt, a middle-aged <a href="/wiki/Realtor" class="mw-redirect" title="Realtor">realtor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boosterism" title="Boosterism">booster</a>, and joiner in the Midwestern city of Zenith, who – despite being unimaginative, self-important, and hopelessly conformist and middle-class – is aware that there must be more to life than money and the <a href="/wiki/Conspicuous_consumption" title="Conspicuous consumption">consumption</a> of the best things that money can buy. Nevertheless, he fears <a href="/wiki/Ostracism" title="Ostracism">being excluded</a> from the mainstream of society more than he does living for himself, by <a href="/wiki/Authenticity_(philosophy)" title="Authenticity (philosophy)">being true to himself</a> – his heart-felt flirtations with independence (dabbling in <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal politics</a> and a love affair with a pretty widow) come to naught because he is existentially afraid.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG/170px-Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG" width="170" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG/255px-Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG/340px-Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG 2x" data-file-width="577" data-file-height="905" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Spanish cinéast <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a> (1900–83) depicted the tortuous mentality and self-destructive hypocrisy of the bourgeoisie</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Yet, George F. Babbitt sublimates his desire for self-respect, and encourages his son to rebel against the conformity that results from bourgeois prosperity, by recommending that he be true to himself:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Don't be scared of the family. No, nor all of Zenith. Nor of yourself, the way I've been.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bourgeoisie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The comedy films by the Spanish film director <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a> (1900–83) examine the mental and moral effects of the bourgeois mentality, its culture, and the stylish way of life it provides for its practitioners.</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/L%27%C3%82ge_d%27or" class="mw-redirect" title="L'Âge d'or">L'Âge d'or</a></i> (<i>The Golden Age</i>, 1930) illustrates the madness and self-destructive hypocrisy of bourgeois society.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Le_charme_discret_de_la_bourgeoisie" class="mw-redirect" title="Le charme discret de la bourgeoisie">Le charme discret de la bourgeoisie</a></i> (<i>The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoisie</i>, 1972) explores the timidity instilled by middle-class <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">values</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Cet_obscur_objet_du_d%C3%A9sir" class="mw-redirect" title="Cet obscur objet du désir">Cet obscur objet du désir</a></i> (<i>That Obscure Object of Desire</i>, 1977) illuminates the practical self-deceptions required for buying love as marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
