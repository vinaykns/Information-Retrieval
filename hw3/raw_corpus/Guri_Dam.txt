<p>The <b>Guri Dam</b> is a concrete gravity and <a href="/wiki/Embankment_dam" title="Embankment dam">embankment dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bol%C3%ADvar_State_(Venezuela)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolívar State (Venezuela)">Bolívar State</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Caroni_River_(Venezuela)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caroni River (Venezuela)">Caroni River</a> built from 1963 to 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-3">[3]</a></sup> It is 7,426 metres long and 162 m high.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It impounds the large Guri Reservoir (<i>Embalse de Guri</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with a surface area of 4,250 square kilometres (1,641&#160;sq&#160;mi)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_design">History and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Embalse_Guri.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Embalse_Guri.JPG/200px-Embalse_Guri.JPG" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Embalse_Guri.JPG/300px-Embalse_Guri.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Embalse_Guri.JPG/400px-Embalse_Guri.JPG 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="640" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Embalse_Guri.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The dam and reservoir as seen from space.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1963, construction began for the hydroelectric power station Guri in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Necuima_Canyon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Necuima Canyon (page does not exist)">Necuima Canyon</a>, about 100 kilometers upstream from the mouth of the Caroní River in the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a>. By 1969, a 106 m high and 690 m long dam with the official name of <i>Central Hidroeléctrica <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Simón Bolívar">Simón Bolívar</a></i> (previously named <i>Central Hidroeléctrica <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Leoni" title="Raúl Leoni">Raúl Leoni</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> from 1978 to 2000) had been built. It created a reservoir which is the largest fresh water body of water in Venezuela and the eleventh largest man-made lake in the world<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with its water level at 215 metres above sea level. The power station had a combined <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">installed capacity</a> of 1750 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a> (MW).<sup id="cite_ref-eb_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-3">[3]</a></sup> By 1978, the capacity had been upgraded to 2065 MW, generated by ten turbines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Because the electricity demand grew so fast, 1976 saw the beginning of a second building stage: a 1300 m long <a href="/wiki/Gravity_dam" title="Gravity dam">gravity dam</a> was built, another spillway channel and a second powerhouse containing 10 turbines of 630 MW each. The powerhouse´s inside walls were decorated by the Venezuelan kinetic artist <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Cruz-D%C3%ADez" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlos Cruz-Díez">Carlos Cruz-Díez</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This increased the dam's dimensions to 162 m in height and to 7426 m (according to other sources 11,409 m<sup id="cite_ref-eb_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-3">[3]</a></sup>) in crest length. The water level rose to 272 m<sup id="cite_ref-Cifras2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cifras2006-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:12</sup> and the reservoir grew in size and volume to a capacity of 138 billion cubic m for flood storage or floodwater evacuation.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-3">[3]</a></sup> The structure was inaugurated on 8 November 1986.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The total projected hydroelectric capacity is 10,300 megawatts, one of the largest in the world.</p>
<p>Since 2000, there is an ongoing refurbishment project to extend the operation of Guri Power Plant by 30 years. This project is to create 5 new runners and main components on Powerhouse II, and close to the end of 2007 is starting the rehabilitation of four units on Powerhouse I.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_and_financing">Ownership and financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ownership and financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1978, C.V.G. Electrificación del Caroní, C.A.(EDELCA) awarded <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a> the contract to install the 10 turbines of Phase 2. In 1985, Hitachi installed a computer system for the powerhouse for then owner C A E International; it also designed, manufactured and installed a monument called "Torre Solar".</p>
<p>In 1989 Hitachi installed baffles and draft tubes for twelve 730 MW units in Power House 2.<sup id="cite_ref-perf_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perf-7">[7]</a></sup> It did a major overhaul of 180 MW generating water turbine units number 1, 2 and 3 in Power House 1 and revamped guide rings on 730 MW generating units number 13 to 20 in Power House number 2 until 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-perf_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perf-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1992 until 1997 C.V.G. Electrificación del Caroní, C.A.(EDELCA) revamped runner cones of 730 MW generating units in Power House 2, units 11, 13 until 20. From 1997 until 1998 Hitachi did a major overhaul of 223 MW generating water turbine units number 5 and 6 in Power House 1 and sent welders for repairs in runner vanes of unit 11 and 12. In 1998, EDELCA refurbished two floodgates, and from 1999 - 2001, it overhauled the 270 MW generating water turbine units number 4 and 7 in Power House 1.<sup id="cite_ref-perf_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perf-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, EDELCA painted two metallic stairways located in earth preys. From 2002 - 2005 G.E. NORCAN Hydraulic Turbine Inc Proyecto Guri II (Venezuela) disassembled, refurbished and reassembled 5 units: During 2002, <a href="/wiki/Mitsui" title="Mitsui">Mitsui</a> de Venezuela, C.A. (for Toshiba) helped remove Unit 13 rotor blocks and EDELCA supplied steel for the extension of metallic lining of Unit 18's aspiration tube in Power House 2. Fromm 2002-2003, CVG rehabilitated valves and pipes of Unit 19's generator cooling system in Power House 2; Mitsui repaired and assembled the rotor of unit 13 and took corrective measures for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stator_(Turbine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stator (Turbine) (page does not exist)">stator</a> in Power House 2, while CVG rehabilitated valves and pipes of unit 13's generator cooling system.<sup id="cite_ref-perf_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perf-7">[7]</a></sup> During 2003, CVG machined holes of unit 19's stator base in Power House 2. From 2003-2004, Mitsui overhauled unit 17's generator in Power House 2. From 2004-2005, CVG rehabilitated the cooling system in units 14 and 17 in Power House 2. During 2006, CVG refurbished runners of units 16 and 20 and later 9. From 2006-2009, <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a> Hydro Venezuela S.A. worked on rehabilitation of an overhead crane in Power House 1 and 2 and other rehabilitation until 2011. From 2007-2008, CVG repaired runner blades of Unit 7.<sup id="cite_ref-perf_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perf-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988, <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>, Ltd founded affiliate company HPC Venezuela C.A (or VHPC), which stands for <b>H</b>itachi <b>P</b>lant Engineering and <b>C</b>onstruction.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guri Reservoir is one of <a href="/wiki/Reservoir#List_of_reservoirs" title="Reservoir">the largest on earth</a>. The hydroelectric power station ranks among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_hydroelectric_power_stations" title="List of largest hydroelectric power stations">List of largest hydroelectric power stations</a>. It was once the largest worldwide in terms of installed capacity, replacing <a href="/wiki/Grand_Coulee_Dam" title="Grand Coulee Dam">Grand Coulee</a> HPP, but was surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Itaipu_Dam" title="Itaipu Dam">Itaipu</a> HPP.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_contribution">Energy contribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy contribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to government policy in effect from the 1960s to minimize power production from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> in order to export as much oil as possible, 74% of Venezuela's electricity comes from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> like <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cifras2006_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cifras2006-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup> As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the Guri Dam alone supplied more than a third of Venezuela's electricity<sup id="cite_ref-Cifras2006_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cifras2006-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:33</sup> Parts of the power generated at Guri is exported to <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cifras2006_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cifras2006-6">[6]</a></sup> The risks of this strategy became apparent in 2010, when, due to a prolonged drought, water levels were too low to produce enough electricity to meet demand.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In January 2010, the Venezuelan government imposed <a href="/wiki/Rolling_blackout" title="Rolling blackout">rolling blackouts</a> of two hours every day throughout the country to combat low water levels behind the dam due to drought.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In April 2016, water levels again became low, and the government announced blackouts of 4 hours per day, for 40 days or until water labels stabilized,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and government employees were told not to come to work on Fridays.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_controversy">Environmental controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guri_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dam has long been the focus of much controversy, because the lake it created forever destroyed thousands of square miles of a forest that was renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and rare <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>, including the only place where the recently discovered <a href="/wiki/Carrizal_seedeater" title="Carrizal seedeater">Carrizal seedeater</a> (a finch-like <a href="/wiki/Tanager" title="Tanager">tanager</a>) was ever found.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
