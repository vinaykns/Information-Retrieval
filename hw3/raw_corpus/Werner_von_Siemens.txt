<p><b>Ernst Werner Siemens</b> (<b><a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a> Siemens</b> from 1888; <small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈziːmɛns]</a></span>; 13 December 1816 – 6 December 1892) was a German <a href="/wiki/Inventor" class="mw-redirect" title="Inventor">inventor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialist">industrialist</a>. Siemens’s name has been adopted as the <a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> unit of electrical conductance, the <a href="/wiki/Siemens_(unit)" title="Siemens (unit)">siemens</a>. He was also the founder of the electrical and telecommunications company <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens AG">Siemens</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ernst Werner Siemens was born in Lenthe, today part of <a href="/wiki/Gehrden" title="Gehrden">Gehrden</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Hannover" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannover">Hannover</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a> in the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>, the fourth child (of fourteen) of a tenant <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Siemens_family" title="Siemens family">Siemens family</a>, an old family of <a href="/wiki/Goslar" title="Goslar">Goslar</a>, documented since 1384. He was a brother of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Heinrich_von_Siemens" title="Carl Heinrich von Siemens">Carl Heinrich von Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Siemens" title="Carl Wilhelm Siemens">Carl Wilhelm Siemens</a>, sons of Christian Ferdinand Siemens (31 July 1787 – 16 January 1840) and wife Eleonore Deichmann (1792 – 8 July 1839).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_years">Middle years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After finishing school, Siemens intended to study at the <a href="/wiki/Bauakademie" title="Bauakademie">Bauakademie</a> Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, since his family was highly indebted and thus could not afford to pay the tuition fees, he chose to join the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Military_Academy" title="Prussian Military Academy">Prussian Military Academy</a>'s School of Artillery and Engineering, between the years 1835-1838, instead, where he received his officers training.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Siemens was thought of as a good soldier, receiving various medals, and inventing electrically-charged <a href="/wiki/Sea_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea mine">sea mines</a>, which were used to combat a Danish blockade of <a href="/wiki/Kiel" title="Kiel">Kiel</a>. Upon returning home from war, he put his mind to other uses. He is known world-wide for his advances in various technologies, and chose to work on perfecting technologies that had already been established. In 1843 he sold the rights to his first invention to <a href="/wiki/Elkington_of_Birmingham" class="mw-redirect" title="Elkington of Birmingham">Elkington of Birmingham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Siemens invented a <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraph</a> that used a needle to point to the right letter, instead of using <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a>. Based on this invention, he founded the company <i><a href="/wiki/Telegraphen-Bauanstalt_von_Siemens_%26_Halske" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraphen-Bauanstalt von Siemens &amp; Halske">Telegraphen-Bauanstalt von Siemens &amp; Halske</a></i> on 1 October 1847, with the company opening a workshop on 12 October.</p>
<p>The company was internationalised soon after its founding. One brother of Werner represented him in England (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Siemens" title="Carl Wilhelm Siemens">Sir William Siemens</a>) and another in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carl_Heinrich_von_Siemens" title="Carl Heinrich von Siemens">Carl von Siemens</a>), each earning recognition. Following his industrial career, he was ennobled in 1888, becoming Werner von Siemens. He retired from his company in 1890 and died in 1892 in Berlin.</p>
<p>The company, reorganized as <a href="/wiki/Siemens_%26_Halske_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens &amp; Halske AG">Siemens &amp; Halske AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens-Schuckertwerke" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens-Schuckertwerke">Siemens-Schuckertwerke</a> and – since 1966 – <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens AG">Siemens AG</a> was later led by his brother Carl, his sons <a href="/wiki/Arnold_von_Siemens" title="Arnold von Siemens">Arnold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_von_Siemens" title="Georg Wilhelm von Siemens">Wilhelm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_von_Siemens" title="Carl Friedrich von Siemens">Carl Friedrich</a>, his grandsons <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Siemens" title="Hermann von Siemens">Hermann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_von_Siemens" title="Ernst von Siemens">Ernst</a> and his great-grandson <a href="/wiki/Peter_von_Siemens" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter von Siemens">Peter von Siemens</a>. Siemens AG is one of the largest electrotechnological firms in the world. The von Siemens family still owns 6% of the company shares (as of 2013) and holds a seat on the supervisory board, being the largest shareholder.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from the pointer telegraph Siemens made several contributions to the development of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> and is therefore known as the founding father of the discipline in Germany. He built the world's first electric <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevator</a> in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His company produced the tubes with which <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Conrad_R%C3%B6ntgen" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen">Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen</a> investigated x-rays. He claimed invention of the <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> although others invented it earlier. On 14 December 1877 he received German patent No. 2355 for an electromechanical "dynamic" or moving-coil transducer, which was adapted by A. L. Thuras and E. C. Wente for the <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> in the late 1920s for use as a <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Wente's adaptation was issued US patent 1,707,545 in 1929. Siemens is also the father of the <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">trolleybus</a> which he initially tried and tested with his "<a href="/wiki/Elektromote" class="mw-redirect" title="Elektromote">Elektromote</a>" on 29 April 1882.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was married twice, first in 1852 to Mathilde Duman (died 1 July 1867) and second in 1869 to his relative Antonie Siemens (1840–1900). His children from first marriage were <a href="/wiki/Arnold_von_Siemens" title="Arnold von Siemens">Arnold von Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_von_Siemens" title="Georg Wilhelm von Siemens">Georg Wilhelm von Siemens</a>, and his children from second marriage were Hertha von Siemens (1870 - 5 January 1939), married in 1899 to <a href="/wiki/Carl_Dietrich_Harries" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Dietrich Harries">Carl Dietrich Harries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_von_Siemens" title="Carl Friedrich von Siemens">Carl Friedrich von Siemens</a>.</p>
<p>Siemens was an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">Social Democracy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and he hoped that <a href="/wiki/Industrial_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial development">industrial development</a> would not be used in favour of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, stating:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A number of great factories in the hands of rich capitalists, in which "slaves of work" drag out their miserable existence, is not, therefore, the goal of the development of the age of natural science, but a return to individual labour, or where the nature of things demands it, the carrying on of common workshops by unions of workmen, who will receive a sound basis only through the general extension of knowledge and civilization, and through the possibility of obtaining cheaper capital.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He also rejected the claim that science lead to <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a>, stating instead:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Equally unfounded is the complaint that the study of science and the technical application of the forces of nature gives to mankind a thoroughly material direction, makes them proud of their knowledge and power, and alienates ideal endeavours. The deeper we penetrate into the harmonious action of natural forces regulated by eternal unalterable laws, and yet so thickly veiled from our complete comprehension, the more we feel on the contrary moved to humble modesty, the smaller appears to us the extent of our knowledge, the more active is our endeavour to draw more from the inexhaustible fountain of knowledge, and understanding, and the higher rises our admiration of the endless wisdom which ordains and penetrates the whole creation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patents">Patents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_von_Siemens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Patents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
