			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cradle-to-cradle design</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Cradle to Cradle" redirects here. For the 2002 book, see <a href="/wiki/Cradle_to_Cradle:_Remaking_the_Way_We_Make_Things" title="Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things">Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things</a>.</div>
<p><b>Cradle-to-cradle design</b> (also referred to as <b>Cradle to Cradle</b>, <b>C2C</b>, <b>cradle 2 cradle</b>, or <b><a href="/wiki/Regenerative_design" title="Regenerative design">regenerative design</a></b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Biomimetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomimetic">biomimetic</a> approach to the design of products and systems. It models human industry on nature's processes viewing materials as nutrients circulating in healthy, safe <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolisms</a>. It suggests that industry must protect and enrich <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and nature's biological metabolism while also maintaining a safe, productive technical metabolism for the high-quality use and circulation of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> and technical nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-Cradle_2002_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle_2002-1">[1]</a></sup> Put simply, it is a <a href="/wiki/Holistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic">holistic</a> economic, industrial and social framework that seeks to create systems that are not only efficient but also essentially waste free.<sup id="cite_ref-hunter_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hunter-2">[2]</a></sup> The model in its broadest sense is not limited to <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>; it can be applied to many aspects of human civilization such as <a href="/wiki/Urban_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban environment">urban environments</a>, buildings, economics and <a href="/wiki/Social_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Social systems">social systems</a>.</p>
<p>The term Cradle to Cradle is a registered trademark of McDonough Braungart Design Chemistry (MBDC) consultants. Cradle to Cradle product certification began as a proprietary system; however, in 2012 MBDC turned the certification over to an independent non-profit called the Cradle to Cradle Products Innovation Institute. Independence, openness, and transparency are the Institute's first objectives for the certification protocols.<sup id="cite_ref-treehugger.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-treehugger.com-3">[3]</a></sup> The phrase "cradle to cradle" itself was coined by <a href="/wiki/Walter_R._Stahel" title="Walter R. Stahel">Walter R. Stahel</a> in the 1970s. The current model is based on a system of "lifecycle development" initiated by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Braungart" title="Michael Braungart">Michael Braungart</a> and colleagues at the <i>Environmental Protection Encouragement Agency</i> (EPEA) in the 1990s and explored through the publication <i>A Technical Framework for <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life-Cycle Assessment</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2002, Braungart and <a href="/wiki/William_McDonough" title="William McDonough">William McDonough</a> published a book called <i><a href="/wiki/Cradle_to_Cradle:_Remaking_the_Way_We_Make_Things" title="Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things">Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a> for cradle to cradle design that gives specific details of how to achieve the model. The model has been implemented by a number of companies, organizations and governments around the world, predominantly in the European Union, China and the United States. Cradle to cradle has also been the subject of many documentary films, including the critically acclaimed <i>Waste=Food</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the cradle to cradle model, all materials used in industrial or commercial processes—such as metals, <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fibers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a>—fall into one of two categories: "technical" or "biological" nutrients. <i>Technical nutrients</i> are strictly limited to non-toxic, non-harmful synthetic materials that have no negative effects on the natural environment; they can be used in continuous cycles as the same product without losing their integrity or quality. In this manner these materials can be used over and over again instead of being "<a href="/wiki/Downcycling" title="Downcycling">downcycled</a>" into lesser products, ultimately becoming waste.</p>
<p><i>Biological Nutrients</i> are <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic material">organic materials</a> that, once used, can be disposed of in any natural environment and decompose into the soil, providing food for small life forms without affecting the natural environment. This is dependent on the ecology of the region; for example, organic material from one country or landmass may be harmful to the ecology of another country or landmass.<sup id="cite_ref-Cradle_2002_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle_2002-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Biological_and_technical_nutrients_(C2C).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg/220px-Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg/330px-Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg/440px-Biological_and_technical_nutrients_%28C2C%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2347" data-file-height="1693" /></a>

<p>The two types of materials each follow their own cycle in the regenerative economy envisioned by Keunen and Huizing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Initially defined by McDonough and Braungart, the Cradle to Cradle Products Innovation Institute's five certification criteria are:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Material health, which involves identifying the chemical composition of the materials that make up the product. Particularly hazardous materials (e.g. heavy metals, pigments, halogen compounds etc.) have to be reported whatever the concentration, and other materials reported where they exceed 100 ppm. For wood, the forest source is required. The risk for each material is assessed against criteria and eventually ranked on a scale with green being materials of low risk, yellow being those with moderate risk but are acceptable to continue to use, and red for materials that have high risk and need to be phased out. Grey for materials with incomplete data. The method uses the term 'risk' in the sense of hazard (as opposed to consequence and likelihood).</li>
<li>Material reutilization, which is about recovery and recycling at the end of product life.</li>
<li>Assessment of energy required for production, which for the highest level of certification needs to be based on at least 50% renewable energy for all parts and subassemblies.</li>
<li>Water, particularly usage and discharge quality.</li>
<li>Social responsibility, which assesses fair labor practices.</li>
</ul>
<p>The certification is available at several levels: basic, silver, gold, platinum, with more stringent requirements at each. Prior to 2012, MBDC controlled the certification protocol.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, many human beings come into contact or consume, directly or indirectly, many harmful materials and chemicals daily. In addition, countless other forms of plant and animal life are also exposed. C2C seeks to remove dangerous <i>technical nutrients</i> (synthetic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Mutagenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutagenic">mutagenic</a> materials, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> and other dangerous chemicals) from current life cycles. If the materials we come into contact with and are exposed to on a daily basis are not toxic and do not have long term health effects, then the health of the overall system can be better maintained. For example, a fabric factory can eliminate all harmful <i>technical nutrients</i> by carefully reconsidering what chemicals they use in their dyes to achieve the colours they need and attempt to do so with fewer base chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-Cradle_2002_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle_2002-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of a C2C model often lowers the financial cost of systems. For example, in the redesign of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_River_Rouge_Complex" title="Ford River Rouge Complex">Ford River Rouge Complex</a>, the planting of Sedum (stonecrop) vegetation on assembly plant roofs retains and cleanses rain water. It also moderates the internal temperature of the building in order to save energy. The roof is part of an $18 million rainwater treatment system designed to clean 20&#160;billion US gallons (76,000,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of rainwater annually. This saved Ford $50 million that would otherwise have been spent on mechanical treatment facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> If products are designed according to C2C design principles, they can be manufactured and sold for less than alternative designs. They eliminate the need for waste disposal such as landfills.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Cradle to Cradle</b> a play on the phrase "Cradle to Grave", implying that the C2C model is sustainable and considerate of life and future generations.</li>
<li><b>Technical nutrients</b> are basically <a href="/wiki/Inorganic" class="mw-redirect" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a> or synthetic materials manufactured by humans—such as plastics and metals—that can be used many times over without any loss in quality, staying in a continuous cycle.</li>
<li><b>Biological nutrients</b> and materials are <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic material">organic materials</a> that can decompose into the natural environment, soil, water, etc. without affecting it in a negative way, providing food for bacteria and microbiological life.</li>
<li><b>Materials</b> are usually referred to as the building blocks of other materials, such as the dyes used in colouring fibers or rubbers used in the sole of a shoe.</li>
<li><b>Downcycling</b> is the reuse of materials into lesser products. For example, a plastic computer case could be downcycled into a plastic cup, which then becomes a park bench, etc.; this may eventually lead to waste. In conventional understanding, this is no different from <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> that produces a supply of the same product or material.</li>
<li><b>Waste = Food</b> is a basic concept of organic waste materials becoming food for bugs, insects and other small forms of life who can feed on it, decompose it and return it to the natural environment which we then indirectly use for food ourselves.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Existing_synthetic_materials">Existing synthetic materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Existing synthetic materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The question of how to deal with the countless existing <i>technical nutrients</i> (synthetic materials) that cannot be recycled or reintroduced to the natural environment is dealt with in C2C design. The materials that can be reused and retain their quality can be used within the technical nutrient cycles while other materials are far more difficult to deal with, such as plastics in the Pacific Ocean.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothetical_examples">Hypothetical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hypothetical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One effective example is a shoe that is designed and mass-produced using the C2C model. The sole might be made of "biological nutrients" while the upper parts might be made of "technical nutrients." The shoe is mass-produced at a manufacturing plant that utilises its waste material by putting it back into the cycle; an example of this is using off-cuts from the rubber soles to make more soles instead of merely disposing of them (this is dependent on the technical materials not losing their quality as they are reused). Once the shoes have been manufactured, they are distributed to retail outlets where the customer buys the shoe at a fraction of the price they would normally pay for a shoe of comparable aspects; the customer is only paying for the use of the materials in the shoe for the period of time that they will be using the shoe. When they outgrow the shoe or it is damaged, they return it to the manufacturer. When the manufacturer separates the sole from the upper parts (separating the technical and biological nutrients), the biological nutrients are returned to the natural environment while the technical nutrients are used to create the sole of another shoe.</p>
<p>Another example of C2C design is a disposable cup, bottle, or wrapper made entirely out of biological materials. When the user is finished with the item, it can be disposed of and returned to the natural environment; the cost of disposal of waste such as landfill and recycling is eliminated. The user could also potentially return the item for a refund so it can be used again.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_U" title="Ford Model U">Ford Model U</a> is a design concept of a car, made completely from cradle-to-cradle materials. It also uses <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_propulsion" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen propulsion">hydrogen propulsion</a>.</p>

<li>Cradle-to-cradle shoes have been made through the <a href="/wiki/Nike_Considered" title="Nike Considered">Nike Considered</a> project.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The C2C model can be applied to almost any system in modern society: <a href="/wiki/Urban_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban environment">urban environments</a>, buildings, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Social systems">social systems</a>. 5 steps are outlined in <i>Cradle to Cradle – Remaking the way we make things</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-Cradle_2002_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cradle_2002-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Get "free of" known culprits</li>
<li>Follow informed personal preferences</li>
<li>Create "passive positive" lists – lists of materials used categorised according to their safety level</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The X List – substances that must be phased out, such as <a href="/wiki/Teratogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Teratogenic">teratogenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mutagenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutagenic">mutagenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a>.</dd>
<dd>The Gray List – problematic substances that are not so urgently in need of phasing out</dd>
<dd>The P List – the "positive" list, substances actively defined as safe for use</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Activate the positive list</li>
<li>Reinvent – the redesign of the former system</li>
</ul>
<p>Products that adhere to all steps can generally be granted a certification. Two certifications used for cradle-to-cradle products include <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=BRE_Environmental_Assessment_Method&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="BRE Environmental Assessment Method (page does not exist)">BRE Environmental Assessment Method</a> (BREEAM).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>C2C principles were first applied to systems in the early 1990s by Braungart's <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hamburger_Umweltinstitut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hamburger Umweltinstitut (page does not exist)">Hamburger Umweltinstitut</a> (HUI) and <a href="/wiki/The_Environmental_Institute" title="The Environmental Institute">The Environmental Institute</a> in Brazil for biomass nutrient recycling of effluent to produce agricultural products and clean water as a byproduct.</p>
<p>In 2005, William McDonough helped found the Center for Eco-Intelligent Management at Instituto de Empresa Business School. The center's research produced the <a href="/wiki/The_Biosphere_Rules" title="The Biosphere Rules">Biosphere Rules</a>, a set of five implementation principles that facilitate the adoption of closed loop production approaches with a minimum of disruption for established companies.</p>
<p>In 2007, MBDC and the EPEA formed a strategic partnership with global materials consultancy <a href="/wiki/Material_ConneXion" title="Material ConneXion">Material ConneXion</a> to help promote and disseminate C2C design principles by providing greater global access to C2C material information, certification and product development.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of January 2008, Material ConneXion's Materials Libraries in New York, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daegu" title="Daegu">Daegu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> started to feature C2C assessed and certified materials and, in collaboration with MBDC and EPEA, the company now offers C2C Certification, and C2C product development.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the C2C model has influenced the construction or redevelopment of many smaller buildings, several large companies, organisations and governments have also implemented the C2C model and its ideas and concepts:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_with_other_models">Coordination with other models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coordination with other models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cradle to Cradle model can be viewed as a framework that considers systems as a whole or <a href="/wiki/Holistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic">holistically</a>. It can be applied to many aspects of human society, and is related to <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle assessment">Life cycle assessment</a>. See for instance the LCA based model of the <a href="/wiki/Eco-costs" title="Eco-costs">Eco-costs</a>, which has been designed to cope with analyses of recycle systems.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Cradle to Cradle model in some implementations is closely linked with the <a href="/wiki/Car-free_movement" title="Car-free movement">Car-free movement</a>, such as in the case of large-scale building projects or the construction or redevelopment of urban environments. It is closely linked with <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_design" class="mw-redirect" title="Passive solar design">passive solar design</a> in the building industry and with <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> in agriculture within or near urban environments. An <a href="/wiki/Earthship" title="Earthship">earthship</a> is a perfect example where different re-use models are used, cradle to cradle and permaculture.</p>
<p>In 2005, IE Business School in Madrid launched the Center for Eco-Intelligent Innovation in collaboration with William McDonough to study the implementation of Cradle to Cradle design approaches in pioneering businesses. The academic research of companies lead to the elaboration of the <a href="/wiki/The_Biosphere_Rules" title="The Biosphere Rules">Biosphere Rules</a>, a set of five principles derived from nature that guide the implementation of circular models in production.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constraints">Constraints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Constraints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A major constraint in the optimal recycling of materials is that at civic amenity sites, products are not disassembled by hand and have each individual part sorted into a bin, but instead have the entire product sorted into a certain bin.</p>
<p>This makes the extraction of rare earth elements and other materials uneconomical (at recycling sites, products typically get crushed after which the materials are extracted by means of magnets, chemicals, special sorting methods, ...) and thus optimal recycling of, for example metals is impossible (an optimal recycling method for metals would require to sort all similar alloys together rather than mixing plain iron with alloys).</p>
<p>Obviously, disassembling products is not feasible at currently designed civic amenity sites, and a better method would be to send back the broken products to the manufacturer, so that the manufacturer can disassemble the product. These disassembled product can then be used for making new products or at least to have the components sent separately to recycling sites (for proper recycling, by the exact type of material). At present though, few laws are put in place in any country to oblige manufacturers to <a href="/wiki/Ecoleasing" title="Ecoleasing">take back their products for disassembly</a>, nor are there even such obligations for manufacturers of cradle-to-cradle products. One process where this is happening is in the EU with the <a href="/wiki/Waste_Electrical_and_Electronic_Equipment_Directive" title="Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive">Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_response">Criticism and response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cradle-to-cradle_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism and response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Criticism has been advanced<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> on the fact that McDonough and Braungart previously kept C2C consultancy and certification in their inner circle. Critics argued that this lack of competition prevented the model from fulfilling its potential. Many critics pleaded for a public-private partnership overseeing the C2C concept, thus enabling competition and growth of practical applications and services.</p>
<p>McDonough and Braungart responded to this criticism by giving control of the certification protocol to a non-profit, independent Institute called the Cradle to Cradle Products Innovation Institute. McDonough said the new institute "will enable our protocol to become a public certification program and global standard."<sup id="cite_ref-treehugger.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-treehugger.com-3">[3]</a></sup> The new Institute announced the creation of a Certification Standards Board in June 2012. The new board, under the auspices of the Institute, will oversee the certification moving forward.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experts in the field of environment protection have questioned the practicability of the concept. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friedrich_Schmidt-Bleek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Friedrich Schmidt-Bleek (page does not exist)">Friedrich Schmidt-Bleek</a>, head of the German <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Wuppertal Institute">Wuppertal Institute</a> called his assertion, that the "old" environmental movement had hindered innovation with its pessimist approach "pseudo-psychological humbug".</p>
<blockquote style="font-style: italic">
<p><i>I can feel very nice on Michael's seat covers<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in the airplane. Nevertheless I am still waiting for a detailed proposal for a design of the other 99.99 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_380" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus 380">Airbus 380</a> after his principles.</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 2009 Schmidt-Bleek stated that it is out of the question that the concept can be realized on a bigger scale.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some claim that C2C certification may not be entirely sufficient in all <a href="/wiki/Eco-design" class="mw-redirect" title="Eco-design">eco-design</a> approaches. Quantitative methodologies (LCAs) and more adapted tools (regarding the product type which is considered) could be used in tandem. The C2C concept ignores the use phase of a product. According to <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment#Variants" title="Life-cycle assessment">the Variants of Life Cycle Assessment</a> the entire life cycle of a product or service has to be evaluated, not only the material itself. For many goods e.g. in transport, the use phase has the most influence on the environmental footprint. E.g. the more lightweight a car or a plane the less fuel it consumes and consequently the less impact it has. Braungart fully ignores the use phase.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is safe to say that every production step or resource-transformation step needs a certain amount of energy.</p>
<p>The C2C concept foresees an own certification of its analysis<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and therefore is in contradiction to international ISO standards <a href="/w/index.php?title=SO_14000&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="SO 14000 (page does not exist)">14040 and 14044</a> for <a href="/wiki/Life_Cycle_Assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Life Cycle Assessment">Life Cycle Assessment</a> whereas an independent and critical review is needed in order to obtain comparative and resilient results. Independent external review.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
