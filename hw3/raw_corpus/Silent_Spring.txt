<p><i><b>Silent Spring</b></i> is an environmental science book by <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-1">[1]</a></sup> The book was published on 27 September 1962 and it documented the detrimental effects on the environment of the indiscriminate use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>. Carson accused the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemical industry</a> of spreading <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a> and public officials of accepting industry <a href="/wiki/Marketing_claim" class="mw-redirect" title="Marketing claim">claims</a> unquestioningly.</p>
<p>In the late 1950s, Carson turned her attention to <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a>, especially environmental problems that she believed were caused by synthetic pesticides. The result was <i>Silent Spring</i> (1962), which brought environmental concerns to the American public. <i>Silent Spring</i> was met with fierce opposition by chemical companies, but it spurred a reversal in national pesticide policy, led to a nationwide ban on <a href="/wiki/DDT" class="mw-redirect" title="DDT">DDT</a> for agricultural uses,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and inspired an environmental movement that led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CarsonPaull_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarsonPaull-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, a follow-up book, <i>Beyond Silent Spring</i>, co-written by H.F. van Emden and <a href="/wiki/David_Peakall" title="David Peakall">David Peakall</a>, was published.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman_.26_Hall_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman_.26_Hall-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_H_269.E2.80.93270_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_H_269.E2.80.93270-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2006, <i>Silent Spring</i> was named one of the 25 greatest science books of all time by the editors of <i><a href="/wiki/Discover_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Discover Magazine">Discover Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-discovermagazine.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discovermagazine.com-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_writing">Research and writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Research and writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rachel-Carson.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Rachel-Carson.jpg/200px-Rachel-Carson.jpg" width="200" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Rachel-Carson.jpg/300px-Rachel-Carson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Rachel-Carson.jpg/400px-Rachel-Carson.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1117" data-file-height="1414" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rachel-Carson.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rachel Carson, 1940<br />
<a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="United States Fish and Wildlife Service">Fish and Wildlife Service</a> employee photo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the mid-1940s, Carson became concerned about the use of synthetic pesticides, many of which had been developed through the <a href="/wiki/Military_funding_of_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Military funding of science">military funding of science</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The United States Department of Agriculture's 1957 <a href="/wiki/Fire_ant" title="Fire ant">fire ant</a> eradication program, which involved aerial spraying of <a href="/wiki/DDT" class="mw-redirect" title="DDT">DDT</a> and other pesticides mixed with <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a> and included the spraying of private land, prompted Carson to devote her research, and her next book, to pesticides and environmental poisons.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Landowners in Long Island filed a suit to have the spraying stopped, and many in affected regions followed the case closely.<sup id="cite_ref-CarsonPaull_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarsonPaull-3">[3]</a></sup> Though the suit was lost, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> granted petitioners the right to gain injunctions against potential environmental damage in the future, laying the basis for later environmental actions.<sup id="cite_ref-CarsonPaull_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarsonPaull-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The impetus for <i>Silent Spring</i> was a letter written in January 1958 by Carson's friend, Olga Owens Huckins, to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Herald" class="mw-redirect" title="The Boston Herald">The Boston Herald</a></i>, describing the death of birds around her property resulting from the aerial spraying of DDT to kill mosquitoes, a copy of which Huckins sent to Carson.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Himaras07_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Himaras07-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Himaras07_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Himaras07-13">[13]</a></sup> Carson later wrote that this letter prompted her to study the environmental problems caused by chemical pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Audubon_Naturalist_Society" title="Audubon Naturalist Society">Washington, D.C. chapter of the Audubon Society</a> actively opposed chemical spraying programs and recruited Carson to help publicize the U.S. government's spraying practices and related research.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Carson began the four-year project of <i>Silent Spring</i> by gathering examples of environmental damage attributed to DDT. She tried to enlist essayist <a href="/wiki/E._B._White" title="E. B. White">E. B. White</a> and a number of journalists and scientists to her cause. By 1958, Carson had arranged a book deal, with plans to co-write with <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> science journalist Edwin Diamond. However, when <i>The New Yorker</i> commissioned a long and well-paid article on the topic from Carson, she began considering writing more than the introduction and conclusion as planned; soon it became a solo project. Diamond would later write one of the harshest critiques of <i>Silent Spring</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As her research progressed, Carson found a sizable community of scientists who were documenting the physiological and environmental effects of pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-CarsonPaull_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarsonPaull-3">[3]</a></sup> She took advantage of her personal connections with many government scientists, who supplied her with confidential information on the subject. From reading the scientific literature and interviewing scientists, Carson found two scientific camps; those who dismissed the possible danger of pesticide spraying barring conclusive proof and those who were open to the possibility of harm and were willing to consider alternative methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological pest control</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1959, the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Research_Service" title="Agricultural Research Service">Agricultural Research Service</a> responded to the criticism by Carson and others with a public service film, <i>Fire Ants on Trial</i>; Carson called it "flagrant propaganda" that ignored the dangers that spraying pesticides posed to humans and wildlife. That spring, Carson wrote a letter, published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, that attributed the recent decline in bird populations—in her words, the "silencing of birds"—to pesticide overuse.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The same year, the 1957, 1958, and 1959 crops of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberries</a> were found to contain high levels of the herbicide <a href="/wiki/Aminotriazole" class="mw-redirect" title="Aminotriazole">aminotriazole</a> and the sale of all cranberry products was halted. Carson attended the ensuing FDA hearings on revising pesticide regulations; she was discouraged by the aggressive tactics of the chemical industry representatives, which included expert testimony that was firmly contradicted by the bulk of the scientific literature she had been studying. She also wondered about the possible "financial inducements behind certain pesticide programs".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">Library of Medicine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> brought Carson into contact with medical researchers investigating the gamut of cancer-causing chemicals. Of particular significance was the work of <a href="/wiki/National_Cancer_Institute" title="National Cancer Institute">National Cancer Institute</a> researcher and founding director of the environmental cancer section <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hueper" title="Wilhelm Hueper">Wilhelm Hueper</a>, who classified many pesticides as <a href="/wiki/Carcinogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Carcinogens">carcinogens</a>. Carson and her research assistant Jeanne Davis, with the help of NIH librarian Dorothy Algire, found evidence to support the pesticide-cancer connection; to Carson the evidence for the toxicity of a wide array of synthetic pesticides was clear-cut, though such conclusions were very controversial beyond the small community of scientists studying pesticide <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenesis" title="Carcinogenesis">carcinogenesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1960, Carson had sufficient research material and the writing was progressing rapidly. She had investigated hundreds of individual incidents of pesticide exposure and the resulting human sickness and ecological damage. In January 1960, she suffered an illness which kept her bedridden for weeks, delaying the book. As she was nearing full recovery in March, she discovered cysts in her left breast, requiring a <a href="/wiki/Mastectomy" title="Mastectomy">mastectomy</a>. By December that year, Carson discovered that she had breast cancer, which had <a href="/wiki/Metastasized" class="mw-redirect" title="Metastasized">metastasized</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Her research was also delayed by revision work for a new edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sea_Around_Us" title="The Sea Around Us">The Sea Around Us</a></i>, and by a collaborative photo essay with <a href="/wiki/Erich_Hartmann_(photographer)" title="Erich Hartmann (photographer)">Erich Hartmann</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Most of the research and writing was done by the fall of 1960, except for a discussion of recent research on <a href="/wiki/Biological_controls" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological controls">biological controls</a> and investigations of some new pesticides. However, further health troubles delayed the final revisions in 1961 and early 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its title was inspired by a poem by <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>, "<a href="/wiki/La_Belle_Dame_sans_Merci" title="La Belle Dame sans Merci">La Belle Dame sans Merci</a>", which contained the lines "The sedge is wither'd from the lake, And no birds sing."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> "Silent Spring" was initially suggested as a title for the chapter on birds. By August 1961, Carson agreed to the suggestion of her literary agent Marie Rodell: <i>Silent Spring</i> would be a metaphorical title for the entire book—suggesting a bleak future for the whole natural world—rather than a literal chapter title about the absence of birdsong.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> With Carson's approval, editor Paul Brooks at <a href="/wiki/Houghton_Mifflin" class="mw-redirect" title="Houghton Mifflin">Houghton Mifflin</a> arranged for illustrations by Louis and Lois Darling, who also designed the cover. The final writing was the first chapter, "A Fable for Tomorrow", which was intended to provide a gentle introduction to a serious topic. By mid-1962, Brooks and Carson had largely finished the editing and were planning to promote the book by sending the manuscript to select individuals for final suggestions.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In Silent Spring, Carson relied on evidence from two New York state organic farmers, <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Spock" title="Marjorie Spock">Marjorie Spock</a> and Mary Richards, and that of <a href="/wiki/Biodynamic_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodynamic farming">biodynamic farming</a> advocate <a href="/wiki/Ehrenfried_Pfeiffer" title="Ehrenfried Pfeiffer">Ehrenfried Pfeiffer</a> in developing her case against DDT.<sup id="cite_ref-CarsonPaull_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CarsonPaull-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The overriding theme of <i>Silent Spring</i> is the powerful—and often negative—effect humans have on the natural world.<sup id="cite_ref-Lytle_2007_166.E2.80.937_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lytle_2007_166.E2.80.937-28">[28]</a></sup> Carson's main argument is that pesticides have detrimental effects on the environment; she says these are more properly termed "<a href="/wiki/Biocide" title="Biocide">biocides</a>" because their effects are rarely limited to the target pests. DDT is a prime example, but other synthetic pesticides—many of which are subject to <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a>—are scrutinized. Carson accuses the chemical industry of intentionally spreading disinformation and public officials of accepting industry claims uncritically. Most of the book is devoted to pesticides' effects on natural ecosystems, but four chapters detail cases of human pesticide poisoning, cancer, and other illnesses attributed to pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> About DDT and cancer, Carson says only:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In laboratory tests on animal subjects, DDT has produced suspicious liver tumors. Scientists of the Food and Drug Administration who reported the discovery of these tumors were uncertain how to classify them, but felt there was some "justification for considering them low grade hepatic cell carcinomas." Dr. Hueper [author of <i>Occupational Tumors and Allied Diseases</i>] now gives DDT the definite rating of a "chemical carcinogen."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Carson predicts increased consequences in the future, especially since targeted pests may develop <a href="/wiki/Resistance_to_pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Resistance to pesticides">resistance to pesticides</a> and weakened ecosystems fall prey to unanticipated invasive species. The book closes with a call for a <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic</a> approach to pest control as an alternative to chemical pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carson never called for an outright ban on DDT. She said in <i>Silent Spring</i> that even if DDT and other insecticides had no environmental side effects, their indiscriminate overuse was counterproductive because it would create insect resistance to pesticides, making them useless in eliminating the target insect populations:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>No responsible person contends that insect-borne disease should be ignored. The question that has now urgently presented itself is whether it is either wise or responsible to attack the problem by methods that are rapidly making it worse. The world has heard much of the triumphant war against disease through the control of insect vectors of infection, but it has heard little of the other side of the story—the defeats, the short-lived triumphs that now strongly support the alarming view that the insect enemy has been made actually stronger by our efforts. Even worse, we may have destroyed our very means of fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Carson also said that "Malaria programmes are threatened by resistance among mosquitoes",<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and quoted the advice given by the director of Holland's Plant Protection Service: "Practical advice should be 'Spray as little as you possibly can' rather than 'Spray to the limit of your capacity'. Pressure on the pest population should always be as slight as possible."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_and_reception">Promotion and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Promotion and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carson and the others involved with publication of <i>Silent Spring</i> expected fierce criticism and were concerned about the possibility of being sued for <a href="/wiki/Libel" class="mw-redirect" title="Libel">libel</a>. Carson was undergoing <a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">radiation therapy</a> for her cancer and expected to have little energy to defend her work and respond to critics. In preparation for the anticipated attacks, Carson and her agent attempted to amass prominent supporters before the book's release.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the book's scientific chapters were reviewed by scientists with relevant expertise, among whom Carson found strong support. Carson attended the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> Conference on Conservation in May 1962; Houghton Mifflin distributed proof copies of <i>Silent Spring</i> to many of the delegates and promoted the upcoming serialization in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>. Carson also sent a proof copy to Supreme Court Associate Justice <a href="/wiki/William_O._Douglas" title="William O. Douglas">William O. Douglas</a>, a long-time environmental advocate who had argued against the court's rejection of the Long Island pesticide spraying case and had provided Carson with some of the material included in her chapter on herbicides.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though <i>Silent Spring</i> had generated a fairly high level of interest based on pre-publication promotion, this became more intense with its serialization, which began in the June 16, 1962, issue. This brought the book to the attention of the chemical industry and its lobbyists, as well as the American public. Around that time, Carson learned that <i>Silent Spring</i> had been selected as the <a href="/wiki/Book-of-the-Month" class="mw-redirect" title="Book-of-the-Month">Book-of-the-Month</a> for October; she said this would "carry it to farms and hamlets all over that country that don't know what a bookstore looks like—much less <i>The New Yorker</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Other publicity included a positive editorial in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and excerpts of the serialized version were published in <i>Audubon Magazine</i>. There was another round of publicity in July and August as chemical companies responded. The story of the birth defect-causing drug <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a> had broken just before the book's publication, inviting comparisons between Carson and <a href="/wiki/Frances_Oldham_Kelsey" title="Frances Oldham Kelsey">Frances Oldham Kelsey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> reviewer who had blocked the drug's sale in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG/220px-Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG/330px-Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG/440px-Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="531" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Spring_Book-of-the-Month-Club_edition.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Book-of-the-Month_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Book-of-the-Month Club">Book-of-the-Month Club</a> edition of <i>Silent Spring</i>, including an endorsement by Justice Douglas, had a first print run of 150,000 copies, two-and-a-half times the combined size of the two conventional printings of the initial release<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the weeks before the September 27, 1962, publication, there was strong opposition to <i>Silent Spring</i> from the chemical industry. <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, a major manufacturer of DDT and <a href="/wiki/2,4-D" class="mw-redirect" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Velsicol_Chemical_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Velsicol Chemical Company">Velsicol Chemical Company</a>, the only manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Chlordane" title="Chlordane">chlordane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heptachlor" title="Heptachlor">heptachlor</a>, were among the first to respond. DuPont compiled an extensive report on the book's press coverage and estimated impact on public opinion. Velsicol threatened legal action against Houghton Mifflin, and <i>The New Yorker</i> and <i>Audubon Magazine</i> unless their planned <i>Silent Spring</i> features were canceled. Chemical industry representatives and lobbyists lodged a range of non-specific complaints, some anonymously. Chemical companies and associated organizations produced brochures and articles promoting and defending pesticide use. However, Carson's and the publishers' lawyers were confident in the vetting process <i>Silent Spring</i> had undergone. The magazine and book publications proceeded as planned, as did the large Book-of-the-Month printing, which included a pamphlet by William O. Douglas endorsing the book.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_Cyanamid" title="American Cyanamid">American Cyanamid</a> biochemist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_White-Stevens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert White-Stevens (page does not exist)">Robert White-Stevens</a> and former Cyanamid chemist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_H._Jukes" title="Thomas H. Jukes">Thomas Jukes</a> were among the most aggressive critics, especially of Carson's analysis of DDT.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> According to White-Stevens, "If man were to follow the teachings of Miss Carson, we would return to the Dark Ages, and the insects and diseases and vermin would once again inherit the earth".<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-1">[1]</a></sup> Others attacked Carson's personal character and scientific credentials, her training being in marine biology rather than biochemistry. White-Stevens called her "a fanatic defender of the cult of the balance of nature",<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> while former U.S. Secretary of Agriculture <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Taft_Benson" title="Ezra Taft Benson">Ezra Taft Benson</a> in a letter to former President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> reportedly said that because she was unmarried despite being physically attractive, she was "probably a Communist".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many critics repeatedly said Carson was calling for the elimination of all pesticides, but she had made it clear she was not advocating this but was instead encouraging responsible and carefully managed use with an awareness of the chemicals' impact on ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> She concludes her section on DDT in <i>Silent Spring</i> with advice for spraying as little as possible to limit the development of resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Hamilton_Lytle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark Hamilton Lytle (page does not exist)">Mark Hamilton Lytle</a> writes, Carson "quite self-consciously decided to write a book calling into question the <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_progress" title="Scientific progress">scientific progress</a> that defined <a href="/wiki/Postwar" class="mw-redirect" title="Postwar">postwar</a> American culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Lytle_2007_166.E2.80.937_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lytle_2007_166.E2.80.937-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The academic community—including prominent defenders such as <a href="/wiki/H._J._Muller" class="mw-redirect" title="H. J. Muller">H. J. Muller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loren_Eiseley" title="Loren Eiseley">Loren Eiseley</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clarence_Cottam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clarence Cottam (page does not exist)">Clarence Cottam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Egler" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Egler">Frank Egler</a>—mostly backed the book's scientific claims and public opinion backed Carson's text. The chemical industry campaign was counterproductive because the controversy increased public awareness of the potential dangers of pesticides. Pesticide use became a major public issue after a <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_Reports" title="CBS Reports">CBS Reports</a></i> television special, "<a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Silent_Spring_of_Rachel_Carson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Silent Spring of Rachel Carson (page does not exist)">The Silent Spring of Rachel Carson</a>", which was broadcast on April 3, 1963. The program included segments of Carson reading from <i>Silent Spring</i> and interviews with other experts, mostly critics including White-Stevens. According to biographer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Lear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Linda Lear (page does not exist)">Linda Lear</a>, "in juxtaposition to the wild-eyed, loud-voiced Dr. Robert White-Stevens in white lab coat, Carson appeared anything but the hysterical alarmist that her critics contended".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Reactions from the estimated audience of ten to fifteen million were overwhelmingly positive and the program spurred a congressional review of pesticide hazards and the public release of a pesticide report by the <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Science_Advisory_Committee" title="President's Science Advisory Committee">President's Science Advisory Committee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Within a year of publication, attacks on the book and on Carson had lost momentum.<sup id="cite_ref-time100_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time100-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In one of her last public appearances, Carson testified before President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>'s Science Advisory Committee, which issued its report on May 15, 1963, largely backing Carson's scientific claims.<sup id="cite_ref-nwhp_bio_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwhp_bio-50">[50]</a></sup> Following the report's release, Carson also testified before a <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> subcommittee to make policy recommendations. Though Carson received hundreds of other speaking invitations, she was unable to accept most of them because her health was steadily declining, with only brief periods of remission. She spoke as much as she could, and appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Today (NBC program)">The Today Show</a></i> and gave speeches at several dinners held in her honor. In late 1963, she received a flurry of awards and honors: the <a href="/wiki/Audubon_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Audubon Medal">Audubon Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/National_Audubon_Society" title="National Audubon Society">National Audubon Society</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cullum_Geographical_Medal" title="Cullum Geographical Medal">Cullum Geographical Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/American_Geographical_Society" title="American Geographical Society">American Geographical Society</a>, and induction into the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Letters" title="American Academy of Arts and Letters">American Academy of Arts and Letters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries_and_languages">Other countries and languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other countries and languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book has been translated into German (under the title: <i>Der stumme Frühling</i>), with the first German edition appearing in 1963, followed by a number of later editions.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was translated into French (as <i>Le printemps silencieux</i>), with the first French edition also appearing in 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965 <i>Silent Spring</i> had been published in Russian (under the title <i><span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Безмолвная весна</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book's Italian title is <i>Primavera silenziosa</i>.;<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and the Spanish title is <i>Primavera silenciosa</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grassroots_environmentalism_and_the_EPA">Grassroots environmentalism and the EPA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grassroots environmentalism and the EPA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carson's work had a powerful impact on the environmental movement. <i>Silent Spring</i> became a rallying point for the new social movement in the 1960s. According to environmental engineer and Carson scholar H. Patricia Hynes, "<i>Silent Spring</i> altered the balance of power in the world. No one since would be able to sell pollution as the necessary underside of progress so easily or uncritically."<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Carson's work and the activism it inspired are partly responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> movement and the strength of the grassroots environmental movement since the 1960s. It was also influential on the rise of <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a> and on many feminist scientists.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Carson's most direct legacy in the environmental movement was the campaign to ban the use of DDT in the United States, and related efforts to ban or limit its use throughout the world. The 1967 formation of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defense_Fund" title="Environmental Defense Fund">Environmental Defense Fund</a> was the first major milestone in the campaign against DDT. The organization brought lawsuits against the government to "establish a citizen's right to a clean environment", and the arguments against DDT largely mirrored Carson's. By 1972, the Environmental Defense Fund and other activist groups had succeeded in securing a phase-out of DDT use in the United States, except in emergency cases.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>The creation of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Nixon_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Nixon Administration">Nixon Administration</a> in 1970 addressed another concern that Carson had written about. Until then, the USDA was responsible both for regulating pesticides and promoting the concerns of the agriculture industry; Carson saw this as a <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a>, since the agency was not responsible for effects on wildlife or other environmental concerns beyond farm policy. Fifteen years after its creation, one journalist described the EPA as "the extended shadow of <i>Silent Spring</i>". Much of the agency's early work, such as enforcement of the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Federal_Insecticide,_Fungicide,_and_Rodenticide_Act" title="Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act">Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act</a>, was directly related to Carson's work.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Contrary to the position of the pesticide industry, the DDT phase-out action taken by the EPA (led by William Ruckelhaus) implied that there was no way to adequately regulate DDT use. Ruckelhaus' conclusion was that DDT could not be used safely.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> History professor Gary Kroll wrote, "Rachel Carson's <i>Silent Spring</i> played a large role in articulating ecology as a 'subversive subject'—as a perspective that cuts against the grain of materialism, <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">scientism</a>, and the technologically engineered control of nature."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2013 interview, Ruckelshaus briefly recounted his decision to ban DDT except for emergency uses, noting that Carson’s book featured DDT and for that reason the issue drew considerable public attention. <sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Former Vice President of the United States and environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> wrote an introduction to the 1992 edition of <i>Silent Spring</i>. He wrote: "<i>Silent Spring</i> had a profound impact&#160;...&#160;Indeed, Rachel Carson was one of the reasons that I became so conscious of the environment and so involved with environmental issues &#160;...&#160; [she] has had as much or more effect on me than any, and perhaps than all of them together."<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_environmentalism_and_DDT_restrictions">Criticisms of environmentalism and DDT restrictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticisms of environmentalism and DDT restrictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carson and the environmental movement were—and continue to be—criticized by some who argue that restrictions on the use of pesticides—specifically DDT—have caused tens of millions of needless deaths and hampered agriculture, and implicitly that Carson was responsible for inciting such restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Conservative_criticism_of_Carson_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conservative_criticism_of_Carson-66">[66]</a></sup> These arguments have been dismissed as "outrageous" by former WHO scientist Socrates Litsios. May Berenbaum, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Illinois">University of Illinois</a> entomologist, says, "to blame environmentalists who oppose DDT for more deaths than Hitler is worse than irresponsible."<sup id="cite_ref-salon_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-67">[67]</a></sup> Investigative journalist Adam Sarvana and others characterize this notion as a "myth" promoted principally by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bate" title="Roger Bate">Roger Bate</a> of the pro-DDT advocacy group <a href="/wiki/Africa_Fighting_Malaria" title="Africa Fighting Malaria">Africa Fighting Malaria</a> (AFM).<sup id="cite_ref-NRNS_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRNS-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guts_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guts-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2000s, criticism of the bans of DDT that her work prompted intensified.<sup id="cite_ref-quig_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quig-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Erik_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Erik-71">[71]</a></sup> In 2009, the libertarian <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> <a href="/wiki/Competitive_Enterprise_Institute" title="Competitive Enterprise Institute">Competitive Enterprise Institute</a> set up a website saying, "Millions of people around the world suffer the painful and often deadly effects of malaria because one person sounded a false alarm. That person is Rachel Carson."<sup id="cite_ref-Erik_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Erik-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Slate_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slate-72">[72]</a></sup> A 2012 review article in <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a> by Rob Dunn<sup id="cite_ref-dunn_2012_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dunn_2012-73">[73]</a></sup> commemorating the 50th anniversary of <i>Silent Spring</i> prompted a response in a letter written by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Trewavas" title="Anthony Trewavas">Anthony Trewavas</a> and co-signed by 10 others, including <a href="/wiki/Chris_J._Leaver" title="Chris J. Leaver">Christopher Leaver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Ames" title="Bruce Ames">Bruce Ames</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Tren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Richard Tren (page does not exist)">Richard Tren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lachmann" title="Peter Lachmann">Peter Lachmann</a>, who quote estimates of 60 to 80 million deaths "as a result of misguided fears based on poorly understood evidence".<sup id="cite_ref-trewavas_2012_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trewavas_2012-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biographer Hamilton Lytle believes these estimates are unrealistic, even if Carson can be "blamed" for worldwide DDT policies.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Quiggin" title="John Quiggin">John Quiggin</a> and Tim Lambert wrote, "the most striking feature of the claim against Carson is the ease with which it can be refuted". DDT was never banned for anti-malarial use, and its ban for agricultural use in the United States in 1972 did not apply outside the U.S. nor to anti-malaria spraying.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The international treaty that banned most uses of DDT and other organochlorine pesticides—the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention_on_Persistent_Organic_Pollutants" title="Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants">Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants</a>—included an exemption for the use of DDT for malaria control until affordable substitutes could be found.<sup id="cite_ref-quig_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quig-70">[70]</a></sup> Mass outdoor spraying of DDT was abandoned in poor countries subject to malaria, such as Sri Lanka, in the 1970s and 1980s; this was not because of government prohibitions but because the DDT had lost its ability to kill the mosquitoes.<sup id="cite_ref-quig_70-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quig-70">[70]</a></sup> Because of insects' very short breeding cycle and large number of offspring, the most resistant insects survive and pass on their genetic traits to their offspring, which replace the pesticide-slain insects relatively rapidly. Agricultural spraying of pesticides produces <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_resistance" title="Pesticide resistance">pesticide resistance</a> in seven to ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some experts have said that restrictions placed on the agricultural use of DDT have increased its effectiveness for malaria control. According to pro-DDT advocate <a href="/wiki/Amir_Attaran" title="Amir Attaran">Amir Attaran</a>, the result of the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockholm Convention">Stockholm Convention</a> banning DDT's use in agriculture "is arguably better than the status quo&#160;...&#160;For the first time, there is now an insecticide which is restricted to vector control only, meaning that the selection of resistant mosquitoes will be slower than before."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silent_Spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Silent Spring</i> has been featured in many lists of the best nonfiction books of the twentieth century. It was fifth in the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Library_List_of_Best_20th-Century_Nonfiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Library List of Best 20th-Century Nonfiction">Modern Library List of Best 20th-Century Nonfiction</a> and number 78 in the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a>'s</i> 100 best non-fiction books of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In 2006, <i>Silent Spring</i> was named one of the 25 greatest science books of all time by the editors of <i><a href="/wiki/Discover_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Discover Magazine">Discover Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-discovermagazine.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discovermagazine.com-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2012, the American Chemical Society designated the legacy of <i>Silent Spring</i> a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.acs.org/content/acs/en/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/rachel-carson-silent-spring.html">National Historic Chemical Landmark</a> at Chatham University in Pittsburgh.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, a follow-up book, <i>Beyond Silent Spring</i>, co-written by H.F. van Emden and <a href="/wiki/David_Peakall" title="David Peakall">David Peakall</a>, was published.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman_.26_Hall_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman_.26_Hall-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards_H_269.E2.80.93270_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards_H_269.E2.80.93270-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, the American composer <a href="/wiki/Steven_Stucky" title="Steven Stucky">Steven Stucky</a> wrote the eponymously titled <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a> <a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring_(composition)" title="Silent Spring (composition)"><i>Silent Spring</i></a> to commemorate the fiftieth anniversary of the book's publication. The piece was given its world premiere in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> on February 17, 2012, with the <a href="/wiki/Conducting" title="Conducting">conductor</a> <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Honeck" title="Manfred Honeck">Manfred Honeck</a> leading the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Pittsburgh Symphony Orchestra">Pittsburgh Symphony Orchestra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>Naturalist <a href="/wiki/Sir_David_Attenborough" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir David Attenborough">Sir David Attenborough</a> has stated that <i>Silent Spring</i> was probably the book that had changed the scientific world the most, after the <i><a href="/wiki/Origin_of_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of Species">Origin of Species</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BI2014_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI2014-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
