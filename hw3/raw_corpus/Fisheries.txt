			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Fishery</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">Fisheries</a>)</span></div>

Salmon spawn in a salmon fishery within the <a href="/wiki/Becharof_Wilderness" title="Becharof Wilderness">Becharof Wilderness</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Alaska" title="Southwest Alaska">Southwest Alaska</a>.</div>

<p>Generally, a <b>fishery</b> is an entity engaged in raising or harvesting <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> which is determined by some authority to be a fishery.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/FAO" class="mw-redirect" title="FAO">FAO</a>, a fishery is typically defined in terms of the "people involved, species or type of fish, area of water or seabed, method of fishing, class of boats, purpose of the activities or a combination of the foregoing features".<sup id="cite_ref-fao_glossary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao_glossary-2">[2]</a></sup> The definition often includes a combination of fish and <a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fishers</a> in a region, the latter fishing for similar species with similar gear types.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A fishery may involve the capture of <a href="/wiki/Wild_fishery" class="mw-redirect" title="Wild fishery">wild fish</a> or raising fish through <a href="/wiki/Fish_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish farm">fish farming</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fao_glossary_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao_glossary-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Directly or indirectly, the livelihood of over 500 million people in developing countries depends on fisheries and aquaculture. <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a>, including the taking of fish beyond <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable fishing">sustainable levels</a>, is reducing <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> and employment in many world regions.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A report by <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a>' International Sustainability Unit, the New York-based <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defense_Fund" title="Environmental Defense Fund">Environmental Defense Fund</a> and 50in10 published in July 2014 estimated global fisheries were adding $270 billion a year to global GDP, but by full implementation of sustainable fishing, that figure could rise by as much as $50 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalFisheries_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalFisheries-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_term_fish">The term <i>fish</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The term fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fish#Terminology" title="Fish">Fish §&#160;Terminology</a></div>
<ul>
<li><b>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a></b> – the term <i>fish</i> is most strictly used to describe any animal with a <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_column" title="Vertebral column">backbone</a> that has <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> throughout life and has <a href="/wiki/Limb_(anatomy)" title="Limb (anatomy)">limbs</a>, if any, in the shape of <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Many types of <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic animals</a> commonly referred to as <i>fish</i> are not fish in this <a href="/wiki/Fish#Terminology" title="Fish">strict sense</a>; examples include <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">cuttlefish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starfish" title="Starfish">starfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>. In earlier times, even <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologists</a> did not make a distinction — sixteenth century <a href="/wiki/Natural_historian" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural historian">natural historians</a> classified also <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, even <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamuses</a>, as well as a host of <a href="/wiki/Marine_invertebrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine invertebrate">marine invertebrates</a>, as fish.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>In fisheries</b> – the term <i>fish</i> is used as a collective term, and includes <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and any <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic animal</a> which is harvested.<sup id="cite_ref-fao_glossary_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao_glossary-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>True fish</b> – The strict biological definition of a fish, above, is sometimes called a true fish. True fish are also referred to as <i>finfish</i> or <i>fin fish</i> to distinguish them from other <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquatic life">aquatic life</a> harvested in fisheries or aquaculture.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fishery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Faina_de_Pesca,Sesimbra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg/250px-Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg/375px-Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg/500px-Faina_de_Pesca%2CSesimbra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Faina_de_Pesca,Sesimbra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fishermen in <a href="/wiki/Sesimbra" title="Sesimbra">Sesimbra</a>, Portugal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fisheries are harvested for their value (<a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational</a> or <a href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence</a>). They can be <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saltwater</a> or <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wild</a> or <a href="/wiki/Farmed" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmed">farmed</a>. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> fishery of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">cod</a> fishery off the <a href="/wiki/Lofoten" title="Lofoten">Lofoten</a> islands, the <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a> fishery of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Eastern Pacific</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Shrimp_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrimp farm">shrimp farm</a> fisheries in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Capture fisheries can be broadly classified as industrial scale, small-scale or artisanal, and recreational.</p>
<p>Close to 90% of the world’s fishery catches come from <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a>, as opposed to inland waters. These marine catches have remained relatively stable since the mid-nineties (between 80 and 86 million tonnes).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Most marine fisheries are based near the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coast</a>. This is not only because harvesting from relatively shallow waters is easier than in the open ocean, but also because fish are much more abundant near the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal shelf</a>, due to the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> available there from <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">coastal upwelling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_runoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Land runoff">land runoff</a>. However, productive wild fisheries also exist in open oceans, particularly by <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a>, and inland in lakes and rivers.</p>
<p>Most fisheries are <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fisheries</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Fish_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish farm">farmed fisheries</a> are increasing. Farming can occur in coastal areas, such as with <a href="/wiki/Oyster_farming" title="Oyster farming">oyster farms</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but more typically occur inland, in lakes, ponds, tanks and other enclosures.</p>
<p>There are species fisheries worldwide for finfish, <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, and by extension, <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">aquatic plants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>. However, a very small number of species support the majority of the world’s fisheries. Some of these species are <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a>, tuna, <a href="/wiki/Flounder" title="Flounder">flounder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, salmon, <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallops</a>. All except these last four provided a worldwide catch of well over a <a href="/wiki/Million" title="Million">million</a> <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in 1999, with herring and <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a> together providing a harvest of over 22 million metric tons in 1999. Many other species are harvested in smaller numbers.</p>
