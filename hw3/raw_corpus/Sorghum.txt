<p><i><b>Sorghum</b></i> is a genus of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Flowering plants">flowering plants</a> in the grass family <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>. Seventeen of the twenty-five species are native to <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with the range of some extending to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, and certain islands in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Oceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>One species is grown for <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a>, while many others are used as <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a> plants, either intentionally cultivated or allowed to grow naturally, in <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture lands</a>. The plants are cultivated in warm climates worldwide and naturalized in many places.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Sorghum</i> is in the subfamily <a href="/wiki/Panicoideae" title="Panicoideae">Panicoideae</a> and the tribe <a href="/wiki/Andropogoneae" title="Andropogoneae">Andropogoneae</a> (the tribe of <a href="/wiki/Andropogon_gerardi" title="Andropogon gerardi">big bluestem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorghum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One species, <i><a href="/wiki/Sorghum_bicolor" title="Sorghum bicolor">Sorghum bicolor</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> native to Africa with many cultivated forms now,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is an important crop worldwide, used for <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> (as grain and in <a href="/wiki/Sweet_sorghum" title="Sweet sorghum">sorghum syrup or "sorghum molasses"</a>), animal <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a>, the production of <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>, and biofuels. Most varieties are <a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">drought</a>- and heat-tolerant, and are especially important in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions, where the grain is one of the <a href="/wiki/Staple_food" title="Staple food">staples</a> for poor and rural people. These varieties form important components of pastures in many tropical regions. <i>S. bicolor</i> is an important <a href="/wiki/Food_crop" class="mw-redirect" title="Food crop">food crop</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, and is the fifth-most important <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> crop grown in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some species of sorghum can contain levels of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hordenine" title="Hordenine">hordenine</a>, and nitrates lethal to grazing animals in the early stages of the plants' growth. When stressed by drought or heat, plants can also contain toxic levels of cyanide and/or nitrates at later stages in growth.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another <i>Sorghum</i> species, <a href="/wiki/Johnson_grass" title="Johnson grass">Johnson grass</a> (<i>S. halapense</i>), is classified as an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_Species" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasive Species">invasive species</a> in the US by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">Department of Agriculture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sorghum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sorghum is efficient in converting <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a>, and also uses less water compared to other grain crops.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a>, using sweet sorghum as a high sugar content from its stalk for ethanol production, is being developed with biomass which can be turned into <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bio-oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Bio-oil">bio-oil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>In a 100 gram amount, raw sorghum provides 329 <a href="/wiki/Calories" class="mw-redirect" title="Calories">calories</a>, 72% <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a>, 4% <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> and 11% <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> (table). Sorghum supplies numerous <a href="/wiki/Essential_nutrients" class="mw-redirect" title="Essential nutrients">essential nutrients</a> in rich content (20% or more of the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Value" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a>, DV), including protein, <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>, the <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niacin" title="Niacin">niacin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thiamin" class="mw-redirect" title="Thiamin">thiamin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B6" title="Vitamin B6">vitamin B6</a>, and several <a href="/wiki/Dietary_minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Dietary minerals">dietary minerals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (26% DV) and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> (76% DV) (table). Sorghum nutrient contents generally are similar to those of raw <a href="/wiki/Oats" class="mw-redirect" title="Oats">oats</a> (see nutrition table). Among other similarities to oats, sorghum contains no <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a>, making it useful for <a href="/wiki/Gluten-free_diet" title="Gluten-free diet">gluten-free diets</a>.</p>
