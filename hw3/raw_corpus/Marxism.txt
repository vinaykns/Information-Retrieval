<p><b>Marxism</b> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomics" title="Socioeconomics">socioeconomic</a> analysis that analyzes <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a> relations and societal conflict using a <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">materialist interpretation of historical development</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectical</a> view of social transformation. It originates from the mid-to-late 19th century works of German philosophers <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>.</p>
<p>Marxist methodology originally used a method of <a href="/wiki/Economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociopolitical" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociopolitical">sociopolitical</a> inquiry known as <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">historical materialism</a> to analyze and critique the development of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and the role of <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a> in systemic economic change. According to Marxist perspective, class conflict within capitalism arises due to intensifying contradictions between the highly productive mechanized and <a href="/wiki/Socialization_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialization (economics)">socialized production</a> performed by the <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">proletariat</a>, and the private ownership and appropriation of the <a href="/wiki/Surplus_product" title="Surplus product">surplus product</a> (profit) by a small minority of the population who are private owners called the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>. As the contradiction becomes apparent to the proletariat through the <a href="/wiki/Alienation_of_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Alienation of labor">alienation of labor</a>, social unrest between the two antagonistic classes will intensify, until it culminates in <a href="/wiki/Social_revolution" title="Social revolution">social revolution</a>. The eventual long-term outcome of this revolution would be the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> – a socioeconomic system based on <a href="/wiki/Social_ownership" title="Social ownership">social ownership</a> of the means of production, <a href="/wiki/To_each_according_to_his_contribution" title="To each according to his contribution">distribution based on one's contribution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Production_for_use" title="Production for use">production organized directly for use</a>. As the productive forces and technology continued to advance, Marx hypothesized that socialism would eventually give way to a <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> stage of social development, which would be a classless, stateless, humane society erected on <a href="/wiki/Common_ownership" title="Common ownership">common ownership</a> and the principle of "<a href="/wiki/From_each_according_to_his_ability,_to_each_according_to_his_need" class="mw-redirect" title="From each according to his ability, to each according to his need">From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs</a>".</p>
<p>Marxism has since developed into different branches and schools of thought, and there is now no single definitive Marxist theory.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Different Marxian schools place a greater emphasis on certain aspects of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Marxism" title="Classical Marxism">classical Marxism</a> while de-emphasizing or rejecting other aspects, and sometimes combine Marxist analysis with non-Marxian concepts; as a result, they might reach contradictory conclusions from each other.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lately, however, there is movement toward the recognition that the main aspect of Marxism is philosophy of dialectical materialism and historicism,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which should result in more agreement between different schools.</p>
<p>Marxist analyses and methodologies have influenced multiple political ideologies and social movements, and <a href="/wiki/Marxist_historiography" title="Marxist historiography">Marxist understandings of history</a> and society have been adopted by some academics in the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Marxist_archaeology" title="Marxist archaeology">archaeology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Media_studies" title="Media studies">media studies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Theater">theater</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxist_sociology" title="Marxist sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_history" title="Art history">art history</a> <a href="/wiki/Art_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Art theory">and theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxian_economics" title="Marxian economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxist_geography" title="Marxist geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxist_literary_criticism" title="Marxist literary criticism">literary criticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxist_aesthetics" title="Marxist aesthetics">aesthetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Critical_psychology" title="Critical psychology">critical psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">philosophy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marxism has been adopted by a wide number of academics and theorists working in various disciplines,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and has also received a variety of <a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_Marxism" title="Criticisms of Marxism">criticisms</a> from within academia. Critics have taken issue with particular Marxist claims or accused Marxism as a whole of being internally inconsistent,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> refuted based on new information,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> and a pseudoscience.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/220px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg" width="220" height="316" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/330px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Karl_Marx_001.jpg/440px-Karl_Marx_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1428" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_Marx_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Marxian analysis begins with an analysis of the material conditions and the economic activities required to satisfy society's material needs. It is assumed that the form of economic organization, or <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">mode of production</a>, gives rise to, or at least directly influences, most other social phenomena&#160;– including social relations, political and legal systems, moral codes and ideology. The economic system and these social relations form a <a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">base and superstructure</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Productive_forces" title="Productive forces">forces of production</a>, most notably <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, improve, existing forms of social organization become inefficient and stifle further progress. As <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> observed: "At a certain stage of development, the material productive forces of society come into conflict with the existing relations of production or – this merely expresses the same thing in legal terms – with the property relations within the framework of which they have operated hitherto. From forms of development of the productive forces these relations turn into their fetters. Then begins an era of social revolution."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These inefficiencies manifest themselves as social contradictions in society in the form of <a href="/wiki/Class_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ComparingEconomic_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComparingEconomic-12">[12]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Capitalist_mode_of_production_(Marxist_theory)" title="Capitalist mode of production (Marxist theory)">capitalist mode of production</a>, this struggle materializes between the minority (the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>) who own the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>, and the vast majority of the population (the <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">proletariat</a>) who produce goods and services. Starting with the assumption that <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">social change</a> occurs because of the struggle between different <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">classes</a> within society who are under contradiction against each other, a Marxist analyst would summarize by saying that <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> exploits and oppresses the proletariat, which leads to a <a href="/wiki/Proletarian_revolution" title="Proletarian revolution">proletarian revolution</a>.</p>
<p>Capitalism (according to Marxist theory) can no longer sustain the living standards of the population due to its need to compensate for <a href="/wiki/Tendency_of_the_rate_of_profit_to_fall" title="Tendency of the rate of profit to fall">falling rates of profit</a> by driving down wages, cutting social benefits and pursuing military aggression. The <a href="/wiki/Socialism_(Marxism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialism (Marxism)">socialist</a> system would succeed capitalism as humanity's mode of production through workers' <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolution</a>. According to Marxism, especially arising from <a href="/wiki/Crisis_theory" title="Crisis theory">crisis theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> is a historical necessity (but not an inevitability).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a socialist society <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a>, in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>, would be replaced by co-operative ownership. A socialist economy would not base production on the creation of private profits, but on the criteria of satisfying human needs – that is, <a href="/wiki/Production_for_use" title="Production for use">production would be carried out directly for use</a>. As Engels said: "Then the capitalist mode of appropriation in which the product enslaves first the producer, and then appropriator, is replaced by the mode of appropriation of the product that is based upon the nature of the modern means of production; upon the one hand, direct social appropriation, as means to the maintenance and extension of production on the other, direct individual appropriation, as means of subsistence and of enjoyment."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concepts">Concepts</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_materialism">Historical materialism</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Historical_materialism" title="Historical materialism">Historical materialism</a></div>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:246px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #c6dbf7;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"The discovery of the materialist conception of history, or rather, the consistent continuation and extension of materialism into the domain of social phenomenon, removed two chief defects of earlier historical theories. In the first place, they at best examined only the ideological motives of the historical activity of human beings, without grasping the objective laws governing the development of the system of social relations&#160;... in the second place, the earlier theories did not cover the activities of the <i>masses</i> of the population, whereas historical materialism made it possible for the first time to study with the accuracy of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a> the social conditions of the life of the masses and the changes in these conditions."</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Russian Marxist theoretician and revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, 1913.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></cite></div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Society does not consist of individuals, but expresses the sum of interrelations, the relations within which these individuals stand.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— &#160;<a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Grundrisse" title="Grundrisse">Grundrisse</a></i>, 1858<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The historical materialist theory of history<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> analyses the underlying causes of societal development and change from the perspective of the collective ways that humans make their living. All constituent features of a society (social classes, political pyramid, ideologies) are assumed to stem from economic activity, an idea often portrayed with the metaphor of the <a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">base and superstructure</a>.</p>
<p>The base and superstructure metaphor portrays the totality of social relations by which humans produce and re-produce their social existence. According to Marx, "The sum total of the forces of production accessible to men determines the condition of society," and forms a society's economic <i>base</i>. The base includes the material forces of production, that is, the labour and material means of production, and relations of production, i.e. the social and political arrangements that regulate production and distribution. From this base rises a <i>superstructure</i> of legal and political "forms of social consciousness" of political and legal institutions that derive from the economic base which conditions the superstructure and a society's dominant ideology. Conflicts between the development of material productive forces and the relations of production provokes social revolutions, and thus, the resultant changes to the economic base will lead to the transformation of the superstructure.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This relationship is reflexive; at first the base gives rise to the superstructure and remains the foundation of a form of social organization. Hence, that formed social organization can act again upon both parts of the base and superstructure, so, that relationship not one-way but a dialogue (a <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectic</a>), expressed and driven by conflicts and contradictions. As <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> clarified: "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight, a fight that each time ended, either in a revolutionary reconstitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the contending classes."'<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marx considered these socio-economic conflicts as the driving force of human history since these recurring conflicts have manifested themselves as distinct transitional stages of development in Western Europe. Accordingly, Marx designated human history as encompassing four stages of development in relations of production.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Primitive_communism" title="Primitive communism">Primitive Communism</a>:</i> as in co-operative tribal societies.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave">Slave</a> Society:</i> a development of tribal to city-state; aristocracy is born.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">Feudalism</a>:</i> aristocrats are the ruling class; merchants evolve into capitalists.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">Capitalism</a>:</i> capitalists are the ruling class, who create and employ the proletariat.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_capitalism">Criticism of capitalism</span></h3>
<p>According to the Marxist theoretician and <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, "the principal content of Marxism" was "Marx's economic doctrine".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Marx believed that the capitalist bourgeois and their economists were promoting what he saw as the lie that "The interests of the capitalist and of the worker are&#160;... one and the same"; he believed that they did this by purporting the concept that "the fastest possible growth of productive <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a>" was best not only for the wealthy capitalists but also for the workers because it provided them with employment.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exploitation is a matter of <a href="/wiki/Surplus_labour" title="Surplus labour">surplus labour</a>&#160;– the amount of labour one performs beyond what one receives in goods. Exploitation has been a socioeconomic feature of every class society, and is one of the principal features distinguishing the social classes. The power of one social class to control the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a> enables its exploitation of the other classes.</p>
<p>In capitalism, the <a href="/wiki/Labor_theory_of_value" title="Labor theory of value">labour theory of value</a> is the operative concern; the <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Commodity_(Marxism)" title="Commodity (Marxism)">commodity</a> equals the socially necessary labour time required to produce it. Under that condition, <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">surplus value</a> (the difference between the value produced and the value received by a labourer) is synonymous with the term "surplus labour"; thus, capitalist exploitation is realised as deriving surplus value from the worker.</p>
<p>In pre-capitalist economies, exploitation of the worker was achieved via physical coercion. In the capitalist mode of production, that result is more subtly achieved; because the worker does not own the means of production, he or she must voluntarily enter into an exploitive work relationship with a capitalist in order to earn the necessities of life. The worker's entry into such employment is voluntary in that he or she chooses which capitalist to work for. However, the worker must work or starve. Thus, exploitation is inevitable, and the "voluntary" nature of a worker participating in a capitalist society is illusory.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation" title="Marx's theory of alienation">Alienation</a> is the estrangement of people from their humanity (<small>German:</small> <i>Gattungswesen</i>, "species-essence", "species-being"), which is a systematic result of capitalism. Under capitalism, the fruits of production belong to the employers, who expropriate the surplus created by others, and so generate alienated labourers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In Marx's view, alienation is an objective characterization of the worker's situation in capitalism&#160;– his or her self-awareness of this condition is not prerequisite.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_classes">Social classes</span></h3>
<p>The identity of a social class derives from its relationship to the <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">means of production</a>; Marx describes the social classes in capitalist societies:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">Proletariat</a>: "the class of modern wage labourers who, having no means of production of their own, are reduced to selling their labour power in order to live".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As Andrei Platonov expressed "The working class is my home country and my future is linked with the proletariat."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The capitalist mode of production establishes the conditions enabling the bourgeoisie to <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_(Marxism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Exploitation (Marxism)">exploit</a> the proletariat because the workers' labour generates a <a href="/wiki/Surplus_value" title="Surplus value">surplus value</a> greater than the workers' <a href="/wiki/Wages" class="mw-redirect" title="Wages">wages</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">Bourgeoisie</a>: those who "own the means of production" and buy labour power from the proletariat, thus exploiting the proletariat; they subdivide as bourgeoisie and the petite bourgeoisie.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Petite_bourgeoisie" title="Petite bourgeoisie">Petite bourgeoisie</a> are those who work and can afford to buy little labour power i.e. small business owners, peasant landlords, trade workers et al. Marxism predicts that the continual reinvention of the means of production eventually would destroy the petite bourgeoisie, degrading them from the middle class to the proletariat.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lumpenproletariat" title="Lumpenproletariat">Lumpenproletariat</a>: the outcasts of society such as criminals, vagabonds, beggars, prostitutes, et al., who have no stake in the economy and no mind of their own and so are decoyed by every bidder.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Landlords" class="mw-redirect" title="Landlords">Landlords</a>: a historically important social class whose members retain some wealth and power.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peasantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Peasantry">Peasantry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Farmers" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmers">farmers</a>: a scattered class incapable of organizing and effecting socio-economic change, most of whom would enter the proletariat while some became landlords.</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Class_consciousness" title="Class consciousness">Class consciousness</a> denotes the awareness&#160;– of itself and the social world&#160;– that a social class possesses, and its capacity to rationally act in their best interests; hence, class consciousness is required <i>before</i> they can effect a successful revolution.</p>
<p>Without defining <a href="/wiki/Ideology#Ideology_as_an_instrument_of_social_reproduction" title="Ideology">ideology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Marx used the term to denote the production of images of social reality; according to Engels, "ideology is a process accomplished by the so-called thinker consciously, it is true, but with a false consciousness. The real motive forces impelling him remain unknown to him; otherwise it simply would not be an ideological process. Hence he imagines false or seeming motive forces".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Because the ruling class controls the society's means of production, the superstructure of society (the ruling social ideas), are determined by the best interests of the ruling class. In <i>The German Ideology</i>, "the ideas of the ruling class are in every epoch the ruling ideas, i.e. the class which is the ruling material force of society, is, at the same time, its ruling intellectual force".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>" originally denoted the study of the conditions under which economic production was organised in the capitalist system. In Marxism, political economy is the study of the means of production, specifically of capital, and how that manifests as economic activity.</p>

<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">

<p>"Marxism taught me what society was. I was like a blindfolded man in a forest, who doesn't even know where north or south is. If you don't eventually come to truly understand the history of the class struggle, or at least have a clear idea that society is divided between the rich and the poor, and that some people subjugate and exploit other people, you're lost in a forest, not knowing anything."</p>
</div>

<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Cuban revolutionary and Marxist-Leninist politician <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> on discovering Marxism, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></cite></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_industrial_revolution">Relation with industrial revolution</span></h2>
<p>This new way of thinking was invented because socialists believed that public ownership will help workers that were abused by their employers. They also thought that the government having control of factories, mines, textile industries and other businesses would abolish poverty and increase equality. These thoughts argued that the government should plan the economy rather than depend on the free market capitalism (invisible hand). One of Marx's arguments was that the bourgeoisie abused the proletariat. Marx thought that with socialism this situation would stop. He believed that capitalism will destroy itself, because the proletariat would revolt and produce what society needs. This would give economic equality to all the population and for this to happen there would not be private property and this will cause social classes to disappear. This would bring an equal share of goods and services.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolution.2C_socialism.2C_and_communism">Revolution, socialism, and communism</span></h2>
<p>Marxists believe that the transition from capitalism to socialism is an inevitable part of the development of human society; as Lenin stated, "it is evident that Marx deduces the inevitability of the transformation of capitalist society [into a socialist society] wholly and exclusively from the economic law of motion of contemporary society."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marxists believe that a socialist society will be far better for the majority of the populace than its capitalist counterpart. Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_(1917)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution (1917)">Russian revolution</a> of 1917, Lenin wrote that "The socialization of production is bound to lead to the conversion of the means of production into the property of society&#160;... This conversion will directly result in an immense increase in productivity of labour, a reduction of working hours, and the replacement of the remnants, the ruins of small-scale, primitive, disunited production by collective and improved labour."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The failure of 1905 revolution and the failure of socialist movements to resist the outbreak of World War 1 led to renewed theoretical effort and valuable contributions from <a href="/wiki/Lenin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lenin">Lenin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg" title="Rosa Luxemburg">Rosa Luxemburg</a> towards an appreciation of Marx's <a href="/wiki/Crisis_theory" title="Crisis theory">crisis theory</a> and efforts to formulate a theory of imperialism.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Marxism">Classical Marxism</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_Marxism" title="Classical Marxism">Classical Marxism</a></div>
<p>The term Classical Marxism denotes the collection of socio-eco-political theories expounded by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. "Marxism," as <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Mandel" title="Ernest Mandel">Ernest Mandel</a> remarked, "is always open, always critical, always self-critical." As such, Classical Marxism distinguishes between "Marxism" as broadly perceived, and "what Marx believed;" thus, in 1883, Marx wrote to the French labour leader <a href="/wiki/Jules_Guesde" title="Jules Guesde">Jules Guesde</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lafargue" title="Paul Lafargue">Paul Lafargue</a> (Marx's son-in-law)&#160;– both of whom claimed to represent Marxist principles&#160;– accusing them of "revolutionary phrase-mongering" and of denying the value of reformist struggle; from Marx's letter derives the paraphrase: "If that is Marxism, then I am not a Marxist."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> American Marxist scholar <a href="/wiki/Hal_Draper" title="Hal Draper">Hal Draper</a> responded to this comment by saying: "There are few thinkers in modern history whose thought has been so badly misrepresented, by Marxists and anti-Marxists alike."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On the other hand, the book <i>Communism: The Great Misunderstanding</i> argues that the source of such misrepresentations lays in ignoring the philosophy of Marxism, which is dialectical materialism. In large, this was due to the fact that <i>The German Ideology</i>, in which Marx and Engels developed this philosophy, did not find a publisher for almost a hundred years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_Marxism" title="Criticisms of Marxism">Criticisms of Marxism</a></div>
<p>Some Marxists have criticised the academic <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutionalisation</a> of Marxism for being too shallow and detached from political action. For instance, Zimbabwean <a href="/wiki/Trotskyism" title="Trotskyism">Trotskyist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alex_Callinicos" title="Alex Callinicos">Alex Callinicos</a>, himself a professional academic, stated that "Its practitioners remind one of <a href="/wiki/Narcissus_(mythology)" title="Narcissus (mythology)">Narcissus</a>, who in the Greek legend fell in love with his own reflection&#160;... Sometimes it is necessary to devote time to clarifying and developing the concepts that we use, but indeed for Western Marxists this has become an end in itself. The result is a body of writings incomprehensible to all but a tiny minority of highly qualified scholars."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_Marxism">Academic Marxism</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gordon_Childe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Gordon_Childe.jpg/220px-Gordon_Childe.jpg" width="220" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Gordon_Childe.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="382" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gordon_Childe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the 20th century's most prominent Marxist academics; the Australian archaeologist <a href="/wiki/V._Gordon_Childe" title="V. Gordon Childe">V. Gordon Childe</a></div>

<p>Marxism has been adopted by a large number of academics and other scholars working in various disciplines.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_theory" title="Archaeological theory">theoretical development</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marxist_archaeology" title="Marxist archaeology">Marxist archaeology</a> was first developed in the Soviet Union in 1929, when a young archaeologist named Vladislav I. Ravdonikas (1894–1976) published a report entitled "For a Soviet history of material culture". Within this work, the very discipline of archaeology as it then stood was criticised as being inherently bourgeoisie and therefore anti-socialist, and so, as a part of the academic reforms instituted in the Soviet Union under the administration of Premier <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, a great emphasis was placed on the adoption of Marxist archaeology throughout the country.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> These theoretical developments were subsequently adopted by archaeologists working in capitalist states outside of the Leninist bloc, most notably by the Australian academic <a href="/wiki/V._Gordon_Childe" title="V. Gordon Childe">V. Gordon Childe</a> (1892–1957), who used Marxist theory in his understandings of the development of human society.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The term "Marxism" was popularized by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kautsky" title="Karl Kautsky">Karl Kautsky</a> who considered himself an "orthodox" Marxist during the dispute between the orthodox and revisionist followers of Marx.<sup id="cite_ref-Georges_Haupt_2010._pp._18.E2.80.9319_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georges_Haupt_2010._pp._18.E2.80.9319-39">[39]</a></sup> Kautsky's revisionist rival <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Bernstein" title="Eduard Bernstein">Eduard Bernstein</a> also later adopted use of the term.<sup id="cite_ref-Georges_Haupt_2010._pp._18.E2.80.9319_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georges_Haupt_2010._pp._18.E2.80.9319-39">[39]</a></sup> Engels did not support the use of the term "Marxism" to describe either Marx's or his views.<sup id="cite_ref-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12-40">[40]</a></sup> Engels claimed that the term was being abusively used as a rhetorical qualifier by those attempting to cast themselves as "real" followers of Marx while casting others in different terms, such as "Lassallians".<sup id="cite_ref-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12-40">[40]</a></sup> In 1882, Engels claimed that Marx had criticized self-proclaimed "Marxist" <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lafargue" title="Paul Lafargue">Paul Lafargue</a>, by saying that if Lafargue's views were considered "Marxist", then "[o]ne thing is certain and that is that I am not a Marxist".<sup id="cite_ref-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georges_Haupt_2010._Pp._12-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Karl_Marx_and_Friedrich_Engels">Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Engels.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Engels.jpg/220px-Engels.jpg" width="220" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Engels.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="422" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Engels.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Karl Marx (5 May 1818&#160;– 14 March 1883) was a German <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosopher</a>, political economist, and socialist revolutionary, who addressed the matters of <a href="/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation" title="Marx's theory of alienation">alienation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">exploitation</a> of the working class, the <a href="/wiki/Capitalist_mode_of_production_(Marxist_theory)" title="Capitalist mode of production (Marxist theory)">capitalist mode of production</a>, and historical materialism. He is famous for analysing history in terms of class struggle, summarised in the initial line introducing the <i><a href="/wiki/Communist_Manifesto" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Manifesto">Communist Manifesto</a></i> (1848): "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles".</p>
<p>Friedrich Engels (28 November 1820&#160;– 5 August 1895) was a German <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosopher</a> who together with Marx co-developed communist theory. Marx and Engels first met in September 1844. Discovering that they had similar views of philosophy and socialism, they collaborated and wrote works such as <i>Die heilige Familie</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Family_(book)" title="The Holy Family (book)">The Holy Family</a></i>). After Marx was deported from France in January 1845, they moved to Belgium, which then permitted greater <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_expression" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom of expression">freedom of expression</a> than other European countries; in January 1846, they returned to Brussels to establish the Communist Correspondence Committee.</p>
<p>In 1847, they began writing <i>The Communist Manifesto</i> (1848), based on Engels' <i>The Principles of Communism</i>; six weeks later, they published the 12,000-word pamphlet in February 1848. In March, Belgium expelled them, and they moved to <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, where they published the <i><a href="/wiki/Neue_Rheinische_Zeitung" title="Neue Rheinische Zeitung">Neue Rheinische Zeitung</a></i>, a politically <a href="/wiki/Political_radicalism" title="Political radicalism">radical</a> newspaper. Again, by 1849, they had to leave Cologne for London. The Prussian authorities pressured the British government to expel Marx and Engels, but Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Russell,_1st_Earl_Russell" title="John Russell, 1st Earl Russell">Lord John Russell</a> refused.</p>
<p>After Marx's death in 1883, Engels became the <a href="/wiki/Editor_in_chief" class="mw-redirect" title="Editor in chief">editor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translator</a> of Marx's writings. With his <i><a href="/wiki/Origins_of_the_Family,_Private_Property,_and_the_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Origins of the Family, Private Property, and the State">Origins of the Family, Private Property, and the State</a></i> (1884)&#160;– analysing <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">monogamous</a> <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> as guaranteeing male social domination of women, a concept analogous, in communist theory, to the capitalist class's economic domination of the working class&#160;– Engels made <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectually</a> significant contributions to <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theory" title="Feminist theory">feminist theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marxist_feminism" title="Marxist feminism">Marxist feminism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th_century">Late 20th century</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Marxism">Political Marxism</span></h4>
<p>In 1959, the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a> led to the victory of <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> and his <a href="/wiki/July_26_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="July 26 Movement">July 26 Movement</a>. Although the revolution had not been explicitly socialist, upon victory Castro ascended to the position of Prime Minister and eventually adopted the Leninist model of socialist development, forging an alliance with the Soviet Union.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> One of the leaders of the revolution, the Argentine Marxist revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Che_Guevara" title="Che Guevara">Che Guevara</a> (1928–1967), subsequently went on to aid revolutionary socialist movements in <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo-Kinshasa</a> and Bolivia, eventually being killed by the Bolivian government, possibly on the orders of the CIA, though the CIA agent sent to search for Guevara, Felix Rodriguez expressed a desire to keep him alive as a possible bargaining tool with the Cuban government; he would posthumously go on to become an internationally recognised icon.</p>
<p>In the People's Republic of China, the Maoist government undertook the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> from 1966 through to 1976 in order to purge capitalist elements from Chinese society and entrench socialism. However, upon Mao's death, his rivals seized political power and under the Premiership of Deng Xiaoping (1978–1992), many of Mao's Cultural Revolution era policies were revised or abandoned and much of the state sector privatised.</p>
<p>The late 1980s and early 1990s saw the collapse of most of those socialist states that had professed a Marxist–Leninist ideology. In the late 1970s and 1980s, the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/New_Right" title="New Right">New Right</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal capitalism</a> as the dominant ideological trends in western politics – championed by U.S. <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidency of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">U.K. Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> – led the west to take a more aggressive stand against the Soviet Union and its Leninist allies. Meanwhile, in the Soviet Union, the reformist <a href="/wiki/Mikhael_Gorbachev" class="mw-redirect" title="Mikhael Gorbachev">Mikhael Gorbachev</a> (1931–) became Premier in March 1985, and began to move away from Leninist-based models of development towards social democracy. Ultimately, Gorbachev's reforms, coupled with rising levels of popular ethnic nationalism in the Soviet Union, led to the state's dissolution in late 1991 into a series of constituent nations, all of which abandoned Marxist–Leninist models for socialism, with most converting to capitalist economies.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Marxism_2">Political Marxism</span></h4>
<p>At the turn of the 21st century, China, Cuba, Laos and Vietnam remained the only officially Marxist–Leninist states remaining, although a Maoist government led by <a href="/wiki/Prachanda" class="mw-redirect" title="Prachanda">Prachanda</a> (1954–) was elected into power in Nepal in 2008 following a long guerrilla struggle. The early 21st century also saw the election of socialist governments in several Latin American nations, in what has come to be known as the "<a href="/wiki/Pink_tide" title="Pink tide">Pink tide</a>". Dominated by the Venezuelan government of <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>, this trend also saw the election of <a href="/wiki/Evo_Morales" title="Evo Morales">Evo Morales</a> in Bolivia, <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Correa" title="Rafael Correa">Rafael Correa</a> in Ecuador and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Ortega" title="Daniel Ortega">Daniel Ortega</a> in Nicaragua; forging political and economic alliances through international organisations like the <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Alliance_for_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolivarian Alliance for the Americas">Bolivarian Alliance for the Americas</a>, these socialist governments allied themselves with Marxist–Leninist Cuba, and although none of them espoused a Leninist path directly, most admitted to being significantly influenced by Marxist theory. For Italian Marxist Gianni Vattimo in his 2011 book <i><a href="/wiki/Hermeneutic_Communism" title="Hermeneutic Communism">Hermeneutic Communism</a></i> "this new weak communism differs substantially from its previous Soviet (and current Chinese) realization, because the South American countries follow democratic electoral procedures and also manage to decentralize the state bureaucratic system through the <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Missions" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolivarian Missions">misiones (social missions for community projects)</a>. In sum, if weakened communism is felt as a specter in the West, it is not only because of media distortions but also for the alternative it represents through the same democratic procedures that the West constantly professes to cherish but is hesitant to apply"<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criticisms_of_Marxism" title="Criticisms of Marxism">Criticisms of Marxism</a></div>
<p>Criticisms of Marxism have come from various political ideologies. Additionally, there are intellectual critiques of Marxism that contest certain assumptions prevalent in Marx's thought and Marxism after him, without exactly rejecting Marxist politics.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Other, contemporary supporters of Marxism argue that many aspects of Marxist thought are viable, but that the corpus is incomplete or outdated in regards to certain aspects of economic, political or <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">social theory</a>. They may therefore combine some Marxist concepts with the ideas of other theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt school">Frankfurt school</a> is one example.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_criticisms">General criticisms</span></h3>
<p>Philosopher and historian of ideas <a href="/wiki/Leszek_Ko%C5%82akowski" title="Leszek Kołakowski">Leszek Kołakowski</a> criticizes the laws of dialectics as fundamentally erroneous. Stating that some are "truisms with no specific Marxist content", others "philosophical dogmas that cannot be proved by scientific means", and some just "nonsense". He believes that some Marxist laws can be interpreted differently, but that these interpretations still in general fall into one of the two categories of error.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Okishio%27s_theorem" title="Okishio's theorem">Okishio's theoreom</a> shows that if capitalists use cost-cutting techniques and real wages do not increase, the rate of profit has to rise which casts doubt about Marx's view that the rate of profit would tend to fail.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The allegations of inconsistency have been a large part of Marxian economics and the debates around it since the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Andrew Kliman argues that this undermines Marx's critiques and the correction of the alleged inconsistencies, because internally inconsistent theories cannot be right by definition.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemological_and_empirical_critiques">Epistemological and empirical critiques</span></h3>
<p>Marx's predictions have been criticized because they have allegedly failed, with some pointing towards the GDP per capita increasing generally in capitalist economies compared to less market oriented economics, the capitalist economies not suffering worsening economic crises leading to the overthrow of the capitalist system, and communist revolutions not occurring in the most advanced capitalist nations, but instead in undeveloped regions.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Philosopher of science <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a> has argued that historical materialism is not falsifiable and therefore, pseudoscience.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> Popper believed that Marxism was originally scientific, in that it was a theory that was genuinely predictive. When these predictions did not happen, Popper argues that the theory avoided falsification by ad hoc hypotheses which make it fit with the facts. Because of this, Popper believes it had degenerated into pseudoscientific dogma from a genuine science.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socialist_critiques">Socialist critiques</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialists" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic socialists">Democratic socialists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_democrats" class="mw-redirect" title="Social democrats">social democrats</a> reject the idea that socialism can be accomplished only through extra-legal class conflict and a proletarian revolution. The relationship between Marx and other socialist thinkers and organizations—rooted in Marxism's "scientific" and anti-utopian socialism, among other factors—has divided Marxists from other socialists since Marx's life. Also, after Marx's death, and with the emergence of Marxism, there have been dissensions within Marxism itself; a notable example is the splitting of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Social_Democratic_Labour_Party" title="Russian Social Democratic Labour Party">Russian Social Democratic Labour Party</a> into <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mensheviks" title="Mensheviks">Mensheviks</a>. <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;"><a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Marxism" title="Orthodox Marxism">Orthodox Marxism</a> became counterposed to a less dogmatic, more innovative, or even <a href="/wiki/Revisionism_(Marxism)" title="Revisionism (Marxism)">revisionist</a> Marxism.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The use of &quot;counterposed&quot; may be confusing to some readers. A better sentence structure or more clarification may be needed. (October 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anarchist_and_libertarian_critiques">Anarchist and libertarian critiques</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_and_Marxism" title="Anarchism and Marxism">Anarchism and Marxism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">Libertarian socialism</a></div>
<p>Anarchism has had a strained relationship with Marxism since Marx's life. <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">Anarchists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_socialism" title="Libertarian socialism">libertarian socialists</a> reject the need for a <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship_of_the_proletariat" title="Dictatorship of the proletariat">transitory state phase</a>, claiming that socialism can only be established through decentralized, non-coercive organization. <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism" title="Individualist anarchism">Individualist anarchists</a>, who are often neither socialists nor capitalists, reject Marxism as a <a href="/wiki/Statism" title="Statism">statist</a> ideology. Anarchist <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakunin" title="Mikhail Bakunin">Mikhail Bakunin</a> criticized Marx for his authoritarian bent.<sup id="cite_ref-Bakunin1872_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bakunin1872-49">[49]</a></sup> The phrases "barracks socialism" or "<a href="/wiki/Barracks_communism" title="Barracks communism">barracks communism</a>" became a shorthand for this critique, evoking the image of citizens' lives being as regimented as the lives of <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscripts</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sperber_2013_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sperber_2013-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_critiques">Economic critiques</span></h3>
<p>Other critiques come from an economic standpoint. V. K. Dmitriev, writing in 1898,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ladislaus_Bortkiewicz" title="Ladislaus Bortkiewicz">Ladislaus von Bortkiewicz</a>, writing in 1906–07,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and subsequent critics have alleged that Marx's <a href="/wiki/Value_theory" title="Value theory">value theory</a> and law of the <a href="/wiki/Tendency_of_the_rate_of_profit_to_fall" title="Tendency of the rate of profit to fall">tendency of the rate of profit to fall</a> are internally inconsistent. In other words, the critics allege that Marx drew conclusions that actually do not follow from his theoretical premises. Once these alleged errors are corrected, his conclusion that aggregate price and profit are determined by, and equal to, aggregate value and surplus value no longer holds true. This result calls into question his theory that the exploitation of workers is the sole source of profit.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Marxism and socialism have received considerable critical analysis from multiple generations of <a href="/wiki/Austrian_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian economics">Austrian economists</a> in terms of scientific methodology, economic theory, and political implications.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_utility#The_Marginal_Revolution" title="Marginal utility">marginal revolution</a>, subjective value theory was rediscovered by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Menger" title="Carl Menger">Carl Menger</a>, a development which fundamentally undermined the British cost theories of value. The restoration of subjectivism and <a href="/wiki/Praxeology" title="Praxeology">praxeological</a> methodology previously used by classical economists including <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cantillon" title="Richard Cantillon">Richard Cantillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne-Robert-Jacques_Turgot" class="mw-redirect" title="Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot">Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Say" title="Jean-Baptiste Say">Jean-Baptiste Say</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Bastiat" title="Frédéric Bastiat">Frédéric Bastiat</a> led Menger to criticise <a href="/wiki/Historicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Historicist">historicist</a> methodology in general. Second-generation Austrian economist <a href="/wiki/Eugen_B%C3%B6hm_von_Bawerk" title="Eugen Böhm von Bawerk">Eugen Böhm von Bawerk</a> used praxeological and subjectivist methodology to attack the law of value fundamentally. Non-Marxist economists have regarded his criticism as definitive, with <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Haberler" title="Gottfried Haberler">Gottfried Haberler</a> arguing that Böhm-Bawerk's critique of Marx's economics was so thorough and devastating that as of the 1960s no Marxian scholar had conclusively refuted it.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Third-generation Austrian <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_Mises" title="Ludwig von Mises">Ludwig von Mises</a> sparked the <a href="/wiki/Economic_calculation_problem" title="Economic calculation problem">economic calculation debate</a> by identifying that without price signals in capital goods, all other aspects of the market economy are irrational. This led him to declare "...&#160;that rational economic activity is impossible in a socialist <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">commonwealth</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Mises_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mises-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
