<p><b>Small hydro</b> is the development of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> on a scale suitable for local community and industry, or to contribute to <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">distributed generation</a> in a regional electricity grid.<sup id="cite_ref-Thesis_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thesis-1">[1]</a></sup> The definition of a small hydro project varies, but a generating capacity of 1 to 20 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a> (MW) is common. In contrast many hydroelectric projects are of enormous size, such as the generating plant at the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> at 22,500 megawatts or the vast multiple projects of the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a>.</p>
<p>Small hydro projects may be built in isolated areas that would be uneconomic to serve from a national electricity grid, or in areas where a national grid does not exist.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Small_hydro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of the term "small hydro" varies considerably around the world, the maximum limit is usually somewhere between 10 and 30 MW. While a minimum limit is not usually set, the US National Hydropower Association specifies a minimum limit of 5 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The "small hydro" description may be stretched up to <span class="nowrap">50 MW</span> in the United States, Canada and China.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Small hydro can be further subdivided into mini hydro, usually defined as 100 to 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a> (kW), and <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">micro hydro</a> which is 5 to 100&#160;kW. Micro hydro is usually the application of hydroelectric power sized for smaller communities, single families or small enterprise. The smallest installations are <a href="/wiki/Pico_hydro" title="Pico hydro">pico hydro</a>, below 5&#160;kW.</p>
<p>Since small hydro projects usually have correspondingly small civil construction work and little or no reservoir, they are seen as having a relatively low environmental impact compared to large hydro.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Small_hydro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a report by REN21, during 2008 small hydro installations grew by 28% over year 2005 to raise the total world small hydro capacity to 85 <a href="/wiki/Gigawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigawatt">gigawatts</a> (GW). Over 70% of this was in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (with 65 GW), followed by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (3.5 GW), the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (3 GW) and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (2 GW).<sup id="cite_ref-REN21-2006_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REN21-2006-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>China planned to electrify a further 10,000 villages between 2005 and 2010 under their <a href="/wiki/China_Village_Electrification_Program" title="China Village Electrification Program">China Village Electrification Program</a>, including further investments in small hydro and <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-REN21-2006_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REN21-2006-4">[4]</a></sup> By 2010, China had 45,000 small hydro installations, especially in rural areas, producing 160 Twh annually.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Over 50% of the world's potential small hydro power was found in Asia however the report noted "It is possible in the future that more small hydropower potential might be identified both on the African and American continents".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mountains and rain forests of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada there are a great many sites suitable for hydro development. However environmental concerns towards large reservoirs after the 1980s halted new dam construction. The solution to coping with increased demand, was to offer contracts to <a href="/wiki/Independent_power_producers_in_British_Columbia" title="Independent power producers in British Columbia">independent power producers</a>, who have built 100 run of the river projects under 50MW. Power production without reservoirs varies dramatically, but older conventional dams retain or release water to average out production though the year. In 2014 these independent producers generated 18,000 GWh from 4,500 MW of capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hydroelectric power is the generation of electric power from the movement of water. A hydroelectric facility requires a dependable flow of water and a reasonable height for the water to fall, called the <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">head</a>. In a typical installation, water is fed from a reservoir through a pipe into a <a href="/wiki/Hydro_turbine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydro turbine">turbine</a>. The water flowing through the turbine causes a <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a> to rotate, converting the motion into electrical energy.</p>
<p>Small hydro may developed by constructing new facilities or through re-development of existing dams whose primary purpose is flood control, or irrigation. Old hydro sites may be re-developed, sometimes salvaging substantial investment in the installation such as penstock pipe and turbines, or just re-using the water rights associated with an abandoned site. Either of these cost saving advantages can make the return on investment for a small hydro site well worth the use of existing sites.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_design">Project design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Small_hydro&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Project design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many companies offer standardized turbine generator packages in the approximate size range of 200&#160;kW to 10 MW. These "water to wire" packages simplify the planning and development of the site since one vendor looks after most of the equipment supply. Since non-recurring engineering costs are minimized and development cost is spread over multiple units, the cost of such package systems is reduced. While synchronous generators capable of isolated plant operation are often used, small hydro plants connected to an electrical grid system can use economical <a href="/wiki/Induction_generator" title="Induction generator">induction generators</a> to further reduce installation cost and simplify control and operation.</p>
<p>Small <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">"run of the river"</a> projects do not have a conventional dam with a reservoir, only a <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a> to form a headpond for diversion of inlet water to the turbine, unused water simply flows over the weir and the headpond may only be capable of a single days storage, not enough for dry summers or frozen winters when generation may come to a halt. A preferred scenario is to have the inlet in an existing lake.</p>
<p>Countries like India and China have policies in favor of small hydro, the regulatory process allows for building dams and reservoirs. In North America and Europe the regulatory process is too long and expensive to consider having a dam and a reservoir for a small project.</p>
<p>Small hydro projects usually have faster environmental and licensing procedures, and since the equipment is usually in serial production, standardized and simplified, and since the civil works construction is also reduced, small hydro projects may be developed very rapidly. The physically smaller size of equipment makes it easier to transport to remote areas without good road or rail access.</p>
<p>One measure of decreased environmental impact with lakes and reservoirs depends on the balance between stream flow and power production. Reducing water diversions helps the river's ecosystem, but reduces the hydro system's <a href="/wiki/Return_on_Investment" class="mw-redirect" title="Return on Investment">return on Investment</a> (ROI). The hydro system design must strike a balance to maintain both the health of the stream and the economics.</p>
