			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Footbridge</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridges&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Footbridges">Footbridges</a>)</span></div>

<p>A <b>footbridge</b> (also called a <b>pedestrian bridge</b>, <b>pedestrian overpass</b>, or <b>pedestrian overcrossing</b>) is a bridge designed for pedestrians and in some cases cyclists, animal traffic, and horse riders, instead of vehicular traffic. Footbridges complement the <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and can be used decoratively to visually link two distinct areas or to signal a transaction. In many <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, footbridges are both functional and can be beautiful works of art and sculpture. For poor rural communities in the developing world, a footbridge may be a community's only access to medical clinics, schools and markets, which would otherwise be unreachable when rivers are too high to cross. <a href="/wiki/Simple_suspension_bridge" title="Simple suspension bridge">Simple suspension bridge</a> designs have been developed to be <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a> and easily constructible in such rural areas using only local materials and labor.</p>
<p>An enclosed footbridge between two buildings is sometimes known as a <a href="/wiki/Skyway" title="Skyway">skyway</a>. Bridges providing for both pedestrians and cyclists are often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_separation_structure" title="Pedestrian separation structure">greenbridges</a> and form an important part of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">sustainable transport</a> movement towards more sustainable<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> cities. Footbridges are often situated to allow pedestrians to cross water or railways in areas where there are no nearby roads to necessitate a road bridge. They are also located across roads to let pedestrians cross safely without slowing down the traffic. The latter is a type of <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_separation_structure" title="Pedestrian separation structure">pedestrian separation structure</a>, examples of which are particularly found near schools, to help prevent children running in front of moving cars.</p>
<p>Small footbridges can also be used for a technical effect in ornamental gardens.</p>
<p>Types footbridges include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simple_suspension_bridge" title="Simple suspension bridge">Simple suspension bridge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clapper_bridge" title="Clapper bridge">Clapper bridge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moon_bridge" title="Moon bridge">Moon bridge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Step-stone_bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Step-stone bridge">Step-stone bridge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zig-zag_bridge" title="Zig-zag bridge">Zig-zag bridge</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wood_plank" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood plank">Plank</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boardwalk" title="Boardwalk">Boardwalk</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joist" title="Joist">Joisted</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">Simple truss</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The residential-scale footbridges all <a href="/wiki/Span_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Span (architecture)">span</a> a short distance and can be used for a broad range of applications. Complicated <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> is not needed and the footbridges are built with readily available materials and basic tools.<sup id="cite_ref-Jeswald_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeswald-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different types of design footbridges include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">Timber</a> footbridges</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">Steel</a> footbridges</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">Concrete</a> footbridge</li>
</ul>
<p>Footbridges can also be built in the same ways as road or rail bridges; particularly <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beam_bridge" title="Beam bridge">beam bridges</a>. Some former road bridges have had their traffic diverted to alternative crossings and have become pedestrian bridges; examples in the UK include <a href="/wiki/The_Iron_Bridge" title="The Iron Bridge">The Iron Bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge" title="Ironbridge">Ironbridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bridge,_Pontypridd" title="Old Bridge, Pontypridd">Old Bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pontypridd" title="Pontypridd">Pontypridd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Bridge" title="Windsor Bridge">Windsor Bridge</a> at <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Berkshire" title="Windsor, Berkshire">Windsor, Berkshire</a>.</p>
<p>Most footbridges are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Guard_rail" title="Guard rail">guard rails</a> to reduce the risk of pedestrians falling. Where they pass over busy roads or railways, they may also include a fence or <a href="/wiki/Suicide_barrier" title="Suicide barrier">other such barrier</a> to prevent pedestrians from jumping, or throwing projectiles onto the traffic below.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Provides safe and sustainable crossings and provides technical assistance to local government and communities need simple, easily applied guidelines on the selection and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> of effective water crossings. Much rural travel takes place on local paths, tracks and village roads. These provide essential access to water, firewood, farm plots and the classified road network. Communities and/or local government are generally responsible for this <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since April 2012">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dennis_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pedestrian overpasses over highways or railroads are expensive, especially when elevators or long ramps for wheelchair users are required. Without elevators or ramps, people with mobility handicaps will not be able to use the structure. Often, people will prefer to walk across a busy road at grade rather than expend the effort to climb up the bridge and go over it. Overpasses should only be used where the number of users justify the costs.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Narrow, enclosed structures can result in perceptions of low personal security among users. Wider structures and good lighting can help reduce this.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Footbridges are small, but important, because they are usually presented in <a href="/wiki/Townscape" class="mw-redirect" title="Townscape">townscape</a>. The appearance of footbridges, and indeed of any other bridges, in a town, is a major concern for designers. People have to live with these structures, usually seeing them every day. In the towns that are big on architectural or scenic interest, conflicting demands may arise and bridges will be built.</p>
<p>Footbridges, in fact, can be elegant or beautiful, and are built on a more human scale than large road and <a href="/wiki/Railway_bridges" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway bridges">railway bridges</a>. Railway footbridges tend to be somewhat <a href="/wiki/Utilitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a>, which were present in the earlier years. Apart from those in stations and in towns, they are generally not much seen, even by the passengers who go under them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-brantacan_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brantacan-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_developing_countries">In developing countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In developing countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tilak_Nagar_Station,_Mumbai.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg/220px-Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg/330px-Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg/440px-Tilak_Nagar_Station%2C_Mumbai.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>

<p>Since the early 1980s, several charities have developed manualized footbridge designs that are <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> for use in <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. The first charity to develop such standardized designs was Helvetas, located in Zurich, Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-helvetas1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-helvetas1-6">[6]</a></sup> Designs that can be sustainably and efficiently used in developing countries are typically made available to the public gratis. The number of bridges built using such <a href="/wiki/Wire_rope" title="Wire rope">wire rope</a> technology in developing countries number over 6,000 (versus a worldwide need of 500,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG/220px-Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG/330px-Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG/440px-Walkway_Over_the_Hudson_4.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>

<p>The record for the longest footbridge in the world is claimed by the <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Poughkeepsie Bridge">Poughkeepsie Bridge</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> at <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Originally built for trains, it was recently restored as a pedestrian walkway. The footbridge has a total length of 2,063 meters (6,768 feet). Before it was demolished in 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Hornibrook_Bridge" title="Hornibrook Bridge">Hornibrook Bridge</a> which crossed <a href="/wiki/Bramble_Bay" title="Bramble Bay">Bramble Bay</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia was longer than the Poughkeepsie Bridge at 2.8&#160;km (1.7&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other examples include:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bank_Bridge" title="Bank Bridge">Bank Bridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_Four_Lions" title="Bridge of Four Lions">Bridge of Four Lions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Big_Dam_Bridge" title="Big Dam Bridge">Big Dam Bridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CoveredFootbridge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CoveredFootbridge.jpg/200px-CoveredFootbridge.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CoveredFootbridge.jpg/300px-CoveredFootbridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/CoveredFootbridge.jpg/400px-CoveredFootbridge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CoveredFootbridge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A covered footbridge over the railway lines at <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a>, England</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was originally usual for passengers to cross from one <a href="/wiki/Railway_platform" title="Railway platform">railway platform</a> to another by stepping over the tracks, but from the mid-19th century onwards safety demanded the provision of a footbridge (or <a href="/wiki/Subway_(underpass)" title="Subway (underpass)">underpass</a>) at busier places. However, in some quieter areas, crossing the line by walking over the tracks is possible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Design of footbridges normally follows the same principles as for other bridges. However, because they are normally significantly lighter than vehicular bridges, they are more vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a> and therefore <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamic (physics)">dynamics</a> effects are often given more attention in design.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlaich_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlaich-9">[9]</a></sup> International attention has been drawn to this issue in recent years by problems on the <a href="/wiki/Pont_de_Solf%C3%A9rino" class="mw-redirect" title="Pont de Solférino">Pont de Solférino</a> in Paris and the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Bridge_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennium Bridge (London)">Millennium Bridge</a> in London.</p>
<p>To ensure footbridges are accessible to <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disabled</a> and other mobility-impaired people, careful consideration is also given to provision of access <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">lifts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair_ramp" title="Wheelchair ramp">ramps</a>, as required by relevant legislation (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Disability_Discrimination_Act_1995" title="Disability Discrimination Act 1995">Disability Discrimination Act 1995</a> in the UK).<sup id="cite_ref-Schlaich_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlaich-9">[9]</a></sup> Some old bridges in Venice are now equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Stairlift" title="Stairlift">stairlift</a> so that residents with a disability can cross them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catwalk">Catwalk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Footbridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Catwalk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Catwalk_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Catwalk (disambiguation)">catwalk (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>Narrow footbridges or walkways to allow workers access to parts of a structure otherwise difficult to reach are referred as <b>catwalks</b> or <b>cat walks</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Such catwalks are located above a stage (<a href="/wiki/Catwalk_(theater)" title="Catwalk (theater)">theater catwalk</a>) in a theater, between parts of a building, along the side of a bridge, on the inside of a tunnel, on the outside of any large <a href="/wiki/Storage_tank" title="Storage tank">storage tank</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Refinery" title="Refinery">refinery</a> or elsewhere, etc. The walkway on the outside (top) of a <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">railroad cars</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Boxcar" title="Boxcar">boxcars</a>, before <a href="/wiki/Railway_air_brake" title="Railway air brake">air brakes</a> came into use, or on top of some <a href="/wiki/Covered_hopper" title="Covered hopper">covered hopper</a> cars is also called a <b>catwalk</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> With the exception of those on top of railroad cars, catwalks are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Guard_rail" title="Guard rail">railings</a> or <a href="/wiki/Handrail" title="Handrail">handrails</a>.</p>
