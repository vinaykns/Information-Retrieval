			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecological debt</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Ecological debt</b> is the level of <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_discharge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Waste discharge (page does not exist)">waste discharge</a> by a population <i>in excess</i> of locally <a href="/wiki/Sustainable" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> natural production and <a href="/wiki/Assimilative_capacity" title="Assimilative capacity">assimilative capacity</a>.</p>
<p>The term has been used since 1992 by some <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">environmental organizations</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Global_south" class="mw-redirect" title="Global south">Global south</a>. The first one to use this term was the Instituto de Ecologia Politica from Chile.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> J.M. Borrero, from Colombia, a lawyer, wrote a book on the ecological debt in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This referred to the environmental liabilities of Northern countries for the excessive per capita production of greenhouse gases, historically and at present. Campaigns on the Ecological Debt were launched since 1997 by <a href="/wiki/Accion_Ecologica" class="mw-redirect" title="Accion Ecologica">Accion Ecologica</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecofeminist scholar <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Salleh" title="Ariel Salleh">Ariel Salleh</a> explains how the <a href="/wiki/Capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> processes at work in the global North <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_natural_resources" title="Exploitation of natural resources">exploit nature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">people</a> simultaneously, ultimately sustaining a large ecological debt in her article,“ Ecological Debt: Embodied Debt”.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> At the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Rio_Earth_Summit" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Earth Summit">Rio Earth Summit</a>, politicians and corporate leaders from the global North introduced the supposed solution for the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_debt" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign debt">foreign debt</a> crisis in the global South.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> They proposed ‘debt for nature swaps’, which essentially means that those countries that possess abundant <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">environmental resources</a> would give them up to the global North in return for the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> reducing their debt.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Feminist <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a>, indigenous activists, and peasants from the global South, primarily in Ecuador, exposed how the global North is much more indebted to the global South.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> Salleh justifies this by explaining how the 500-year-long colonialisation process involving the extraction of resources has caused immense damage and destruction to the ecosystem of the global South.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> In fact, scientists at the US National Academy for Sciences state that in the time period of 1961 – 2000, analyzing the cost of greenhouse gas emissions created by the rich (the global North) alone, it has become apparent that the rich have imposed climate changes on the poor that greatly outweigh the poor’s foreign debt.<sup id="cite_ref-srinivasan_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srinivasan-5">[5]</a></sup> All of this <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> amounts to ecological debt, seizing the people’s livelihood resources in the global South.</p>
<p>The ecological debt manifested in the destruction of the environment and associated climate change the North has created is made possible through the process of modernization and capitalism.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> There is a disassociation between the wealthy capitalists in the North and the environment. Men in particular through industrialization have viewed themselves as separate from nature, and further, they view nature as a tool to profit from, and continually use and abuse without consequences. The notion of humans being embedded in the ecosystem that they live in is crucial to the discipline of <a href="/wiki/Political_ecology" title="Political ecology">political ecology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-salleh_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salleh-4">[4]</a></sup> In political ecology, which reconnects nature and the economy, ecological debt is crucial because it recognizes that colonialisation has not only resulted in a loss of culture, way of life, and language for Indigenous peoples, but it has shaped the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a> into one that <a href="/wiki/Monetization" title="Monetization">monetizes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">commodifies</a> the environment. For example, when the <a href="/wiki/Colonialisation_of_South_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonialisation of South America">colonialisation of South America</a> occurred over 500 years ago, Western Europeans<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> brought with them their <a href="/wiki/Eurocentric" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurocentric">eurocentric</a> values, seeing themselves as better than and therefore entitled to the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous people">indigenous people</a>'s knowledge and the land they lived on. In a perceived post colonial world, large corporations and Western governments tend to present solutions to global warming by commodifying nature and hoping to make a profit. This better-than-thou attitude has created the conditions for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to occur, making the North’s <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> soar,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> while also constructing an ecological debt so large as to completely rid the entire global South of their financial debt.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_debt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Academic work on calculations of the Ecological Debt came later. A remarkable article with the title "The debt of nations and the distribution of ecological impacts from human activities" was published in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-srinivasan_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srinivasan-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies were also produced at regional level, for instance for Orissa, India.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some government officials from developing countries have argued - at meetings on Climate Change - that the principle of shared responsibility demands that rich nations go beyond donations or adaptation credits and make reparations that recognize an ecological debt for excessive emissions over several decades. The top US ambassador to the <a href="/wiki/Conference_of_the_parties" title="Conference of the parties">COP</a> in Copenhagen in December 2009, <a href="/wiki/Todd_Stern" title="Todd Stern">Todd Stern</a>, flatly rejected arguments by diplomats from poor lands that the United States owed such a debt.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological Debt has been used to describe the consumption of resources from within an ecosystem that exceeds the system's regenerative capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This is seen in particular in <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resources</a> wherein consumption outstrips production. In a general sense, it can be used to refer to the overall depletion of global resources beyond the Earth's ability to regenerate them. The concept in this sense is based on the bio-physical <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of an ecosystem; through measuring <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprints" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological footprints">ecological footprints</a> human society can determine the rate at which it is depleting natural resources. Ultimately, the imperative of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> requires human society to live within the means of the ecological system to support life over the long term. Ecological debt is a feature of unsustainable economic systems.</p>
