<p><b>Grid energy storage</b> (also called <b>large-scale energy storage</b>) is a collection of methods used to <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">store electrical energy</a> on a large scale within an <a href="/wiki/Grid_(electricity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grid (electricity)">electrical power grid</a>. Electrical energy is stored during times when production (especially from intermittent power plants such as <a href="/wiki/Renewable_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable electricity">renewable electricity</a> sources such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>) exceeds consumption, and returned to the grid when production falls below consumption.</p>
<p>As of 2016 by far the largest form of grid energy storage on grids is dammed hydroelectricity, with both conventional hydroelectric generation as well as pumped storage.</p>
<p>An alternative to grid storage is the use of <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking power plants</a> to fill in.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_storage_and_managing_peak_load">Benefits of storage and managing peak load</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Benefits of storage and managing peak load">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stores are used - feeding power to the grids - at times when consumption that cannot be deferred or delayed exceeds production. In this way, electricity production need not be drastically scaled up and down to meet momentary consumption&#160;– instead, transmission from the combination of generators plus storage facilities is maintained at a more constant level.</p>
<p>An alternate and complementary approach to achieve the same effect as grid energy storage is to use a <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a> communication infrastructure to enable <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">Demand response</a> (DR). Both of these technologies shift energy usage and transmission of power on the grid from one time (when it's not useful) to another (when it's desperately immediately needed).</p>
<p>Any <a href="/wiki/Grid_(electricity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grid (electricity)">electrical power grid</a> must adapt energy production to energy consumption, both of which vary drastically over time. Any combination of energy storage and demand response has these advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>fuel-based power plants (i.e. coal, oil, gas, nuclear) can be more efficiently and easily operated at constant production levels</li>
<li>electricity generated by (or with the potential to be generated by) intermittent sources can be stored and used later, whereas it would otherwise have to be transmitted for sale elsewhere, or simply wasted</li>
<li>peak generating or transmission capacity can be reduced by the total potential of all storage plus deferrable loads (see <a href="/wiki/Demand_side_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand side management">demand side management</a>), saving the expense of this capacity</li>
<li>more stable pricing: the cost of the storage and/or demand management is included in pricing so there is less variation in power rates charged to customers, or alternatively (if rates are kept stable by law) less loss to the utility from expensive on-peak wholesale power rates when peak demand must be met by imported wholesale power</li>
<li>emergency preparedness—vital needs can be met reliably even with no transmission or generation going on while non-essential needs are deferred</li>
</ul>
<p>Energy derived from photovoltaic, tidal and wind sources inherently varies &#160;– the amount of electrical energy produced varies with time of day, moon phase, season, and random factors such as the weather. Thus, renewables present special challenges to electric utilities. While hooking up many separate wind sources can reduce the overall variability, solar is reliably not available at night, except when stored in <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy#Molten_salt_storage" title="Solar thermal energy">molten salt</a>, and tidal power shifts with the moon, so it is never reliably available on peak demand.</p>
<p>How much this affects any given utility varies significantly. In a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Summer_peak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Summer peak (page does not exist)">summer peak</a> utility, more solar can generally be absorbed and matched to demand. In <a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_peak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Winter peak (page does not exist)">winter peak</a> utilities, to a lesser degree, wind correlates to heating demand and can be used to meet that demand. Depending on these factors, beyond about 20-40% of total generation, grid-connected <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">intermittent energy sources</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind turbines</a> tend to require investment in either grid energy storage or demand side management or both.</p>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Grid_(electricity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grid (electricity)">electrical power grid</a> without energy storage, energy sources that rely on energy stored within fuels (coal, oil, gas, nuclear) must be scaled up and down to match the rise and fall of energy production from intermittent energy sources (see <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">load following power plant</a>). While oil and gas plants can be scaled up when wind dies down quickly, coal and nuclear plants take considerable time to respond to load. Utilities with less gas or oil power generation are thus more reliant on demand management and grid storage.</p>
<p>The French consulting firm Yole Développement figures this “stationary storage” market could be a $13.5 billion opportunity by 2023, compared with less than $1 billion in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demand_side_management_and_grid_storage">Demand side management and grid storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demand side management and grid storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The demand side can also store energy from the grid, for example charging a <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery electric vehicle</a> stores energy for a vehicle and <a href="/wiki/Storage_heater" title="Storage heater">storage heaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/District_heating#Heat_accumulators_and_storage" title="District heating">district heating storage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ice_storage_air_conditioning" title="Ice storage air conditioning">ice storage</a> provide thermal storage for buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At present this storage serves only to shift consumption to the off-peak time of day, no energy is returned to the grid.</p>
<p>The need for grid storage to provide peak power is reduced by demand side <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_pricing#Time-based_utility_pricing" title="Dynamic pricing">time of use</a> pricing, one of the benefits of smart meters. At the household level, consumers may choose less expensive off-peak times for clothes washer/dryers, dishwashers, showers and cooking. As well commercial and industrial users will take advantage of cost savings by deferring some processes to off-peak times.</p>
<p>Regional impacts from the unpredictable operation of wind power has created a new need for interactive <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">demand response</a>, where the utility communicates with the demand. Historically this was only done in cooperation with large industrial consumers, but now may be expanded to entire grids.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For instance a few large scale projects in Europe link variations in wind power to change industrial food freezer loads, causing small variations in temperature. If communicated on a grid-wide scale, small changes to heating/cooling temperatures would instantly change consumption across the grid. Whether or not to consider more-deeply-frozen food or more-heated water a form of energy storage is debatable.</p>
<p>A report released in December 2013 by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> further describes the potential benefits of energy storage and demand side technologies to the electric grid: “Modernizing the electric system will help the nation meet the challenge of handling projected energy needs—including addressing climate change by integrating more energy from renewable sources and enhancing efficiency from non-renewable energy processes. Advances to the electric grid must maintain a robust and resilient electricity delivery system, and energy storage can play a significant role in meeting these challenges by improving the operating capabilities of the grid, lowering cost and ensuring high reliability, as well as deferring and reducing infrastructure investments. Finally, energy storage can be instrumental for emergency preparedness because of its ability to provide backup power as well as grid stabilization services.” <sup id="cite_ref-energy-department-releases-grid-energy-storage-report_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy-department-releases-grid-energy-storage-report-4">[4]</a></sup> The report was written by a core group of developers representing <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Electricity_Delivery_and_Energy_Reliability" title="Office of Electricity Delivery and Energy Reliability">Office of Electricity Delivery and Energy Reliability</a>, <a href="/wiki/ARPA-E" title="ARPA-E">ARPA-E</a>, <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science" title="Office of Science">Office of Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Energy_Efficiency_and_Renewable_Energy" title="Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy">Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest_National_Laboratory" title="Pacific Northwest National Laboratory">Pacific Northwest National Laboratory</a>; all of whom are engaged in the development of grid energy storage.<sup id="cite_ref-energy-department-releases-grid-energy-storage-report_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy-department-releases-grid-energy-storage-report-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms">Forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air">Air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressed_air">Compressed air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compressed air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air_energy_storage" title="Compressed air energy storage">Compressed air energy storage</a></div>
<p>Another grid energy storage method is to use off-peak or renewably generated electricity to <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compress air</a>, which is usually stored in an old <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mine</a> or some other kind of geological feature. When electricity demand is high, the compressed air is heated with a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and then goes through <a href="/wiki/Turboexpander" title="Turboexpander">turboexpanders</a> to generate electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Compressed air storage is typically around 60 - 90% efficient<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_air">Liquid air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liquid air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">Liquid air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_energy_storage" title="Cryogenic energy storage">Cryogenic energy storage</a></div>
<p>Another electricity storage method is to compress and cool air, turning it into liquid air,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which can be stored, and expanded when needed, turning a turbine, generating electricity, with a storage efficiency of up to 70%.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batteries">Batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">Battery (electricity)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">Rechargeable battery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Battery_storage_power_station" title="Battery storage power station">Battery storage power station</a></div>

<p>Battery storage was used in the early days of <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> electric power. Where AC grid power was not readily available, isolated lighting plants run by wind turbines or internal combustion engines provided lighting and power to small motors. The battery system could be used to run the load without starting the engine or when the wind was calm. A bank of lead-acid batteries in glass jars both supplied power to illuminate lamps, as well as to start an engine to recharge the batteries.</p>
<p>Battery systems connected to large solid-state converters have been used to stabilize power distribution networks. For example, in Puerto Rico a system with a capacity of 20 megawatts for 15 minutes (5 megawatt hour) stabilizes the frequency of electric power produced on the island. A 27 megawatt 15-minute (6.75 megawatt hour) nickel-cadmium battery bank was installed at Fairbanks Alaska in 2003 to stabilize voltage at the end of a long transmission line.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Many "off-the-grid" domestic systems rely on battery storage (see <a href="/wiki/Battery_storage_power_station" title="Battery storage power station">Battery storage power station</a>).</p>
<p>Other possible large-scale storage technologies include <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid metal battery">liquid metal</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium-ion_battery" title="Sodium-ion battery">sodium-Ion</a> batteries. <a href="/wiki/NaS_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="NaS battery">Sodium-sulfur batteries</a> could also be inexpensive to implement on a large scale and have been used for grid storage in Japan and in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Magnesium-antimony batteries are under development.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016 a <a href="/wiki/Zinc_ion_battery" title="Zinc ion battery">zinc-ion battery</a> was proposed for use in grid storage applications.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2017 the <a href="/wiki/California_Public_Utilities_Commission" title="California Public Utilities Commission">California Public Utilities Commission</a> installed 396 refrigerator-sized stacks of Tesla batteries at the Mira Loma substation in <a href="/wiki/Ontario,_California" title="Ontario, California">Ontario, California</a> that are capable of powering 15,000 homes for over four hours.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_batteries">Flow batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flow batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Rechargeable <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow batteries</a> can be used as a rapid-response storage medium.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_redox_battery" title="Vanadium redox battery">Vanadium redox batteries</a> is another flow battery.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> They are installed at <a href="/wiki/Huxley_Hill_Wind_Farm,_Tasmania" class="mw-redirect" title="Huxley Hill Wind Farm, Tasmania">Huxley Hill wind farm</a> (Australia), Tomari Wind Hills at <a href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a> (Japan), as well as in non-wind farm applications. A 12 MW·h flow battery was to be installed at the <a href="/wiki/Sorne_Hill_wind_farm" title="Sorne Hill wind farm">Sorne Hill wind farm</a> (<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These storage systems are designed to smooth out transient wind fluctuations. Hydrogen Bromide has been proposed for use in a utility-scale flow-type battery.<sup id="cite_ref-EERE_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EERE-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Battery_storage_power_station#BYD_in_Hongkong" title="Battery storage power station">BYD</a> proposes to use conventional consumer battery technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Lithium_iron_phosphate" title="Lithium iron phosphate">lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) battery</a>, connecting many batteries in parallel.</p>
<p>Some grid batteries are co-located with renewable energy plants, either to smooth the power supplied by the intermittent wind or solar output, or to shift the power output into other hours of the day when the renewable plant cannot produce power directly (see <a href="/wiki/Battery_storage_power_station#Installation_examples" title="Battery storage power station">Installation examples</a>).</p>
<p>The largest grid storage batteries in the United States include the 31.5MW battery at Grand Ridge Power plant in Illinois and the 31.5 MW battery at Beech Ridge, West Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Two batteries under construction in 2015 include the 400MWh (100MW for 4 hours) <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Edison" title="Southern California Edison">Southern California Edison</a> project and the 52 MWh project on Kauai, Hawaii to entirely time shift a 13MW solar farm's output to the evening.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Two batteries are in <a href="/wiki/Fairbanks,_Alaska" title="Fairbanks, Alaska">Fairbanks, Alaska</a> (40 MW for 7 minutes using <a href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">Ni-Cd</a> cells),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Notrees,_Texas" title="Notrees, Texas">Notrees, Texas</a> (36 MW for 40 minutes using <a href="/wiki/Lead-acid_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead-acid batteries">lead-acid batteries</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A 13 MWh battery made of worn batteries from electric cars is being constructed in Germany, with an expected second life of 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, a 221 MW battery storage was installed in the USA, with total capacity expected to reach 1.7 GW in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>Battery storage technology is typically around 50 - &gt;85% efficient <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_vehicles">Electric vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electric vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">Electric vehicle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg/220px-Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg/330px-Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg/440px-Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nissan_Leaf_aan_Amsterdamse_laadpaal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a>, the world's top-selling highway-capable <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric car</a> as of 2015</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Companies are researching the possible use of electric vehicles to meet peak demand. A parked and plugged-in electric vehicle could sell the electricity from the battery during peak loads and charge either during night (at home) or during off-peak.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">Plug-in hybrid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">electric cars</a> could be used <sup id="cite_ref-RSC_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> for their energy storage capabilities. <a href="/wiki/Vehicle-to-grid" title="Vehicle-to-grid">Vehicle-to-grid</a> technology can be employed, turning each vehicle with its 20 to 50&#160;kWh <a href="/wiki/Battery_pack" title="Battery pack">battery pack</a> into a distributed load-balancing device or emergency power source. This represents 2 to 5 days per vehicle of average household requirements of 10&#160;kWh per day, assuming annual consumption of 3650&#160;kWh. This quantity of energy is equivalent to between 40 and 300 miles (64 and 483&#160;km) of range in such vehicles consuming 0.5 to 0.16&#160;kWh per mile. These figures can be achieved even in home-made <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle_conversion" title="Electric vehicle conversion">electric vehicle conversions</a>. Some electric utilities plan to use old plug-in vehicle batteries (sometimes resulting in a giant battery) to store electricity<sup id="cite_ref-woody_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woody-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> However, a large disadvantage of using vehicle to grid energy storage is the fact that each storage cycle stresses the battery with one complete charge-discharge cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-32">[32]</a></sup> Conventional (cobalt-based) lithium ion batteries break down with the number of cycles - newer li-ion batteries do not break down significantly with each cycle, and so have much longer lives. One approach is to reuse unreliable vehicle batteries in dedicated grid storage <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/peterdetwiler/2014/03/18/the-afterlife-for-electric-vehicle-batteries-a-future-source-of-energy-storage/">[3]</a> as they are expected to be good in this role for ten years <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/energy/2012/11/121116-second-life-for-used-electric-car-batteries/">[4]</a>. If such storage is done on a large scale it becomes much easier to guarantee replacement of a vehicle battery degraded in mobile use, as the old battery has value and immediate use.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flywheel">Flywheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flywheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Flywheel_storage_power_system" title="Flywheel storage power system">Flywheel storage power system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flywheel_energy_storage" title="Flywheel energy storage">Flywheel energy storage</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:G2_front2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/G2_front2.jpg/220px-G2_front2.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/G2_front2.jpg/330px-G2_front2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/G2_front2.jpg/440px-G2_front2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:G2_front2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NASA G2 flywheel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mechanical inertia is the basis of this storage method. When the electric power flows into the device, an <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a> accelerates a heavy rotating disc. The motor acts as a generator when the flow of power is reversed, slowing down the disc and producing electricity. Electricity is stored as the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of the disc. <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">Friction</a> must be kept to a minimum to prolong the storage time. This is often achieved by placing the flywheel in a vacuum and using <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bearing" title="Magnetic bearing">magnetic bearings</a>, tending to make the method expensive. Greater flywheel speeds allow greater storage capacity but require strong materials such as <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> to resist the <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">centrifugal forces</a>. The ranges of power and energy storage technology that make this method economic, however, tends to make flywheels unsuitable for general power system application; they are probably best suited to load-leveling applications on railway power systems and for improving <a href="/wiki/Power_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Power quality">power quality</a> in <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> systems such as the 20MW system in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Applications that use flywheel storage are those that require very high bursts of power for very short durations such as <a href="/wiki/Tokamak" title="Tokamak">tokamak</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> experiments where a motor generator is spun up to operating speed and is partially slowed down during discharge.</p>
<p>Flywheel storage is also currently used in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_rotary_uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Diesel rotary uninterruptible power supply">Diesel rotary uninterruptible power supply</a> to provide <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply#Rotary" title="Uninterruptible power supply">uninterruptible power supply</a> systems (such as those in large <a href="/wiki/Data_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Data Center">datacenters</a>) for ride-through power necessary during transfer<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>&#160;– that is, the relatively brief amount of time between a loss of power to the mains and the warm-up of an alternate source, such as a <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">diesel generator</a>.</p>
<p>This potential solution has been implemented by EDA<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (December 2015)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Graciosa" title="Graciosa">Graciosa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">Flores</a>. This system uses an 18 megawatt-second flywheel to improve <a href="/wiki/Power_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Power quality">power quality</a> and thus allow increased renewable energy usage. As the description suggests, these systems are again designed to smooth out transient fluctuations in supply, and could never be used to cope with an outage exceeding a couple of days.</p>
<p>Powercorp in Australia have been developing applications using wind turbines, flywheels and low load diesel (LLD) technology to maximise the wind input to small grids. A system installed in Coral Bay, Western Australia, uses wind turbines coupled with a flywheel based control system and LLDs to achieve better than 60% wind contribution to the town grid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">Hydrogen economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_storage" title="Hydrogen storage">Hydrogen storage</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> is being developed as an electrical energy storage medium.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RSC2_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC2-42">[42]</a></sup> Hydrogen is produced, then compressed or liquefied, cryogenicly stored at −252.882&#160;°C, and then converted back to electrical energy or heat. Hydrogen can be used as a fuel for portable (vehicles) or stationary energy generation. Compared to pumped water storage and batteries, hydrogen has the advantage that it is a high energy density fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC2_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC2-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hydrogen can be produced either by <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">reforming natural gas with steam</a> or by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis of water</a> into hydrogen and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a>). Reforming natural gas produces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a by-product. <a href="/wiki/High_temperature_electrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="High temperature electrolysis">High temperature electrolysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_electrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure electrolysis">high pressure electrolysis</a> are two techniques by which the efficiency of hydrogen production may be able to be increased. Hydrogen is then converted back to electricity in an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a>.</p>
<p>The AC-to-AC efficiency of hydrogen storage has been shown to be on the order of 20 to 45%, which imposes economic constraints.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC2_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC2-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-anscombe_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anscombe-43">[43]</a></sup> The price ratio between purchase and sale of electricity must be at least proportional to the efficiency in order for the system to be economic. Hydrogen fuel cells can respond quickly enough to correct rapid fluctuations in electricity demand or supply and regulate frequency. Whether hydrogen can use natural gas infrastructure depends on the network construction materials, standards in joints, and storage pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The equipment necessary for hydrogen energy storage includes an electrolysis plant, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_compressor" title="Hydrogen compressor">hydrogen compressors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquifiers</a>, and storage tanks.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biohydrogen" title="Biohydrogen">Biohydrogen</a> is a process being investigated for producing hydrogen using biomass.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Micro_combined_heat_and_power" title="Micro combined heat and power">Micro combined heat and power</a> (microCHP) can use hydrogen as a fuel.</p>
<p>Some nuclear power plants may be able to benefit from a symbiosis with hydrogen production. High temperature (950 to 1,000&#160;°C) gas cooled nuclear <a href="/wiki/Generation_IV_reactor" title="Generation IV reactor">generation IV reactors</a> have the potential to electrolyze hydrogen from water by thermochemical means using nuclear heat as in the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur-iodine_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulfur-iodine cycle">sulfur-iodine cycle</a>. The first commercial reactors are expected in 2030.</p>
<p>A community based pilot program using <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbines" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbines">wind turbines</a> and hydrogen generators was started in 2007 in the remote community of <a href="/wiki/Ramea,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramea, Newfoundland and Labrador">Ramea, Newfoundland and Labrador</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A similar project has been going on since 2004 in <a href="/wiki/Utsira" title="Utsira">Utsira</a>, a small Norwegian island municipality.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Underground_hydrogen_storage">Underground hydrogen storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Underground hydrogen storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Underground_hydrogen_storage" title="Underground hydrogen storage">Underground hydrogen storage</a> is the practice of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_storage" title="Hydrogen storage">hydrogen storage</a> in underground <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caverns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt domes</a> and depleted oil and gas fields.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Large quantities of gaseous hydrogen have been stored in underground caverns by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemical Industries</a> (ICI) for many years without any difficulties.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The European project <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyunder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hyunder (page does not exist)">Hyunder</a><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> indicated in 2013 that for the storage of wind and solar energy an additional 85 caverns are required as it can't be covered by <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">PHES</a> and <a href="/wiki/CAES" class="mw-redirect" title="CAES">CAES</a> systems.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_gas">Power to gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Power to gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">Power to gas</a> is a technology which converts <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> power to a gas <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>. There are 2 methods, the first is to use the electricity for <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">water splitting</a> and inject the resulting hydrogen into the natural gas grid. The second less efficient method is used to convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) using <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>. The excess power or off peak power generated by wind generators or solar arrays is then used for load balancing in the energy grid. Using the existing natural gas system for hydrogen, fuel cell maker <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenics" title="Hydrogenics">Hydrogenics</a> and natural gas distributor <a href="/wiki/Enbridge" title="Enbridge">Enbridge</a> have teamed up to develop such a <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">power to gas</a> system in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-anscombe_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anscombe-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pipeline storage of hydrogen where a natural gas network is used for the storage of hydrogen. Before switching to <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, the German gas networks were operated using <a href="/wiki/Towngas" class="mw-redirect" title="Towngas">towngas</a>, which for the most part consisted of hydrogen. The storage capacity of the German natural gas network is more than 200,000 GW·h which is enough for several months of energy requirement. By comparison, the capacity of all German pumped storage power plants amounts to only about 40 GW·h. The transport of energy through a gas network is done with much less loss (&lt;0.1%) than in a power network (8%)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The use of the existing <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_gas_pipelines" title="List of natural gas pipelines">natural gas pipelines</a> for hydrogen was studied by NaturalHy<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_power-to-ammonia_concept">The power-to-ammonia concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The power-to-ammonia concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The power-to-ammonia concept offers a carbon-free energy storage route with a diversified application palette. At times when there is a surplus of renewable electricity, it can be converted to ammonia locally using small-scale plants. Existing technology can be used to produce ammonia by splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen with the help of electricity, then using high temperature and pressure to convert the hydrogen plus nitrogen from the air into ammonia. Ammonia is similar to propane when stored in liquid form, unlike Hydrogen which is difficult to liquefy and store cryogenicly at −252.882&#160;°C.</p>
<p>Just like natural gas, the produced and stored ammonia can be used by energy companies at any time as fuel for electricity generation. Ammonia can be stored as a liquid; a standard tank of 60,000 m3 contains about 211 GWh of energy, equivalent to the annual production of roughly 30 wind turbines on land. Ammonia can be burned cleanly: water and nitrogen are released, but no CO2 and little or no nitrogen oxides. Ammonia can be further used as a flexible chemical as a fertilizer, chemical commodity, de-NOx agent and energy carrier. Given this flexibility of usage, and given the fact that the supporting infrastructure for the transport, distribution and usage of ammonia is already in place, it makes ammonia a good candidate to be a large-scale, non-carbon, energy carrier of the future.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pumped_water">Pumped water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pumped water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">Pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a></div>

<p>In 2008 world pumped storage generating capacity was 104 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">GW</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-eia_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eia-51">[51]</a></sup> while other sources claim 127 GW, which comprises the vast majority of all types of grid electric storage - all other types combined are some hundreds of MW.<sup id="cite_ref-epri_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epri-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In many places, pumped storage hydroelectricity is used to even out the daily generating load, by pumping water to a high storage reservoir during off-peak hours and weekends, using the excess base-load capacity from coal or nuclear sources. During peak hours, this water can be used for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> generation, often as a high value rapid-response reserve to cover transient peaks in demand. Pumped storage recovers about 70% to 85% of the energy consumed, and is currently the most cost effective form of mass power storage.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The chief problem with pumped storage is that it usually requires two nearby reservoirs at considerably different heights, and often requires considerable capital expenditure.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pumped water systems have high <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_power_source#Terminology" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermittent power source">dispatchability</a>, meaning they can come on-line very quickly, typically within 15 seconds,<sup id="cite_ref-Dinorwig_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dinorwig-55">[55]</a></sup> which makes these systems very efficient at soaking up variability in electrical <i>demand</i> from consumers. There is over 90 GW of pumped storage in operation around the world, which is about 3% of <i>instantaneous</i> global generation capacity. Pumped water storage systems, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dinorwig_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinorwig power station">Dinorwig</a> storage system in Britain, hold five or six hours of generating capacity,<sup id="cite_ref-Dinorwig_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dinorwig-55">[55]</a></sup> and are used to smooth out demand variations.</p>
<p>Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Tianhuangping_Pumped_Storage_Power_Station" title="Tianhuangping Pumped Storage Power Station">Tianhuangping Pumped-Storage Hydro Plant</a> in China, which has a reservoir capacity of eight million cubic meters (2.1 billion U.S. gallons or the volume of water over <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a> in 25 minutes) with a vertical distance of 600 m (1970&#160;feet). The reservoir can provide about 13 GW·h of stored gravitational potential energy (convertible to electricity at about 80% efficiency), or about 2% of China's daily electricity consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>A new concept in pumped-storage is utilizing <a href="/wiki/Wind_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind energy">wind energy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> to pump water. <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">Wind turbines</a> or solar cells that direct drive <a href="/wiki/Water_pump" class="mw-redirect" title="Water pump">water pumps</a> for an energy storing wind or solar <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dam</a> can make this a more efficient process but are limited. Such systems can only increase kinetic water volume during windy and daylight periods.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectric_dams">Hydroelectric dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hydroelectric dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FeiCueiReservoir.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/FeiCueiReservoir.jpg/220px-FeiCueiReservoir.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/FeiCueiReservoir.jpg/330px-FeiCueiReservoir.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/FeiCueiReservoir.jpg/440px-FeiCueiReservoir.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>

<p>Hydroelectric dams with large reservoirs can also be operated to provide peak generation at times of peak demand. Water is stored in the reservoir during periods of low demand and released through the plant when demand is higher. The net effect is the same as pumped storage, but without the pumping loss. Depending on the reservoir capacity the plant can provide daily, weekly, or seasonal load following.</p>
<p>Many existing hydroelectric dams are fairly old (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">Hoover Dam</a> was built in the 1930s), and their original design predated the newer intermittent power sources such as wind and solar by decades. A hydroelectric dam originally built to provide <a href="/wiki/Baseload_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseload power">baseload power</a> will have its generators sized according to the average flow of water into the reservoir. Uprating such a dam with additional generators increases its peak power output capacity, thereby increasing its capacity to operate as a virtual grid energy storage unit.<sup id="cite_ref-burec_hydro_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burec_hydro-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sccpa_hoover_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sccpa_hoover-58">[58]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Reclamation" title="United States Bureau of Reclamation">United States Bureau of Reclamation</a> reports an investment cost of $69 per kilowatt capacity to uprate an existing dam,<sup id="cite_ref-burec_hydro_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burec_hydro-57">[57]</a></sup> compared to more than $400 per kilowatt for oil-fired peaking generators. While an uprated hydroelectric dam does not directly store excess energy from other generating units, it behaves equivalently by accumulating its own fuel – incoming river water – during periods of high output from other generating units. Functioning as a virtual grid storage unit in this way, the uprated dam is one of the most efficient forms of energy storage, because it has no pumping losses to fill its reservoir, only increased losses to evaporation and leakage.</p>
<p>A dam which impounds a large reservoir can store and release a correspondingly large amount of energy, by controlling river outflow and raising or lowering its reservoir level a few meters. Limitations do apply to dam operation, their releases are commonly subject to government <a href="/wiki/Water_right" title="Water right">regulated water rights</a> to limit downstream effect on rivers. For example, there are grid situations where baseload thermal plants, nuclear or wind turbines are already producing excess power at night, dams are still required to release enough water to maintain adequate river levels, whether electricity is generated or not. Conversely there's a limit to peak capacity, which if excessive could cause a river to flood for a few hours each day.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superconducting_magnetic_energy">Superconducting magnetic energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Superconducting magnetic energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnetic_energy_storage" title="Superconducting magnetic energy storage">Superconducting magnetic energy storage</a></div>
<p>Superconducting magnetic energy storage (SMES) systems store energy in the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> created by the flow of <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> coil which has been <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenically</a> cooled to a temperature below its superconducting critical temperature. A typical SMES system includes three parts: superconducting coil, power conditioning system and cryogenically cooled refrigerator. Once the superconducting coil is charged, the current will not decay and the magnetic energy can be stored indefinitely. The stored energy can be released back to the network by discharging the coil. The power conditioning system uses an <a href="/wiki/Inverter_(electrical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverter (electrical)">inverter</a>/<a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">rectifier</a> to transform <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) power to direct current or convert DC back to AC power. The inverter/rectifier accounts for about 2–3% energy loss in each direction. SMES loses the least amount of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> in the energy storage process compared to other methods of storing energy. SMES systems are highly efficient; the round-trip efficiency is greater than 95%. The high cost of superconductors is the primary limitation for commercial use of this energy storage method.</p>
<p>Due to the energy requirements of <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a>, and the limits in the total energy able to be stored, SMES is currently used for short duration energy storage. Therefore, SMES is most commonly devoted to improving <a href="/wiki/Power_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Power quality">power quality</a>. If SMES were to be used for <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">utilities</a> it would be a <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">diurnal</a> storage device, charged from <a href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Base load power plant">base load</a> power at night and meeting <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peak loads</a> during the day.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnetic_energy_storage#Technical_challenges" title="Superconducting magnetic energy storage">Superconducting magnetic energy storage technical challenges</a> are yet to be solved for it to become practical.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal">Thermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Thermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy_storage" title="Thermal energy storage">Thermal energy storage</a></div>
<p>In Denmark the direct storage of electricity is perceived as too expensive for very large scale usage, albeit significant usage is made of existing Norwegian Hydro. Instead, the use of existing hot water storage tanks connected to district heating schemes, heated by either electrode boilers or heat pumps, is seen as a preferable approach. The stored heat is then transmitted to dwellings using <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> pipes.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">Molten salt</a> is used to store heat collected by a <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar power tower</a> so that it can be used to generate electricity in bad weather or at night.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Off-peak electricity can be used to make <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> from water, and the ice can be stored. The stored ice can be used to cool the air in a large building which would have normally used electric AC, thereby shifting the electric load to off-peak hours. On other systems stored ice is used to cool the intake air of a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">generator</a>, thus increasing the on-peak generation capacity and the on-peak efficiency.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Pumped_Heat_Electricity_Storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Pumped Heat Electricity Storage">Pumped Heat Electricity Storage</a> system uses a highly reversible heat engine/heat pump to pump heat between two storage vessels, heating one and cooling the other. The UK-based engineering company Isentropic that is developing the system claims a potential electricity-in to electricity-out round-trip efficiency of 72-80%.<sup id="cite_ref-Isentropic_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Isentropic-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_potential_energy_storage_with_solid_masses"><a href="/wiki/Energy_storage#Gravitational_potential_energy_storage_with_solid_masses" title="Energy storage">Gravitational potential energy storage with solid masses</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gravitational potential energy storage with solid masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <b><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></b>, ski lifts and trains tracks are among places being considered to store energy by moving heavy objects up or down <a href="/wiki/Grade_(slope)" title="Grade (slope)">inclines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American-2014.03.25_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-2014.03.25-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The levelized cost of storing electricity depends highly on storage type and purpose; as subsecond-scale <a href="/wiki/Utility_frequency" title="Utility frequency">frequency regulation</a>, minute/hour-scale peaker plants, or day/week-scale season storage.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Using battery storage is said to be U$0.12<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>-0.17<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> per kWh.</p>
<p>Generally speaking, energy storage is economical when the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">marginal cost</a> of electricity varies more than the costs of storing and retrieving the energy plus the price of energy lost in the process. For instance, assume a <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage reservoir</a> can pump to its upper reservoir a volume of water capable of producing 1,200 <a href="/wiki/MW%C2%B7h" class="mw-redirect" title="MW·h">MW·h</a> after all losses are factored in (evaporation and seeping in the reservoir, efficiency losses, etc.). If the marginal cost of electricity during off-peak times is $15 per MW·h, and the reservoir operates at 75% efficiency (i.e., 1,600 MW·h are consumed and 1,200 MW·h of energy are retrieved), then the total cost of filling the reservoir is $24,000. If all of the stored energy is sold the following day during peak hours for an average $40 per MW·h, then the reservoir will see revenues of $48,000 for the day, for a <a href="/wiki/Gross_profit" title="Gross profit">gross profit</a> of $24,000.</p>
<p>However, the marginal cost of electricity varies because of the varying operational and fuel costs of different classes of generators. At one extreme, <a href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Base load power plant">base load power plants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coal_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal power">coal</a>-fired power plants and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plants are low marginal cost generators, as they have high capital and maintenance costs but low fuel costs. At the other extreme, <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking power plants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas#Power_generation" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> plants burn expensive fuel but are cheaper to build, operate and maintain. To minimize the total operational cost of generating power, base load generators are dispatched most of the time, while peak power generators are dispatched only when necessary, generally when energy demand peaks. This is called "economic dispatch".</p>
<p>Demand for <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> from the world's various grids varies over the course of the day and from season to season. For the most part, variation in electric demand is met by varying the amount of electrical energy supplied from primary sources. Increasingly, however, operators are storing lower-cost energy produced at night, then releasing it to the grid during the peak periods of the day when it is more valuable.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In areas where hydroelectric dams exist, release can be delayed until demand is greater; this form of storage is common and can make use of existing reservoirs. This is not storing "surplus" energy produced elsewhere, but the net effect is the same - although without the efficiency losses. Renewable supplies with variable production, like <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, tend to increase the net variation in electric load, increasing the opportunity for grid energy storage.</p>
<p>It may be more economical to find an alternative market for unused electricity, rather than try and store it. <a href="/wiki/HVDC" class="mw-redirect" title="HVDC">High Voltage Direct Current</a> allows for transmission of electricity, losing only 3% per 1000&#160;km.</p>
<p>The United States Department of Energy's International Energy Storage Database provides a free list of grid energy storage projects, many of which show funding sources and amounts.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Load_leveling">Load leveling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Load leveling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The demand for electricity from consumers and industry is constantly changing, broadly within the following categories:</p>
<ul>
<li>Seasonal (during dark winters more electric lighting and heating is required, while in other climates hot weather boosts the requirement for air conditioning)</li>
<li>Weekly (most industry closes at the weekend, lowering demand)</li>
<li>Daily (such as the morning peak as offices open and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioners" class="mw-redirect" title="Air conditioners">air conditioners</a> get switched on)</li>
<li>Hourly (one method for estimating television viewing figures in the United Kingdom is to measure the power spikes during advertisement breaks or after programmes when viewers go to switch a kettle on <sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>Transient (fluctuations due to individual's actions, differences in power transmission efficiency and other small factors that need to be accounted for)</li>
</ul>
<p>There are currently three main methods for dealing with changing demand:</p>
<ul>
<li>Electrical devices generally having a working <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> range that they require, commonly 110–120&#160;V or 220–240&#160;V. Minor variations in load are automatically smoothed by slight variations in the voltage available across the system.</li>
<li>Power plants can be run below their normal output, with the facility to increase the amount they generate almost instantaneously. This is termed 'spinning reserve'.</li>
<li>Additional generation can be brought online. Typically, these would be hydroelectric or gas turbines, which can be started in a matter of minutes.</li>
</ul>
<p>The problem with standby gas turbines is higher costs, expensive generating equipment is unused much of the time. Spinning reserve also comes at a cost, plants run below maximum output are usually less efficient. Grid energy storage is used to shift generation from times of peak load to off-peak hours. Power plants are able to run at their peak efficiency during nights and weekends.</p>
<p>Supply-demand leveling strategies may be intended to reduce the cost of supplying peak power or to compensate for the intermittent generation of wind and solar power.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_demand_management">Energy demand management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Energy demand management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_demand_management" title="Energy demand management">Energy demand management</a></div>
<p>In order to keep the supply of electricity consistent and to deal with varying electrical loads it is necessary to decrease the difference between generation and demand. If this is done by changing loads it is referred to as demand side management (DSM). For decades, utilities have sold off-peak power to large consumers at lower rates, to encourage these users to shift their loads to off-peak hours, in the same way that telephone companies do with individual customers. Usually, these time-dependent prices are negotiated ahead of time. In an attempt to save more money, some utilities are experimenting with selling electricity at minute-by-minute <a href="/wiki/Spot_price" class="mw-redirect" title="Spot price">spot prices</a>, which allow those users with monitoring equipment to detect demand peaks as they happen, and shift demand to save both the user and the utility money. Demand side management can be manual or automatic and is not limited to large industrial customers. In residential and small business applications, for example, appliance control modules can reduce energy usage of <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">water heaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> units, refrigerators, and other devices during these periods by turning them off for some portion of the peak demand time or by reducing the power that they draw. Energy demand management includes more than reducing overall energy use or shifting loads to off-peak hours. A particularly effective method of energy demand management involves encouraging electric consumers to install more <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficient</a> equipment. For example, many utilities give rebates for the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weatherstripping" title="Weatherstripping">weatherstripping</a>, and appliances and <a href="/wiki/Light_bulb" class="mw-redirect" title="Light bulb">light bulbs</a> that are energy efficient. Some utilities subsidize the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">geothermal heat pumps</a> by their customers, to reduce electricity demand during the summer months by making air conditioning up to 70% more efficient, as well as to reduce the winter electricity demand compared to conventional air-sourced heat pumps or resistive heating.<sup id="cite_ref-geothermal_incentive_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geothermal_incentive-70">[70]</a></sup> Companies with factories and large buildings can also install such products, but they can also buy energy efficient industrial equipment, like <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>, or use more efficient processes to produce products. Companies may get incentives like rebates or low interest loans from utilities or the government for the installation of energy efficient industrial equipment. Facilities may shift their demand by enlisting a third party to provide <a href="/wiki/Energy_Storage_as_a_Service_(ESaaS)" title="Energy Storage as a Service (ESaaS)">Energy Storage as a Service (ESaaS)</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portability">Portability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Portability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the area of greatest success for current energy storage technologies. Single-use and rechargeable batteries are ubiquitous, and provide power for devices with demands as varied as digital watches and cars. Advances in battery technology have generally been slow, however, with much of the advance in battery life that consumers see being attributable to efficient power management rather than increased storage capacity. Portable <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> have benefited greatly from size and power reductions associated with <a href="/wiki/Moore%27s_law" title="Moore's law">Moore's law</a>. Unfortunately, Moore's law does not apply to hauling people and freight; the underlying energy requirements for transportation remain much higher than for information and entertainment applications. Battery capacity has become an issue as pressure grows for alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> in cars, trucks, buses, trains, ships, and aeroplanes. These uses require far more <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> (the amount of energy stored in a given volume or weight) than current battery technology can deliver. Liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fuel (such as <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>/<a href="/wiki/Petrol" class="mw-redirect" title="Petrol">petrol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>), as well as alcohols (<a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Lipids" class="mw-redirect" title="Lipids">lipids</a> (<a href="/wiki/Straight_vegetable_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Straight vegetable oil">straight vegetable oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>) have much higher energy densities.</p>
<p>There are synthetic pathways for using electricity to reduce carbon dioxide and water to liquid hydrocarbon or alcohol fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-great_lakes_wind_potential_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-great_lakes_wind_potential-71">[71]</a></sup> These pathways begin with electrolysis of water to generate hydrogen, and then reducing carbon dioxide with excess hydrogen in variations of the reverse <a href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a>. Non-fossil sources of carbon dioxide include <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> plants and <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> plants. Converting electrical energy to carbon-based liquid fuel has potential to provide portable energy storage usable by the large existing stock of motor vehicles and other engine-driven equipment, without the difficulties of dealing with hydrogen or another exotic <a href="/wiki/Energy_carrier" title="Energy carrier">energy carrier</a>. These synthetic pathways may attract attention in connection with attempts to improve <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a> in nations that rely on imported petroleum, but have or can develop large sources of renewable or nuclear electricity, as well as to deal with possible future <a href="/wiki/Export_land_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Export land model">declines in the amount of petroleum</a> available to import.</p>
<p>Because the transport sector uses the energy from petroleum very inefficiently, replacing petroleum with electricity for mobile energy will not require very large investments over many years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Virtually all devices that operate on electricity are adversely affected by the sudden removal of their power supply. Solutions such as UPS (<a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">uninterruptible power supplies</a>) or backup generators are available, but these are expensive. Efficient methods of power storage would allow for devices to have a built-in backup for power cuts, and also reduce the impact of a failure in a generating station. Examples of this are currently available using <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> and flywheels.</p>
