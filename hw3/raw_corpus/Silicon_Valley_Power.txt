<p><b>Silicon Valley Power</b> (SVP) is a <a href="/wiki/Not-for-profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Not-for-profit">not-for-profit</a> municipal <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric utility</a> owned and operated by the City of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>, USA. SVP provides electricity service to approximately 52,000 residential and business customers, including large corporations such as Intel, Yahoo!, Applied Materials, Owens Corning and NVIDIA. SVP also owns and maintains a dark fiber network named SVP Fiber Enterprise, and provides citywide free outdoor Wi-Fi access as part of its installed wireless network communications system that supports SVP MeterConnect®, SVP’s Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) program.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The City of Santa Clara electric department was founded in 1896 when it installed 46 streetlights powered by a direct current generator. In January 1904 to 1965 the electric department began purchasing energy for resale to Santa Clara’s customers from the United Gas and Electric Company of San Jose, which later became part of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric_Company" title="Pacific Gas and Electric Company">Pacific Gas and Electric Company</a> (PG&amp;E).<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-1">[1]</a></sup> PG&amp;E supplied Santa Clara’s electric needs until 1965, when the electric department began to purchase its power from the <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_Project" title="Central Valley Project">Central Valley Project</a> (CVP) supervised by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Reclamation" title="United States Bureau of Reclamation">United States Bureau of Reclamation</a>. In 1968, Santa Clara became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_California_Power_Agency" title="Northern California Power Agency">Northern California Power Agency</a> (NCPA) in order to work with other municipal electric utilities to jointly develop cost-effective energy sources.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980 the Santa Clara electric department became an energy producing utility for the first time since 1903 when it launched its own 6-<a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> (MW) <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> project, the first of three natural gas-fueled electricity generation plants in the City of Santa Clara. In 1983 Santa Clara and its NCPA partners became the first cities in the U.S. to invest in and operate a publicly owned geothermal plant,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the 110 MW NCPA Geothermal Project, one of 22 geothermal power plants at <a href="/wiki/The_Geysers" title="The Geysers">The Geysers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with Santa Clara having a 55% ownership interest.</p>
<p>In 1998 the Santa Clara electric department was renamed Silicon Valley Power (SVP). Subsequent efforts to expand and diversify its electricity supply led to construction of the <a href="/wiki/Donald_Von_Raesfeld_Power_Plant" title="Donald Von Raesfeld Power Plant">Donald Von Raesfeld combined cycle natural gas plant</a> (2005), various partnerships in wind and hydroelectric generation sources,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and 25 percent ownership of the Lodi Energy Center combined cycle natural gas plant (2012).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2007, SVP launched Santa Clara Green Power to provide residents and businesses the option to use only renewable energy. SVP continually pursues affordable renewable power options as the city general plan states an objective to be sustainable.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On average, in 2012 over 38 percent of the electricity distributed by SVP was from green resources,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the City’s utility is nationally recognized for its reliable power, low rates, and customer satisfaction.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the City owns an extensive dark fiber optic network to serve business customers. The SVP Fiber Enterprise is a department of SVP. SVP introduced two new services in 2013. Santa Clara was the first city in the U.S. to provide free citywide outdoor Wi-Fi access via an AMI wireless system<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> branded as SVP MeterConnect®, and SVP helped fund electric vehicle (EV) charging stations at the Central Park Library and the Santa Clara Convention Center.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> SVP’s complete AMI project is being rolled out in the 2015-2016 time frame.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_Portfolio">Power Portfolio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Power Portfolio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013 the SVP power mix consisted of 24.2% from eligible renewal resources as defined by the California Energy Commission,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 43.7% natural gas, 17.7% large hydroelectric, 8.4% coal, and 6.0% from unspecified sources.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Total kilowatt-hour (kWh) retail sales in 2014 were 3,052,050,846 kWh.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities_and_Generation_Sources">Facilities and Generation Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Facilities and Generation Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generating facilities owned by the City of Santa Clara and located in the city provided 32.5% of the electricity consumed in Santa Clara. Natural gas-fueled facilities are the <a href="/wiki/Donald_Von_Raesfeld_Power_Plant" title="Donald Von Raesfeld Power Plant">Donald Von Raesfeld natural gas power plant</a> (147.8 MW), Gianera Generating Station (49.5 MW) and Cogeneration Plant #1 (7 MW).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In addition, power is also generated by the Jenny Strand Solar Research and Development Park (100&#160;kW),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> the Tasman Parking Structure Solar PV (400&#160;kW)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and by the capture and burning of methane gas from a closed City of Santa Clara landfill (750&#160;kW).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>SVP also owns a 230-kilovolt (kV) transmission line that brings electricity into the city from non-local sources.</p>
<p>Generating sources owned by SVP and located outside the city include the Stony Creek Hydroelectric System and Grizzly Hydroelectric Project.</p>
<p>Joint Power Agencies (JPA) of which SVP is a member include the NCPA (hydroelectric, natural gas, geothermal projects) and M-S-R (coal and wind). SVP also contracts to receive electricity through power purchase agreements with such entities as Iberdrola and Seawest LLC (wind); Western Area Power Association, Tri-Dam Project, Friant Power Authority (hydroelectric); Recurrent Energy (solar); and G2 Energy and Ameresco (landfill gas).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>SVP receives generation produced outside Santa Clara via transmission facilities owned and operated by PG&amp;E under the direction of the California Independent System Operator (CAISO).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> SVP also participates in the Transmission Agency of Northern California (TANC)<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> which is interconnected to PG&amp;E’s transmission facilities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_Rates">Electricity Rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electricity Rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of January 1, 2015, SVP and the City of Santa Clara have the lowest average system rates for electricity in California for any electric utility serving over 10,000 customers.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> For residential customers, the monthly fixed meter charge is $3.20, and electricity rates are $0.09787/kWh for the first 300 kWh used in one month, and $0.11251/kWh for usage exceeding 300 kWh within the same month.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> With basic service, commercial customers pay a monthly $3.47 meter charge, $0.16688/kWh for the first 800 kWh, and $0.15150/kWh thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Neighboring communities pay 19% to 44% more for their electricity from the region’s dominant utility.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_Agencies">Regulatory Agencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regulatory Agencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>SVP must adhere to the laws and regulations of the U.S. and the State of California and is involved, in various ways, with multiple entities, including: Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC); North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC); California Energy Commission (CEC); CAISO; California Division of Occupational Safety and health (Cal/OSHA); Bay Area Air Quality Management District; California Department of Toxic Substances Control; California Department of Transportation; and the California Air Resources Board.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance_and_Key_Executives">Governance and Key Executives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Governance and Key Executives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The City of Santa Clara City Council<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> has governing authority over SVP. The city manager is Julio Fuentes.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The senior executive of the utility is John Roukema,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Director of Electric Utility, who reports to the city manager.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Responsibility">Environmental Responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental Responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In response to the objective to be a sustainable city stated in the city’s general plan prepared in 2010, the city has adopted a Climate Action Plan.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In addition, SVP has for many years acquired renewable resources and has implemented programs such as Santa Clara Green Power, which allows customers the option to purchase 100% green power,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and multiple energy efficiency rebate programs for residences<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> SVP maintains a renewable energy portfolio that exceeds state-mandated levels by approximately 25%. In 2013, the State of California required utilities to provide at least 20% of their power from eligible renewable resources,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> which includes hydropower plants that generate up to 30 MW of power. SVP’s eligible renewable portfolio in 2012 was 24.2%.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> When SVP’s hydropower plants that generate over 30 MW are factored in, SVP’s total “green” resource profile rises to 41.9%.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2011, 2012, and 2013, SVP was ranked among the top 10 municipal utilities in the U.S. by the Solar Electric Power Association for new solar power capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In late 2013, SVP was recognized by the independent survey company RKS Research for scoring the highest business customer satisfaction ratings of any California utility included in its surveys since RKS began polling utility customers in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> SVP also earned the U.S. Department of Energy’s 2014 Public Power Wind Award for the pursuit and creative utilization of wind power resources.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
