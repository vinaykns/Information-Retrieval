			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">1979 energy crisis</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>The <b>1979</b> (or <b>second</b>) <b>oil crisis</b> or <b>oil shock</b> occurred in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> due to decreased <a href="/wiki/Oil_output" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil output">oil output</a> in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian Revolution</a>. Despite the fact that <a href="/wiki/Global_oil_supply" class="mw-redirect" title="Global oil supply">global oil supply</a> decreased by only ~4%, widespread panic resulted, driving the price far higher. The <a href="/wiki/Price_of_crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Price of crude oil">price of crude oil</a> more than doubled to $39.50 per barrel over the next 12 months, and long lines once again appeared at <a href="/wiki/Gas_stations" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas stations">gas stations</a>, as they had in the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, following the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a>, oil production in Iran nearly stopped, and <a href="/wiki/Iraq%27s_oil_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraq's oil production">Iraq's oil production</a> was severely cut as well. Economic recessions were triggered in the US and other countries. Oil prices did not subside to pre-crisis levels until the mid-1980s.</p>
<p>After 1980, oil prices began a <a href="/wiki/1980s_oil_glut" title="1980s oil glut">20-year decline</a>, eventually reaching a 60 percent fall-off during the 1990s. As with the 1973 crisis, global politics and power balance were impacted. Oil exporters such as Mexico, Nigeria, and Venezuela expanded production; the USSR became the top world producer; North Sea and Alaskan oil flooded the market; and OPEC lost influence.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Revolution" title="Iranian Revolution">Iranian Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a></div>

<p>Amid massive protests, the <a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_dynasty" title="Pahlavi dynasty">Shah of Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Mohammad Reza Pahlavi</a>, fled his country in early 1979 and the <a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">Ayatollah</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruhollah_Khomeini" title="Ruhollah Khomeini">Khomeini</a> soon became the new leader of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. Protests severely disrupted the Iranian oil sector, with production being greatly curtailed and exports suspended. In November 1978, a strike by 37,000 workers at Iran's nationalized oil refineries initially reduced production from 6 million barrels (950,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) per day to about 1.5 million barrels (240,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Foreign_worker" title="Foreign worker">Foreign workers</a> (including skilled oil workers) fled the country. On January 16, 1979, the Shah and his wife left Iran at the behest of Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Shapour_Bakhtiar" title="Shapour Bakhtiar">Shapour Bakhtiar</a> (a longtime opposition leader himself), who sought to calm the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-exile-bbc_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-exile-bbc-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_OPEC_members">Other OPEC members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other OPEC members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Fluctuations of OPEC net oil export revenues since 1972<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The rise in oil price benefited other OPEC members, which made record profits. When oil exports were later resumed under the new Iranian government, they were inconsistent and at a lower volume, pushing prices up. <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and other <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a> nations, under the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Mana_Al_Otaiba" title="Mana Al Otaiba">Mana Al Otaiba</a>, increased production to offset most of the decline, and in early 1979 the overall loss in worldwide production was about 4 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>OPEC failed to hold on to its preeminent position, especially after Iran and Iraq went to war in 1980 and caused a further 10% drop in worldwide production – and by 1981, OPEC production was surpassed by other exporters. Additionally, its own member nations were divided among themselves. Saudi Arabia, a "<a href="/wiki/Swing_producer" title="Swing producer">swing producer</a>" trying to gain back market share after 1985, increased production and caused downward pressure on prices, making high-cost oil production facilities less profitable or even unprofitable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Line_at_a_gas_station,_June_15,_1979.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg/220px-Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg/330px-Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg/440px-Line_at_a_gas_station%2C_June_15%2C_1979.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4472" data-file-height="3008" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Line_at_a_gas_station,_June_15,_1979.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Line at a gas station in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, June 15, 1979.</div>

<p>The oil crisis had mixed effects in the United States, due to some parts of the country being oil-producing regions and other parts being oil-consuming regions. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> had imposed <a href="/wiki/Price_controls" title="Price controls">price controls</a> on domestic oil. Gasoline controls were repealed, but controls on domestic US oil remained.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> administration began a phased <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> of oil prices on April 5, 1979, when the average <a href="/wiki/Price_of_crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Price of crude oil">price of crude oil</a> was <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>15.85 per barrel (42 US gallons (160&#160;L)). Starting with the Iranian revolution, the price of crude oil rose to $39.50 per barrel over the next 12 months (its all-time highest <a href="/wiki/Real_price" class="mw-redirect" title="Real price">real price</a> until March 3, 2008.)<sup id="cite_ref-Mouawad_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mouawad-8">[8]</a></sup> Deregulating domestic oil price controls allowed U.S. oil output to rise sharply from the large <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Prudhoe Bay">Prudhoe Bay</a> fields, while oil imports fell sharply.</p>
<p>And although not directly related, the near-disaster at <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">Three Mile Island</a> on March 28, 1979, also increased anxiety about energy policy and availability.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to memories of oil shortage in 1973, motorists soon began panic buying, and long lines appeared at gas stations, as they had six years earlier during the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the average vehicle of the time consumed between two and three liters (about 0.5-0.8 gallons) of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> (petrol) an hour while idling, it was estimated that Americans wasted up to 150,000 barrels (24,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of oil per day idling their engines in the lines at <a href="/wiki/Gas_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas station">gas stations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gascoupon.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Gascoupon.png/200px-Gascoupon.png" width="200" height="98" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Gascoupon.png/300px-Gascoupon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Gascoupon.png/400px-Gascoupon.png 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="329" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gascoupon.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gas coupon printed but not issued during the 1979 energy crisis</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the period, many people believed the oil companies artificially created oil shortages to drive up prices, rather than factors beyond human control or the US' own price controls. The amount of oil sold in the United States in 1979 was only 3.5 percent less than the record set for oil sold the year previously.<sup id="cite_ref-sowell_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sowell-12">[12]</a></sup> A telephone poll of 1,600 American adults conducted by the Associated Press and NBC News and released in early May 1979 found that only 37% of Americans thought the energy shortages were real, 9% were not sure, and 54% thought the energy shortages were a hoax.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many politicians proposed gas <a href="/wiki/Rationing" title="Rationing">rationing</a>; one such proponent was <a href="/wiki/Harry_Hughes" title="Harry Hughes">Harry Hughes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Maryland" title="Governor of Maryland">Governor of Maryland</a>, who proposed <a href="/wiki/Odd-even_rationing" class="mw-redirect" title="Odd-even rationing">odd-even rationing</a> (only people with an odd-numbered license plate could purchase gas on an odd-numbered day), as was used during the <a href="/wiki/1973_Oil_Crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="1973 Oil Crisis">1973 Oil Crisis</a>. Several states actually implemented odd-even gas rationing, including <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Coupons for gasoline rationing were printed but were never actually used during the 1979 crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 15, 1979, President Carter outlined his plans to reduce oil imports and improve energy efficiency in his "Crisis of Confidence" speech (sometimes known as the "<a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">malaise</a>" speech).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is often said that during the speech, Carter wore a <a href="/wiki/Cardigan_(sweater)" title="Cardigan (sweater)">cardigan</a> (he actually wore a blue suit) <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and encouraged citizens to do what they could to reduce their use of energy. He had already installed solar hot water panels on the roof of the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Wood_fuel" title="Wood fuel">wood-burning stove</a> in the living quarters. However, the panels were removed in 1986, reportedly for roof maintenance, during the administration of his successor, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carter's speech argued the oil crisis was "the moral equivalent of war". Critics, then and now, argued that his varied proposals would make the situation worse, not better.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In November 1979, Iranian revolutionaries seized the American Embassy, and Carter imposed an embargo against Iranian oil.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In January 1980, he issued the <a href="/wiki/Carter_Doctrine" title="Carter Doctrine">Carter Doctrine</a>, declaring: "An attempt by any outside force to gain control of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> region will be regarded as an assault on the vital interests of the United States."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Additionally, as part of his administration's efforts at deregulation, Carter proposed removing price controls that had been imposed by the administration of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> before the 1973 crisis. Carter agreed to remove price controls in phases; they were finally dismantled in 1981 under Reagan.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Carter also said he would impose a windfall profit tax on oil companies.<sup id="cite_ref-tax_history_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tax_history-22">[22]</a></sup> While the regulated price of domestic oil was kept to $6 a barrel, the world market price was $30.<sup id="cite_ref-tax_history_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tax_history-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1980, the U.S. Government established the <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Fuels_Corporation" title="Synthetic Fuels Corporation">Synthetic Fuels Corporation</a> to produce an alternative to imported fossil fuels.</p>
<p>When the price of <a href="/wiki/West_Texas_Intermediate" title="West Texas Intermediate">West Texas Intermediate</a> crude oil increased 250 percent between 1978 and 1980, the oil-producing areas of Texas, Oklahoma, Louisiana, Colorado, Wyoming, and Alaska began experiencing an economic boom and population inflows.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_oil-consuming_nations">Other oil-consuming nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other oil-consuming nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In response to the high oil prices of the 1970s, industrial nations took steps to reduce their dependence on OPEC oil.<sup id="cite_ref-Mouawad_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mouawad-8">[8]</a></sup> Electric utilities worldwide switched from oil to coal, natural gas, or nuclear power;<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> national governments initiated multibillion-dollar research programs to develop alternatives to oil;<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and commercial exploration developed major non-OPEC oilfields in Siberia, Alaska, North Sea, and Gulf of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> By 1986, daily worldwide demand for oil dropped by 5&#160;million barrels, non-OPEC production rose by an even-larger amount,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and OPEC's market share sank from 50% in 1979 to just 29% in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automobile_fuel_economy">Automobile fuel economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1979_energy_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Automobile fuel economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">Fuel economy in automobiles</a></div>

<p>At the time, Detroit's "Big Three" automakers (<a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">GM</a>) were marketing downsized full-sized automobiles like the <a href="/wiki/GM_B_platform" title="GM B platform">Chevrolet Caprice</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Panther_platform" title="Ford Panther platform">Ford LTD Crown Victoria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_R_platform" title="Chrysler R platform">Dodge St. Regis</a> which met the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Average_Fuel_Economy" title="Corporate Average Fuel Economy">CAFE</a> fuel economy mandates passed in 1978. Detroit's response to the growing popularity of imported compacts like the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Corolla" title="Toyota Corolla">Toyota Corolla</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Rabbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkswagen Rabbit">Volkswagen Rabbit</a> were the <a href="/wiki/GM_X_platform_(FWD)" class="mw-redirect" title="GM X platform (FWD)">Chevrolet Citation</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fox_platform" title="Ford Fox platform">Ford Fairmont</a>; Ford replaced the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Pinto" title="Ford Pinto">Ford Pinto</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_CE14_platform" title="Ford CE14 platform">Ford Escort</a> and Chrysler, on the verge of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Chrysler#1980s_and_the_Government_loan_guarantees" title="History of Chrysler">bankruptcy</a>, introduced the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_K_platform" title="Chrysler K platform">Dodge Aries K</a>. GM was having unfavorable market reactions to the Citation, and introduced the <a href="/wiki/GM_L_platform" title="GM L platform">Chevrolet Corsica and Chevrolet Beretta</a> in 1987 which did sell better. GM also replaced the <a href="/wiki/GM_H_platform_(RWD)" class="mw-redirect" title="GM H platform (RWD)">Chevrolet Monza</a>, introducing the 1982 <a href="/wiki/GM_J_platform" title="GM J platform">Chevrolet Cavalier</a> which was better received. Ford experienced a similar market rejection of the Fairmont, and introduced the front wheel drive <a href="/wiki/Ford_Tempo" title="Ford Tempo">Ford Tempo</a> in 1984. <a href="/wiki/Checker_Motors" class="mw-redirect" title="Checker Motors">Checker Motors</a>, known for its iconic <a href="/wiki/Checker_Marathon" title="Checker Marathon">Marathon</a> sedans used for the taxicab livery, ceased its automotive production in 1982 transitioning to stamping sheetmetal for GM. <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a>, the final independent manufacturer outside of Detroit's Big Three, entered into a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> where its mass market automobiles were sold alongside the remaining AMC product lineup which have declined in sales while AMC's Jeep division was profiting, especially with the introduction of its downsized XJ sport utilities which led to the company's demise (its homegrown compacts dating back to 1970 - they were phased out in 1983 (with the exception of the Eagle 4WD wagon making it the final AMC designed product) and financial woes with the Renault partnership ended the reign of the final independent - Renault ended up owning 100% of AMC in 1982 (resulting in the divestment of <a href="/wiki/AM_General" title="AM General">AM General</a>) until late 1986 where they sold AMC's shares to Chrysler Corporation - they later absorbed AMC in late 1987 where the Jeep division is now part of Chrysler (now FCA automobiles).</p>
<p>Detroit was not well prepared for the sudden rise in fuel prices, and imported brands (primarily the Asian marques which were mass marketed and had a lower manufacturing cost as opposed to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_German" class="mw-redirect" title="West German">West German</a> brands - the rising value of the Deutsche Mark and English Pound resulted in the transition to the rise of Japanese manufacturers where exporting their product from Japan at a lower cost would yield profitable gains despite accusations of <a href="/wiki/Price_dumping" class="mw-redirect" title="Price dumping">price dumping</a>) were now more widely available in North America and had developed a loyal <a href="/wiki/Brand_loyalty" title="Brand loyalty">customer base</a> - the Japanese Big Three launched their respective advertisement campaigns (Honda with its 'We Make It Simple' tagline, Datsun (Nissan after 1984) with the tagline 'We Are Driven', and Toyota with 'Oh What A Feeling' (they ran a previous ad campaign prior to 1979 where the company mocked the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Volare" class="mw-redirect" title="Plymouth Volare">Plymouth Volare</a> with the tagline 'You Asked For It - You Got It') - luring away traditional Big Three consumers (Subaru in the late 1970s ran an ad campaign where former owners of a Big Three automobile drove their products - one TV ad started with the tagline 'Ford drives Subaru'). A year after the 1979 Iranian Revolution, Japanese manufacturers surpassed Detroit's production totals becoming first in the world. Japanese exports would later displace the automotive market once dominated by lowered tier European manufacturers (<a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo" title="Alfa Romeo">Alfa Romeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiat_automobiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiat automobiles">Fiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a>, <a href="/wiki/MG_Cars" title="MG Cars">MG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Motor_Company" title="Triumph Motor Company">Triumph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citroen" class="mw-redirect" title="Citroen">Citroen</a>) - some would declare bankruptcy (e.g. Triumph, <a href="/wiki/Simca" title="Simca">Simca</a>) or withdraw from the U.S. market, especially in the wake of <a href="/wiki/Grey_market" title="Grey market">grey market</a> automobiles or the inability of the vehicle to meet DOT requirements (from emission requirements to automotive lighting). Many imported brands utilized fuel saving technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection" title="Fuel injection">fuel injection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Multi-valve" title="Multi-valve">multi-valve engines</a> over the common use of <a href="/wiki/Carburetors" class="mw-redirect" title="Carburetors">carburetors</a>. Also, the imported brands used their innovative business ethic e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Just_in_time_(business)" class="mw-redirect" title="Just in time (business)">just-in-time</a> inventory system but the U.S. Government imposed <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Export_Restraint" class="mw-redirect" title="Voluntary Export Restraint">import quotas</a> where the Japanese brands (later extended to South Korean and European marques) began outsourcing their operations by opening assembly plants in the United States (especially the Southern U.S. where import automakers were not on friendly terms with labor unions from the Rust Belt states), Canada, and Mexico to produce their mass market automobiles and light trucks. Import brands also complied with <a href="/wiki/Import_substitution_industrialization" title="Import substitution industrialization">local content laws</a> where an import automobile must have a percentage of automotive components (in the United States automobiles with 70 percent local content manufacture is considered a domestic build regardless of manufacturer) sourced from the United States, Canada, or Mexico (prior to the establishment of <a href="/wiki/NAFTA" class="mw-redirect" title="NAFTA">NAFTA</a>) and the American Automobile Labeling Act of 1994 which mandated the percentage of automotive parts content printed on the Monroney sticker of an automobile sold through a dealership. The import quota resulted in the Japanese automakers importing a limited amount of automobiles but to comply with the U.S. Government imposition of the 1981 Voluntary Export Restraints, the automakers established their respective luxury marques (Acura, Lexus, Infiniti) but run respectively by their parent manufacturers (Honda, Toyota, Nissan). GM's <a href="/wiki/Cadillac" title="Cadillac">Cadillac</a> division experimented with their <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_V8_engine#368_and_V8-6-4" title="Cadillac V8 engine">V8-6-4</a> power plant (the ancestor of the modern-day Active Fuel Management and/or variable displacement), which was a market failure.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Nonetheless, overall fuel economy increased, which was one factor leading to the subsequent <a href="/wiki/1980s_oil_glut" title="1980s oil glut">1980s oil glut</a>.</p>
