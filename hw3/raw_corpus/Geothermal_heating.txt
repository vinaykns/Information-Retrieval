<p><b>Geothermal heating</b> is the direct use of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a> for heating some applications. Humans have taken advantage of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal">geothermal</a> heat this way since the Paleolithic era. Approximately seventy countries made direct use of a total of 270 <a href="/wiki/Petajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Petajoule">PJ</a> of geothermal heating in 2004. As of 2007, 28 <a href="/wiki/Gigawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigawatt">GW</a> of geothermal heating capacity is installed around the world, satisfying 0.07% of global primary energy consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">Thermal efficiency</a> is high since no energy conversion is needed, but <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factors</a> tend to be low (around 20%) since the heat is mostly needed in the winter.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geothermal_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal (geology)">Geothermal energy</a> originates from the heat retained within the Earth since the original formation of the planet, from <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of minerals, and from <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> absorbed at the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-heatpumps9-3_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heatpumps9-3-2">[2]</a></sup> Most high temperature geothermal heat is harvested in regions close to <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate_boundaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonic plate boundaries">tectonic plate boundaries</a> where volcanic activity rises close to the surface of the Earth. In these areas, ground and groundwater can be found with temperatures higher than the target temperature of the application. However, even cold ground contains heat, below 6 metres (20&#160;ft) the undisturbed ground temperature is consistently at the Mean Annual Air Temperature<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it may be extracted with a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>.</p>

<p>There are a wide variety of applications for cheap geothermal heat. In 2004 more than half of direct geothermal heat was used for space heating, and a third was used for spas.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-1">[1]</a></sup> The remainder was used for a variety of industrial processes, desalination, domestic hot water, and agricultural applications. The cities of <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a> and <a href="/wiki/Akureyri" title="Akureyri">Akureyri</a> pipe hot water from geothermal plants under roads and pavements to melt snow. <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_desalination" title="Geothermal desalination">Geothermal desalination</a> has been demonstrated.</p>
<p>Geothermal systems tend to benefit from economies of scale, so space heating power is often distributed to multiple buildings, sometimes whole communities. This technique, long practiced throughout the world in locations such as <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Boise" class="mw-redirect" title="Boise">Boise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Klamath_Falls" class="mw-redirect" title="Klamath Falls">Klamath Falls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is known as <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EERE1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EERE1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction">Extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geothermal_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ground-coupled_heat_exchanger" title="Ground-coupled heat exchanger">Ground-coupled heat exchanger</a></div>
<p>Some parts of the world, including substantial portions of the western USA, are underlain by relatively shallow geothermal resources.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Similar conditions exist in Iceland, parts of Japan, and other geothermal hot spots around the world. In these areas, water or steam may be captured from natural <a href="/wiki/Hot_springs" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot springs">hot springs</a> and piped directly into <a href="/wiki/Radiators" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiators">radiators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>. Alternatively, the heat may come from <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> supplied by <a href="/wiki/Co-generation" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-generation">co-generation</a> from a geothermal electrical plant or from deep wells into hot aquifers. Direct geothermal heating is far more efficient than geothermal electricity generation and has less demanding temperature requirements, so it is viable over a large geographical range. If the shallow ground is hot but dry, air or water may be circulated through <a href="/wiki/Earth_tubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth tubes">earth tubes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Downhole_heat_exchanger" title="Downhole heat exchanger">downhole heat exchangers</a> which act as heat exchangers with the ground.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">Steam under pressure</a> from deep geothermal resources is also used to generate electricity from geothermal power. The <a href="/wiki/Iceland_Deep_Drilling_Project" title="Iceland Deep Drilling Project">Iceland Deep Drilling Project</a> struck a pocket of magma at 2,100m. A cemented steelcase was constructed in the hole with a perforation at the bottom close to the magma. The high temperatures and pressure of the magma steam were used to generate 36MW of electricity, making IDDP-1 the world’s first magma-enhanced geothermal system.<sup id="cite_ref-Geothermics_Magazine.2C_Vol._49_.28January_2014.29_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geothermics_Magazine.2C_Vol._49_.28January_2014.29-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In areas where the shallow ground is too cold to provide comfort directly, it is still warmer than the winter air. The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_inertia" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal inertia">thermal inertia</a> of the shallow ground retains solar energy accumulated in the summertime, and seasonal variations in ground temperature disappear completely below 10m of depth. That heat can be extracted with a geothermal heat pump more efficiently than it can be generated by conventional furnaces.<sup id="cite_ref-EERE1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EERE1-8">[8]</a></sup> Geothermal heat pumps are economically viable essentially anywhere in the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground-source_heat_pumps">Ground-source heat pumps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geothermal_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ground-source heat pumps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">Geothermal heat pump</a></div>
<p>In regions without any high temperature geothermal resources, a <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">ground-source heat pump</a> (GSHP) can provide space heating and space cooling. Like a refrigerator or air conditioner, these systems use a heat pump to force the transfer of heat from the ground to the building. Heat can be extracted from any source, no matter how cold, but a warmer source allows higher efficiency. A ground-source heat pump uses the shallow ground or ground water (typically starting at 10–12&#160;°C or 50–54&#160;°F) as a source of heat, thus taking advantage of its seasonally moderate temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-heatpumps9-4_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heatpumps9-4-11">[11]</a></sup> In contrast, an <a href="/wiki/Air-source_heat_pump" class="mw-redirect" title="Air-source heat pump">air-source heat pump</a> draws heat from the air (colder outside air) and thus requires more energy.</p>
<p>Ground source heat pumps (GSHPs) are <i>not</i> geothermal, i.e. there is no geyser providing heat to be captured. A GSHP merely accesses stored solar heat energy in the soil or rock. GSHPs circulate a carrier fluid (usually a mixture of water and small amounts of antifreeze) through closed pipe loops buried in the ground. Single-home systems can be "vertical loop field" systems with bore holes 50–400 feet (15–120&#160;m) deep <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or, if adequate land is available for extensive trenches, a "horizontal loop field" is installed approximately six feet subsurface. As the fluid circulates underground it absorbs heat from the ground and, on its return, the warmed fluid passes through the heat pump which uses electricity to extract heat from the fluid. The re-chilled fluid is sent back into the ground thus continuing the cycle. The heat extracted and that generated by the heat pump appliance as a byproduct is used to heat the house. The addition of the ground heating loop in the energy equation means that significantly more heat can be transferred to a building than if electricity alone had been used directly for heating.</p>
<p>Switching the direction of heat flow, the same system can be used to circulate the cooled water through the house for cooling in the summer months. The heat is exhausted to the relatively cooler ground (or groundwater) rather than delivering it to the hot outside air as an air conditioner does. As a result, the heat is pumped across a larger temperature difference and this leads to higher efficiency and lower energy use.<sup id="cite_ref-heatpumps9-4_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heatpumps9-4-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>This technology makes ground source heating economically viable in any geographical location. In 2004, an estimated million ground-source heat pumps with a total capacity of 15 GW extracted 88 PJ of heat energy for space heating. Global ground-source heat pump capacity is growing by 10% annually.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geothermal_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oldest_geothermal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/220px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/330px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Oldest_geothermal.jpg/440px-Oldest_geothermal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oldest_geothermal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The oldest known pool fed by a hot spring, built in the Qin dynasty in the 3rd century BC.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hot_springs" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot springs">Hot springs</a> have been used for bathing at least since Paleolithic times.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The oldest known spa is a stone pool on <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mount_Li" title="Mount Li">Mount Li</a> built in the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> in the 3rd century BC, at the same site where the <a href="/wiki/Huaqing_Chi" class="mw-redirect" title="Huaqing Chi">Huaqing Chi</a> palace was later built. Geothermal energy supplied channeled <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> for baths and houses in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> around 0 AD.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the first century AD, Romans conquered <a href="/wiki/Aquae_Sulis" title="Aquae Sulis">Aquae Sulis</a> in England and used the hot springs there to feed <a href="/wiki/Thermae" title="Thermae">public baths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">underfloor heating</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-eere_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eere-15">[15]</a></sup> The admission fees for these baths probably represents the first commercial use of geothermal power. A 1,000-year-old hot tub has been located in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, where it was built by one of the island's original settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The world's oldest working geothermal district heating system in <a href="/wiki/Chaudes-Aigues" title="Chaudes-Aigues">Chaudes-Aigues</a>, France, has been operating since the 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-utilization_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utilization-4">[4]</a></sup> The earliest industrial exploitation began in 1827 with the use of geyser steam to extract <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> from volcanic mud in <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a>, Italy.</p>
<p>In 1892, America's first district heating system in <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a> was powered directly by geothermal energy, and was soon copied in <a href="/wiki/Klamath_Falls,_Oregon" title="Klamath Falls, Oregon">Klamath Falls, Oregon</a> in 1900. A deep geothermal well was used to heat greenhouses in Boise in 1926, and geysers were used to heat greenhouses in Iceland and Tuscany at about the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-Dickson_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-17">[17]</a></sup> Charlie Lieb developed the first <a href="/wiki/Downhole_heat_exchanger" title="Downhole heat exchanger">downhole heat exchanger</a> in 1930 to heat his house. Steam and hot water from the geysers began to be used to heat homes in Iceland in 1943.</p>
<p>By this time, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Kelvin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Kelvin">Lord Kelvin</a> had already invented the <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a> in 1852, and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Zoelly" title="Heinrich Zoelly">Heinrich Zoelly</a> had patented the idea of using it to draw heat from the ground in 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-zogg_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zogg-18">[18]</a></sup> But it was not until the late 1940s that the geothermal heat pump was successfully implemented. The earliest one was probably Robert C. Webber's home-made 2.2&#160;kW direct-exchange system, but sources disagree as to the exact timeline of his invention.<sup id="cite_ref-zogg_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zogg-18">[18]</a></sup> J. Donald Kroeker designed the first commercial geothermal heat pump to heat the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Building_(Portland,_Oregon)" title="Commonwealth Building (Portland, Oregon)">Commonwealth Building (Portland, Oregon)</a> and demonstrated it in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomquist_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomquist-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Professor Carl Nielsen of <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a> built the first residential open loop version in his home in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The technology became popular in Sweden as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, and has been growing slowly in worldwide acceptance since then. The 1979 development of <a href="/wiki/Polybutylene" title="Polybutylene">polybutylene</a> pipe greatly augmented the heat pump’s economic viability.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomquist_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomquist-19">[19]</a></sup> As of 2004, there are over a million geothermal heat pumps installed worldwide providing 12 GW of thermal capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-world_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world-22">[22]</a></sup> Each year, about 80,000 units are installed in the USA and 27,000 in Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-world_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geothermal_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Geothermaldrilling.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Geothermaldrilling.jpg/220px-Geothermaldrilling.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Geothermaldrilling.jpg/330px-Geothermaldrilling.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Geothermaldrilling.jpg/440px-Geothermaldrilling.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1068" data-file-height="712" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Geothermaldrilling.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Geothermal drill machine.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Geothermal energy is a type of renewable energy that encourages conservation of natural resources. According to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geo-exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Geo-exchange">geo-exchange</a> systems save homeowners 30–70 percent in heating costs, and 20–50 percent in cooling costs, compared to conventional systems.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Geo-exchange systems also save money because they require much less maintenance. In addition to being highly reliable they are built to last for decades.</p>
<p>Some utilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Power_and_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="Kansas City Power and Light">Kansas City Power and Light</a>, offer special, lower winter rates for geothermal customers, offering even more savings.<sup id="cite_ref-heatpumps9-4_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heatpumps9-4-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_drilling_risks">Geothermal drilling risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geothermal_heating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geothermal drilling risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Staufen.Cracks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Staufen.Cracks.jpg/220px-Staufen.Cracks.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Staufen.Cracks.jpg/330px-Staufen.Cracks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Staufen.Cracks.jpg/440px-Staufen.Cracks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Staufen.Cracks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cracks at the historic Town Hall of <a href="/wiki/Staufen_im_Breisgau" title="Staufen im Breisgau">Staufen im Breisgau</a> presumed due to damage from geothermal drilling</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In geothermal heating projects the underground is penetrated by trenches or drillholes. As with all underground work, projects may cause problems if the geology of the area is poorly understood.</p>
<p>In the spring of 2007 an exploratory geothermal drilling operation was conducted to provide geothermal heat to the town hall of <a href="/wiki/Staufen_im_Breisgau" title="Staufen im Breisgau">Staufen im Breisgau</a>. After initially sinking a few millimeters, a process called <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the city center has started to rise gradually<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> causing considerable damage to buildings in the city center, affecting numerous historic houses including the town hall. It is hypothesized that the drilling perforated an <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a> layer bringing high-pressure <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> to come into contact with the anhydrite, which then began to expand. Currently no end to the rising process is in sight.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Data from the <a href="/wiki/TerraSAR-X" title="TerraSAR-X">TerraSAR-X</a> radar satellite before and after the changes confirmed the localised nature of the situation.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A geochemical process called <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a> swelling has been confirmed as the cause of these <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplifts</a>. This is a transformation of the mineral anhydrite (anhydrous calcium sulphate) into <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> (hydrous calcium sulphate). A pre-condition for this transformation is that the anhydrite is in contact with water, which is then stored in its crystalline structure.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
