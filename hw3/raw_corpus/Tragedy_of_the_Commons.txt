			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tragedy of the commons</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_Commons&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>)</span></div>

<p>The <b>tragedy of the commons</b> is an economic theory of a situation within a shared-resource system where individual users acting independently according to their own self-interest behave contrary to the common good of all users by depleting or spoiling that resource through their collective action. The concept and name originate in an essay written in 1833 by the Victorian economist <a href="/wiki/William_Forster_Lloyd" title="William Forster Lloyd">William Forster Lloyd</a>, who used a hypothetical example of the effects of unregulated grazing on <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common land</a> (then colloquially called "the commons") in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-forster_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forster-1">[1]</a></sup> The concept became widely known over a century later due to an article written by the ecologist <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Hardin" title="Garrett Hardin">Garrett Hardin</a> in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-hardin68_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hardin68-2">[2]</a></sup> In this context, <a href="/wiki/Commons" title="Commons">commons</a> is taken to mean any shared and unregulated resource such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide#In_the_Earth.27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_garbage_patch" title="Great Pacific garbage patch">oceans</a>, rivers, <a href="/wiki/Fish_stocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish stocks">fish stocks</a>, or even an office refrigerator.</p>
<p>It has been argued that the very term 'tragedy of the commons' is a misnomer <i>per se</i>, since 'the commons' originally referred to a resource owned by a community, and no individual outside the community had any access to the resource. However, the term is presently used when describing a problem where <i>all</i> individuals have equal and open access to a resource. Hence, 'tragedy of open access regimes' or simply 'the open access problem' are more apt terms.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:171</sup></p>
<p>The tragedy of the commons is often cited in connection with <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, meshing economic growth and environmental protection, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">debate over global warming</a>. It has also been used in analyzing behavior in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taxation" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxation">taxation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>.</p>
<p>Although commons have been known to collapse due to overuse (such as in over-fishing), abundant examples exist where communities cooperate or regulate to exploit common resources prudently without collapse. According to the political economist <a href="/wiki/Elinor_Ostrom" title="Elinor Ostrom">Elinor Ostrom</a>, although it is often claimed that only private ownership or government regulation can prevent the "tragedy of the commons", prudent users of a commons have a natural incentive to voluntarily cooperate in self-regulation, and history exhibits many examples of complex social schemes to sustain common resources efficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-beyond_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expositions">Expositions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Expositions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lloyd.27s_pamphlet">Lloyd's pamphlet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lloyd's pamphlet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1833, the English economist <a href="/wiki/William_Forster_Lloyd" title="William Forster Lloyd">William Forster Lloyd</a> published a pamphlet which included a hypothetical example of over-use of a common resource. This was the situation of cattle herders sharing a common parcel of land on which they are each entitled to let their cows graze, as was the custom in English villages. He postulated that if a herder put more than his allotted number of cattle on the common, <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a> could result. For each additional animal, a herder could receive additional benefits, but the whole group shared damage to the commons. If all herders made this individually rational economic decision, the common could be depleted or even destroyed, to the detriment of all.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Garrett_Hardin.27s_article">Garrett Hardin's article</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Garrett Hardin's article">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em">
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;text-align:center;">The Tragedy of the Commons</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row">Presented</th>
<td>13 December 1968</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<th scope="row">Location</th>

</tr>

<th scope="row">Author(s)</th>

<tr>
<th scope="row">Media type</th>
<td>Article</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>In 1968, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Hardin" title="Garrett Hardin">Garrett Hardin</a> explored this <a href="/wiki/Social_dilemma" title="Social dilemma">social dilemma</a> in his article "The Tragedy of the Commons", published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hardin68_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hardin68-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The essay derived its title from the pamphlet by Lloyd, which he cites, on the over-grazing of common land.</p>
<p>Hardin discussed problems that cannot be solved by technical means, as distinct from those with solutions that require "a change only in the techniques of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a>, demanding little or nothing in the way of change in <a href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)" class="mw-redirect" title="Value (personal and cultural)">human values</a> or ideas of <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>". Hardin focused on human population growth, the use of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, and the welfare state.<sup id="cite_ref-Intolerable_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intolerable-8">[8]</a></sup> Hardin argued that if individuals relied on themselves alone, and not on the relationship of society and man, then the number of children had by each family would not be of public concern. Parents breeding excessively would leave fewer descendants because they would be unable to provide for each child adequately. Such negative feedback is found in the animal kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-Intolerable_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intolerable-8">[8]</a></sup> Hardin said that if the children of improvident parents starved to death, if overbreeding was its own punishment, then there would be no public interest in controlling the breeding of families.<sup id="cite_ref-Intolerable_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intolerable-8">[8]</a></sup> Hardin blamed the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> for allowing the tragedy of the commons; where the state provides for children and supports overbreeding as a fundamental human right, <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_catastrophe" title="Malthusian catastrophe">Malthusian catastrophe</a> is inevitable. Consequently, in his article, Hardin lamented the following proposal from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> describes the family as the natural and fundamental unit of society. [Article 16<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>] It follows that any choice and decision with regard to the size of the family must irrevocably rest with the family itself, and cannot be made by anyone else.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/U_Thant" title="U Thant">U Thant</a>, Statement on Population by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">Secretary-General of the United Nations</a><sup id="cite_ref-U_Thant_Statement_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U_Thant_Statement-10">[10]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In addition, Hardin also pointed out the problem of individuals acting in rational self-interest by claiming that if all members in a group used common resources for their own gain and with no regard for others, all resources would still eventually be depleted. Overall, Hardin argued against relying on <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">conscience</a> as a means of policing commons, suggesting that this favors <a href="/wiki/Selfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Selfish">selfish</a> individuals&#160;– often known as <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" title="Free rider problem">free riders</a>&#160;– over those who are more altruistic.</p>
<p>In the context of avoiding <a href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-exploitation">over-exploitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Common-pool_resource" title="Common-pool resource">common resources</a>, Hardin concluded by restating <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Hegel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Maxim_(saying)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maxim (saying)">maxim</a> (which was quoted by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Engels</a>), "freedom is the recognition of necessity". He suggested that "freedom" completes the tragedy of the commons. By recognizing resources as commons in the first place, and by recognizing that, as such, they require management, Hardin believed that humans "can preserve and nurture other and more precious freedoms".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22Commons.22_as_a_modern_resource_concept">The "Commons" as a modern resource concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The &quot;Commons&quot; as a modern resource concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hardin's article was the start of the modern use of "Commons" as a term connoting a shared resource. As Frank van Laerhoven &amp; Elinor Ostrom have stated: "Prior to the publication of Hardin’s article on the tragedy of the commons (1968), titles containing the words 'the commons', 'common pool resources,' or 'common property' were very rare in the academic literature." They go on to say: "In 2002, Barrett and Mabry conducted a major survey of biologists to determine which publications in the twentieth century had become classic books or benchmark publications in biology. They report that Hardin’s 1968 article was the one having the greatest career impact on biologists and is the most frequently cited".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaphoric_meaning">Metaphoric meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metaphoric meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like Lloyd and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> before him, Hardin was primarily interested in the problem of <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human population growth</a>. But in his essay, he also focused on the use of larger (though finite) resources such as the Earth's atmosphere and oceans, as well as pointing out the "negative commons" of pollution (i.e., instead of dealing with the deliberate privatization of a positive resource, a "negative commons" deals with the deliberate commonization of a negative cost, pollution).</p>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a>, the tragedy of the commons should not be taken too literally. The "tragedy" is not in the word's conventional or theatric sense, nor a condemnation of the processes that lead to it. Similarly, Hardin's use of "commons" has frequently been misunderstood, leading him to later remark that he should have titled his work "The Tragedy of the Unregulated Commons".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The metaphor illustrates the argument that free access and unrestricted demand for a finite resource ultimately reduces the resource through <a href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-exploitation">over-exploitation</a>, temporarily or permanently. This occurs because the benefits of exploitation accrue to individuals or groups, each of whom is motivated to maximize use of the resource to the point in which they become reliant on it, while the costs of the exploitation are borne by all those to whom the resource is available (which may be a wider class of individuals than those who are exploiting it). This, in turn, causes demand for the resource to increase, which causes the problem to snowball until the resource collapses (even if it retains a capacity to recover). The rate at which depletion of the resource is realized depends primarily on three factors: the number of users wanting to consume the common in question, the consumptiveness of their uses, and the relative robustness of the common.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same concept is sometimes called the "tragedy of the fishers", because fishing too many fish before or during breeding could cause stocks to plummet.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_commons">Modern commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>tragedy of the commons</i> can be considered in relation to environmental issues such as <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. The commons dilemma stands as a model for a great variety of resource problems in society today, such as water, forests,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> fish, and <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-renewable energy">non-renewable energy</a> sources such as oil and coal.</p>
<p>Situations exemplifying the "tragedy of the commons" include the overfishing and destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a>, the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> runs on rivers that have been dammed – most prominently in modern times on the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">Columbia River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwest United States">Northwest United States</a>, and historically in <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> rivers – the devastation of the sturgeon fishery – in modern Russia, but historically in the United States as well – and, in terms of water supply, the limited water available in arid regions (e.g., the area of the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> water system supply, especially at <a href="/wiki/Mono_Lake" title="Mono Lake">Mono Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Owens_Lake" title="Owens Lake">Owens Lake</a>.</p>
<p>In economics, an <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a> is a cost or benefit that affects a party who did not choose to incur that cost or benefit. Negative externalities are a well-known feature of the "tragedy of the commons". For example, driving cars has many negative externalities; these include <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a>, and traffic accidents. Every time 'Person A' gets in a car, it becomes more likely that 'Person Z'&#160;–  and millions of others&#160;–  will suffer in each of those areas.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Economists often urge the government to adopt policies that "internalize" an externality.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_to_evolutionary_biology">Application to evolutionary biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Application to evolutionary biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A parallel was drawn recently between the tragedy of the commons and the competing behaviour of parasites that through acting selfishly eventually diminish or destroy their common host.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The idea has also been applied to areas such as the evolution of <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sexual_conflict" title="Sexual conflict">sexual conflict</a>, where males may fatally harm females when competing for matings.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It is also raised as a question in studies of social insects, where scientists wish to understand why insect workers do not undermine the "common good" by laying eggs of their own and causing a breakdown of the society.</p>
<p>The idea of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_suicide" title="Evolutionary suicide">evolutionary suicide</a>, where adaptation at the level of the individual causes the whole species or population to be driven <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>, can be seen as an extreme form of an evolutionary tragedy of the commons.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> From an evolutionary point of view, the creation of the tragedy of the commons in pathogenic microbes may provide us with advanced therapeutic methods.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commons_dilemma">Commons dilemma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commons dilemma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="The_Commons_Dilemma"></span> The <i>commons dilemma</i> is a specific class of <a href="/wiki/Social_dilemma" title="Social dilemma">social dilemma</a> in which people's short-term selfish interests are at odds with long-term group interests and the <a href="/wiki/Common_good" title="Common good">common good</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In academia, a range of related terminology has also been used as shorthand for the theory or aspects of it, including <i>resource dilemma</i>, <i>take-some dilemma</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Common_pool_resource" class="mw-redirect" title="Common pool resource">common pool resource</a></i>.</p>
<p>Commons dilemma researchers have studied conditions under which groups and communities are likely to under- or <a href="/wiki/Over-harvest" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-harvest">over-harvest</a> common resources in both the laboratory and field. Research programs have concentrated on a number of motivational, strategic, and structural factors that might be conducive to management of commons.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, which constructs mathematical models for individuals' behavior in strategic situations, the corresponding "game", developed by Hardin, is known as the Commonize Costs&#160;– Privatize Profits Game (<a href="/wiki/CC%E2%80%93PP_game" title="CC–PP game">CC–PP game</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_factors">Psychological factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Psychological factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kopelman, Weber, &amp; Messick (2002), in a review of the experimental research on cooperation in commons dilemmas, identify nine classes of independent variables that influence cooperation in commons dilemmas: social motives, gender, payoff structure, uncertainty, power and status, group size, communication, causes, and frames. They organize these classes and distinguish between psychological individual differences (stable personality traits) and situational factors (the environment). Situational factors include both the task (social and decision structure) and the perception of the task.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Empirical findings support the theoretical argument that the cultural group is a critical factor that needs to be studied in the context of situational variables.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Rather than behaving in line with economic incentives, people are likely to approach the decision to cooperate with an appropriateness framework.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> An expanded, four factor model of the Logic of Appropriateness,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> suggests that the cooperation is better explained by the question: "What does a person like me (identity) do (rules) in a situation like this (recognition) given this culture (group)?"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_factors">Strategic factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Strategic factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strategy" title="Strategy">Strategic</a> factors also matter in commons dilemmas. One often-studied strategic factor is the order in which people take harvests from the resource. In simultaneous play, all people harvest at the same time, whereas in sequential play people harvest from the pool according to a predetermined sequence&#160;– first, second, third, etc. There is a clear order effect in the latter games: the harvests of those who come first&#160;– the leaders&#160;– are higher than the harvest of those coming later&#160;– the followers. The interpretation of this effect is that the first players feel entitled to take more. With sequential play, individuals adopt a first come-first served rule, whereas with simultaneous play people may adopt an equality rule. Another strategic factor is the ability to build up reputations. Research<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2011)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> found that people take less from the common pool in public situations than in anonymous private situations. Moreover, those who harvest less gain greater prestige and influence within their group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_factors">Structural factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Structural factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much research has focused on when and why people would like to structurally rearrange the commons to prevent a tragedy. Hardin stated in his analysis of the tragedy of the commons that "Freedom in a commons brings ruin to all."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> One of the proposed solutions is to appoint a leader to regulate access to the common. Groups are more likely to endorse a leader when a common resource is being depleted and when managing a common resource is perceived as a difficult task. Groups prefer leaders who are elected, democratic, and prototypical of the group, and these leader types are more successful in enforcing cooperation. A general aversion to autocratic <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leadership</a> exists, although it may be an effective solution, possibly because of the fear of power abuse and corruption.</p>
<p>The provision of rewards and punishments may also be effective in preserving common resources. Selective punishments for overuse can be effective in promoting domestic water and energy conservation&#160;– for example, through installing water and electricity meters in houses. Selective rewards work, provided that they are open to everyone. An experimental carpool lane in the Netherlands failed because car commuters did not feel they were able to organize a carpool.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The rewards do not have to be tangible. In Canada, utilities considered putting "smiley faces" on electricity bills of customers below the average consumption of that customer`s neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions">Solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Externality#Possible_solutions" title="Externality">Externality §&#160;Possible solutions</a></div>
<p>Articulating solutions to the tragedy of the commons is one of the main problems of <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a>. In many situations, locals implement (often complex) social schemes that work well. The best governmental solution may be to do nothing. When these fail, there are many possible governmental solutions such as privatization, internalizing the externalities, and regulation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-governmental_solution">Non-governmental solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Non-governmental solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometimes the best governmental solution may be to do nothing. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Axelrod" title="Robert Axelrod">Robert Axelrod</a> contends that even self-interested individuals will often find ways to cooperate, because collective restraint serves both the collective and individual interests.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Anthropologist G. N. Appell criticized those who cited Hardin to "impos[e] their own economic and environmental rationality on other social systems of which they have incomplete understanding and knowledge."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Political scientist <a href="/wiki/Elinor_Ostrom" title="Elinor Ostrom">Elinor Ostrom</a>, who was awarded 2009's <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences" title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences">Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences</a> for her work on the issue, and others revisited Hardin's work in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> They found the tragedy of the commons not as prevalent or as difficult to solve as Hardin maintained, since locals have often come up with solutions to the commons problem themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-urlOstrom_revisits_the_commons_in_Science_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlOstrom_revisits_the_commons_in_Science-43">[43]</a></sup> For example, it was found that a commons in the Swiss Alps has been run by a collective of farmers there to their mutual and individual benefit since 1517, in spite of the farmers also having access to their own farmland. In general, it is in the users of a commons interests to keep the common running and complex social schemes are often invented by the users for maintaining them at optimum efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-beyond_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbes_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, Geographer Douglas L. Johnson remarks that many <a href="/wiki/Nomadic_pastoralism" title="Nomadic pastoralism">nomadic pastoralist</a> societies of Africa and the Middle East in fact "balanced local stocking ratios against seasonal rangeland conditions in ways that were ecologically sound", reflecting a desire for lower risk rather than higher profit; in spite of this, it was often the case that "the nomad was blamed for problems that were not of his own making and were a product of alien forces."<sup id="cite_ref-johnson1993_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson1993-44">[44]</a></sup> Independently finding precedent in the opinions of previous scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> as well as common currency in antagonistic cultural attitudes towards non-sedentary peoples,<sup id="cite_ref-johnson1993_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson1993-44">[44]</a></sup> governments and international organizations have made use of Hardin's work to help justify restrictions on land access and the eventual <a href="/wiki/Sedentarization" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedentarization">sedentarization</a> of pastoral nomads despite its weak empirical basis. Examining relations between historically nomadic <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouin</a> Arabs and the <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a> state in the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Dawn_Chatty" title="Dawn Chatty">Dawn Chatty</a> notes that "Hardin's argument [...] was curiously accepted as the fundamental explanation for the degradation of the steppe land" in development schemes for the arid interior of the country, downplaying the larger role of agricultural overexploitation in desertification as it melded with prevailing nationalist ideology which viewed nomads as socially backward and economically harmful.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elinor_Ostrom" title="Elinor Ostrom">Elinor Ostrom</a>, and her colleagues looked at how real-world communities manage communal resources, such as fisheries, land irrigation systems, and farmlands, and they identified a number of factors conducive to successful resource management. One factor is the resource itself; resources with definable boundaries (e.g., land) can be preserved much more easily. A second factor is resource dependence; there must be a perceptible threat of resource depletion, and it must be difficult to find substitutes. The third is the presence of a community; small and stable populations with a thick social network and social norms promoting conservation do better.<sup id="cite_ref-beyond_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-4">[4]</a></sup> A final condition is that there be appropriate community-based rules and procedures in place with built-in incentives for responsible use and punishments for overuse. When the commons is taken over by non-locals, those solutions can no longer be used.<sup id="cite_ref-urlOstrom_revisits_the_commons_in_Science_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlOstrom_revisits_the_commons_in_Science-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governmental_solutions">Governmental solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Governmental solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Governmental solutions may be necessary when the above conditions are not met (such as a community being too big or too unstable to provide a thick social network). Examples of government regulation include privatization, internalizing the externalities, and regulation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privatization">Privatization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Privatization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One solution for some resources is to convert common good into private property, giving the new owner an incentive to enforce its sustainability. <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">Libertarians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberals</a> cite the tragedy of the commons as an example of what happens when <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">Lockean</a> property rights to homestead resources are prohibited by a government.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> They argue that the solution to the tragedy of the commons is to allow individuals to take over the property rights of a resource, that is, to privatize it.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a typical example, governmental regulations can limit the amount of a common good that is available for use by any individual. Permit systems for extractive economic activities including mining, fishing, hunting, livestock raising and timber extraction are examples of this approach. Similarly, limits to pollution are examples of governmental intervention on behalf of the commons. This idea is used by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Moon_Treaty" title="Moon Treaty">Moon Treaty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_Sea_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of the Sea Treaty">Law of the Sea Treaty</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Convention">World Heritage Convention</a> which involves the international law principle that designates some areas or resources the <a href="/wiki/Common_Heritage_of_Mankind" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Heritage of Mankind">Common Heritage of Mankind</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Hardin's essay, he proposed that the solution to the problem of overpopulation must be based on "mutual coercion, mutually agreed upon" and result in "relinquishing the freedom to breed". Hardin discussed this topic further in a 1979 book, <i>Managing the Commons,</i> co-written with <a href="/wiki/John_A._Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="John A. Baden">John A. Baden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> He framed this prescription in terms of needing to restrict the "<a href="/wiki/Reproductive_rights" title="Reproductive rights">reproductive right</a>", to safeguard all other <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a>. Several countries have a variety of <a href="/wiki/Antinatalistic_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Antinatalistic politics">population control laws</a> in place.</p>
<p>German historian <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Radkau" title="Joachim Radkau">Joachim Radkau</a> thought Hardin advocates strict management of common goods via increased government involvement or international regulation bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-Rad_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rad-50">[50]</a></sup> An asserted impending "tragedy of the commons" is frequently warned of as a consequence of the adoption of policies which restrict <a href="/wiki/Property#Theories_of_property" title="Property">private property</a> and espouse expansion of public property.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internalizing_externalities">Internalizing externalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Internalizing externalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Privatization works when the person who owns the property (or rights of access to that property) pays the full price of its exploitation. As discussed above negative externalities (negative results, such as air or water pollution, that do not proportionately affect the user of the resource) is often a feature driving the tragedy of the commons. <i>Internalizing the externalities</i>, in other words ensuring that the users of resource pay for all of the consequences of its use, can provide an alternate solution between privatization and regulation. One example is gasoline taxes which include both the cost of road maintenance and of air pollution. This solution can provide the flexibility of privatization while minimizing the amount of government oversight and overhead that is needed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comedy_of_the_Commons">Comedy of the Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Comedy of the Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In certain cases, exploiting a resource more may be a good thing. Carol M. Rose, in an 1986 article, discussed the concept of the "comedy of the commons", where the public property in question exhibits "increasing returns to scale" in usage ("the more the merrier", hence the term), in that the more people use the resource, the higher the benefit to each one. Rose cites as examples commerce and group recreational activities. According to Rose, public resources with the "comedic" characteristic may suffer from under-investment rather than over usage.<sup id="cite_ref-rose_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rose-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragedy_of_the_commons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Derrick_Jensen" title="Derrick Jensen">Derrick Jensen</a> claims the tragedy of the commons is used as <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> for <a href="/wiki/Private_ownership" class="mw-redirect" title="Private ownership">private ownership</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> He says it has been used by the political <a href="/wiki/Right_wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Right wing">right wing</a> to hasten the final enclosure of the "common resources" of <a href="/wiki/Third_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Third world">third world</a> and indigenous people worldwide, as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Consensus" title="Washington Consensus">Washington Consensus</a>. He argues that in true situations, those who abuse the commons would have been warned to desist and if they failed would have punitive sanctions against them. He says that rather than being called "The Tragedy of the Commons", it should be called "the Tragedy of the Failure of the Commons".</p>
<p>Hardin's work was also criticised<sup id="cite_ref-urlwww.econ.cam.ac.uk_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlwww.econ.cam.ac.uk-55">[55]</a></sup> as historically inaccurate in failing to account for the <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic transition</a>, and for failing to distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Common-pool_resource" title="Common-pool resource">common property</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_access_(infrastructure)" title="Open access (infrastructure)">open access</a> resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Ciriacy_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ciriacy-56">[56]</a></sup> In a similar vein, Carl Dahlman argues that commons were effectively managed to prevent overgrazing.<sup id="cite_ref-urlSpringerLink_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlSpringerLink-57">[57]</a></sup> Likewise, Susan Jane Buck Cox argues that the common land example used to argue this economic concept is on very weak historical ground, and misrepresents what she terms was actually the "triumph of the commons": the successful common usage of land for many centuries. She argues that social changes and agricultural innovation, and not the behaviour of the commoners, led to the demise of the commons.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some authors, like <a href="/wiki/Yochai_Benkler" title="Yochai Benkler">Yochai Benkler</a>, say that with the rise of the Internet and digitalisation, an economics system based on commons becomes possible again. He wrote in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Networks" title="The Wealth of Networks">The Wealth of Networks</a></i> in 2006 that cheap computing power plus networks enable people to produce valuable products through non-commercial processes of interaction: "as human beings and as social beings, rather than as market actors through the price system". He uses the term 'networked <a href="/wiki/Information_economy" title="Information economy">information economy</a>' to describe a "system of production, distribution, and consumption of information goods characterized by decentralized individual action carried out through widely distributed, nonmarket means that do not depend on market strategies."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> He also coined the term '<a href="/wiki/Commons-based_peer_production" title="Commons-based peer production">commons-based peer production</a>' to describe collaborative efforts based on sharing information.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Examples of commons-based peer production are <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open_source_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Free and open source software">free and open source software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open-source_hardware" title="Open-source hardware">open-source hardware</a>.</p>
