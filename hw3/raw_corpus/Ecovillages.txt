<p><b>Ecovillages</b> are <a href="/wiki/Intentional_community" title="Intentional community">intentional communities</a> whose goal is to become more socially, economically and ecologically <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a>. Most range from a population of 50 to 150 individuals, although some are smaller, and larger ecovillages of up to 2,000 individuals exist as networks of smaller subcommunities. Certain ecovillages have grown by the addition of individuals, families, or other small groups who are not necessarily members settling on the periphery of the ecovillage and effectively participating in the ecovillage community.</p>
<p>Ecovillagers are united by shared <a href="/wiki/Ecological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological">ecological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">social-economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_ecology" title="Spiritual ecology">cultural-spiritual</a> values.<sup id="cite_ref-Redefining_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redefining-1">[1]</a></sup> Concretely, ecovillagers seek alternatives to ecologically destructive electrical, water, transportation, and waste-treatment systems, as well as the larger social systems that mirror and support them. Many see the breakdown of traditional forms of community, wasteful <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerist</a> lifestyles, the destruction of natural habitat, <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Factory_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Factory farming">factory farming</a>, and over-reliance on fossil fuels as trends that must be changed to avert ecological disaster and create richer and more fulfilling ways of life.</p>
<p>Ecovillages offer small-scale communities with <a href="/wiki/Low-impact_development_(UK)" title="Low-impact development (UK)">minimal ecological impact</a> or regenerative impacts as an alternative. However, such communities often cooperate with peer villages in networks of their own (<i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/Global_Ecovillage_Network" title="Global Ecovillage Network">Global Ecovillage Network</a> for an example). This model of <a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action</a> is similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Ten_Thousand_Villages" title="Ten Thousand Villages">Ten Thousand Villages</a>, which supports the <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a> of goods worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gilman" title="Robert Gilman">Robert Gilman</a> set out a definition of an ecovillage that became standard. Gilman defined an ecovillage as a:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Human-scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Human-scale">human-scale</a> full-featured settlement in which human activities are harmlessly integrated into the natural world in a way that is supportive of healthy human development, and can be successfully continued into the indefinite future."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In recent years, Gilman has stated that he would also add the criterion that an ecovillage must have <i>multiple centres of initiative</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kosha Joubert, President of the Global Ecovillage Network, more recently has defined an Ecovillage as an intentional or traditional community consciously designed by its inhabitants, in which people consciously value what they have and integrate this with innovative technologies to make their lives more sustainable, and the whole process is owned by the people living there. The aim is to regenerate social and natural environments. In this view, achieving sustainability is not enough; it is vital also to regenerate the social and environmental fabric of life, and across all four dimensions of sustainability: social, environmental, economic and cultural.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eco-villages have developed in recent years as technology has improved so they have more sophisticated structures as noted by Baydoun, M. 2013.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern-day desire for community was most notably characterized by the communal movement of the 1960s and 1970s, which became more focused and organized in the <a href="/wiki/Cohousing" title="Cohousing">cohousing</a> and related alternative-community movements of the mid-1980s. Then, in 1991, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gilman" title="Robert Gilman">Robert Gilman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diane_Gilman" title="Diane Gilman">Diane Gilman</a> co-authored a germinal study called "Ecovillages and Sustainable Communities" for Gaia Trust, in which the ecological and communitarian themes were brought together.</p>
<p>The ecovillage movement began to coalesce at the annual autumn conference of <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Ecovillage" title="Findhorn Ecovillage">Findhorn</a>, in Scotland, in 1995. The conference was called: "Ecovillages and Sustainable Communities", and conference organizers turned away hundreds of applicants. According to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ross_Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ross Jackson (page does not exist)">Ross Jackson</a>, "somehow they had struck a chord that resonated far and wide. The word 'ecovillage'... thus became part of the language of the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Creatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural Creatives">Cultural Creatives</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Movement_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Movement-5">[5]</a></sup> After that conference, many intentional communities, including Findhorn, began calling themselves "ecovillages", giving birth to a new movement. The <a href="/wiki/Global_Ecovillage_Network" title="Global Ecovillage Network">Global Ecovillage Network</a>, formed by a group of about 25 people from various countries who had attended the Findhorn conference, crystallized the event by linking hundreds of small projects from around the world, who had with similar goals but had formerly operated without knowledge of each other. Gaia Trust, Denmark, agreed to fund the network for its first five years.<sup id="cite_ref-Movement_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Movement-5">[5]</a></sup> Today, there are self-identified ecovillages in over 70 countries on six continents.<sup id="cite_ref-Inside_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inside-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 1995 conference a number of the early members of the Global Ecovillage Network have tried other approaches to eco-village building in an attempt to build settlements that would be attractive to mainstream culture in order to make sustainable development more generally accepted. One of these with some degree of success is <a href="/wiki/Living_Villages" title="Living Villages">Living Villages</a> and The Wintles where eco-houses are arranged so that social connectivity is maximised and residents have shared food growing areas, woodland and animal husbandry for greater sustainability.</p>
<p>The principles on which ecovillages rely can be applied to <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural">rural</a> settings, as well as to developing and developed countries. Advocates seek a sustainable lifestyle (for example, of <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_simplicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Voluntary simplicity">voluntary simplicity</a>) for inhabitants with a minimum of trade outside the local area, or <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>. Many advocates also seek independence from existing infrastructures, although others, particularly in more urban settings, pursue more integration with existing infrastructure. Rural ecovillages are usually based on <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> and other approaches which promote <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> function and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Ecovillages, whether urban or rural, tend to integrate community and ecological values within a principle-based approach to sustainability, such as permaculture design.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Johnathan Dawson, former president of the Global Ecovillage Network, describes five ecovillage principles in his 2006 book <i>Ecovillages: New Frontiers for Sustainability</i>:</p>
<ol>
<li>They are not government-sponsored projects, but grassroots initiatives.</li>
<li>Their residents value and practice community living.</li>
<li>Their residents are not overly dependent on government, corporate or other centralized sources for water, food, shelter, power and other basic necessities. Rather, they attempt to provide these resources themselves.</li>
<li>Their residents have a strong sense of shared values, often characterized in spiritual terms.</li>
<li>They often serve as research and demonstration sites, offering educational experiences for others.<sup id="cite_ref-Inside_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inside-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>The imperative for alternatives to radically inefficient energy-use patterns, in particular automobile-enabled <a href="/wiki/Suburban_sprawl" class="mw-redirect" title="Suburban sprawl">suburban sprawl</a>, was brought into focus by the energy crises of the 1970s. The term "eco-village" was introduced by Georgia Institute of Technology Professor George Ramsey in a 1978 address, "Passive Energy Applications for the Built Environment", to the First World Energy Conference of the Association of Energy Engineers,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to describe small-scale, car-free, close-in developments, including suburban infill, arguing that "the great energy waste in the United States is not in its technology; it is in its lifestyle and concept of living."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ramsey's article includes a sketch for a "self-sufficient pedestrian solar village" by one of his students that looks very similar to eco-villages today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Effective government is important to Eco-villages. It provides <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">education charity for promotion in sustainable lifestyle</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2016)">clarification needed</span></a> - Much in this section is unclear or poorly phrased</i>]</sup> (Cunningham and Wearing, 2013). While the first generation of ecovillagers tended to adopt <a href="/wiki/Consensus_decision-making" title="Consensus decision-making">consensus decision-making</a> as a governance method, some difficulties with consensus as an everyday decision-making method emerged: it can be extremely time-intensive, and decisions too often could be blocked by a few intransigent members.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> More recently many ecovillages have moved toward <a href="/wiki/Sociocracy" title="Sociocracy">sociocracy</a> and related alternative decision-making methods.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also, ecovillages look for alternative government which emphasis on deeper connections with ecology than economy.</p>
