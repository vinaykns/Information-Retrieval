			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Plastic recycling</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastics_recycling&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Plastics recycling">Plastics recycling</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Plastic recycling</b> is the process of recovering scrap or waste <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> and reprocessing the material into useful products. Since plastic is non-<a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradable</a>, recycling is a part of global efforts to reduce plastic in the waste stream, especially the approximately eight million metric tonnes of waste plastic that enter the Earth's ocean every year.<sup id="cite_ref-HardestyWilcox_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HardestyWilcox-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jambeck1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jambeck1-2">[2]</a></sup> This helps to reduce the high rates of <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution" title="Plastic pollution">plastic pollution</a>.</p>
<p>Plastic recycling includes taking any type of plastic, sorting it into different polymers and then chipping it and then melting it down into pellets. After this stage, it can then be used to make items of any kind such as plastic chairs and tables. Soft Plastics are also recycled such as <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> film and bags. This closed-loop operation has taken place since the 1970s and has made the production of some plastic products amongst the most efficient operations today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Compared with lucrative recycling of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, and similar to the low value of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, plastic polymers recycling is often more challenging because of low density and low value. There are also numerous technical hurdles to overcome when recycling plastic.</p>
<p>A macro molecule interacts with its environment along its entire length, so total energy involved in mixing it is largely due to the product side stoichiometry (see <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>). Heating alone is not enough to dissolve such a large molecule, so plastics must often be of nearly identical composition to mix efficiently.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>When different types of plastics are melted together, they tend to phase-separate, like oil and water, and set in these layers. The <a href="/wiki/Phase_boundary" title="Phase boundary">phase boundaries</a> cause structural weakness in the resulting material, meaning that <a href="/wiki/Polymer_blend" title="Polymer blend">polymer blends</a> are useful in only limited applications. The two most widely manufactured plastics, <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> behave this way, which limits their utility for recycling. Recently, a method that uses "molecular stitches"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or "macromolecular welding flux" has beeen developed<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> that could revolutionize how these plastics are recycled.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another barrier to recycling is the widespread use of <a href="/wiki/Dyes" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyes">dyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filler_(materials)" title="Filler (materials)">fillers</a>, and other additives in plastics. The polymer is generally too <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> to economically remove fillers, and would be damaged by many of the processes that could cheaply remove the added dyes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Additives are less widely used in beverage containers and <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bag" title="Plastic bag">plastic bags</a>, allowing them to be recycled more often. Yet another barrier to removing large quantities of plastic from the waste stream and landfills is the fact that many common but small plastic items lack the universal triangle recycling symbol and accompanying number. An example is the billions of plastic utensils commonly distributed at fast food restaurants or sold for use at picnics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The percentage of plastic that can be fully recycled, rather than downcycled or go to waste can be increased when manufacturers of packaged goods minimize mixing of packaging materials and eliminate contaminants. The Association of Plastics Recyclers have issued a Design Guide for Recyclability.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable plastics</a> is increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">Processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before recycling, most plastics are sorted according to their resin type. In the past, plastic reclaimers used the <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a> (RIC), a method of categorization of polymer types, which was developed by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Plastics_Industry" title="Society of the Plastics Industry">Society of the Plastics Industry</a> in 1988. <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a>, commonly referred to as PET, for instance, has a resin code of 1. Most plastic reclaimers do not rely on the RIC now; they use automatic sort systems to identify the resin. Ranging from manual sorting and picking of plastic materials; to mechanized automation processes that involve shredding, sieving, separation by rates of density i.e. air, liquid, or magnetic, and complex spectrophotometric distribution technologies e.g. UV/VIS, NIR, Laser, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some plastic products are also separated by color before they are recycled. The plastic recyclables are then shredded. These shredded fragments then undergo processes to eliminate impurities like paper labels. This material is melted and often <a href="/wiki/Extrude" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrude">extruded</a> into the form of pellets which are then used to manufacture other products.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_depolymerization">Thermal depolymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thermal depolymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Depolymerization" title="Depolymerization">Depolymerization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_depolymerization" title="Thermal depolymerization">Thermal depolymerization</a></div>
<p>Another process involves the conversion of assorted polymers into <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> by a much less precise <a href="/wiki/Thermal_depolymerization" title="Thermal depolymerization">thermal depolymerization</a> process. Such a process would be able to accept almost any polymer or mix of polymers, including <a href="/wiki/Thermoset" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermoset">thermoset</a> materials such as vulcanized <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a> separation of wastes and the biopolymers in <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a> and other agricultural waste. Like natural petroleum, the chemicals produced can be made into fuels as well as polymers. A pilot plant of this type exists in <a href="/wiki/Carthage,_Missouri" title="Carthage, Missouri">Carthage, Missouri</a>, United States, using turkey waste as input material. <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">Gasification</a> is a similar process, but is not technically recycling, since polymers are not likely to become the result.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_plastic_pyrolysis_to_fuel_oil">Waste plastic pyrolysis to fuel oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Waste plastic pyrolysis to fuel oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Plastic Pyrolysis can convert petroleum based waste streams such as plastics into quality fuels, carbons. <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given below is the list of suitable plastic raw materials for pyrolysis:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mixed plastic (HDPE, LDPE, PE, PP, Nylon, Teflon, PS, ABS, FRP etc.)</li>
<li>Mixed waste plastic from waste paper mill</li>
<li>Multi Layered Plastic</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_compression">Heat compression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heat compression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yet another process that is gaining ground with startup companies (especially in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>) is heat compression.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The heat compression process takes all unsorted, cleaned plastic in all forms, from soft plastic bags to hard <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a>, and mixes the load in tumblers (large rotating drums resembling giant <a href="/wiki/Clothes_dryer" title="Clothes dryer">clothes dryers</a>). The most obvious benefit to this method is the fact that all plastic is recyclable, not just matching forms. However, criticism rises from the energy costs of rotating the drums, and heating the post-melt pipes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributed_recycling">Distributed recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distributed recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For some waste plastics, recent technical devices called <a href="/wiki/Recyclebot" title="Recyclebot">recyclebots</a><sup id="cite_ref-RPJ_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPJ-15">[15]</a></sup> enable a form of distributed recycling. Preliminary <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Life-cycle analysis">life-cycle analysis</a>(LCA) indicates that such distributed recycling of <a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a> to make filament of <a href="/wiki/3-D_printers" class="mw-redirect" title="3-D printers">3-D printers</a> in rural regions is energetically favorable to either using virgin resin or conventional recycling processes because of reductions in transportation energy<sup id="cite_ref-LCA_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LCA-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_processes">Other processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A process has also been developed in which many kinds of plastic can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> source in the recycling of scrap <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There is also a possibility of mixed recycling of different plastics, which does not require their separation. It is called Compatibilization and requires use of special chemical bridging agents compatibilizers. It can help to keep the quality of recycled material and to skip often expensive and inefficient preliminary scanning of waste plastics streams and their separation/purification.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PET">PET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: PET">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PET_bottle_recycling" title="PET bottle recycling">PET bottle recycling</a></div>
<p>Post-consumer polyethylene terephthalate (PET or PETE) containers are sorted into different colour fractions, and baled for onward sale. PET recyclers further sort the baled bottles and they are washed and flaked (or flaked and then washed). Non-PET fractions such as caps and labels are removed during this process. The clean flake is dried. Further treatment can take place e.g. melt filtering and pelletising or various treatments to produce food-contact-approved recycled PET (RPET).</p>
<p>RPET has been widely used to produce polyester fibres.<sup id="cite_ref-eartheasy1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eartheasy1-20">[20]</a></sup> This sorted post-consumer PET waste is crushed, chopped into flakes, pressed into bales, and offered for sale.<sup id="cite_ref-innovations-report1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-innovations-report1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>One use for this recycled PET that has recently started to become popular is to create fabrics to be used in the clothing industry.<sup id="cite_ref-thisisbrandx1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thisisbrandx1-22">[22]</a></sup> The fabrics are created by spinning the PET flakes into thread and yarn.<sup id="cite_ref-innovations-report1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-innovations-report1-21">[21]</a></sup> This is done just as easily as creating polyester from brand new PET.<sup id="cite_ref-rewarestore1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rewarestore1-23">[23]</a></sup> The recycled PET thread or yarn can be used either alone or together with other fibers to create a very wide variety of fabrics. Traditionally these fabrics are used to create strong, durable, rough, products, such as jackets, coat, shoes, bags, hats, and accessories since they are usually too rough for direct skin contact and can cause irritation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, these types of fabrics have become more popular as a result of the public's growing awareness of environmental issues. Numerous fabric and clothing manufacturers have capitalized on this trend.</p>
<p>Other major outlets for RPET are new containers (food-contact or non-food-contact) produced either by (injection stretch blow) moulding into bottles and jars or by thermoforming APET sheet to produce clam shells, blister packs and collation trays. These applications used 46% of all RPET produced in Europe in 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other applications, such as strapping tape, injection-moulded engineering components and even building materials account for 13% of the 2010 RPET production.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the United States the recycling rate for PET packaging was 31.2% in 2013, according to a report from The National Association for PET Container Resources (NAPCOR) and The Association of Postconsumer Plastic Recyclers (APR). A total of 1,798 million pounds was collected and 475 million pounds of recycled PET used out of a total of 5,764 million pounds of PET bottles.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HDPE">HDPE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: HDPE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plastic #2, high-density polyethylene (<a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a>) is a commonly recycled plastic. It is typically <a href="/wiki/Downcycling" title="Downcycling">downcycled</a> into <a href="/wiki/Plastic_lumber" title="Plastic lumber">plastic lumber</a>, tables, roadside curbs, benches, truck cargo liners, trash receptacles, stationery (e.g. rulers) and other durable plastic products and is usually in demand.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PS">PS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: PS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f7/Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg/90px-Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg.png" width="90" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f7/Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg/135px-Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f7/Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg/180px-Resin-identification-code-6-PS.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="675" data-file-height="824" /></a>

<p>Most polystyrene products are currently not recycled due to the lack of incentive to invest in the compactors and logistical systems required. As a result, manufacturers cannot obtain sufficient scrap. Expanded polystyrene (EPS) scrap can easily be added to products such as EPS insulation sheets and other EPS materials for construction applications. When it is not used to make more EPS, foam scrap can be turned into clothes hangers, park benches, flower pots, toys, rulers, stapler bodies, seedling containers, picture frames, and architectural molding from recycled PS.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recycled EPS is also used in many metal casting operations. <a href="/wiki/Rastra" title="Rastra">Rastra</a> is made from EPS that is combined with cement to be used as an insulating amendment in the making of concrete foundations and walls. Since 1993, American manufacturers have produced insulating concrete forms made with approximately 80% recycled EPS.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_plastics">Other plastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other plastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The white plastic polystyrene <a href="/wiki/Foam_peanut" title="Foam peanut">foam peanuts</a> used as packing material are often accepted by shipping stores for reuse.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Successful trials in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> have shown that plastic films recovered from mixed municipal waste streams can be recycled into useful household products such as <a href="/wiki/Bucket" title="Bucket">buckets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Plasticulture" title="Plasticulture">agricultural plastics</a> such as mulch film, <a href="/wiki/Drip_tape" title="Drip tape">drip tape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a> bags are being diverted from the waste stream and successfully recycled<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> into much larger products for industrial applications such as <a href="/wiki/Railroad_tie#Plastic.2FRubber_Composite" title="Railroad tie">plastic composite railroad ties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Historically, these agricultural plastics have primarily been either landfilled or burned on-site in the fields of individual farms.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> reports that Dr. S. Madhu of the <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> Highway Research Institute, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, has formulated a road surface that includes recycled plastic: aggregate, <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> (asphalt) with plastic that has been shredded and melted at a temperature below 220 degrees C (428&#160;°F) to avoid pollution. This road surface is claimed to be very durable and monsoon rain resistant. The plastic is sorted by hand, which is economical in India. The test road used 60&#160;kg of plastic for an approximately 500m-long, 8m-wide, two-lane road. The process chops thin-film road-waste into a light fluff of tiny flakes that hot-mix plants can uniformly introduce into viscous bitumen with a customized dosing machine. Tests at both Bangalore and the Indian Road Research Centre indicate that roads built using this 'KK process' will have longer useful lives and better resistance to cold, heat, cracking, and rutting, by a factor of three.<sup id="cite_ref-ISEBIndia_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISEBIndia-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling_rates">Recycling rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Recycling rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The quantity of post-consumer plastics recycled has increased every year since at least 1990, but rates lag far behind those of other items, such as newspaper (about 80%) and <a href="/wiki/Corrugated_fiberboard" title="Corrugated fiberboard">corrugated fiberboard</a> (about 70%).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Overall, U.S. post-consumer plastic waste for 2008 was estimated at 33.6 million tons; 2.2 million tons (6.5%) were recycled and 2.6 million tons (7.7%) were burned for energy; 28.9 million tons, or 85.5%, were discarded in landfills.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_and_energy_potential">Economic and energy potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economic and energy potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, the price of PET dropped from $370/ton in the US to $20 in November.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> PET prices had returned to their long-term averages by May 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recycling one ton of plastic can save 5,774 kWh of energy, 98,000,000 btus of energy, 1,000-2,000 gallons of gasoline, 685 gallons of oil, 30 cubic yards of landfill space, 48,000 gallons of water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumer_education">Consumer education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Consumer education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the UK, the amount of post-consumer plastic being recycled is relatively low,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> due in part to a lack of recycling facilities.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plastics_2020_Challenge" title="Plastics 2020 Challenge">Plastics 2020 Challenge</a> was founded in 2009 by the plastics industry with the aim of engaging the British public in a nationwide debate about the use, reuse and disposal of plastics, and hosts a series of online debates on its website framed around the waste hierarchy.</p>
<p>There is a facility in <a href="/wiki/Worksop" title="Worksop">Worksop</a> capable of recycling 60–80 thousand metric tonnes a year.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Northern Ireland, the rate of recycling is relatively low at only 37.4%. However, emerging technologies are helping to increase the recycling rates of items previously landfilled e.g. mixed hard plastics.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_identification_code">Plastic identification code</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Plastic identification code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">Resin identification code</a></div>
<p>Five groups of plastic polymers,<sup id="cite_ref-singapore_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singapore-40">[40]</a></sup> each with specific properties, are used worldwide for packaging applications (see table below). Each group of plastic polymer can be identified by its Plastic Identification code (PIC), usually a number or a letter abbreviation. For instance, Low-Density Polyethylene can be identified by the number "4" or the letters "LDPE". The PIC appears inside a three-chasing-arrow recycling symbol. The symbol is used to indicate whether the plastic can be recycled into new products.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The PIC was introduced by the Society of the Plastics Industry, Inc., to provide a uniform system for the identification of various polymer types and to help recycling companies separate various plastics for reprocessing. Manufacturers of plastic products are required to use PIC labels in some countries/regions and can voluntarily mark their products with the PIC where there are no requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Consumers can identify the plastic types based on the codes usually found at the base or at the side of the plastic products, including food/chemical packaging and containers. The PIC is usually not present on packaging films, since it is not practical to collect and recycle most of this type of waste.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Low national plastic recycling rates have been due to the complexity of sorting and processing, unfavorable economics, and consumer confusion about which plastics can actually be recycled.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Part of the confusion has been due to the use of the <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a> which is not on all plastic parts but just a subset<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> that includes the <a href="/wiki/Recycling_symbol" title="Recycling symbol">recycling symbol</a> as part of its design. The <a href="/wiki/Resin_identification_code" title="Resin identification code">resin identification code</a> is stamped or printed on the bottom of containers and surrounded by a triangle of arrows. (See the table in <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">Plastic</a>.) The intent of these symbols was to make it easier to identify the type of plastics used to make a particular container and to indicate that the plastic is potentially recyclable. The question that remains is which types of plastics can be recycled by your local recycling center. In many communities, not all types of plastics are accepted for sidewalk recycling collection programs due to the high processing costs and complexity of the equipment required to recycle certain materials. There is also sometimes a seemingly low demand for the recycled product depending on a recycling center's proximity to entities seeking recycled materials. Another major barrier is that the cost to recycle certain materials and the corresponding market price for those materials sometimes does not present any opportunity for profit. The best example of this is polystyrene (commonly called styrofoam), although some communities, like Brookline, MA, are moving toward banning the distribution of polystyrene containers by local food and coffee businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
