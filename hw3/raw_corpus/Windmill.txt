<p>A <b>windmill</b> is a mill that converts the <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">energy of wind</a> into <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">rotational energy</a> by means of vanes called <a href="/wiki/Windmill_sail" title="Windmill sail">sails</a> or blades.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Centuries ago, windmills usually were used to <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">mill</a> grain, pump water, or both. Thus they often were <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">gristmills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">windpumps</a>, or both.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup> The majority of modern windmills take the form of <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> used to generate electricity, or <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">windpumps</a> used to pump water, either for land drainage or to extract <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Windmills_in_antiquity">Windmills in antiquity</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg/220px-Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg/330px-Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg/440px-Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1805" data-file-height="1801" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heron%27s_Windwheel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Heron of Alexandria">Heron</a>'s wind-powered <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The windwheel of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_technology" title="Ancient Greek technology">Greek</a> engineer <a href="/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria" class="mw-redirect" title="Heron of Alexandria">Heron of Alexandria</a> in the first century is the earliest known instance of using a wind-driven wheel to power a machine.<sup id="cite_ref-Lohrmann_10f._4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lohrmann_10f.-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Drachmann_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drachmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Another early example of a wind-driven wheel was the <a href="/wiki/Prayer_wheel" title="Prayer wheel">prayer wheel</a>, which has been used in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> since the fourth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Lucas2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lucas2006-6">[6]</a></sup> It has been claimed that the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonian</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Hammurabi" title="Hammurabi">Hammurabi</a> planned to use wind power for his ambitious irrigation project in the seventeenth century BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Sathyajith2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sathyajith2006-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_windmills">Horizontal windmills</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perzsa_malom.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Perzsa_malom.svg/220px-Perzsa_malom.svg.png" width="220" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Perzsa_malom.svg/330px-Perzsa_malom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Perzsa_malom.svg/440px-Perzsa_malom.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="620" /></a>

<p>The first practical windmills had sails that rotated in a horizontal plane, around a vertical axis.<sup id="cite_ref-Wailes.2C_R._Horizontal_Windmills_pp125-145_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wailes.2C_R._Horizontal_Windmills_pp125-145-8">[8]</a></sup> According to Ahmad Y. al-Hassan, these <a href="/wiki/Panemone_windmill" title="Panemone windmill">panemone windmills</a> were invented in eastern <a href="/wiki/Greater_Iran" title="Greater Iran">Persia</a> as recorded by the <a href="/wiki/Geography_in_medieval_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Geography in medieval Islam">Persian</a> geographer <a href="/wiki/Estakhri" title="Estakhri">Estakhri</a> in the ninth century.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Al-Hassan.2C_Hill.2C_p.54f._10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Hassan.2C_Hill.2C_p.54f.-10">[10]</a></sup> The authenticity of an earlier anecdote of a windmill involving the second <a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a> (AD 634–644) is questioned on the grounds that it appears in a tenth-century document.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Made of six to 12 sails covered in reed matting or cloth material, these windmills were used to grind grain or draw up water, and were quite different from the later European vertical windmills. Windmills were in widespread use across the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, and later spread to China and <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">India</a> from there.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A similar type of horizontal windmill with rectangular blades, used for irrigation, can also be found in thirteenth-century China (during the <a href="/wiki/Jurchen_Jin_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Jurchen Jin dynasty">Jurchen Jin dynasty</a> in the north), introduced by the travels of <a href="/wiki/Yel%C3%BC_Chucai" title="Yelü Chucai">Yelü Chucai</a> to <a href="/wiki/Turkestan" title="Turkestan">Turkestan</a> in 1219.<sup id="cite_ref-needham_volume_4_part_2_560_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_4_part_2_560-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Horizontal windmills were built, in small numbers, in Europe during the 18th and nineteenth centuries,<sup id="cite_ref-Wailes.2C_R._Horizontal_Windmills_pp125-145_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wailes.2C_R._Horizontal_Windmills_pp125-145-8">[8]</a></sup> for example <a href="/wiki/Fowler%27s_Mill" title="Fowler's Mill">Fowler's Mill</a> at <a href="/wiki/Battersea" title="Battersea">Battersea</a> in London, and Hooper's Mill at <a href="/wiki/History_of_Margate#Windmills" title="History of Margate">Margate</a> in Kent. These early modern examples seem not to have been directly influenced by the horizontal windmills of the Middle and Far East, but to have been independent inventions by engineers influenced by the Industrial Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_windmills">Vertical windmills</span></h2>
<p>Due to a lack of evidence, debate occurs among historians as to whether or not Middle Eastern horizontal windmills triggered the original development of European windmills.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lucas_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lucas-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In northwestern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the horizontal-axis or vertical windmill (so called due to the plane of the movement of its sails) is believed to date from the last quarter of the twelfth century in the triangle of northern France, eastern England and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The earliest certain reference to a windmill in Europe (assumed to have been of the vertical type) dates from 1185, in the former village of Weedley in Yorkshire which was located at the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/The_Wolds" title="The Wolds">Wold</a> overlooking the Humber estuary.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A number of earlier, but less certainly dated, twelfth-century European sources referring to windmills have also been found.<sup id="cite_ref-Price2005_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price2005-20">[20]</a></sup> These earliest mills were used to <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">grind cereals</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_mill">Post mill</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post_mill" title="Post mill">Post mill</a></div>
<p>The evidence at present is that the earliest type of European windmill was the post mill, so named because of the large upright post on which the mill's main structure (the "body" or "buck") is balanced. By mounting the body this way, the mill is able to rotate to face the wind direction; an essential requirement for windmills to operate economically in north-western Europe, where wind directions are variable. The body contains all the milling machinery. The first post mills were of the sunken type, where the post was buried in an earth mound to support it. Later, a wooden support was developed called the <a href="/wiki/Trestle_(mill)" title="Trestle (mill)">trestle</a>. This was often covered over or surrounded by a roundhouse to protect the trestle from the weather and to provide storage space. This type of windmill was the most common in Europe until the nineteenth century, when more powerful tower and smock mills replaced them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hollow-post_mill">Hollow-post mill</span></h3>
<p>In a hollow-post mill, the post on which the body is mounted is hollowed out, to accommodate the drive shaft.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This makes it possible to drive machinery below or outside the body while still being able to rotate the body into the wind. Hollow-post mills driving scoop wheels were used in the Netherlands to drain wetlands from the fourteenth century onwards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tower_mill">Tower mill</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tower_mill" title="Tower mill">Tower mill</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg/220px-Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg/330px-Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg/440px-Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Molinos_de_Consuegra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tower mills in Spain</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the end of the thirteenth century, the masonry <a href="/wiki/Tower_mill" title="Tower mill">tower mill</a>, on which only the cap is rotated rather than the whole body of the mill, had been introduced. The spread of tower mills came with a growing economy that called for larger and more stable sources of power, though they were more expensive to build. In contrast to the post mill, only the cap of the tower mill needs to be turned into the wind, so the main structure can be made much taller, allowing the sails to be made longer, which enables them to provide useful work even in low winds. The cap can be turned into the wind either by winches or gearing inside the cap or from a winch on the tail pole outside the mill. A method of keeping the cap and sails into the wind automatically is by using a <a href="/wiki/Windmill_fantail" title="Windmill fantail">fantail</a>, a small windmill mounted at right angles to the sails, at the rear of the windmill. These are also fitted to tail poles of post mills and are common in Great Britain and English-speaking countries of the former British Empire, Denmark, and Germany but rare in other places. Around some parts of the Mediterranean Sea, tower mills with fixed caps were built because the wind's direction varied little most of the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smock_mill">Smock mill</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Smock_mill" title="Smock mill">Smock mill</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg/220px-Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg" width="220" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg/330px-Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg/440px-Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3004" data-file-height="3203" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greetsieler_Zwillingsm%C3%BChlen_2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two <a href="/wiki/Smock_mill" title="Smock mill">smock mills</a> with a stage in <a href="/wiki/Greetsiel" title="Greetsiel">Greetsiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The smock mill is a later development of the tower mill, where the tower is replaced by a wooden framework, called the "smock". The smock is commonly of octagonal plan, though examples with more, or fewer, sides exist. The smock is thatched, boarded or covered by other materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Roofing_slate" class="mw-redirect" title="Roofing slate">slate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet metal</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tar_paper" title="Tar paper">tar paper</a>. The lighter construction in comparison to tower mills make smock mills practical as drainage mills as these often had to be built in areas with unstable subsoil. Having originated as a drainage mill, smock mills are also used for a variety of purposes. When used in a built-up area it is often placed on a masonry base to raise it above the surrounding buildings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics">Mechanics</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sails">Sails</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Windmill_sail" title="Windmill sail">Windmill sail</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg/220px-Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg/330px-Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg/440px-Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4521" data-file-height="3014" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuremaa_m%C3%B5isa_tuuleveski.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Windmill in <a href="/wiki/Kuremaa" title="Kuremaa">Kuremaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Common sails consist of a lattice framework on which a sailcloth is spread. The miller can adjust the amount of cloth spread according to the amount of wind available and power needed. In medieval mills, the sailcloth was wound in and out of a ladder type arrangement of sails. Postmedieval mill sails had a lattice framework over which the sailcloth was spread, while in colder climates, the cloth was replaced by wooden slats, which were easier to handle in freezing conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-lowtechmag_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lowtechmag-22">[22]</a></sup> The jib sail is commonly found in Mediterranean countries, and consists of a simple triangle of cloth wound round a spar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In all cases, the mill needs to be stopped to adjust the sails. Inventions in Great Britain in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries led to sails that automatically adjust to the wind speed without the need for the miller to intervene, culminating in patent sails invented by <a href="/wiki/William_Cubitt" title="William Cubitt">William Cubitt</a> in 1807. In these sails, the cloth is replaced by a mechanism of connected shutters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In France, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre-Th%C3%A9ophile_Berton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pierre-Théophile Berton (page does not exist)">Pierre-Théophile Berton</a> invented a system consisting of longitudinal wooden slats connected by a mechanism that lets the miller open them while the mill is turning. In the twentieth century, increased knowledge of aerodynamics from the development of the airplane led to further improvements in efficiency by German engineer Bilau and several Dutch millwrights.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg/220px-Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg/330px-Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg/440px-Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1008" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Windmills_Zaanse_Schans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Windmills Zaanse Schans</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The majority of windmills have four sails. Multiple-sailed mills, with five, six or eight sails, were built in Great Britain (especially in and around the counties of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>), Germany, and less commonly elsewhere. Earlier multiple-sailed mills are found in Spain, Portugal, Greece, parts of Romania, Bulgaria, and Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-Sailno_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sailno-23">[23]</a></sup> A mill with an even number of sails has the advantage of being able to run with a damaged sail and the one opposite removed without resulting in an unbalanced mill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In the Netherlands the stationary position of the sails, i.e. when the mill is not working, has long been used to give signals. A slight tilt of the sails before the main building signals joy, while a tilt after the building signals mourning. Across the Netherlands, windmills were placed in mourning position in honor of the Dutch victims of the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Airlines_Flight_17" class="mw-redirect" title="Malaysian Airlines Flight 17">Malaysian Airlines Flight 17 shootdown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machinery">Machinery</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mill_machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="Mill machinery">Mill machinery</a></div>
<p>Gears inside a windmill convey power from the rotary motion of the sails to a mechanical device. The sails are carried on the horizontal windshaft. Windshafts can be wholly made of wood, or wood with a cast iron poll end (where the sails are mounted) or entirely of cast iron. The brake wheel is fitted onto the windshaft between the front and rear bearing. It has the brake around the outside of the rim and teeth in the side of the rim which drive the horizontal gearwheel called wallower on the top end of the vertical upright shaft. In <a href="/wiki/Grist_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Grist mill">grist mills</a>, the great spur wheel, lower down the upright shaft, drives one or more stone nuts on the shafts driving each <a href="/wiki/Millstone" title="Millstone">millstone</a>. Post mills sometimes have a head and/or tail wheel driving the stone nuts directly, instead of the spur gear arrangement. Additional gear wheels drive a sack hoist or other machinery. The machinery differs if the windmill is used for other applications than milling grain. A <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">drainage mill</a> uses another set of gear wheels on the bottom end of the upright shaft to drive a scoop wheel or <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_screw" title="Archimedes' screw">Archimedes' screw</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">Sawmills</a> use a crankshaft to provide a reciprocating motion to the saws. Windmills have been used to power many other industrial processes, including <a href="/wiki/Papermill" class="mw-redirect" title="Papermill">papermills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Threshing" title="Threshing">threshing</a> mills, and to process oil seeds, wool, paints and stone products.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_and_decline">Spread and decline</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Near_St._Donats.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Near_St._Donats.jpeg/220px-Near_St._Donats.jpeg" width="220" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Near_St._Donats.jpeg/330px-Near_St._Donats.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Near_St._Donats.jpeg/440px-Near_St._Donats.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="463" data-file-height="700" /></a>

</div>
<p>In the 14th century windmills became popular in Europe; the total number of wind-powered mills is estimated to have been around 200,000 at its peak in 1850, which is modest compared to some 500,000 waterwheels.<sup id="cite_ref-lowtechmag_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lowtechmag-22">[22]</a></sup> Windmills were applied in regions where there was too little water, where rivers freeze in winter and in flat lands where the flow of the river was too slow to provide the required power.<sup id="cite_ref-lowtechmag_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lowtechmag-22">[22]</a></sup> With the coming of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, the importance of wind and water as primary industrial energy sources declined and were eventually replaced by steam (in <a href="/wiki/Steam_mill" title="Steam mill">steam mills</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal combustion">internal combustion</a> engines, although windmills continued to be built in large numbers until late in the nineteenth century. More recently, windmills have been preserved for their historic value, in some cases as static exhibits when the antique machinery is too fragile to put in motion, and in other cases as fully working mills.<sup id="cite_ref-Victorian_Farm_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Victorian_Farm-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg/220px-Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg/330px-Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg/440px-Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1137" data-file-height="1421" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Don_Quijote_Illustration_by_Gustave_Dore_VII.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Don Quijote being struck by a windmill, illustration by Paul Gustave Louis Christophe Doré.</div>

<p>Of the 10,000 windmills in use in the Netherlands around 1850,<sup id="cite_ref-Stockhuyzen_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockhuyzen-26">[26]</a></sup> about 1000 are still standing. Most of these are being run by volunteers, though some grist mills are still operating commercially. Many of the drainage mills have been appointed as backup to the modern pumping stations. The <a href="/wiki/Zaanse_Schans" title="Zaanse Schans">Zaan district</a> has been said to have been the first industrialized region of the world with around 600 operating wind-powered industries by the end of the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Stockhuyzen_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockhuyzen-26">[26]</a></sup> Economic fluctuations and the industrial revolution had a much greater impact on these industries than on grain and drainage mills, so only very few are left.</p>
<p>Construction of mills spread to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> in the seventeenth century. The early tower mills did not survive the gales of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>, so in 1717, the <a href="/wiki/Heeren_XVII" class="mw-redirect" title="Heeren XVII">Heeren XVII</a> sent carpenters, masons, and materials to construct a durable mill. The mill, completed in 1718, became known as the <i>Oude Molen</i> and was located between Pinelands Station and the Black River. Long since demolished, its name lives on as that of a Technical school in <a href="/wiki/Pinelands,_Cape_Town" title="Pinelands, Cape Town">Pinelands</a>. By 1863, Cape Town could boast 11 mills stretching from Paarden Eiland to <a href="/wiki/Mowbray,_Cape_Town" title="Mowbray, Cape Town">Mowbray</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbines">Wind turbines</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">Wind power</a> and <a href="/wiki/High-altitude_wind_power" title="High-altitude wind power">High-altitude wind power</a></div>

<a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8nland_Offshore_Wind_Farm" title="Rønland Offshore Wind Farm">Rønland Offshore Wind Farm</a> in Denmark</div>
</div>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg/220px-%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg" width="220" height="329" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg/330px-%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg/440px-%E8%A5%BF%E7%94%B8%E5%AD%90%E6%A2%81%E9%A3%8E%E8%BD%A6_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3872" /></a>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbine</a> is a windmill-like structure specifically developed to generate electricity. They can be seen as the next step in the development of the windmill. The first wind turbines were built by the end of the nineteenth century by <a href="/wiki/Prof_James_Blyth" class="mw-redirect" title="Prof James Blyth">Prof James Blyth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (1887),<sup id="cite_ref-Shackleton_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shackleton-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Brush" title="Charles F. Brush">Charles F. Brush</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a> (1887–1888)<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Poul_la_Cour" title="Poul la Cour">Poul la Cour</a> in Denmark (1890s). La Cour's mill from 1896 later became the local powerplant of the village Askov. By 1908 there were 72 wind-driven electric generators in Denmark, ranging from 5 to 25&#160;kW. By the 1930s, windmills were widely used to generate electricity on farms in the United States where distribution systems had not yet been installed, built by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Jacobs_Wind" title="Jacobs Wind">Jacobs Wind</a>, Wincharger, Miller Airlite, Universal Aeroelectric, Paris-Dunn, Airline, and Winpower. The Dunlite Corporation produced turbines for similar locations in Australia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Forerunners of modern horizontal-axis utility-scale wind generators were the WIME-3D in service in <a href="/wiki/Balaklava" title="Balaklava">Balaklava</a> <a href="/wiki/USSR" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR">USSR</a> from 1931 until 1942, a 100-kW generator on a 30-m (100-ft) tower,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Smith-Putnam_wind_turbine" title="Smith-Putnam wind turbine">Smith-Putnam wind turbine</a> built in 1941 on the mountain known as Grandpa's Knob in <a href="/wiki/Castleton,_Vermont" title="Castleton, Vermont">Castleton, Vermont</a>, United States of 1.25 MW<sup id="cite_ref-NP_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NP-32">[32]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/NASA_wind_turbines" title="NASA wind turbines">NASA wind turbines</a> developed from 1974 through the mid-1980s. The development of these 13 experimental wind turbines pioneered many of the <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_design" title="Wind turbine design">wind turbine design</a> technologies in use today, including: steel tube towers, variable-speed generators, composite blade materials, and partial-span pitch control, as well as aerodynamic, structural, and acoustic engineering design capabilities. The modern <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_industry" title="Wind power industry">wind power industry</a> began in 1979 with the serial production of wind turbines by Danish manufacturers Kuriant, <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a>, <a href="/wiki/NEG_Micon" title="NEG Micon">Nordtank</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Bonus</a>. These early turbines were small by today's standards, with capacities of 20–30&#160;kW each. Since then, commercial turbines have increased greatly in size, with the Enercon E-126 capable of delivering up to 7 MW, while wind turbine production has expanded to many countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As the 21st century began, rising concerns over <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and eventual <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">fossil fuel depletion</a> led to an expansion of interest in all available forms of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. Worldwide, many thousands of wind turbines are now operating, with a total <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">nameplate capacity</a> of 194,400&#160;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind power in Europe">Europe</a> accounted for 48% of the total in 2009.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Windpumps">Windpumps</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">Windpump</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Windmill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Old_Windmill.jpg/220px-Old_Windmill.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Old_Windmill.jpg/330px-Old_Windmill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Old_Windmill.jpg/440px-Old_Windmill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Windmill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Aermotor_Windmill_Company" title="Aermotor Windmill Company">Aermotor</a>-style windpump in <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, USA</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Windpumps were used to pump water since at least the 9th century in what is now <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The use of wind pumps became widespread across the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Muslim world</a> and later spread to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Windmills were later used extensively in Europe, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, from the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> onwards, to drain land for agricultural or building purposes.</p>
<p>The <b>American windmill</b>, or <b>wind engine</b>, was invented by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Halladay" title="Daniel Halladay">Daniel Halladay</a> in 1854<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and was used mostly for lifting water from wells. Larger versions were also used for tasks such as sawing wood, chopping hay, and shelling and grinding grain.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In early California and some other states, the windmill was part of a self-contained domestic water system which included a hand-dug well and a wooden water tower supporting a redwood tank enclosed by wooden siding known as a <a href="/wiki/Tankhouse" title="Tankhouse">tankhouse</a>. During the late 19th century steel blades and steel towers replaced wooden construction. At their peak in 1930, an estimated 600,000 units were in use.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Firms such as U.S. Wind Engine and Pump Company, Challenge Wind Mill and Feed Mill Company, Appleton Manufacturing Company, Star, <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_windmill" title="Eclipse windmill">Eclipse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairbanks-Morse" title="Fairbanks-Morse">Fairbanks-Morse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dempsters" title="Dempsters">Dempster Mill Manufacturing Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aermotor_Windmill_Company" title="Aermotor Windmill Company">Aermotor</a> became the main suppliers in North and South America. These windpumps are used extensively on farms and ranches in the United States, Canada, Southern Africa, and Australia. They feature a large number of blades, so they turn slowly with considerable <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> in low winds and are self-regulating in high winds. A tower-top <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">gearbox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshaft</a> convert the rotary motion into reciprocating strokes carried downward through a rod to the pump cylinder below. Such mills pumped water and powered feed mills, saw mills, and agricultural machinery.</p>
<p>In Australia, the Griffiths Brothers at <a href="/wiki/Toowoomba" title="Toowoomba">Toowoomba</a> manufactured windmills of the American pattern from 1876, with the trade name Southern Cross Windmills in use from 1903. These became an icon of the Australian rural sector by utilizing the water of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Artesian_Basin" title="Great Artesian Basin">Great Artesian Basin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BM_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BM-39">[39]</a></sup> Another well-known maker was <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Metters" title="Frederick Metters">Metters Ltd.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>.</p>
