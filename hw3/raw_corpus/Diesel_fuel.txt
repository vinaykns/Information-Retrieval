			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Diesel fuel</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Diesel oil" redirects here. Sometimes "diesel oil" is used to mean <a href="/wiki/Lubricating_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Lubricating oil">lubricating oil</a> for <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>.</div>

<p><b>Diesel fuel</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/əl/ 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span></span>/</a></span></span> in general is any liquid <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>, whose fuel ignition takes place, without any spark, as a result of compression of the inlet air mixture and then injection of fuel. (<a href="/wiki/Glow_plug" class="mw-redirect" title="Glow plug">Glow plugs</a>, grid heaters and heater blocks help achieve high temperatures for combustion during engine startup in cold weather.) Diesel engines have found broad use as a result of higher <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermodynamic efficiency</a> and thus <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>. This is particularly noted where diesel engines are run at part-load; as their air supply is not throttled as in a <a href="/wiki/Petrol_engine" title="Petrol engine">petrol engine</a>, their efficiency still remains very high.</p>
<p>The most common type of diesel fuel is a specific <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillate</a> of petroleum <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, but alternatives that are not derived from petroleum, such as <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass_to_liquid" title="Biomass to liquid">biomass to liquid</a> (BTL) or <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquid" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas to liquid">gas to liquid</a> (GTL) diesel, are increasingly being developed and adopted. To distinguish these types, petroleum-derived diesel is increasingly called <b>petrodiesel</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ultra-low-sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low-sulfur diesel">Ultra-low-sulfur diesel</a> (ULSD) is a standard for defining diesel fuel with substantially lowered <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> contents. As of 2016, almost all of the petroleum-based diesel fuel available in UK, Europe and North America is of a ULSD type. In the UK, diesel fuel for on-road use is commonly abbreviated <b>DERV</b>, standing for <i>diesel-engined road vehicle</i>, which carries a tax premium over equivalent fuel for <a href="/wiki/Non-road_diesel_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-road diesel engine">non-road use</a> (see <a href="#Taxation">§&#160;Taxation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> diesel fuel is also known as <b>distillate</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, it is known as <b>Solar</b>, a trademarked name by the local oil company <a href="/wiki/Pertamina" title="Pertamina">Pertamina</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diesel fuel originated from experiments conducted by German scientist and inventor <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Diesel" title="Rudolf Diesel">Rudolf Diesel</a> for his <a href="/wiki/Compression-ignition_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Compression-ignition engine">compression-ignition engine</a> he invented in 1892. Diesel originally designed his engine to use coal dust as fuel,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and experimented with other fuels including vegetable oils<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Peanut_oil" title="Peanut oil">peanut oil</a>, which was used to power the engines which he exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1900)" title="Exposition Universelle (1900)">1900 Paris Exposition</a> and the 1911 World's Fair in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diesel fuel is produced from various sources, the most common being <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. Other sources include <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_fat" title="Animal fat">animal fat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coal_liquefaction" title="Coal liquefaction">coal liquefaction</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum_diesel">Petroleum diesel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Petroleum diesel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Essodiesel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Essodiesel.jpg/220px-Essodiesel.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Essodiesel.jpg/330px-Essodiesel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Essodiesel.jpg/440px-Essodiesel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p><b>Petroleum diesel</b>, also called <b>petrodiesel</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or fossil diesel is the most common type of diesel fuel. It is produced from the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> between 200&#160;°C (392&#160;°F) and 350&#160;°C (662&#160;°F) at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, resulting in a mixture of carbon chains that typically contain between 8 and 21 <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> per <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_diesel">Synthetic diesel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthetic diesel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">Synthetic fuel</a></div>
<p>Synthetic diesel can be produced from any carbonaceous material, including biomass, biogas, natural gas, coal and many others. The raw material is gasified into <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">synthesis gas</a>, which after purification is converted by the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> to a synthetic diesel.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The process is typically referred to as <a href="/wiki/Biomass_to_liquid" title="Biomass to liquid">biomass-to-liquid</a> (BTL), <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquid" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas to liquid">gas-to-liquid</a> (GTL) or <a href="/wiki/Coal_to_liquid" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal to liquid">coal-to-liquid</a> (CTL), depending on the raw material used.</p>
<p>Paraffinic synthetic diesel generally has a near-zero content of sulfur and very low aromatics content, reducing unregulated emissions of toxic hydrocarbons, nitrous oxides and particulate matter (PM).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

</div>

<p>Fatty-acid methyl ester (FAME), more widely known as <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>, is obtained from <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a> or animal fats (bio<a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>) which have been <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterified</a> with <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. It can be produced from many types of oils, the most common being <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a> oil (rapeseed methyl ester, RME) in Europe and <a href="/wiki/Soybean_oil" title="Soybean oil">soybean oil</a> (soy methyl ester, SME) in the US. Methanol can also be replaced with ethanol for the transesterification process, which results in the production of ethyl esters. The transesterification processes use catalysts, such as sodium or potassium hydroxide, to convert vegetable oil and methanol into FAME and the undesirable byproducts glycerine and water, which will need to be removed from the fuel along with methanol traces. FAME can be used pure (B100) in engines where the manufacturer approves such use, but it is more often used as a mix with diesel, BXX where XX is the biodiesel content in percent.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosch_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosch-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acea.be_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acea.be-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>FAME as a fuel is specified in <a href="/wiki/EN_14214" title="EN 14214">DIN EN 14214</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/ASTM_D6751" title="ASTM D6751">ASTM D6751</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fuel equipment manufacturers (FIE) have raised several concerns regarding FAME fuels, identifying FAME as being the cause of the following problems: corrosion of fuel injection components, low-pressure fuel system blockage, increased dilution and <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> of engine sump oil, pump seizures due to high fuel viscosity at low temperature, increased injection pressure, elastomeric seal failures and fuel injector spray blockage.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Pure biodiesel has an energy content about 5–10% lower than petroleum diesel.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The loss in power when using pure biodiesel is 5–7%.<sup id="cite_ref-acea.be_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acea.be-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unsaturated_fatty_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Unsaturated fatty acid">Unsaturated fatty acids</a> are the source for the lower oxidation stability; they react with oxygen and form peroxides and result in degradation byproducts, which can cause sludge and lacquer in the fuel system.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>As FAME contains low levels of sulfur, the emissions of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, major components of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, are low. Use of biodiesel also results in reductions of unburned hydrocarbons, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO), and particulate matter. CO emissions using biodiesel are substantially reduced, on the order of 50% compared to most petrodiesel fuels. The exhaust emissions of particulate matter from biodiesel have been found to be 30% lower than overall particulate matter emissions from petrodiesel. The exhaust emissions of total hydrocarbons (a contributing factor in the localized formation of smog and ozone) are up to 93% lower for biodiesel than diesel fuel.</p>
<p>Biodiesel also may reduce health risks associated with petroleum diesel. Biodiesel emissions showed decreased levels of <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon</a> (PAH) and nitrited PAH compounds, which have been identified as potential cancer-causing compounds. In recent testing, PAH compounds were reduced by 75–85%, except for <a href="/wiki/Benz(a)anthracene" title="Benz(a)anthracene">benz(a)anthracene</a>, which was reduced by roughly 50%. Targeted nPAH compounds were also reduced dramatically with biodiesel fuel, with <a href="/wiki/2-nitrofluorene" class="mw-redirect" title="2-nitrofluorene">2-nitrofluorene</a> and <a href="/wiki/1-nitropyrene" class="mw-redirect" title="1-nitropyrene">1-nitropyrene</a> reduced by 90%, and the rest of the nPAH compounds reduced to only trace levels.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogenated_oils_and_fats">Hydrogenated oils and fats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hydrogenated oils and fats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This category of diesel fuels involves converting the <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a> in vegetable oil and animal fats into alkanes by <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil_refining" title="Vegetable oil refining">refining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/H-Bio" title="H-Bio">H-Bio</a>. The produced fuel has many properties that are similar to synthetic diesel, and are free from the many disadvantages of FAME.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DME">DME</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DME">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">Dimethyl ether</a>, DME, is a synthetic, gaseous diesel fuel that results in clean combustion with very little soot and reduced NOx emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Bosch_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bosch-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the US, diesel is recommended to be stored in a yellow container to differentiate it from <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, which are typically kept in blue and red containers, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-bhm199702_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bhm199702-19">[19]</a></sup> In the UK, diesel is normally stored in a black container, to differentiate it from unleaded petrol (which is commonly stored in a green container) and leaded petrol (which is stored in a red container).<sup id="cite_ref-hseuk_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hseuk-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements_and_pricing">Measurements and pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Measurements and pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cetane_number">Cetane number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cetane number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cetane_number" title="Cetane number">Cetane number</a></div>
<p>The principal measure of diesel fuel quality is its <a href="/wiki/Cetane_number" title="Cetane number">cetane number</a>. A cetane number is a measure of the delay of ignition of a diesel fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-ufa1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufa1-21">[21]</a></sup> A higher cetane number indicates that the fuel ignites more readily when sprayed into hot compressed air.<sup id="cite_ref-ufa1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufa1-21">[21]</a></sup> European (EN 590 standard) road diesel has a minimum cetane number of 51. Fuels with higher cetane numbers, normally "premium" diesel fuels with additional cleaning agents and some synthetic content, are available in some markets.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_value_and_price">Fuel value and price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fuel value and price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gasoline_and_diesel_usage_and_pricing" title="Gasoline and diesel usage and pricing">Gasoline and diesel usage and pricing</a></div>
<p>As of 2010, the density of petroleum diesel is about 0.832&#160;kg/L (6.943&#160;lb/US&#160;gal), about 11.6% more than ethanol-free <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">petrol (gasoline)</a>, which has a density of about 0.745&#160;kg/L (6.217&#160;lb/US&#160;gal). About 86.1% of the fuel mass is carbon, and when burned, it offers a net heating value of 43.1 MJ/kg as opposed to 43.2 MJ/kg for gasoline. However, due to the higher density, diesel offers a higher volumetric energy density at 35.86 MJ/L (128,700&#160;BTU/US&#160;gal) vs. 32.18 MJ/L (115,500&#160;BTU/US&#160;gal) for gasoline, some 11% higher, which should be considered when comparing the fuel efficiency by volume. The CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from diesel are 73.25 g/MJ, just slightly lower than for gasoline at 73.38 g/MJ.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Diesel is generally simpler to refine from petroleum than gasoline, and contains hydrocarbons having a boiling point in the range of 180–360&#160;°C (360–680&#160;°F). The price of diesel traditionally rises during colder months as demand for <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a> rises, which is refined in much the same way. Because of recent changes in fuel quality regulations, additional refining is required to remove sulfur, which contributes to a sometimes higher cost. In many parts of the United States and throughout the United Kingdom and Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> diesel may be priced higher than petrol.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Cheaper by volume or by energy content? (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Reasons for higher-priced diesel include the shutdown of some refineries in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, diversion of mass refining capacity to gasoline production, and a recent transfer to <a href="/wiki/Ultra-low-sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low-sulfur diesel">ultra-low-sulfur diesel</a> (ULSD), which causes infrastructural complications.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In Sweden, a diesel fuel designated as MK-1 (class 1 environmental diesel) is also being sold; this is a ULSD that also has a lower aromatics content, with a limit of 5%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This fuel is slightly more expensive to produce than regular ULSD.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxation">Taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Diesel fuel is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a>, which is used in <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating</a>. In Europe, the United States, and Canada, <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> on diesel fuel are higher than on heating oil due to the <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tax" title="Fuel tax">fuel tax</a>, and in those areas, heating oil is marked with <a href="/wiki/Fuel_dyes" title="Fuel dyes">fuel dyes</a> and trace chemicals to prevent and detect <a href="/wiki/Tax_fraud" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax fraud">tax fraud</a>. "Untaxed" diesel (sometimes called "off-road diesel") is available in some countries for use primarily in agricultural applications, such as fuel for tractors, recreational and utility vehicles or other <a href="/wiki/Non-commercial" title="Non-commercial">noncommercial</a> vehicles that do not use <a href="/wiki/Public_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Public road">public roads</a>. This fuel may have sulfur levels that exceed the limits for road use in some countries (e.g. US).</p>
<p>This untaxed diesel is dyed red for identification,<sup id="cite_ref-26_CFR_48.4082-1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26_CFR_48.4082-1-27">[27]</a></sup> and using this untaxed diesel fuel for a typically taxed purpose (such as driving use), the user can be fined (e.g. US$10,000 in the US). In the United Kingdom, Belgium and the Netherlands, it is known as <a href="/wiki/Red_diesel" class="mw-redirect" title="Red diesel">red diesel</a> (or gas oil), and is also used in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> vehicles, home heating tanks, refrigeration units on vans/trucks which contain perishable items such as food and medicine and for marine craft. Diesel fuel, or marked gas oil is dyed green in the Republic of Ireland and Norway. The term "diesel-engined road vehicle" (DERV) is used in the UK as a synonym for unmarked road diesel fuel. In India, taxes on diesel fuel are lower than on petrol, as the majority of the transportation for grain and other essential commodities across the country runs on diesel.</p>
<p>Taxes on <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> in the US vary between states; some states (Texas, for example) have no tax on biodiesel and a reduced tax on biodiesel blends equivalent to the amount of biodiesel in the blend, so that B20 fuel is taxed 20% less than pure petrodiesel.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Other states, such as North Carolina, tax biodiesel (in any blended configuration) the same as petrodiesel, although they have introduced new incentives to producers and users of all biofuels.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied petroleum gas</a> engines, diesel engines do not use high-voltage spark ignition (spark plugs). An engine running on diesel compresses the air inside the cylinder to high pressures and temperatures (<a href="/wiki/Compression_ratio" title="Compression ratio">compression ratios</a> from 14:1 to 18:1 are common in current diesel engines); the engine generally injects the diesel fuel directly into the cylinder, starting a few degrees before <a href="/wiki/Top_dead_center" class="mw-redirect" title="Top dead center">top dead center</a> (TDC) and continuing during the combustion event. The high temperatures inside the cylinder cause the diesel fuel to react with the oxygen in the mix (burn or <a href="/wiki/Oxidize" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a>), heating and expanding the burning mixture to convert the thermal/pressure difference into mechanical work, i.e., to move the piston. Engines have <a href="/wiki/Glow_plug" class="mw-redirect" title="Glow plug">glow plugs</a> and grid heaters to help start the engine by preheating the cylinders to a minimum <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>. Diesel engines are <b>lean burn</b> engines,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> burning the fuel in more air than is needed for the chemical reaction. They thus use less fuel than <b>rich burn</b> spark ignition engines which use a <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometric</a> air-fuel ratio (just enough air to react with the fuel). As Professor Harvey of the University of Toronto notes, "due to the absence of throttling [constant amount of air admitted, per unit fuel, with no user-determined variation], and the high compression ratio and lean fuel mixture, diesel engines are substantially more efficient than spark-ignited engines", generally; Harvey cites the side-by-side comparisons of Schipper et al. and the estimates of &gt;20% lower fuel use and (given differences in energy content between fuel types) &gt;15% lower energy use.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">Gas turbine</a> and some other types of internal combustion engines, and <a href="/wiki/External_combustion_engine" title="External combustion engine">external combustion engine</a>, both can also be designed to take diesel fuel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> requirement of diesel fuel is usually specified at 40&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-ufa1_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufa1-21">[21]</a></sup> A disadvantage of diesel as a vehicle fuel in cold climates, is that its viscosity increases as the temperature decreases, changing it into a <a href="/wiki/Gel" title="Gel">gel</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Compression_ignition#Gelling" class="mw-redirect" title="Compression ignition">Compression Ignition – Gelling</a>) that cannot flow in fuel systems. Special <a href="/wiki/Winter_diesel_fuel" title="Winter diesel fuel">low-temperature diesel</a> contains additives to keep it liquid at lower temperatures, but starting a diesel engine in very cold weather may still pose considerable difficulties. Another disadvantage of diesel engines compared to petrol/gasoline engines is the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine_runaway" title="Diesel engine runaway">diesel engine runaway</a> failure. Since diesel engines do not need spark ignition, they can run as long as diesel fuel is supplied. Fuel is typically supplied via a fuel pump. If the pump breaks down in an "open" position, the supply of fuel will be unrestricted, and the engine will run away and risk terminal failure.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Turbocharged" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbocharged">turbocharged</a> engines, the oil seals on the turbocharger may fail, allowing lubricating oil into the combustion chamber, where it is burned like regular diesel fuel. In vehicles or installations that use diesel engines and also <a href="/wiki/Bottled_gas" title="Bottled gas">bottled gas</a>, a gas leak into the engine room could also provide fuel for a runaway, via the engine air intake.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crank case ventilation of modern road-use diesel engines is diverted into the intake manifold, because ventilating the crank case into outside air is inadvisable due to lubricant mist it contains. If the engine's piston rings malfunction, this will cause excessive pressure in the crank case forcing mist of engine lubricant into the intake manifold. Since most engines use oil which can be burnt in the same fashion as diesel, this will result in <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine_runaway" title="Diesel engine runaway">diesel engine runaway</a>. To prevent that, more premium crank case ventilation solutions are fitted with a filter to catch out lubricant mist.</p>
<p>Most modern road use diesel engines are provided with an <a href="/wiki/Fibre-reinforced_plastic" title="Fibre-reinforced plastic">FRP</a> valve in the intake manifold (usually mistaken by some as a petrol engine throttle body). In most basic applications this valve will close a flow of air mixture to the engine when the vehicle is switched off, preventing diesel engine runaway by starving the engine of oxygen; this will also make standard shutdown much smoother by eliminating compression and decompression rattle by making the pistons effectively work in vacuum. In more advanced control systems this FRP valve can be shut by an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_control_unit" title="Electronic control unit">electronic control unit</a> when it senses runaway scenario.</p>

<p>Diesel fuel is widely used in most types of <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">Trucks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>, which were often gasoline-powered in the 1920s through 1950s, are now almost exclusively diesel-powered. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railroad">Railroad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Railroad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

With Captain <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lionel_M._Woolson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lionel M. Woolson (page does not exist)">Lionel M. Woolson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_E._Lees" title="Walter E. Lees">Walter Lees</a> at the controls the first "official" test flight was taken the next morning, flying a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stinson_SM1B&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stinson SM1B (page does not exist)">Stinson SM1B</a> (X7654), powered by a <a href="/wiki/Packard_DR-980" title="Packard DR-980">Packard DR-980</a> 9-cylinder diesel <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a>, designed by Woolson. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a> flew the same aircraft and in 1929, it was flown 621 miles (999&#160;km) nonstop from <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> to <a href="/wiki/Langley_Field" title="Langley Field">Langley Field</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>. In 1931, Walter Lees and Fredrick Brossy set the nonstop flight record flying a <a href="/wiki/Bellanca" class="mw-redirect" title="Bellanca">Bellanca</a> powered by a Packard diesel for 84 hours and 32 minutes. <i>X7654</i> is now owned by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Herrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Greg Herrick (page does not exist)">Greg Herrick</a> and is at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_Wings_Flying_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Golden Wings Flying Museum (page does not exist)">Golden Wings Flying Museum</a> near <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>.</p>
<p>Diesel engines for airships were developed in both Germany and the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Beardmore_and_Company" title="William Beardmore and Company">Beardmore</a> produced the <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz_DB_602" title="Daimler-Benz DB 602">Daimler-Benz DB 602</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beardmore_Typhoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Beardmore Typhoon">Beardmore Typhoon</a> respectively. The <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">LZ 129 Hindenburg</a> rigid airship was powered by four <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz_DB_602" title="Daimler-Benz DB 602">Daimler-Benz DB 602</a> 16-cylinder diesel engines, each with 1,200&#160;hp (890&#160;kW) available in bursts and 850 horsepower (630&#160;kW) available for cruising.<sup id="cite_ref-daimler_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daimler-36">[36]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beardmore_Typhoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Beardmore Typhoon">Beardmore Typhoon</a> powered the ill-fated <a href="/wiki/R101" title="R101">R101</a> airship, built for the Empire airship programme in 1931.</p>
<p>With a production run of at least 900 engines, the most-produced aviation diesel engine in history was probably the <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Jumo_205" title="Junkers Jumo 205">Junkers Jumo 205</a>. Similar developments from the <a href="/wiki/Junkers_(Aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Junkers (Aircraft)">Junkers Motorenwerke</a> and licence-built versions of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jumo_204&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jumo 204 (page does not exist)">Jumo 204</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jumo_205" class="mw-redirect" title="Jumo 205">Jumo 205</a>, boosted German diesel aero-engine production to at least 1000 examples, the vast majority of which were liquid-cooled, opposed-piston, two-stroke engines.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> significant progress towards practical diesel aero-engines was made by the <a href="/wiki/TsIAM" class="mw-redirect" title="TsIAM">TsIAM</a> (<i>Tsentral'nyy Institut Aviatsionnovo Motorostroyeniya</i> – central institute of aviation motors) and particularly by <a href="/w/index.php?title=A.D._Charomskiy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A.D. Charomskiy (page does not exist)">A.D. Charomskiy</a>, who nursed the <a href="/wiki/Charomskiy_ACh-30" title="Charomskiy ACh-30">Charomskiy ACh-30</a> into production and limited operational use.<sup id="cite_ref-Kotelnikov_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kotelnikov-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_vehicles">Military vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Military vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Armored_fighting_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Armored fighting vehicle">Armored fighting vehicles</a> use diesel because of its lower flammability risks and the engines' higher provision of torque and lower likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Stall_(engine)" title="Stall (engine)">stalling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cars">Cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dieselgate" class="mw-redirect" title="Dieselgate">Dieselgate</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">Diesel-powered</a> cars generally have a better <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel economy</a> than equivalent gasoline engines and produce less <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emission.<sup id="cite_ref-argus_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-argus-40">[40]</a></sup> Their greater economy is due to the higher energy per-litre content of diesel fuel and the intrinsic efficiency of the diesel engine.<sup id="cite_ref-cice_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cice-41">[41]</a></sup> While petrodiesel's higher density results in higher greenhouse gas emissions per litre compared to gasoline,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> the 20–40% better fuel economy achieved by modern diesel-engined automobiles offsets the higher per-litre emissions of greenhouse gases, and a diesel-powered vehicle emits 10–20 percent less greenhouse gas than comparable gasoline vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-dieselforum_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dieselforum-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a>-powered diesel engines offer substantially improved emission reductions compared to petrodiesel or gasoline-powered engines, while retaining most of the fuel economy advantages over conventional gasoline-powered automobiles.<sup id="cite_ref-dieselforum_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dieselforum-43">[43]</a></sup> However, the increased compression ratios mean there are increased emissions of oxides of nitrogen (NO<sub>x</sub>) from diesel engines.<sup id="cite_ref-wpc1_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wpc1-46">[46]</a></sup> This is compounded by biological nitrogen in biodiesel to make NO<sub>x</sub> emissions the main drawback of diesel versus gasoline engines.<sup id="cite_ref-wpc1_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wpc1-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tractors_and_heavy_equipment">Tractors and heavy equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Tractors and heavy equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today's <a href="/wiki/Tractor" title="Tractor">tractors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_equipment" title="Heavy equipment">heavy equipment</a> are mostly diesel-powered. Among tractors, only the smaller classes may also offer gasoline engines. The <a href="/wiki/Dieselization" class="mw-redirect" title="Dieselization">dieselization</a> of tractors and heavy equipment began in Germany before World War II but was unusual in the United States until after that war. During the 1950s and 1960s, it progressed in the US as well.</p>
<p>Tractors and heavy equipment were often <a href="/wiki/Multifuel" title="Multifuel">multifuel</a> in the 1920s through 1940s, running spark-ignition and low-compression engines. Thus many farm tractors of the era could burn gasoline, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">alcohol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>, and any light grade of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a> such as diesel fuel, <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tractor_vaporising_oil" title="Tractor vaporising oil">tractor vaporising oil</a>, according to whichever was most affordable in any region at any given time. On U.S. farms during this era, the name "distillate" often referred to any of the aforementioned light fuel oils. The engines did not start as well on distillate, so typically a small auxiliary gasoline tank was used for cold starting, and the fuel valves were adjusted several minutes later, after warm-up, to switch to distillate. Engine accessories such as <a href="/wiki/Carburetor#Vaporizers" title="Carburetor">vaporizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(engine_cooling)#Radiator_blind" title="Radiator (engine cooling)">radiator shrouds</a> were also used, both with the aim of capturing heat, because when such an engine was run on distillate, it ran better when both it and the air it inhaled were warmer rather than at ambient temperature. Dieselization with dedicated diesel engines (high-compression with mechanical fuel injection and compression ignition) replaced such systems and made more <a href="/wiki/Engine_efficiency" title="Engine efficiency">efficient</a> use of the diesel fuel being burned.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Poor quality diesel fuel has been used as an extraction agent for <a href="/wiki/Liquid%E2%80%93liquid_extraction" title="Liquid–liquid extraction">liquid–liquid extraction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> mixtures.<sup id="cite_ref-chemabstracts_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chemabstracts-47">[47]</a></sup> Such use has been proposed as a means of separating the <a href="/wiki/Fission_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Fission product">fission product</a> palladium from <a href="/wiki/PUREX" title="PUREX">PUREX</a> <a href="/wiki/Raffinate" title="Raffinate">raffinate</a> which comes from used <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chemabstracts_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chemabstracts-47">[47]</a></sup> In this system of solvent extraction, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> of the diesel act as the <a href="/wiki/Diluent" title="Diluent">diluent</a> while the di<a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a> act as the extractant.<sup id="cite_ref-chemabstracts_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chemabstracts-47">[47]</a></sup> This extraction operates by a <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">solvation</a> mechanism.<sup id="cite_ref-chemabstracts_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chemabstracts-47">[47]</a></sup> So far, neither a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_plant" title="Pilot plant">pilot plant</a> nor full scale plant has been constructed to recover palladium, <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear waste">nuclear wastes</a> created by the use of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diesel fuel is also often used as the main ingredient in oil-base mud drilling fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-eisbdf_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eisbdf-49">[49]</a></sup> The advantage of using diesel is its low cost and that it delivers excellent results when drilling a wide variety of difficult strata including shale, salt and gypsum formations.<sup id="cite_ref-eisbdf_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eisbdf-49">[49]</a></sup> Diesel-oil mud is typically mixed with up to 40% brine water.<sup id="cite_ref-bfaf_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bfaf-50">[50]</a></sup> Due to health, safety and environmental concerns, Diesel-oil mud is often replaced with vegetable, mineral, or synthetic food-grade oil-base drilling fluids, although diesel-oil mud is still in widespread use in certain regions.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>During development of rocket engines in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> J-2 Diesel fuel was used as the fuel component in several engines including the <a href="/wiki/BMW_109-718" title="BMW 109-718">BMW 109-718</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CIOSCBMW_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIOSCBMW-52">[52]</a></sup> J-2 diesel fuel was also used as a fuel for gas turbine engines.<sup id="cite_ref-CIOSCBMW_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIOSCBMW-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_analysis">Chemical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Chemical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_composition">Chemical composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chemical composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Petroleum-derived diesel is composed of about 75% <a href="/wiki/Saturated_hydrocarbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturated hydrocarbon">saturated hydrocarbons</a> (primarily <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">paraffins</a> including <a href="/wiki/Isoparaffin#Linear_alkanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Isoparaffin"><i>n</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoalkane#Isomerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Isoalkane"><i>iso</i></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">cycloparaffins</a>), and 25% <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkylbenzenes" title="Alkylbenzenes">alkylbenzenes</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The average chemical formula for common diesel fuel is C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>23</sub>, ranging approximately from C<sub>10</sub>H<sub>20</sub> to C<sub>15</sub>H<sub>28</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">Diesel exhaust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gel_point_(petroleum)" title="Gel point (petroleum)">Gel point (petroleum)</a></div>
<p>Most diesel fuels freeze at common winter temperatures, while the temperatures greatly vary.<sup id="cite_ref-nrel_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel-55">[55]</a></sup> Petrodiesel typically freezes around temperatures of −8.1&#160;°C (17.5&#160;°F), whereas biodiesel freezes between temperatures of 2° to 15&#160;°C (35° to 60&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-nrel_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrel-55">[55]</a></sup> The viscosity of diesel noticeably increases as the temperature decreases, changing it into a gel at temperatures of −19&#160;°C (−2.2&#160;°F) to −15&#160;°C (5&#160;°F), that cannot flow in fuel systems. Conventional diesel fuels vaporise at temperatures between 149&#160;°C and 371&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-ufa1_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufa1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conventional diesel <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash points</a> vary between 52 and 96&#160;°C, which makes it safer than petrol and unsuitable for spark-ignition engines.<sup id="cite_ref-gasfp_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gasfp-56">[56]</a></sup> Unlike petrol, the flash point of a diesel fuel has no relation to its performance in an engine nor to its auto ignition qualities.<sup id="cite_ref-ufa1_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufa1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: NOx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a></div>
<p>Diesel engines as with other forms of combustion, produce the mono-nitrogen oxides <span class="chemf nowrap">NO</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>, collectively known as NO<sub>x</sub>. NO<sub><i>x</i></sub> reacts with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, moisture, and other compounds to form <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> vapor and related particles. Modern diesel engines (Euro 6 &amp; EPA stds) use Urea Injetion to turn NOx into N2 and Water. A diesel engine produces a consistent level of NOx over it's lifespan. A gasoline engine starts off cleaner than the diesel in NOx terms but increases such that beyond 50,000 miles the gas/petrol engine produces more NOx than the equivalent diesel.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particulates">Particulates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Particulates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Particulates:&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Particulates: (page does not exist)">PM10/PM2.5</a></div>
<p>Small particles (particulate matter also known as PM 10 or PM 2.5 depending on size) can penetrate deeply into sensitive lung tissue and damage it, causing premature death in extreme cases. Inhalation of such particles may cause or worsen respiratory diseases, such as <a href="/wiki/Emphysema" class="mw-redirect" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, or may also aggravate existing heart disease. Unlike direct injection gasoline/petrol enignes, modern diesel engines are fitted with Particulate traps that help to eliminate PM 10 and PM 2.5 <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment_hazards_of_sulfur">Environment hazards of sulfur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Environment hazards of sulfur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High levels of sulfur in diesel are harmful for the environment because they prevent the use of catalytic <a href="/wiki/Diesel_particulate_filter" title="Diesel particulate filter">diesel particulate filters</a> to control <a href="/wiki/Diesel_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Diesel particulate matter">diesel particulate emissions</a>, as well as more advanced technologies, such as nitrogen oxide (<a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a>) <a href="/wiki/Adsorb" class="mw-redirect" title="Adsorb">adsorbers</a> (still under development), to reduce emissions. Moreover, sulfur in the fuel is oxidized during combustion, producing <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a>, that in presence of water rapidly convert to <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, one of the chemical processes that results in <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>. However, the process for lowering sulfur also reduces the <a href="/wiki/Lubrication" title="Lubrication">lubricity</a> of the fuel, meaning that <a href="/wiki/Oil_additive" title="Oil additive">additives</a> must be put into the fuel to help lubricate engines. <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a> and biodiesel/petrodiesel blends, with their higher lubricity levels, are increasingly being utilized as an alternative. The U.S. annual consumption of diesel fuel in 2006 was about 190 billion litres (42 billion imperial gallons or 50 billion US gallons).<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the past, diesel fuel contained higher quantities of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>. <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">European emission standards</a> and preferential taxation have forced <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a> to dramatically reduce the level of sulfur in diesel fuels. In the European Union, the sulfur content has dramatically reduced during the last 20 years. Automotive diesel fuel is covered in the European Union by standard <a href="/wiki/EN_590" title="EN 590">EN 590</a>. In the 1990s specifications allowed a content of 2000 ppm max of sulphur, reduced to a limit of 350 ppm by the beginning of the 21st century with the introduction of Euro 3 specifications. The limit was lowered with the introduction of Euro 4 by 2006 to 50 ppm (<a href="/wiki/ULSD" class="mw-redirect" title="ULSD">ULSD</a>, Ultra Low Sulfur Diesel). The standard currently in force in European Europe for Diesel Fuel is the Euro 5, with a maximum content of 10 ppm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In the United States, more stringent emission standards have been adopted with the transition to <a href="/wiki/Ultra-low-sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low-sulfur diesel">ULSD</a> starting in 2006, and becoming mandatory on June 1, 2010 (see also <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">diesel exhaust</a>). U.S. diesel fuel typically also has a lower <a href="/wiki/Cetane_number" title="Cetane number">cetane number</a> (a measure of ignition quality) than European diesel, resulting in worse cold weather performance and some increase in emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algae.2C_microbes.2C_and_water_contamination">Algae, microbes, and water contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Algae, microbes, and water contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has been much discussion and misunderstanding of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> in diesel fuel. Algae need light to live and grow. As there is no sunlight in a closed fuel tank, no algae can survive, but some <a href="/wiki/Microbes" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbes">microbes</a> can survive and feed on the diesel fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>These microbes form a colony that lives at the interface of fuel and water. They grow quite fast in warmer temperatures. They can even grow in cold weather when fuel tank heaters are installed. Parts of the colony can break off and clog the fuel lines and fuel filters.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Water in fuel can damage a fuel <a href="/wiki/Injection_pump" title="Injection pump">injection pump</a>; some diesel <a href="/wiki/Fuel_filter" title="Fuel filter">fuel filters</a> also trap water. Water contamination in diesel fuel can lead to freezing while in the fuel tank. The freezing water that saturates the fuel will sometimes clog the fuel injector pump.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Once the water inside the fuel tank has started to freeze, gelling is more likely to occur. When the fuel is gelled it is not effective until the temperature is raised and the fuel returns to a liquid state.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_hazard">Road hazard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diesel_fuel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Road hazard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike gasoline/petroleum spirit, diesel is not flammable. However,a lack of evaporation means diesel spilled on a roadway can pose slip hazard to vehicles,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> due to its high evaporation temperature. After the light fractions have evaporated, a greasy slick is left on the road which can destabilize moving vehicles, causing them to skid. Diesel spills severely reduce <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a> grip and traction, and have been implicated in many accidents. The loss of traction is similar to that encountered on <a href="/wiki/Black_ice" title="Black ice">black ice</a>. Diesel slicks are especially dangerous for two-wheeled vehicles such as <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This whole paragraph is not cited. It is especially important to cite the fact that diesel has been implicated in many accidents (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
