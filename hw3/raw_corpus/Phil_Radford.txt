<p><b>Philip David "Phil" Radford</b> (born January 2, 1976) is an American environmental, clean energy and <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> leader<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> who served as the youngest Executive Director of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> USA.<sup id="cite_ref-The_National_Journal_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_National_Journal-4">[4]</a></sup> He is a co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Initiative" title="Democracy Initiative">Democracy Initiative</a>, Membership Drive,<sup id="cite_ref-MembershipDrive_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MembershipDrive-5">[5]</a></sup> was founder and Executive Director of Power Shift, and is a board member of <a href="/wiki/Green_Corps" title="Green Corps">Green Corps</a><sup id="cite_ref-Green_Corps_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Corps-6">[6]</a></sup> and the Mertz Gilmore Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-Mertz_Gilmore_Foundation_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mertz_Gilmore_Foundation-7">[7]</a></sup> He has a background in grassroots organizing, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SustMeme_CSR_.26_Business_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SustMeme_CSR_.26_Business-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean energy">clean energy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SustMeme_Climate_Change_and_Energy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SustMeme_Climate_Change_and_Energy-9">[9]</a></sup> Radford lives in Washington, DC with his wife Eileen.<sup id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Radford’s <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_change" title="Theory of change">theory of change</a> shifted from viewing governments as arbitrators between public and private interests on environmental issues, to finding that most governments are captured by industry. Rather than fighting first for new laws, which could be blocked by industries, he has focused on pressuring large companies to change their practices and enlisted them as allies in pushing for strong environmental protections.<sup id="cite_ref-CleanTechIQ_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CleanTechIQ-11">[11]</a></sup> Examples include <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> campaigns that convinced <a href="/wiki/Apple,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Apple, Inc.">Apple, Inc.</a> and similar companies to shift to 100% clean energy and lobby utilities and regulators to make that possible,<sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Business_Insider_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Insider-13">[13]</a></sup> as well as work to protect both the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_rainforest" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian rainforest">Indonesian rainforest</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea_Canyons" class="mw-redirect" title="Bering Sea Canyons">Bering Sea Canyons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Environmental_Leader_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Leader-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Radford argues that the combination of creating industry champions and “outside pressure” focused on the government are the keys to passing new laws to protect the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-CleanTechIQ_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CleanTechIQ-11">[11]</a></sup> However, Radford has also been a vocal leader calling for the United States to pass <a href="/wiki/Campaign_finance_reform_in_the_United_States" title="Campaign finance reform in the United States">campaign finance reform</a> and respect all Americans' voting rights to shift power in politics from corporations towards people and fulfill "the promise of American democracy." Radford is considered by many to be a modern-day transcendentalist, in that he is very focused on nature and the environment, and he is an advocate of democracy.<sup id="cite_ref-C-SPAN_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C-SPAN-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radford received his B.A. from <a href="/wiki/Washington_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington University">Washington University</a> in St. Louis in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Washington_University_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_University-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_activism">Early activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radford began his environmental activism as a high school student at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Park_and_River_Forest_High_School" title="Oak Park and River Forest High School">Oak Park and River Forest High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Park,_Illinois" title="Oak Park, Illinois">Oak Park</a>, a Chicago suburb, volunteering for an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> campaign to stop the building of trash incinerators in the West Side of Chicago near his family's Oak Park home.<sup id="cite_ref-Oak_Park_and_River_Forest_High_School_Tradition_of_Excellence_Past_Award_Recipients_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oak_Park_and_River_Forest_High_School_Tradition_of_Excellence_Past_Award_Recipients-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>His first job as a grassroots organizer came as a canvasser for Illinois <a href="/wiki/PIRG" class="mw-redirect" title="PIRG">PIRG</a>. While studying political science and business at <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_in_St._Louis" title="Washington University in St. Louis">Washington University in St. Louis</a>, he directed campaign and canvass offices during summers for the <a href="/wiki/Fund_for_Public_Interest_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Fund for Public Interest Research">Fund for Public Interest Research</a> for clients including the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Campaign" title="Human Rights Campaign">Human Rights Campaign</a>, <a href="/wiki/PIRGIM" class="mw-redirect" title="PIRGIM">PIRGIM</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_PIRG" class="mw-redirect" title="Ohio PIRG">Ohio PIRG</a> and worked part-time during school for the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduating college in 1998, Radford became a Lead Organizer at <a href="/wiki/Green_Corps" title="Green Corps">Green Corps</a>, the field school for environmental organizing.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Field_director_of_Ozone_Action">Field director of Ozone Action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Field director of Ozone Action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1999 to 2001 Radford was Field Director for Ozone Action, an organization dedicated to working on the atmospheric threats of global warming and ozone depletion. As field director, Radford planned and executed a number of grassroots campaigns, including a campaign during the 2000 presidential primaries, which was the initial impetus for Senator John McCain sponsoring the Climate Stewardship Act.<sup id="cite_ref-Canvassing_Works_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canvassing_Works-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Living_on_Earth_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Living_on_Earth-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Radford also managed the grassroots mobilization for the Global Warming Divestiture Campaign, which resulted in <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texaco" title="Texaco">Texaco</a>, and other companies ending their funding the <a href="/wiki/Global_Climate_Coalition" title="Global Climate Coalition">Global Climate Coalition</a>, which spread misinformation about global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-Industry_Ignored_Its_Scientists_on_Climate_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Industry_Ignored_Its_Scientists_on_Climate-23">[23]</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, the result of the campaign was “the latest sign of divisions within heavy industry over how to respond to global warming.”<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founder_of_Power_Shift">Founder of Power Shift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Founder of Power Shift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Radford founded Power Shift,<sup id="cite_ref-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest-19">[19]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organization</a> dedicated to driving <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean energy">clean energy</a> market breakthroughs and building the <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> base to stop <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Power_Shift_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power_Shift-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Executive Director of Power Shift, Radford worked closely with the cities of San Diego, Chula Vista, and Berkeley, CA, as well as nine other municipalities, to secure investments for installation of solar energy systems and implementation of energy efficiency measures in municipal buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fund_for_the_Public_Interest-19">[19]</a></sup> Radford also helped to convince <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> to adopt innovative new means of financing clean energy infrastructure for wind and solar installations that made them affordable to average Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-The_National_Journal_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_National_Journal-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leading_Greenpeace_USA">Leading Greenpeace USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leading Greenpeace USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 2009, at the age of 33, Radford was selected as the youngest ever Executive Director of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian-28">[28]</a></sup> Radford’s tenure at Greenpeace USA is best known for convincing over 100 corporations to change their environmental practices;<sup id="cite_ref-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally._Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper.E2.80.94the_notorious_destroyer_of_pristine_tiger_and_orangutan_habitat.E2.80.94says_it.27s_changing_its_ways._29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally._Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper.E2.80.94the_notorious_destroyer_of_pristine_tiger_and_orangutan_habitat.E2.80.94says_it.27s_changing_its_ways.-29">[29]</a></sup> exposing the anti-environmental influence of the <a href="/wiki/Koch_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Koch Brothers">Koch Brothers</a>, making them a household name;<sup id="cite_ref-Politico_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico-30">[30]</a></sup> increasing the organization's net income by 80%;<sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace-12">[12]</a></sup> launching the organization’s grassroots organizing and canvass programs;<sup id="cite_ref-Source_Watch_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source_Watch-31">[31]</a></sup> and serving as a founder of the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Initiative" title="Democracy Initiative">Democracy Initiative</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Democracy_Initiative_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Democracy_Initiative-2">[2]</a></sup> a national coalition of major unions, environmental groups, civil rights and government reform organizations working for universal voter registration, to get money out of politics, and to reform Senate rules. In September 2013, Radford announced that he would step down on April 30, 2014, once he had completed five years of service as Executive Director.<sup id="cite_ref-The_National_Journal_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_National_Journal-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a> reporter <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Revkin" title="Andrew Revkin">Andrew Revkin</a> referred to a Greenpeace campaign during Radford's tenure as “Activism at Its Best.”<sup id="cite_ref-dotearth.blogs.nytimes.com_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dotearth.blogs.nytimes.com-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ben_Jealous" class="mw-redirect" title="Ben Jealous">Ben Jealous</a>, former president and chief executive officer of the <a href="/wiki/NAACP" class="mw-redirect" title="NAACP">NAACP</a> as well as co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Initiative" title="Democracy Initiative">Democracy Initiative</a> with Radford, described Radford at the helm of Greenpeace as "a modern movement building giant. He has built powerful diverse coalitions to bolster the fights for the environment and voting rights. In the process he has shown himself to be unmatched in mobilizing everyday people to fund their movements directly.” Environmental leader <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a> stated: “During Radford’s tenure, Greenpeace has been helping the whole <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> shift back towards its roots: local, connected, tough.”<sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before becoming Executive Director of Greenpeace USA, Radford served as the director of the organization’s <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">Grassroots</a> Program.<sup id="cite_ref-Grist.org_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grist.org-34">[34]</a></sup> In that capacity, he started and directed the organization's street and door-to-door canvasses, online-to-offline organizing team, social media team, the Greenpeace Student Network, and the Greenpeace Semester.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_USA_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace_USA-35">[35]</a></sup> Under Radford, the street and door-to-door canvassing programs grew to include nearly 400 canvassers in almost 20 cities across the country and was responsible for doubling the organization's budget.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_USA_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace_USA-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_corporate_behavior">Changing corporate behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changing corporate behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Climate_Coalition">Global Climate Coalition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global Climate Coalition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Radford managed the grassroots efforts of a national <a href="/wiki/Divestment" title="Divestment">divestment</a>/<a href="/wiki/Disinvestment" title="Disinvestment">disinvestment</a> campaign,<sup id="cite_ref-Meet_Philip_Radford.2C_Executive_Director_of_Greenpeace_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meet_Philip_Radford.2C_Executive_Director_of_Greenpeace-36">[36]</a></sup> which forced <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texaco" title="Texaco">Texaco</a>, and other companies to stop funding the <a href="/wiki/Global_Climate_Coalition" title="Global Climate Coalition">Global Climate Coalition</a>, which spread misinformation about global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-Industry_Ignored_Its_Scientists_on_Climate_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Industry_Ignored_Its_Scientists_on_Climate-23">[23]</a></sup> Soon thereafter, the GCC ended operations.<sup id="cite_ref-RIP:_Global_Climate_Coalition.3B_Global_Climate_Coalition_Ends_Its_Work.3B_Voice_for_Industry_Opposed_Global_Treaty_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIP:_Global_Climate_Coalition.3B_Global_Climate_Coalition_Ends_Its_Work.3B_Voice_for_Industry_Opposed_Global_Treaty-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>–winning journalist <a href="/wiki/Ross_Gelbspan" title="Ross Gelbspan">Ross Gelbspan</a> called the industry defections a "Technical Knockout" of the Global Climate Coalition.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Heat_is_Online_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Heat_is_Online-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citigroup">Citigroup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Citigroup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, while running Power Shift, Radford launched a campaign to push Citibank to offer and promote Energy Efficient Mortgages (EEMs).<sup id="cite_ref-Last_Call_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Last_Call-39">[39]</a></sup> Citi was "missing the opportunity to help stop global warming by phasing out fossil fuel investments and promoting clean energy now," Radford said. "The irony is that if Citi financed solar for people’s homes, solar energy could be made immediately affordable for millions of Americans today."<sup id="cite_ref-Multinational_Monitor.2C_April_2002_-_VOLUME_23_-_NUMBER_4_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Multinational_Monitor.2C_April_2002_-_VOLUME_23_-_NUMBER_4-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2004, Citigroup agreed to offer and promote EEMs for residential wind, energy efficiency, and solar installations that would make clean energy affordable for millions of Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kimberly-Clark">Kimberly-Clark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kimberly-Clark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Radford oversaw the grassroots mobilization efforts on the <a href="/wiki/Kleercut" title="Kleercut">Kleercut</a> Campaign in the United States and, later, the entire U.S. component of the global campaign when he became Greenpeace’s Executive Director,<sup id="cite_ref-Grist.org_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grist.org-34">[34]</a></sup> targeting Kimberly-Clark for sourcing 22% of its paper pulp from Canadian <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest_of_Canada" title="Boreal forest of Canada">boreal forest</a>s containing 200-year-old trees. The campaign included intervening in Kleenex commercial shoots,<sup id="cite_ref-Kleenex_Gets_Punk.E2.80.99d_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kleenex_Gets_Punk.E2.80.99d-42">[42]</a></sup> convincing twenty-two universities and colleges to take action such as cancelling contracts,<sup id="cite_ref-About_the_Student_Network_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_the_Student_Network-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace-44">[44]</a></sup> recruiting 500 companies to boycott Kimberly-Clark,<sup id="cite_ref-Forest_Friendly_500_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forest_Friendly_500-45">[45]</a></sup> over 1,000 protests of the company, and more.<sup id="cite_ref-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-More_Than_Tissues_in_a_Box_of_Kleenex_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-More_Than_Tissues_in_a_Box_of_Kleenex-46">[46]</a></sup> The result: On August 5, 2009, Kimberly-Clark announced that it would source 40% of its paper fiber from recycled content or other sustainable sources – a 71% increase from 2007 levels. The demand created by Kimberly-Clark for sustainably logged fiber was greater than the supply, enabling the company to convince logging companies to change their practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclusive:_How_Kimberly-Clark_Ditched_its_Forest-Destroying_Reputation_and_Embraced_Greenpeace-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_and_Kimberly-Clark_Settle_Feud_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace_and_Kimberly-Clark_Settle_Feud-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_Pulp_and_Paper">Asia Pulp and Paper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asia Pulp and Paper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2010 to 2013, Radford managed Greenpeace US team that persuaded major U.S. companies to cancel their contracts with <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pulp_and_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Pulp and Paper">Asia Pulp and Paper</a> (APP) – the world's third largest paper company<sup id="cite_ref-Paper_Producer_to_Stop_Clearing_of_Indonesian_Forests_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paper_Producer_to_Stop_Clearing_of_Indonesian_Forests-48">[48]</a></sup> – to push APP to stop destroying ancient forests.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Greenpeace and its allies succeeded in convincing more than 100 corporate customers of APP to sever their ties with the company,<sup id="cite_ref-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally._Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper.E2.80.94the_notorious_destroyer_of_pristine_tiger_and_orangutan_habitat.E2.80.94says_it.27s_changing_its_ways._29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally._Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper.E2.80.94the_notorious_destroyer_of_pristine_tiger_and_orangutan_habitat.E2.80.94says_it.27s_changing_its_ways.-29">[29]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-A_Roaring_Thank_You_on_Behalf_of_the_Sumatran_Tigers_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Roaring_Thank_You_on_Behalf_of_the_Sumatran_Tigers-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hasbro_Turns_Over_a_New_Leaf.2C_Steps_Up_For_Rainforests_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasbro_Turns_Over_a_New_Leaf.2C_Steps_Up_For_Rainforests-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lego" title="Lego">Lego</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kmart" title="Kmart">Kmart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Attention_K-Mart_Shoppers.21_Get_With_the_Program_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Attention_K-Mart_Shoppers.21_Get_With_the_Program-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IGA_(supermarkets)" title="IGA (supermarkets)">IGA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_Lion" title="Food Lion">Food Lion</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pressure_on_Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper_yielding_corporate_responsibility.2C_new_strategies_on_rainforest_policy_.5BUPDATED.5D_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pressure_on_Asia_Pulp_.26_Paper_yielding_corporate_responsibility.2C_new_strategies_on_rainforest_policy_.5BUPDATED.5D-53">[53]</a></sup> In total, the campaign against APP cut nearly 80% of APP’s U.S. market. On February 5, 2013, Asia Pulp and Paper announced a deforestation policy protecting Indonesian rainforests.<sup id="cite_ref-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paper_Giant_Pledges_to_Leave_the_Poor_Rainforest_Alone._Finally-54">[54]</a></sup> Referring to the victory, <i>New York Times</i> reporter <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Revkin" title="Andrew Revkin">Andrew Revkin</a> heralded the campaign with a piece titled: "Activism at Its Best: Greenpeace’s Push to Stop the Pulping of Rainforests".<sup id="cite_ref-dotearth.blogs.nytimes.com_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dotearth.blogs.nytimes.com-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apple.2C_Facebook.2C_and_cloud_computing_go_100.25_clean_energy">Apple, Facebook, and cloud computing go 100% clean energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Apple, Facebook, and cloud computing go 100% clean energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 21, 2011, Greenpeace released a report highlighting data centers, which consumed up to 2% of all global electricity and this amount was projected to increase. Radford stated “we are concerned that this new explosion in electricity use could lock us into old, polluting energy sources instead of the clean energy available today.”<sup id="cite_ref-Dirty_Data_Report_Card_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dirty_Data_Report_Card-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Business_Insider" title="Business Insider">Business Insider</a> reported that after <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_USA" title="Greenpeace USA">Greenpeace USA</a> campaigns, “tech giants like Apple, Google, Facebook, and Salesforce have promised to power their data centers with renewable energy, a pledge that led Duke Energy, the nation's largest power utility and one of the most flagrant emitters of CO2, to begin providing clean energy to win their business.”<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Insider_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Insider-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procter_.26_Gamble.2C_Colgate_Palmolive.2C_palm_oil_and_Indonesian_rainforests">Procter &amp; Gamble, Colgate Palmolive, palm oil and Indonesian rainforests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Procter &amp; Gamble, Colgate Palmolive, palm oil and Indonesian rainforests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, deforestation in Indonesia, which accounts for .1% of the world’s surface, caused 4% of global warming pollution. One of the major drivers of deforestation was clearing the forest to grow palm oil plantations.<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Insider_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Insider-13">[13]</a></sup> Under Radford, the Greenpeace USA team persuaded <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Insider_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Insider-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colgate_Palmolive" class="mw-redirect" title="Colgate Palmolive">Colgate Palmolive</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mondelez" class="mw-redirect" title="Mondelez">Mondelez</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and other major companies to demand sustainably grown palm oil.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_U.S._supermarkets">Major U.S. supermarkets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phil_Radford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Major U.S. supermarkets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under Radford, Greenpeace ran a campaign targeting supermarket chains<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> – which sell 50% of all seafood in the U.S. – to convince them to stop selling threatened fish, adopt sustainable seafood policies, and lobby for policies such as <a href="/wiki/Marine_reserve" title="Marine reserve">marine reserves</a> to protect the oceans. Some retailers simply moved from being scored in a report;<sup id="cite_ref-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7-59">[59]</a></sup> others like <a href="/wiki/Costco" title="Costco">Costco</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Trader_Joe%27s" title="Trader Joe's">Trader Joe's</a><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> required public campaigns to change them. The result: major seafood retailers such as <a href="/wiki/Whole_Foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Whole Foods">Whole Foods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safeway_Inc." title="Safeway Inc.">Safeway Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wegmans" title="Wegmans">Wegmans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harris_Teeter" title="Harris Teeter">Harris Teeter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meijer" title="Meijer">Meijer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a> have implemented sustainable seafood purchasing policies;<sup id="cite_ref-Environmental_Leader_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Leader-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Where_to_Buy_the_Best_Fish_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Where_to_Buy_the_Best_Fish-62">[62]</a></sup> Trader Joe's, <a href="/wiki/Aldi" title="Aldi">Aldi</a>, Costco, <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target Corporation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/A%26P" class="mw-redirect" title="A&amp;P">A&amp;P</a> have dramatically cut the threatened fish that they sell; Whole Foods, Safeway Inc., Trader Joe’s, <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hy-Vee" title="Hy-Vee">Hy-Vee</a> introduced sustainably caught canned tuna;<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and Wegmans, Whole Foods, Safeway Inc., Target, and Trader Joe's have lobbied for strong ocean policies, such as protecting the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">Ross Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> Canyons as <a href="/wiki/Marine_reserve" title="Marine reserve">marine reserves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Environmental_Leader_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Leader-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carting_Away_the_Oceans_7-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Where_to_Buy_the_Best_Fish_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Where_to_Buy_the_Best_Fish-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
