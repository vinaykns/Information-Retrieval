<p>In <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>, the term <b>built environment</b> refers to the man-made surroundings that provide the setting for human activity, ranging in scale from buildings to parks. It has been defined as "the humanitarian-made space in which people live, work, and recreate on a day-to-day basis."<sup id="cite_ref-roof_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roof-1">[1]</a></sup> The "built environment encompasses places and spaces created or modified by people including buildings, parks, and transportation systems." In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> research has expanded the definition of "built environment" to include <a href="/wiki/Healthy_diet" title="Healthy diet">healthy</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food access</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_gardening" title="Community gardening">community gardens</a>,<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://s3.amazonaws.com/academia.edu.documents/44398590/14-IJTPE-Issue26-Vol8-No1-Mar2016-pp81-87.pdf?AWSAccessKeyId=AKIAJ56TQJRTWSMTNPEA&amp;Expires=1483131396&amp;Signature=gIDntD2bsxFSNNgi%2BR2REeETjFc%3D&amp;response-content-disposition=inline%3B%20filename%3DIJTPE_Journal_IMPACT_OF_BUILT_ENVIRONMEN.pdf">mental health</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Walkability" title="Walkability">walkability</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Bikeability" title="Bikeability">bikeability</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Built_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Early concepts of built environment date to <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a>: <a href="/wiki/Hippodamus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hippodamus">Hippodamus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Miletos" class="mw-redirect" title="Miletos">Miletos</a>, known as the "father of urban planning,"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> developed <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek cities</a> from 498&#160;BC to 408&#160;BC that created order by using grid plans that mapped the city. These early city plans eventually gave way to the City Beautiful movement in the late&#160;1800s and early&#160;1900s, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Hudson_Burnham" class="mw-redirect" title="Daniel Hudson Burnham">Daniel Hudson Burnham</a>, a reformist for the Progressivism movement who actively promoted "a reform of the landscape in tandem with political change."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The effort was in partnership with others who believed that beautifying American cities would improve the moral compass of the cities and encourage the upper class to spend their money in cities. This beautification process included parks and architectural design.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By mid-century modernist "indifferent" design influenced the character of work and public spaces, followed by what Alexander describes as a late twentieth century "revival of interest relating to the concept of place (including the built environment), and its relevance to mental health and other fields of study."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_built_environment">Modern built environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Built_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern built environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, built environments are typically used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> field that addresses the design, construction, management, and use of these man-made surroundings as an interrelated whole as well as their relationship to human activities over time (rather than a particular element in isolation or at a single moment in time). The field is generally not regarded as a traditional profession or <a href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic discipline">academic discipline</a> in its own right, instead drawing upon areas such as <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, law, <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a>, public health, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, design, engineering, technology, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sustainability" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental sustainability">environmental sustainability</a>. Within the field of public health, built environments are referred to as building or renovating areas in an effort to improve the community’s well-being through construction of “aesthetically, health improved, and environmentally improved landscapes and living structures”.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Adaptation_in_Nepal" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate Change Adaptation in Nepal">For example; community forest user group in Nepal is multidimensional institution, which serves goods and services to the communities through natural resource management</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health">Public health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Built_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In public health, built environment refers to physical environments that are designed with health and wellness as integral parts of the communities. Research has indicated that the way neighborhoods are created can affect both the physical activity and mental health of the communities’ residents.<sup id="cite_ref-renalds_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renalds-8">[8]</a></sup> Studies have shown that built environments that were expressly designed to improve physical activity are linked to higher rates of physical activity, which in turn, positively affects health.<sup id="cite_ref-carlson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carlson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WTM3_Gnarly_0028.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A separated bike lane in New York City.</div>

<p>Neighborhoods with more <a href="/wiki/Walkability" title="Walkability">walkability</a> had lower rates of <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> as well as increased physical activity among its residents. They also had lower rates of <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, higher <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">social capital</a>, and less <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol abuse</a>. Walkability features in these neighborhoods include safety, <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalk</a> construction, as well as destinations in which to walk.<sup id="cite_ref-renalds_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renalds-8">[8]</a></sup> In addition, the perception of a walkable neighborhood, one that is perceived to have good sidewalks and connectivity, is correlated with higher rates of physical activity.<sup id="cite_ref-carlson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carlson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Assessments of walkability have been completed through the use of <a href="/wiki/GIS" class="mw-redirect" title="GIS">GIS</a> programs, such as the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://Street%20Smart%20Walk%20Score">Street Smart Walk Score</a>. This example of a walkability assessment tool determines distances to grocery stores and other amenities, as well as connectivity and intersection frequency using specific addresses.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Assessments such as the Street Smart Walk Score can be utilized by city and country planning departments to improve existing walkability of communities.</p>

<p>Public health also addresses additional components of built environments including “<a href="/wiki/Bikeability" title="Bikeability">bikeability</a>” and healthy food access such as proximity to grocery stores and <a href="/wiki/Community_garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Community garden">community gardens</a>. Bikeability refers to the access that an area has granted to safe biking through multiple bike paths and <a href="/wiki/Bike_lane" title="Bike lane">bike lanes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Both walkability and bikeability have been cited as determinants of physical activity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Access to healthy food is also an important component of the built environment. A higher density of convenience stores has been associated with obesity in children.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In contrast, improved access to community supermarkets and farmer’s markets is correlated with lower overweight status.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Specifically in low income neighborhoods, the presence of a local grocery store is correlated with lower BMI/overweight risk.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Community gardens are also considered a part of the built environment, and have been shown to increase fruit and vegetable intake among gardeners.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Scholars say that community gardens have also been shown to have positive social and psychological impacts that lead to lower levels of <a href="/wiki/Stress_(psychological)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress (psychological)">stress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, and an improved sense of wellness, affecting the overall health of the individual and the community.</p>
<p>The intersection of public health with other disciplines is evident in the design process of built environments which includes <a href="/wiki/Environmental_planning" title="Environmental planning">environmental planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Policy_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Policy development">policy development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land-use_planning" title="Land-use planning">land-use planning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-roof_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roof-1">[1]</a></sup> Research suggests that people are more active in mixed-use communities or those that incorporate retail and residential and densely populated areas as well as those with good street connectivity.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Those who preferred to walk and live in walkable environments often have lower obesity rates and drive less compared to those who preferred living in auto-dependent environments.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The strength of the evidence for reducing obesity through environment has been highlighted by the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Disease_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Center for Disease Control">Center for Disease Control</a> in its Common Community Measures for Obesity Prevention Project, which includes measures of healthy food access and physical activity environments.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscape_architecture">Landscape architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Built_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landscape architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
