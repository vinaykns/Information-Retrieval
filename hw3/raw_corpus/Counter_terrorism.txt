<p><b>Counter-terrorism</b> (also spelled <b>counterterrorism</b>) (also called <b>anti-terrorism</b>) incorporates the practice, <a href="/wiki/Military_tactics" title="Military tactics">military tactics</a>, techniques, and strategy that government, military, law enforcement, business, and <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">intelligence agencies</a> use to combat or prevent terrorism. Counter-terrorism strategies include attempts to counter financing of terrorism.</p>
<p>If <a href="/wiki/Definitions_of_terrorism" title="Definitions of terrorism">terrorism</a> is part of a broader <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgency</a>, counter-terrorism may employ <a href="/wiki/Counter-insurgency" title="Counter-insurgency">counter-insurgency</a> measures. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> use the term <a href="/wiki/Foreign_internal_defense" title="Foreign internal defense">foreign internal defense</a> for programs that support other countries in attempts to suppress insurgency, lawlessness, or subversion or to reduce the conditions under which these threats to security may develop.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Special_Irish_Branch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Special_Irish_Branch.jpg/220px-Special_Irish_Branch.jpg" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Special_Irish_Branch.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="234" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Special_Irish_Branch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Special_Branch" title="Special Branch">Special Branch</a> detectives on an undercover operation at the <a href="/wiki/London_Docks" title="London Docks">London Docks</a>, 1911.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In response to the escalating <a href="/wiki/Fenian_dynamite_campaign" title="Fenian dynamite campaign">terror campaign</a> in Britain carried out by the militant <a href="/wiki/Fenians" class="mw-redirect" title="Fenians">Irish Fenians</a> in the 1880s, the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Vernon_Harcourt_(politician)" title="William Vernon Harcourt (politician)">Sir William Harcourt</a>, established the first counter-terrorism unit ever. The <a href="/wiki/Special_Irish_Branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Irish Branch">Special Irish Branch</a> was initially formed as a section of the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Investigation_Department" title="Criminal Investigation Department">Criminal Investigation Department</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Police">London Metropolitan Police</a> in 1883, to combat Irish republican terrorism through infiltration and subversion.</p>
<p>Harcourt envisioned a permanent unit dedicated to the prevention of politically motivated violence through the use of modern techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Undercover_operation" title="Undercover operation">undercover infiltration</a>. This pioneering branch was the first to be trained in counter-terrorism techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its name was changed to <a href="/wiki/Special_Branch" title="Special Branch">Special Branch</a> as it had its remit gradually expanded<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to incorporate a general role in counterterrorism, combating foreign subversion and infiltrating <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a>. <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_agency" title="Law enforcement agency">Law enforcement agencies</a>, in Britain and elsewhere, established similar units.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Counterterrorism forces expanded with the perceived growing threat of terrorism in the late 20th century. Specifically, after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, Western governments made counter-terrorism efforts a priority, including more foreign cooperation, shifting tactics involving <a href="/wiki/Red_team" title="Red team">red teams</a> <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and preventive measures.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning">Planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence.2C_surveillance_and_reconnaissance">Intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_cycle_management" title="Intelligence cycle management">Intelligence cycle management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_analysis" title="Intelligence analysis">Intelligence analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/HUMINT" class="mw-redirect" title="HUMINT">HUMINT</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Counterintelligence" title="Counterintelligence">Counterintelligence</a></div>
<p>Most counter-terrorism strategies involve an increase in standard police and domestic intelligence. The central activities are traditional: <a href="/wiki/Telephone_tapping" title="Telephone tapping">interception of communications</a>, and the tracing of persons. New technology has, however, expanded the range of <a href="/wiki/Manhunt_(military)" title="Manhunt (military)">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manhunt_(law_enforcement)" title="Manhunt (law enforcement)">law enforcement</a> operations.</p>

<p>Domestic intelligence is often directed at specific groups, defined on the basis of origin or religion, which is a source of political controversy. <a href="/wiki/Mass_surveillance" title="Mass surveillance">Mass surveillance</a> of an entire population raises objections on <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> grounds. <a href="/wiki/Homegrown_terrorism" class="mw-redirect" title="Homegrown terrorism">Homegrown terrorists</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Lone-wolf_fighter" class="mw-redirect" title="Lone-wolf fighter">lone wolves</a> are often harder to detect because of their citizenship or legal status and ability to stay under the radar.</p>
<p>To select the effective action when terrorism appears to be more of an isolated event, the appropriate government organizations need to understand the source, motivation, methods of preparation, and tactics of terrorist groups. Good intelligence is at the heart of such preparation, as well as political and social understanding of any grievances that might be solved. Ideally, one gets information from inside the group, a very difficult challenge for <a href="/wiki/HUMINT" class="mw-redirect" title="HUMINT">HUMINT</a> because <a href="/wiki/Clandestine_cell_system" title="Clandestine cell system">operational terrorist cells</a> are often small, with all members known to one another, perhaps even related.<sup id="cite_ref-Feiler2007_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feiler2007-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Counterintelligence" title="Counterintelligence">Counterintelligence</a> is a great challenge with the security of cell-based systems, since the ideal, but nearly impossible, goal is to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Clandestine_HUMINT" title="Clandestine HUMINT">clandestine source</a> within the cell. Financial tracking can play a role, as can <a href="/wiki/SIGINT" class="mw-redirect" title="SIGINT">communications intercept</a>, but both of these approaches need to be balanced against legitimate expectations of privacy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_contexts">Legal contexts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal contexts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anti-terrorism_legislation" title="Anti-terrorism legislation">Anti-terrorism legislation</a></div>
<p>In response to the growing legislation.</p>
<dl>
<dt>United Kingdom</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The United Kingdom has had anti-terrorism legislation in place for more than thirty years. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Prevention_of_Violence_Act_1939&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Prevention of Violence Act 1939 (page does not exist)">Prevention of Violence Act 1939</a> was brought in response to an Irish Republican Army (IRA) campaign of violence under the <a href="/wiki/S-Plan" title="S-Plan">S-Plan</a>. This act had been allowed to expire in 1953 and was repealed in 1973 to be replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Prevention_of_Terrorism_Acts" title="Prevention of Terrorism Acts">Prevention of Terrorism Acts</a> a response to <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> in Northern Ireland. From 1974 to 1989 the temporary provisions of the act were renewed annually.</li>
<li>In 2000 the Acts were replaced with the more permanent <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_Act_2000" title="Terrorism Act 2000">Terrorism Act 2000</a>, which contained many of their powers, and then the <a href="/wiki/Prevention_of_Terrorism_Act_2005" title="Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005">Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Anti-terrorism,_Crime_and_Security_Act_2001" title="Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001">Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001</a> was formally introduced into the Parliament November 19, 2001 two months after the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a> in the United States. It received royal assent and went into force on December 13, 2001. On December 16, 2004 the Law Lords ruled that Part 4 was incompatible with the European Convention on Human Rights, but under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a> it remained in force. The <a href="/wiki/Prevention_of_Terrorism_Act_2005" title="Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005">Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005</a> was drafted to answer the Law Lords ruling and the <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_Act_2006" title="Terrorism Act 2006">Terrorism Act 2006</a> creates new offences related to terrorism, and amends existing ones. The Act was drafted in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/7_July_2005_London_bombings" title="7 July 2005 London bombings">7 July 2005 London bombings</a>, and like its predecessors some of its terms have proven to be highly controversial.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since 1978 the UK's terrorism laws have been regularly reviewed by a security-cleared Independent Reviewer of Terrorism Legislation, whose reports are submitted to Parliament and published in full.</p>
<dl>
<dt>United States</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>U.S. legal issues surrounding this issue include rulings on the domestic employment of <a href="/wiki/Deadly_force" title="Deadly force">deadly force</a> by law enforcement organizations.</li>
<li>Search and seizure is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a>.</li>
<li>The U.S. passed the <a href="/wiki/USA_PATRIOT_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="USA PATRIOT Act">USA PATRIOT Act</a> after the September 11 attacks, as well as a range of other legislation and <a href="/wiki/Executive_orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive orders">executive orders</a> relating to national security.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> was established to consolidate domestic security agencies to coordinate anti-terrorism, as well as national response to major natural disasters and accidents.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Posse_Comitatus_Act" title="Posse Comitatus Act">Posse Comitatus Act</a> limits domestic employment of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>, requiring Presidential approval prior to deploying the Army and/or the Air Force. <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a> policy also applies this limitation to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, because the Posse Comitatus Act doesn't cover naval services, even though they are federal military forces. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> can be employed domestically on Presidential order, as was done during the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_riots_of_1992" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles riots of 1992">Los Angeles riots of 1992</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Beltway_Sniper" class="mw-redirect" title="Beltway Sniper">Beltway Sniper</a> incidents.</li>
<li>External or international use of lethal force would require a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_finding" title="Presidential finding">Presidential finding</a>.</li>
<li>In February 2017, sources claimed that the Trump administration intends to rename and revamp the U.S. government program <i>Countering Violent Extremism</i> (CVE) to solely focus on Islamist extremism.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Australia</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Australia has passed several anti-terrorism acts. In 2004, a bill comprising three acts <a href="/wiki/Australian_anti-terrorism_legislation,_2004" title="Australian anti-terrorism legislation, 2004">Anti-terrorism Act, 2004, (No 2) and (No 3)</a> was passed. Then Attorney-General, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Ruddock" title="Philip Ruddock">Philip Ruddock</a>, introduced the <i>Anti-terrorism bill, 2004</i> on March 31. He described it as <i>"a bill to strengthen Australia's counter-terrorism laws in a number of respects&#160;– a task made more urgent following the recent tragic <a href="/wiki/11_March_2004_Madrid_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="11 March 2004 Madrid attacks">terrorist bombings in Spain</a>."</i> He said that Australia's counter-terrorism laws "require review and, where necessary, updating if we are to have a legal framework capable of safeguarding all Australians from the scourge of terrorism." The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Anti-Terrorism_Act_2005" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Anti-Terrorism Act 2005">Australian Anti-Terrorism Act 2005</a> supplemented the powers of the earlier acts. The Australian legislation allows police to detain suspects for up to two weeks without charge and to electronically track suspects for up to a year. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Anti-Terrorism_Act_2005#The_.22Shoot_to_Kill.22_Clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Anti-Terrorism Act 2005">Australian Anti-Terrorism Act of 2005</a> included a "shoot-to-kill" clause. In a country with entrenched <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democratic</a> traditions, the measures are controversial and have been criticized by <a href="/wiki/Civil_libertarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil libertarian">civil libertarians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> groups.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a></dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Israel monitors a list of designated <a href="/wiki/Terrorist_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrorist organization">terrorist organizations</a> and has laws forbidding membership in such organizations, funding or helping them in any way.</li>
<li>On December 14, 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Israeli Supreme Court">Israeli Supreme Court</a> ruled <a href="/wiki/Israeli_targeted_killings" class="mw-redirect" title="Israeli targeted killings">targeted killings</a> were a permitted form of self-defense.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2016 the Israeli <a href="/wiki/Knesseth" class="mw-redirect" title="Knesseth">Knesseth</a> passed a comprehensive law against terrorism, forbidding any kind of terrorism and support of terrorism, and setting severe punishments for terrorists. The law also regulate legal efforts against terrorism.<sup id="cite_ref-Israel-ani-terror-law_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Israel-ani-terror-law-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights">Human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg/170px-John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg" width="170" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg/255px-John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg/340px-John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Walker_Lindh_Custody.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/John_Walker_Lindh" title="John Walker Lindh">John Walker Lindh</a> was captured as an enemy combatant during the United States' <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">2001 invasion of Afghanistan</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the primary difficulties of implementing effective counter-terrorist measures is the waning of civil liberties and individual privacy that such measures often entail, both for citizens of, and for those detained by states attempting to combat terror.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At times, measures designed to tighten security have been seen as <a href="/wiki/Abuses_of_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Abuses of power">abuses of power</a> or even violations of human rights.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of these problems can include prolonged, incommunicado detention without judicial review; risk of subjecting to torture during the transfer, return and extradition of people between or within countries; and the adoption of security measures that restrain the rights or freedoms of citizens and breach principles of non-discrimination.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup> Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li>In November 2003 <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> passed new counter-terrorism laws that were widely criticized by local human rights groups for being vague and overbroad. Critics claim that the laws put the basic rights of free expression, association, and assembly at risk. Malaysia persisted in holding around 100 alleged militants without trial, including five Malaysian students detained for alleged terrorist activity while studying in Karachi, Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>In November 2003 a Canadian-Syrian national, Maher Arar, alleged publicly that he had been tortured in a Syrian prison after being handed over to the Syrian authorities by U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>In December 2003 Colombia's congress approved legislation that would give the military the power to arrest, tap telephones and carry out searches without warrants or any previous judicial order.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Images of unpopular treatment of detainees in US custody in Iraq and other locations have encouraged international scrutiny of US operations in the war on terror.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Hundreds of foreign nationals remain in prolonged indefinite detention without charge or trial in Guantánamo Bay, despite international and US constitutional standards some groups believe outlaw such practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Hundreds of people suspected of connections with the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> or al Qa'eda remain in long-term detention in Pakistan or in US-controlled centers in Afghanistan.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>China has used the "war on terror" to justify its policies in the predominantly Muslim Xinjiang Uighur Autonomous Region to stifle Uighur identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Morocco, Saudi Arabia, Tunisia, Yemen and other countries, scores of people have been arrested and arbitrarily detained in connection with suspected terrorist acts or links to opposition armed groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Until 2005 eleven men remained in high security detention in the UK under the Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Many would argue that such violations could exacerbate rather than counter the terrorist threat.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup> Human rights advocates argue for the crucial role of human rights protection as an intrinsic part to fight against terrorism.<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This suggests, as proponents of <a href="/wiki/Human_security" title="Human security">human security</a> have long argued, that respecting human rights may indeed help us to incur security. <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> included a section on confronting terrorism in the recommendations in the Madrid Agenda arising from the Madrid Summit on Democracy and Terrorism (Madrid March 8–11, 2005):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Democratic principles and values are essential tools in the fight against terrorism. Any successful strategy for dealing with terrorism requires terrorists to be isolated. Consequently, the preference must be to treat terrorism as criminal acts to be handled through existing systems of law enforcement and with full respect for human rights and the rule of law. We recommend: (1) taking effective measures to make impunity impossible either for acts of terrorism or for the abuse of human rights in counter-terrorism measures. (2) the incorporation of human rights laws in all anti-terrorism programmes and policies of national governments as well as international bodies."<sup id="cite_ref-Amnesty_International_2005_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amnesty_International_2005-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While international efforts to combat terrorism have focused on the need to enhance cooperation between states, proponents of human rights (as well as <a href="/wiki/Human_security" title="Human security">human security</a>) have suggested that more effort needs to be given to the effective inclusion of human rights protection as a crucial element in that cooperation. They argue that international human rights obligations do not stop at borders and a failure to respect human rights in one state may undermine its effectiveness in the international effort to cooperate to combat terrorism.<sup id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_News_2004_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_News_2004-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preemptive_neutralization">Preemptive neutralization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Preemptive neutralization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some countries see preemptive attacks as a legitimate strategy. This includes capturing, killing, or disabling suspected terrorists before they can mount an attack. <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Russia have taken this approach, while Western European states generally do not.</p>
<p>Another major method of preemptive neutralization is <a href="/wiki/Interrogation" title="Interrogation">interrogation</a> of known or suspected terrorists to obtain information about specific plots, targets, the identity of other terrorists, whether or not the interrogation subjects himself is guilty of terrorist involvement. Sometimes more extreme methods are used to increase <a href="/wiki/Suggestibility" title="Suggestibility">suggestibility</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sleep_deprivation" title="Sleep deprivation">sleep deprivation</a> or drugs. Such methods may lead captives to offer false information in an attempt to stop the treatment, or due to the confusion brought on by it. These methods are not tolerated by European powers. In 1978 the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> ruled in the <a href="/wiki/Ireland_v._United_Kingdom_(1978)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland v. United Kingdom (1978)">Ireland v. United Kingdom</a> case that <a href="/wiki/Five_techniques" title="Five techniques">such methods</a> amounted to a practice of inhuman and degrading treatment, and that such practices were in breach of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> Article 3 (art. 3).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-military">Non-military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_security" title="Human security">human security</a> paradigm outlines a non-military approach which aims to address the enduring underlying inequalities which fuel terrorist activity. Causal factors need to be delineated and measures implemented which allow equal access to resources and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> for all people. Such activities empower citizens providing 'freedom from fear' and 'freedom from want'.</p>
<p>This can take many forms including the provision of clean drinking water, education, vaccination programs, provision of food and shelter and protection from violence, military or otherwise. Successful human security campaigns have been characterized by the participation of a diverse group of actors including governments, <a href="/wiki/NGOs" class="mw-redirect" title="NGOs">NGOs</a>, and citizens.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Foreign_internal_defense" title="Foreign internal defense">Foreign internal defense</a> programs provide outside expert assistance to a threatened government. FID can involve both non-military and military aspects of counter-terrorism.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

</div>
<p>Terrorism has often been used to justify military intervention in countries like Pakistan where terrorists are said to be based. That was the main stated justification for the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">U.S. invasion of Afghanistan</a>. It was also a stated justification for the <a href="/wiki/Second_Russian_invasion_of_Chechnya" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Russian invasion of Chechnya">second Russian invasion of Chechnya</a>.</p>
<p>Military intervention has not always been successful in stopping or preventing future terrorism, like during the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mau_Mau_uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="Mau Mau uprising">Mau Mau uprising</a>, and most of the campaigns against the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/S-Plan" title="S-Plan">S-Plan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Border_Campaign_(IRA)" class="mw-redirect" title="Border Campaign (IRA)">Border Campaign (IRA)</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> in Northern Ireland. Although military action can disrupt a terrorist group's operations temporarily, it sometimes doesn't end the threat completely.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus repression by the military in itself (particularly if it is not accompanied by other measures) usually leads to short term victories, but tend to be unsuccessful in the long run (e.g. the French's doctrine described in <a href="/wiki/Roger_Trinquier" title="Roger Trinquier">Roger Trinquier</a>'s book <i>Modern War</i><sup id="cite_ref-Trinquier_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trinquier-17">[17]</a></sup> used in <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>). However, new methods (see the new <i>Counterinsurgency</i> Field Manual<sup id="cite_ref-FM3-24_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FM3-24-18">[18]</a></sup>) such as those taken in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> have yet to be seen as beneficial or ineffectual.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Police, fire, and emergency medical response organizations have obvious roles. Local <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighters</a> and emergency medical personnel (often called "first responders") have plans for mitigating the effects of terrorist attacks, although police may deal with threats of such attacks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Target-hardening">Target-hardening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Target-hardening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whatever the target of terrorists, there are multiple ways of hardening the targets to prevent the terrorists from hitting their mark, or reducing the damage of attacks. One method is to place <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Barrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Jersey Barrier">Jersey Barrier</a> or other sturdy obstacles outside tall or politically sensitive buildings to prevent car and <a href="/wiki/Truck_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Truck bomb">truck bombing</a>. Another way to reduce the impact of attacks is to design buildings for rapid evacuation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aircraft cockpits are kept locked during flights, and have reinforced doors, which only the pilots in the cabin are capable of opening. UK <a href="/wiki/Railway_stations" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway stations">railway stations</a> removed their <a href="/wiki/Waste_container" title="Waste container">rubbish bins</a> in response to the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional IRA</a> threat, as convenient locations for depositing bombs.</p>
<p>Scottish stations removed theirs after the <a href="/wiki/7_July_2005_London_Bombings" class="mw-redirect" title="7 July 2005 London Bombings">7 July 2005 London Bombings</a> as a precautionary measure. The <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Transportation_Authority" title="Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority">Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority</a> purchased bomb-resistant barriers after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_terrorist_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11 terrorist attacks">September 11 terrorist attacks</a>.</p>
<p>A more sophisticated target-hardening approach must consider industrial and other critical industrial infrastructure that could be attacked. Terrorists need not import chemical weapons if they can cause a major industrial accident such as the <a href="/wiki/Bhopal_disaster" title="Bhopal disaster">Bhopal disaster</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Explosion" title="Halifax Explosion">Halifax Explosion</a>. Industrial chemicals in manufacturing, shipping, and storage need greater protection, and some efforts are in progress.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> To put this risk into perspective, the first <a href="/wiki/Poison_gas_in_World_War_I#1915:_large_scale_use_and_lethal_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Poison gas in World War I">major lethal chemical attack in WWI</a> used 160 tons of chlorine. Industrial shipments of chlorine, widely used in water purification and the chemical industry, travel in 90 or 55 ton tank cars.</p>
<p>To give one more example, the North American electrical grid has already demonstrated, in the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Blackout_of_2003" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeast Blackout of 2003">Northeast Blackout of 2003</a>, its vulnerability to natural disasters coupled with inadequate, possibly insecure, <a href="/wiki/SCADA" title="SCADA">SCADA</a> (supervisory control and data acquisition) networks. Part of the vulnerability is due to deregulation leading to much more interconnection in a grid designed for only occasional power-selling between utilities. A small number of terrorists, attacking key power facilities when one or more engineers have infiltrated the power control centers, could wreak havoc.</p>
<p>Equipping likely targets with containers (i.e., bags) of pig lard has been utilized to discourage attacks by Islamist suicide bombers. The technique was apparently used on a limited scale by British authorities in the 1940s.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The approach stems from the idea that Muslims perpetrating the attack would not want to be "soiled" by the lard in the moment prior to dying. The idea has been suggested more recently as a deterrent to suicide bombings in Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, the actual effectiveness of this tactic is probably limited as it is possible that a sympathetic Islamic scholar could issue a <a href="/wiki/Fatwa" title="Fatwa">fatwa</a> proclaiming that a suicide bomber would not be polluted by the swine products.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Command_and_control">Command and control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Command and control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In North America and other continents, for a threatened or completed terrorist attack, the <a href="/wiki/Incident_Command_System" title="Incident Command System">Incident Command System</a> (ICS) is apt to be invoked to control the various services that may need to be involved in the response. ICS has varied levels of escalation, such as might be needed for multiple incidents in a given area (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Attacks_on_the_London_Underground#2005_terrorist_bomb_attacks" title="Attacks on the London Underground">the 2005 bombings in London</a> or the <a href="/wiki/2004_Madrid_train_bombings" title="2004 Madrid train bombings">2004 Madrid train bombings</a>, or all the way to a <a href="/wiki/National_Response_Plan" title="National Response Plan">National Response Plan</a> invocation if national-level resources are needed. National response, for example, might be needed for a nuclear, biological, radiological, or large chemical attack.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage_mitigation">Damage mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Damage mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">Fire departments</a>, perhaps supplemented by public works agencies, utility providers (e.g., gas, water, electricity), and heavy construction contractors, are most apt to deal with the physical consequences of an attack.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_security">Local security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Local security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Again under an incident command model, local police can isolate the incident area, reducing confusion, and specialized police units can conduct tactical operations against terrorists, often using <a href="#Counter-terrorism_tactical_units">specialized counter-terrorist tactical units</a>. Bringing in such units will normally involve civil or military authority beyond the local level.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_services">Medical services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Medical services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services" title="Emergency medical services">Emergency medical services</a> will triage, treat, and transport the more seriously affected victims to hospitals, which will also need to have mass casualty and <a href="/wiki/Triage" title="Triage">triage</a> plans in place.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Public_health_surveillance" title="Public health surveillance">Public health agencies</a>, from local to national level, may be designated to deal with identification, and sometimes mitigation, of possible biological attacks, and sometimes chemical or radiologic contamination.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactical_units">Tactical units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tactical units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Besides various <a href="/wiki/Security_agency" title="Security agency">security agencies</a>, there are <a href="/wiki/Special_Weapons_And_Tactics" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Weapons And Tactics">elite tactical units</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Special_Mission_Unit" title="Special Mission Unit">special mission units</a>, whose role is to directly engage terrorists and prevent terrorist attacks.</p>

<p>Such units perform both in preventive actions, <a href="/wiki/Hostage_rescue" class="mw-redirect" title="Hostage rescue">hostage rescue</a> and responding to on-going attacks. Countries of all sizes can have highly trained counter-terrorist teams. Tactics, techniques and procedures for <a href="/wiki/Manhunt_(military)" title="Manhunt (military)">manhunting</a> are under constant development.</p>
<p>Most of these measures deal with terrorist attacks that affect an area, or threaten to do so. It is far harder to deal with assassination, or even reprisals on individuals, due to the short (if any) warning time and the quick exfiltration of the assassins.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>These units are specially trained in <a href="/wiki/Military_tactics" title="Military tactics">tactics</a> and are very well equipped for <a href="/wiki/Close_quarters_battle" class="mw-redirect" title="Close quarters battle">CQB</a> with emphasis on stealth and performing the mission with minimal casualties. The units include take-over force (assault teams), <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">snipers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Explosive_Ordnance_Disposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive Ordnance Disposal">EOD</a> experts, dog handlers and intelligence officers. See <a href="/wiki/Counter-intelligence_and_counter-terrorism_organizations" title="Counter-intelligence and counter-terrorism organizations">Counter-intelligence and counter-terrorism organizations</a> for national command, intelligence, and incident mitigation.</p>
<p>The majority of counter-terrorism operations at the tactical level, are conducted by state, federal and national <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">law enforcement agencies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">intelligence agencies</a>. In some countries, the military may be called in as a last resort. Obviously, for countries whose military are legally permitted to conduct police operations, this is a non-issue, and such counter-terrorism operations are conducted by their military.</p>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/Counter-intelligence" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-intelligence">counter-intelligence</a> for command, intelligence and warning, and incident mitigation aspects of counter-terror.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designing_Anti-terrorism_systems">Designing Anti-terrorism systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Designing Anti-terrorism systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The scope for Anti-terrorism systems is very large in physical terms (long borders, vast areas, high traffic volumes in busy cities, etc.) as well as in other dimensions, such as type and degree of terrorism threat, political and diplomatic ramifications, and legal issues. In this environment, the development of a persistent Anti-terrorism protection system is a daunting task. Such a system should bring together diverse state-of-the-art technologies to enable persistent intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance missions, and enable potential actions. Designing such a system-of-systems comprises a major technological project.</p>
<p>A particular design problem for this system is that it will face many <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainties</a> in the future. The threat of terrorism may increase, decrease or remain the same, the type of terrorism and location are difficult to predict, and there are technological uncertainties. Yet we want to design a terrorism system conceived and designed today in order to prevent acts of terrorism for a decade or more. A potential solution is to incorporate <a href="/wiki/Flexibility_(engineering)" title="Flexibility (engineering)">flexibility</a> into system design for the reason that the flexibility embedded can be exercised in future as <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> unfolds and updated information arrives. And the design and valuation of a protection system should not be based on a single scenario, but an array of scenarios. Flexibility can be incorporated in the design of the terrorism system in the form of options that can be exercised in the future when new information is available. Using these '<a href="/wiki/Real_options" class="mw-redirect" title="Real options">real options</a>' will create a flexible Anti-terrorism system that is able to cope with new requirements that may arise.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement.2FPolice">Law enforcement/Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Law enforcement/Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While some countries with longstanding terrorism problems, such as Israel, have law enforcement agencies primarily designed to prevent and respond to terror attacks,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> in other nations, counter-terrorism is a relatively more recent objective of civilian police and law enforcement agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-Deflem_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deflem-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>While some civil-libertarians and criminal justice scholars have called-out efforts of law enforcement agencies to combat terrorism as futile and expensive<sup id="cite_ref-HelmsCostanzaJohnson_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HelmsCostanzaJohnson-31">[31]</a></sup> or as threats to civil liberties,<sup id="cite_ref-HelmsCostanzaJohnson_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HelmsCostanzaJohnson-31">[31]</a></sup> other scholars have begun describing and analyzing the most important dimensions of the policing of terrorism as an important dimension of counterterrorism, especially in the post-9/11 era, and have argued how police institutions view terrorism as a matter of crime control.<sup id="cite_ref-Deflem_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deflem-29">[29]</a></sup> Such analyses bring out the civilian police role in counterterrorism next to the military model of a 'war on terror'.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>Pursuant to passage of the <a href="/wiki/Homeland_Security_Act_of_2002" class="mw-redirect" title="Homeland Security Act of 2002">Homeland Security Act of 2002</a>, federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies began to systemically reorganize.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Two primary federal agencies (the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice</a> (DOJ) and the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> (DHS)) house most of the federal agencies that are prepared to combat domestic and international terrorist attacks. These include the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Border_Patrol" title="United States Border Patrol">Border Patrol</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> and the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a>.</p>
<p>Following suit from federal changes pursuant to 9/11, however, most state and local law enforcement agencies began to include a commitment to "fighting terrorism" in their mission statements.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Local agencies began to establish more patterned lines of communication with federal agencies. Some scholars have doubted the ability of local police to help in the war on terror and suggest their limited manpower is still best utilized by engaging community and targeting street crimes.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>While counter-terror measures (most notably heightened airport security, immigrant <a href="/wiki/Racial_profiling" title="Racial profiling">profiling</a><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and border patrol) have been adapted during the last decade, to enhance counter-terror in law enforcement, there have been remarkable limitations to assessing the actual utility/effectiveness of law enforcement practices that are ostensibly preventative.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Thus, while sweeping changes in counter-terrorism rhetoric redefined most American post 9/11 law enforcement agencies in theory, it is hard to assess how well such hyperbole has translated into practice.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Intelligence-led_policing" title="Intelligence-led policing">intelligence-led policing</a>(ILP) efforts, the most quantitatively amenable starting point for measuring the effectiveness of any policing strategy (i.e.: Neighborhood Watch, Gun Abatement, Foot Patrols, etc.) is usually to assess total financial costs against clearance rates or arrest rates. Since terrorism is such a rare event phenomena,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> measuring arrests or clearance rates would be a non-generalizable and ineffective way to test enforcement policy effectiveness. Another methodological problem in assessing counter-terrorism efforts in law enforcement hinges on finding operational measures for key concepts in the study of homeland security. Both terrorism and homeland security are relatively new concepts for criminologists, and academicians have yet to agree on the matter of how to properly define these ideas in a way that is accessible.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_2">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-terrorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_special_forces_units" class="mw-redirect" title="List of special forces units">List of special forces units</a>.</div>

<p>Given the nature of operational counter-terrorism tasks national military organizations do not generally have dedicated units whose sole responsibility is the prosecution of these tasks. Instead the counter-terrorism function is an element of the role, allowing flexibility in their employment, with operations being undertaken in the domestic or international context.</p>
<p>In some cases the legal framework within which they operate prohibits military units conducting operations in the domestic arena; <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> policy, based on the <a href="/wiki/Posse_Comitatus_Act" title="Posse Comitatus Act">Posse Comitatus Act</a>, forbids domestic counter-terrorism operations by the U.S. military. Units allocated some operational counter-terrorism task are frequently <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Forces">Special Forces</a> or similar assets.</p>
<p>In cases where military organisations do operate in the domestic context some form of formal handover from the law enforcement community is regularly required, to ensure adherence to the legislative framework and limitations. such as the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Embassy_Siege" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian Embassy Siege">Iranian Embassy Siege</a>, the British police formally turned responsibility over to the <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">Special Air Service</a> when the situation went beyond police capabilities.</p>
