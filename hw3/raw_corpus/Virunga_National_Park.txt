<p>The <b>Virunga National Park</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Parc National des Virunga</i></span>), formerly named <b>Albert National Park</b>, is a 7,800-square-kilometre (3,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) National Park that stretches from the <a href="/wiki/Virunga_Mountains" title="Virunga Mountains">Virunga Mountains</a> in the south, to the <a href="/wiki/Rwenzori_Mountains" title="Rwenzori Mountains">Rwenzori Mountains</a> in the north, in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, bordering <a href="/wiki/Volcanoes_National_Park" title="Volcanoes National Park">Volcanoes National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rwenzori_Mountains_National_Park" title="Rwenzori Mountains National Park">Rwenzori Mountains National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_National_Park" title="Queen Elizabeth National Park">Queen Elizabeth National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.</p>
<p>The park was established in 1925 as Africa's first national park and is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>-designated <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> since 1979. In recent years, <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ituri_conflict" title="Ituri conflict">Congo Civil War</a> have seriously damaged its wildlife population. The park is managed by the Congolese National Park Authorities, the <a href="/wiki/Institut_Congolais_pour_la_Conservation_de_la_Nature" title="Institut Congolais pour la Conservation de la Nature">Institut Congolais pour la Conservation de la Nature</a> (ICCN) and its partner the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Virunga Foundation (page does not exist)">Virunga Foundation</a>, formerly known as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Africa_Conservation_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Africa Conservation Fund (page does not exist)">Africa Conservation Fund</a> (UK). The current park director since 2008 is the Belgian Prince <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_de_Merode" title="Emmanuel de Merode">Emmanuel de Merode</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The park was created in 1925 by <a href="/wiki/King_Albert_I_of_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="King Albert I of Belgium">King Albert I of Belgium</a> as the first national park on the continent of Africa. It was founded primarily to protect the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">mountain gorillas</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> living in the forests of the Virunga Mountains controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a>, but later expanded north to include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rwindi_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rwindi Plains (page does not exist)">Rwindi Plains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Edward" title="Lake Edward">Lake Edward</a> and the Rwenzori Mountains in the far north.</p>

<p>In the first 35 years, the boundary of the park took shape, poaching was kept to a minimum, and sustainable tourism thrived due to the work of a large body of hand-picked Congolese rangers and dedicated wardens. Land remuneration and the use of park resources such as fishing and hunting by the local population became an ongoing problem and attempts were made to solve these issues.</p>
<p>When the Belgians granted Congo independence in 1960 the new state deteriorated rapidly, and so did the park. It was only in 1969 when President <a href="/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Mobutu</a> began to take a personal interest in conservation, that the park was revived. In the process of Mobutu's <a href="/wiki/Africanization" title="Africanization">Africanization</a> campaign, it was renamed Virunga National Park, and the first Congolese Wildlife Authority was established.</p>
<p>Virunga fared well for the better part of the 1970s. Foreign investment helped to improve the park's infrastructure and training facilities, and the park became a popular destination for tourists, receiving on average 6500 visitors a year. In 1979 UNESCO designated the park as a World Heritage Site.<sup id="cite_ref-unesco_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1980s the Mobutu regime began to lose its hold on power and the country began a long slide into chaos. The park suffered terribly. Poaching depleted Virunga's large mammal populations, infrastructure was destroyed, and many rangers were killed. The Congolese Wildlife Authority slowly lost control of Virunga and UNESCO changed the World Heritage Site status to "endangered."<sup id="cite_ref-unesco_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013 the <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> raised concerns about plans by the UK based <a href="/wiki/Soco_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Soco International">Soco International</a> to carry out exploration for oil in the park.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Currently more than 80% of Virunga National Park has been allocated as oil concessions.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Soco_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Soco International">Soco International</a>'s own environmental impact assessment reports admit that oil exploration is likely to cause pollution, irreparably damage habitats and bring poaching to the park. The <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> have launched a campaign to petition<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Soco to refrain exploring the world heritage area for oil, and thereby avoid these outcomes. As of August 30, 2014, SOCO demobilized its operations in the DRC.</p>
<p>World Wildlife Fund executives now acknowledge that the battle over Virunga is hardly over. SOCO has yet to relinquish its operating permits or commit to an unconditional withdrawal.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg/220px-Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg/330px-Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg/440px-Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="678" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Virunga_National_Park_Gorilla.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A mountain gorilla in the park</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The park is known for its exceptional (bio)diversity, containing more bird, mammal and reptile species than any protected area on the African continent. Although <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">mountain gorillas</a> are now extremely rare and listed as one of the most critically endangered species, successful conservation work has helped to secure the remaining populations. Their populations actually increased during the years of political upheaval in the region (1994–2004), and have continued to do so even throughout the difficult period of 2007-2008. The 2010 mountain gorilla census has indicated that the conservation efforts of Virunga have been very successful regarding the gorilla population. Both savanna and <a href="/wiki/Forest_elephant" class="mw-redirect" title="Forest elephant">forest elephants</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a> and low land gorillas can still be found in Virunga, along with <a href="/wiki/Okapi" title="Okapi">okapi</a>, giraffes, <a href="/wiki/African_buffalo" title="African buffalo">buffaloes</a> and many endemic <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. The neighboring <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hoyo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mount Hoyo (page does not exist)">Mount Hoyo</a> area was managed with the park and is home to a population of Bambuti <a href="/wiki/Pygmy" class="mw-redirect" title="Pygmy">pygmy</a> people, <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_conflict">Human conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the civil wars, the park has suffered somewhat. Land invasions and intense poaching have challenged the park authorities to the limit, but most rangers have remained active. Since 1994, about 140 rangers have been killed in the line of duty protecting the park from illegal poaching and land acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Amongst other military activity, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Forces_for_the_Liberation_of_Rwanda" title="Democratic Forces for the Liberation of Rwanda">Democratic Forces for the Liberation of Rwanda</a> (FDLR) used the park as a safe location when they have come under sustained attack, such as <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Nkunda" title="Laurent Nkunda">Laurent Nkunda</a>'s offensives against them in April–May 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The park was occupied by Nkunda's forces on 26 October 2008, during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Goma" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Goma">Battle of Goma</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Current situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>However over the last three years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the park has seen remarkable regeneration, with heavy investment in tourism development, social infrastructure as well as safety. Currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> over 3000 tourists a year visit the southern sector of Virunga National Park to admire the gorillas as well as the lava lake of the Nyiragongo Volcano. The park has been<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> closed until 20 July because of political insecurity. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Virunga Foundation (page does not exist)">Virunga Foundation</a> signed a 25-year management agreement to manage the park in March 2015. The park currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> receives most of its funding from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the Howard G Buffett Foundation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A Mai Mai militia attacked a park facility and killed two park rangers and a Congolese soldier in October 2012. Five of the Mai Mai militiamen died in the attack. Congolese Revolutionary Army (also known as M23) allegedly has a base camp inside the park.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_de_M%C3%A9rode" class="mw-redirect" title="Emmanuel de Mérode">Emmanuel de Mérode</a>, a respected naturalist and member of the Belgian <a href="/wiki/House_of_M%C3%A9rode" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Mérode">House of Mérode</a>, has been the director of the park since 2008. On April 15, 2014 he was <a href="/wiki/Ambush" title="Ambush">ambushed</a> on a road in the park and shot four times in the stomach and legs by unknown assailants, but he survived the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beni_massacre" title="Beni massacre">Beni massacre</a> occurred inside the national park.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_Industry_threat">Oil Industry threat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oil Industry threat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Virunga is currently under threat from UK oil company SOCO who want to undergo oil exploration within the park.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Seismic tests were carried out by SOCO, and confirmed the presence of oil.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> This announcement has reignited the debate to the merits of exploring oil in the park.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> This could involve disruptive seismic testing, forest clearing, and deep underground drilling.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> This can put at risk hundreds of lesser known fragile species which are found in no other country. Also, if Lake Edwards is drilled, it can have a detrimental impact on the people of the region. 30,000 people benefit economically from fishing in the park. Another 20,000 benefit from commercial activities related to the fishing industry.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In response, a joint plea was launched by International environment and right groups to the Ugandan government and Republic of Congo to prevent oil drilling in or around the Virunga National park.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> Regardless, the Ugandan government plans to receive bids on six new oil licenses.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> To this day, no oil exploration has occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 13, 2015, BBC was reporting that the Democratic Republic of Congo says it wants to redraw the boundaries of Virunga National Park to allow for oil exploration.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charcoal_Trade">Charcoal Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virunga_National_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Charcoal Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Trees are being cut down at a rate of 2.3%. The country has lost 14,331 square miles of forest between 2001 and 2010. The primary cause has been the charcoal industry.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The charcoal industry is worth almost 2 billion dollars. Virunga is the only source of charcoal for Rwanda because they passed a law banning the production of charcoal within its border. Trees are being cut down, covered in mud, and set on fire in order to make charcoal. There is also a strong military presence near Virunga, and many of these soldiers have reportedly not received paychecks for years. Therefore, these soldiers have turned to charcoal trade and other illegal activities in order to support themselves. Many of the gorilla killings are fueled by the charcoal trade.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">[15]</a></sup> There have been reported executions of gorillas as act of sabotage by people in the charcoal business who want to see the gorillas dead.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Many park officials risk their lives to protect the park. However, some are in collaboration with the military and poachers. Poaching is a source of income for many people due to the economic instability in the country. Many of poachers are members of local militias. Most of money goes back to militias, and use the money to buy weapons.</p>
