<p>The <b>Brookings Institution</b> is a century-old <a href="/wiki/US" class="mw-redirect" title="US">American</a> research group on <a href="/wiki/Think_Tank_Row_(Washington_DC)" title="Think Tank Row (Washington DC)">Think Tank Row</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-about_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup> It conducts research and education in the social sciences, primarily in economics, metropolitan policy, governance, foreign policy, and global economy and development.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RSB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSB-3">[3]</a></sup> Its stated mission is to "provide innovative and practical recommendations that advance three broad goals: strengthen American democracy; foster the economic and social welfare, security and opportunity of all Americans; and secure a more open, safe, prosperous, and cooperative international system."<sup id="cite_ref-about_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brookings has five research programs at its Washington, D.C. campus (Economic Studies,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Foreign Policy<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Governance Studies,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Global Economy and Development,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and Metropolitan Policy<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>) and three international centers based in <a href="/wiki/Doha" title="Doha">Doha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brookings_Doha_Center" title="Brookings Doha Center">Brookings Doha Center</a>);<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (Brookings-Tsinghua Center for Public Policy);<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (Brookings India).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>'s <i>2015 Global Go To <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">Think Tanks</a> Report</i>, Brookings was named "Think Tank of the Year."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brookings states that its staff "represent diverse points of view" and describes itself as <a href="/wiki/Non-partisan" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-partisan">non-partisan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-about_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-research_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-research-13">[13]</a></sup> while the media sometimes describes Brookings as "liberal".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> An academic analysis of Congressional records from 1993 to 2002 found that Brookings was referenced by conservative politicians almost as frequently as liberal politicians, earning a score of 53 on a 1–100 scale with 100 representing the most liberal score.<sup id="cite_ref-sscnet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sscnet-15">[15]</a></sup> The same study found Brookings to be the most frequently cited think tank by the US media and politicians.<sup id="cite_ref-sscnet_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sscnet-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_stance">Political stance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political stance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/501(c)(3)" class="mw-redirect" title="501(c)(3)">501(c)(3)</a> non-profit organization, Brookings describes itself as independent and non-partisan. A 2005 academic study by UCLA concluded it was centrist in that it was referenced as an authority almost equally by both conservative and liberal politicians in congressional records from 1993 to 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-sscnet_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sscnet-15">[15]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> has referred to the organization as liberal, liberal-centrist, centrist, and conservative.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> has described Brookings as centrist and liberal.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> has described Brookings as liberal-leaning and centrist before opining that it did not believe such labels mattered.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1977, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a></i> described it as the "nation's pre-eminent liberal think tank".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> has described Brookings as centrist<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/Politico_(newspaper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Politico (newspaper)">Politico</a></i> has used the term "center-left".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The left-wing, media watchdog group <a href="/wiki/Fairness_and_Accuracy_in_Reporting" title="Fairness and Accuracy in Reporting">Fairness and Accuracy in Reporting</a> describes it as conservative.<sup id="cite_ref-fairreport_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fairreport-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAIR_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAIR-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAIR91_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAIR91-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAIR02_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAIR02-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some liberals argue, however, that despite its left-of-center reputation, Brookings foreign policy scholars were overly supportive of <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration" title="Foreign policy of the George W. Bush administration">Bush administration policies abroad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yglesias_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yglesias-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-greenwald070812_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwald070812-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Yglesias" title="Matthew Yglesias">Matthew Yglesias</a>, a liberal blogger, has pointed out that Brookings's <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._O%27Hanlon" title="Michael E. O'Hanlon">Michael E. O'Hanlon</a> frequently agrees with scholars from conservative organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Weekly_Standard" title="The Weekly Standard">The Weekly Standard</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Project_for_a_New_American_Century" class="mw-redirect" title="Project for a New American Century">Project for a New American Century</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yglesias_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yglesias-37">[37]</a></sup> Similarly, Brookings fellow and research director <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Wittes" title="Benjamin Wittes">Benjamin Wittes</a> is a member of the conservative <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Institution" title="Hoover Institution">Hoover Institution</a>'s Task Force on National Security and Law.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brookings scholars have served in Republican and Democratic administrations, including <a href="/wiki/Mark_McClellan" title="Mark McClellan">Mark McClellan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Ron Haskins<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Indyk" title="Martin Indyk">Martin Indyk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Brookings Board of Trustees is composed mainly of Democrats, such as <a href="/wiki/Laura_Tyson" title="Laura Tyson">Laura Tyson</a>, former chair of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Economic_Advisers" title="Council of Economic Advisers">Council of Economic Advisers</a> under Bill Clinton, but has a few centrist Republicans such as <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Duberstein" title="Kenneth Duberstein">Kenneth Duberstein</a>, a former chief of staff to Ronald Reagan. Aside from political figures, the board of trustees includes leaders in business and industry, including <a href="/wiki/Phil_Knight" title="Phil Knight">Philip H. Knight</a>, Chairman of <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc" class="mw-redirect" title="Nike, Inc">Nike, Inc</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1916.E2.80.931979">1916–1979</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1916–1979">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_S._Brookings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Robert_S._Brookings.jpg/220px-Robert_S._Brookings.jpg" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Robert_S._Brookings.jpg/330px-Robert_S._Brookings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Robert_S._Brookings.jpg/440px-Robert_S._Brookings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3605" data-file-height="5048" /></a>

<p>Brookings was founded in 1916 as the Institute for Government Research (IGR), with the mission of becoming "the first private organization devoted to analyzing public policy issues at the national level".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Institution's founder, philanthropist <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Brookings" title="Robert S. Brookings">Robert S. Brookings</a> (1850–1932), originally financed the formation of three organizations: the Institute for Government Research, the Institute of Economics, and the Robert Brookings Graduate School affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_in_St._Louis" title="Washington University in St. Louis">Washington University in St. Louis</a>. The three were merged into the Brookings Institution on December 8, 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-RSB_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSB-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Critchlow_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Critchlow-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> economists at Brookings embarked on a large scale study commissioned by President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> to understand the underlying causes of the depression. Brookings's first president Harold Moulton and other Brookings scholars later led an effort to oppose President Roosevelt's <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> policies because they thought such measures were impeding economic recovery.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the entry into World War II in 1941, Brookings researchers turned their attention to aiding the administration with a series of studies on mobilization. In 1948, Brookings was asked to submit a plan for the administration of the European Recovery Program. The resulting organization scheme assured that the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Plan" title="Marshall Plan">Marshall Plan</a> was run carefully and on a businesslike basis.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1952, Robert Calkins succeeded Moulton as president of the Brookings Institution. He secured grants from the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a> that put the Institution on a strong financial basis. He reorganized the Institution around the Economic Studies, Government Studies, and Foreign Policy Programs. In 1957, the Institution moved from Jackson Avenue to a new research center near <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Circle" title="Dupont Circle">Dupont Circle</a> in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kermit_Gordon" title="Kermit Gordon">Kermit Gordon</a> assumed the presidency of Brookings in 1967. He began a series of studies of program choices for the federal budget in 1969 entitled "Setting National Priorities". He also expanded the Foreign Policy Studies Program to include research in national security and defense. After the election of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> to the presidency in 1968, the relationship between the Brookings Institution and the White House deteriorated; at one point Nixon's aide <a href="/wiki/Charles_Colson" title="Charles Colson">Charles Colson</a> proposed a firebombing of the Institution. Yet throughout the 1970s, Brookings was offered more federal research contracts than it could handle.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>

<p>By the 1980s, the Institution faced an increasingly competitive and ideologically charged intellectual environment. The need to reduce the federal budget <a href="/wiki/Government_budget_deficit" class="mw-redirect" title="Government budget deficit">deficit</a> became a major research theme as well as investigating problems with national security and government inefficiency. Bruce MacLaury,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> fourth president of Brookings, also established the Center for Public Policy Education to develop workshop conferences and public forums to broaden the audience for research programs.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Armacost" title="Michael Armacost">Michael Armacost</a> became the fifth president of the Brookings Institution and led an effort to refocus the Institution's mission heading into the 21st century. Under Armacost's direction, Brookings created several interdisciplinary research centers such as the Center on Urban and Metropolitan Policy (now the Metropolitan Policy Program, led by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_J._Katz" title="Bruce J. Katz">Bruce J. Katz</a>), brought attention to the strengths of cities and metropolitan areas, and the Center for Northeast Asian Policy Studies, which brings together specialists from different Asian countries to examine regional problems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strobe_Talbott" title="Strobe Talbott">Strobe Talbott</a> became president of Brookings in 2002. Shortly thereafter, Brookings launched the <a href="/wiki/Saban_Center_for_Middle_East_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Saban Center for Middle East Policy">Saban Center for Middle East Policy</a> and the <b>John L. Thornton China Center</b>. In October 2006, Brookings announced the establishment of the Brookings-Tsinghua Center in Beijing. In July 2007, the Institution announced the creation of the Engelberg Center for Health Care Reform to be directed by senior fellow <a href="/wiki/Mark_McClellan" title="Mark McClellan">Mark McClellan</a>, and then in October 2007, the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Doha_Center" title="Brookings Doha Center">Brookings Doha Center</a> directed by fellow <a href="/wiki/Hady_Amr" title="Hady Amr">Hady Amr</a> in <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Brookings found itself at the center of controversy over the <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary_obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiduciary obligation">fiduciary obligation</a> of brokers to savers. A nonresident senior fellow, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Litan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Litan (page does not exist)">Robert Litan</a>, presented a study to Congress in July 2015 that contained a cost/benefit analysis regarding a new agency rule. He resigned that position as a result of a firestorm, months later in September 2015, kicked up by Senator <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Warren" title="Elizabeth Warren">Elizabeth Warren</a> (D-MA) charging Litan — who had testified against a fiduciary obligation rule. Litan was unaware of the brand new rule when he presented, and settled with the Brookings Institute immediately after the presentation when they brought it to his attention. Litan's defenders continue to point out that he did disclose, and that Warren's real motive was to silence a reasonable concern over the cost/benefit balance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brookings as an institution produces an Annual Report.<sup id="cite_ref-annualreport_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annualreport-52">[52]</a></sup> The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.brookings.edu/press.aspx">Brookings Institution Press</a> publishes books and journals from the institution's own research as well as authors outside the organization.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The books and journals they publish include <i><a href="/wiki/Brookings_Papers_on_Economic_Activity" title="Brookings Papers on Economic Activity">Brookings Papers on Economic Activity</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <i>Brookings Review</i> (1982-2003, <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Serial_Number" title="International Standard Serial Number">ISSN</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.worldcat.org/search?fq=x0:jrnl&amp;q=n2:0745-1253">0745-1253</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <i>America Unbound: The Bush Revolution in Foreign Policy</i>, <i>Globalphobia: Confronting Fears about Open Trade</i>, <i>India: Emerging Power</i>, <i>Through Their Eyes</i>, <i>Taking the High Road</i>, <i>Masses in Flight</i>, US Public Policy Regarding Sovereign Wealth Fund Investment in the United States<sup id="cite_ref-Rebuilding_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebuilding-57">[57]</a></sup> and <i>Stalemate</i> to name a few. In addition, books, papers, articles, reports, policy briefs and opinion pieces are produced by Brookings research programs, centers, projects and, for the most part, by experts.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_influence">Policy influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policy influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brookings traces its history back to 1916 and has contributed to the creation of the United Nations, the Marshall Plan, and the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a>, as well as influenced policies of <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a>, broad-based tax reform, <a href="/wiki/Welfare_reform" title="Welfare reform">welfare reform</a>, and foreign aid.<sup id="cite_ref-Brookings_Institution_History_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brookings_Institution_History-60">[60]</a></sup> It is ranked the number one think tank in the U.S. in the annual think tank index published by <i>Foreign Policy</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Foreign_Policy:_The_Think_Tank_Index_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foreign_Policy:_The_Think_Tank_Index-61">[61]</a></sup> and number one in the world in the Global Go To Think Tank Index;<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> of the 200 most prominent think tanks in the U.S., the Brookings Institution's research is the most widely cited by the media.<sup id="cite_ref-fairreport_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fairreport-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mediabias_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mediabias-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 1997 survey of congressional staff and journalists, Brookings ranked as the second-most influential and first in credibility among 27 think tanks.<sup id="cite_ref-standford_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-standford-64">[64]</a></sup> Moreover, “Brookings and its researchers are not so concerned, in their work, in affecting the ideological direction of the nation” and rather tend “to be staffed by researchers with strong academic credentials”.<sup id="cite_ref-standford_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-standford-64">[64]</a></sup> Along with the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Foreign_Relations" title="Council on Foreign Relations">Council on Foreign Relations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Endowment_for_International_Peace" title="Carnegie Endowment for International Peace">Carnegie Endowment for International Peace</a>, Brookings is generally considered one of the most influential policy institutes in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-FAIR2_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAIR2-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saban_Center_for_Middle_East_Policy">Saban Center for Middle East Policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saban Center for Middle East Policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, the Brookings Institution established the <a href="/wiki/Saban_Center_for_Middle_East_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Saban Center for Middle East Policy">Saban Center for Middle East Policy</a> "to promote a better understanding of the policy choices facing American decision makers in the Middle East".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The Center is directed by <a href="/wiki/Tamara_Cofman_Wittes" title="Tamara Cofman Wittes">Tamara Cofman Wittes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brookings-Tsinghua_Center_for_Public_Policy">Brookings-Tsinghua Center for Public Policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Brookings-Tsinghua Center for Public Policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, the Brookings Institution established the Brookings-Tsinghua Center (BTC) for Public Policy as a partnership between the Brookings Institution in Washington, D.C. and Tsinghua University's School of Public Policy and Management in Beijing, China. The Center seeks to produce high quality and high impact policy research in areas of fundamental importance for China's development and for U.S.-China relations.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The BTC is directed by Qi Ye.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<p>Based in Qatar, the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Doha_Center" title="Brookings Doha Center">Brookings Doha Center</a> undertakes independent, policy-oriented research on socioeconomic and geopolitical issues facing Muslim-majority states and communities, including relations with the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The center was formally inaugurated by H. E. Sheikh Hamad bin Jassim bin Jabr Al-Thani, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Qatar" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister of Qatar">Prime Minister</a> and minister of foreign affairs of <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, on February 17, 2008. Salman Shaikh is the Center's Director.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_Century_Defense_Initiative">21st Century Defense Initiative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 21st Century Defense Initiative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The 21st Century Defense Initiative (21CDI) is aimed at producing research, analysis, and outreach that address three core issues: the future of war, the future of U.S. defense needs and priorities, and the future of the U.S. defense system.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Initiative draws on the knowledge from regional centers, including the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Center_on_the_United_States_and_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Center on the United States and Europe (page does not exist)">Center on the United States and Europe</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Center_for_Northeast_Asian_Policy_Studies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Center for Northeast Asian Policy Studies (page does not exist)">Center for Northeast Asian Policy Studies</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thornton_China_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thornton China Center (page does not exist)">Thornton China Center</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Saban_Center_for_Middle_East_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Saban Center for Middle East Policy">Saban Center for Middle East Policy</a>, allowing the integration of regional knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-21st_Century_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21st_Century-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/P._W._Singer" title="P. W. Singer">P. W. Singer</a>, author of <a href="/wiki/Wired_for_War" title="Wired for War">Wired for War</a>, serves as Director of the 21st Century Defense Initiative, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._O%27Hanlon" title="Michael E. O'Hanlon">Michael O'Hanlon</a> serves as Director of Research.<sup id="cite_ref-21st_Century_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21st_Century-73">[73]</a></sup> Senior Fellow <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Cohen" title="Stephen P. Cohen">Stephen P. Cohen</a> and Vanda Felbab-Brown<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> are also affiliated with 21CDI.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brookings_Executive_Education">Brookings Executive Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Brookings Executive Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under Brookings President Bruce MacLaury's leadership in the 1980s, the Center for Public Policy Education (CPPE) was formed to develop workshop conferences and public forums to broaden the audience for research programs. In 2005, the Center was renamed the Brookings Center for Executive Education (BCEE), which was shortened to Brookings Executive Education (BEE) with the launch of a partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Olin_Business_School" title="Olin Business School">Olin Business School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_in_St._Louis" title="Washington University in St. Louis">Washington University in St. Louis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_policy_tools">Public policy tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public policy tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On its website, the Brookings Institution provides an interactive web tool, The Fiscal Barometer. The tool shows spending trends for the federal government and local and state governments. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_funding">Foreign funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Foreign funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An investigation by <i>The New York Times</i>, reported on September 6, 2014, found the Brookings Institution to be among more than a dozen Washington research groups to have received tens of millions of dollars from foreign governments in recent years, while producing policy papers, hosting forums, and organizing private briefings with U.S. officials to encourage support for policies aligned with those foreign governments' agenda.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The New York Times</i> published documents showing that Brookings Institution accepted grants from Norway with specific policy requests and helped the country gain access to U.S. government officials, as well as other "deliverables".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-82">[82]</a></sup> In June 2014, Norway agreed to make an additional $4 million donation to Brookings.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-80">[80]</a></sup> Several legal specialists, who examined the documents between the Norway government and Brookings at <i>The Times'</i> request, told the paper that the language of the transactions "appeared to necessitate Brookings filing as a foreign agent" under the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Agent_Registration_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Agent Registration Act">Foreign Agent Registration Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Qatari government was named by <i>The New York Times</i> as "the single biggest foreign donor to Brookings", having reportedly made a $14.8 million, four-year contribution in 2013. A former visiting fellow at a Brookings affiliate in Qatar reportedly said "he had been told during his job interview that he could not take positions critical of the Qatar government in papers".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_80-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-80">[80]</a></sup> Brookings officials denied any connection between the views of their funders and their scholars' work, citing reports that questioned the Qatari government's education reform efforts and criticized its support of militants in Syria. However, Brookings officials reportedly acknowledged that they meet with Qatari government officials regularly to discuss the center's activities and budget, and that the former prime minister of Qatar currently serves on the center's advisory board.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_80-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookings_Institution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main building of the Institution was erected in 1959 on 1775 Massachusetts Avenue. In 2009, Brookings acquired a building across the street, a former mansion built by the Ingalls family in 1922 on a design by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Henri_de_Sibour" title="Jules Henri de Sibour">Jules Henri de Sibour</a>.
