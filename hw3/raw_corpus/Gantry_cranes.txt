<p><b>Gantry cranes</b> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">crane</a> built atop a gantry, which is a structure used to straddle an object or workspace. Gantry cranes for marine application are imposing, multi-story structures prominent at most <a href="/wiki/Container_terminal" title="Container terminal">container terminals</a>, used to load <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_container" title="Intermodal container">intermodal containers</a> on and off <a href="/wiki/Container_ship" title="Container ship">container ships</a>. They can range from these enormous "full" gantry cranes, capable of lifting some of the heaviest loads in the world, to small shop cranes, used for tasks such as lifting automobile engines out of vehicles.</p>
<p>They are also called <b>portal cranes</b>, the "portal" being the empty space straddled by the gantry. The terms gantry crane and <a href="/wiki/Overhead_crane" title="Overhead crane">overhead crane</a> (or bridge crane) are often used interchangeably, as both types of crane straddle their workload. The usual distinction drawn between the two is that with gantry cranes, the entire structure (including gantry) is usually wheeled (often on <a href="/wiki/Track_(rail_transport)" title="Track (rail transport)">rails</a>). By contrast, the supporting structure of an overhead crane is fixed in location, often in the form of the walls or ceiling of a building, to which is attached a movable <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">hoist</a> running overhead along a rail or beam (which may itself move). Further confusing the issue is that gantry cranes may also incorporate a movable beam-mounted hoist in addition to the entire structure being wheeled, and some overhead cranes are suspended from a freestanding gantry.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gantry_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ship-to-Shore_Gantry_Crane">Ship-to-Shore Gantry Crane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gantry_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ship-to-Shore Gantry Crane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Container_crane" title="Container crane">Container crane</a></div>
<p>Ship-to-shore gantry cranes are some of the largest examples of gantry cranes in the world and serve the special purpose of moving shipping containers from container ships to berths for unloading and from berths to container ships for loading. They operate along two rails (waterside and landside designations) spaced based on the size of crane to be used.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Ship-to-Shore Crane Elements</dt>
</dl>
<p>Lateral movement system:</p>
<dl>
<dd>A combination of two sets of typically ten (10) rail wheels. The lateral movement is controlled by a cabin along the landside wheel. During any lateral movement, lights and sirens operate to ensure safety of the crew operating adjacent to the crane. The wheels are mounted to the bottom of the vertical frame/bracing system.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Vertical frame and braces:</p>
<dl>
<dd>A structurally designed system of beams assembled to support the boom, cabin, operating machinery, and the cargo being lifted. They display signage describing restrictions, requirements and identifiers.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Crane boom:</p>
<dl>
<dd>A horizontal beam that runs transversely to the berth. It spans from landside of the landside rail wheels to a length over the edge of the berth. The waterside span is based on the size of ship that it can successfully load/unload. Beams also have the ability to be raised for storage purposes.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Hook:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Device which moves vertically to raise and lower cargo as well as horizontally along the boom's length. For container cranes, a spreader is attached to span the container and lock it safely in place during movement.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Operating cabin:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Encased setup with glass paneled flooring for operator to view the cargo being moved. Elevators which are located along vertical frame members are used to get crew up and down from the cabin.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Storage Equipment:</p>
<dl>
<dd>For temporary storage options between vessel operations, one steel pin is inserted into anchorage arm dropped from each wheel set into a stow pin assembly. This setup is designed to prevent lateral movement along the rails. During hurricanes and other emergency shut down situations, tie down assemblies are used. Two (2) angled arms are anchored at each end of each set of wheels. This setup prevents longitudinal movement along the rails as well as prevents tipping of the crane due to uplift from high velocity winds.</dd>
</dl>
<p><br />
Ship-to-shore gantry cranes are often used in pairs or teams of cranes in order to minimize the time required to load and unload vessels. As container ship sizes and widths have increased throughout the 20th Century, ship-to-shore gantry cranes and the implementation of ship-to-shore gantry cranes has become more unique in order to effectively load and unload vessels while maximizing profitability and minimizing time in port. One example are systems where specialized berths built that accommodate one vessel at a time with ship-to-shore gantry cranes on both sides of the vessel. This allows for more cranes and double the workspace under the cranes to be used for transporting cargo off dock.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first quayside container gantry crane was developed in 1959 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paceco_Inc&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paceco Inc (page does not exist)">Paceco Inc</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Paceco's name for their line of quayside cranes, "Portainer", has since become something of a <a href="/wiki/Genericised_trademark" class="mw-redirect" title="Genericised trademark">genericised trademark</a>, used to refer to any quayside container gantry crane.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_Gantry_Crane">Full Gantry Crane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gantry_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Full Gantry Crane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG/220px-TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG/330px-TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG/440px-TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TAISUN_with_SCARABEO_9.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Taisun" title="Taisun">Taisun</a>, the world's strongest gantry crane, at <a href="/wiki/Yantai_Raffles_Shipyard" class="mw-redirect" title="Yantai Raffles Shipyard">Yantai Raffles Shipyard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yantai" title="Yantai">Yantai</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></div>

<p>Full gantry cranes (where the load remains beneath the gantry structure, supported from a beam) are well suited to lifting massive objects such as ships' engines, as the entire structure can resist the <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> created by the load, and <a href="/wiki/Counterweight" title="Counterweight">counterweights</a> are generally not required. These are often found in shipyards where they are used to move large ship components together for construction. They use a complex system of cables and attachments to support the massive loads undertaken by the full gantry cranes.</p>
<p>Some full gantry cranes of note are Samson and Goliath and Taisun. <a href="/wiki/Samson_and_Goliath_(cranes)" title="Samson and Goliath (cranes)">Samson and Goliath</a> are two full gantry cranes located in the <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">Harland and Wolff</a> <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> have spans of 140 metres and can lift loads of up to 840 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> to a height of 70 metres. In 2008, the world's strongest gantry crane, <a href="/wiki/Taisun" title="Taisun">Taisun</a>, which can lift 20,000 metric tons, was installed in <a href="/wiki/Yantai" title="Yantai">Yantai</a>, China at the <a href="/wiki/Yantai_Raffles_Shipyard" class="mw-redirect" title="Yantai Raffles Shipyard">Yantai Raffles Shipyard</a>. In 2012, a 22,000-ton capacity crane, the "Honghai Crane" was planned for construction in <a href="/wiki/Qidong_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Qidong City">Qidong City</a>, China.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Smaller gantry cranes are also available running on rubber tyres so that tracks are not needed. Rubber tyred gantry cranes are essential for moving containers from berths throughout the rest of the yard. For this task they come in large sizes, as pictured to the right, that are used for moving to straddle multiple lanes of rail, road, or container storage. They also are capable of lifting fully loaded containers to great heights. Smaller rubber tyred gantry cranes come in the form of <a href="/wiki/Straddle_carrier" title="Straddle carrier">straddle carriers</a> which are used when moving individual containers or vertical stacks of containers.</p>
<p>Portable gantry crane systems, such as rubber tyred gantry cranes, are in high demand in terminals and ports restricted in size and reliant on maximizing vertical space and not needing to haul containers long distances. This is due to the relatively slow speed yet high reach of rubber tyred gantry cranes when compared to other forms of container terminal equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Workstation_Gantry_Crane">Workstation Gantry Crane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gantry_crane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Workstation Gantry Crane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Workstation gantry cranes are used to lift and transport smaller items around a working area in a factory or machine shop. Some workstation gantry cranes are equipped with an enclosed track, while others use an I-beam, or other extruded shapes, for the running surface. Most workstation gantry cranes are intended to be stationary when loaded, and mobile when unloaded. Workstation Gantry Cranes can be outfitted with either a wire rope hoist or a lower capacity chain hoist.</p>
