<p><b>Consumption</b> is major concept in economics and is also studied by many other social sciences. Economists are particularly interested in the relationship between consumption and income, as modeled with the <i>consumption function</i>.</p>
<p>Different schools of economists define <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> and consumption differently. According to <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economists</a>, only the final purchase of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> by individuals constitutes consumption, while other types of expenditure — in particular, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_investment" title="Fixed investment">fixed investment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_consumption" title="Intermediate consumption">intermediate consumption</a>, and government spending — are placed in separate categories (See <a href="/wiki/Consumer_choice" title="Consumer choice">consumer choice</a>). Other economists define consumption much more broadly, as the aggregate of all economic activity that does not entail the design, production and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> (e.g. the selection, adoption, use, disposal and recycling of goods and services).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_function">Consumption function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Consumption function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumption_function" title="Consumption function">Consumption function</a></div>
<p>The consumption function is a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathematical function">mathematical function</a> that expresses <a href="/wiki/Consumer_spending" title="Consumer spending">consumer spending</a> in terms of its determinants, such as <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> and accumulated <a href="/wiki/Wealth#Economic_analysis" title="Wealth">wealth</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioural_economics_and_consumption">Behavioural economics and consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behavioural economics and consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Keynesian <a href="/wiki/Consumption_function" title="Consumption function">consumption function</a> is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_income_hypothesis" title="Absolute income hypothesis">absolute income hypothesis</a>, as it only bases consumption on current income and ignores potential future income (or lack of). Criticism of this assumption led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Permanent_income_hypothesis" title="Permanent income hypothesis">permanent income hypothesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franco_Modigliani" title="Franco Modigliani">Franco Modigliani</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle hypothesis">life cycle hypothesis</a>. More recent theoretical approaches are based on <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_economics" title="Behavioral economics">behavioral economics</a> and suggest that a number of behavioural principles can be taken as microeconomic foundations for a behaviourally-based aggregate consumption function.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_and_household_production">Consumption and household production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consumption and household production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Consumption is defined in part by comparison to <a href="/wiki/Production_theory_basics" class="mw-redirect" title="Production theory basics">production</a>. In the tradition of the Columbia School of Household Economics, also known as the <a href="/wiki/New_Home_Economics" title="New Home Economics">New Home Economics</a>, commercial consumption has to be analyzed in the context of household production. The opportunity cost of time affects the cost of home-produced substitutes and therefore demand for commercial goods and services.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The elasticity of demand for consumption goods is also a function of who performs chores in households and how their spouses compensate them for opportunity costs of home production.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different schools of economists define <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> and consumption differently. According to <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economists</a>, only the final purchase of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> by individuals constitutes consumption, while other types of expenditure — in particular, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_investment" title="Fixed investment">fixed investment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_consumption" title="Intermediate consumption">intermediate consumption</a>, and government spending — are placed in separate categories (See <a href="/wiki/Consumer_choice" title="Consumer choice">consumer choice</a>). Other economists define consumption much more broadly, as the aggregate of all economic activity that does not entail the design, production and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> (e.g. the selection, adoption, use, disposal and recycling of goods and services).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Consumption can also be measured by a variety of different ways such as <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">energy economics</a> metrics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_consumption">Effects of consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects of consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aggregate consumption is a component of <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to the UN, "today’s consumption is undermining the environmental resource base. It is exacerbating <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">inequalities</a>. And the dynamics of the consumption-<a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>-inequality-environment nexus are accelerating. If the trends continue without change — not redistributing from <a href="/wiki/High-income" class="mw-redirect" title="High-income">high-income</a> to <a href="/wiki/Low-income" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-income">low-income</a> consumers, not shifting from <a href="/wiki/Polluting" class="mw-redirect" title="Polluting">polluting</a> to cleaner goods and production technologies, not shifting priority from consumption for conspicuous display to meeting <a href="/wiki/Basic_needs" title="Basic needs">basic needs</a> — today’s problems of consumption and human development will worsen." Developing countries like India, as they move down the path of copying the consumption patterns of developed economies, will basically create demands that earth will not be able to fulfill. Some economists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> talk about putting a price on <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">using earth's resources</a> which is in addition to the cost of just extracting them.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old-age_spending">Old-age spending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Old-age spending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Spending the Kids' Inheritance</i> (originally the title of a book on the subject by <a href="/wiki/Annie_Hulley" title="Annie Hulley">Annie Hulley</a>) and the acronyms SKI and SKI'ing refer to the growing number of <a href="/wiki/Retire" class="mw-redirect" title="Retire">older people</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western society</a> spending their <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> on <a href="/wiki/Travel" title="Travel">travel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cars">cars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, in contrast to previous <a href="/wiki/Generations" class="mw-redirect" title="Generations">generations</a> who tended to leave that money to their <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>.</p>
<p><i>Die Broke</i> (from the book <i>Die Broke: A Radical Four-Part Financial Plan</i> by Stephen Pollan and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Levine_(economist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark Levine (economist) (page does not exist)">Mark Levine</a>) is a similar idea.</p>
