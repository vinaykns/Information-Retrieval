			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ethanol fermentation</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/330px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png" width="330" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/495px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/660px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="344" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In ethanol fermentation, (1) one glucose molecule breaks down into two pyruvates. The energy from this exothermic reaction is used to bind inorganic phosphates to ADP and convert NAD+ to NADH. (2) The two pyruvates are then broken down into two acetaldehydes and give off two CO2 as a waste product. (3) The two acetaldehydes are then converted to two ethanol by using the H- ions from NADH, converting NADH back into NAD+.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Ethanol fermentation/fermentation</b>, also called <b>alcoholic fermentation</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Biological_process" title="Biological process">biological process</a> which converts <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> into <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">cellular energy</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a side-effect. Because <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeasts</a> perform this conversion in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, alcoholic <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> is considered an <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism#Metabolism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> process. It also takes place in some species of fish (including <a href="/wiki/Goldfish" title="Goldfish">goldfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>) where (along with lactic acid fermentation) it provides energy when oxygen is scarce.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ethanol fermentation has many uses, including the production of <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>, the production of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> baking.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemical_process_of_fermentation_of_sucrose">Biochemical process of fermentation of sucrose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biochemical process of fermentation of sucrose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%A6ring.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/G%C3%A6ring.png/170px-G%C3%A6ring.png" width="170" height="340" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/G%C3%A6ring.png/255px-G%C3%A6ring.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/G%C3%A6ring.png/340px-G%C3%A6ring.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="3000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%A6ring.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A laboratory vessel being used for the fermentation of <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equation" title="Chemical equation">chemical equations</a> below summarize the fermentation of sucrose (C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub>) into ethanol (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH). Alcoholic fermentation converts one <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> into two moles of ethanol and two moles of carbon dioxide, producing two moles of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> in the process.</p>
<p>The overall chemical formula for alcoholic fermentation is:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> → 2 C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + 2 CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Sucrose is a <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a> of glucose and fructose molecules. In the first step of alcoholic fermentation, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Invertase" title="Invertase">invertase</a> cleaves the <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">glycosidic linkage</a> between the glucose and fructose molecules.</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + invertase → 2 C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Next, each glucose molecule is broken down into two <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> molecules in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stryer_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-2">[2]</a></sup> Glycolysis is summarized by the equation:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 2 ADP + 2 P<sub>i</sub> + 2 NAD<sup>+</sup> → 2 CH<sub>3</sub>COCOO<sup>−</sup> + 2 ATP + 2 NADH + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + 2 H<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The chemical formula of pyruvate is CH<sub>3</sub>COCOO<sup>−</sup>. P<sub>i</sub> stands for the inorganic <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>.</p>
<p>Finally, pyruvate is converted to ethanol and CO<sub>2</sub> in two steps, regenerating oxidized NAD+ needed for glycolysis:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>1. CH<sub>3</sub>COCOO<sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> → CH<sub>3</sub>CHO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_decarboxylase" title="Pyruvate decarboxylase">pyruvate decarboxylase</a></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>2. CH<sub>3</sub>CHO + NADH+H<sup>+</sup> → C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + NAD<sup>+</sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>This reaction is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Alcohol dehydrogenase">alcohol dehydrogenase</a> (ADH1 in baker's yeast).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As shown by the reaction equation, glycolysis causes the reduction of two molecules of <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NAD<sup>+</sup></a> to <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NADH</a>. Two <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> molecules are also converted to two ATP and two water molecules via <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_processes">Related processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">Fermentation (biochemistry)</a></div>
<p>Fermentation of sugar to ethanol and CO<sub>2</sub> can also be done by <a href="/wiki/Zymomonas_mobilis" title="Zymomonas mobilis">Zymomonas mobilis</a>, however the path is slightly different since formation of pyruvate does not happen by glycolysis but instead by the <a href="/wiki/Entner%E2%80%93Doudoroff_pathway" title="Entner–Doudoroff pathway">Entner–Doudoroff pathway</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> can produce ethanol from sugars by fermentation but often only as a side product. Examples are<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">Heterolactic acid fermentation</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Leuconostoc" title="Leuconostoc">Leuconostoc</a> bacterias produce Lactate + Ethanol + CO<sub>2</sub></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mixed_acid_fermentation" title="Mixed acid fermentation">Mixed acid fermentation</a> where <a href="/wiki/Escherichia" title="Escherichia">Escherichia</a> produce Ethanol mixed with Lactate, Acetate, Succinate, Formate, CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2,3-butanediol_fermentation" class="mw-redirect" title="2,3-butanediol fermentation">2,3-butanediol fermentation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Enterobacter" title="Enterobacter">Enterobacter</a> producing Ethanol, Butanediol, Lactate, Formate, CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_oxygen">Effect of oxygen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effect of oxygen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fermentation does not require oxygen. If oxygen is present, some species of yeast (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Kluyveromyces_lactis" title="Kluyveromyces lactis">Kluyveromyces lactis</a></i> or <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kluyveromyces_lipolytica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kluyveromyces lipolytica (page does not exist)">Kluyveromyces lipolytica</a></i>) will oxidize <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvate</a> completely to carbon dioxide and water in a process called <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, hence these species of yeast will produce ethanol only in an anaerobic environment (not cellular respiration).</p>
<p>However, many yeasts such as the commonly used baker's yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, or fission yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces_pombe" title="Schizosaccharomyces pombe">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</a></i>, prefer fermentation to respiration. These yeasts will produce ethanol even under <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic</a> conditions, if they are provided with the right kind of nutrition. During batch fermentation, the rate of ethanol production per milligram of cell protein is maximal for a brief period early in this process and declines progressively as ethanol accumulates in the surrounding broth. Studies demonstrate that the removal of this accumulated ethanol does not immediately restore fermentative activity, and they provide evidence that the decline in metabolic rate is due to physiological changes (including possible ethanol damage) rather than to the presence of ethanol. Several potential causes for the decline in fermentative activity have been investigated. Viability remained at or above 90%, internal pH remained near neutrality, and the specific activities of the glycolytic and alcohologenic enzymes (measured in vitro) remained high throughout batch fermentation. None of these factors appears to be causally related to the fall in fermentative activity during batch fermentation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bread_baking">Bread baking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bread baking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ethanol fermentation causes bread dough to rise. Yeast organisms consume sugars in the dough and produce ethanol and carbon dioxide as waste products. The carbon dioxide forms bubbles in the dough, expanding it into something of a foam. Less than 2% ethanol remains after baking. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcoholic_beverages">Alcoholic beverages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alcoholic beverages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Primary_fermentation_cellar,_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg/170px-Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg" width="170" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg/255px-Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg/340px-Primary_fermentation_cellar%2C_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1530" data-file-height="1866" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Primary_fermentation_cellar,_Budweiser_Brewery.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Primary fermentation cellar, Budweiser Brewery, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Collins,_Colorado" title="Fort Collins, Colorado">Fort Collins, Colorado</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>All ethanol contained in <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> (including ethanol produced by <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_maceration" title="Carbonic maceration">carbonic maceration</a>) is produced by means of fermentation induced by yeast.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">Wine</a> is produced by fermentation of the natural sugars present in grapes; <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">cider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perry" title="Perry">perry</a> are produced by similar fermentation of natural sugar in <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pears</a>, respectively; and other <a href="/wiki/Fruit_wine" title="Fruit wine">fruit wines</a> are produced from the fermentation of the sugars in any other kinds of fruit. <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">Brandy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eau_de_vie" title="Eau de vie">eaux de vie</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Slivovitz" title="Slivovitz">slivovitz</a>) are produced by <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of these fruit-fermented beverages.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">Mead</a> is produced by fermentation of the natural sugars present in <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">Beer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whiskey" class="mw-redirect" title="Whiskey">whiskey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">vodka</a> are produced by fermentation of grain starches that have been converted to sugar by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, which is present in grain kernels that have been <a href="/wiki/Malting" class="mw-redirect" title="Malting">malted</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinated</a>). Other sources of starch (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> and unmalted grain) may be added to the mixture, as the amylase will act on those starches as well. Whiskey and vodka are also distilled; <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a> and related beverages are produced by the addition of flavoring agents to a vodka-like feedstock during distillation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rice_wine" title="Rice wine">Rice wines</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a>) are produced by the fermentation of grain starches converted to sugar by the mold <i><a href="/wiki/Aspergillus_oryzae" title="Aspergillus oryzae">Aspergillus oryzae</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Baijiu" title="Baijiu">Baijiu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dch%C5%AB" title="Shōchū">shōchū</a></i> are distilled from the product of such fermentation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">Rum</a> and some other beverages are produced by fermentation and distillation of <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>. Rum is usually produced from the sugarcane product <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In all cases, fermentation must take place in a vessel that allows carbon dioxide to escape but prevents outside air from coming in. This is because exposure to oxygen would prevent the formation of ethanol, while a buildup of carbon dioxide creates a risk the vessel will rupture or fail catastrophically, causing injury and property damage.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedstocks_for_fuel_production">Feedstocks for fuel production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Feedstocks for fuel production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeast fermentation of various carbohydrate products is also used to produce the ethanol that is added to <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.</p>
<p>The dominant ethanol feedstock in warmer regions is <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usda1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda1-7">[7]</a></sup> In temperate regions, <a href="/wiki/Corn_ethanol" title="Corn ethanol">corn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a> are used.<sup id="cite_ref-usda1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usda2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda2-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the main feedstock for the production of ethanol is currently corn.<sup id="cite_ref-usda1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda1-7">[7]</a></sup> Approximately 2.8 gallons of ethanol are produced from one bushel of corn (0.42 liter per kilogram). While much of the corn turns into ethanol, some of the corn also yields by-products such as <a href="/wiki/Distillers_grains" title="Distillers grains">DDGS</a> (distillers dried grains with solubles) that can be used as feed for livestock. A bushel of corn produces about 18&#160;pounds of DDGS (320 kilograms of DDGS per metric ton of maize).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Although most of the fermentation plants have been built in corn-producing regions, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> is also an important feedstock for ethanol production in the Plains states. <a href="/wiki/Pearl_millet" title="Pearl millet">Pearl millet</a> is showing promise as an ethanol feedstock for the southeastern U.S. and the potential of <a href="/wiki/Duckweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Duckweed">duckweed</a> is being studied.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In some parts of Europe, particularly France and Italy, <a href="/wiki/Grapes" class="mw-redirect" title="Grapes">grapes</a> have become a <i>de facto</i> feedstock for fuel ethanol by the <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of surplus <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In Japan, it has been proposed to use rice normally made into <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a> as an ethanol source.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cassava_as_ethanol_feedstock">Cassava as ethanol feedstock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethanol_fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cassava as ethanol feedstock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ethanol can be made from <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a> or from sugars or starches. Starches are cheapest. The starchy crop with highest energy content per acre is <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>, which grows in tropical countries.</p>
<p>Thailand already had a large cassava industry in the 1990s, for use as cattle feed and as a cheap admixture to wheat flour. Nigeria and Ghana are already establishing cassava-to-ethanol plants. Production of ethanol from cassava is currently economically feasible when crude oil prices are above US$120 per barrel.</p>
<p>New varieties of cassava are being developed, so the future situation remains uncertain. Currently, cassava can yield between 25-40 tonnes per hectare (with irrigation and fertilizer),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and from a tonne of cassava roots, circa 200 liters of ethanol can be produced (assuming cassava with 22% starch content). A liter of ethanol contains circa 21.46<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Megajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Megajoule">MJ</a> of energy. The overall energy efficiency of cassava-root to ethanol conversion is circa 32%.</p>
<p>The yeast used for processing cassava is <i>Endomycopsis fibuligera</i>, sometimes used together with bacterium <i>Zymomonas mobilis</i>.</p>
