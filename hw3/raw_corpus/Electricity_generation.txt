<p><b>Electricity generation</b> is the process of generating <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> from sources of <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">primary energy</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric utilities</a>, it is the first process in the delivery of electricity to consumers. The other processes, electricity <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">distribution</a>, and electrical <a href="/wiki/Power_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Power storage">power storage</a> and recovery using <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage</a> methods are normally carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_industry" title="Electric power industry">electric power industry</a>. Electricity is most often generated at a <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a> by <a href="/wiki/Electromechanical" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromechanical">electromechanical</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">generators</a>, primarily driven by <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a> fuelled by <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> but also by other means such as the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of flowing water and wind. Other energy sources include solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fundamental principles of electricity generation were discovered during the 1820s and early 1830s by the British scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>. This method is still used today: electricity is generated by the movement of a loop of wire, or <a href="/wiki/Faraday_disc" class="mw-redirect" title="Faraday disc">disc of copper</a> between the poles of a <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a>. Central power stations became economically practical with the development of <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> power transmission, using power <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a> to transmit power at high voltage and with low loss. Electricity has been generated at central stations since 1882. The first power plants were run on water power or coal,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and today rely mainly on <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind generator">wind generators</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, with supplementary amounts from <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a> sources. The use of power-lines and power-poles have been significantly important in the distribution of electricity.</p>

<p>There are seven fundamental methods of directly transforming other forms of energy into electrical energy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_electricity">Static electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Static electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">Static electricity</a>, form the physical separation and transport of charge (examples: <a href="/wiki/Triboelectric_effect" title="Triboelectric effect">triboelectric effect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>). It was the first form discovered and investigated, and the <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_generator" title="Electrostatic generator">electrostatic generator</a> is still used even in modern devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Van_de_Graaff_generator" title="Van de Graaff generator">Van de Graaff generator</a> and <a href="/wiki/MHD_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="MHD generator">MHD generators</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetic_induction">Electromagnetic induction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electromagnetic induction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">Electromagnetic induction</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a> transforms <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> into electricity. This is the most used form for generating electricity and is based on <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">Faraday's law</a>. It can be experimented by rotating a magnet within closed loops of a conducting material (e.g. copper wire). Almost all commercial electrical generation is done using electromagnetic induction, in which <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> forces a generator to rotate.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turbines">Turbines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Turbines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almost all electrical power on Earth is generated with a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, driven by wind, water, steam or burning gas. The turbine drives a generator. There are many different methods of developing mechanical energy, including <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a>, hydro, wind and tidal power. Most electric generation is driven by <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a>. The combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> supplies most of the heat to these engines, with a significant fraction from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> and some from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable source">renewable sources</a>. The modern <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> (invented by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Algernon_Parsons" title="Charles Algernon Parsons">Sir Charles Parsons</a> in 1884) currently generates about 80% of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> in the world using a variety of heat sources. Power sources include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Steam

Large dams such as <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> in China can provide large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> power; it has a 22.5 <a href="/wiki/Watt#Gigawatt" title="Watt">GW</a> capability.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>Water is boiled by <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> burned in a <a href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil-fuel power station">thermal power plant</a>, about 41% of all electricity is generated this way.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">Nuclear fission</a> heat created in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> creates steam. Less than 15% of electricity is generated this way.</li>
<li>Renewables. The steam is generated by <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">Solar thermal energy</a> where <a href="/wiki/Solar_parabolic_trough" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar parabolic trough">solar parabolic troughs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar power towers</a> concentrate sunlight to heat a heat transfer fluid, which is then used to produce steam, or <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">Geothermal power</a>.</li>
<li>Natural gas: turbines are driven directly by gases produced by combustion. <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">Combined cycle</a> are driven by both steam and natural gas. They generate power by burning natural gas in a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> and use residual heat to generate steam. At least 20% of the worlds electricity is generated by natural gas.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Small turbines can be powered by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">Diesel engines</a>. This is used for back up generation, usually at low voltages. Most large power grids also use <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">diesel generators</a>, originally provided as emergency back up for a specific facility such as a hospital, to feed power into the grid during certain circumstances.</li>
<li>Water Energy is captured from the movement of water. From falling water, the rise and fall of tides or <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">ocean thermal currents</a>. Each driving a <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbine</a> to produce approximately 16% of the world's electricity. The <a href="/wiki/CETO#Perth_Wave_Energy_Project" title="CETO">Perth Wave Energy Project</a> is an early production, submerged buoy, electrical power and direct desalination installation supplying power to <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Stirling" title="HMAS Stirling">HMAS Stirling</a> in Western Australia.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a> was a very early <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbine</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Solar_updraft_tower" title="Solar updraft tower">solar updraft tower</a> wind is artificially produced. Before 2010 less than 2% of the worlds electricity was produced from wind.</li>
</ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">Electrochemistry</a> is the direct transformation of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> into electricity, as in a <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a>. Electrochemical electricity generation is important in portable and mobile applications. Currently, most electrochemical power comes from batteries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Primary_cell" title="Primary cell">Primary cells</a>, such as the common <a href="/wiki/Zinc-carbon_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Zinc-carbon batteries">zinc-carbon batteries</a>, act as power sources directly, but many types of cells are used as <a href="/wiki/Electricity_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity storage">storage</a> systems rather than primary generation systems. Open electrochemical systems, known as <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>, can be used to extract power either from natural fuels or from synthesized fuels. <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_power" title="Osmotic power">Osmotic power</a> is a possibility at places where salt and fresh water merges.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photovoltaic_effect">Photovoltaic effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Photovoltaic effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect" title="Photovoltaic effect">photovoltaic effect</a> is the transformation of light into electrical energy, as in <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>. <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">Photovoltaic panels</a> convert sunlight directly to electricity. Although sunlight is free and abundant, solar electricity is still usually more expensive to produce than large-scale mechanically generated power due to the cost of the panels. Low-efficiency silicon solar cells have been decreasing in cost and multijunction cells with close to 30% conversion efficiency are now commercially available. Over 40% efficiency has been demonstrated in experimental systems.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Until recently, photovoltaics were most commonly used in remote sites where there is no access to a commercial power grid, or as a supplemental electricity source for individual homes and businesses. Recent advances in manufacturing efficiency and photovoltaic technology, combined with subsidies driven by environmental concerns, have dramatically accelerated the deployment of solar panels. Installed capacity is growing by 40% per year led by increases in Germany, Japan, and the United States.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect" title="Thermoelectric effect">Thermoelectric effect</a> is the direct conversion of temperature differences to electricity, as in <a href="/wiki/Thermocouple" title="Thermocouple">thermocouples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermopile" title="Thermopile">thermopiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thermionic_converter" title="Thermionic converter">thermionic converters</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Piezoelectric_effect">Piezoelectric effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Piezoelectric effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Piezoelectric effect">Piezoelectric effect</a> generates electricity from the mechanical strain of electrically <a href="/wiki/Anisotropic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anisotropic">anisotropic</a> molecules or crystals. Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a> have developed a <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Piezoelectric">piezoelectric</a> generator sufficient to operate a <a href="/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display" title="Liquid-crystal display">liquid-crystal display</a> using thin films of M13 bacteriophage.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Piezoelectric">Piezoelectric</a> devices are used for power generation from mechanical strain, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Power_harvesting" class="mw-redirect" title="Power harvesting">power harvesting</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_transformation">Nuclear transformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nuclear transformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nuclear transformation is the creation and acceleration of charged particles (examples: <a href="/wiki/Betavoltaics" class="mw-redirect" title="Betavoltaics">betavoltaics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particle</a> emission). The direct conversion of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_potential_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear potential energy">nuclear potential energy</a> to electricity by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> is used only on a small scale. In a full-size nuclear power plant, the heat of a nuclear reaction is used to run a heat engine. This drives a generator, which converts mechanical energy into electricity by magnetic induction. <a href="/wiki/Betavoltaics" class="mw-redirect" title="Betavoltaics">Betavoltaics</a> are a type of solid-state power generator which produces electricity from radioactive decay. Fluid-based <a href="/wiki/MHD_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="MHD generator">magnetohydrodynamic</a> (MHD) power generation has been studied as a method for extracting electrical power from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> and also from more conventional fuel combustion systems.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics_of_generation_and_production_of_electricity">Economics of generation and production of electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economics of generation and production of electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_electricity_by_source" title="Cost of electricity by source">Cost of electricity by source</a></div>
<p>The selection of electricity production modes and their economic viability varies in accordance with demand and region. The economics vary considerably around the world, resulting in widespread selling prices, e.g. the price in Venezuela is 3 cents per kWh while in Denmark it is 40 cents per kWh. <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric plant">Hydroelectric plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal power plant">thermal power plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable source">renewable sources</a> have their own pros and cons, and selection is based upon the local power requirement and the fluctuations in demand. All power grids have varying loads on them but the daily minimum is the base load, supplied by plants which run continuously. Nuclear, coal, oil and gas plants can supply base load.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">Thermal energy</a> is economical in areas of high industrial density, as the high demand cannot be met by renewable sources. The effect of localized pollution is also minimized as industries are usually located away from residential areas. These plants can also withstand variation in load and consumption by adding more units or temporarily decreasing the production of some units. Nuclear power plants can produce a huge amount of power from a single unit. However, recent disasters in Japan have raised concerns over the safety of nuclear power, and the capital cost of nuclear plants is very high. Hydroelectric power plants are located in areas where the potential energy from falling water can be harnessed for moving turbines and the generation of power. It is not an economically viable source of production where the load varies too much during the annual production cycle and the ability to store the flow of water is limited.</p>
<p>Due to advancements in technology, and with mass production, renewable sources other than hydroelectricity (solar power, wind energy, tidal power, etc.) experienced decreases in cost of production, and the energy is now in many cases cost-comparative with fossil fuels. Many governments around the world provide subsidies to offset the higher cost of any new power production, and to make the installation of renewable energy systems economically feasible. However, their use is frequently limited by their <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">intermittent nature</a>. If natural gas prices are below $3 per million <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">British thermal units</a>, generating electricity from natural gas is cheaper than generating power by burning coal.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electricity_generation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_electricity_generation" title="Environmental impact of electricity generation">Environmental impact of electricity generation</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal_phase_out" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal phase out">Coal phase out</a></div>
<p>Variations between countries generating electrical power affect concerns about the environment. In France only 10% of electricity is generated from fossil fuels, the US is higher at 70% and China is at 80%.<sup id="cite_ref-Statistics_and_Balances_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Statistics_and_Balances-12">[12]</a></sup> The cleanliness of electricity depends on its source. Most scientists agree that emissions of pollutants and greenhouse gases from fossil fuel-based electricity generation account for a significant portion of world greenhouse gas emissions; in the United States, electricity generation accounts for nearly 40% of emissions, the largest of any source. Transportation emissions are close behind, contributing about one-third of U.S. production of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the United States, fossil fuel combustion for electric power generation is responsible for 65% of all emissions of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, the main component of acid rain.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Electricity generation is the fourth highest combined source of <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In July 2011, the UK parliament tabled a motion that "levels of (carbon) emissions from nuclear power were approximately three times lower per <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hour</a> than those of solar, four times lower than clean coal and 36 times lower than conventional coal".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
