			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Grid plan</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>grid plan</b>, <b>grid street plan</b> or <b>gridiron plan</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> plan in which <a href="/wiki/Street" title="Street">streets</a> run at <a href="/wiki/Angle#Types_of_angles" title="Angle">right angles</a> to each other, forming a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/grid" class="extiw" title="wikt:grid">grid</a>. In the context of the culture of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, the grid plan method of land measurement was called <a href="/wiki/Centuriation" title="Centuriation">centuriation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_grid_plans">Ancient grid plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient grid plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The grid plan dates from antiquity and originated in multiple cultures; some of the earliest planned cities were built using grid plans.</p>
<p>By 2600 BC, <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harappa" title="Harappa">Harappa</a>, major cities of the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley Civilization">Indus Valley Civilization</a> (in what is now Pakistan and Northwestern India), were built with blocks divided by a grid of straight streets, running north-south and east-west. Each block was subdivided by small lanes. The cities and monasteries of <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhara</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sirkap" title="Sirkap">Sirkap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taxila" title="Taxila">Taxila</a>), dating from the 1st millennium BC to the 11th century AD, also had grid-based designs. <a href="/wiki/Islamabad" title="Islamabad">Islamabad</a>, the capital of Pakistan since 1959, was also founded on the grid-plan of the nearby ruined city of Sirkap.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A workers' village (2570-2500 BC) at <a href="/wiki/Giza" title="Giza">Giza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, housed a rotating labor force and was laid out in blocks of long galleries separated by streets in a formal grid. Many pyramid-cult cities used a common orientation: a north-south axis from the royal palace and an east-west axis from the temple, meeting at a central plaza where King and God merged and crossed.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hammurabi" title="Hammurabi">Hammurabi</a> (17th century BC) was a king of the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonian Empire</a> who made <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a> one of the greatest cities in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">antiquity</a>. He rebuilt Babylon, building and restoring temples, city walls and public buildings, and building canals for irrigation. The streets of Babylon were wide and straight, intersected approximately at right angles, and were paved with bricks and <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a>.</p>
<p>The tradition of grid plans is continuous in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> from the 15th century BC onward in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_urban_planning" title="Ancient Chinese urban planning">traditional urban planning</a> of various ancient Chinese states. Guidelines put into written form in the <a href="/wiki/Kaogongji" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaogongji">Kaogongji</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (770-476 BC) stated: "a capital city should be square on plan. Three gates on each side of the perimeter lead into the nine main streets that crisscross the city and define its grid-pattern. And for its layout the city should have the Royal Court situated in the south, the Marketplace in the north, the Imperial Ancestral Temple in the east and the Altar to the Gods of Land and Grain in the west."</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a>, near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, is the largest ancient grid-plan site in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. The city's grid covered eight square miles.</p>
<p>Perhaps the most well-known grid system is that spread through the colonies of the Roman Empire. The archetypal <a href="/wiki/Roman_centuriation" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman centuriation">Roman Grid</a> was introduced to Italy first by the Greeks, with such information transferred by way of trade and conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanislawski_116_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanislawski_116-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_ancient_Greek_grid">The ancient Greek grid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The ancient Greek grid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the idea of the grid was present in Hellenic societal and city planning, it was not pervasive prior to the 5th century BC. However, it slowly gained primacy through the work of <a href="/wiki/Hippodamus_of_Miletus" title="Hippodamus of Miletus">Hippodamus of Miletus</a>, who planned and replanned many Greek cities in accordance with this form.<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_39_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns_39-3">[3]</a></sup> The concept of a grid as the ideal method of town planning had become widely accepted by the time of Alexander the Great. His conquests were a step in the propagation of the grid plan throughout colonies, some as far-flung as Taxila in Pakistan,<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_39_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns_39-3">[3]</a></sup> that would later be mirrored by the expansion of the Roman Empire. The Greek grid had its streets aligned roughly in relation to the cardinal points<sup id="cite_ref-Burns_39_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burns_39-3">[3]</a></sup> and generally looked to take advantage of visual cues based on the hilly landscape typical of Greece and Asia Minor.<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60-4">[4]</a></sup> This was probably best exemplified in <a href="/wiki/Priene" title="Priene">Priene</a>, in present-day western Turkey, where the orthogonal city grid was based on the cardinal points, on sloping terrain that struck views out<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> towards a river and the city of <a href="/wiki/Miletus" title="Miletus">Miletus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Roman_civilization">Ancient Roman civilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Roman civilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Etruscan people, whose territories in Italy encompassed what would eventually become Rome,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> founded what is now the city of Marzabotto at the end of the 6th century BC. It was based on Greek Ionic ideas, and it was here that the main east-west and north-south axes of a town (the <i>decumanus maximus</i> and <i>cardo maximus</i> respectively) could first be seen in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanislawski_116_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanislawski_116-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Stanislawski,<sup id="cite_ref-Stanislawski_116_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanislawski_116-2">[2]</a></sup> there is little evidence that the Romans adopted the Etruscan model at Marzabatto early in their expansion. Instead, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_centuriation" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman centuriation">Roman Grid</a> was spread around the Mediterranean and into northern Europe later on, during the late Republic and early Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanislawski_116_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanislawski_116-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spirano-Stezzano.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Spirano-Stezzano.jpg/220px-Spirano-Stezzano.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Spirano-Stezzano.jpg/330px-Spirano-Stezzano.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Spirano-Stezzano.jpg/440px-Spirano-Stezzano.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spirano-Stezzano.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Straight road in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Bergamo" title="Province of Bergamo">Province of Bergamo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, following line of <a href="/wiki/Roman_centuriation" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman centuriation">Roman Grid</a></div>
</div>

<p>The military expansion of this period facilitated the adoption of the grid form as standard: the Romans established <i><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castra</a></i> (forts or camps) first as military centres; some of them developed into administrative hubs. The Roman grid was similar in form to the Greek version of a grid, but allowed for practical considerations. For example, Roman <i>castra</i> were often sited on flat land, especially close to or on important nodes like river crossings or intersections of trade routes.<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60-4">[4]</a></sup> The dimensions of the <i>castra</i> were often standard, with each of its four walls generally having a length of 2,150 feet (660 metres). Familiarity was the aim of such standardisation: soldiers could be stationed anywhere around the Empire, and orientation would be easy within established towns if they had a standard layout. Each would have the aforementioned <i><a href="/wiki/Decumanus_maximus" class="mw-redirect" title="Decumanus maximus">decumanus maximus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cardo_maximus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardo maximus">cardo maximus</a></i> at its heart, and their intersection would form the forum, around which would be sited important public buildings. Indeed, such was the degree of similarity between towns that Higgins states that soldiers "would be housed at the same address as they moved from <i>castra</i> to <i>castra</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60-4">[4]</a></sup> Pompeii has been cited by both Higgins<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins.2C_Hannah_2009_p._60-4">[4]</a></sup> and Laurence<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> as the best preserved example of the Roman grid.</p>
<p>Outside of the castra, large tracts of land were also divided in accordance with the grid within the walls. These were typically 2,400 feet (730 metres) per side (called <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_units_of_measurement" title="Ancient Roman units of measurement"><i>centuria</i></a>), and contained 100 parcels of land (each called <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_units_of_measurement" title="Ancient Roman units of measurement"><i>heredium</i></a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Gelernter_15_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gelernter_15-8">[8]</a></sup> The <i>decumanus maximus</i> and <i>cardo maximus</i> extended from the town gates out towards neighbouring settlements. These were lined up to be as straight as possible, only deviating from their path due to natural obstacles that prevented a direct route.<sup id="cite_ref-Gelernter_15_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gelernter_15-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the imposition of only one town form regardless of region could be seen as an imposition of imperial authority, there is no doubting the practical reasoning behind the formation of the Roman grid. Under Roman guidance, the grid was designed for efficiency and interchangeability, both facilitated by and aiding the expansion of their empire.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_from_the_first_millennium_AD">Asia from the first millennium AD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Asia from the first millennium AD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a> became politically centralized in the 7th century AD, those societies adopted Chinese grid-planning principles in numerous locations. In Korea, <a href="/wiki/Gyeongju" title="Gyeongju">Gyeongju</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Unified_Silla" class="mw-redirect" title="Unified Silla">Unified Silla</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sanggyeong" title="Sanggyeong">Sanggyeong</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Balhae" title="Balhae">Balhae</a>, adapted the <a href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a> Chinese model. The ancient capitals of Japan, such as <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara-kyo" class="mw-redirect" title="Fujiwara-kyo">Fujiwara-Kyô</a> (AD 694-710), <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a> (Heijô-Kyô, AD 710-784), and <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> (Heian-Kyô, AD 794-1868) also adapted from Tang's capital, <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang'an">Chang'an</a>. However, for reasons of defense, the planners of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> eschewed the grid, opting instead for an irregular network of streets surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Edo_Castle" title="Edo Castle">Edo Castle</a> grounds. In later periods, some parts of Tokyo were grid-planned, but grid plans are generally rare in Japan, and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_addressing_system" title="Japanese addressing system">Japanese addressing system</a> is accordingly based on increasingly fine subdivisions, rather than a grid.</p>
<p>The grid-planning tradition in Asia continued through the beginning of the 20th century, with <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a>, Japan (est. 1868) following a grid plan under American influence.</p>

<p>New <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> towns were planned using grids beginning in the 12th century, most prodigiously in the <a href="/wiki/Bastides" class="mw-redirect" title="Bastides">bastides</a> of southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> that were built during the 13th and 14th centuries. Medieval European <a href="/wiki/New_town" class="mw-redirect" title="New town">new towns</a> using grid plans were widespread, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> region. Many were built on ancient grids originally established as Roman colonial outposts.</p>
<p>The Roman model was also used in Spanish settlements during the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a> of Ferdinand and Isabella. It was subsequently applied in the new cities established during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization of the Americas</a>, after the founding of <a href="/wiki/San_Crist%C3%B3bal_de_La_Laguna" title="San Cristóbal de La Laguna">San Cristóbal de La Laguna</a> (Canary Islands) in 1496. In 1573, King Phillip II of Spain compiled the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.arc.miami.edu/Law%20of%20Indies.html">Laws of the Indies</a> to guide the construction and administration of colonial communities. The Laws specified a square or rectangular central plaza with eight principal streets running from the plaza's corners. Hundreds of grid-plan communities throughout the Americas were established according to this pattern, echoing the practices of earlier Indian civilizations.</p>
<p>The grid plan became popular with the start of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> in Northern Europe. In 1606, the newly founded city of <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> was the first Renaissance city laid out on the grid plan. Later came the New Town in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and almost the entire city centre of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, and many <a href="/wiki/Planned_communities" class="mw-redirect" title="Planned communities">planned communities</a> and cities in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> such as <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> capital city of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valletta" title="Valletta">Valletta</a>, dating back to the 16th Century, was built following a rigid grid plan of uniformly designed houses, dotted with palaces, churches and squares.</p>

<p>Many of the earliest cities in the United States, such as <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, did not start with a grid system.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, even in pre-revolutionary days some cities saw the benefits of such a layout. <a href="/wiki/New_Haven_Colony" title="New Haven Colony">New Haven Colony</a>, one of the earliest colonies in America, was designed with a tiny 9-square grid at its founding in 1638. On a grander scale, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> was designed on a rectilinear street grid in 1682: one of the first cities in North America to use a grid system.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At the urging of city founder <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a>, surveyor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Holme" title="Thomas Holme">Thomas Holme</a> designed a system of wide streets intersecting at right angles between the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a> to the west and the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> to the east, including five squares of dedicated parkland. Penn advertised this orderly design as a safeguard against overcrowding, fire, and disease, which plagued European cities. Holme drafted an ideal version of the grid,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but alleyways sprouted within and between larger blocks as the city took shape. Arguably the most famous grid plan in history is the plan for <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> formulated in the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners%27_Plan_of_1811" title="Commissioners' Plan of 1811">Commissioners' Plan of 1811</a>, a visionary proposal by the state <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="New York (state)">New York</a> for the development of most of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> above <a href="/wiki/Houston_Street" title="Houston Street">Houston Street</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, was planned under <a href="/wiki/French-American" class="mw-redirect" title="French-American">French-American</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Charles_L%27Enfant" title="Pierre Charles L'Enfant">Pierre Charles L'Enfant</a>. Under the L'Enfant plan, the original <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> was developed using a grid plan that is interrupted by diagonal avenues, most famously <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Avenue_(Washington,_D.C.)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pennsylvania Avenue (Washington, D.C.)">Pennsylvania Avenue</a>. These diagonals are often connected by <a href="/wiki/Traffic_circle" title="Traffic circle">traffic circles</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Circle" title="Dupont Circle">Dupont Circle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Circle" title="Washington Circle">Washington Circle</a>. As the city grew, the plan was duplicated to cover most of the remainder of the capital. Meanwhile, the core of the city faced disarray and the <a href="/wiki/McMillan_Plan" title="McMillan Plan">McMillan Plan</a>, led by Senator <a href="/wiki/James_McMillan_(politician)" title="James McMillan (politician)">James McMillan</a>, was adopted to build a <a href="/wiki/National_Mall" title="National Mall">National Mall</a> and a parks system that is still today a jewel of the city.</p>
<p>Often, some of the streets in a grid are numbered (First, Second, etc.), lettered, or arranged in alphabetical order. Downtown San Diego uses all three schemes: north-south streets are numbered from west to east, and east-west streets are split between a lettered series running southward from A through L and a series of streets named after trees or plants, running northward alphabetically from Ash to Walnut. As in many cities, some of these streets have been given new names violating the system (the former D Street is now Broadway, the former 12th Avenue is now Park Boulevard, etc.); this has meant that 2nd, not 1st, is the most common street name in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>An exception to the typical, uniform grid is the plan of <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a> (1733), known as the <a href="/wiki/Oglethorpe_Plan" title="Oglethorpe Plan">Oglethorpe Plan</a>. It is a composite, cellular city block consisting of four large corner blocks, four small blocks in between and a public square in the centre; the entire composition of approximately ten acres (four hectares) is known as a ward.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Its cellular structure includes all the primary land uses of a neighborhood and has for that reason been called <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Its street configuration presages modern traffic calming techniques applied to uniform grids where certain selected streets become discontinuous or narrow, thus discouraging through traffic. The configuration also represents an example of functional <a href="/wiki/Shared_space" title="Shared space">shared space</a>, where pedestrian and vehicular traffic can safely and comfortably coexist.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the westward development of the United States, the use of the grid plan was nearly universal in the construction of new settlements, such as in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> (1870), <a href="/wiki/Dodge_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodge City">Dodge City</a> (1872) and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a> (1890). In these western cities the streets were numbered even more carefully than in the east to suggest future prosperity and metropolitan status.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the main advantages of the grid plan was that it allowed the rapid <a href="/wiki/Subdivision_(land)" title="Subdivision (land)">subdivision</a> and <a href="/wiki/Auction" title="Auction">auction</a> of a large parcel of land. For example, when the legislature of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Republic of Texas</a> decided in 1839 to move the capital to a new site along the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(Texas)" title="Colorado River (Texas)">Colorado River</a>, the functioning of the government required the rapid population of the town, which was named <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a>. Charged with the task, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Waller" title="Edwin Waller">Edwin Waller</a> designed a fourteen-block grid that fronted the river on 640 acres (exactly 1 square mile; about 2.6&#160;km²). After surveying the land, Waller organized the almost immediate sale of 306 lots, and by the end of the year the entire Texas government had arrived by <a href="/wiki/Cart" title="Cart">oxcart</a> at the new site. Apart from the speed of surveying advantage, the rationale at the time of the grid's adoption in this and other cities remains obscure.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_19th_century_to_the_present">Late 19th century to the present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late 19th century to the present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ildefonso_Cerd%C3%A1" class="mw-redirect" title="Ildefonso Cerdá">Ildefonso Cerdá</a>, a Spanish civil engineer, defined a concept of urban planning, based on the grid, that he applied to the <a href="/wiki/Eixample" title="Eixample">Eixample</a> of <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>. The Eixample grid introduced innovative design elements which were exceptional at the time and even unique among subsequent grid plans:</p>
<ul>
<li>a very large block measuring 113 by 113&#160;m (371 by 371&#160;ft), far larger than the old city blocks and larger than any Roman, Greek blocks and their mutations (see drawing below);</li>
<li>a 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft) road width (right of way) compared to mostly 3 m in the old city;</li>
<li>square blocks with truncated corners; and</li>
<li>major roads, perpendicular and diagonal, measuring 50&#160;m (160&#160;ft) in width.</li>
</ul>
<p>These innovations he based on functional grounds: the block size, to enable the creation of a quiet interior open space (60 m by 60 m) and allow ample sunlight and ventilation to its perimeter buildings; the rectilinear geometry, the wide streets and boulevards to sustain high mobility and the truncated corners to facilitate turning of carts and coaches and particularly vehicles on fixed rails.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In maps of larger American cities the downtown areas are almost always grids. These areas represent the original land dimensions of the founded city, generally around one square mile. Some cities expanded the grid further out from the centre, but maps also show that, in general, as the distance from the centre increases, a variety of patterns emerge in no particular discernible order. In juxtaposition to the grid they appear random. These new patterns have been systematically classified and their design characteristics measured<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the grid system was widely used in most major cities and their <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a> until the 1960s. However, during the 1920s, the rapid adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> caused a panic among <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planners</a>, who, based on observation, claimed that speeding cars would eventually kill tens of thousands of small children per year. Apparently, at this early stage of the car's entry into the grid, the streets of major cities worldwide were the scene of virtual "slaughter" as the fatality rate in proportion to population was more than double the current rate.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2009, after several decades of road safety improvements and a continuous decline in fatalities, an estimated 33,963 people died in motor vehicle traffic crashes and, according to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, "Motor vehicle crashes are the leading cause of death for children from 3 to 14 years old.".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Planners, therefore, called for an inwardly focused "<a href="/wiki/City_block#Superblock" title="City block">superblock</a>" arrangement that minimized through automobile traffic and discouraged cars from traveling on anything but <a href="/wiki/Arterial_road" title="Arterial road">arterial roads</a>; traffic generators, such as apartment complexes and shops, would be restricted to the edges of the superblock, along the arterial. This paradigm prevailed between about 1930 and 1960, especially in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a>, where notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Leimert_Park,_Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Leimert Park, Los Angeles, California">Leimert Park</a> (an early example) and <a href="/wiki/Panorama_City,_Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Panorama City, Los Angeles, California">Panorama City</a> (a late-period one).</p>
<p>A prominent 20th century urbanist, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a>, severely criticized some of the grid's characteristics: "With a T-square and a triangle, finally, the municipal engineer could, without the slightest training as either an architect or a sociologist, 'plan' a metropolis, with its standard lots, its standard blocks, its standard street widths, in short, with its standardized comparable, and replaceable parts. The new gridiron plans were spectacular in their inefficiency and waste. By usually failing to discriminate sufficiently between main arteries and residential streets, the first were not made wide enough while the second were usually too wide for purely neighborhood functions... as for its contribution to the permanent social functions of the city, the anonymous gridiron plan proved empty."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_engineering_(transportation)" title="Traffic engineering (transportation)">traffic engineers</a> and urban planners abandoned the grid virtually wholesale in favor of a "<a href="/wiki/Street_hierarchy" title="Street hierarchy">street hierarchy</a>". This is a thoroughly "asymmetric" street arrangement in which a residential subdivision—often surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Noise_barrier" title="Noise barrier">noise wall</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Gated_community" title="Gated community">security gate</a>—is completely separated from the road network except for one or two connections to arterial roads. In a way, this is a return to <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> styles: as noted in <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Kostof" title="Spiro Kostof">Spiro Kostof</a>'s seminal history of <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a>, <i>The City Shaped</i>, there is a strong resemblance between the street arrangements of modern American suburbs and those of medieval <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moorish" class="mw-redirect" title="Moorish">Moorish</a> cities. In each case, the community unit at hand—the clan or extended family in the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> world, the economically homogeneous <a href="/wiki/Subdivision_(land)" title="Subdivision (land)">subdivision</a> in modern suburbia—isolates itself from the larger urban scene by using dead ends and <i><a href="/wiki/Cul-de-sac" class="mw-redirect" title="Cul-de-sac">culs-de-sac</a></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milton_Keynes">Milton Keynes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Milton Keynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes_grid_road_system" title="Milton Keynes grid road system">Milton Keynes grid road system</a></div>
<p>One famous grid system is in the British new town of <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes" title="Milton Keynes">Milton Keynes</a>. In this planned city, which began construction in 1967, a system of ten "horizontal" (roughly east-west) and eleven "vertical" (roughly north-south) roads was used, with <a href="/wiki/Roundabout" title="Roundabout">roundabouts</a> at each intersection. The horizontal roads were all given names ending in 'way' and H numbers (for 'horizontal', e.g. H3 Monks Way). The vertical roads were given names ending in 'street' and V numbers (for 'vertical', e.g. <a href="/wiki/V6_Grafton_Street" class="mw-redirect" title="V6 Grafton Street">V6 Grafton Street</a>). Each grid road was spaced roughly one kilometre along from the next, forming squares of approximately one square kilometre. Each square and each roundabout was given its own name. The system provided very easy transport within the city, although it confused visitors who were unfamiliar with the system. The grid squares thus formed are far larger than the city blocks described earlier, and the road layouts within the grid squares are generally 'organic' in form – matching the street hierarchy model described above.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_and_criticisms">Benefits and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Benefits and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_cost">Financial cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financial cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The infrastructure cost for regular grid patterns is generally higher than for patterns with discontinuous streets. Costs for streets depend largely on four variables: street width, street length, <a href="/wiki/City_block" title="City block">block</a> width and pavement width.</p>

<p><i>Street width</i>, or right of way (ROW), influences the amount of land that is devoted to streets, which becomes unavailable for development and therefore represents an opportunity cost. The wider the street the higher the opportunity cost. Street width is determined by circulation and aesthetic considerations and is not dependent on the pattern configuration. Any configuration can have wide or narrow streets.</p>
<p><i>Street length</i> influences proportionately the amount of street components that have to be constructed such as pavement, curbs and sidewalks, storm sewers and drains, light poles, and trees. The street length of a given area of development depends on the frequency at which streets occur which in turn depends on the length and width of a block. The higher the frequency of streets the longer is their total length. The smaller the block dimensions the higher the frequency of the streets. As the frequency of street increases so does the number of intersections. Intersections normally cost more than straight street length because they are labour-intensive and require street and traffic signage.</p>
<p><i>Pavement width</i> influences the cost by affecting the amount of materials and labour required to provide a finished road surface. Pavement width is generally based on traffic engineering considerations and is not dependent on pattern configuration. As with the street width, any pattern can have wide or narrow pavements. Of all three factors that affect cost, street width, street length and pavement width, only street length is pattern dependent. An objective cost comparison would, therefore, rely on this variable with the full understanding that the other variables, though optional, can play a role.</p>
<p>Traditional orthogonal grid patterns generally have greater street frequencies than discontinuous patterns. For example, Portland's block is 200 feet × 200 feet while Miletus' is half that size and Timgad's half again (see diagram). Houston, Sacramento and Barcelona are progressively bigger reaching up to four times the area of Portland's block. New York's 1811 plan (see above) has blocks of 200&#160;ft. in width and variable lengths ranging from about 500 to 900 feet. The corresponding frequency of streets for each of these block sizes affects the street length.</p>
<p>A simple example of a grid street pattern (see diagram) illustrates the progressive reduction in <i>total</i> street length (the sum of all individual street lengths) and the corresponding increase in block length. For a corresponding reduction of one, two, three and four streets within this 40-acre (16&#160;ha) parcel, the street length is reduced from an original total of 12,600 to 7,680 linear feet, a 39% reduction. Simultaneously, block lengths increase from 200 × 200 feet to 1240 × 200 feet. When all five blocks have reached the ultimate size of 1240 feet, four street lengths out of total eight have been eliminated. Block lengths of 1000 feet or larger rarely appear in grid plans and are not recommended as they hinder pedestrian movement (Pedestrianism, below). From the pedestrian perspective, the smaller the block is, the easier the navigation and the more direct the route. Consequently, the finer grids are preferred.</p>
<p>Patterns that incorporate discontinuous street types such as crescents and <a href="/wiki/Cul-de-sac" class="mw-redirect" title="Cul-de-sac">culs-de-sac</a> have not, in general, regarded pedestrian movement as a priority and, consequently, have produced blocks that are usually in the 1000-foot range and often exceed it. As a result, street frequency drops and so does the <i>total</i> street length and, therefore, the cost. In general, it is not the street pattern per se that affects costs but the frequency of streets that it either necessitates or purposely incorporates.</p>
<p>An inherent advantage of the orthogonal geometry of a proper grid is its tendency to yield regular lots in well-packed sequences. This maximizes the use of the land of the block; it does not, however, affect street frequency. Any frequency of orthogonal streets produces the same <a href="/wiki/Packing_problems" title="Packing problems">packing</a> effect. Orthogonal geometry also minimizes disputes over lot boundaries and maximizes the number of lots that could front a given street. <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Randal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Randal (page does not exist)">John Randal</a> said Manhattan's grid plan facilitated "buying, selling and improving real estate".<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another important aspect of street grids and the use of rectilinear blocks is that traffic flows of either pedestrians, cars, or both, only cross at right angles. This is an important traffic safety feature, since no one entering the intersection needs to look over their shoulder to see oncoming traffic. Any time traffic flows meet at an acute angle, someone cannot see traffic approaching them. The grid is thus a geometric response to our human physiology. It is very likely the original purpose of grid layouts comes from the Athenian Agora. Before the grid organization, markets were laid out randomly in a field with traffic approaches at odd angles. This caused carts and wagons to turn over due to frequent collisions. Laying out the market stalls into regularized rows at right angles solved this problem and was later built into the Athenian Agora and copied ever since.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_features.2C_rain_water_absorption_and_pollutant_generation">Ecological features, rain water absorption and pollutant generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecological features, rain water absorption and pollutant generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typical uniform grids are unresponsive to <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>. <a href="/wiki/Priene" title="Priene">Priene</a>'s plan, for example, is set on a hill side and most of its north-south streets are stepped, a feature that would have made them inaccessible to carts, chariots and loaded animals. Many modern cities follow Priene's example, <i>e.g.</i> <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a>. In a modern context, steep grades limit accessibility by car, and more so by bicycle, on foot, or wheelchair, particularly in cold climates.</p>
<p>The same inflexibility of the grid leads to disregarding <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmentally</a> sensitive areas such as small streams and <a href="/wiki/Creek_(stream)" class="mw-redirect" title="Creek (stream)">creeks</a> or mature woodlots in preference for the application of the immutable geometry. It is said of the NY grid plan that it flattened all obstacles in its way. By contrast, recent discontinuous street patterns follow the configuration of natural features without disrupting them. The grid represents a rationalist, <a href="/wiki/Reductionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Reductionist">reductionist</a> solution to a multifaceted issue.</p>
<p>The grid's inherent high street and intersection frequencies produce large areas of <a href="/wiki/Impermeable_surface" class="mw-redirect" title="Impermeable surface">impermeable surfaces</a> in the street pavement and the <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalks</a>. In comparison to recent networks with discontinuous street types, grids can be up to 30% higher in impermeable surfaces attributable to roads. The emerging environmental priority of retaining as much as 90% of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain water</a> on site becomes problematic with high percentages of impermeable surfaces. And since roads constitute the largest share of the total impermeable surfaces of a development, the difficulty is compounded by the grid type of layout. For these reasons modern planners have attempted to modify the rigid, uniform, classic grid.</p>
<p>Some cities, notably <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, have devised means to improve a street's retention capacity. However, frequent intersections as they occur in a regular grid would pose an obstacle to their effective application.</p>
<p>A street network pattern can affect the production of pollutants by the amount of car travel that it necessitates and the speed at which cars can travel. The grid plan with its frequent intersections may displace a portion of the local car trips with walking or biking due to the directness of route that it offers to <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrians</a>. But it also makes the same routes more direct for cars, which could be an enticement for driving. The potential car trip displacement would result in a reduction of <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutant</a> <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">emissions</a>. The advantage of the intersection density for pedestrians, however, can have a contrary effect for cars due to its potential for reducing speeds. Low speeds below 20&#160;mph have a significantly higher coefficient of pollutant production than above 30, though the coefficient after levelling off tends to increase gradually after 50&#160;mph.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This effect is accentuated with high traffic density in areas with commercial uses where speeds come to a crawl. Since the grid plan is non-hierarchical and intersections are frequent, all streets can be subject to this potential reduction of average speeds, leading to a high production of pollutants. Greenhouse and noxious gases can be detrimental to the environment and to resident health.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_environment_and_security">Social environment and security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Social environment and security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his seminal study (1982) on livable streets that was conducted in neighbourhoods with a grid, Donald Appleyard showed that social networking and street playing degraded as traffic increased on a street. His research provided the groundwork for <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a> and for several initiatives such as <a href="/wiki/Living_street" title="Living street">living streets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Home_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Zone">Home Zones</a>, all of which are aimed at improving a street's social milieu. The amount of traffic on a street depends on variables such as the population density of the neighbourhood, car ownership and its proximity to commercial, institutional or recreational edifices. Most importantly, however, it depends on whether a street is or could become a through road to a destination. As a through road, it could sustain unpredictable levels of traffic that may fluctuate during the day and increase over time.</p>
<p>A key characteristic of the grid pattern is that any and all streets are equally accessible to traffic (non-hierarchical) and could be chosen at will as alternative routes to a destination. Cut-through driving, or shortcutting, has been resisted by residents.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Cities responded by making modifications to prevent it. Current recommended design practice suggests the use of 3-way intersections to alleviate it.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The geometry of the normal, open grid is evidently unsuitable for protecting or enhancing the social environment of a street from the negative influence of traffic. Similarly, a 1972 ground-breaking study by Oscar Newman on a <a href="/wiki/Defensible_Space_Theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Defensible Space Theory">Defensible Space Theory</a> described ways to improve the social environment and security of neighbourhoods and streets. In a practical application of his theory at Five Oaks, the neighbourhood's grid pattern was modified to prevent through traffic and create identifiable smaller enclaves while maintaining complete pedestrian freedom of movement. The positive outcome of these changes reinforces Appleyard's findings and the need to reduce or prevent through traffic on neighbourhood streets; a need that cannot be met with a typical, uniform, open grid.</p>
<p>The question of neighbourhood security has been a constant focus of research since Oscar Newman's work. New research has expanded the discussion on this disputed issue. A recent study<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> did extensive spatial analysis and correlated several building, site plan and social factors with crime frequencies and identified subtle nuances to the contrasting positions. The study looked at, among others, dwelling types, unit density (site density) movement on the street, culs–de-sac or grids and the permeability of a residential area. Among its conclusions are, respectively, that flats are always safer than houses and the wealth of inhabitants matters, density is generally beneficial but more so at ground level, local movement is beneficial, but not larger scale movement, relative affluence and the number of neighbours have a greater effect than either being on a cul-de-sac or being on a through street. It also re-established that simple, linear culs-de-sac with good numbers of dwellings that are joined to through streets tend to be safe. As for permeability, it suggests that residential areas should be permeable enough to allow movement in all directions but no more. The overprovision of poorly used permeability is a crime hazard. The open, uniform grid could be seen as an example of undifferentiated permeability.</p>
<p>A recent study in California<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> examined the amount of child play that occurred on the streets of neighbourhoods with different characteristics; grid pattern and culs-de-sac. The findings indicate that the open grid streets showed substantially lower play activity than the cul-de-sac street type. Culs-de-sac reduce perceived danger from traffic thereby encouraging more outdoor play. It pointed the way toward the development of hybrid street network patterns that improve pedestrian movement but restrict cut-through driving. Similar studies in Europe<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and most recently in Australia<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> found that children's outdoor play is significantly reduced on through roads where traffic is, or perceived by parents to be, a risk.</p>
<p>Traditional street functions such as kids' play, strolling and socializing are incompatible with traffic flow, which the open, uniform grid geometry encourages. For these reasons, cities such as <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vancouver,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Vancouver, British Columbia">Vancouver, British Columbia</a>, among many others, transformed existing residential streets part of a grid plan into permeable, linked culs-de-sac. This transformation retains the <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(spatial_and_transport_planning)" title="Permeability (spatial and transport planning)">permeability</a> and connectivity of the grid for the active modes of transport but filters and restricts car traffic on the cul-de-sac street to residents only.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pedestrian_and_bicycle_movement">Pedestrian and bicycle movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pedestrian and bicycle movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Two inherent characteristics of the grid plan, frequent intersections and orthogonal geometry, assist <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrian</a> movement. The geometry helps with orientation and <a href="/wiki/Wayfinding" title="Wayfinding">wayfinding</a> and its frequent intersections with the choice and directness of route to desired destinations. Street networks of old cities that grew organically, though admired for being picturesque, can be confusing for visitors but rarely for the original inhabitants (see plan). Similarly confusing to visitors are the plans of modern subdivisions with discontinuous and curvilinear streets. Change of street orientation, particularly when gradual or arbitrary, cannot be "mapped" in the mind. Impasses, crescents or <a href="/wiki/Cul-de-sacs" class="mw-redirect" title="Cul-de-sacs">cul-de-sacs</a> frustrate the traveler especially when they are long, forcing an arduous retracing of steps.</p>
<p>Frequency of intersections, however, becomes also a disadvantage for pedestrians and bicycles. It disrupts the relaxed canter of walking and forces pedestrians repeatedly onto the road, a hostile, anxiety-generating territory. People with physical limitations or frailties, children and seniors for example, can find such walk challenging. For bicycles this disadvantage is accentuated as their normal speed is at least double that of pedestrians. Frequent stops negate the speed advantage and the physical benefit of bicycling and add to frustration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Intersections are not only unpleasant but also dangerous. Most <a href="/wiki/Traffic_collisions" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic collisions">traffic collisions</a> and injuries occur at intersections and the majority of the injuries to pedestrians crossing <i>with</i> the right of way.</p>
<p>A dilemma arises from trying to meet important planning objectives when using the grid: pedestrianism, cost efficiency and environmental responsiveness. To serve pedestrians well, a rectangular configuration and high frequency of streets and intersections is the preferred route, which the orthogonal grid geometry provides. To reduce development costs and environmental impact, lower frequency of streets is the logical path. Since these two design objectives are contradictory a balance needs to be struck. Such balance has been achieved in leading modern projects such as <a href="/wiki/Vauban,_Freiburg" title="Vauban, Freiburg">Vauban, Freiburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Village_Homes" title="Village Homes">Village Homes</a>, Davis. Both score high in pedestrian and bike mode share and, at the same time, in reducing negative development externalities. Their layout configurations represent a fusion of the classic grid plan with recent street network patterns.</p>
<p>Examining the issue of walkability, a recent comparison of seven neighbourhood layouts found a 43 and 32 percent increase in walking with respect to a grid plan and conventional suburban layout in a <a href="/wiki/Fused_grid" title="Fused grid">fused grid</a> layout, which has greater permeability for pedestrians than for cars due to its inclusion of dedicated pedestrian paths. It also showed a 7 to 10 percent range of reduction in driving with respect to the remainder six neighbourhood layouts in the set, an environmental benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perceived and actual safety play a role in the use of the street. Perceived safety, though perhaps an inaccurate reflection of the number of injuries or fatalities, influences parents' decision to allow their children to play, walk or bike on the street. Actual levels of safety as measured by the total number of collisions and the number and severity of injuries are a matter of public concern. Both should inform the layout, if the street network is to achieve its optimum use.</p>
<p>Recent studies have found higher traffic fatality rates in outlying suburban areas than in central cities and inner suburbs with smaller blocks and more-connected street patterns.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> While some of this disparity is the result of distance from <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services" title="Emergency medical services">emergency medical</a> facilities (hospitals are usually built in a fairly late stage of the development of a suburban area), it is clear<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that the lower speeds encouraged by the frequency of intersections decrease the severity of accidents occurring on streets within a grid plan.</p>
<p>An earlier study<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> found significant differences in recorded accidents between residential neighbourhoods that were laid out on a grid and those that included culs-de-sac and crescents. The frequency of accidents was significantly higher in the grid neighbourhoods.</p>
<p>Two newer studies examined the frequency of collisions in two regional districts using the latest analytical tools. They investigated the potential correlation between street network patterns and frequency of collisions. In one study,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> cul-de-sac networks appeared to be much safer than grid networks, by nearly three to one. A second study<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> found the grid plan to be the least safe by a significant margin with respect to all other street patterns.</p>
<p>A 2009 study<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> suggests that land use patterns play a significant role in traffic safety and should be considered in conjunction with the network pattern. While all intersection types in general reduce the incidence of fatal crashes, four-way intersections, which occur regularly in a grid, increase <b>total and injurious crashes</b> significantly. The study recommends hybrid street networks with dense concentrations of T-intersections and concludes that a return to the 19th century gridiron is undesirable.</p>
<p>Stringent adherence to the grid plan can cause steep inclines since the topology of the land is not taken into account. This may be unsafe for drivers, pedestrians and bicycles since it is more difficult to control speed and braking, particularly in winter conditions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_and_development">Reconstruction and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grid_plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reconstruction and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the greatest difficulties with grid plans is their lack of specialisation, most of the important amenities being concentrated along the city's main arteries. Often grid plans are found in <a href="/wiki/Ribbon_development" title="Ribbon development">linear settlements</a>, with a main street connecting between the perpendicular roads. However, this can be mitigated by allowing mixed use development so that destinations become closer to home. Many cities, especially in Latin America, still successfully retain their grid plans. Recently, planners in the United States and Canada have revisited the idea of reintroducing grid patterns to many cities and towns.</p>
