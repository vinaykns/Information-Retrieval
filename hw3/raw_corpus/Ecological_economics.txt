<p><b>Ecological economics</b>/<b>eco-economics</b> (also called <b>Ecolonomy</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Bioeconomics" class="mw-disambig" title="Bioeconomics">Bioeconomics</a></b> of <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Georgescu-Roegen</a>) refers to both a <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Transdisciplinary">transdisciplinary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> field of academic research addressing the interdependence and <a href="/wiki/Coevolution" title="Coevolution">coevolution</a> of human <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economies</a> and natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, both intertemporally and spatially.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> By treating the economy as a subsystem of Earth's larger ecosystem, and by emphasizing the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>, the field of <b>ecological economics</b> is differentiated from <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economic</a> analysis of the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> One survey of German economists found that ecological and environmental economics are different <a href="/wiki/Schools_of_economic_thought" title="Schools of economic thought">schools of economic thought</a>, with ecological economists emphasizing strong <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and rejecting the proposition that natural capital can be substituted by human-made capital (see the section on <a href="#Weak_versus_strong_sustainability">Weak versus strong sustainability</a> below).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological economics was founded in the 1980s as a modern discipline in the works of and interactions between various European and American academics (see the section on <a href="#History_and_development">History and development</a> below). The related field of <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economics</a> is, in general, a more politically applied form of the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to ecological economist <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malte_Michael_Faber" class="extiw" title="de:Malte Michael Faber">Malte Faber</a>, ecological economics is defined by its focus on nature, justice, and time. Issues of <a href="/wiki/Intergenerational_equity" title="Intergenerational equity">intergenerational equity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irreversibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Irreversibility">irreversibility</a> of environmental change, <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> of long-term outcomes, and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> guide ecological economic analysis and valuation.<sup id="cite_ref-Faber2008_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faber2008-6">[6]</a></sup> Ecological economists have questioned fundamental mainstream economic approaches such as <a href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a>, and the separability of economic values from scientific research, contending that economics is unavoidably <a href="/wiki/Normative_ethics" title="Normative ethics">normative</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Positive_economics" title="Positive economics">positive</a> (i.e. descriptive).<sup id="cite_ref-Victor2008_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Victor2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Positional analysis, which attempts to incorporate time and justice issues, is proposed as an alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Ecological economics shares many of its perspectives with <a href="/wiki/Feminist_economics" title="Feminist economics">feminist economics</a>, including the focus on sustainability, nature, justice and care values.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first principles, deriving from the radiochemist FA Soddy, were laid out in his 1926 book "Wealth, Money (Virtual Wealth) and Debt" in 1926. Early modern interest in ecology and economics dates back to the 1940s in the work of <a href="/wiki/K._William_Kapp" class="mw-redirect" title="K. William Kapp">K. William Kapp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Polanyi" title="Karl Polanyi">Karl Polanyi</a> and the 1960s in work by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Boulding" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenneth Boulding">Kenneth Boulding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>. However, the first organized meetings of modern ecological economists occurred in the 1980s. These began in 1982, at the instigation of Lois Banner,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with a meeting held in Sweden (including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_A_S_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles A S Hall">Charles Hall</a>, Bruce Hannon, <a href="/wiki/H.T._Odum" class="mw-redirect" title="H.T. Odum">H.T. Odum</a>, and David Pimentel).<sup id="cite_ref-EarlyHistory_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EarlyHistory-12">[12]</a></sup> Most were ecosystem ecologists or mainstream environmental economists, with the exception of Daly. In 1987, Daly and Costanza edited an issue of <i>Ecological Modeling</i> to test the waters. A book entitled <i>Ecological Economics</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Joan_Martinez_Alier" title="Joan Martinez Alier">Juan Martinez-Alier</a>, was published later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-EarlyHistory_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EarlyHistory-12">[12]</a></sup> 1989 saw the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Ecological_Economics" title="International Society for Ecological Economics">International Society for Ecological Economics</a> and publication of its journal, <i><a href="/wiki/Ecological_Economics_(journal)" title="Ecological Economics (journal)">Ecological Economics</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a> was the first president of the society and first editor of the journal, currently edited by Richard Howarth.</p>
<p>European conceptual founders include <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> (1971), <a href="/wiki/K._William_Kapp" class="mw-redirect" title="K. William Kapp">K. William Kapp</a> (1950)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Polanyi" title="Karl Polanyi">Karl Polanyi</a> (1944).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some key concepts of what is now ecological economics are evident in the writings of <a href="/wiki/E.F._Schumacher" class="mw-redirect" title="E.F. Schumacher">E.F. Schumacher</a>, whose book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a> – A Study of Economics as if People Mattered</i> (1973) was published just a few years before the first edition of <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>'s comprehensive and persuasive <i><a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy#Herman_Daly.27s_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy" title="Steady-state economy">Steady-State Economics</a></i> (1977).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Other figures include ecologists <a href="/wiki/C.S._Holling" class="mw-redirect" title="C.S. Holling">C.S. Holling</a>, <a href="/wiki/H.T._Odum" class="mw-redirect" title="H.T. Odum">H.T. Odum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a>, biologist Gretchen Daily and physicist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ayres_(scientist)" title="Robert Ayres (scientist)">Robert Ayres</a>. CUNY geography professor <a href="/wiki/David_Harvey_(geographer)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Harvey (geographer)">David Harvey</a> explicitly added ecological concerns to political economic literature. This parallel development in <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> has been continued by analysts such as sociologist <a href="/wiki/John_Bellamy_Foster" title="John Bellamy Foster">John Bellamy Foster</a>.</p>
<p>The antecedents can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Romantics" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantics">Romantics</a> of the 19th century as well as some <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> political economists of that era. Concerns over population were expressed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a>, while <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> predicted the <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy#John_Stuart_Mill.27s_concept_of_the_stationary_state" title="Steady-state economy">desirability of the <i>stationary state</i> of an economy</a>. Mill thereby anticipated later insights of modern ecological economists, but without having had their experience of the social and ecological costs of the <a href="/wiki/Post%E2%80%93World_War_II_economic_expansion" title="Post–World War II economic expansion">Post–World War II economic expansion</a>. As Martinez-Alier explores in his book the debate on energy in economic systems can also be traced into the 19th century e.g. Nobel prize-winning chemist, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Soddy" title="Frederick Soddy">Frederick Soddy</a> (1877–1956). Soddy criticized the prevailing belief of the economy as a perpetual motion machine, capable of generating infinite wealth—a criticism echoed by his intellectual heirs in the now emergent field of ecological economics.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Romanian economist <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> (1906–1994), who was among Daly's teachers at <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_University" title="Vanderbilt University">Vanderbilt University</a>, provided ecological economics with a modern conceptual framework based on the material and energy flows of economic production and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>. His <i>magnum opus</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Magnum_opus_on_The_Entropy_Law_and_the_Economic_Process" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">The Entropy Law and the Economic Process</a></i> (1971), has been highly influential.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Articles by Inge Ropke (2004, 2005)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clive_Spash&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clive Spash (page does not exist)">Clive Spash</a> (1999)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> cover the development and modern history of ecological economics and explain its differentiation from resource and environmental economics, as well as some of the controversy between American and European schools of thought. An article by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a>, David Stern, Lining He, and Chunbo Ma<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> responded to a call by Mick Common to determine the foundational literature of ecological economics by using citation analysis to examine which books and articles have had the most influence on the development of the field. However, citations analysis has itself proven controversial and similar work has been criticized by Clive Spash for attempting to pre-determine what is regarded as influential in ecological economics through study design and data manipulation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In addition, the journal Ecological Economics has itself been criticized for swamping the field with mainstream economics.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_and_ecology">Nature and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nature and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/260px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" width="260" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/390px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6e/Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg/520px-Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="421" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_natural_resource_flows.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Natural resources flow through the economy and end up as waste and pollution</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A simple <a href="/wiki/Circular_flow_of_income" title="Circular flow of income">circular flow of income</a> diagram is replaced in ecological economics by a more complex flow diagram reflecting the input of solar energy, which sustains natural inputs and environmental services which are then used as units of <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>. Once consumed, natural inputs pass out of the economy as pollution and waste. The potential of an environment to provide services and materials is referred to as an "environment's source function", and this function is depleted as resources are consumed or pollution contaminates the resources. The "sink function" describes an environment's ability to absorb and render harmless waste and pollution: when waste output exceeds the limit of the sink function, long-term damage occurs.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:8</sup> Some persistent pollutants, such as some organic pollutants and nuclear waste are absorbed very slowly or not at all; ecological economists emphasize minimizing "cumulative pollutants".<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:28</sup> Pollutants affect human health and the health of the climate.</p>
<p>The economic value of natural capital and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> is accepted by mainstream environmental economics, but is emphasized as especially important in ecological economics. Ecological economists may begin by estimating how to maintain a stable environment before assessing the cost in dollar terms.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9</sup> Ecological economist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a> led an attempted valuation of the global ecosystem in 1997. Initially published in <i>Nature</i>, the article concluded on $33 trillion with a range from $16 trillion to $54 trillion (in 1997, total global <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> was $27 trillion).<sup id="cite_ref-Costanza1998_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costanza1998-26">[26]</a></sup> Half of the value went to <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a>. The open oceans, continental shelves, and estuaries had the highest total value, and the highest per-hectare values went to estuaries, swamps/floodplains, and seagrass/algae beds. The work was criticized by articles in <i><a href="/wiki/Ecological_Economics_(journal)" title="Ecological Economics (journal)">Ecological Economics</a></i> Volume 25, Issue 1, but the critics acknowledged the positive potential for economic valuation of the global ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:129</sup></p>
<p>The Earth's <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> is a central issue in ecological economics. Early economists such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> pointed out the finite carrying capacity of the earth, which was also central to the MIT study <i><a href="/wiki/Limits_to_Growth" class="mw-redirect" title="Limits to Growth">Limits to Growth</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Diminishing_returns" title="Diminishing returns">Diminishing returns</a> suggest that productivity increases will slow if major technological progress is not made. Food production may become a problem, as <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, an impending <a href="/wiki/Water_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Water crisis">water crisis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">soil salinity</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>) reduce the productivity of agriculture. Ecological economists argue that <a href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a>, which exacerbates these problems, is not <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>, and are generally inclined favorably to <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>, which also reduces the output of carbon.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26</sup></p>
<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fisheries</a> are believed to have peaked and begun a decline, with valuable habitat such as estuaries in critical condition.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:28</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fish_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish farm">farming</a> of <a href="/wiki/Piscivorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Piscivorous">piscivorous</a> fish, like <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, does not help solve the problem because they need to be fed products from other fish. Studies have shown that <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquaculture of salmon">salmon farming</a> has major <a href="/wiki/Salmon_farming_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Salmon farming issues">negative impacts</a> on wild salmon, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">forage fish</a> that need to be caught to feed them.<sup id="cite_ref-Salmon_Run_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salmon_Run-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are higher on the <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>, they are less efficient sources of food energy. Reduced consumption of meat would reduce the demand for food, but as nations develop, they tend to adopt high-meat diets similar to that of the United States. <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_food" title="Genetically modified food">Genetically modified food</a> (GMF) a conventional solution to the problem, presents numerous problems – <a href="/wiki/Bt_corn" class="mw-redirect" title="Bt corn">Bt corn</a> produces its own <a href="/wiki/Bacillus_thuringiensis" title="Bacillus thuringiensis">Bacillus thuringiensis</a> toxin/protein, but the pest resistance is believed to be only a matter of time.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2006_25-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2006-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31</sup> The overall effect of GMF on yields is contentious, with the USDA and FAO acknowledging that GMFs do not necessarily have higher yields and may even have reduced yields.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> is now widely acknowledged as a major issue, with all national scientific academies <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">expressing agreement</a> on the importance of the issue. As the population growth intensifies and energy demand increases, the world faces an <a href="/wiki/Energy_crisis" title="Energy crisis">energy crisis</a>. Some economists and scientists forecast a global ecological crisis if energy use is not contained – the <a href="/wiki/Stern_report" class="mw-redirect" title="Stern report">Stern report</a> is an example. The disagreement has sparked a vigorous debate on issue of <a href="/wiki/Discounting" title="Discounting">discounting</a> and intergenerational equity.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">Mainstream economics</a> has attempted to become a value-free '<a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_science" title="Hard and soft science">hard science</a>', but ecological economists argue that value-free economics is generally not realistic. Ecological economics is more willing to entertain alternative conceptions of <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefits</a> such as positional analysis or multi-criteria analysis. Ecological economics is typically viewed as economics for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and may have goals similar to <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools_of_thought">Schools of thought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Schools of thought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various competing schools of thought exist in the field. Some are close to resource and environmental economics while others are far more heterodox in outlook. An example of the latter is the <i>European Society for Ecological Economics</i>. An example of the former is the Swedish <i>Beijer International Institute of Ecological Economics.</i> Clive Spash has argued for the classification of the ecological economics movement, and more generally work by different economic schools on the environment, into three main categories. These are the mainstream new resource economists, the new environmental pragmatists,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and the more radical social ecological economists.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> International survey work comparing the relevance of the categories for mainstream and heterodox economists shows some clear divisions between environmental and ecological economists.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differentiation_from_mainstream_schools">Differentiation from mainstream schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Differentiation from mainstream schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some ecological economists prioritise adding natural capital to the typical <a href="/wiki/Capital_asset" title="Capital asset">capital asset</a> analysis of land, labor, and financial capital. These ecological economists then use tools from <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_economics" title="Mathematical economics">mathematical economics</a> as in mainstream economics, but may apply them more closely to the natural world. Whereas mainstream economists tend to be technological optimists, ecological economists are inclined to be technological sceptics. They reason that the natural world has a limited carrying capacity and that its resources may run out. Since destruction of important environmental resources could be practically irreversible and catastrophic, ecological economists are inclined to justify cautionary measures based on the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most cogent example of how the different theories treat similar assets is <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a> ecosystems, most obviously the Yasuni region of <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. While this area has substantial deposits of <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> it is also one of the most diverse ecosystems on Earth and some estimates establish it has over 200 undiscovered medical substances in its genomes - most of which would be destroyed by logging the forest or mining the bitumen. Effectively, the <a href="/wiki/Instructional_capital" title="Instructional capital">instructional capital</a> of the genomes is undervalued by analyses which view the rainforest primarily as a source of wood, oil/tar and perhaps food. Increasingly the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">carbon credit</a> for leaving the extremely carbon-intensive ("dirty") bitumen in the ground is also valued - the government of Ecuador set a price of US$350M for an oil lease with the intent of selling it to someone committed to never exercising it at all and instead preserving the rainforest.</p>
<p>While this natural capital and ecosystems services approach has proven popular amongst many it has also been contested as failing to address the underlying problems with mainstream economics, growth, market capitalism and monetary valuation of the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Critiques concern the need to create a more meaningful relationship with Nature and the non-human world than evident in the instrumentalism of shallow ecology and the environmental economists <a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">commodification</a> of everything external to the market system.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topics">Topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the topics addressed by ecological economics are methodology, allocation of resources, weak versus strong sustainability, energy economics, energy accounting and balance, environmental services, cost shifting, and modeling.</p>

<p>A primary objective of ecological economics (EE) is to ground economic thinking and practice in physical reality, especially in the laws of physics (particularly the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">laws of thermodynamics</a>) and in knowledge of biological systems. It accepts as a goal the improvement of human well-being through development, and seeks to ensure achievement of this through planning for the sustainable development of ecosystems and societies. Of course the terms development and sustainable development are far from lacking controversy. <a href="/wiki/Richard_B._Norgaard" title="Richard B. Norgaard">Richard B. Norgaard</a> argues traditional economics has hi-jacked the development terminology in his book <i>Development Betrayed</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Well-being in ecological economics is also differentiated from welfare as found in mainstream economics and the 'new welfare economics' from the 1930s which informs resource and environmental economics. This entails a limited preference utilitarian conception of value i.e., Nature is valuable to our economies, that is because people will pay for its services such as clean air, clean water, encounters with wilderness, etc.</p>
<p>Ecological economics is distinguishable from <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> primarily by its assertion that the economy is embedded within an environmental system. Ecology deals with the energy and matter transactions of life and the Earth, and the human economy is by definition contained within this system. Ecological economists argue that <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> has ignored the environment, at best considering it to be a subset of the human economy.</p>
<p>The neoclassical view ignores much of what the natural sciences have taught us about the contributions of nature to the creation of wealth e.g., the planetary endowment of scarce matter and energy, along with the complex and biologically diverse ecosystems that provide goods and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> directly to human communities: micro- and macro-climate regulation, water recycling, water purification, storm water regulation, waste absorption, food and medicine production, pollination, protection from solar and cosmic radiation, the view of a starry night sky, etc.</p>
<p>There has then been a move to regard such things as <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> and ecosystems functions as goods and services.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> However, this is far from uncontroversial within ecology or ecological economics due to the potential for narrowing down values to those found in mainstream economics and the danger of merely regarding Nature as a commodity. This has been referred to as ecologists 'selling out on Nature'.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> There is then a concern that ecological economics has failed to learn from the extensive literature in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">environmental ethics</a> about how to structure a plural value system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allocation_of_resources">Allocation of resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Allocation of resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Resource_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource economics">Resource</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> focus primarily on the efficient allocation of resources, and less on two other fundamental economic problems which are central to ecological economics: <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a> (<a href="/wiki/Equity_(economics)" title="Equity (economics)">equity</a>) and the scale of the economy relative to the ecosystems upon which it is reliant.<sup id="cite_ref-Daly_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daly-45">[45]</a></sup> Ecological Economics also makes a clear distinction between growth (quantitative increase in economic output) and development (qualitative improvement of the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>) while arguing that <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical economics</a> confuses the two. Ecological economists point out that, beyond modest levels, increased per-capita <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> (the typical economic measure of "standard of living") does not necessarily lead to improvement in human well-being, while this same consumption can have harmful effects on the environment and broader societal well-being.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weak_versus_strong_sustainability">Weak versus strong sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Weak versus strong sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Ecological economics challenges the conventional approach towards natural resources, claiming that it undervalues natural capital by considering it as interchangeable with human-made capital—labor and technology.</p>
<p>The impending depletion of natural resources and increase of climate-changing greenhouse gasses should motivate us to examine how political, economic and social policies can benefit from alternative energy. Interestingly enough, shifting our dependence on fossil fuels with specific interest within just one of the above-mentioned factors easily benefits at least one other. For instance, photo voltaic (or solar) panels have a 15% efficiency when absorbing the sun's energy, but its construction demand has increased 120% within both commercial and residential properties. Additionally, this construction has led to a roughly 30% increase in work demands (Chen).</p>
<p>The potential for the substitution of man-made capital for natural capital is an important debate in ecological economics and the economics of sustainability. There is a continuum of views among economists between the strongly neoclassical positions of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Weitzman" title="Martin Weitzman">Martin Weitzman</a>, at one extreme and the <a href="/wiki/Pessimism#Entropy_pessimism" title="Pessimism">'entropy pessimists'</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, at the other.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neoclassical economists tend to maintain that man-made capital can, in principle, replace all types of natural capital. This is known as the <a href="/wiki/Strong_vs_Weak_sustainability" class="mw-redirect" title="Strong vs Weak sustainability">weak sustainability</a> view, essentially that every technology can be improved upon or replaced by innovation, and that there is a substitute for any and all scarce materials.</p>
<p>At the other extreme, the <a href="/wiki/Strong_vs_Weak_sustainability" class="mw-redirect" title="Strong vs Weak sustainability">strong sustainability</a> view argues that the stock of natural resources and ecological functions are irreplaceable. From the premises of strong sustainability, it follows that <a href="/wiki/Economic_policy" title="Economic policy">economic policy</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary" title="Fiduciary">fiduciary</a> responsibility to the greater ecological world, and that sustainable development must therefore take a different approach to valuing natural resources and ecological functions.</p>
<p>Recently, Stanislav Shmelev developed a new methodology for the assessment of progress at the macro scale based on multi-criteria methods, which allows consideration of different perspectives, including strong and weak sustainability or conservationists vs industrialists and aims to search for a 'middle way' by providing a strong neo-Keynsian economic push without putting excessive pressure on the natural resources, including water or producing emissions, both directly and indirectly.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_economics">Energy economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Energy economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A key concept of energy economics is <a href="/wiki/Net_energy_gain" title="Net energy gain">net energy gain</a>, which recognizes that all energy requires energy to produce. To be useful the energy return on energy invested (<i><a href="/wiki/EROEI" class="mw-redirect" title="EROEI">EROEI</a></i>) has to be greater than one. The net energy gain from production coal, oil and gas has declined over time as the easiest to produce sources have been most heavily depleted.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological economics generally rejects the view of <a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">energy economics</a> that growth in the energy supply is related directly to well being, focusing instead on <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity</a> - or natural capital and <a href="/wiki/Individual_capital" title="Individual capital">individual capital</a>, in the terminology sometimes adopted to describe these economically. In practice, ecological economics focuses primarily on the key issues of <a href="/wiki/Uneconomic_growth" title="Uneconomic growth">uneconomic growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. Ecological economists are inclined to acknowledge that much of what is important in human well-being is not analyzable from a strictly economic standpoint and suggests an interdisciplinary approach combining social and natural sciences as a means to address this.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermoeconomics" title="Thermoeconomics">Thermoeconomics</a> is based on the proposition that the role of energy in <a href="/wiki/Biological_evolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological evolution">biological evolution</a> should be defined and understood through the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, but also in terms of such economic criteria as <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_efficiency" title="Economic efficiency">efficiency</a>, and especially the costs and benefits (or profitability) of the various mechanisms for capturing and utilizing available energy to build biomass and do work.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> As a result, thermoeconomics is often discussed in the field of ecological economics, which itself is related to the fields of sustainability and sustainable development.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">Exergy</a> analysis is performed in the field of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">industrial ecology</a> to use energy more efficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The term <i>exergy</i>, was coined by <a href="/wiki/Zoran_Rant" title="Zoran Rant">Zoran Rant</a> in 1956, but the concept was developed by <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">J. Willard Gibbs</a>. In recent decades, utilization of exergy has spread outside of physics and engineering to the fields of industrial ecology, ecological economics, <a href="/wiki/Systems_ecology" title="Systems ecology">systems ecology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Energetics" title="Energetics">energetics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_accounting_and_balance">Energy accounting and balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Energy accounting and balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Net_energy_gain" title="Net energy gain">Net energy gain</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Energy_accounting" title="Energy accounting">energy balance</a> can be used to track energy through a system, and is a very useful tool for determining resource use and environmental impacts, using the First and Second laws of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, to determine how much energy is needed at each point in a system, and in what form that energy is a cost in various environmental issues.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The energy accounting system keeps track of energy in, energy out, and non-useful energy versus <a href="/wiki/Work_done" class="mw-redirect" title="Work done">work done</a>, and transformations within the system.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists have written and speculated on different aspects of energy accounting.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_services">Environmental services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">Ecosystem valuation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Price_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Price of life">Price of life</a></div>
<p>A study was carried out by Costanza and colleagues<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> to determine the 'price' of the services provided by the environment. This was determined by averaging values obtained from a range of studies conducted in very specific context and then transferring these without regard to that context. Dollar figures were averaged to a per hectare number for different types of ecosystem e.g. wetlands, oceans. A total was then produced which came out at 33 trillion US dollars (1997 values), more than twice the total <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> of the world at the time of the study. This study was criticized by pre-ecological and even some <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economists</a> - for being inconsistent with assumptions of <a href="/wiki/Financial_capital" title="Financial capital">financial capital</a> <a href="/wiki/Valuation_(finance)" title="Valuation (finance)">valuation</a> - and ecological economists - for being inconsistent with an ecological economics focus on biological and physical indicators.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The whole idea of treating ecosystems as goods and services to be valued in monetary terms remains controversial. A common objection<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> is that life is precious or priceless, but this demonstrably degrades to it being worthless under the assumptions of any branch of economics. Reducing human bodies to financial values is a necessary part of every branch of economics and not always in the direct terms of <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wages" class="mw-redirect" title="Wages">wages</a>. Economics, in principle, assumes that conflict is reduced by agreeing on voluntary contractual relations and prices instead of simply fighting or coercing or tricking others into providing goods or services. In doing so, a provider agrees to surrender time and take bodily risks and other (reputation, financial) risks. Ecosystems are no different from other bodies economically except insofar as they are far less replaceable than typical labour or commodities.</p>
<p>Despite these issues, many ecologists and conservation biologists are pursuing <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">ecosystem valuation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a> measures in particular appear to be the most promising way to reconcile financial and ecological values, and there are many active efforts in this regard. The growing field of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_finance" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity finance">biodiversity finance</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> began to emerge in 2008 in response to many specific proposals such as the Ecuadoran Yasuni proposal<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> or similar ones in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Congo</a>. US news outlets treated the stories as a "threat"<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> to "drill a park"<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> reflecting a previously dominant view that NGOs and governments had the primary responsibility to protect ecosystems. However <a href="/wiki/Peter_Barnes_(entrepreneur)" title="Peter Barnes (entrepreneur)">Peter Barnes</a> and other commentators have recently argued that a guardianship/trustee/commons model is far more effective and takes the decisions out of the political realm.</p>
<p>Commodification of other ecological relations as in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">carbon credit</a> and direct payments to farmers to preserve <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> are likewise examples that enable private parties to play more direct roles protecting biodiversity. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> achieved near-universal agreement in 2008<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> that such payments directly valuing ecosystem preservation and encouraging <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> were the only practical way out of a food crisis. The holdouts were all English-speaking countries that export <a href="/wiki/GMO" class="mw-redirect" title="GMO">GMOs</a> and promote "<a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a>" agreements that facilitate their own control of the world transport network: The US, UK, Canada and Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Not_.27externalities.27.2C_but_cost_shifting">Not 'externalities', but cost shifting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Not 'externalities', but cost shifting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecological economics is founded upon the view that the neoclassical economics (NCE) assumption that environmental and community costs and benefits are mutually canceling <i>"<a href="/wiki/Externalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Externalities">externalities</a>"</i> is not warranted. <a href="/wiki/Joan_Martinez_Alier" title="Joan Martinez Alier">Joan Martinez Alier</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> for instance shows that the bulk of consumers are automatically excluded from having an impact upon the prices of commodities, as these consumers are future generations who have not been born yet. The assumptions behind future discounting, which assume that future goods will be cheaper than present goods, has been criticized by Fred Pearce<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and by the recent <a href="/wiki/Stern_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Stern Report">Stern Report</a> (although the Stern report itself does employ discounting and has been criticized for this and other reasons by ecological economists such as Clive Spash).<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>Concerning these externalities, some like the eco-businessman <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hawken" title="Paul Hawken">Paul Hawken</a> argue an orthodox economic line that the only reason why goods produced unsustainably are usually cheaper than goods produced sustainably is due to a hidden subsidy, paid by the non-monetized human environment, community or future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> These arguments are developed further by Hawken, Amory and Hunter Lovins to promote their vision of an environmental capitalist utopia in <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism:_Creating_the_Next_Industrial_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution">Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast, ecological economists, like Joan Martinez-Alier, appeal to a different line of reasoning.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Rather than assuming some (new) form of capitalism is the best way forward, an older ecological economic critique questions the very idea of internalizing externalities as providing some corrective to the current system. The work by <a href="/wiki/Karl_William_Kapp" title="Karl William Kapp">Karl William Kapp</a> explains why the concept of "externality" is a misnomer.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In fact the modern business enterprise operates on the basis of shifting costs onto others as normal practice to make profits.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Charles Einsentein has argued that this method of privatising profits while socialising the costs through externalities, passing the costs to the community, to the natural environment or to future generations is inherently destructive<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> As social ecological economist Clive Spash has noted, externality theory fallaciously assumes environmental and social problems are minor aberrations in an otherwise perfectly functioning efficient economic system.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Internalizing the odd externality does nothing to address the structural systemic problem and fails to recognize the all pervasive nature of these supposed 'externalities'.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological-economic_modeling">Ecological-economic modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ecological-economic modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">Mathematical modeling</a> is a powerful tool that is used in ecological economic analysis. Various approaches and techniques include:<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary" class="mw-redirect" title="Evolutionary">evolutionary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Input/output_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Input/output model">input-output</a>, neo-Austrian modeling, <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermodynamic">thermodynamic</a> models,<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Multi-criteria_decision_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-criteria decision analysis">multi-criteria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agent-based" class="mw-redirect" title="Agent-based">agent-based</a> modeling, the environmental <a href="/wiki/Kuznets_curve" title="Kuznets curve">Kuznets curve</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stock-Flow_consistent_model" title="Stock-Flow consistent model">Stock-Flow consistent model</a> frameworks. <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">System dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/GIS" class="mw-redirect" title="GIS">GIS</a> are techniques applied, among other, to spatial dynamic landscape simulation modeling.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The Matrix accounting methods of Christian Felber provide a more sophisticated method for identifying "the common good"<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_economics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Assigning monetary value to natural resources such as <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and the emergent <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> is often viewed as a key process in influencing economic practices, policy, and decision-making.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> While this idea is becoming more and more accepted among ecologists and conservationist, some argue that it is inherently false.</p>
<p>McCauley argues that ecological economics and the resulting ecosystem service based conservation can be harmful.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> He describes four main problems with this approach:</p>
<p>Firstly, it seems to be assumed that all ecosystem services are financially beneficial. This is undermined by a basic characteristic of ecosystems: they do not act specifically in favour of any single species. While certain services might be very useful to us, such as <a href="/wiki/Coastal_protection" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal protection">coastal protection</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hurricanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> for example, others might cause financial or personal harm, such as wolves hunting <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The complexity of Eco-systems makes it challenging to weigh up the value of a given species. Wolves play a critical role in regulating prey populations, the absence of such an apex predator in the Scottish Highlands have caused the over population of deer, preventing afforestation, which increases the risk of flooding and damage to property.</p>
<p>Secondly, allocating monetary value to nature would make its conservation reliant on markets that fluctuate. This can lead to <a href="/wiki/Devaluation" title="Devaluation">devaluation</a> of services that were previously considered financially beneficial. Such is the case of the bees in a forest near former <a href="/wiki/Coffee_plantation" class="mw-redirect" title="Coffee plantation">coffee plantations</a> in Finca Santa Fe, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> services were valued to over US$60,000 a year, but soon after the study, coffee prices dropped and the fields were replanted with pineapple.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Pineapple does not require bees to be pollinated, so the value of their service dropped to zero.</p>
<p>Thirdly, conservation programmes for the sake of financial benefit underestimate human ingenuity to invent and replace ecosystem services by artificial means. McCauley argues that such proposals are deemed to have a short lifespan as the history of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> is about how Humanity developed <a href="/wiki/Artificial" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial">artificial</a> alternatives to nature’s services and with time passing the cost of such services tend to decrease. This would also lead to the devaluation of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>.</p>
<p>Lastly, it should not be assumed that conserving ecosystems is always financially beneficial as opposed to alteration. In the case of the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Nile_perch" title="Nile perch">Nile perch</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a>, the ecological consequence was decimation of native <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>. However, this same event is praised by the local communities as they gain significant financial benefits from trading the fish.</p>
<p>McCauley argues that, for these reasons, trying to convince decision-makers to conserve nature for <a href="/wiki/Monetary" class="mw-redirect" title="Monetary">monetary</a> reasons is not the path to be followed, and instead appealing to morality is the ultimate way to campaign for the protection of nature.</p>
