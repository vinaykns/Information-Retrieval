<p><b>Romanian Americans</b> (<a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>: <i>român american</i>) are <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a> who have <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanian</a> ancestry. According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census,_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Census, 2000">2000 US Census</a>, 367,310 Americans indicated Romanian as their first ancestry,<sup id="cite_ref-USCensus_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USCensus-6">[6]</a></sup> while 518,653 persons declared to have Romanian ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-Census_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census-1">[1]</a></sup> Other sources provide higher estimates for the numbers of Romanian Americans in the contemporary US; for example, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://ro-am.net/roam/">Romanian-American Network Inc.</a> supplies a rough estimate of 1.1&#160;million who are fully or partially of Romanian ethnicity.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There is also a significant number of persons of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> ancestry, estimated at about 225,000.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanian_Americans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first Romanian known to have been to what is now the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> was <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Damian" title="Samuel Damian">Samuel Damian</a> (also spelled Domien), a priest.<sup id="cite_ref-bookref1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bookref1-9">[9]</a></sup> Samuel Damian's name appears as far back as 1748, when he placed an advertisement in the <i><a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Gazette" title="South Carolina Gazette">South Carolina Gazette</a></i> announcing the electrical demonstrations he planned to give and inviting the public to attend. Letters written in 1753 and 1755 by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> attest to the fact that the two had met and had carried on discussions concerning electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-bookref1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bookref1-9">[9]</a></sup> Damian remained in the States some years living in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, then travelled on to Jamaica and disappeared from historical record.<sup id="cite_ref-history_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were several Romanian generals who became ranking officers in the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, Brigadier general <a href="/wiki/George_Pomutz" title="George Pomutz">George Pomutz</a>, Commander of the 15th Iowa Regiment, Captain <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Dunca" title="Nicolae Dunca">Nicolae Dunca</a>, who fought in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cross_Keys" title="Battle of Cross Keys">Battle of Cross Keys</a> in Virginia. Another Romanian soldier, Eugen Teodoresco, fought in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> in 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-history_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first major wave of Romanian immigrants to the United States took place between 1895 and 1920, in which 145,000 Romanians entered the country. They came from various regions in <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a>, Transylvania and neighboring countries such as Ukraine and Serbia with significant Romanian population.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The majority of these immigrants particularly those from <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banat" title="Banat">Banat</a> that were under <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian</a> rule left their native regions because of economic depression and forced assimilation, a policy practiced by Hungarian rulers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>They settled mostly in the industrial centers in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> as well as in areas around the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>. The migrants from the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Old_Kingdom" title="Romanian Old Kingdom">Romanian Old Kingdom</a> were mostly Jews, most of whom settled in New York. One of their prominent organizations was the United Rumanian Jews of America. 75,000 Romanian Jews emigrated in the period 1881–1914, mostly to the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a>, the number of ethnic Romanians who migrated to the US decreased as a consequence of the economic development in Romania, but the number of Jews who migrated to the US increased, mostly after the rise of the fascist <a href="/wiki/Iron_Guard" title="Iron Guard">Iron Guard</a>.</p>
<p>After the Second World War, the number of Romanians who migrated to the United States increased again. This time, they settled mostly in California, Florida and New York and they came from throughout Romania.</p>
<p>Over 53% of all foreign-born Romanian Americans came to the US after 1980. Some sources supply estimates of particular Romanian American community populations which are considerably higher than the most recently available U.S. census count. The estimated numbers depend on the reliability of the estimation method used and how membership of the Romanian American community is defined.</p>
<p>In the 2000 United States census, 340,000 Americans of age 5 years and older (or 0.11% of the total US population) were identified as speakers of <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, ranking it 21st among languages spoken in the US.</p>

<p>Romanian Americans are distributed throughout the U.S., with concentrations found in the Midwest, such as in the states of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, while in the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeast</a>, communities are found in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Metro_Atlanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Metro Atlanta">Metro Atlanta</a>), <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> (<a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> (<a href="/wiki/Montgomery_metropolitan_area" title="Montgomery metropolitan area">Montgomery</a>). There are also significant communities of Romanian Americans in the far east and west of the United States, particularly in <a href="/wiki/New_York_metropolitan_area" title="New York metropolitan area">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> (<a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_metropolitan_area" title="Los Angeles metropolitan area">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_metropolitan_area" title="Sacramento metropolitan area">Sacramento</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanian_American_culture">Romanian American culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Romanian_Americans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Romanian American culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Romanian_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanian culture">Romanian culture</a> has merged with American culture, characterized by <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a>-born Americans adopting American culture or American-born people having strong Romanian heritage.</p>
<p>The Romanian culture can be seen in many different kinds, like Romanian music, newspapers, churches, cultural organizations and groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Romanian-American_Congress" title="Romanian-American Congress">Romanian-American Congress</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Round_Table_Society_NFP" class="mw-redirect" title="Round Table Society NFP">Round Table Society NFP</a>. Religion, predominantly within the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox_Church" title="Romanian Orthodox Church">Romanian Orthodox Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Romanian Greek Catholic Church">Romanian Greek Catholic Church</a>, is an important trace of the Romanian presence in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with churches in almost all bigger cities throughout the country. One of the best known foods of Romanian origin is <a href="/wiki/Pastrama" class="mw-redirect" title="Pastrama">Pastrama</a>.</p>

<td class="navbox-abovebelow" colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">
<div><sup>1</sup> Poles came to the United States legally as Austrians, Germans, Prussians or Russians throughout the 19th century, because from 1772–1795 till 1918, all Polish lands <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">had been partitioned</a> between imperial Austria, Prussia (a protoplast of Germany) and Russia until Poland regained its sovereignty in the wake of World War I.
<p><sup>2</sup> Russia is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_transcontinental_countries" title="List of transcontinental countries">transcontinental country</a> in Eastern Europe and Northern Asia. The vast majority of its population (80%) lives in <a href="/wiki/European_Russia" title="European Russia">European Russia</a>, therefore Russia as a whole is included as a European country here.</p>
<p><sup>3</sup> <a href="/wiki/Yugoslav_Americans" title="Yugoslav Americans">Yugoslav Americans</a> are the American people from the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.</p>
<p><sup>4</sup> <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> is a transcontinental country in the Middle East and Southeast Europe. Has a small part of its territory (3%) in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Europe" title="Southeast Europe">Southeast Europe</a> called <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Thrace" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkish Thrace">Turkish Thrace</a>.</p>
<p><sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> are transcontinental countries. They have a small part of their territories in the European part of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>.</p>
<p><sup>6</sup> <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> is technically a <a href="/wiki/Bicontinental" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicontinental">bicontinental</a> country, having a small portion in European hands.</p>
<sup>7</sup> Disputed; <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Roma</a> have recognized origins and historic ties to Asia (specifically to Northern India), but they experienced at least some distinctive identity development while in <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> among Europeans.</div>
