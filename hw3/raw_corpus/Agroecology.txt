			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Agroecology</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Agroecology</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Applied_ecology" title="Applied ecology">ecological processes applied</a> to agricultural production systems. The prefix <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/agro-" class="extiw" title="wikt:agro-">agro-</a></i> refers to <i><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a></i>. Bringing <a href="/wiki/Ecology#Fundamental_principles_of_ecology" title="Ecology">ecological principles</a> to bear in <a href="/wiki/Agroecosystem" title="Agroecosystem">agroecosystems</a> can suggest novel management approaches that would not otherwise be considered. The term is often used imprecisely and may refer to "a science, a movement, [or] a practice".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Agroecologists study a variety of agroecosystems, and the field of agroecology is not associated with any one particular method of <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a>, whether it be <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_farming" title="Integrated farming">integrated</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial agriculture">conventional</a>; <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Extensive_farming" title="Extensive farming">extensive</a>. Although it has much more common thinking and principles with some of the before mentioned farming systems.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_strategy">Ecological strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ecological strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agroecologists do not unanimously oppose technology or inputs in agriculture but instead assess how, when, and if technology can be used in conjunction with natural, social and human assets.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Agroecology proposes a context- or site-specific manner of studying agroecosystems, and as such, it recognizes that there is no universal formula or recipe for the success and maximum well-being of an agroecosystem. Thus, agroecology is not defined by certain management practices, such as the use of <a href="/wiki/Biological_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological control">natural enemies</a> in place of <a href="/wiki/Insecticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Insecticides">insecticides</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a> in place of <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a>.</p>
<p>Instead, agroecologists may study questions related to the four system properties of agroecosystems: <a href="/wiki/Ecology#Ecosystem_productivity" title="Ecology">productivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_stability" title="Ecological stability">stability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and equitability.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As opposed to disciplines that are concerned with only one or some of these properties, agroecologists see all four properties as interconnected and integral to the success of an agroecosystem. Recognizing that these properties are found on varying spatial scales, agroecologists do not limit themselves to the study of agroecosystems at any one scale: gene-organism-population-community-ecosystem-landscape-biome, field-farm-community-region-state-country-continent-global.</p>
<p>Agroecologists study these four properties through an interdisciplinary lens, using natural sciences to understand elements of agroecosystems such as soil properties and plant-insect interactions, as well as using social sciences to understand the effects of farming practices on rural communities, economic constraints to developing new production methods, or cultural factors determining farming practices.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches">Approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agroecologists do not always agree about what agroecology is or should be in the long-term. Different definitions of the term agroecology can be distinguished largely by the specificity with which one defines the term "ecology", as well as the term's potential political connotations. Definitions of agroecology, therefore, may be first grouped according to the specific contexts within which they situate agriculture. Agroecology is defined by the <a href="/wiki/OECD" class="mw-redirect" title="OECD">OECD</a> as "the study of the relation of agricultural crops and environment."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This definition refers to the "-ecology" part of "agroecology" narrowly as the natural environment. Following this definition, an agroecologist would study agriculture's various relationships with soil health, water quality, air quality, meso- and micro-fauna, surrounding flora, environmental toxins, and other environmental contexts.</p>
<p>A more common definition of the word can be taken from Dalgaard et al., who refer to agroecology as the study of the interactions between plants, animals, humans and the environment within agricultural systems. Consequently, agroecology is inherently multidisciplinary, including factors from <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and related disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-dalgaard_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalgaard-6">[6]</a></sup> In this case, the "-ecology" portion of "agroecology is defined broadly to include social, cultural, and economic contexts as well. Francis et al. also expand the definition in the same way, but put more emphasis on the notion of food systems.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agroecology is also defined differently according to geographic location. In the global south, the term often carries overtly political connotations. Such political definitions of the term usually ascribe to it the goals of social and economic justice; special attention, in this case, is often paid to the traditional farming knowledge of indigenous populations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> North American and European uses of the term sometimes avoid the inclusion of such overtly political goals. In these cases, agroecology is seen more strictly as a scientific discipline with less specific social goals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agro-population_ecology">Agro-population ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agro-population ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This approach is derived from the science of ecology primarily based on <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology" title="Population ecology">population ecology</a>, which over the past three decades has been displacing the ecosystems biology of <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Odum</a>. Buttel explains the main difference between the two categories, saying that "the application of population ecology to agroecology involves the primacy not only of analyzing agroecosystems from the perspective of the <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a> of their constituent species, and their relationships to <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemistry" title="Biogeochemistry">biogeochemistry</a>, but also there is a major emphasis placed on the role of <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-buttel_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buttel-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_agroecology">Indigenous agroecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Indigenous agroecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This concept was proposed by political ecologist Josep Garí to recognise and uphold the integrated agro-ecological practices of many indigenous peoples, who simultaneously and sustainably safeguard, manage and use ecosystems for agricultural, food, biodiversity and cultural purposes at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Indigenous agroecologies are not systems and practices halted in time, but keep co-evolving with new knowledge and resources, such as that provided by development projects, research initiatives and agro-biodiversity exchanges. In fact, the first agro-ecologists were indigenous peoples that advocated development policies and programmes to support their systems, rather than replacing them.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inclusive_agroecology">Inclusive agroecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Inclusive agroecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rather than viewing agroecology as a subset of agriculture, Wojtkowski<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> takes a more encompassing perspective. In this, natural ecology and agroecology are the major headings under ecology. Natural ecology is the study of organisms as they interact with and within natural environments. Correspondingly, agroecology is the basis for the land-use sciences. Here humans are the primary governing force for organisms within planned and managed, mostly terrestrial, environments.</p>
<p>As key headings, natural ecology and agroecology provide the theoretical base for their respective sciences. These theoretical bases overlap but differ in a major way. Economics has no role in the functioning of natural ecosystems whereas economics sets direction and purpose in agroecology.</p>
<p>Under agroecology are the three land-use sciences, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a>. Although these use their plant components in different ways, they share the same theoretical core.</p>
<p>Beyond this, the land-use sciences further subdivide. The subheadings include agronomy, <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>, traditional agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a>. Within this system of subdivisions, agroecology is philosophically neutral. The importance lies in providing a theoretical base hitherto lacking in the land-use sciences. This allows progress in biocomplex agroecosystems including the multi-species plantations of forestry and agroforestry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To arrive at a point of view about a particular way of farming, an agroecologist would first seek to understand the contexts in which the farm(s) is(are) involved. Each farm may be inserted in a unique combination of factors or contexts. Each farmer may have their own premises about the meanings of an agricultural endeavor, and these meanings might be different from those of agroecologists. Generally, farmers seek a configuration that is viable in multiple contexts, such as family, financial, technical, political, logistical, market, environmental, spiritual. Agroecologists want to understand the behavior of those who seek livelihoods from plant and animal increase, acknowledging the organization and planning that is required to run a farm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_organic_and_non-organic_milk_production">Views on organic and non-organic milk production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Views on organic and non-organic milk production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because <a href="/wiki/Organic_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic agriculture">organic agriculture</a> proclaims to sustain the health of soils, ecosystems, and people,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> it has much in common with Agroecology;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> this does not mean that Agroecology is synonymous with organic agriculture, nor that Agroecology views organic farming as the 'right' way of farming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Also, it is important to point out that there are large differences in <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic standards</a> among countries and certifying agencies.</p>
<p>Three of the main areas that agroecologists would look at in farms, would be: the environmental impacts, animal welfare issues, and the social aspects.</p>
<p>Environmental impacts caused by organic and non-organic milk production can vary significantly. For both cases, there are positive and negative environmental consequences.</p>
<p>Compared to conventional milk production, <a href="/wiki/Organic_milk" title="Organic milk">organic milk</a> production tends to have lower <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> potential per ton of milk or per hectare of farmland, because it potentially reduces leaching of nitrates (NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>) and phosphates (PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>) due to lower fertilizer application rates. Because organic milk production reduces pesticides utilization, it increases land use per ton of milk due to decreased crop yields per hectare. Mainly due to the lower level of concentrates given to cows in organic herds, organic dairy farms generally produce less milk per cow than conventional dairy farms. Because of the increased use of roughage and the, on-average, lower milk production level per cow, some research has connected organic milk production with increases in the emission of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">Animal welfare</a> issues vary among dairy farms and are not necessarily related to the way of producing milk (organically or conventionally).</p>
<p>A key component of animal welfare is freedom to perform their innate (natural) behavior, and this is stated in one of the basic principles of organic agriculture. Also, there are other aspects of animal welfare to be considered – such as freedom from hunger, thirst, discomfort, injury, fear, distress, disease and pain. Because organic standards require loose housing systems, adequate bedding, restrictions on the area of slatted floors, a minimum <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> proportion in the <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminant</a> diets, and tend to limit stocking densities both on <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> and in housing for dairy cows, they potentially promote good foot and hoof health. Some studies show lower incidence of placenta retention, milk fever, abomasums displacement and other diseases in organic than in conventional dairy herds.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, the level of infections by parasites in organically managed herds is generally higher than in conventional herds.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Social aspects of dairy enterprises include life quality of farmers, of farm labor, of rural and urban communities, and also includes public health.</p>
<p>Both organic and non-organic farms can have good and bad implications for the <a href="/wiki/Life_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Life quality">life quality</a> of all the different people involved in that <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. Issues like labor conditions, labor hours and labor rights, for instance, do not depend on the organic/non-organic characteristic of the farm; they can be more related to the socio-economical and cultural situations in which the farm is inserted, instead.</p>
<p>As for the public health or <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">food safety</a> concern, <a href="/wiki/Organic_foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic foods">organic foods</a> are intended to be healthy, free of contaminations and free from agents that could cause human diseases. Organic milk is meant to have no chemical residues to consumers, and the restrictions on the use of antibiotics and chemicals in organic food production has the purpose to accomplish this goal. Although dairy cows in both organic and conventional farming practices can be exposed to pathogens, it has been shown that, because antibiotics are not permitted as a preventative measure in organic practices, there are far fewer antibiotic resistant pathogens on organic farms. This dramatically increases the efficacy of antibiotics when/if they are necessary.</p>
<p>In an organic dairy farm, an agroecologist could evaluate the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>Can the farm minimize environmental impacts and increase its level of sustainability, for instance by efficiently increasing the <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> of the animals to minimize <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a> of feed and of land use?</li>
<li>Are there ways to improve the <a href="/wiki/Animal_health" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal health">health status</a> of the herd (in the case of organics, by using <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological controls</a>, for instance)?</li>
<li>Does this way of farming sustain good <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmers</a>, their families, <a href="/wiki/Farmworker" title="Farmworker">rural labor</a> and communities involved?</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_no-till_farming">Views on no-till farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Views on no-till farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">No-tillage</a> is one of the components of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_agriculture" title="Conservation agriculture">conservation agriculture</a> practices and is considered more environmental friendly than complete <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Garcia-Torres_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia-Torres-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There is a general consensus that no-till can increase soils capacity of acting as a carbon sink, especially when combined with cover crops.<sup id="cite_ref-Garcia-Torres_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garcia-Torres-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>No-till can contribute to higher <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">soil organic matter</a> and organic carbon content in soils,<sup id="cite_ref-Calegari_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calegari-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> though reports of no-effects of no-tillage in organic matter and organic carbon soil contents also exist, depending on environmental and crop conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition, no-till can indirectly reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by decreasing the use of fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-Calegari_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calegari-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most crops can benefit from the practice of no-till, but not all crops are suitable for complete no-till agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-Fleizo_et_al._2002_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fleizo_et_al._2002-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Koller_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koller-26">[26]</a></sup> Crops that do not perform well when competing with other plants that grow in untilled soil in their early stages can be best grown by using other conservation tillage practices, like a combination of <a href="/wiki/Strip-till" title="Strip-till">strip-till</a> with no-till areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Koller_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koller-26">[26]</a></sup> Also, crops which harvestable portion grows underground can have better results with strip-tillage,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> mainly in soils which are hard for plant roots to penetrate into deeper layers to access water and nutrients.</p>
<p>The benefits provided by no-tillage to predators may lead to larger predator populations,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> which is a good way to control pests (biological control), but also can facilitate predation of the crop itself. In corn crops, for instance, predation by caterpillars can be higher in no-till than in conventional tillage fields.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In places with rigorous winter, untilled soil can take longer to warm and dry in spring, which may delay planting to less ideal dates.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Another factor to be considered is that organic residue from the prior year's crops lying on the surface of untilled fields can provide a favorable environment to <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>, helping to increase the risk of transmitting diseases to the future crop. And because no-till farming provides good environment for pathogens, insects and weeds, it can lead farmers to a more intensive use of chemicals for <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">pest control</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other disadvantages of no-till include underground rot, low soil temperatures and high moisture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Based on the balance of these factors, and because each farm has different problems, agroecologists will not atest that only no-till or complete tillage is the right way of farming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Yet, these are not the only possible choices regarding soil preparation, since there are intermediate practices such as strip-till, <a href="/wiki/Mulch-till" title="Mulch-till">mulch-till</a> and ridge-till, all of them – just as no-till – categorized as conservation tillage. Agroecologists, then, will evaluate the need of different practices for the contexts in which each farm is inserted.</p>
<p>In a no-till system, an agroecologist could ask the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>Can the farm minimize environmental impacts and increase its level of sustainability; for instance by efficiently increasing the productivity of the crops to minimize land use?</li>
<li>Does this way of farming sustain good quality of life for the farmers, their families, rural labor and <a href="/wiki/Communities" class="mw-redirect" title="Communities">rural communities</a> involved?</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-WWII">Pre-WWII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pre-WWII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The notions and ideas relating to crop ecology have been around since at least 1911 when <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Hiram_King" title="Franklin Hiram King">F.H. King</a> released <i>Farmers of Forty Centuries</i>. King was one of the pioneers as a proponent of more quantitative methods for characterization of water relations and physical properties of soils.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup> In the late 1920s the attempt to merge agronomy and ecology was born with the development of the field of crop ecology. Crop ecology's main concern was where crops would be best grown.<sup id="cite_ref-gliess_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-31">[31]</a></sup> Actually, it was only in 1928 that agronomy and ecology were formally linked by Klages.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first mention of the term agroecology was in 1928, with the publication of the term by Bensin in 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-wezel_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wezel-33">[33]</a></sup> The book of Tischler (1965), was probably the first to be actually titled 'agroecology'.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> He analysed the different components (plants, animals, soils and climate) and their interactions within an agroecosystem as well as the impact of human <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural management">agricultural management</a> on these components. Other books dealing with agroecology, but without using the term explicitly were published by the German zoologist Friederichs (1930) with his book on agricultural zoology and related ecological/environmental factors for plant protection,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and by American crop physiologist Hansen in 1939<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> when both used the word as a synonym for the application of ecology within agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-dalgaard_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalgaard-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This sentence seems self-contradictory. I.e., did they use the term or not? (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-WWII">Post-WWII</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Post-WWII">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gliessman mentions that post-WWII, groups of scientists with ecologists gave more focus to experiments in the natural environment, while <a href="/wiki/Agronomist" class="mw-redirect" title="Agronomist">agronomists</a> dedicated their attention to the cultivated systems in agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-gliess_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-31">[31]</a></sup> According to Gliessman,<sup id="cite_ref-gliess_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-31">[31]</a></sup> the two groups kept their research and interest apart until books and articles using the concept of agroecosystems and the word agroecology started to appear in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-wezel_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wezel-33">[33]</a></sup> Dalgaard<sup id="cite_ref-dalgaard_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalgaard-6">[6]</a></sup> explains the different points of view in ecology schools, and the fundamental differences, which set the basis for the development of agroecology. The early ecology school of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gleason" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Gleason">Henry Gleason</a> investigated plant populations focusing in the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchical" class="mw-redirect" title="Hierarchical">hierarchical</a> levels of the organism under study.</p>
<p>Friederich Clement's ecology school, however included the organism in question as well as the higher hierarchical levels in its investigations, a "landscape perspective". However, the ecological schools where the roots of agroecology lie are even broader in nature. The ecology school of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tansley" title="Arthur Tansley">Tansley</a>, whose view included both the biotic organism and their environment, is the one from which the concept of agroecosystems emerged in 1974 with Harper.<sup id="cite_ref-dalgaard_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalgaard-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s and 1970s the increasing awareness of how humans manage the landscape and its consequences set the stage for the necessary cross between agronomy and ecology. Even though, in many ways the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> in the US was a product of the times, the <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Green Decade,</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> spread an environmental awareness of the <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequence" class="mw-redirect" title="Unintended consequence">unintended consequences</a> of changing ecological processes. Works such as <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>, and changes in legislation such as the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a> caused the public to be aware of societal growth patterns, agricultural production, and the overall capacity of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-dalgaard_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dalgaard-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fusion_with_ecology">Fusion with ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fusion with ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the 1970s, when agronomists saw the value of ecology and ecologists began to use the agricultural systems as study plots, studies in agroecology grew more rapidly.<sup id="cite_ref-gliess_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-31">[31]</a></sup> Gliessman describes that the innovative work of Prof. Efraim Hernandez X., who developed research based on <a href="/wiki/Traditional_knowledge" title="Traditional knowledge">indigenous systems of knowledge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, led to education programs in agroecology.<sup id="cite_ref-qFran_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qFran-38">[38]</a></sup> In 1977 Prof. Efraim Hernandez X. explained that modern agricultural systems had lost their ecological foundation when <a href="/wiki/Socio-economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Socio-economic">socio-economic</a> factors became the only driving force in the <a href="/wiki/Food_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Food system">food system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup> The acknowledgement that the socio-economic interactions are indeed one of the fundamental components of any agroecosystems came to light in 1982, with the article Agroecologia del Tropico Americano by Montaldo. The author argues that the socio-economic context cannot be separated from the agricultural systems when designing agricultural practices.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1995 Edens et al. in Sustainable Agriculture and Integrated Farming Systems solidified this idea proving his point by devoting special sections to economics of the systems, ecological impacts, and ethics and values in agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup> Actually, 1985 ended up being a fertile and creative year for the new discipline. For instance in the same year, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Altieri" title="Miguel Altieri">Miguel Altieri</a> integrated how consolidation of the farms, and cropping systems impact pest populations. In addition, Gliessman highlighted that socio-economic, technological, and ecological components give rise to producer choices of food production systems.<sup id="cite_ref-francis_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francis-7">[7]</a></sup> These pioneering agroecologists have helped to frame the foundation of what we today consider the interdisciplinary field of agroecology and have led to advances in a number of farming systems. In Asian rice, for example, crop diversification by growing flowering crops in strips beside rice fields has recently been demonstrated to reduce pests so effectively (by the flower nectar attracting and supporting parasitoids and predators) that insecticide spraying is reduced by 70%, yields increase by 5%, together resulting in an economic advantage of 7.5% <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nature.com/articles/nplants201614">(Gurr et al., 2016)</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_region">By region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: By region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principles of agroecology are expressed differently depending on local ecological and social contexts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agroecology_in_Latin_America" title="Agroecology in Latin America">Agroecology in Latin America</a></div>
<p>Latin America's experiences with North American Green Revolution agricultural techniques have opened space for agroecologists. Traditional or <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_knowledge" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous knowledge">indigenous knowledge</a> represents a wealth of possibility for agroecologists, including "exchange of wisdoms". See Miguel Alteiri's <i>Enhancing the Productivity of Latin American Traditional Peasant Farming Systems Through an Agroecological Approach</i> for information on agroecology in Latin America.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, agroecology has had low traction in Africa, as governments, international organisations, extension services and farmers' organisations tended to focus on issues of inputs and outputs as the defining factors to deal with recurrent food crises and chronic malnutrition in the continent. Agrocecology was only a minor proposal from a few, non-governmental, small-scale projects and a sort of "experimental" idea of the Farmer Field Schools programme.</p>
<p>In the early 2000s, when the AIDS pandemic was creating a major rural crisis across Africa, Josep Garí proposed FAO to consider an agroecological approach as the most effective way to empower farmers cope with the impacts of the AIDS pandemic on agriculture and food production: in particular, he proposed agro-biodiversity as a key resource and knowledge for farmers to address the labour and malnutrition crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The proposal was rapidly adopted by the Farmer Field Schools scheme across the world, and even presented and translated in China.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most recently, agroecology has started to permeate projects and discourses on farming and natural-resource management in Africa. In 2011, the 1st encounter of agroecology trainers took place in Zimbabwe and issued the Shashe Declaration.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Madagascar">Madagascar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agroecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Madagascar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agroecology_in_Madagascar" title="Agroecology in Madagascar">Agroecology in Madagascar</a></div>
<p>Most of the historical farming in Madagascar has been conducted by indigenous peoples. The French <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonial</a> period disturbed a very small percentage of land area, and even included some useful experiments in <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_forestry" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable forestry">Sustainable forestry</a>. <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">Slash-and-burn</a> techniques, a component of some <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a> systems have been practised by natives in Madagascar for centuries. As of 2006 some of the major agricultural products from slash-and-burn methods are wood, charcoal and grass for <a href="/wiki/Zebu" title="Zebu">Zebu</a> grazing. These practices have taken perhaps the greatest toll on land fertility since the end of French rule, mainly due to <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a> pressures.</p>
