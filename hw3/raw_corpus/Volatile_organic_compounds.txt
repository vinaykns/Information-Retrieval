			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Volatile organic compound</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compounds&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Volatile organic compounds">Volatile organic compounds</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Volatile organic compounds</b> (<b>VOCs</b>) are <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic chemicals</a> that have a high <a href="/wiki/Vapour_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Vapour pressure">vapor pressure</a> at ordinary <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>. Their high vapor pressure results from a low boiling point, which causes large numbers of molecules to <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimate</a> from the liquid or solid form of the compound and enter the surrounding air, a trait known as <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatility</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, which evaporates from <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a>, has a boiling point of only –19&#160;°C (–2&#160;°F).</p>
<p>VOCs are numerous, varied, and ubiquitous. They include both human-made and naturally occurring chemical compounds. Most <a href="/wiki/Odour" class="mw-redirect" title="Odour">scents or odors</a> are of VOCs. VOCs play an important role in communication between plants,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and messages from plants to animals. Some VOCs are dangerous to human health or cause harm to the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_behaviour" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic behaviour">Anthropogenic</a> VOCs are regulated by law, especially indoors, where concentrations are the highest. Harmful VOCs typically are not acutely <a href="/wiki/Toxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic">toxic</a>, but have compounding long-term health effects. Because the concentrations are usually low and the symptoms slow to develop, research into VOCs and their effects is difficult.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diverse definitions of the term VOC are in use.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The definitions of VOCs used for control of precursors of photochemical smog used by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) and state agencies in the US with independent outdoor air pollution regulations include exemptions for VOCs that are determined to be non-reactive, or of low-reactivity in the smog formation process.</p>
<p>In the US, regulatory requirements for VOCs vary among the states. Most prominent is the VOC regulation issued by the <a href="/wiki/South_Coast_Air_Quality_Management_District" title="South Coast Air Quality Management District">South Coast Air Quality Management District</a> in California and by the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> (ARB).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, this specific use of the term VOCs can be misleading, especially when applied to <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">indoor air quality</a> because many chemicals that are not regulated as outdoor air pollution can still be important for indoor air pollution.</p>
<p>California's ARB uses the term "reactive organic gases" (ROG) to measure organic gases after public hearing in September 1995. The ARB revised the definition of "Volatile Organic Compounds" used in the consumer products regulations, based on their committee's findings.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Health_Canada" title="Health Canada">Health Canada</a> classes VOCs as organic compounds that have boiling points roughly in the range of 50 to 250&#160;°C (122 to 482&#160;°F). The emphasis is placed on commonly encountered VOCs that would have an effect on air quality.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> defines a VOC as "any organic compound having an initial boiling point less than or equal to 250&#160;°C (482&#160;°F) measured at a standard atmospheric pressure of 101.3 kPa." The VOC Solvents Emissions Directive is the main policy instrument for the reduction of industrial emissions of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) in the European Union. It covers a wide range of solvent using activities, e.g. printing, surface cleaning, vehicle coating, dry cleaning and manufacture of footwear and pharmaceutical products. The VOC Solvents Emissions Directive requires installations in which such activities are applied to comply either with the emission limit values set out in the Directive or with the requirements of the so-called reduction scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<a href="/wiki/Thermal_oxidiser" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal oxidiser">Thermal oxidizers</a> provide an air pollution abatement option for VOCs from industrial air flows.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div>

<p>VOCs (or specific subsets of the VOCs) are legally defined in the various laws and codes under which they are regulated. Other definitions may be found from government agencies investigating or advising about VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> EPA regulates VOCs in the air, water, and land. The federal regulations issued under the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a> list several organic compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> EPA also publishes testing methods for chemical compounds, including a range of VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to drinking water, VOCs are regulated in pollutant discharges to surface waters (both directly and via sewage treatment plants),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as hazardous waste,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but not in non-industrial indoor air.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-indoor-VOC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-indoor-VOC-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) regulates VOC exposure in the workplace. Volatile organic compounds that are classified as <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Hazardous material">hazardous materials</a> are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_and_Hazardous_Materials_Safety_Administration" title="Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration">Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration</a> while being transported.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biologically_generated_VOCs">Biologically generated VOCs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biologically generated VOCs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Not counting <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, biological sources emit an estimated 1150 <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(mass)#106_to_1011_kg" title="Orders of magnitude (mass)">teragrams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> per year in the form of VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-unexplored_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unexplored-14">[14]</a></sup> The majority of VOCs are produced by plants, the main compound being <a href="/wiki/Isoprene" title="Isoprene">isoprene</a>. The remainder are produced by animals, microbes, and <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Molds" class="mw-redirect" title="Molds">molds</a>.</p>
<p>The strong odor emitted by many plants consists of <a href="/wiki/Green_leaf_volatiles" title="Green leaf volatiles">green leaf volatiles</a>, a subset of VOCs. Emissions are affected by a variety of factors, such as temperature, which determines rates of volatilization and growth, and sunlight, which determines rates of <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>. Emission occurs almost exclusively from the leaves, the <a href="/wiki/Stomata" class="mw-redirect" title="Stomata">stomata</a> in particular. A major class of VOCs is <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Myrcene" title="Myrcene">myrcene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Providing a sense of scale, a forest 62,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> in area (the US state of Pennsylvania) is estimated to emit 3,400,000 kilograms of terpenes on a typical August day during the growing season.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> VOCs should be a factor in choosing which trees to plant in urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Induction of genes producing volatile organic compounds, and subsequent increase in volatile terpenes has been achieved in maize using (Z)-3-Hexen-1-ol and other plant hormones.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_sources">Anthropogenic sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic">Anthropogenic</a> sources emit about 142 <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(mass)#106_to_1011_kg" title="Orders of magnitude (mass)">teragrams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> per year in the form of VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-unexplored_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unexplored-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_components">Specific components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specific components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Paints_and_coatings">Paints and coatings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Paints and coatings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A major source of man-made VOCs are coatings, especially paints and protective coatings. Solvents are required to spread a protective or decorative film. Approximately 12 billion litres of paints are produced annually. Typical solvents are aliphatic hydrocarbons, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_acetate" title="Ethyl acetate">ethyl acetate</a>, glycol ethers, and <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>. Motivated by cost, environmental concerns, and regulation, the paint and coating industries are increasingly shifting toward <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solvents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chlorofluorocarbons_and_chlorocarbons">Chlorofluorocarbons and chlorocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chlorofluorocarbons and chlorocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">Chlorofluorocarbons</a>, which are banned or highly regulated, were widely used cleaning products and refrigerants. Tetrachloroethene is used widely in dry cleaning and by industry.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuels">Fossil fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fossil fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> produces VOC's either directly as products (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>) or indirectly as byproducts (e.g., automobile <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gas</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Benzene">Benzene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Benzene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">Benzene</a></div>
<p>One VOC that is a known human carcinogen is benzene, which is a chemical found in environmental tobacco smoke, stored fuels, and exhaust from cars. Benzene also has natural sources such as volcanoes and forest fires. It is frequently used to make other chemicals in the production of plastics, resins, and synthetic fibers. Benzene evaporates into the air quickly and the vapor of benzene is heavier than air allowing the compound to sink into low-lying areas. Benzene has also been known to contaminate food and water and if digested can lead to vomiting, dizziness, sleepiness, rapid heartbeat, and at high levels, even death may occur.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Methylene_chloride">Methylene chloride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Methylene chloride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methylene_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Methylene chloride">Methylene chloride</a> can be found in adhesive removers and aerosol spray paints. In the human body, methylene chloride is metabolized to carbon monoxide. If a product that contains methylene chloride needs to be used the best way to protect human health is to use the product outdoors. If it must be used indoors, proper ventilation will help to keep exposure levels down.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the United States, methylene chloride is listed as exempt from VOC status.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Perchloroethylene">Perchloroethylene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Perchloroethylene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Perchloroethylene" class="mw-redirect" title="Perchloroethylene">Perchloroethylene</a> is a volatile organic compound that has been linked to causing cancer in animals. It is also suspected to cause many of the breathing related symptoms of exposure to VOCs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Perchloroethylene is used mostly in dry cleaning. While dry cleaners recapture perchloroethylene in the dry cleaning process to reuse it, some environmental release is unavoidable.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="MTBE">MTBE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: MTBE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/MTBE" class="mw-redirect" title="MTBE">MTBE</a> was banned in certain states within the US around 2004 in order to limit further contamination of drinking water <a href="/wiki/Aquifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>) primarily from leaking underground gasoline storage tanks where MTBE was used as an octane booster and oxygenated-additive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formaldehyde">Formaldehyde</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Formaldehyde">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many building materials such as paints, adhesives, wall boards, and ceiling tiles slowly emit <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, which irritates the mucous membranes and can make a person irritated and uncomfortable.<sup id="cite_ref-Bernstein2008_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein2008-21">[21]</a></sup> Formaldehyde emissions from wood are in the range of 0.02–0.04 ppm. Relative humidity within an indoor environment can also affect the emissions of formaldehyde. High relative humidity and high temperatures allow more vaporization of formaldehyde from wood-materials.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_air">Indoor air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Indoor air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">Indoor air quality</a></div>
<p>Since many people spend much of their time indoors, long-term exposure to VOCs in the indoor environment can contribute to <a href="/wiki/Sick_building_syndrome" title="Sick building syndrome">sick building syndrome</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang.2C_S._2007_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang.2C_S._2007-23">[23]</a></sup> In offices, VOC results from new furnishings, wall coverings, and office equipment such as photocopy machines, which can <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">off-gas</a> VOCs into the air.<sup id="cite_ref-Yu1998_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yu1998-24">[24]</a></sup> Good ventilation and air-conditioning systems are helpful at reducing VOCs in the indoor environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Yu1998_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yu1998-24">[24]</a></sup> Studies also show that relative <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma" title="Lymphoma">lymphoma</a> can increase through prolonged exposure of VOCs in the indoor environment.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, there are two standardized methods for measuring VOCs, one by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) and another by OSHA. Each method uses a single component solvent; <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> cannot be sampled, however, on the same sample matrix using the NIOSH or OSHA method.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The aromatic VOC compound <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, emitted from exhaled cigarette smoke, is labeled as carcinogenic and is ten times higher in smokers than in nonsmokers.<sup id="cite_ref-Dales2008_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dales2008-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>EPA has found concentrations of VOCs in indoor air to be 2 to 5 times greater than in outdoor air and sometimes far greater.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-indoor-VOC_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-indoor-VOC-13">[13]</a></sup> During certain activities indoor levels of VOCs may reach 1,000 times that of the outside air. Studies have shown that individual VOC emissions by themselves are not that high in an indoor environment, but the indoor total VOC (TVOC) concentrations can be up to five times higher than the VOC outdoor levels.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> New buildings especially, contribute to the highest level of VOC off-gassing in an indoor environment because of the abundant new materials generating VOC particles at the same time in such a short time period.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang.2C_S._2007_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang.2C_S._2007-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition to new buildings, we also use many consumer products that emit VOC compounds, therefore the total concentration of VOC levels is much greater within the indoor environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang.2C_S._2007_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang.2C_S._2007-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>VOC concentration in an indoor environment during winter is three to four times higher than the VOC concentrations during the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> High indoor VOC levels are attributed to the low rates of air exchange between the indoor and outdoor environment as a result of tight-shut windows and the increasing use of <a href="/wiki/Humidifier" title="Humidifier">humidifiers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_air_quality_measurements">Indoor air quality measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Indoor air quality measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Measurement of VOCs from the indoor air is done with sorption tubes e. g. Tenax® (for VOCs and SVOCs) or DNPH-cartridges (for carbonyl-compounds). The VOCs adsorb on these materials and are afterwards desorbed either thermally (Tenax®) or by elution (DNPH) and then analyzed by <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry">GC-MS</a>/<a href="/wiki/Flame_ionization_detector" title="Flame ionization detector">FID</a> or <a href="/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High-performance liquid chromatography">HPLC</a>. Reference gas mixtures are required for quality control of these VOC-measurements.<sup id="cite_ref-KEYVOCs_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEYVOCs-31">[31]</a></sup> Furthermore, VOC emitting products used indoors, e. g. building products and furniture, are investigated in emission test chambers under controlled climatic conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> For quality control of these measurements round robin tests are carried out, therefore reproducibly emitting reference materials are ideally required.<sup id="cite_ref-KEYVOCs_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEYVOCs-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_of_indoor_VOC_emissions">Regulation of indoor VOC emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Regulation of indoor VOC emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In most countries, a separate definition of VOCs is used with regard to indoor air quality that comprises each organic chemical compound that can be measured as follows: Adsorption from air on Tenax TA, thermal desorption, gas chromatographic separation over a 100% nonpolar column (dimethylpolysiloxane). VOC (volatile organic compounds) are all compounds that appear in the gas chromatogram between and including n-hexane and n-hexadecane. Compounds appearing earlier are called VVOC (very volatile organic compounds); compounds appearing later are called SVOC (semi-volatile organic compounds). See also these standards: ISO 16000-6, ISO 13999-2, VDI 4300-6, German <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090818001614/http://www.umweltbundesamt.de/building-products/agbb.htm">AgBB evaluating scheme</a>, German <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.eurofins.com/agbb.aspx">DIBt approval scheme</a>, GEV testing method for the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.eurofins.com/emicode.aspx">EMICODE</a>. Some overviews over VOC emissions rating schemes<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> have been <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.eurofins.com/ecolabels-comparison.aspx">collected and compared</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> have enacted regulations to limit VOC emissions from commercial products, and industry has developed numerous voluntary ecolabels and rating systems, such as EMICODE,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> M1,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Blue Angel<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and Indoor Air Comfort<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, several standards exist; California Standard CDPH Section 01350<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> is the most popular one. Over the last few decades, these regulations and standards changed the marketplace, leading to an increasing number of low-emitting products: The leading voluntary labels report that licenses to several hundreds of low-emitting products have been issued (see the respective webpages such as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.mascertifiedgreen.com/">MAS Certified Green</a>.- Certified Products<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_risks">Health risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Health risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Respiratory, allergic, or immune effects in infants or children are associated with man-made VOCs and other indoor or outdoor air pollutants.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some VOCs, such as <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">limonene</a>, can react with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> or with ozone to produce new oxidation products and secondary aerosols, which can cause sensory irritation symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolkoff2006_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolkoff2006-41">[41]</a></sup> Unspecified VOCs are important in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Photochemical_smog" class="mw-redirect" title="Photochemical smog">smog</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Health effects include eye, nose, and <a href="/wiki/Throat_irritation" title="Throat irritation">throat irritation</a>; headaches, loss of coordination, nausea; and damage to the liver, kidney, and central nervous system. Some organics can cause cancer in animals; some are suspected or known to cause cancer in humans. Key signs or symptoms associated with exposure to VOCs include conjunctival irritation, nose and throat discomfort, headache, allergic skin reaction, <a href="/wiki/Dyspnea" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a>, declines in serum <a href="/wiki/Cholinesterase" title="Cholinesterase">cholinesterase</a> levels, nausea, vomiting, nose bleeding, fatigue, dizziness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The ability of organic chemicals to cause health effects varies greatly from those that are highly toxic, to those with no known health effects. As with other pollutants, the extent and nature of the health effect will depend on many factors including level of exposure and length of time exposed. Eye and respiratory tract irritation, headaches, dizziness, visual disorders, and memory impairment are among the immediate symptoms that some people have experienced soon after exposure to some organics. At present, not much is known about what health effects occur from the levels of organics usually found in homes. Many organic compounds are known to cause cancer in animals; some are suspected of causing, or are known to cause, cancer in humans.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>To reduce exposure to these toxins, one should buy products that contain Low-VOCs or No VOCs. Only the quantity which will soon be needed should be purchased, eliminating stockpiling of these chemicals. Use products with VOCs in well ventilated areas. When designing homes and buildings, design teams can implement the best possible ventilation plans, call for the best mechanical systems available, and design assemblies to reduce the amount of infiltration into the building. These methods will help improve indoor air quality, but by themselves they cannot keep a building from becoming an unhealthy place to breathe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limit_values_for_VOC_emissions">Limit values for VOC emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Limit values for VOC emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.eurofins.com/lci-cli-nik-crel.aspx">Limit values for VOC emissions into indoor air</a> are published by e.g. AgBB, AFSSET, California Department of Public Health, and others. These regulations have prompted several companies to adapt with VOC level reductions in products that have VOCs in their formula, such as BEHR, KILZ, and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Moore_%26_Co." title="Benjamin Moore &amp; Co.">Benjamin Moore &amp; Co.</a> in the paint industry and <a href="/wiki/Weld-On" title="Weld-On">Weld-On</a> in the adhesive industry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_fingerprinting">Chemical fingerprinting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chemical fingerprinting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exhaled human breath contains a few hundred volatile organic compounds and is used in breath analysis to serve as a VOC <a href="/wiki/Biomarker" title="Biomarker">biomarker</a> to test for diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doi:_10.1002.2Fbmc.835_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi:_10.1002.2Fbmc.835-44">[44]</a></sup> One study has shown that "volatile organic compounds ... are mainly blood borne and therefore enable monitoring of different processes in the body."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> And it appears that VOC compounds in the body "may be either produced by metabolic processes or inhaled/absorbed from exogenous sources" such as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_tobacco_smoke" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental tobacco smoke">environmental tobacco smoke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doi:_10.1002.2Fbmc.835_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi:_10.1002.2Fbmc.835-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Research is still in the process to determine whether VOCs in the body are contributed by cellular processes or by the cancerous tumors in the lung or other organs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="VOC_sensors">VOC sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: VOC sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/VOC_sensors" class="mw-redirect" title="VOC sensors">VOC sensors</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle_and_measurement_methods">Principle and measurement methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Principle and measurement methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>VOCs in the environment or certain atmospheres can be detected based on different principles and interactions between the organic compounds and the sensor components. There are electronic devices that can detect ppm concentrations despite the non-selectivity. Others can predict with reasonable accuracy the molecular structure of the volatile organic compounds in the environment or enclosed atmospheres<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and could be used as accurate monitors of the Chemical Fingerprint and further as health monitoring devices.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solid-phase_microextraction" title="Solid-phase microextraction">Solid-phase microextraction</a> (SPME) techniques are used to collect VOCs at low concentrations for analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Direct injection <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> techniques are frequently utilized for the rapid detection and accurate quantification of VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Proton-transfer-reaction_mass_spectrometry" title="Proton-transfer-reaction mass spectrometry">PTR-MS</a> is among the methods that have been used most extensively for the on-line analysis of biogenic and antropogenic VOCs.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Recent PTR-MS instruments based on <a href="/wiki/Time-of-flight_mass_spectrometry" title="Time-of-flight mass spectrometry">time-of-flight mass spectrometry</a> have been reported to reach <a href="/wiki/Detection_limit" title="Detection limit">detection limits</a> of 20 pptv after 100 ms and 750 ppqv after 1 min. measurement (signal integration) time. The <a href="/wiki/Resolution_(mass_spectrometry)" title="Resolution (mass spectrometry)">mass resolution</a> of these devices is between 7000 and 10,500 m/Δm, thus it is possible to separate most common isobaric VOCs and quantify them independently.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accuracy_and_traceability">Accuracy and traceability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Accuracy and traceability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrology_for_VOC_measurements">Metrology for VOC measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volatile_organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Metrology for VOC measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To achieve comparability of VOC measurements, reference standards traceable to <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI-units</a> are required. For a number of VOCs gaseous reference standards are available from specialty gas suppliers or <a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">national metrology institutes</a>, either in the form of cylinders or dynamic generation methods. However, for many VOCs, such as oxygenated VOCs, <a href="/wiki/Monoterpene" title="Monoterpene">monoterpenes</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, no standards are available at the appropriate amount of fraction due to the chemical reactivity or <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> of these molecules. Currently, several national <a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">metrology</a> institutes are working on the lacking standard gas mixtures at trace level concentration, minimising adsorption processes, and improving the zero gas.<sup id="cite_ref-KEYVOCs_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEYVOCs-31">[31]</a></sup> The final scopes are for the <a href="/wiki/Traceability" title="Traceability">traceability</a> and the long-term stability of the standard gases to be in accordance with the data quality objectives (DQO, maximum uncertainty of 20% in this case) required by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">WMO</a>/<a href="/wiki/Global_Atmosphere_Watch" title="Global Atmosphere Watch">GAW</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoerger2015_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoerger2015-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
