<p><b>Political corruption</b> is the use of powers by government officials for illegitimate private gain. An illegal act by an officeholder constitutes political corruption only if the act is directly related to their official duties, is done under <a href="/wiki/Color_of_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Color of law">color of law</a> or involves <a href="/wiki/Influence_peddling" title="Influence peddling">trading in influence</a>.</p>
<p>Forms of <a href="/wiki/Corruption" title="Corruption">corruption</a> vary, but include <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extortion" title="Extortion">extortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cronyism" title="Cronyism">cronyism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parochialism" title="Parochialism">parochialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">patronage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Influence_peddling" title="Influence peddling">influence peddling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graft_(politics)" title="Graft (politics)">graft</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">embezzlement</a>. Corruption may facilitate <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">criminal enterprise</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Drug_trafficking" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug trafficking">drug trafficking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a>, though is not restricted to these activities. Misuse of <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> power for other purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Political_repression" title="Political repression">repression</a> of political opponents and general <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a>, is also considered political corruption.</p>
<p>The activities that constitute illegal corruption differ depending on the country or jurisdiction. For instance, some political funding practices that are legal in one place may be illegal in another. In some cases, government officials have broad or ill-defined powers, which make it difficult to distinguish between legal and illegal actions. Worldwide, bribery alone is estimated to involve over 1 trillion US dollars annually.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A state of unrestrained political corruption is known as a <a href="/wiki/Kleptocracy" title="Kleptocracy">kleptocracy</a>, literally meaning "rule by thieves".</p>
<p>Some forms of corruption – now called "institutional corruption"<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> – are distinguished from bribery and other kinds of obvious personal gain. A similar problem of corruption arises in any institution that depends on financial support from people who have interests that may conflict with the primary purpose of the institution.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_politics.2C_administration.2C_and_institutions">Effects on politics, administration, and institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects on politics, administration, and institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In politics, corruption undermines democracy and <a href="/wiki/Good_governance" title="Good governance">good governance</a> by flouting or even subverting formal processes. Corruption in elections and in the legislature reduces accountability and distorts representation in policymaking; corruption in the judiciary compromises the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>; and corruption in <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a> results in the inefficient provision of services. It violates a basic principle of <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republicanism</a> regarding the centrality of civic virtue.<sup id="cite_ref-www-wds.worldbank_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www-wds.worldbank-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>More generally, corruption erodes the institutional capacity of government if procedures are disregarded, resources are siphoned off, and public offices are bought and sold. Corruption undermines the legitimacy of government and such democratic values as trust and tolerance. Recent evidence suggests that variation in the levels of corruption amongst high-income democracies can vary significantly depending on the level of accountability of decision-makers.<sup id="cite_ref-www-wds.worldbank_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www-wds.worldbank-3">[3]</a></sup> Evidence from <a href="/wiki/Fragile_state" title="Fragile state">fragile states</a> also shows that corruption and bribery can adversely impact trust in institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-bath.ac.uk_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bath.ac.uk-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton.2C_A_2014_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton.2C_A_2014-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_effects">Economic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_corruption" title="Economics of corruption">Economics of corruption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_crime" title="Corporate crime">Corporate crime</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a>, corruption increases the cost of business through the price of illicit payments themselves, the management cost of negotiating with officials and the risk of breached agreements or detection. Although some claim corruption reduces costs by cutting <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>, the availability of bribes can also induce officials to contrive new rules and delays. Openly removing costly and lengthy regulations are better than covertly allowing them to be bypassed by using bribes. Where corruption inflates the cost of business, it also distorts the playing field, shielding firms with connections from competition and thereby sustaining inefficient firms.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corruption also generates economic distortion in the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public sector</a> by diverting public investment into capital projects where bribes and <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">kickbacks</a> are more plentiful. Officials may increase the technical complexity of public sector projects to conceal or pave the way for such dealings, thus further distorting investment.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Corruption also lowers compliance with construction, environmental, or other regulations, reduces the quality of government services and infrastructure, and increases budgetary pressures on government.</p>
<p>Economists argue that one of the factors behind the differing <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> is that in Africa, corruption has primarily taken the form of <a href="/wiki/Rent_seeking" class="mw-redirect" title="Rent seeking">rent extraction</a> with the resulting <a href="/wiki/Financial_capital" title="Financial capital">financial capital</a> moved overseas rather than invested at home (hence the stereotypical, but often accurate, image of African dictators having <a href="/wiki/Swiss_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss bank">Swiss bank accounts</a>). In <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, for example, more than $400 billion was stolen from the treasury by Nigeria's leaders between 1960 and 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a> researchers estimated that from 1970 to 1996, <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a> from 30 <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan" class="mw-redirect" title="Sub-Saharan">Sub-Saharan</a> countries totaled $187bn, exceeding those nations' external debts.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (The results, expressed in retarded or suppressed development, have been modeled in theory by economist <a href="/wiki/Mancur_Olson" title="Mancur Olson">Mancur Olson</a>.) In the case of Africa, one of the factors for this behavior was political instability, and the fact that new governments often confiscated previous government's corruptly obtained assets. This encouraged officials to stash their wealth abroad, out of reach of any future <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">expropriation</a>. In contrast, Asian administrations such as <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Orde_Baru" class="mw-redirect" title="Orde Baru">New Order</a> often took a cut on business transactions or provided conditions for development, through infrastructure investment, law and order, etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_social_effects">Environmental and social effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental and social effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Human impact on the environment</a></div>

<p>Corruption is often most evident in countries with the smallest per capita incomes, relying on foreign aid for health services. Local political interception of donated money from overseas is especially prevalent in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan African</a> nations, where it was reported in the 2006 <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank Report</a> that about half of the funds that were donated for health usages were never invested into the health sectors or given to those needing medical attention.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead, the donated money was expended through "<a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_medications" title="Counterfeit medications">counterfeit drugs</a>, siphoning off of drugs to the black market, and payments to ghost employees". Ultimately, there is a sufficient amount of money for health in developing countries, but local corruption denies the wider citizenry the resource they require.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corruption facilitates environmental destruction. While corrupt societies may have formal legislation to protect the environment, it cannot be enforced if officials can easily be bribed. The same applies to social rights worker protection, <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionization</a> prevention, and <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">child labor</a>. Violation of these laws rights enables corrupt countries to gain illegitimate economic advantage in the international market.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a>-winning economist <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a> has observed that "there is no such thing as an apolitical food problem." While drought and other naturally occurring events may trigger <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> conditions, it is government action or inaction that determines its severity, and often even whether or not a famine will occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Governments with strong tendencies towards <a href="/wiki/Kleptocracy" title="Kleptocracy">kleptocracy</a> can undermine <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> even when harvests are good. Officials often steal state property. In <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, more than 80% of the subsidized food aid to poor is stolen by corrupt officials.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-11">[11]</a></sup> Similarly, food aid is often robbed at gunpoint by governments, criminals, and warlords alike, and sold for a profit. The 20th century is full of many examples of governments undermining the food security of their own nations – sometimes intentionally.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_Humanitarian_Aid">Effects on Humanitarian Aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects on Humanitarian Aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The scale of <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_aid" title="Humanitarian aid">humanitarian aid</a> to the poor and unstable regions of the world grows, but it is highly <a href="/wiki/Social_vulnerability" title="Social vulnerability">vulnerable</a> to corruption, with food aid, construction and other highly valued assistance as the most at risk.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup> Food aid can be directly and physically diverted from its intended destination, or indirectly through the manipulation of assessments, targeting, registration and distributions to favor certain groups or individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In construction and shelter there are numerous opportunities for diversion and profit through substandard workmanship, kickbacks for contracts and favouritism in the provision of valuable shelter material.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup> Thus while humanitarian aid agencies are usually most concerned about aid being diverted by including too many, recipients themselves are most concerned about exclusion.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup> Access to aid may be limited to those with connections, to those who pay bribes or are forced to give sexual favors.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup> Equally, those able to do so may manipulate statistics to inflate the number of beneficiaries and siphon off additional assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Corruption is not specific to poor, developing, or transition countries. In western countries, cases of bribery and other forms of corruption in all possible fields exist: under-the-table payments made to reputed surgeons by patients attempting to be on top of the list of forthcoming surgeries,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> bribes paid by suppliers to the automotive industry in order to sell low-quality connectors used for instance in safety equipment such as airbags, bribes paid by suppliers to manufacturers of defibrillators (to sell low-quality capacitors), contributions paid by wealthy parents to the "social and culture fund" of a prestigious university in exchange for it to accept their children, bribes paid to obtain diplomas, financial and other advantages granted to unionists by members of the executive board of a car manufacturer in exchange for employer-friendly positions and votes, etc. Examples are endless.</p>
<p>These various manifestations of corruption can ultimately present a danger for public health; they can discredit specific, essential institutions or social relationships. Osipian summarized a 2008 "study of corruption perceptions among Russians ... .30 percent of the respondents marked the level of corruption as very high, while another 44 percent as high. 19 percent considered it as average and only 1 percent as low. The most corrupt in people's minds are traffic police (33 percent), local authorities (28 percent), police (26 percent), healthcare (16 percent), and education (15 percent). 52 percent of the respondents had experiences of giving money or gifts to medical professionals while 36 percent made informal payments to educators." He claimed that this corruption lowered the rate of economic growth in Russia, because the students disadvantaged by this corruption could not adopt better work methods as quickly, lowering thereby <a href="/wiki/Total_factor_productivity" title="Total factor productivity">total factor productivity</a> for Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Corruption can also affect the various components of sports activities (referees, players, medical and laboratory staff involved in anti-doping controls, members of national sport federation and international committees deciding about the allocation of contracts and competition places).</p>
<p>Cases exist against (members of) various types of non-profit and non-government organizations, as well as religious organizations.</p>
<p>Ultimately, the distinction between public and private sector corruption sometimes appears rather artificial, and national anti-corruption initiatives may need to avoid legal and other loopholes in the coverage of the instruments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bribery">Bribery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bribery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">Bribery</a></div>
<p>In the context of political corruption, a bribe may involve a payment given to a government official in exchange of his use of official powers. <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">Bribery</a> requires two participants: one to give the bribe, and one to take it. Either may initiate the corrupt offering; for example, a customs official may demand bribes to let through allowed (or disallowed) goods, or a smuggler might offer bribes to gain passage. In some countries the culture of corruption extends to every aspect of public life, making it extremely difficult for individuals to operate without resorting to bribes. Bribes may be demanded in order for an official to do something he is already paid to do. They may also be demanded in order to bypass laws and regulations. In addition to their role in private financial gain, bribes are also used to intentionally and maliciously cause harm to another (i.e. no financial incentive).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some developing nations, up to half of the population has paid bribes during the past 12 months.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/International_community" title="International community">international community</a> has made efforts to encourage countries to dissociate active and passive bribery and to incriminate them as separate offences.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>One can define <b>active bribery</b> as "the promising, offering or giving by any person, directly or indirectly, of any undue advantage to any of its public officials, for himself or herself or for anyone else, for him or her to act or refrain from acting in the exercise of his or her functions" (article 2 of the Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 173)<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>).</li>
<li><b>Passive bribery</b> can be defined as "when committed intentionally, the request or receipt by any [...] public officials, directly or indirectly, of any undue advantage, for himself or herself or for anyone else, or the acceptance of an offer or a promise of such an advantage, to act or refrain from acting in the exercise of his or her functions" (article 3 of the Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 173)).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>This dissociation aims to make the early steps (offering, promising, requesting an advantage) of a corrupt deal already an offence and, thus, to give a clear signal (from a criminal-policy point-of-view) that bribery is not acceptable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Furthermore, such a dissociation makes the prosecution of bribery offences easier since it can be very difficult to prove that two parties (the bribe-giver and the bribe-taker) have formally agreed upon a corrupt deal. In addition, there is often no such formal deal but only a mutual understanding, for instance when it is common knowledge in a municipality that to obtain a building permit one has to pay a "fee" to the decision maker to obtain a favorable decision. A working definition of corruption is also provided as follows in article 3 of the Civil Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 174):<sup id="cite_ref-conventions_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conventions-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>For the purpose of this Convention, "corruption" means requesting, offering, giving or accepting, directly or indirectly, a bribe or any other undue advantage or prospect thereof, which distorts the proper performance of any duty or behavior required of the recipient of the bribe, the undue advantage or the prospect thereof.</i></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trading_in_influence">Trading in influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trading in influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Influence_peddling" title="Influence peddling">Influence peddling</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg/250px-The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg" width="250" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg/375px-The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg/500px-The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="397" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Bosses_of_the_Senate_by_Joseph_Keppler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reformers like the American <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Keppler" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Keppler">Joseph Keppler</a> depicted the Senate as controlled by the giant moneybags, who represented the nation's financial trusts and monopolies.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Trading in influence, or <a href="/wiki/Influence_peddling" title="Influence peddling">influence peddling</a>, refers a person selling his/her influence over the decision making process to benefit a third party (person or institution). The difference with bribery is that this is a tri-lateral relation. From a legal point of view, the role of the third party (who is the target of the influence) does not really matter although he/she can be an accessory in some instances. It can be difficult to make a distinction between this form of corruption and some forms of extreme and loosely regulated <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> where for instance law- or decision-makers can freely "sell" their vote, decision power or influence to those lobbyists who offer the highest compensation, including where for instance the latter act on behalf of powerful clients such as industrial groups who want to avoid the passing of specific environmental, social, or other regulations perceived as too stringent, etc. Where lobbying is (sufficiently) regulated, it becomes possible to provide for a distinctive criteria and to consider that trading in influence involves the use of "improper influence", as in article 12 of the Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 173)<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage">Patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">Patronage</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">Patronage</a> refers to favoring supporters, for example with government employment. This may be legitimate, as when a newly elected government changes the top officials in the administration in order to effectively implement its policy. It can be seen as corruption if this means that incompetent persons, as a payment for supporting the regime, are selected before more able ones. In nondemocracies many government officials are often selected for loyalty rather than ability. They may be almost exclusively selected from a particular group (for example, <a href="/wiki/Sunni" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> Arabs in <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a>'s Iraq, the <a href="/wiki/Nomenklatura" title="Nomenklatura">nomenklatura</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Junker" title="Junker">Junkers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Germany">Imperial Germany</a>) that support the regime in return for such favors. A similar problem can also be seen in Eastern Europe, for example in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, where the government is often accused of <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">patronage</a> (when a new government comes to power it rapidly changes most of the officials in the public sector).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nepotism_and_cronyism">Nepotism and cronyism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nepotism and cronyism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">Nepotism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cronyism" title="Cronyism">Cronyism</a></div>
<p>Favoring relatives (<a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a>) or personal friends (<a href="/wiki/Cronyism" title="Cronyism">cronyism</a>) of an official is a form of illegitimate private gain. This may be combined with <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribery</a>, for example demanding that a business should employ a <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">relative</a> of an official controlling regulations affecting the business. The most extreme example is when the entire state is inherited, as in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>. A lesser form might be in the Southern United States with <a href="/wiki/Good_ol%27_boy" title="Good ol' boy">Good ol' boys</a>, where women and minorities are excluded. A milder form of cronyism is an "<a href="/wiki/Old_boy_network" title="Old boy network">old boy network</a>", in which appointees to official positions are selected only from a closed and exclusive social network – such as the alumni of particular universities – instead of appointing the most competent candidate.</p>
<p>Seeking to harm enemies becomes corruption when official powers are illegitimately used as means to this end. For example, trumped-up charges are often brought up against journalists or writers who bring up politically sensitive issues, such as a politician's acceptance of bribes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gombeenism_and_Parochialism">Gombeenism and Parochialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Gombeenism and Parochialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Gombeen_man" title="Gombeen man">Gombeen man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parochialism" title="Parochialism">Parochialism</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gombeen_man" title="Gombeen man">Gombeenism</a> refers to an individual who is dishonest and corrupt for the purpose of personal gain, more often through monetary, while, <a href="/wiki/Parochialism" title="Parochialism">parochialism</a> which is also known as parish pump politics relates to placing local or vanity projects ahead of the national interest.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For instance in Irish politics, <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_populism" title="Left-wing populism">populist left wing political parties</a> will often apply these terms to <a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">mainstream establishment political parties</a> and will cite the many cases of <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_Ireland" title="Corruption in Ireland">Corruption in Ireland</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Post-2008_Irish_banking_crisis" title="Post-2008 Irish banking crisis">Irish Banking crisis</a>, which found evidence of <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cronyism" title="Cronyism">cronyism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collusion" title="Collusion">collusion</a>, where in some cases politicians who were coming to the end of their political careers would receive a senior management or committee position in a company they had dealings with.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_fraud">Electoral fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Electoral fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electoral_fraud" title="Electoral fraud">Electoral fraud</a></div>
<p><b>Electoral fraud</b> is illegal interference with the process of an <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">election</a>. Acts of <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a> affect vote counts to bring about an election result, whether by increasing the vote share of the favored candidate, depressing the vote share of the rival candidates, or both. Also called <b>voter fraud</b>, the mechanisms involved include illegal voter registration, intimidation at polls, voting computer <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (computer security)">hacking</a>, and improper vote counting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embezzlement">Embezzlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Embezzlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">Embezzlement</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">Embezzlement</a> is the theft of entrusted funds. It is political when it involves public money taken by a public official for use by anyone not specified by the public. A common type of embezzlement is that of personal use of entrusted government resources; for example, when an official assigns public employees to renovate his own house.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kickbacks">Kickbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kickbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">Kickback (bribery)</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Anti-competitive_practices" title="Anti-competitive practices">Anti-competitive practices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bid_rigging" title="Bid rigging">Bid rigging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">Fraud</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charbonneau_Commission" title="Charbonneau Commission">Charbonneau Commission</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Money_trail" class="mw-redirect" title="Money trail">kickback</a> is an official's share of misappropriated funds allocated from his or her organization to an organization involved in corrupt <a href="/wiki/Bidding" title="Bidding">bidding</a>. For example, suppose that a politician is in charge of choosing how to spend some public funds. He can give a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> to a <a href="/wiki/General_contractor" title="General contractor">company</a> that is not the best bidder, or allocate more than they deserve. In this case, the company benefits, and in exchange for betraying the public, the official receives a kickback payment, which is a portion of the sum the company received. This sum itself may be all or a portion of the difference between the actual (inflated) payment to the company and the (lower) market-based price that would have been paid had the bidding been competitive.</p>
<p>Another example of a kickback would be if a judge receives a portion of the profits that a business makes in exchange for his judicial decisions.</p>
<p>Kickbacks are not limited to government officials; any situation in which people are entrusted to spend funds that do not belong to them are susceptible to this kind of corruption.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unholy_alliance">Unholy alliance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Unholy alliance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unholy_alliance_(geopolitical)" title="Unholy alliance (geopolitical)">Unholy alliance (geopolitical)</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Unholy_alliance_(geopolitical)" title="Unholy alliance (geopolitical)">unholy alliance</a> is a coalition among seemingly antagonistic groups for <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a> or hidden gain, generally some influential non-governmental group forming ties with political parties, supplying funding in exchange for the favorable treatment. Like patronage, unholy alliances are not necessarily illegal, but unlike patronage, by its deceptive nature and often great financial resources, an unholy alliance can be much more dangerous to the <a href="/wiki/Public_interest" title="Public interest">public interest</a>. An early use of the term was by former US President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore "Teddy" Roosevelt</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"To destroy this invisible Government, to dissolve the <b>unholy alliance</b> between corrupt business and corrupt politics is the first task of the statesmanship of the day." – 1912 <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Party_(United_States,_1912)" title="Progressive Party (United States, 1912)">Progressive Party</a> Platform, attributed to Roosevelt<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and quoted again in his autobiography,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> where he connects <a href="/wiki/Trust_(19th_century)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trust (19th century)">trusts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopolies</a> (sugar interests, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil" title="Standard Oil">Standard Oil</a>, etc.) to <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Taft" class="mw-redirect" title="Howard Taft">Howard Taft</a>, and consequently both major <a href="/wiki/Political_parties" class="mw-redirect" title="Political parties">political parties</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement_in_organized_crime">Involvement in organized crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Involvement in organized crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An illustrative example of official involvement in <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a> can be found from 1920s and 1930s <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, where Huang Jinrong was a police chief in the <a href="/wiki/French_concession" class="mw-redirect" title="French concession">French concession</a>, while simultaneously being a gang boss and co-operating with <a href="/wiki/Du_Yuesheng" title="Du Yuesheng">Du Yuesheng</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Gang" title="Green Gang">local gang</a> ringleader. The relationship kept the flow of profits from the gang's gambling dens, prostitution, and protection rackets undisturbed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The United States accused <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Noriega" title="Manuel Noriega">Manuel Noriega</a>'s government in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> of being a "<a href="/wiki/Narcokleptocracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Narcokleptocracy">narcokleptocracy</a>", a corrupt government profiting on <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">illegal drug trade</a>. Later the U.S. invaded Panama and captured Noriega.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_favorable_for_corruption">Conditions favorable for corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conditions favorable for corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Prose" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div>

<p>It is argued that the following conditions are favorable for corruption:</p>
<ul>
<li>Information deficits
<ul>
<li>Lacking <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_information_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom of information legislation">freedom of information legislation</a>. In contrast, for example: The Indian <a href="/wiki/Right_to_Information_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Right to Information Act">Right to Information Act</a> 2005 is perceived to have "already engendered mass movements in the country that is bringing the lethargic, often corrupt bureaucracy to its knees and changing power equations completely."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lack of investigative reporting in the local media.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Contempt for or negligence of exercising <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a>.</li>
<li>Weak <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> practices, including lack of timely financial management.</li>
<li>Lack of measurement of corruption. For example, using regular surveys of households and businesses in order to quantify the degree of perception of corruption in different parts of a nation or in different government institutions may increase awareness of corruption and create pressure to combat it. This will also enable an evaluation of the officials who are fighting corruption and the methods used.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tax_haven" title="Tax haven">Tax havens</a> which tax their own citizens and companies but not those from other nations and refuse to disclose information necessary for foreign taxation. This enables large-scale political corruption in the foreign nations.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Lacking control of the government.
<ul>
<li>Lacking <a href="/wiki/Civic_society" title="Civic society">civic society</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a> which monitor the government.</li>
<li>An individual voter may have a <a href="/wiki/Rational_ignorance" title="Rational ignorance">rational ignorance</a> regarding politics, especially in nationwide elections, since each vote has little weight.</li>
<li>Weak <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">civil service</a>, and slow pace of <a href="/wiki/Civil_service_reform_in_developing_countries" title="Civil service reform in developing countries">reform</a>.</li>
<li>Weak <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>.</li>
<li>Weak <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">legal profession</a>.</li>
<li>Weak <a href="/wiki/Judicial_independence" title="Judicial independence">judicial independence</a>.</li>
<li>Lacking protection of <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">whistleblowers</a>.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Project" title="Government Accountability Project">Government Accountability Project</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Benchmarking" title="Benchmarking">benchmarking</a>, that is continual detailed evaluation of procedures and comparison to others who do similar things, in the same government or others, in particular comparison to those who do the best work. The Peruvian organization Ciudadanos al Dia has started to measure and compare transparency, costs, and efficiency in different government departments in Peru. It annually awards the best practices which has received widespread media attention. This has created competition among government agencies in order to improve.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Individual officials routinely handle cash, instead of handling payments by <a href="/wiki/Giro" title="Giro">giro</a> or on a separate cash desk – illegitimate withdrawals from supervised bank accounts are much more difficult to conceal.</li>
<li>Public funds are centralized rather than distributed. For example, if $1,000 is embezzled from a local agency that has $2,000 funds, it is easier to notice than from a national agency with $2,000,000 funds. See the <a href="/wiki/Subsidiarity" title="Subsidiarity">principle of subsidiarity</a>.</li>
<li>Large, unsupervised public investments.</li>
<li>Pay disproportionately lower than that of the average citizen.</li>
<li>Government licenses needed to conduct business, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Import_license" title="Import license">import licenses</a>, encourage bribing and kickbacks.</li>
<li>Long-time work in the same position may create relationships inside and outside the government which encourage and help conceal corruption and favoritism. Rotating government officials to different positions and geographic areas may help prevent this; for instance certain high rank officials in French government services (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9sor_public" title="Trésor public">treasurer-paymasters general</a>) must rotate every few years.</li>
<li>Costly <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">political campaigns</a>, with expenses exceeding normal sources of political funding, especially when funded with taxpayer money.</li>
<li>A single group or family controlling most of the key government offices. Lack of laws forbidding and limiting number of members of the same family to be in office .</li>
<li>Less interaction with officials reduces the opportunities for corruption. For example, using the Internet for sending in required information, like applications and tax forms, and then processing this with automated computer systems. This may also speed up the processing and reduce unintentional human errors. See <a href="/wiki/E-Government" class="mw-redirect" title="E-Government">e-Government</a>.</li>
<li>A windfall from exporting abundant natural resources may encourage corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <i>(See <a href="/wiki/Resource_curse" title="Resource curse">Resource curse</a>)</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/War" title="War">War</a> and other forms of conflict correlate with a breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Public_security" title="Public security">public security</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Social conditions
<ul>
<li>Self-interested closed cliques and "<a href="/wiki/Old_boy_network" title="Old boy network">old boy networks</a>".</li>
<li>Family-, and clan-centered social structure, with a tradition of <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a>/favouritism being acceptable.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">gift economy</a>, such as the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Blat_(term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Blat (term)">blat</a> system, emerges in a Communist <a href="/wiki/Centrally_planned_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Centrally planned economy">centrally planned economy</a>.</li>
<li>Lacking <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> among the population.</li>
<li>Frequent <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bullying</a> among the population.</li>
<li>Tribal solidarity, giving benefits to certain ethnic groups. In India for example, the political system, it has become common that the leadership of national and regional parties are passed from generation to generation.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>creating a system in which a family holds the center of power. Some examples are most of the Dravidian parties of south India and also the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Congress party</a>, which is one of the two major political parties in India.</li>
<li>Lack of strong laws which forbid members of the same family to contest elections and be in office as in India where local elections are often contested between members of the same powerful family by standing in opposite parties so that whoever is elected that particular family is at tremendous benefit.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" class="extiw" title="q:Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> observed a tendency for "The functionaries of every government ... to command at will the liberty and property of their constituents. There is no safe deposit [for liberty and property] ... without information. Where the press is free, and every man able to read, all is safe."</p>
<p>Recent research supports Jefferson's claim. Brunetti and Weder found "evidence of a significant relationship between more press freedom and less corruption in a large cross-section of countries." They also presented "evidence which suggests that the direction of causation runs from higher press freedom to lower corruption."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Adserà, Boix, and Payne found that increases in newspaper readership led to increased <a href="/wiki/Political_accountability" class="mw-redirect" title="Political accountability">political accountability</a> and lower corruption in data from roughly 100 countries and from different states in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Snyder and Strömberg found "that a poor fit between newspaper markets and political districts reduces press coverage of politics. ... Congressmen who are less covered by the local press work less for their constituencies: they are less likely to stand witness before congressional hearings ... . Federal spending is lower in areas where there is less press coverage of the local members of congress."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Schulhofer-Wohl and Garrido found that the year after the Cincinnati Post closed in 2007, "fewer candidates ran for municipal office in the Kentucky suburbs most reliant on the Post, incumbents became more likely to win reelection, and voter turnout and campaign spending fell.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>An analysis of the evolution of mass media in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> since World War II noted mixed results from the growth of the Internet: "The digital revolution has been good for freedom of expression [and] information [but] has had mixed effects on freedom of the press": It has disrupted traditional sources of funding, and new forms of Internet journalism have replaced only a tiny fraction of what's been lost.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_of_public_sector">Size of public sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Size of public sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Extensive and diverse public spending is, in itself, inherently at risk of cronyism, kickbacks, and embezzlement. Complicated regulations and arbitrary, unsupervised official conduct exacerbate the problem. This is one argument for <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a>. Opponents of privatization see the argument as ideological. The argument that corruption necessarily follows from the opportunity is weakened by the existence of countries with low to non-existent corruption but large public sectors, like the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> These countries score high on the <a href="/wiki/Ease_of_Doing_Business_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Ease of Doing Business Index">Ease of Doing Business Index</a>, due to good and often simple regulations, and have <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a> firmly established. Therefore, due to their lack of corruption in the first place, they can run large public sectors without inducing political corruption. Recent evidence that takes both the size of expenditures and regulatory complexity into account has found that high-income democracies with more expansive state sectors do indeed have higher levels of corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-www-wds.worldbank_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www-wds.worldbank-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like other governmental economic activities, also privatization, such as in the sale of government-owned property, is particularly at the risk of cronyism. Privatizations in Russia, Latin America, and East Germany were accompanied by large-scale corruption during the sale of the state owned companies. Those with political connections unfairly gained large wealth, which has discredited privatization in these regions. While media have reported widely the grand corruption that accompanied the sales, studies have argued that in addition to increased operating efficiency, daily petty corruption is, or would be, larger without privatization, and that corruption is more prevalent in non-privatized sectors. Furthermore, there is evidence to suggest that extralegal and unofficial activities are more prevalent in countries that privatized less.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the European Union, the principle of subsidiarity is applied: a government service should be provided by the lowest, most local authority that can competently provide it. An effect is that distribution of funds into multiple instances discourages embezzlement, because even small sums missing will be noticed. In contrast, in a centralized authority, even minute proportions of public funds can be large sums of money.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governmental_corruption">Governmental corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Governmental corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the highest echelons of the governments also take advantage from corruption or embezzlement from the state's treasury, it is sometimes referred with the <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Kleptocracy" title="Kleptocracy">kleptocracy</a></i>. Members of the government can take advantage of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> (e.g., diamonds and oil in a few prominent cases) or state-owned productive industries. A number of corrupt governments have enriched themselves via foreign aid, which is often spent on showy buildings and armaments.</p>
<p>A corrupt <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorship</a> typically results in many years of general hardship and suffering for the vast majority of citizens as <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a> disintegrate. In addition, corrupt dictators routinely ignore economic and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">social</a> problems in their quest to amass ever more wealth and power.</p>
<p>The classic case of a corrupt, exploitive dictator often given is the regime of <a href="/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Marshal Mobutu Sese Seko</a>, who ruled the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> (which he renamed <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>) from 1965 to 1997. It is said that usage of the term <a href="/wiki/Kleptocracy" title="Kleptocracy">kleptocracy</a> gained popularity largely in response to a need to accurately describe Mobutu's regime. Another classic case is <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, especially under the rule of General <a href="/wiki/Sani_Abacha" title="Sani Abacha">Sani Abacha</a> who was <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> president of Nigeria from 1993 until his death in 1998. He is reputed to have stolen some <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>3–4 billion. He and his relatives are often mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Advance-fee_fraud" class="mw-redirect" title="Advance-fee fraud">Nigerian 419 letter scams</a> claiming to offer vast fortunes for "help" in laundering his stolen "fortunes", which in reality turn out not to exist.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> More than $400 billion was stolen from the treasury by Nigeria's leaders between 1960 and 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, articles in various financial periodicals, most notably <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine, have pointed to <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, General Secretary of the Republic of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> since 1959, of likely being the beneficiary of up to $900 million, based on "his control" of state-owned companies.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Opponents of his regime claim that he has used money amassed through weapons sales, narcotics, international loans, and confiscation of private property to enrich himself and his political cronies who hold his dictatorship together, and that the $900 million published by <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> is merely a portion of his assets, although that needs to be proven.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judiciary_corruption">Judiciary corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Judiciary corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two methods of corruption of the judiciary: the state (through budget planning and various privileges), and the private. <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">Budget</a> of the judiciary in many transitional and <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> is almost completely controlled by the executive. The latter undermines the separation of powers, as it creates a critical financial dependence of the judiciary. The proper national wealth distribution including the government spending on the judiciary is subject of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_economics" title="Constitutional economics">constitutional economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Judicial corruption can be difficult to completely eradicate, even in developed countries.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposing_corruption">Opposing corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Opposing corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:161029_Madagaskar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/161029_Madagaskar.jpg/220px-161029_Madagaskar.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/161029_Madagaskar.jpg/330px-161029_Madagaskar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/161029_Madagaskar.jpg/440px-161029_Madagaskar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1950" data-file-height="2600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:161029_Madagaskar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A poster against corruption in Madagascar</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mobile_telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile telecommunications">Mobile telecommunications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio broadcasting</a> help to fight corruption, especially in developing regions like <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> where other forms of <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communications</a> are limited. In India, the anti-corruption bureau fights against corruption, and a new ombudsman bill called <a href="/wiki/Jan_Lokpal_Bill" title="Jan Lokpal Bill">Jan Lokpal Bill</a> is being prepared.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, initiatives were taken at an international level (in particular by the <a href="/wiki/European_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="European Community">European Community</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/OECD" class="mw-redirect" title="OECD">OECD</a>) to put a ban on corruption: in 1996, the Committee of Ministers of the Council of Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> for instance, adopted a comprehensive Programme of Action against Corruption and, subsequently, issued a series of anti-corruption standard-setting instruments:</p>
<ul>
<li>the Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 173);<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Civil Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 174);<sup id="cite_ref-conventions_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conventions-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Additional Protocol to the Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS 191);<sup id="cite_ref-conventions_a_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conventions_a-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Twenty Guiding Principles for the Fight against Corruption (Resolution (97) 24);<sup id="cite_ref-coe.int_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coe.int-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Recommendation on Codes of Conduct for Public Officials (Recommendation No. R (2000) 10);<sup id="cite_ref-Rec_2000_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rec_2000-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Recommendation on Common Rules against Corruption in the Funding of Political Parties and Electoral Campaigns (Rec(2003)4)<sup id="cite_ref-Rec_2003_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rec_2003-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The purpose of these instruments was to address the various forms of corruption (involving the public sector, the private sector, the financing of political activities, etc.) whether they had a strictly domestic or also a transnational dimension. To monitor the implementation at national level of the requirements and principles provided in those texts, a monitoring mechanism – the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_States_Against_Corruption" title="Group of States Against Corruption">Group of States Against Corruption</a> (also known as GRECO) (French: Groupe d'Etats contre la corruption) was created.</p>
<p>Further conventions were adopted at the regional level under the aegis of the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a> (OAS or OEA), the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>, and in 2003, at the universal level under that of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_against_Corruption" title="United Nations Convention against Corruption">United Nations Convention against Corruption</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Whistleblowers">Whistleblowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Whistleblowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">Whistleblower</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_corruption">Measuring corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Measuring corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Measuring corruption statistically is difficult if not impossible due to the illicit nature of the transaction and imprecise definitions of corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-commons.globalintegrity.org_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commons.globalintegrity.org-52">[52]</a></sup> While "corruption" indices first appeared in 1995 with the <a href="/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index" title="Corruption Perceptions Index">Corruption Perceptions Index</a> CPI, all of these metrics address different proxies for corruption, such as public perceptions of the extent of the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-Galtung.2C_Fredrik_2006_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galtung.2C_Fredrik_2006-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transparency_International" title="Transparency International">Transparency International</a>, an anti-corruption <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGO</a>, pioneered this field with the CPI, first released in 1995. This work is often credited with breaking a taboo and forcing the issue of corruption into high level development policy discourse. Transparency International currently publishes three measures, updated annually: a CPI (based on aggregating third-party polling of public perceptions of how corrupt different countries are); a Global Corruption Barometer (based on a survey of general public attitudes toward and experience of corruption); and a <a href="/wiki/Bribe_Payers_Index" title="Bribe Payers Index">Bribe Payers Index</a>, looking at the willingness of foreign firms to pay bribes. The Corruption Perceptions Index is the best known of these metrics, though it has drawn much criticism<sup id="cite_ref-Galtung.2C_Fredrik_2006_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galtung.2C_Fredrik_2006-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and may be declining in influence.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 2013 <a href="/wiki/Transparency_International" title="Transparency International">Transparency International</a> published a report on the "Government Defence Anti-corruption Index". This index evaluates the risk of corruption in countries' military sector.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The World Bank collects a range of data on corruption,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> including survey responses from over 100,000 firms worldwide<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and a set of indicators of governance and institutional quality.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Moreover, one of the six dimensions of governance measured by the <a href="/wiki/Worldwide_Governance_Indicators" title="Worldwide Governance Indicators">Worldwide Governance Indicators</a> is Control of Corruption, which is defined as "the extent to which power is exercised for private gain, including both petty and grand forms of corruption, as well as 'capture' of the state by <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">elites</a> and private interests."<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> While the definition itself is fairly precise, the data aggregated into the Worldwide Governance Indicators is based on any available polling: questions range from "is corruption a serious problem?" to measures of public access to information, and not consistent across countries. Despite these weaknesses, the global coverage of these datasets has led to their widespread adoption, most notably by the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Challenge_Corporation" title="Millennium Challenge Corporation">Millennium Challenge Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-commons.globalintegrity.org_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commons.globalintegrity.org-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of parties have collected survey data, from the public and from experts, to try and gauge the level of corruption and bribery, as well as its impact on political and economic outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-bath.ac.uk_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bath.ac.uk-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton.2C_A_2014_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton.2C_A_2014-5">[5]</a></sup> A second wave of corruption metrics has been created by <a href="/wiki/Global_Integrity" title="Global Integrity">Global Integrity</a>, the International Budget Partnership,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and many lesser known local groups. These metrics include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Integrity_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Global Integrity Index (page does not exist)">Global Integrity Index</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> first published in 2004. These second wave projects aim to create policy change by identifying resources more effectively and creating checklists toward incremental reform. Global Integrity and the International Budget Partnership<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> each dispense with public surveys and instead uses in-country experts to evaluate "the opposite of corruption" – which Global Integrity defines as the public policies that prevent, discourage, or expose corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> These approaches compliment the first wave, awareness-raising tools by giving governments facing public outcry a checklist which measures concrete steps toward improved governance.<sup id="cite_ref-commons.globalintegrity.org_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commons.globalintegrity.org-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typical second wave corruption metrics do not offer the worldwide coverage found in first wave projects, and instead focus on localizing information gathered to specific problems and creating deep, "unpackable"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> content that matches quantitative and qualitative data.</p>
<p>Alternative approaches, such as the British aid agency's Drivers of Change research, skips numbers and promotes understanding corruption via political economy analysis of who controls power in a given society.<sup id="cite_ref-commons.globalintegrity.org_52-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commons.globalintegrity.org-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_corruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
