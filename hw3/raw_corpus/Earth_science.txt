<p><b>Earth science</b> or <b>geoscience</b> is a widely embraced term for the fields of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Earth science can be considered to be a branch of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>, but with a much older history. There are both <a href="/wiki/Reductionism" title="Reductionism">reductionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> approaches to Earth sciences. The Earth sciences can include the study of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, and the large-scale structure of the Earth's interior, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Typically, Earth scientists use tools from <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">chronology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> to build a quantitative understanding of how the <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_science" title="Earth system science">Earth system</a> works and evolves.</p>

<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Branches_of_science" title="Branches of science">fields of science</a> are generally categorized within the Earth sciences:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physical geography</a>, covers aspects of <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">soil study</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocky</a> parts of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>) and its historic development. Major subdisciplines are <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_geology" title="Engineering geology">engineering geology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sedimentology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-smith_5_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith_5-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a> investigate the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">shape of the Earth</a>, its reaction to forces and its <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravity_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravity field">gravity fields</a>. Geophysicists explore the <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">Earth's core</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismic</a> activity of the lithosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-smith_5_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith_5-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Geophysics is commonly used to supplement the work of <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a> in developing a comprehensive understanding of crustal geology, particularly in mineral and petroleum exploration. See <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_survey" title="Geophysical survey">geophysical survey</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_science" title="Soil science">Soil science</a> covers the outermost layer of the Earth's crust that is subject to soil formation processes (or <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Edaphology" title="Edaphology">edaphology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">pedology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> covers the interactions between the <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>, with their natural environment. This field of study differentiates the study of the Earth, from the study of other planets in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>; the Earth being the only planet teeming with life.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">Hydrology</a> (includes <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">limnology</a>) describe the marine and freshwater domains of the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">watery</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>). Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Hydrogeology" title="Hydrogeology">hydrogeology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">physical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oceanography" title="Chemical oceanography">chemical</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">biological</a> oceanography.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">Glaciology</a> covers the <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">icy</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">Atmospheric sciences</a> cover the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> parts of the Earth (or <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>) between the surface and the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> (about 1000&#160;km). Major subdisciplines include <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_physics" title="Atmospheric physics">atmospheric physics</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_interior">Earth's interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earth's interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Volcano_q.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/200px-Volcano_q.jpg" width="200" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/300px-Volcano_q.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/400px-Volcano_q.jpg 2x" data-file-width="678" data-file-height="449" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Volcano_q.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Volcanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> eruption is the release of stored energy from below the surface of Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> ranges, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological phenomena</a> that can be explained in terms of physical and chemical processes in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beneath the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> lies the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> which is heated by the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy elements">heavy elements</a>. The mantle is not quite solid and consists of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> which is in a state of semi-perpetual <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convection" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:convection">convection</a>. This convection process causes the lithospheric plates to move, albeit slowly. The resulting process is known as <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plate tectonics might be thought of as the process by which the Earth is resurfaced. As the result of <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a>, new crust and lithosphere is created by the flow of magma from the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> to the near surface, through fissures, where it cools and solidifies. Through <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a>, oceanic crust and lithosphere returns to the convecting mantle.<sup id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Areas of the crust where new crust is created are called <i>divergent boundaries</i>, those where it is brought back into the Earth are <i>convergent boundaries</i> and those where plates slide past each other, but no new lithospheric material is created or destroyed, are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Transform_fault" title="Transform fault">transform</a> (or conservative) boundaries<sup id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oldroyd_tectonics_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_tectonics-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Earthquakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</a> result from the movement of the lithospheric plates, and they often occur near convergent boundaries where parts of the crust are forced into the Earth as part of subduction.<sup id="cite_ref-smith_subduction_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith_subduction-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volcanoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</a> result primarily from the melting of subducted crust material. Crust material that is forced into the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> melts, and some portion of the melted material becomes light enough to rise to the surface—giving birth to volcanoes.<sup id="cite_ref-adams_tectonics_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adams_tectonics-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-smith_subduction_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith_subduction-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_magnetic_field">Earth's magnetic field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Earth's magnetic field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a> is a magnet that is created by an electric current.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Earth has a solid iron <a href="/wiki/Earth_core" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth core">inner core</a> surrounded by fluid <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer core</a> that convects;<sup id="cite_ref-oldroyd_core_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_core-19">[19]</a></sup> therefore, the Earth is an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnet</a>. The motion of fluid convection <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">sustains</a> the Earth's magnetic field.<sup id="cite_ref-oldroyd_core_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldroyd_core-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dynamo_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dynamo-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earth's atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Atmosphere of Earth</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/200px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg" width="200" height="109" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/300px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/400px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="656" /></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> shields the surface of Earth from the charged particles of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<br />
(<i>image not to scale.</i>)</div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> are the five layers which make up Earth's atmosphere. In all, the atmosphere is made up of about 78.0% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 20.9% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and 0.92% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. 75% of the gases in the atmosphere are located within the troposphere, the bottom-most layer. The remaining one percent of the atmosphere (all but the nitrogen, oxygen, and argon) contains small amounts of other gases including CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapors.<sup id="cite_ref-adams_atmosphere_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adams_atmosphere-21">[21]</a></sup> Water vapors and CO<sub>2</sub> allow the Earth's atmosphere to catch and hold the Sun's energy through a phenomenon called the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This allows Earth's surface to be warm enough to have liquid water and support life.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> created by the internal motions of the core produces the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> which protects the Earth's atmosphere from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As the Earth is <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of the earth">4.5 billion</a> years old,<sup id="cite_ref-age_earth_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-age_earth-24">[24]</a></sup> it would have lost its atmosphere by now if there were no protective magnetosphere.</p>
<p>In addition to storing heat, the atmosphere also protects living organisms by shielding the Earth's surface from <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. Note that the level of protection is high enough to prevent <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> from destroying all life on Earth, yet low enough to aid the <a href="/wiki/Mutations" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutations">mutations</a> that have an important role in pushing forward <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">diversity</a> in the biosphere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methodologies vary depending on the nature of the subjects being studied. Studies typically fall into one of three categories: observational, experimental, or theoretical. Earth scientists often conduct sophisticated computer analysis or go to many of the world's most exotic locations to study Earth phenomena (e.g. Antarctica or hot spot island chains).</p>
<p>A foundational idea within the study Earth science is the notion of uniformitarianism. Uniformitarianism dictates that "ancient geologic features are interpreted by understanding active processes that are readily observed."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In other words, any geologic processes at work in the present have operated in the same ways throughout geologic time. This enables those who study Earth's history to apply knowledge of how Earth processes operate in the present to gain insight into how the planet has evolved and changed throughout deep history.</p>

<p>Earth science generally recognizes four spheres, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-wheeling_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wheeling-25">[25]</a></sup> these correspond to <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. Also included by some are the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>) as a distinct portion of the hydrosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>) as an active and intermixed sphere.</p>
