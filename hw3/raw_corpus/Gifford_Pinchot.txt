<p><b>Gifford Pinchot</b> (August 11, 1865&#160;– October 4, 1946) was an American forester and politician. Pinchot served as the first Chief of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a> from 1905 until his firing in 1910, and was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Pennsylvania" title="List of Governors of Pennsylvania">28th Governor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, serving from 1923 to 1927, and again from 1931 to 1935. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Republican Party">Republican Party</a> for most of his life, though he also joined the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Party_(United_States,_1912)" title="Progressive Party (United States, 1912)">Progressive Party</a> for a brief period.</p>
<p>Pinchot is known for reforming the management and development of forests in the United States and for advocating the conservation of the nation's reserves by planned use and renewal. He called it "the art of producing from the forest whatever it can yield for the service of man." Pinchot coined the term <a href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation ethic">conservation ethic</a> as applied to natural resources. Pinchot's main contribution was his leadership in promoting scientific forestry and emphasizing the controlled, profitable use of forests and other natural resources so they would be of maximum benefit to mankind. He was the first to demonstrate the practicality and profitability of managing forests for continuous cropping. His leadership put conservation of forests high on America's priority list.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Asked how to say his name, he told <i>The <a href="/wiki/Literary_Digest" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary Digest">Literary Digest</a></i> "as though it were spelled <i>pin'cho</i>, with slight emphasis on the first syllable."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_life">Education and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gifford Pinchot was born August 11, 1865, to Episcopalian parents in <a href="/wiki/Simsbury,_Connecticut" title="Simsbury, Connecticut">Simsbury, Connecticut</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the son of <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site#James_Pinchot" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">James W. Pinchot</a>, a successful New York City wallpaper merchant, and Mary Eno, daughter of one of New York City's wealthiest real estate developers, <a href="/wiki/Amos_Eno" title="Amos Eno">Amos Eno</a>. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Exeter_Academy" title="Phillips Exeter Academy">Phillips Exeter Academy</a> and in 1889, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-collier_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collier-6">[6]</a></sup> where he was a member of <a href="/wiki/Skull_and_Bones" title="Skull and Bones">Skull and Bones</a>. He had a brother <a href="/wiki/Amos_Pinchot" title="Amos Pinchot">Amos Pinchot</a> and a sister Antoinette (who married the diplomat <a href="/wiki/Alan_Johnstone" title="Alan Johnstone">Alan Johnstone</a>).</p>
<p>The Pinchots made a great fortune from lumbering and land speculation, and Pinchot's father regretted the damage his family's work had done to the land.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-7">[7]</a></sup> James made conservation a family affair and suggested that Gifford should become a forester. Gifford studied as a postgraduate at the <a href="/wiki/French_National_School_of_Forestry" title="French National School of Forestry">French National School of Forestry</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a>, for a year. He returned home and plunged into the nascent forestry movement, intent on shaping a national forest policy. At Gifford's urging, together James and Gifford endowed the <a href="/wiki/Yale_School_of_Forestry" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale School of Forestry">Yale School of Forestry</a> in 1900, and James turned <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers</a>, the family estate at <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Pennsylvania" title="Milford, Pennsylvania">Milford, Pennsylvania</a>, into a "nursery" for the American forestry movement. Family financial affairs were managed by brother <a href="/wiki/Amos_Pinchot" title="Amos Pinchot">Amos Pinchot</a>, thus freeing Gifford to do the more important work of developing forest management concepts. Unlike some others in the forestry movement, Gifford's wealth allowed him to singly pursue this goal without worry of income.</p>
<p>Pinchot's approach set him apart from the other leading forestry experts, especially <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_E._Fernow" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernhard E. Fernow">Bernhard E. Fernow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_A._Schenck" title="Carl A. Schenck">Carl A. Schenck</a>. Fernow had been Pinchot's predecessor in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a>'s Division of Forestry before leaving in 1898 to become the first Dean of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_College_of_Forestry_at_Cornell" title="New York State College of Forestry at Cornell">New York State College of Forestry at Cornell</a>. Schenck was Pinchot's successor at the <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Estate" title="Biltmore Estate">Biltmore Estate</a> (widely recognized as the "cradle of American forestry") and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Forest_School" title="Biltmore Forest School">Biltmore Forest School</a> on Biltmore Estate. Their schools largely reflected their approaches to introducing forestry in the United States: Fernow advocated a regional approach and Schenck a private enterprise effort in contrast to Pinchot's national vision.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps, the men who had the most influence on his development as a forester were Sir <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Brandis" title="Dietrich Brandis">Dietrich Brandis</a>, who had brought forestry to the British Empire, and Sir <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Schlich" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm Schlich">Wilhelm Schlich</a>, Brandis' successor. Pinchot relied heavily upon Brandis' advice for introducing professional forest management in the U.S. and on how to structure the Forest Service when Pinchot established it in 1905.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_policy_and_institutions">Forestry policy and institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forestry policy and institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg/220px-Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg" width="220" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg/330px-Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg/440px-Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg 2x" data-file-width="703" data-file-height="1065" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Gifford_Pinchot.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Portrait of Gifford Pinchot</i> by Benjamin Johnston, c. 1901</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1896, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> formed the National Forest Commission. Pinchot was the only non-Academy member. President <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> later asked Pinchot to develop a plan for managing the nation's Western forest reserves.</p>
<p>In 1897, Pinchot became a member of <a href="/wiki/Boone_and_Crockett_Club" title="Boone and Crockett Club">Boone and Crockett Club</a> one of North America's first conservation organizations, which was founded by <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1898, Gifford Pinchot succeeded <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Fernow" title="Bernhard Fernow">Bernhard Fernow</a> as chief of the Division of Forestry, later renamed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a> in 1905. Thus, management of the federal forests changed from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a> to this agency within the Department of Agriculture. Pinchot introduced better forestry methods into the operations of private owners, large and small, by using new forestry school graduates to demonstrate good practices and help make working plans.</p>
<p>In 1900, Pinchot established the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_American_Foresters" title="Society of American Foresters">Society of American Foresters</a>. This helped bring credibility to the new profession of forestry, and was part of the broader <a href="/wiki/Professionalization" title="Professionalization">professionalization</a> movement underway in the United States at the turn of the twentieth century. Until 1900, only two American schools trained professional foresters, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_College_of_Forestry_at_Cornell" title="New York State College of Forestry at Cornell">New York State College of Forestry at Cornell</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Forest_School" title="Biltmore Forest School">Biltmore Forest School</a>. The Pinchot family endowed a 2-year graduate-level School of Forestry at Yale University (now the <a href="/wiki/Yale_School_of_Forestry_%26_Environmental_Studies" title="Yale School of Forestry &amp; Environmental Studies">School of Forestry and Environmental Studies</a>), and at Pinchot's urging, fellow Yale alumnus <a href="/wiki/Henry_S._Graves" title="Henry S. Graves">Henry S. Graves</a>, along with James W. Toumey, left the Division in 1900 to start the school. By the fall of 1900, Cornell's forestry program had 24 students, Biltmore 9, and Yale 7.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One unique feature of the Pinchot/Yale approach was requiring students to first experience the forest at a camp at Grey Towers before beginning their academic studies.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinchot sought to turn public land policy from one that dispersed resources among private holdings, to one that maintained federal ownership and management of public land. As a progressive, Pinchot strongly believed in the <a href="/wiki/Efficiency_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Efficiency movement">efficiency movement</a>. Waste was his great enemy, and he well knew the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>, the destruction of resources for short-term gains. His successes, in part, were grounded in the personal networks that he started developing as a student at Yale and continued developing throughout his career. His personal involvement in the recruitment process led to high morale in the Forest Service and allowed him to avoid partisan political patronage. Pinchot capitalized on his professional expertise to gain adherents in an age when professionalism and science were greatly valued.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinchot_Roosevelt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Pinchot_Roosevelt.jpg/220px-Pinchot_Roosevelt.jpg" width="220" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3f/Pinchot_Roosevelt.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="267" data-file-height="338" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pinchot_Roosevelt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Theodore Roosevelt and Gifford Pinchot on the steamer <i>Mississippi</i>, 1907</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pinchot used the rhetoric of the market economy to disarm critics of efforts to expand the role of government: scientific management of forests and natural resources was profitable. While most of his battles were with timber companies that he thought had too narrow a time horizon, he also battled the forest preservationists like <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a>, who were deeply opposed to commercializing nature.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinchot was generally opposed to preservation for the sake of wilderness or scenery, a fact perhaps best illustrated by the important support he offered to the damming of <a href="/wiki/Hetch_Hetchy_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Hetch Hetchy Valley">Hetch Hetchy Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinchot rose to national prominence under the patronage of President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>. In 1905, his department also gained control of the national forest reserves, thereby dramatically increasing the authority of the Forest Service. Pinchot developed a plan by which the forests could be developed by private interests, under set terms, in exchange for a fee. Pinchot embarked on many publicity campaigns to direct national discussions of natural resource management issues. In 1907 Roosevelt appointed Pinchot a member of the <a href="/wiki/Inland_Waterways_Commission" title="Inland Waterways Commission">Inland Waterways Commission</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Era" title="Progressive Era">Progressive Era</a> investigation and study of water resources usage from a multi-purpose approach.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Central to his publicity work was his creation of news for magazines and newspapers, as well as debates with opponents such as <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a>. His effectiveness in manipulating information hostile to his boss, President <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>, led to his firing in January 1910. But his successes became a model for other bureaucrats on how to influence public opinion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinchot's policies encountered some opposition. Preservationists were opposed to massive timber cutting while Congress was increasingly hostile to conservation of the forests, bowing to local commercial pressures for quicker exploitation. In 1907, Congress forbade the creation of more forest reserves in the Western states. Roosevelt designated 16&#160;million acres (65,000&#160;km²) of new National Forests just minutes before his power to do so was stripped by a congressionally mandated amendment to the Agriculture Bill. These were called the <a href="/wiki/Midnight_forests" title="Midnight forests">Midnight forests</a>. For his contributions to conservation, Pinchot was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Public_Welfare_Medal" title="Public Welfare Medal">Public Welfare Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-PublicWelfare_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PublicWelfare-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pinchot.E2.80.93Ballinger_controversy">Pinchot–Ballinger controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pinchot–Ballinger controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pinchot%E2%80%93Ballinger_controversy" title="Pinchot–Ballinger controversy">Pinchot–Ballinger controversy</a></div>
<p>Pinchot's authority was substantially undermined by the election of President <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1908" title="United States presidential election, 1908">1908</a>. Taft later dismissed Pinchot<sup id="cite_ref-Dismissal_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dismissal-18">[18]</a></sup> for speaking out against his policies and those of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ballinger" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Ballinger">Richard Ballinger</a>, Secretary of the Interior.</p>
<p>Pinchot launched a series of public attacks to discredit Ballinger and force him from office in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Pinchot%E2%80%93Ballinger_controversy" title="Pinchot–Ballinger controversy">Pinchot–Ballinger controversy</a>. That episode hastened the split in the Republican Party that led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Party_(United_States,_1912)" title="Progressive Party (United States, 1912)">Progressive Party</a>, of which Pinchot and his brother were top leaders.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_storm_of_1910_and_the_descent_of_the_Forest_Service">Fire storm of 1910 and the descent of the Forest Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fire storm of 1910 and the descent of the Forest Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg/300px-Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg" width="300" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg/450px-Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="326" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gifford_Pinchot_visiting_students_at_School_of_Forestry_camp_at_Gray_Towers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Pinchot visiting summer campers attending the School of Forestry camp at <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers</a> (Pinchot is with the dog), 1910</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pinchot hand-picked <a href="/wiki/William_Greeley" class="mw-redirect" title="William Greeley">William Greeley</a>, the son of a Congregational minister, who finished at the top of that first Yale forestry graduating class of 1904, to be the Forest Service's Region 1 forester, with responsibility over 41&#160;million acres (170,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) in 22 National Forests in four western states (all of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, and a corner of <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>).</p>
<p>One year after the 1910 forest fire inferno, <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_1910" title="Great Fire of 1910">Great Fire of 1910</a>, the religious Greeley succeeded in receiving a promotion to a high administration job in Washington. In 1920, he became Chief of the Forest Service. The fire of 1910 convinced him that <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a> was at work, the fire converted him into a fire extinguishing partisan who elevated firefighting to the <i>raison d'être</i> — the overriding mission — of the Forest Service. Under Greeley, the Service became the fire engine company, protecting trees so the timber industry could cut them down later at government expense. Pinchot was appalled. The timber industry successfully oriented the Forestry Service toward policies favorable to large-scale harvesting via <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_capture" title="Regulatory capture">regulatory capture</a>, and metaphorically, the timber industry was now the fox in the chicken coop.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Pinchot and Roosevelt had envisioned, at the least, that public timber should be sold only to small, family-run logging outfits, not to big syndicates. Pinchot had always preached of a "working forest" for working people and small-scale logging at the edge, preservation at the core. In 1928 Bill Greeley left the Forest Service for a position in the timber industry, becoming an executive with the West Coast Lumberman's Association.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Pinchot traveled west in 1937, to view those forests with <a href="/wiki/Henry_S._Graves" title="Henry S. Graves">Henry S. Graves</a>, what they saw "tore his heart out." Greeley's legacy, combining modern chain saws and government-built forest roads, had allowed industrial-scale clear-cuts to become the norm in the western national forests of Montana and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. Entire mountainsides, mountain after mountain, were treeless. "So this is what saving the trees was all about." "Absolute devastation", Pinchot wrote in his diary. "The Forest Service should absolutely declare against clear- cutting in Washington and Oregon as a defensive measure", Pinchot wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_political_activity">Subsequent political activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsequent political activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pinchot founded the <a href="/wiki/National_Conservation_Commission" title="National Conservation Commission">National Conservation Commission</a>, of which he was president from 1910 to 1925. Pinchot ran for Senate in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Pennsylvania,_1914" title="United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 1914">1914, for the state of Pennsylvania</a> on the Progressive Party ticket. He finished second, behind incumbent Senator <a href="/wiki/Boies_Penrose" title="Boies Penrose">Boies Penrose</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Republican_State_Committee_of_Pennsylvania" title="Republican State Committee of Pennsylvania">State Republican</a> powerhouse, and ahead of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Democratic_Party" title="Pennsylvania Democratic Party">Democratic</a> nominee and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Congressman</a> <a href="/wiki/A._Mitchell_Palmer" title="A. Mitchell Palmer">A. Mitchell Palmer</a>. Pinchot would later express interest in seeking the presidency, and promoted American involvement in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, in opposition to <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>'s neutrality.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_term_as_Governor_of_Pennsylvania">First term as Governor of Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First term as Governor of Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the disbandment of the Progressive Party, Pinchot returned to the Republican Party, and focused on Pennsylvania state politics. Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Cameron_Sproul" title="William Cameron Sproul">William Sproul</a> appointed him state Commissioner of Forestry in 1920. Pinchot's aim, however, was to become governor. His 1922 campaign for the office concentrated on popular reforms: government economy, enforcement of <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">Prohibition</a> and regulation of public utilities. He won by a wide margin. In 1924, Pinchot considered challenging President <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a> for the Republican nomination, but ultimately declined to run for the presidency.</p>
<p>In 1926, Governor Pinchot proposed his quasi-public "Giant Power" scheme for the state of Pennsylvania – which was very similar to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Steinmetz" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Steinmetz">Charles Steinmetz</a>'s plan to transmit electricity by high-voltage lines from power plants located adjacent to Pennsylvania coal mines – but critics dismissed it as "<a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Under President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> a similar scheme, albeit for a different region, was enacted by Congress and signed into law as the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a> (TVA).</p>
<p>Pinchot temporarily retired from public office at the end of his term on January 18, 1927, as Pennsylvania Governors were prohibited from seeking a second consecutive term in office at that time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_road_to_a_second_term">Long road to a second term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Long road to a second term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the completion of his term as governor, Pinchot and his wife took a seven-month cruise to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">South Seas</a>. In <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Pennsylvania,_1926" title="United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 1926">1926</a>, Pinchot had competed in a combative Republican Senate <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primary</a> for the seat which he had also sought a dozen years earlier as a Progressive. Boies Penrose, who had defeated Pinchot in the general election of 1914, died at the end of 1921, and Governor Sproul had appointed lecturer and <a href="/wiki/Republican_National_Committee" title="Republican National Committee">Republican National Committeeman</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Pepper" title="George W. Pepper">George Pepper</a> to the seat. Pepper won a special election held in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_special_election_in_Pennsylvania,_1922" title="United States Senate special election in Pennsylvania, 1922">1922</a>, but was challenged by both Pinchot and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Congressman</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Scott_Vare" title="William Scott Vare">William Scott Vare</a>, the head of Philadelphia's powerful political <a href="/wiki/Machine_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Machine politics">machine</a>. Pinchot took a strongly supportive stance on the issue of Prohibition, while Vare vowed to <a href="/wiki/Twenty-first_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-first Amendment to the United States Constitution">repeal</a> the <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Eighteenth Amendment</a> within two years, and Pepper sought to straddle the issue. In the end, Pinchot finished a distant third, behind Pepper and Vare,<sup id="cite_ref-th_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-th-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bft_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bft-25">[25]</a></sup> who went on to defeat former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Labor" title="United States Secretary of Labor">Labor Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Bauchop_Wilson" title="William Bauchop Wilson">William Wilson</a> in the general election. However, while Vare's election victory was recognized by the Senate, he was never officially seated, as allegations of impropriety were eventually brought before a special Senate committee. In January 1927, Pinchot testified before the committee, producing several thousand illegal paper ballots, and in 1929, Vare's election credentials were rejected by Senate, and the seat was declared vacant.</p>
<p>In 1930, Pinchot sought a second term as governor, battling for regulation of public utilities, the continuance of Prohibition, relief for the unemployed, and construction of paved roads to "get the farmers out of the mud." He was worried about mounting a political comeback that year. "I don't want to make a fool of myself," he said a month before announcing his candidacy.<sup id="cite_ref-uncoveredpolitics.com_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uncoveredpolitics.com-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pitted against <a href="/wiki/Francis_Shunk_Brown" title="Francis Shunk Brown">Francis Shunk Brown</a>, the candidate of Vare's Philadelphia machine, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wharton_Phillips,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Wharton Phillips, Jr.">Thomas Phillips</a>, a former two-term congressman from western Pennsylvania, who was enthusiastically supported by the state's "wet" forces, Pinchot overcame a deficit of nearly 200,000 votes in traditionally Republican Philadelphia to pull into a 12,000-vote lead on election night.<sup id="cite_ref-uncoveredpolitics.com_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uncoveredpolitics.com-26">[26]</a></sup> The 281,000 votes cast for former congressman Phillips, most of which came at Brown's expense, appeared to have given Pinchot a narrow victory in the primary. Brown's attorneys immediately challenged the results, contending that some 60,000 ballots in <a href="/wiki/Luzerne_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Luzerne County, Pennsylvania">Luzerne County</a> should be tossed out because they had been perforated beforehand by county election officials in an attempt to prevent fraud. The legal battle ensued throughout the summer and nobody knew for sure who the Republican candidate for governor would be, especially since both men periodically claimed to be the nominee. Ending four months of litigation and political turmoil on August 20, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Pennsylvania Supreme Court">Pennsylvania Supreme Court</a>, while castigating the Luzerne Common Pleas Court for marking (perforating) the ballots in the first place, upheld an earlier lower court ruling and declared that all 60,000 perforated ballots were valid, thereby certifying Pinchot as the winner of the May 20 Republican primary.</p>
<p>As expected, a number of key Republicans abandoned the former governor during the autumn campaign, one of the most significant defections being that of the wealthy <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_Atterbury" title="William Wallace Atterbury">W. W. Atterbury</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>, who resigned his seat on the Republican National Committee to actively support John Hemphill, Pinchot's Democratic opponent. Despite the opposition of many in his own party, including not only Vare's powerful Philadelphia machine, but also many "wets" who revolted and created a separate Liberal Party that autumn — thereby giving the Democratic candidate two lines on the November ballot — Pinchot narrowly prevailed, defeating Hemphill by a margin of 1,068,874 to 1,010,204.<sup id="cite_ref-uncoveredpolitics.com_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uncoveredpolitics.com-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_term_as_Governor_of_Pennsylvania">Second term as Governor of Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second term as Governor of Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his second term in office, January 20, 1931 to January 15, 1935, Pinchot cooperated with President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a>, despite his being a Democrat and Prohibition opponent. Under Governor Pinchot's leadership, Pennsylvania welcomed the <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">Civilian Conservation Corps</a>, which established 113 camps to work on public lands in Pennsylvania (second only to California). Working with the <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>, Pinchot helped expand <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pennsylvania_State_Parks" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Pennsylvania State Parks">Pennsylvania's state parks</a>, and also helped Pennsylvania's struggling farmers and unemployed workers by paving rural roads, which became known as Pinchot Roads.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Governor Pinchot also abolished the thuggish Coal and Iron Police, established by his predecessor, Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Stuchell_Fisher" title="John Stuchell Fisher">John Fisher</a>.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Prohibition</a> was nationally <a href="/wiki/Twenty-first_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-first Amendment to the United States Constitution">repealed</a> in 1933, and four days before the sale of alcohol became legal in Pennsylvania again, Pinchot called the Pennsylvania General Assembly into special session to debate regulations regarding the manufacture and sale of alcohol. This session led to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Liquor_Control_Board" title="Pennsylvania Liquor Control Board">Pennsylvania Liquor Control Board</a> and its system of state-run liquor stores, reflecting Pinchot's desire to "discourage the purchase of alcoholic beverages by making it as inconvenient and expensive as possible."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Pennsylvania,_1934" title="United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 1934">1934</a>, Pinchot ran unsuccessfully for the Senate a third time, and lost the Republican nomination to incumbent Senator <a href="/wiki/David_A._Reed" title="David A. Reed">David Reed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ocone_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocone-30">[30]</a></sup> Pinchot's final campaign, a bid for the GOP nomination for governor in 1938, was also unsuccessful, as he came in second to former <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Pennsylvania" title="Lieutenant Governor of Pennsylvania">Lieutenant Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_H._James" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur H. James">Arthur James</a>.</p>
<p>In his remaining years, the ex-governor gave advice to President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, wrote a book about his life as a forester,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and devised a fishing kit to be used in lifeboats during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He even instructed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> on how to extract fresh water from fish.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gifford_Pinchot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg/220px-Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg" width="220" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg/330px-Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg/440px-Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1809" data-file-height="2116" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers</a> near <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Pennsylvania" title="Milford, Pennsylvania">Milford, Pennsylvania</a>, a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Sites_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Historic Sites (United States)">National Historic Site</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Gifford Pinchot died on October 4, 1946, aged 81, from <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>. He was survived by his wife, Cornelia Bryce,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and his son Gifford Bryce Pinchot. He is interred at Milford Cemetery, <a href="/wiki/Pike_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Pike County, Pennsylvania">Pike County, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Gifford Pinchot was named for <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River_School" title="Hudson River School">Hudson River School</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Sanford_Robinson_Gifford" title="Sanford Robinson Gifford">Sanford Robinson Gifford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Proud of the first Gifford Pinchot's legacy, the family has continued to name their sons Gifford, down to Gifford Pinchot IV.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gifford Senior and his then thirteen-year-old son co-wrote a scientific travel-adventure book, entitled <i>Giff and Stiff in the South Seas</i>, copyright 1933, by the John C. Winston Co. of Philadelphia. Junior Gifford narrates the adventure, relating in a young boy's language the <a href="/wiki/Pinchot_South_Sea_Expedition" title="Pinchot South Sea Expedition">scientific studies, observations, and adventures encountered by a family sailing on the <i>Mary Pinchot</i></a>—from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a> and on to the <a href="/wiki/Galapagos" class="mw-redirect" title="Galapagos">Galapagos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marquesas" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquesas">Marquesas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">Society Islands</a>. Photos also document the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a>-like odyssey. Although the book is currently out of print, it can be found.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot_National_Forest" title="Gifford Pinchot National Forest">Gifford Pinchot National Forest</a> in Washington and <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot_State_Park" title="Gifford Pinchot State Park">Gifford Pinchot State Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lewisberry,_Pennsylvania" title="Lewisberry, Pennsylvania">Lewisberry, Pennsylvania</a>, are named in his honor, as is Pinchot Hall at <a href="/wiki/Penn_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn State">Penn State University</a>. A large <a href="/wiki/Coast_Redwood" class="mw-redirect" title="Coast Redwood">Coast Redwood</a> in <a href="/wiki/Muir_Woods" class="mw-redirect" title="Muir Woods">Muir Woods</a>, California, is also named in his honor, as is Pinchot Pass on the <a href="/wiki/John_Muir_Trail" title="John Muir Trail">John Muir Trail</a> in the Kings Canyon National Park in California.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pinchot_Sycamore" title="Pinchot Sycamore">Pinchot Sycamore</a>, the largest tree in his native state of <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> and second-largest <a href="/wiki/American_sycamore" class="mw-redirect" title="American sycamore">sycamore</a> on the Atlantic coast, still stands in <a href="/wiki/Simsbury,_Connecticut" title="Simsbury, Connecticut">Simsbury</a>. The house where Pinchot was born belonged to his grandfather, Captain Elisha Phelps, and is also on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> accepted the family's summer retreat house, <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers National Historic Site</a>, which his son Dr. Gifford Bryce Pinchot and the Pinchot family donated to the U.S. Forest Service. It remains the only <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> operated by that federal agency. Despite budget cuts and damage due to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>, and with the assistance of a Heritage Association, tours are offered in the summer months.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The Pinchot family also dedicated <a href="/wiki/The_Pinchot_Institute_for_Conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="The Pinchot Institute for Conservation">The Pinchot Institute for Conservation</a>, which maintains offices both at Grey Towers and headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>. The Institute continues Pinchot's legacy of conservation leadership and sustainable forestry.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot_III" title="Gifford Pinchot III">Gifford Pinchot III</a>, grandson of the first Gifford Pinchot, is co-founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/Bainbridge_Graduate_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Bainbridge Graduate Institute">Bainbridge Graduate Institute</a>, which offers a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> degree integrating environmental <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a> with innovation and profit.</p>
<p>Gifford Pinchot is commemorated in the scientific name of a species of Caribbean lizard, <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Anolis_lizards" title="List of Anolis lizards">Anolis pinchoti</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
