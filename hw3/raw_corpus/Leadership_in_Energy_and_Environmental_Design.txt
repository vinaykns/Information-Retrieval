<p><b>Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</b> (<b>LEED</b>) is one of the most popular <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> <a href="/wiki/Certification" title="Certification">certification</a> programs used worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Developed by the non-profit <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a> (USGBC) it includes a set of rating systems for the design, construction, operation, and maintenance of green buildings, homes, and neighborhoods<sup id="cite_ref-berkeley_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-8">[8]</a></sup> that aims to help building owners and operators be <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_responsible" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmentally responsible">environmentally responsible</a> and use resources efficiently.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1994 to 2015, LEED grew from one standard for new construction to a comprehensive system of interrelated standards covering aspects from the design and construction to the maintenance and operation of buildings. LEED also has grown from six volunteers on one committee to 119,924 staff, volunteers and professionals.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup> LEED standards have been applied to approximately 83,452 registered and certified LEED projects worldwide, covering around 13.8 billion square feet (1.28 billion square meters).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many U.S. federal agencies and states and local governments require or reward LEED certification. However, four states (Alabama, Georgia, Maine, and Mississippi) have effectively banned the use of LEED in new public buildings, preferring other industry standards that the USGBC considers too lax.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike model building codes, such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Building_Code" title="International Building Code">International Building Code</a>, only members of the USGBC and specific "in-house" committees may add to, subtract from, or edit the standard, subject to an internal review process. Proposals to modify the LEED standards are offered and publicly reviewed by USGBC's member organizations, which number almost 12,216.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>USGBC's <a href="/wiki/Green_Business_Certification_Inc." title="Green Business Certification Inc.">Green Business Certification Inc.</a> (GBCI) offers various accreditation to people who demonstrate knowledge of the LEED rating system, including LEED Accredited Professional (LEED AP), LEED Green Associate,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and since 2011, LEED Fellows, the highest designation for LEED professionals. GBCI also certifies projects pursuing LEED.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rating_systems">Rating systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rating systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>LEED has evolved since 1998 to more accurately represent and incorporate emerging green building technologies. The pilot version, LEED New Construction (NC) v1.0, led to LEED NCv2.0, LEED NCv2.2 in 2005, and LEED 2009 (previously named LEED v3) in 2009. LEED v4 was introduced in November, 2013. Until October 31, 2016, new projects may choose between LEED 2009 and LEED v4. New projects registering after October 31, 2016 must use LEED v4.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>LEED 2009 encompasses ten rating systems for the design, construction and operation of buildings, homes and neighborhoods.<sup id="cite_ref-berkeley_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-8">[8]</a></sup> Five overarching categories correspond to the specialties available under the LEED professional program. That suite currently consists of:</p>
<p><b>Green Building Design &amp; Construction</b></p>
<ul>
<li>LEED for New Construction</li>
<li>LEED for Core &amp; Shell</li>
<li>LEED for Schools</li>
<li>LEED for Retail: New Construction and Major Renovations</li>
<li>LEED for Healthcare</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Green Interior Design &amp; Construction</b></p>
<ul>
<li>LEED for Commercial Interiors</li>
<li>LEED for Retail: Commercial Interiors</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Green Building Operations &amp; Maintenance</b></p>
<ul>
<li>LEED for Existing Buildings: Operations &amp; Maintenance</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Green Neighborhood Development</b></p>
<ul>
<li>LEED for Neighborhood Development<sup id="cite_ref-berkeley_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><b>Green Home Design and Construction</b></p>
<ul>
<li>LEED for Homes (The LEED for Homes rating system is different from LEED v3, with different point categories and thresholds that reward efficient residential design.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>)</li>
</ul>
<p>LEED also forms the basis for other sustainability rating systems such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s Labs21.</p>
<p>To make it easier to follow LEED requirements, in 2009 USGBC helped BuildingGreen develop LEEDuser, a guide to the LEED certification process and applying for LEED credits written by professionals in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_2009">LEED 2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: LEED 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After four years of development, aligning credits across all LEED rating systems and weighting credits based on environmental priority, USGBC launched LEED v3,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which consists of a new continuous development process, a new version of LEED Online, a revised third-party certification program and a new suite of rating systems known as LEED 2009.</p>
<p>Under LEED 2009, there are 100 possible base points distributed across six credit categories: Sustainable Sites, Water Efficiency, Energy and Atmosphere, Materials and Resources, Indoor Environmental Quality, Innovation in Design. Up to 10 additional points may be earned: four additional points may be received for Regional Priority Credits, and six additional points for Innovation in Design (which includes exemplary performance credits for existing credit categories).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification_level">Certification level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Certification level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Buildings can qualify for four levels of certification:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Certified:</b> 40–49 points</li>
<li><b>Silver:</b> 50-59 points</li>
<li><b>Gold:</b> 60-79 points</li>
<li><b>Platinum:</b> 80 points and above</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Goal_of_the_credit_system">Goal of the credit system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Goal of the credit system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The LEED 2009 performance credit system aims to allocate points "based on the potential <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impacts</a> and human benefits of each credit." These are weighed using the environmental impact categories of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s Tools for the Reduction and Assessment of Chemical and Other Environmental Impacts (TRACI).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the environmental-impact weighting scheme developed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prerequisites">Prerequisites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prerequisites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To participate in LEED 2009, a building must comply with <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental laws</a> and regulations, occupancy scenarios, building permanence and pre-rating completion, site boundaries and area-to-site ratios. Its owner must share data on the building's energy and water use for five years after occupancy (for new construction) or date of certification (for existing buildings).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each of the performance categories also have mandatory measures in each category, which receive no points.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Credit_weighting_process">Credit weighting process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Credit weighting process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The weighting process has three steps:<sup id="cite_ref-usgbc.org_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usgbc.org-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>A collection of reference buildings are used to estimate the environmental impacts of any building seeking LEED certification in a designated rating scheme.</li>
<li>NIST weightings are used to judge the relative importance of these impacts in each category.</li>
<li>Data regarding actual impacts on environmental and human health are used to assign points to individual categories and measures.</li>
</ol>
<p>This system results in a weighted average for each rating scheme based upon actual impacts and the relative importance of those impacts to human health and environmental quality.</p>
<p>The LEED council also appears to have assigned credit and measure weighting based upon the market implications of point allocation.<sup id="cite_ref-usgbc.org_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usgbc.org-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2010, buildings can use carbon offsets to achieve Green Power Credits for LEED-NC (New Construction Certification)&#160;:<sup id="cite_ref-Green_Power_Credits_for_LEED-NC_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Power_Credits_for_LEED-NC-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_for_Homes">LEED for Homes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: LEED for Homes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Additional performance categories in the LEED for Homes rating system are Locations and Linkages (recognizing the importance of transportation access, open space, and physical activity outdoors) and Awareness and Education (recognizing the need for buildings and settlements to educate occupants).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_Canada">LEED Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: LEED Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Green_Building_Council" title="Canada Green Building Council">Canada Green Building Council</a> received permission to create LEED Canada-NC v1.0, which was based upon LEED-NC 2.0.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Many buildings in Canada are LEED certified in part due to their <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting_in_Canada" title="Rainwater harvesting in Canada">Rainwater harvesting</a> practices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>LEED certification is granted by the <a href="/wiki/Green_Building_Certification_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Building Certification Institute">Green Building Certification Institute</a> (GBCI), which handles the third-party verification of a project's compliance with the LEED requirements.</p>
<p>The certification process for design teams is made up of two consecutive applications: one including design credits, and one including construction credits. All of the LEED credits in each rating system are assigned to either the design application or the construction application. The design credits include those that are the purview of the architect and the engineer, and are documented in the official construction drawings. The construction credits include those that are predominantly under the purview of the contractor, and are documented during the construction and commissioning of the building.</p>
<p>A fee is required to register the building, and to submit the design and construction applications. Total fees are assessed based on building area. Fees range from a minimum of $2,900 to over $1 million for a large project.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> "Soft" costs, i.e., added costs to the building project to quality for LEED certification, range from 1% to 6% of the total project cost. The average cost increase was about 2%, or an extra $3–$5 per square foot.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The application review and certification process is handled on LEED Online, USGBC's web-based service that employs a series of active PDF forms to allow project teams to fill out credit forms and upload supporting documentation online. The GBCI also utilizes LEED Online to conduct their reviews.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_energy_modeling">LEED energy modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: LEED energy modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Design teams have the option of achieving points under the Optimize Energy Performance credit by building an energy model. This energy model must follow the modeling methodologies outlined in Appendix G of the <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE_90.1" title="ASHRAE 90.1">ASHRAE 90.1</a> building energy standard. The guidelines in Appendix G require that the team make two energy models: one representing the building as designed, and a second “baseline” building. The baseline building must be modeled in the same location, and have the same geometry and occupancy as the design building. Depending on location (climate) and building size, the standard provides requirements for HVAC system type, and wall and window definitions. The goal of this methodology is to provide a baseline building to use as a reference point to compare the design building against. It is a way to standardize the baseline, while putting weight on important factors that heavily influence building energy consumption (e.g., location, geometry, and occupancy patterns). The number of points achieved in this credit is correlated with the percent predicted energy cost savings demonstrated by the difference between the design and baseline energy models.</p>
<p>This method of energy modeling has been criticized for inaccurately predicting actually energy usage of LEED-certified buildings. The USGBC admits that "current information indicates that most buildings do not perform as well as design metrics indicate. As a result, building owners might not obtain the benefits promised."<sup id="cite_ref-http_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_for_Homes_Rating_System">LEED for Homes Rating System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: LEED for Homes Rating System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Today, increasing demand towards environmental safety forces LEED certification to play major role. The process of the LEED for Homes Rating System, available in the USA,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Canada and Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> is significantly different from the LEED NC rating system. LEED for Homes projects are low rise residential and are required to work with either an American Provider Organization<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> or a Canadian Provider Organization<sup id="cite_ref-cagbc1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cagbc1-28">[28]</a></sup> and a Green Rater. A Provider Organization helps the project through the process while overseeing the Green Raters. Green Raters are individuals that conduct the two mandatory LEED for Homes site inspections; the Thermal Bypass Inspection and the Final Inspection. Although LEED for Homes is typically viewed by the construction industry as a simpler rating system, especially when compared to LEED NC, LEED NC does not require an on-site inspection. The Provider and the Green Rater do not certify the project, but rather assist in the certification process.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_Performance">LEED Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: LEED Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research papers provide most of what is known about the performance and effectiveness of LEED in two credit category areas – energy and indoor environment quality. In one study of 953 NYC office buildings, 21 LEED certified buildings collectively showed no energy savings compared with non-LEED buildings, although LEED Gold buildings "outperformed other NYC office buildings by 20%".<sup id="cite_ref-2013scofield_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013scofield-29">[29]</a></sup> IEQ-related studies provide two contrasting results - the first used occupant survey results in 65 LEED buildings and 79 non-LEED buildings and it concluded that occupants of LEED certified buildings have equal satisfaction with the building overall and with the workspace than occupants of non-LEED rated buildings<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavon_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavon-30">[30]</a></sup> and the second used occupant interviews and physical site measurements at 12 LEED buildings to report superior indoor environment performance compared with similar 12 conventional buildings (non-LEED).<sup id="cite_ref-Newsham_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsham-31">[31]</a></sup> Buildings certified under LEED do not have to prove energy or water efficiency in practice to receive LEED certification points, but instead LEED uses modeling software to predict future energy use based on intended use. This has led to criticism of LEED’s ability to accurately determine the efficiency of buildings. The USGBC itself says that, “Buildings have a poor track record for performing as predicted during design.<sup id="cite_ref-http_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_performance_research">Energy performance research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Energy performance research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009 Newsham et al. analyzed a database of 100 LEED-certified buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-newsham09_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsham09-32">[32]</a></sup> In this study, each building was paired with a conventional "twin" building within the Commercial Building Energy Consumption Survey (CBECS) database according to building type and occupancy.<sup id="cite_ref-newsham09_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsham09-32">[32]</a></sup> On average, LEED buildings consumed 18 to 39% less energy than their conventional "twin" building, although 28 to 35% of LEED-certified buildings used more energy than their "twin.”<sup id="cite_ref-newsham09_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsham09-32">[32]</a></sup> The paper found no correlation between the number of energy points achieved or LEED certification level and measured building performance.<sup id="cite_ref-newsham09_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsham09-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 Scofield published an article in response to Newsham et al., analyzing the same database of LEED buildings and arriving at different conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-2009scofield_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2009scofield-33">[33]</a></sup> In his analysis, Scofield considered <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.energystar.gov/buildings/facility-owners-and-managers/existing-buildings/use-portfolio-manager/understand-metrics/difference">source energy</a> (accounting for energy losses during generation and transmission) as well as site energy, and used area-weighted energy use intensities, or EUIs (energy per unit area per year), when comparing LEED and non-LEED buildings to account for the fact that larger buildings tend to have larger EUIs.<sup id="cite_ref-2009scofield_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2009scofield-33">[33]</a></sup> Scofield concluded that, collectively, the LEED-certified buildings showed no significant source energy consumption savings or greenhouse gas emission reductions when compared to non-LEED buildings, although they did consume 10-17% less site energy.<sup id="cite_ref-2009scofield_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2009scofield-33">[33]</a></sup> Scofield in 2013 analyzed 21 LEED-certified buildings in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-2013scofield_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013scofield-29">[29]</a></sup> He found that buildings that had achieved LEED Gold used, on average, 20% less source energy than did conventional buildings. Buildings with LEED Silver or LEED Certified ratings actually used 11 to 15% more source energy, on average, than did their conventional counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-2013scofield_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013scofield-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stoppel and Leite<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> developed a study in 2013 to evaluate the predicted and actual energy consumption of two twin buildings using the energy model process documented during the LEED design phase and the utility meter data after 12 months of occupancy. The authors found that energy model predicted 14% and 25% more energy consumption for each building compared to the actual buildings energy use. The study’s results suggests that mechanical systems turnover and occupancy assumptions significantly differing from predicted to actual values.</p>
<p>In 2014, Fuertes and Schiavon<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> developed the first study that analyzes plug loads using LEED documented data from certified projects. The study compared <a href="/wiki/Plug_load" title="Plug load">plug load</a> assumptions made by 92 <a href="/wiki/Energy_Modeling_Forum" title="Energy Modeling Forum">energy modeling</a> practitioners against <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE_Handbook" title="ASHRAE Handbook">ASHRAE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Title_24_(California_code)" class="mw-redirect" title="Title 24 (California code)">Title 24</a> requirements, as well as, the evaluation of the plug loads calculation methodology used by 660 <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.usgbc.org/Docs/Archive/General/Docs5543.pdf">LEED-CI</a> and 429 <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.usgbc.org/Docs/Archive/General/Docs5546.pdf">LEED-NC</a> certified projects. In general, energy modelers considered the energy consumption of plug loads of equipment that are constantly running (such as refrigerators) as well as monitors and computes predictable. Overall the results suggested a disconnection between energy modelers assumptions and the actual performance of buildings. The study also shows that there was no consistency on the method used by LEED-CI and NC projects, while one is based on the power and percentage of <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">ENERGY STAR</a> equipment installed in the building the other one is based on a whole-building-design energy model. In conclusion, the study suggests LEED or ASHRAE to develop guidelines for plug loads calculations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IEQ_performance_research">IEQ performance research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: IEQ performance research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Disease_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Center for Disease Control">Center for Disease Control</a> defines Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ) as "the quality of a building’s environment in relation to the health and wellbeing of those who occupy space within it."<sup id="cite_ref-CDC_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-36">[36]</a></sup> The USGBC includes the following considerations for attaining IEQ credits: <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">indoor air quality</a>, the level of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a>, lighting, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">thermal comfort</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">daylighting</a> and views. In consideration of a building's indoor environmental quality, published studies have also included factors such as: acoustics, building cleanliness and maintenance, colors and textures, workstation size, ceiling height, window access and shading, surface finishes, and furniture adjustability and comfort.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavon_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavon-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Newsham_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsham-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, a paper published by S. Schiavon and S. Altomonte studied LEED verses non-LEED buildings in relation to occupant IEQ satisfaction.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavon_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavon-30">[30]</a></sup> Using occupant surveys from the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.cbe.berkeley.edu/research/survey.htm">Center for the Built Environment</a> at Berkeley database,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> 65 LEED-certified and 79 non-LEED buildings were studied to provide an analysis of 15 IEQ-related factors in the overall building and specific workspaces. These factors include the ease of interaction, building cleanliness, the comfort of furnishing, the amount of light, building maintenance, colors and textures, workplace cleanliness, the amount of space, furniture adjustability, visual comfort, air quality, visual privacy, noise, temperature, and sound privacy. The results showed occupants tend to be slightly more satisfied in LEED buildings for the air quality and slightly more dissatisfied with the amount of light. The overall finding was that there was no significant influence of LEED certification on occupant satisfaction in consideration of the overall building and workspace ratings.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiavon_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiavon-30">[30]</a></sup> The "Limitations and Further Studies" section states that the data may not be representative of the entire building stock and a randomized approach was not used in the data assessment. Furthermore, an analysis across the various certification types (e.g. New Construction, Existing Buildings, etc.) by version release (e.g. 2.1) is recommended.</p>
<p>Based on similar dataset (21,477 occupants), in 2013, Schiavon and Altomonte,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> found that occupants have equivalent satisfaction levels in LEED and non-LEED buildings when evaluated independently from the following nine factors: (1) office type, (2) spatial layout, (3) distance from windows, (4) building size, (5) gender, (6) age, (7) type of work, (8) time at workspace, and (9) weekly working hours.</p>
<p>Conversely, in 2015, a study on indoor environmental quality and the potential health benefits of green certified buildings was developed by Allen et al.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> showing that green buildings provide better indoor environmental quality with direct benefits to human health of occupants of those buildings in comparison to non-green buildings. One of the limitations of the study was the use of subjective health performance indicators since there is a lack of definition on such indicators by current studies.</p>
<p>G. Newsham et al. published a detailed study on IEQ and LEED buildings in August 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsham_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsham-31">[31]</a></sup> Field studies and Post-Occupancy Evaluations (POE) were performed in 12 “green” and 12 “conventional” buildings across Canada and the northern United States. On-site, 974 workstations were measured for thermal conditions, air quality, acoustics, lighting, workstation size, ceiling height, window access and shading, and surface finishes. Responses were positive in the areas of environmental satisfaction, satisfaction with thermal conditions, satisfaction with view from the outside, aesthetic appearance, reduced disturbance from heating, ventilation and air-conditioning noise, workplace image, night-time sleep quality, mood, physical symptoms, and reduced number of airborne particulates. The results showed green buildings exhibited superior performance compared with similar conventional buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Newsham_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsham-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>For LEED BD+C v4 credit, the IEQ category addresses <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">thermal</a>, visual, and acoustic comfort as well as <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">indoor air quality</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">thermal comfort</a> credit applies one point to the following certification types: New Construction, Schools, Retail, Data Centers, Warehouses and Distribution Centers, Hospitality, and Healthcare. The intent of this credit is to "promote occupants' productivity, comfort, and well-being by providing thermal comfort."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> Occupants' satisfaction and performance is directly affected by a building's thermal conditions as shown by laboratory and field research.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Energy reduction goals can be supported while improving thermal satisfaction. For example, research has shown providing occupants control over the thermostat or operable window allows for comfort across a wider range of temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The following steps are guidelines provided by the USGBC for LEED credit in thermal comfort.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> First, thermal comfort goals must be established by determining if indoor thermal variation is acceptable (or if it must be tightly controlled) and if natural conditioning is a benefit desired by the occupants. Based on those goals, a system must be chosen that considers if natural conditioning can fit the project. Then thermal controls should be established that give the occupant control over air speed, <a href="/wiki/Operative_temperature" title="Operative temperature">operative temperature</a>, radiant temperature, and <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">humidity</a> such as ceiling fans, thermostats, operable windows, <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_air_distribution" title="Underfloor air distribution">underfloor diffusers</a>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> Finally, a standard must be chosen that best fits the project from <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE_55" title="ASHRAE 55">ASHRAE Standard 55</a>-2013,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> ISO 7730-2005,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> or EN15251-2007.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> credit was updated in LEED v4 to include a simulation option for daylight analysis that uses Spatial Daylight Autonomy (<a href="/wiki/Daylighting#LEED_documentation" title="Daylighting">SDA</a>) and Annual Sunlight Exposure (<a href="/wiki/Daylighting#LEED_documentation" title="Daylighting">ASE</a>) metrics to evaluate daylight quality in LEED projects. SDA is a metric that measures the annual sufficiency of daylight levels in interior spaces and ASE describes the potential for visual discomfort by direct sunlight and glare. These metrics are approved by <a href="/wiki/Illuminating_Engineering_Society_of_North_America" title="Illuminating Engineering Society of North America">IES</a> and described at the LM-83-12 standard.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> LEED recommend a minimum of 300 <a href="/wiki/Lux" title="Lux">lux</a> for at least 50% of total occupied hours of the year for 55% or more square meters (square feet) of the floor occupied area. The threshold recommended by LEED for ASE is that no more than 10% of regularly occupied floor area can be exposed to more than 1000 lux of direct sunlight for more than 250 hours per year. Additionally, LEED requires window shades to be closed when more than 2% of a space is subject to direct sunlight above 1000 lux. According to Reinhart<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> the direct sunlight requirement is a very stringent approach that can disable good daylight design from achieving this credit. Reinhart propose the application of the direct sunlight criterion only in spaces that require stringent control of sunlight (e.g. desks, white boards and etc.).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_related_research_notes">Other related research notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other related research notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2003 analysis of the savings from green building found from a review of 60 LEED buildings that the buildings were, on average, 25-30% more energy efficient. However, it also attributed substantial benefits to the increased productivity from the better ventilation, temperature control, lighting control, and reduced <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_pollution" class="mw-redirect" title="Indoor air pollution">indoor air pollution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2008, LEED (and similar <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a>) buildings had mostly been evaluated by case studies. From a purely financial perspective, in 2008 several studies found that LEED for-rent office spaces generally charged higher rent and had higher occupancy rates. CoStar collects data on properties. The extra cost for the minimum benefit has been estimated at 3%, with an additional 2.5% for silver.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> More recent studies have confirmed these earlier findings in that certified buildings achieve significantly higher rents, sale prices and occupancy rates as well as lower capitalization rates potentially reflecting lower investment risk.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>LEED focuses on the design of the building and not on its actual energy consumption, and therefore it has suggested that LEED buildings should be tracked to discover whether the potential energy savings from the design are being used in practice.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directory_of_LEED-certified_projects">Directory of LEED-certified projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Directory of LEED-certified projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. Green Building Council provides an online directory of U.S. LEED-certified projects.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Canada Green Building Council provides an online directory<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> of LEED Canada-certified projects.</p>
<p>In 2012 the USGBC launched GBIG, the Green Building Information Gateway,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> in an effort to connect green building efforts and projects from all over the world. It provides searchable access to a database of <i>activities, buildings, places and collections</i> of green building-related information from many sources and programs, as well as, specifically provides information about LEED projects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_accreditation">Professional accreditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Professional accreditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Building Certification Institute describes Professional Accreditation as follows: "LEED Professional Credentials demonstrate current knowledge of green building technologies, best practices, and the rapidly evolving LEED Rating Systems. They show differentiation in a growing and competitive industry, and they allow for varied levels of specialization. A LEED Professional Credential provides employers, policymakers, and other stakeholders with assurances of an individual's level of competence and is the mark of the most qualified, educated, and influential green building professionals in the marketplace."<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Credentials include the LEED Green Associate and the various types of specialized LEED Accredited Professionals (AP).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_and_disadvantages">Benefits and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Benefits and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg/220px-Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg/330px-Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg/440px-Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2178" data-file-height="1581" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Eco-indulgent_apartments.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Real_estate_developers" class="mw-redirect" title="Real estate developers">Real estate developers</a> have begun to use LEED certification and a building's green status as selling points</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>LEED certified buildings are intended to use resources more efficiently when compared to conventional buildings simply built to code. However, analysis of energy and water use data from New York City shows that LEED certification does not necessarily make a building more energy or water efficient.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>Often, when a LEED rating is pursued, the cost of initial design and construction rises. There may be a lack of abundant availability of manufactured building components that meet LEED specifications. Pursuing LEED certification for a project is an added cost in itself as well. This added cost comes in the form of USGBC correspondence, LEED design-aide consultants, and the hiring of the required <a href="/wiki/Building_commissioning" class="mw-redirect" title="Building commissioning">Commissioning Authority</a> (CxA)—all of which would not necessarily be included in an environmentally responsible project, unless it also sought a LEED rating.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, these higher initial costs can be effectively mitigated by the savings incurred over time due to the lower-than-industry-standard operational costs typical of a LEED certified building. This <a href="/wiki/Whole-life_cost" title="Whole-life cost">Life cycle costing</a> is a method for assessing the total cost of ownership, taking into account all costs of acquiring, owning and operating, and the eventual disposal of a building. Additional economic payback may come in the form of employee productivity gains incurred as a result of working in a healthier environment. Studies suggest that an initial up-front investment of 2% extra yields over ten times that initial investment over the life cycle of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further, the USGBC has stated support for the <a href="/wiki/2030_Challenge" class="mw-redirect" title="2030 Challenge">Architecture 2030</a>, an effort that has set a goal of using no fossil-fuel, greenhouse-gas-emitting energy to operate by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the progression of sustainable design from simply meeting local buildings codes to USGBC LEED<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> (Certified, Silver, Gold and Platinum) to the Architecture 2030 Challenge,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Living_Building_Challenge" title="Living Building Challenge">Living Building Challenge</a> is currently the most stringent sustainable design protocol. The LBC<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> sets 20 imperatives that compel building owners, designers, operators and tenants beyond current USGBC<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> LEED rating levels.</p>
<p>LEED is a design tool and not a performance measurement tool. It is also not yet climate-specific, although the newest version hopes to address this weakness partially. Because of this, designers may make materials or design choices that garner a LEED point, even though they may not be the most site- or climate-appropriate choice available. On top of this, LEED is also not energy-specific. Since it only measures the overall performances, builders are free to choose how to achieve points under various categories. A USA TODAY review showed that 7,100 certified commercial building projects targeted easy and cheap green points, such as creating healthy spaces and providing educational displays in the building.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Few builders would really adopt renewable energy because the generators for those energy resources, such as solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a>, are costly. Builders game the rating system and use certain performances to compensate for the others, making energy conservation the weakest part in the overall evaluation.</p>
<p>LEED is a measurement tool for <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and it is developed and continuously modified by workers in the green building industry, especially in the ten largest metro areas in the U.S.; however, LEED certified buildings have been slower to penetrate small and mid-major markets.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Also, some criticism suggests that the LEED rating system is not sensitive and does not vary enough with regard to local environmental conditions. For instance, a building in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> would receive the same credit as a building in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> for water conservation, though the principle is more important in the latter case. Another complaint is that its certification costs require money that could be used to make the building in question even more sustainable. Many critics have noted that compliance and certification costs have grown faster than staff support from the USGBC.</p>
<p>For existing buildings LEED has developed LEED-EB. Research has demonstrated that buildings that can achieve LEED-EB equivalencies can generate a tremendous ROI<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In a 2008 white paper by the Leonardo Academy comparing LEED-EB buildings vs. data from BOMA's Experience Exchange Report 2007 demonstrated LEED-EB certified buildings achieved superior operating cost savings in 63% of the buildings surveyed ranging from $4.94 to $15.59 per square foot of floor space, with an average valuation of $6.68 and a median valuation of $6.07.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition the overall cost of LEED-EB implementation and certification ranged from $0.00 to $6.46 per square foot of floor space, with an average of $2.43 per square foot demonstrating that implementation is not expensive, especially in comparison to cost savings. These costs should be significantly reduced if automation and technology are integrated into the implementation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incentive_programs">Incentive programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Incentive programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many federal, state, and local governments and school districts have adopted various types of LEED initiatives and incentives. A full listing of government and school LEED initiatives can be found online<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and is updated regularly.</p>
<p>Some areas have implemented or are considering incentives for LEED-certified buildings.</p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> adopted a measure providing an automatic 100% real property tax exemption of the assessed property value for newly constructed or rehabilitated commercial or residential properties that earn a minimum of LEED Certified.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the state of <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, construction materials for a qualifying LEED building are exempt from local taxes. Pieces of construction that are deemed "inseparable" parts, such as <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drywall" title="Drywall">drywall</a>, qualify.</p>
<p>The state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> passed its High Performance Buildings Act in 2008, requiring all new public construction and renovation of buildings greater than 7,500 square feet to meet at least the LEED Silver standard, or two Green Globes. Between 2009 and 2014, the state is required to fund half of the required additional cost for public school construction or renovation to attain that standard.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> is considering tax-based incentives for LEED buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many local governments have adopted LEED incentive programs. Program incentives include tax credits, tax breaks, density bonuses, reduced fees, priority or expedited permitting, free or reduced-cost technical assistance, grants and low-interest loans.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2013, the USGBC announced a program called <i>LEED Earth</i> that refunds LEED certification fees to the first LEED-certified project in the countries that so far lack one.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_LEED_buildings">Notable LEED buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Notable LEED buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg" class="image"><img alt="The Sota Construction Corporate Headquarters in Pittsburgh, LEED Platinum 2012." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg/220px-Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg" width="220" height="329" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg/330px-Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg/440px-Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3872" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sota_Construction_Office_Building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sota Construction Corporate Headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> earned LEED Platinum in 2012.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Philip_Merrill_Environmental_Center" title="Philip Merrill Environmental Center">Philip Merrill Environmental Center</a> is recognized as one of the "greenest" buildings ever constructed in the United States at the time when it was built. Sustainability issues ranging from energy use to material selection were given serious consideration throughout design and construction of this facility. It was the first building to receive a Platinum rating through the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a>'s LEED Rating System, version 1.0.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>When it opened in 2003, <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>'s 1,500,000-square-foot (140,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) <a href="/wiki/David_L._Lawrence_Convention_Center" title="David L. Lawrence Convention Center">David L. Lawrence Convention Center</a> was the first Gold LEED-certified convention center and largest "green" building in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The convention center subsequently earned Platinum certification in 2012, becoming the only convention center in the world with certifications for both the original building and new construction.<sup id="cite_ref-DLCC_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DLCC-1">[1]</a></sup> In October 2011 <a href="/wiki/Apogee_Stadium" title="Apogee Stadium">Apogee Stadium</a> on the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Texas" title="University of North Texas">University of North Texas</a> became the first newly built stadium in the country to achieve Platinum-level certification. In <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phipps_Conservatory_%26_Botanical_Gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Phipps Conservatory &amp; Botanical Gardens">Phipps Conservatory &amp; Botanical Gardens</a> visitors center has received a Silver certification,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> its Center for Sustainable Landscapes has received a Platinum certification along with fulfilling the <a href="/wiki/Living_Building_Challenge" title="Living Building Challenge">Living Building Challenge</a> for net-zero energy,<sup id="cite_ref-sustainablelandscapes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sustainablelandscapes-4">[4]</a></sup> and its <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> production facility has received Platinum certification, the first and only greenhouse so certified.<sup id="cite_ref-greenhouse_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenhouse-3">[3]</a></sup> Also in Pittsburgh, Sota Construction Services completed construction on its new 7,500 sq. ft. corporate headquarters,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> which features a super-efficient thermal envelope using <a href="/wiki/Cob_(material)" title="Cob (material)">cob</a> walls, along with other energy-saving features like a <a href="/wiki/Geothermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal">geothermal</a> well, radiant heat flooring, roof-mounted solar panel array, and <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">daylighting</a> features. It earned a LEED Platinum rating in 2012 and received one of the highest scores by percentage of total points earned in any LEED category, making it the "greenest" building in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and in the top ten greenest in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 2009–2010, the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> was undergoing a $550&#160;million renovation, with $120&#160;million spent in an effort to transform the building into a more <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficient</a> and eco-friendly structure.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Receiving a gold LEED rating in September 2011, the Empire State Building was at the time the tallest LEED-certified building in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coastal_Maine_Botanical_Gardens" title="Coastal Maine Botanical Gardens">Coastal Maine Botanical Gardens</a> <a href="/wiki/Bosarge_Family_Education_Center" title="Bosarge Family Education Center">Bosarge Family Education Center</a> completed in 2011 achieved LEED Platinum certification and has become known as Maine's "greenest building."<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2012, <a href="/wiki/Soldier_Field" title="Soldier Field">Soldier Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> stadium certified LEED.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2014, the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_49ers" title="San Francisco 49ers">San Francisco 49ers</a>' <a href="/wiki/Levi%27s_Stadium" title="Levi's Stadium">Levi's Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> became the first venue in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> to earn a LEED Gold certification, setting the bar and expectations for future <a href="/wiki/NFL" class="mw-redirect" title="NFL">NFL</a> stadiums. The <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Vikings" title="Minnesota Vikings">Minnesota Vikings</a> look to match and exceed the expectations set by the 49ers when they open <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bank_Stadium" title="U.S. Bank Stadium">U.S. Bank Stadium</a> in 2016.</p>
<p>The Cashman Equipment building is the first construction equipment dealership to receive LEED certification. It is the largest LEED <a href="/wiki/Industrial_park" title="Industrial park">industrial complex</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>. <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_Inc." title="Caterpillar Inc.">Caterpillar</a> corporate has rewritten their development guidelines for new facilities based on this building.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Letterman_Digital_Arts_Center" title="Letterman Digital Arts Center">Letterman Digital Arts Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Presidio_of_San_Francisco" title="Presidio of San Francisco">Presidio</a>, the headquarters for various <a href="/wiki/Lucasfilm" title="Lucasfilm">Lucasfilm</a> companies, including visual effects house <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Light_and_Magic" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial Light and Magic">Industrial Light and Magic</a>, earned a Gold certification. It was built almost entirely from the recycled remains of the building it replaced, the <a href="/wiki/Letterman_Army_Hospital" title="Letterman Army Hospital">Letterman Army Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cuyahoga_County_Public_Library" title="Cuyahoga County Public Library">Cuyahoga County Public Library</a> system is building newer branches so that they could be LEED Certified. In the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Garfield_Heights,_Ohio" title="Garfield Heights, Ohio">Garfield Heights, Ohio</a> new branch, the library used green materials, the lighting of Garfield Heights Branch lightens or dims when there is ample daylight. The glass windows have light sensors that work with lights inside the library. Also, the library uses a <a href="/wiki/Raised_floor" title="Raised floor">raised floor</a> and has recyclable carpet tiles.</p>
<p>Opening in September 2012, Siemens' <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal" title="The Crystal">The Crystal</a> became the world's first building awarded LEED Platinum and <a href="/wiki/BREEAM" title="BREEAM">BREEAM</a> Outstanding status.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Generating its own energy, using solar power and ground source heat pumps, no fossil fuels are burnt within the building. Its extensive use of <a href="/wiki/KNX_(standard)" title="KNX (standard)">KNX</a> technologies to automate the building's environmental controls has set the benchmark for sustainable buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>When it received LEED Platinum in 2012, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Hydro_Place" title="Manitoba Hydro Place">Manitoba Hydro Place</a> in downtown <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> was the most energy efficient office tower in North America and the only office tower in Canada to receive the Platinum rating. The office tower employs south facing winter gardens to capture the maximum amount of solar energy during the harsh Manitoba winters, and uses glass extensively to maximise natural light.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id=".22LEED_brain.22"><span id="LEED_brain"></span>"LEED brain"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: &quot;LEED brain&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Auden_Schendler" title="Auden Schendler">Auden Schendler</a> and Randy Udall, respectively a LEED-accredited professional who is the director of environmental affairs at the Aspen Skiing Company and a Colorado-based environmentalist, published an article titled "LEED is Broken; Let's Fix It", in which they criticized numerous aspects of the LEED certification process, which they characterized as "costly, slow, brutal, confusing, and unwieldy ... that makes green building more difficult then it needs to be..."<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Schendler and Udall also identify an environmentalist fallacy which they call "LEED brain", in which the public relations value of LEED certification begins to drive the development of buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Writer <a href="/wiki/David_Owen" title="David Owen">David Owen</a>, in his book <i>Green Metropolis</i> give as an example of "LEED brain" the building by <a href="/wiki/Gap_Inc." title="Gap Inc.">Gap Inc.</a> of a green showcase building in <a href="/wiki/San_Bruno,_California" title="San Bruno, California">San Bruno, California</a> that, in itself, was a paradigm of environmentally-friendliness, but by its location, 16 miles from the company's corporate headquarters in downtown <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, and 15 miles from Gap's corporate campus in <a href="/wiki/Mission_Bay_(San_Francisco)" title="Mission Bay (San Francisco)">Mission Bay</a>, was actually harmful to the environment by forcing Gap employees to drive more miles, and the addition of shuttle buses between the various buildings. Owen points out that "no bus is as green as an elevator."<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his book <i>Walkable City</i>, city planner Jeff Speck provides another example of "LEED brain": the federal <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> relocated its Region 7 Headquarters from downtown <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City, Missouri</a>, to a LEED-certified building 20 miles away in the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Lenexa,_Kansas" title="Lenexa, Kansas">Lenexa, Kansas</a>, causing many of the agency's 627 employees to drive additional miles to and from work. Karl Benfield of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a> estimated that the carbon emissions associated with the additional miles driven were almost three times higher than before, a change from 0.39 metric tons per person per month to 1.08 metric tons of carbon dioxide per person per month. Speck writes that "The carbon saved by the new buildings LEED status, if any, will be a small fraction of the carbon wasted by its location."<sup id="cite_ref-speck_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speck-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Speck and Owen make the point that a building-centric standard such as LEED, which doesn't sufficiently account for the location in which the building stands, will inevitably undervalue the strong positive effect of people living together in cities, which are inherently environmentally efficient, especially when compared to the automobile-oriented sprawl.<sup id="cite_ref-speck_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speck-92">[92]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
