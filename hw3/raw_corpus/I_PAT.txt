<p><b>I = PAT</b> is the mathematical notation of a formula put forward to describe the <a href="/wiki/Human_impacts_on_the_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Human impacts on the environment">impact of human activity on the environment</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>I</b> = <b>P</b> × <b>A</b> × <b>T</b></dd>
</dl>
<p>The expression equates <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">human impact on the environment</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Product_(mathematics)" title="Product (mathematics)">product</a> of three <a href="/wiki/Independent_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent variable">factors</a>: <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">Population</a>, <a href="/wiki/Affluence" class="mw-redirect" title="Affluence">Affluence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">Technology</a>. It is similar in form to the <a href="/wiki/Kaya_identity" title="Kaya identity">Kaya identity</a> which applies specifically to emissions of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.</p>
<p>The validity of expressing environmental impact as a simple product of independent factors, and the factors that should be included and their comparative importance, have been the subject of debate among environmentalists. In particular, some have drawn attention to potential inter-relationships among the three factors; and others have wished to stress other factors not included in the formula, such as political and social structures, and the scope for beneficial, as well as harmful, environmental actions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The equation was developed in 1970 during the course of a debate between <a href="/wiki/Barry_Commoner" title="Barry Commoner">Barry Commoner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Holdren" title="John Holdren">John Holdren</a>. Commoner argued that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impacts</a> in the United States were caused primarily by changes in its production technology following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and aimed his thoughts on present day deteriorating environmental conditions in the U.S. Ehrlich and Holdren argued that all three factors were important and emphasized in particular the role of human <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a>, but focused more on a broader scale, being less specific in space and time.<sup id="cite_ref-End_of_Population_Growth_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-End_of_Population_Growth-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The equation can aid in understanding some of the factors affecting human impacts on the environment,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but it has also been cited as a basis for many of the dire environmental predictions of the 1970s by <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Wald" title="George Wald">George Wald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denis_Hayes" title="Denis Hayes">Denis Hayes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lester_R._Brown" title="Lester R. Brown">Lester Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Dubos" title="René Dubos">René Dubos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Dillon_Ripley" title="Sidney Dillon Ripley">Sidney Ripley</a> that did not come to pass.<sup id="cite_ref-bailey_2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bailey_2000-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Neal_Koblitz" title="Neal Koblitz">Neal Koblitz</a> classified equations of this type as "mathematical <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>" and criticized Ehrlich's use of them in the media (e.g. on <a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show" title="The Tonight Show">The Tonight Show</a>) to sway the general public.<sup id="cite_ref-koblitz_1981_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koblitz_1981-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_three_factors">The three factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The three factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">World population</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Human overpopulation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/300px-Population_curve.svg.png" width="300" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/450px-Population_curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/600px-Population_curve.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="325" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Population (est.) 10,000 BC – 2000 AD.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the <b>I=PAT</b> equation, the variable <b>P</b> represents the population of an area, such as the world. Since the rise of industrial societies, human population has been increasing exponentially. This has caused <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> and many others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to postulate that this growth would continue until checked by widespread hunger and famine (see <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_growth_model" title="Malthusian growth model">Malthusian growth model</a>).</p>
<p>The United Nations and the <a href="/wiki/US_Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="US Census Bureau">US Census Bureau</a> project that world population will increase from 7.4 billion today in 2016 (up from 7.0 billion forecast in 2005) to about 9.7 billion by 2050 (up from 9.2 billion).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These projections take into consideration that population growth has slowed in recent years as women are having fewer children. This phenomenon is the result of <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic transition</a> all over the world. The UN projects that human population might stabilize around 11.2 billion by 2100 (up from 9.2 billion).<sup id="cite_ref-End_of_Population_Growth_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-End_of_Population_Growth-1">[1]</a></sup> However, since the world population is set to keep rising for the next few decades, this factor of the <b>I=PAT</b> equation will likely keep increasing <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on the environment</a> for the near future.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts_of_population">Environmental impacts of population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increased population increases humans' environmental impact in many ways, which include but are not limited to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Increased <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> - Results in habitat loss for other species.</li>
<li>Increased <a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_use&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Resource use (page does not exist)">resource use</a> - Results in changes in land cover</li>
<li>Increased <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> - Can cause sickness and damages <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Affluence">Affluence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Affluence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">Consumption (economics)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png/300px-World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png" width="300" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png/450px-World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png/600px-World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png 2x" data-file-width="941" data-file-height="742" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:World_GDP_Capita_1-2003_A.D.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
World GDP per capita (in 1990 <a href="/wiki/Geary-Khamis_dollars" class="mw-redirect" title="Geary-Khamis dollars">Geary-Khamis dollars</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The variable <b>A</b>, in the <b>I=PAT</b> equation stands for <a href="/wiki/Affluence" class="mw-redirect" title="Affluence">affluence</a>. It represents the average consumption of each person in the population. As the consumption of each person increases, the total environmental impact increases as well. A common proxy for measuring consumption is through <a href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a>. While GDP per capita measures production, it is often assumed that consumption increases when production increases. GDP per capita has been rising steadily over the last few centuries and is driving up human impact in the <b>I=PAT</b> equation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts_of_affluence">Environmental impacts of affluence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of affluence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increased consumption significantly increases human environmental impact. This is because each product consumed has wide-ranging effects on the environment. For example, the construction of a car has the following environmental impacts:</p>
<ul>
<li>605,664 gallons of water for parts and tires<sup id="cite_ref-Phillis_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillis-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>682&#160;lbs. of pollution at a mine for the <a href="/wiki/Lead_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead battery">lead battery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillis_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillis-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>2178&#160;lbs. of discharge into water supply for the 22&#160;lbs. of copper contained in the car.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillis_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillis-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The more cars per capita, the greater the impact. Ecological impacts of each product are far reaching, increases in consumption quickly result in large impacts on the environment through direct and indirect sources.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_technology" title="Environmental technology">Environmental technology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">Appropriate technology</a></div>
<p>The <b>T</b> variable in the <b>I=PAT</b> equation represents how resource intensive the production of affluence is; how much environmental impact is involved in creating, transporting and disposing of the goods, services and amenities used. Improvements in efficiency can reduce resource intensiveness, reducing the T multiplier. Since technology can affect environmental impact in many different ways, the unit for <b>T</b> is often tailored for the situation <b>I=PAT</b> is being applied to. For example, for a situation where the human impact on climate change is being measured, an appropriate unit for <b>T</b> might be greenhouse gas emissions per unit of GDP.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts_of_technology">Environmental impacts of technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increases in efficiency can reduce overall environmental impact. However, since <b>P</b> has increased exponentially, and <b>A</b> has also increased drastically, the overall environmental impact, <b>I</b>, has still increased.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Criticisms of the "I=PAT" formula</p>
<ul>
<li>Too Simplistic for complex problem</li>
<li>Inter dependencies between variables</li>
<li>General sweeping assumptions of variables affect toward environmental impact</li>
<li>Cultural differences cause wide variation in impact</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interdependencies">Interdependencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interdependencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The I=PAT equation has been criticized for being too simplistic by assuming that P, A, and T are independent of each other. In reality, at least 7 interdependencies between P, A, and T could exist, indicating that it is more correct to rewrite the equation as I = f(P,A,T).<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott.2C_B._2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott.2C_B._2010-11">[11]</a></sup> For example, a doubling of technological efficiency, or equivalently a reduction of the T-factor by 50%, does not necessarily reduce the environmental impact (I) by 50% if efficiency induced price reductions stimulate additional consumption of the resource that was supposed to be conserved, a phenomenon called the <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effect (conservation)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jevons_Paradox" class="mw-redirect" title="Jevons Paradox">Jevons Paradox</a>. As was shown by Alcott,<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott.2C_B._2010_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott.2C_B._2010-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:Fig. 5</sup> despite significant improvements in the carbon intensity of GDP (i.e., the efficiency in carbon use) since 1980, world fossil energy consumption has increased in line with economic and population growth. Similarly, an extensive historical analysis of technological efficiency improvements has conclusively shown that improvements in the efficiency of energy and material use were almost always outpaced by economic growth, resulting in a net increase in resource use and associated pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neglect_of_beneficial_human_impacts">Neglect of beneficial human impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Neglect of beneficial human impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have also been comments that this model depicts people as being purely detrimental to the environment, ignoring any <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Environmental_restoration" title="Environmental restoration">restoration</a> efforts that societies have made.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neglect_of_political_and_social_contexts">Neglect of political and social contexts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Neglect of political and social contexts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another major criticism of the I=PAT model is that it ignores the political context and decision making structures of countries and groups. This means the equation does not account for varying degrees of power, influence, and responsibility of individuals over environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Also, the P factor does not account for the complexity of social structures or behaviors, resulting in blame being placed on the global poor.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> I=PAT does not account for sustainable resource use among some poor and indigenous populations, unfairly characterizing these populations whose cultures support low impact practices.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_implications">Policy implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I_%3D_PAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Policy implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of the interdependencies between P, A, and T and potential rebound effects, policies aimed at decreasing environmental impacts through reductions in P, A, and T may not only be very difficult to implement (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Population_control" title="Population control">population control</a> and material sufficiency and <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a> movements have been very controversial) but also are likely to be rather ineffective compared to rationing (i.e., quotas) or <a href="/wiki/Pigovian_tax" title="Pigovian tax">Pigouvian taxation</a> of resource use or pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott.2C_B._2010_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott.2C_B._2010-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
