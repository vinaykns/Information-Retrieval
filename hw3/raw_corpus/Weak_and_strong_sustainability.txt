			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Weak and strong sustainability</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>Although related subjects, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> are different concepts. <i>Weak sustainability</i> is the idea within <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a>, which states that '<a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a>' can substitute '<a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>'. It is based upon the work of Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a><sup id="cite_ref-solow74_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solow74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and John Hartwick,.<sup id="cite_ref-hartwick77_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hartwick77-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Contrary to weak sustainability, <i>strong sustainability</i> assumes that "human capital" and "natural capital" are complementary, but not interchangeable.</p>
<p>This idea received more political attention as sustainable development discussions evolved in the late 1980s and early 1990s. A key landmark was the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Summit" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Summit">Rio Summit</a> in 1992 where the vast majority of nation-states committed themselves to sustainable development. This commitment was demonstrated by the signing of <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a>, a global action plan on sustainable development.</p>
<p>Weak sustainability has been defined using concepts like human capital and natural capital.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Human (or produced) capital incorporates resources such as infrastructure, labour and knowledge. Natural capital covers the stock of environmental assets such as fossil fuels, biodiversity and other ecosystem structures and functions relevant for <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>. In very weak sustainability, the overall stock of man-made capital and natural capital remains constant over time. It is important to note that, unconditional substitution between the various kinds of capital is allowed within weak sustainability. This means that natural resources may decline as long as human capital is increased. Examples include the degradation of the ozone layer, tropical forests and coral reefs if accompanied by benefits to human capital. An example of the benefit to human capital could include increased financial profits.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> If capital is left constant over time <a href="/wiki/Intergenerational_equity" title="Intergenerational equity">intergenerational equity</a>, and thus Sustainable Development, is achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-figge_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-figge-9">[9]</a></sup> An example of weak sustainability could be mining coal and using it for production of electricity. The natural resource coal, is replaced by a manufactured good which is electricity. The electricity is then in turn used to improve domestic life quality (e.g. cooking, lighting, heating, refrigeration and operating boreholes to supply water in some villages) and for industrial purposes (growing the economy by producing other resources using machines that are electricity operated.)</p>
<p>Case studies of weak sustainability in practice have had both positive and negative results. The concept of weak sustainability still attracts a lot of criticism. Some even suggest that the concept of sustainability is redundant. Other approaches are advocated, including ‘social bequests’, which focus the attention away from neoclassical theory altogether.</p>
<p>Strong sustainability assumes that the economic and environmental capital is complimentary, but not interchangeable. Strong sustainability accepts there are certain functions that the environment performs that cannot be duplicated by humans or human made capital. The ozone layer is one example of an ecosystem service that is crucial for human existence, forms part of natural capital, but is difficult for humans to duplicate.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike weak sustainability, strong sustainability puts the emphasis on ecological scale over economic gains. This implies that nature has a right to exist and that it has been borrowed and should be passed on from one generation to the next still intact in its original form.</p>
<p>An example of strong sustainability could be the manufacturing of office carpet tiles from used car tyres. In this scenario, office carpets and other products are manufactured from used motorcar tyres that would have been sent to a landfill.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_theory">Origins and theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_approach_to_sustainability_and_intergenerational_equity">Capital approach to sustainability and intergenerational equity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Capital approach to sustainability and intergenerational equity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To understand the concept of weak sustainability, it is first necessary to explore the capital approach to sustainability. This is key to the idea of intergenerational equity. This implies that a fair distribution of resources and assets between generations exists. Decision makers, both in theory and practice, need a concept that enables assessment in order to decide if intergenerational equity is achieved. The capital approach lends itself to this task. In this context we must distinguish between the different types of capital. Human capital (e.g. skills, knowledge) and natural capital (e.g. minerals, water) tend to be the most frequently cited examples. Within the concept it is believed that the amount of capital a generation has at its disposal is decisive for its development. A development is then called sustainable when it leaves the capital stock at least unchanged.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_development">Sustainable development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sustainable development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The weak sustainability paradigm stems from the 1970s. It began as an extension of the neoclassical theory of economic growth, accounting for non-renewable natural resources as a factor of production.<sup id="cite_ref-solow74_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solow74-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hartwick77_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hartwick77-4">[4]</a></sup> However, it only really came into the mainstream in the 1990s within the context of sustainable development discourse. At its inception, sustainability was interpreted as a requirement to preserve, intact, the environment as we find it today in all its forms. The <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Report</a>, for example, stated that ‘The loss of plant and animal species can greatly limit the options of future generations. The result is that sustainable development requires the conservation of plant and animal species’.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_theory">Development of theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development of theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wilfred Beckerman,<sup id="cite_ref-beck94_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beck94-14">[14]</a></sup> posits that the absolutist concept of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> given above is morally repugnant. The largest part of the worlds population live in acute poverty. Taking that as well as the acute degradation into account, one could justify using up vast resources in an attempt to preserve certain species from extinction. These species providing no real benefit for society other than a possible value for the knowledge of their continued existence. He argues that such a task would involve using resources that could have instead been devoted to more pressing world concerns. Examples include - increasing access to clean drinking water or sanitation in the <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a>. mhf shifted their attention to the idea of ‘weak’ sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-beck94_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beck94-14">[14]</a></sup> This allows for some natural resources to decrease as long as sufficient compensation is provided by increases in other resources. The result usually was an increase in human capital. This compensation is in the form of sustained human welfare. This is illustrated in a well-regarded definition provided by <a href="/wiki/David_Pearce_(economist)" title="David Pearce (economist)">David Pearce</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> who is the author of numerous works on sustainability. He defines sustainability as implying something about maintaining the level of human welfare (or well-being) so that it may improve, but never declines (or, not more than temporarily). This implies sustainable development will not decrease over time.</p>
<p>Inter-generational equity assumes each following generation has at least as much capital at its disposal as the preceding generation. The idea of leaving capital stock at least unchanged is widely accepted. The question arises, whether or not one form of capital may be substituted by another.<sup id="cite_ref-figge_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-figge-9">[9]</a></sup> This is the focus of the debate between ‘weak’ and ‘strong’ sustainability, and how intergenerational equity is to be achieved.</p>
<p>It is also important to note that strong sustainability does not share the notion of inter-changeability. Since the nineties, there has been an ardent debate on the substitutability between natural and human-made capital. While "Weak Sustainability" supporters mainly believe that these are substitutable, "Strong Sustainability" followers generally contest the possibility of inter-changeability.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_governance_and_policy_recommendations">Role of governance and policy recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role of governance and policy recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The implementation of weak sustainability in <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> can be viewed theoretically and practically through Hartwick’s rule.<sup id="cite_ref-hartwick77_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hartwick77-4">[4]</a></sup> In resource economics, Hartwick’s rule defines the amount of investment in human capital that is needed to offset declining stocks of non-renewable resources. Solow <sup id="cite_ref-solow74_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solow74-1">[1]</a></sup> showed that, given a degree of substitutability between human capital and natural capital, one way to design a sustainable consumption program for an economy is to accumulate man-made capital. When this accumulation is sufficiently rapid the effect from the shrinking exhaustible resource stock is countered by the services from the increased human capital stock. Hartwick's rule, is often referred to as "invest resource rents", where ‘rent’ is payment to a factor of production (in this case capital) in excess of that needed to keep it in its present use. This requires that a nation invest all rent earned from exhaustible resources currently extracted.</p>
<p>Later, Pearce and Atkinson <sup id="cite_ref-p.26a_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.26a-17">[17]</a></sup> and Hamilton <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> added to Hartwick’s rule, by setting out a theoretical and empirical measure of net investment in human and natural capital (and later human capital) that became known as genuine savings. Genuine savings measures net changes in produced, natural and human capital stocks, valued in monetary terms.</p>
<p>The aim of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> therefore should be to keep genuine savings above or equal to zero. In this sense it is similar to <a href="/wiki/Green_accounting" title="Green accounting">green accounting</a>, which attempts to factor environmental costs into the financial results of operations. A key example of this is the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, who now regularly publishes a comparative and comprehensive set of genuine savings estimates for over 150 countries which is called ‘adjusted savings’.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weak_sustainability_in_practice">Weak sustainability in practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Weak sustainability in practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A prime example of a weak sustainability is the Government Pension Fund of Norway. <a href="/wiki/Statoil" title="Statoil">Statoil</a> ASA, a state-owned Norwegian oil company invested its surplus profits from petroleum into a pension portfolio to date worth over $325 billion. The oil, a type of natural capital, was exported in vast quantities by Norway. The resultant fund allows for long-lasting income for the population in exchange for a finite resource, actually increasing the total capital available for Norway above the original levels. This example shows how weak sustainability and substitution can be cleverly applied on a national scale, although it is recognised that its applications are very restricted on a global scale.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In this application, Hartwick’s rule would state that the pension fund was sufficient capital to offset the depletion of the oil resources.</p>
<p>A less positive case is that of the small Pacific nation of <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>. A substantial phosphate deposit was found on the island in 1900, and now approximately 80% of the island has been rendered uninhabitable after over 100 years of mining.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Concurrent with this extraction, Nauru’s inhabitants, over the last few decades of the twentieth century, have enjoyed a high <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a>. Money from the mining of phosphate enabled the establishment of a trust fund, which was estimated to be as much as $1 billion. However, chiefly as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Asian_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian financial crisis">Asian financial crisis</a>, the trust fund was almost entirely wiped out. This ‘development’ of Nauru followed the logic of weak sustainability, and almost led to complete environmental destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-ayres_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ayres-22">[22]</a></sup> This case presents a telling argument against weak sustainability, suggesting that a substitution of natural for man-made capital may not be reversible in the long-term.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_the_strong_vs._weak_sustainability_model">Criticisms of the strong vs. weak sustainability model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticisms of the strong vs. weak sustainability model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics#Weak_and_strong_sustainability" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics §&#160;Weak and strong sustainability</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Criticising_neoclassical_economics_.28weak_versus_strong_sustainability.29" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen §&#160;Criticising neoclassical economics (weak versus strong sustainability)</a></div>
<p>Martinez-Allier's address<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> concerns over the implications of measuring weak sustainability, after results of work conducted by Pearce &amp; Atkinson in the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-p.26a_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p.26a-17">[17]</a></sup> By their measure, most of the Northern, <a href="/wiki/Industrialised_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialised countries">industrialised countries</a> are deemed sustainable, as is the world economy as a whole. This point of view can be considered to be flawed since the world would (arguably) not be sustainable if all countries have the resource intensity rate and pollution rate of many industrialised countries. Industrialization does not necessarily equate to sustainability.</p>
<p>According to Pearce and Atkinson’s calculations, the Japanese economy is one of the most sustainable economies in the world. The reason for this is that its saving rate is so high. This trend still remains today and therefore exceeds depreciation on both natural and man-made capital. Thus, they suggest that it is the gross negligence of factors other than savings in measuring sustainability that makes weak sustainability an inappropriate concept.</p>
<p>The integrative sustainability model has the economy completely located within society and society completely located within the environment. In other words, the economy is a subset of society and society is completely dependent upon the environment. This interdependence means that any sustainability-related issue must be considered holistically.</p>

<p>Other inadequacies of the paradigm include the difficulties in measuring savings rates and the inherent problems in quantifying the many different attributes and functions of the biophysical world in monetary terms.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> By including all human and biophysical resources under the same heading of ‘capital’, the depleting of fossil fuels, reduction of biodiversity and so forth, are potentially compatible with sustainability. As Gowdy &amp; O'Hara <sup id="cite_ref-gowdy_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gowdy-26">[26]</a></sup> so aptly put it, "As long as the criterion of weak sustainability is met, with savings outstripping capital depletion, there is no conflict between the destruction of species and ecosystems or the depletion of fossil fuels, and the goal of sustainability."</p>
<p>Opposing weak sustainability, strong sustainability supporters contend that we need "a more small-scale decentralized way of life based upon greater self-reliance, so as to create a social and economic system less destructive towards nature." Strong sustainability does not make allowances for the substitution of human, and human made capital for Earth's land, water, and their biodiversity. The products created by mankind cannot replace the natural capital found in ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another critical weakness of the concept is related to environmental resilience. According to Van Den Bergh,<sup id="cite_ref-vdb_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vdb-28">[28]</a></sup> resilience can be considered as a global, structural stability concept, based on the idea that multiple, locally stable ecosystems can exist. Sustainability can thus be directly related to resilience. With this in mind, weak sustainability can cause extreme sensitivity to either natural disturbances (such as diseases in agriculture with little crop diversity) or economic disturbances (as outlined in the case study of Nauru above). This high level of sensitivity within regional systems in the face of external factors brings to attention an important inadequacy of weak sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-vdb_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vdb-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rejection_of_both_weak_and_strong_models">Rejection of both weak and strong models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weak_and_strong_sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rejection of both weak and strong models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some critics have gone one step further, dismissing the entire concept of sustainability. Beckerman’s influential work concludes that weak sustainability is, “redundant and illogical”.<sup id="cite_ref-beck94_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beck94-14">[14]</a></sup> He holds that sustainability only makes sense in its 'strong' form, but that "requires subscribing to a morally repugnant and totally impracticable objective." He goes as far to say that he regrets so much time has been wasted on the entire concept of sustainable development. Contradictorily, it could be argued that even weak sustainability measures are better than having no measures or action at all.</p>
<p>Others have suggested a better approach to sustainability would be that of "social bequests". This change would "free us from a 'zero-sum' game in which our gain is an automatic loss for future generations".<sup id="cite_ref-bromley_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bromley-29">[29]</a></sup> The social bequest approach looks at the problem in a different light by changing to what, rather than how much, we leave to future generations. When the problem is phrased as ‘how much’ this always implies that some amount of a resource should be used and some left. Daniel Bromley <sup id="cite_ref-bromley_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bromley-29">[29]</a></sup> uses the example of rainforests to illustrate his argument. If we decide to use 25% of a rainforest and leave the rest, but then the next time we make a decision we start all over again and use 25% of what’s left, and so on, eventually there will be no rainforest left. By focusing on bequests of specific rights and opportunities for future generations, we can remove ourselves from the "straightjacket of substitution and marginal tradeoffs of neoclassical theory".<sup id="cite_ref-ayres_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ayres-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
