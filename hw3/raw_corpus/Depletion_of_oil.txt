			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Oil depletion</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Depletion_of_oil&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Depletion of oil">Depletion of oil</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Oil depletion</b> is the decline in oil production of a well, <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil field</a>, or geographic area.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_peak_theory" title="Hubbert peak theory">Hubbert peak theory</a> makes predictions of production rates based on prior discovery rates and anticipated production rates. <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_curve" title="Hubbert curve">Hubbert curves</a> predict that the production curves of non-renewing resources approximate a <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">bell curve</a>. Thus, according to this theory, when the <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak of production</a> is passed, production rates enter an irreversible decline.<sup id="cite_ref-mkinghubbert1956_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mkinghubbert1956-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hirsch_report_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hirsch_report-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">Energy Information Administration</a> predicted in 2006 that world consumption of oil will increase to 98.3 million barrels per day (15,630,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/d) (mbd) in 2015 and 118 million barrels per day in 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With 2009 world oil consumption at 84.4 mbd,<sup id="cite_ref-tonto.eia.doe.gov_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tonto.eia.doe.gov-5">[5]</a></sup> reaching the projected 2015 level of consumption would represent an average annual increase between 2009 and 2015 of 2.7% per year.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_availability">Resource availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Resource availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">Oil reserves</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">World</a>'s natural oil supply is fixed because petroleum is <a href="/wiki/Formation_of_petroleum" class="mw-redirect" title="Formation of petroleum">naturally formed</a> far too slowly to be replaced at the rate at which it is being extracted. Over many millions of years, <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, and other plant and animal matter became buried in sediments on the ocean floor. When conditions were right – a lack of oxygen for decomposition, and sufficient depth and temperature of burial – these organic remains were converted into petroleum compounds, while the sediment accompanying them was converted into sandstone, siltstone, and other porous sedimentary rock. When capped by impermeable rocks such as shale, salt, or igneous intrusions, they formed the petroleum reservoirs which are exploited today.<sup id="cite_ref-national_geographic_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-national_geographic-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_decline_models">Production decline models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production decline models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_peak_theory" title="Hubbert peak theory">Hubbert peak theory</a></div>
<p>For the short and medium-term, oil production decline occurs in a predictable manner based on geological circumstances, governmental policies, and engineering practices. The shape of the decline curve varies depending upon whether one considers a well, a field, or a set of fields. In the longer term, technological developments have defied some of the predictions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_well_production_decline">Oil well production decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oil well production decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png/150px-Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png/225px-Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png/300px-Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Theoretical_Oil_Well_Production_Curve.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Theoretical oil production curve for a well with exponential decline</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An individual oil well usually produces at its maximum rate at the start of its life; the production rate eventually declines to a point at which it no longer <a href="/wiki/EROEI" class="mw-redirect" title="EROEI">produces profitable amounts</a>. The shape of the decline curve depends on the oil reservoir and the reservoir drive mechanism. Wells in water-drive and gas-cap drive reservoirs often produce at a near constant rate until the encroaching water or expanding gas cap reaches the well, causing a sudden decline in oil production. Wells in gas solution drive and oil expansion drive reservoirs have exponential or hyperbolic declines: rapid declines at first, then leveling off.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shape of production curve of an oil well can also be affected by a number of nongeologic factors:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>Well may be restricted by choice by lack of <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">market demand</a> or government <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>. This decreases the rate of decline, but will not change the well's total production significantly.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">Hydraulic fracturing</a> (fracking) or <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid#Other_applications" title="Hydrochloric acid">acidizing</a> may be used to cause a sharp spike in production, and may increase the recoverable reserves of a given well.</li>
<li>The field may undergo a secondary or <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">tertiary recovery</a> project, discussed in the next section.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_field_production_decline">Oil field production decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oil field production decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Individual oil wells are typically within multi-well <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>. As with individual wells, the production curves for oil fields vary depending on geology and how they are developed and produced. Some fields have symmetric bell-shaped production profiles, but it is more common that the period of inclining production is briefer and steeper than the subsequent decline. More than half the production usually occurs after a field has reached a peak or plateau.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Production profiles of many fields show distinct peaks, but for giant oil fields, it is more common for production to reach and maintain a plateau before declining. Once a field declines, it usually follows an exponential decline.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As this decline levels off, production can continue at relatively low rates. A number of oil fields in the U.S. have been producing for over 100 years.<sup id="cite_ref-McClintock_No._1_oil_well_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McClintock_No._1_oil_well-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Florence_Oil_Field_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Florence_Oil_Field-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oil field production curves can be modified by a number of factors:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>Production may be restricted by market conditions or government regulation.</li>
<li>A secondary recovery project, such as <a href="/wiki/Water_injection_(oil_production)" title="Water injection (oil production)">water</a> or gas injection, can repressurize the field and increase the total recovery.</li>
<li>the field may undergo an <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> project, such as drilling of wells for injection of <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>. This allows more oil to be coaxed out of the rock, increasing the ultimate production of the field.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-field_production_decline">Multi-field production decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multi-field production decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png/150px-Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png/225px-Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png/300px-Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Theoretical_Multiple_Oil_Field_Production_Curve.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hubbert-theory graph of multiple oil field production</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most oil is found in a small number of very large oil fields. According to Hubbert peak theory, production starts off slowly, rises faster and faster, then slows down and flattens until it reaches a peak, after which production declines. In the late stage, production often enters a period of exponential decline in which the decline becomes less and less steep. Oil production may never actually reach zero, but eventually becomes very low. Factors which can modify this curve include:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>Inadequate demand for oil, which reduces steepness of the curve and pushes its peak into the future.</li>
<li>Sharp price increases when the production peak is reached, as production fails to meet demand. If price increases cause a sharp drop in demand, a dip in the top of the curve may occur.</li>
<li>Development of new drilling technology or marketing of <a href="/wiki/Unconventional_oil" title="Unconventional oil">unconventional oil</a> can reduce the steepness of the decline as more oil is produced than initially anticipated.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>

<p>Oil production in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, provided one excludes Alaska, began by following the theoretical Hubbert curve for a few decades but is now deviating strongly from it. U.S. oil production reached a peak in 1970 and by the mid-2000s it had fallen to 1940s levels. In 1950, the United States produced over half the world's oil, but by 2005 that proportion had dropped to about 8%. In 2005, U.S. crude oil imports peaked at a rate twice as high as domestic production; since then, U.S. oil production has increased, and imports have fallen 41%.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The production peak in 1970 was predicted by one of the two projections put forward in 1956 by Hubbert. By 1972 all import quotas and controls on U.S. domestic production had been removed. Despite this, and despite the quadrupling of prices during the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, the production decline was not reversed in the lower 48 states until 2009. Crude oil production has since risen sharply from 2009 through 2014, so that the rate of US oil production in October 2014 was 81% higher than the average rate in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The actual U.S. production curve deviates from Hubbert's 1956 curve in significant ways:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>When oil surpluses created a glut on the market and low prices began causing demand and production curves to rise, regulatory agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Railroad_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Railroad Commission">Texas Railroad Commission</a> stepped in to restrain production.<sup id="cite_ref-national_geographic_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-national_geographic-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The curve peaked at a higher rate and sharper point than predicted.</li>
<li>Production fell after 1970, but started to recover and reached a lower secondary peak in 1988. This occurred because the supergiant <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay_Oil_Field" title="Prudhoe Bay Oil Field">Prudhoe Bay</a> field in Alaska was only discovered in 1968, and the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Alaska_Pipeline_System" title="Trans-Alaska Pipeline System">Trans-Alaska Pipeline System</a> (TAPS) was not completed until 1977. After 1988, Alaska production peaked and total U.S. production began to decline again. By 2005, Prudhoe Bay had produced over 75% of its oil.</li>
<li>Production increases in the 2010s</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_oil_production">World oil production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World oil production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The 1970 production peak in the U.S. caused many people to begin to question when the world production peak would occur. The peak of world production is known as <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">Peak oil</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_of_a_world_peak">Implications of a world peak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Implications of a world peak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Implications_of_peak_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Implications of peak oil">Possible effects and consequences of Peak Oil</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak</a> in oil production could result in a worldwide oil shortage, or it could not even be noticed as demand decreases in conjunction with increased prices. While past shortages stemmed from a temporary insufficiency of supply, crossing Hubbert's Peak would mean that the production of oil would continue to decline, and that demand for these products must be reduced to meet supply. The effects of such a shortage would depend on the rate of decline and the development and adoption of effective alternatives.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catastrophe">Catastrophe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Catastrophe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of fossil fuels allows humans to participate in takedown, which is the consumption of energy at a greater rate than it is being replaced. The industrial economy is currently heavily dependent on oil as a fuel and chemical feedstock. For example, over 90% of transportation in the United States relies on oil.</p>
<p>Since the 1940s, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> has dramatically increased its productivity, due largely to the use of chemical <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, fertilizers, and increased mechanisation. This process has been called the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>. The increase in food production has allowed world population to grow dramatically over the last 50 years. Pesticides rely upon oil as a critical ingredient, and fertilizers require natural gas. Farm machinery also requires oil.</p>
<p>Most or all of the uses of fossil fuels in agriculture can be replaced with alternatives. For example, by far the biggest fossil fuel input to agriculture is the use of natural gas as a hydrogen source for the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber-Bosch</a> fertilizer-creation process.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Natural gas is used simply because it is the cheapest currently available source of hydrogen; were that to change, other sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> powered by <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, could be used to provide the hydrogen for creating fertilizer without relying on fossil fuels.</p>
<p>Oil shortages may force a move to lower input "<a href="/wiki/Organic_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic agriculture">organic agriculture</a>" methods, which may be more <a href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour (economics)">labor</a>-intensive and require a population shift from urban to rural areas, reversing the trend towards <a href="/wiki/Urbanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a> which has predominated in industrial societies; however, some organic farmers using modern organic-farming methods have reported yields as high as those available from conventional farming, but without the use of fossil-fuel-intensive artificial fertilizers or pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible effect would derive from modern transportation and housing infrastructure. A large proportion of the developed world's population live in <a href="/wiki/Suburbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Suburbs">suburbs</a>, a type of low-density settlement designed with the <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> in mind. A movement to deal with this problem early, called "<a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>," seeks to develop the suburbs into higher density neighborhoods and use high density, mixed-use forms for new building projects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recession">Recession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Recession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A more modest scenario, assuming a slower rate of depletion or a smoother transition to alternative energy sources, could still cause substantial economic hardship such as a <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> or <a href="/wiki/Economic_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic depression">depression</a> due to higher energy prices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">Inflation</a> has also been linked to oil price spikes. However, economists disagree on the strength and causes of this association. See <a href="/wiki/Energy_crisis" title="Energy crisis">Energy crisis</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_food_prices">Rising food prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rising food prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Food vs fuel">food vs fuel</a></div>
<p>Rising oil prices cause rising food prices in three ways. First, increased equipment fuel costs drive higher prices. Second, transportation costs increase retail prices. Third, higher oil prices are causing farmers to switch from producing food crops to producing <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> crops. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Law_of_supply_and_demand" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of supply and demand">law of supply and demand</a> predicts that if fewer farmers are producing food the price of food will rise.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacement">Replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative considered likely by some is that oil will be replaced with <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> during the first half of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The replacement <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> would likely be <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a> would then replace the current oil-based economy. Another possible replacement fuel is <a href="/wiki/Sewage_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Sewage gas">biogas</a>, which is composed of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Methane has boiling point of −161&#160;°C, rather than hydrogen's -252.87&#160;°C, making methane a much easier fuel to condense.</p>
<p>Other people consider that the whole idea of "the hydrogen economy" is flawed. Compressed hydrogen has an <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of only 5.6 megajoules per liter. Robert Zubrin looks at the practical problems of using hydrogen as an energy storage medium in <i>Energy Victory: Winning the War on Terror by Breaking Free of Oil</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He considers that hydrogen is a very poor form of storage, and that <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a> would be better. This point is reiterated in <i>Beyond Oil and Gas: The Methanol Economy</i><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and in David MacKay's book described below.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geothermal_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal electricity">Geothermal power</a> is one source of sustainable energy that can produce hydrogen. Note that David MacKay has shown in his book <i>Sustainable Energy: Without the Hot Air</i><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> that geothermal can only provide a tiny fraction of the world's needs sustainably. In some areas located over <a href="/wiki/Geological_hotspot" class="mw-redirect" title="Geological hotspot">geological hotspots</a> (such as Iceland), geothermal makes more sense.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Solar</a> energy is a source of inexhaustible energy. There is more solar energy that reaches the surface of the Earth each hour than the amount of energy consumed by the world in a year.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The challenges of using the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>'s energy – energy which can be obtained either from <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> or from <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> – is that the energy needs to either be (1) stored in physical form of fuel for when it can be used in the future, or (2) transported directly as electricity, through transmission lines. Neither is <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">dispatchable</a>, as there is no control over when the sun will shine or when the wind will blow. There are, however, <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> plants using thermal storage that can store energy efficiently for up to 24 hours.</p>
