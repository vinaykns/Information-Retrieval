<p><b>Seasonal thermal energy storage</b> (or <b>STES</b>) is the storage of heat or cold for periods of up to several months. The thermal energy can be collected whenever it is available and be used whenever needed, such as in the opposing season. For example, heat from solar collectors or <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> from air conditioning equipment can be gathered in hot months for space heating use when needed, including during winter months. Waste heat from industrial process can similarly be stored and be used much later.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Or the natural cold of winter air can be stored for summertime air conditioning.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> STES stores can serve district heating systems, as well as single buildings or complexes. Among seasonal storages used for heating, the design peak annual temperatures generally are in the range of 27 to 80&#160;°C (80.6 to 176.0&#160;°F), and the temperature difference occurring in the storage over the course of a year can be several tens of degrees. Some systems use a heat pump to help charge and discharge the storage during part or all of the cycle. For cooling applications, often only circulation pumps are used. A less common term for STES technologies is interseasonal thermal energy storage<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> include <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> where ground storage provides 97% of yearly consumption without <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pumps</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-drake1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drake1-5">[5]</a></sup> and Danish pond storage with boosting.<sup id="cite_ref-ing2015-06-14_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing2015-06-14-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="STES_technologies">STES technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: STES technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several types of STES technology, covering a range of applications from single small buildings to community district heating networks. Generally, efficiency increases and the specific construction cost decreases with size.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Underground_thermal_energy_storage">Underground thermal energy storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Underground thermal energy storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ground-coupled_heat_exchanger" title="Ground-coupled heat exchanger">Ground-coupled heat exchanger</a></div>
<ul>
<li><b>UTES</b> (underground thermal energy storage), in which the storage medium may be geological strata ranging from earth or sand to solid bedrock, or aquifers. UTES technologies include:
<ul>
<li><b>ATES</b> (<a href="/wiki/Aquifer_thermal_energy_storage" title="Aquifer thermal energy storage">aquifer thermal energy storage</a>). An ATES store is composed of a doublet, totaling two or more wells into a deep aquifer that is contained between impermeable geological layers above and below. One half of the doublet is for water extraction and the other half for reinjection, so the aquifer is kept in hydrological balance, with no net extraction. The heat (or cold) storage medium is the water and the substrate it occupies. Germany’s <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_building" title="Reichstag building">Reichstag building</a> has been both heated and cooled since 1999 with ATES stores, in two aquifers at different depths.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the Netherlands there are now well over 1,000 ATES systems, which are now a standard construction option.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A significant system has been operating at Richard Stockton College (New Jersey) for several years.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-2">[2]</a></sup> ATES has a lower installation cost than BTES because usually fewer holes are drilled, but ATES has a higher operating cost. Also, ATES requires particular underground conditions to be feasible, including the presence of an aquifer.</li>
<li><b>BTES</b> (borehole thermal energy storage). BTES stores can be constructed wherever <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">boreholes</a> can be drilled, and are composed of one to hundreds of vertical boreholes, typically 155&#160;mm (6.102&#160;in) in diameter. Systems of all sizes have been built, including many quite large.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The strata can be anything from sand to crystalline hardrock, and depending on engineering factors the depth can be from 50 to 300 metres (164 to 984&#160;ft). Spacings have ranged from 3 to 8 metres (9.8 to 26.2&#160;ft). Thermal models can be used to predict seasonal temperature variation in the ground, including the establishment of a stable temperature regime which is achieved by matching the inputs and outputs of heat over one or more annual cycles. Warm-temperature seasonal heat stores can be created using borehole fields to store surplus heat captured in summer to actively raise the temperature of large thermal banks of soil so that heat can be extracted more easily (and more cheaply) in winter. Interseasonal Heat Transfer<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> uses water circulating in pipes embedded in asphalt solar collectors to transfer heat to Thermal Banks<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> created in borehole fields. A ground source heat pump is used in winter to extract the warmth from the Thermal Bank to provide space heating via <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_heating" title="Underfloor heating">underfloor heating</a>. A high Coefficient of Performance is obtained because the heat pump starts with a warm temperature of 25&#160;°C (77&#160;°F) from the thermal store, instead of a cold temperature of 10&#160;°C (50&#160;°F) from the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A BTES operating at Richard Stockton College since 1995 at a peak of about 29&#160;°C (84.2&#160;°F) consists of 400 boreholes 130 metres (427&#160;ft) deep under a 3.5-acre (1.4&#160;ha) parking lot. It has a heat loss of 2% over six months.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The upper temperature limit for a BTES store is 85&#160;°C (185&#160;°F) due to characteristics of the PEX pipe used for BHEs, but most do not approach that limit. Boreholes can be either grout- or water-filled depending on geological conditions, and usually have a life expectancy in excess of 100 years. Both a BTES and its associated district heating system can be expanded incrementally after operation begins, as at Neckarsulm, Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> BTES stores generally do not impair use of the land, and can exist under buildings, agricultural fields and parking lots. An example of one of the several kinds of STES illustrates well the capability of interseasonal heat storage. In Alberta, Canada, the homes of the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> (in operation since 2007), get 97% of their year-round heat from a district heat system that is supplied by solar heat from solar-thermal panels on garage roofs. This feat – a world record – is enabled by interseasonal heat storage in a large mass of native rock that is under a central park. The thermal exchange occurs via a cluster of 144 boreholes, drilled 37 metres (121&#160;ft) into the earth. Each borehole is 155&#160;mm (6.1&#160;in) in diameter and contains a simple heat exchanger made of small diameter plastic pipe, through which water is circulated. No heat pumps are involved.<sup id="cite_ref-drake1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drake1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>CTES</b> (cavern or mine thermal energy storage). STES stores are possible in flooded mines, purpose-built chambers, or abandoned underground oil stores (e.g. those mined into crystalline hardrock in Norway), if they are close enough to a heat (or cold) source and market.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Energy Pilings</b>. During construction of large buildings, BHE heat exchangers much like those used for BTES stores have been spiraled inside the cages of reinforcement bars for pilings, with concrete then poured in place. The pilings and surrounding strata then become the storage medium.</li>
<li><b>GIITS</b> (geo interseasonal insulated thermal storage). During construction of any building with a primary slab floor, an area approximately the footprint of the building to be heated, and &gt;1M in depth, is insulated on all 6 sides typically with <a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a> closed cell insulation. Pipes are used to transfer solar energy into the insulated area, as well as extracting heat as required on demand. If there is significant internal ground water flow, remedial actions are needed to prevent it.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_and_above_ground_technologies">Surface and above ground technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surface and above ground technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Pit Storage</b>. Lined, shallow dug pits that are filled with gravel and water as the storage medium are used for STES in many Danish district heating systems. Storages pits are covered with a layer of insulation and then soil, and are used for agriculture or other purposes. Marstal, Denmark’s system is a case study, initially providing 20% of the village’s year-round heat but now being expanded to provide twice that.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The worlds largest pit store (200,000 m3) was <a href="/wiki/Project_commissioning" title="Project commissioning">commissioned</a> in Denmark in 2015, and allows solar heat to provide 50% of the annual energy for the world's largest solar-enabled district heating system.<sup id="cite_ref-ing2015-06-14_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing2015-06-14-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><b>Large-scale thermal storage with water</b>. Large scale STES water storage tanks can be built above ground, insulated, and then covered with soil.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Horizontal heat exchangers</b>. For small installations, a heat exchanger of corrugated plastic pipe can be shallow-buried in a trench to create a STES.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Earth-bermed buildings</b>, with passive heat storage in surrounding soil.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conferences_and_organizations">Conferences and organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conferences and organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency's</a> <i>Energy Conservation through Energy Storage (ECES) Programme</i><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> has held triennial global energy conferences since 1981. The conferences originally focused exclusively on STES, but now that those technologies are mature other topics such as <a href="/wiki/Phase-change_material" title="Phase-change material">phase change materials (PCM)</a> and electrical energy storage are also being covered. Since 1985 each conference has had "stock" (for storage) at the end of its name; e.g. Ecostock, Thermastock.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> They are held at various locations around the world. Most recent was Innostock 2012 (the 12th International Conference on Thermal Energy Storage) in Lleida, Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Greenstock 2015 will be held in Beijing.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IEA-ECES programme continues the work of the earlier <i>International Council for Thermal Energy Storage</i> which from 1978 to 1990 had a quarterly newsletter and was initially sponsored by the U.S. Department of Energy. The newsletter was initially called <i>ATES Newsletter,</i> and after BTES became a feasible technology it was changed to <i>STES Newsletter.</i><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_STES_for_small.2C_passively_heated_buildings">Use of STES for small, passively heated buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use of STES for small, passively heated buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Small passively heated buildings typically use the soil adjoining the building as a low-temperature seasonal heat store that in the annual cycle reaches a maximum temperature similar to average annual air temperature, with the temperature drawn down for heating in colder months. Such systems are a feature of building design, as some simple but significant differences from 'traditional' buildings are necessary. At a depth of about 20 feet (6.1&#160;m) in the soil, the temperature is naturally stable within a year-round range,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> if the draw down does not exceed the natural capacity for solar restoration of heat. Such storage systems operate within a narrow range of storage temperatures over the course of a year, as opposed to the other STES systems described above for which large annual temperature differences are intended.</p>
<p>Two basic passive solar building technologies were developed in the US during the 1970s and 1980s. They utilize direct heat conduction to and from thermally isolated, moisture-protected soil as a seasonal storage medium for space heating, with direct conduction as the heat return method. In one method, "passive annual heat storage" (PAHS),<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> the building’s windows and other exterior surfaces capture solar heat which is transferred by conduction through the floors, walls, and sometimes the roof, into adjoining thermally buffered soil.</p>
<p>When the interior spaces are cooler than the storage medium, heat is conducted back to the living space.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The other method, “annualized geothermal solar” (AGS) uses a separate solar collector to capture heat. The collected heat is delivered to a storage device (soil, gravel bed or water tank) either passively by the convection of the heat transfer medium (e.g. air or water) or actively by pumping it. This method is usually implemented with a capacity designed for six months of heating.</p>
<p>A number of examples of the use of solar thermal storage from across the world include: <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_One" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffolk One">Suffolk One</a> a college in East Anglia, England that uses a thermal collector of pipe buried in the bus turning area to collect solar energy that is then stored in 18 100 metres (330&#160;ft) boreholes for use in winter heating. <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> in Canada uses solar thermal collectors on the garage roofs of 52 homes, which is then stored in an array of 35 metres (115&#160;ft) deep boreholes. The ground can reach temperatures in excess of 70&#160;°C which is then used to heat the houses passively. The scheme has been running successfully since 2007. In <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.solarge.org/index.php?id=1646">Brædstrup</a>, Denmark some 8,000 square metres (86,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) of solar thermal collectors are used to collect some 4,000,000 kWh/a similarly stored in an array of 50 metres (160&#160;ft) deep boreholes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_engineering">Liquid engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liquid engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Architect Matyas Gutai<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> obtained an EU grant to construct a house in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> which uses extensive water filled wall panels as heat collectors and reservoirs with underground heat storage water tanks. The design uses microprocessor control.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_buildings_with_internal_STES_water_tanks">Small buildings with internal STES water tanks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Small buildings with internal STES water tanks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of homes and small apartment buildings have demonstrated combining a large internal water tank for heat storage with roof-mounted solar-thermal collectors. Storage temperatures of 90&#160;°C (194&#160;°F) are sufficient to supply both domestic hot water and space heating. The first such house was MIT Solar House #1, in 1939. An eight-unit apartment building in <a href="/wiki/Oberburg" title="Oberburg">Oberburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> was built in 1989, with three tanks storing a total of 118&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (4,167 cubic feet) that store more heat than the building requires. Since 2011, that design is now being replicated in new buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, the “Zero Heating Energy House”, was built in 1997 in as part of the <a href="/wiki/IEA_Task_13" class="mw-redirect" title="IEA Task 13">IEA Task 13</a> low energy housing demonstration project. It stores water at temperatures up to 90&#160;°C (194&#160;°F) inside a 20&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (706 cubic feet) tank in the <a href="/wiki/Basement" title="Basement">basement</a>,.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>A similar example was built in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> in 2009, as a prototype. The <i>solar seasonal store</i><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> consists of a 23&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (812&#160;cu&#160;ft) tank, filled with water,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> which was installed in the ground, heavily insulated all around, to store heat from <a href="/wiki/Evacuated_tube#Evacuated_tube_collectors" class="mw-redirect" title="Evacuated tube">evacuated solar tubes</a> during the year. The system was installed as an experiment to heat the <i>world's first standardized pre-fabricated passive house</i><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway, Ireland</a>. The aim was to find out if this heat would be sufficient to eliminate the need for any electricity in the already highly efficient home during the winter months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_STES_in_greenhouses">Use of STES in greenhouses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use of STES in greenhouses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>STES is also used extensively for the heating of greenhouses.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> ATES is the kind of storage commonly in use for this application. In summer, the greenhouse is cooled with ground water, pumped from the “cold well” in the aquifer. The water is heated in the process, and is returned to the “warm well” in the aquifer. When the greenhouse needs heat, such as to extend the growing season, water is withdrawn from the warm well, becomes chilled while serving its heating function, and is returned to the cold well. This is a very efficient system of <a href="/wiki/Free_cooling" title="Free cooling">free cooling</a>, which uses only circulation pumps and no heat pumps.</p>
