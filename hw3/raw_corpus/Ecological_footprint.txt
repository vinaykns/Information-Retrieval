<p>The <b>ecological footprint</b> measures how much nature it takes to support people. It is an ecological accounting system. It contrasts how much biologically productive area people use for their consumption to how much biologically productive area is available (<a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a>).</p>
<p>More specifically, the ecological footprint is the biologically productive area needed to provide for everything people use: fruits and vegetables, fish, wood, fibers, absorption of carbon dioxide from fossil fuel use, and space for buildings and roads. Biocapacity is the productive area that can regenerate what people demand from nature. Footprint and biocapacity can be compared at the individual, regional, national or global scale. Both footprint and biocapacity change every year with number of people, per person consumption, efficiency of production, and productivity of ecosystems.</p>
<p>At a global scale, footprint assessments show how big humanity's demand is compared to what planet Earth can renew. <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a> calculates the global ecological footprint from UN and other data for over 200 nations. They estimate that as of 2012 humanity has been using natural capital 1.6 times as fast as nature can renew it.<sup id="cite_ref-Data_Sources_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Data_Sources-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological footprint analysis is widely applied around the Earth as an indicator of environmental <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-London_Ecological_Footprint_2003_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-London_Ecological_Footprint_2003-2">[2]</a></sup> It can be used to measure and manage the use of resources throughout the economy and explore the sustainability of individual lifestyles, goods and services, organizations, industry sectors, neighborhoods, cities, regions and nations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Since 2006, a first set of ecological footprint standards exist that detail both communication and calculation procedures.<sup id="cite_ref-Footprint_application_standards_2006_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Footprint_application_standards_2006-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footprint_measurements">Footprint measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Footprint measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, the Global Footprint Network estimated the global ecological footprint as 1.6 planet Earths; that is, they judged that <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecological services</a> were being used 1.6 times as quickly as they were being renewed.<sup id="cite_ref-Data_Sources_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Data_Sources-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg/300px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg" width="300" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg/450px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg/600px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg 2x" data-file-width="850" data-file-height="522" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ecological footprint for different nations compared to their <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ecological footprints can be calculated at any scale: for an activity, a person, a community, a city, a region, a nation or humanity as a whole. Cities, due to population concentration, have large ecological footprints and have become ground zero for footprint reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Global Footprints:</b> Currently there is no fixed way to measure global footprints, and any attempts to describe the capacity of an ecosystem in a single number is a massive simplification of thousands of key renewable resources, which are not used or replenished at the same rate. However, there has been some convergence of metrics and standards since 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-Footprint_application_standards_2006_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Footprint_application_standards_2006-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>City Ecological Footprints:</b> are being measured. There are two types of measurements in use. The first measures ecosystem displacement which is defined as City Area minus remaining green spaces. This is an area measurement that does not include human or other biological activity. The Second attempts to quantify surviving ecosystem health. Specifically, it attempts to quantify both area and biological health of ecosystems surviving inside city areas such as nature reserves, parks, other green spaces. City footprints are being calculated and scored with city ecological indexes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first academic publication about ecological footprints was by <a href="/wiki/William_Rees_(academic)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Rees (academic)">William Rees</a> in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The ecological footprint concept and calculation method was developed as the PhD dissertation of <a href="/wiki/Mathis_Wackernagel" title="Mathis Wackernagel">Mathis Wackernagel</a>, under Rees' supervision at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> in Vancouver, Canada, from 1990–1994.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Originally, Wackernagel and Rees called the concept "appropriated carrying capacity".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To make the idea more accessible, Rees came up with the term "ecological footprint", inspired by a computer technician who praised his new computer's "small footprint on the desk".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In early 1996, Wackernagel and Rees published the book <i>Our Ecological Footprint: Reducing Human Impact on the Earth</i> with illustrations by Phil Testemale.<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_.26_Rees_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel_.26_Rees-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Footprint values at the end of a survey are categorized for Carbon, Food, Housing, and Goods and Services as well as the total footprint number of Earths needed to sustain the world's population at that level of consumption. This approach can also be applied to an activity such as the manufacturing of a product or driving of a car. This resource accounting is similar to <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle analysis</a> wherein the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> (<a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>), <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resources</a> are converted into a normalized measure of land area called <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> (gha).</p>
<p>Per capita ecological footprint (EF), or ecological footprint analysis (EFA), is a means of comparing consumption and lifestyles, and checking this against nature's ability to provide for this consumption. The tool can inform policy by examining to what extent a nation uses more (or less) than is available within its territory, or to what extent the nation's lifestyle would be replicable worldwide. The footprint can also be a useful tool to educate people about <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, with the aim of altering personal behavior. Ecological footprints may be used to argue that many current lifestyles are not <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable</a>. Such a global comparison also clearly shows the inequalities of resource use on this planet at the beginning of the twenty-first century.</p>
<p>In 2007, the average biologically productive area per person worldwide was approximately 1.8 <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> (gha) per capita. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> footprint per capita was 9.0 gha, and that of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> was 5.6 gha, while <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>'s was 1.8 gha.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a> claims that the human footprint has exceeded the <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> (the available supply of natural resources) of the planet by 20%.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Wackernagel and Rees originally estimated that the available biological capacity for the 6 billion people on Earth at that time was about 1.3 hectares per person, which is smaller than the 1.8 global hectares published for 2006, because the initial studies neither used global hectares nor included bioproductive marine areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_.26_Rees_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel_.26_Rees-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of NGOs offer ecological footprint calculators (<i>see</i> <a href="#External_links">Footprint Calculator</a>, below).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ecological footprint accounting method at the national level is described in the l Footprint Atlas 2010<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> or in greater detail in the Calculation Methodology for the National Footprint Accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The National Accounts Review Committee has also published a research agenda on how the method will be improved.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Jason Venetoulis, Carl Mas, Christopher Gaudet, Dahlia Chazan, and John Talberth developed Footprint 2., which offers a series of theoretical and methodological improvements to the standard footprint approach. The four primary improvements were that they included the entire surface of the Earth in biocapacity estimates, allocated space for other (i.e., non-human) species, updated the basis of equivalence factors from agricultural land to net primary productivity (NPP), and refined the carbon component of the footprint based on the latest global carbon models.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_in_the_United_Kingdom">Studies in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Studies in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The UK's average ecological footprint is 5.45 <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> per capita (gha) with variations between regions ranging from 4.80 gha (Wales) to 5.56 gha (East England).<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two recent studies<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have examined relatively low-impact small communities. <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">BedZED</a>, a 96-home <a href="/wiki/Mixed-income_housing" title="Mixed-income housing">mixed-income housing</a> development in South <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, was designed by Bill Dunster Architects and sustainability consultants BioRegional for the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Trust" title="Peabody Trust">Peabody Trust</a>. Despite being populated by relatively "mainstream" home-buyers, BedZED was found to have a footprint of 3.20 gha due to on-site renewable energy production, energy-efficient architecture, and an extensive green lifestyles program that included on-site London's first <a href="/wiki/Carsharing" title="Carsharing">carsharing</a> club. The report did not measure the added footprint of the 15,000 visitors who have toured BedZED since its completion in 2002. <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Ecovillage" title="Findhorn Ecovillage">Findhorn Ecovillage</a>, a rural <a href="/wiki/Intentional_community" title="Intentional community">intentional community</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, had a total footprint of 2.56 gha, including both the many guests and visitors who travel to the community to undertake residential courses there and the nearby campus of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Hill" title="Cluny Hill">Cluny Hill</a> College. However, the residents alone have a footprint of 2.71 gha, a little over half the UK national average and one of the lowest ecological footprints of any community measured so far in the industrialized world.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Keveral Farm, an organic farming community in Cornwall, was found to have a footprint of 2.4 gha, though with substantial differences in footprints among community members.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques">Critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early criticism was published by <a href="/wiki/Jeroen_C.J.M._van_den_Bergh" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeroen C.J.M. van den Bergh">van den Bergh</a> and Verbruggen in 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-van_den_Bergh_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_den_Bergh-23">[23]</a></sup> which was updated in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-van_den_BerghGrazi2014_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_den_BerghGrazi2014-24">[24]</a></sup> Another criticism was published in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Fiala2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fiala2008-25">[25]</a></sup> A more complete review commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_the_Environment_(European_Commission)" class="mw-redirect" title="Directorate-General for the Environment (European Commission)">Directorate-General for the Environment (European Commission)</a> was published in June 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>A recent critique of the concept is due to Blomqvist et al., 2013a,<sup id="cite_ref-Blomqvist1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blomqvist1-27">[27]</a></sup> with a reply from Rees and Wackernagel, 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-Rees_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rees-28">[28]</a></sup> and a rejoinder by Blomqvist et al., 2013b.<sup id="cite_ref-Blomqvist2_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blomqvist2-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>An additional strand of critique is due to Giampietro and Saltelli (2014a),<sup id="cite_ref-G.26S1_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26S1-30">[30]</a></sup> with a reply from Goldfinger et al., 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-Goldfinger_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldfinger-31">[31]</a></sup> a rejoinder by Giampietro and Saltelli (2014a),<sup id="cite_ref-G.26S2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26S2-32">[32]</a></sup> and additional comments from van den Bergh and Grazi (2015).<sup id="cite_ref-vdBerg.26Grazi_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vdBerg.26Grazi-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of countries have engaged in research collaborations to test the validity of the method. This includes Switzerland, Germany, United Arab Emirates, and Belgium.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grazi et al. (2007) have performed a systematic comparison of the ecological footprint method with spatial welfare analysis that includes environmental <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externalities</a>, agglomeration effects and trade advantages.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> They find that the two methods can lead to very distinct, and even opposite, rankings of different spatial patterns of economic activity. However this should not be surprising, since the two methods address different research questions.</p>
<p>Calculating the ecological footprint for densely populated areas, such as a city or small country with a comparatively large population — e.g. New York and Singapore respectively — may lead to the perception of these populations as "parasitic". This is because these communities have little intrinsic biocapacity, and instead must rely upon large <i><a href="/wiki/Hinterland" title="Hinterland">hinterlands</a></i>. Critics argue that this is a dubious characterization since mechanized rural farmers in developed nations may easily consume more resources than urban inhabitants, due to transportation requirements and the unavailability of <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>. Furthermore, such moral conclusions seem to be an argument for <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a>. Some even take this train of thought a step further, claiming that the Footprint denies the benefits of trade. Therefore, the critics argue that the Footprint can only be applied globally.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The method seems to reward the replacement of original ecosystems with high-productivity agricultural <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monocultures</a> by assigning a higher biocapacity to such regions. For example, replacing ancient woodlands or tropical forests with monoculture forests or plantations may improve the ecological footprint. Similarly, if <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> yields were lower than those of conventional methods, this could result in the former being "penalized" with a larger ecological footprint.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Of course, this insight, while valid, stems from the idea of using the footprint as one's only metric. If the use of ecological footprints are complemented with other indicators, such as one for <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, the problem could maybe be solved. Indeed, <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Index" title="Living Planet Index">Living Planet Report</a> complements the biennial Footprint calculations with the Living Planet Index of biodiversity.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Manfred Lenzen and Shauna Murray have created a modified Ecological Footprint that takes biodiversity into account for use in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the ecological footprint model prior to 2008 treated <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> in the same manner as coal power,<sup id="cite_ref-Questions_and_Answers_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Questions_and_Answers-40">[40]</a></sup> the actual real world effects of the two are radically different. A life cycle analysis centered on the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Forsmark_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant">Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant</a> estimated carbon dioxide emissions at 3.10 g/kWh<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and 5.05 g/kWh in 2002 for the <a href="/wiki/Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Torness Nuclear Power Station">Torness Nuclear Power Station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This compares to 11 g/kWh for hydroelectric power, 950 g/kWh for installed coal, 900 g/kWh for oil and 600 g/kWh for natural gas generation in the United States in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Figures released by Mark Hertsgaard, however, show that because of the delays in building nuclear plants and the costs involved, investments in energy efficiency and renewable energies have seven times the return on investment of investments in nuclear energy.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG" class="image" title="The Vattenfall study found Nuclear, Hydro, and Wind to have far less greenhouse emissions than other sources represented."><img alt="The Vattenfall study found Nuclear, Hydro, and Wind to have far less greenhouse emissions than other sources represented." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG/500px-Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG" width="500" height="284" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG/750px-Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG 2x" data-file-width="944" data-file-height="537" /></a></div>
<p>The Swedish utility <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a> did a study of full <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_greenhouse-gas_emissions_of_energy_sources" title="Life-cycle greenhouse-gas emissions of energy sources">life-cycle greenhouse-gas emissions of energy sources</a> the utility uses to produce electricity, namely: Nuclear, Hydro, Coal, Gas, Solar Cell, Peat and Wind. The net result of the study was that nuclear power produced 3.3&#160;grams of carbon dioxide per KW-Hr of produced power. This compares to 400 for <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and 700 for <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> (according to this study). The study also concluded that nuclear power produced the smallest amount of CO<sub>2</sub> of any of their electricity sources.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Claims exist that the problems of nuclear waste do not come anywhere close to approaching the problems of fossil fuel waste.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> A 2004 article from the BBC states: "The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) says 3 million people are killed worldwide by outdoor air pollution annually from vehicles and industrial emissions, and 1.6 million indoors through using solid fuel."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In the U.S. alone, fossil fuel waste kills 20,000 people each year.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> A coal power plant releases 100 times as much radiation as a nuclear power plant of the same wattage.<sup id="cite_ref-colmain_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colmain-50">[50]</a></sup> It is estimated that during 1982, US coal burning released 155 times as much radioactivity into the atmosphere as the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Mile Island">Three Mile Island</a> incident.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In addition, fossil fuel waste causes <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which leads to increased deaths from hurricanes, flooding, and other weather events. The <a href="/wiki/World_Nuclear_Association" title="World Nuclear Association">World Nuclear Association</a> provides a comparison of deaths due to accidents among different forms of energy production. In their comparison, deaths per TW-yr of electricity produced (in UK and USA) from 1970 to 1992 are quoted as 885 for hydropower, 342 for coal, 85 for natural gas, and 8 for nuclear.<sup id="cite_ref-wna_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wna-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Western Australian government State of the Environment Report included an Ecological Footprint measure for the average Western Australian seven times the average footprint per person on the planet in 2007, a total of about 15 hectares.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<p>The world-average ecological footprint in 2012 was 1.8 global hectares per person. The average per country ranges from over 10 to under 1 hectares per person. There is also a high variation within countries, based on individual lifestyle and economic situation.<sup id="cite_ref-Humanity_Now_Demanding_1.4_Earths_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Humanity_Now_Demanding_1.4_Earths-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/GHG_footprint" title="GHG footprint">GHG footprint</a> differs from the ecological footprint in that the former is expressed in units of GHG warming potential (GGWP) and is generated by products or services, whereas the latter is expressed in units of land area and is generated by whole societies.<sup id="cite_ref-Guidance_on_how_to_measure_and_report_your_greenhouse_gas_emissions_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guidance_on_how_to_measure_and_report_your_greenhouse_gas_emissions-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
