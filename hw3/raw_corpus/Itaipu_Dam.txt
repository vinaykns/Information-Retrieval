<p>The <b>Itaipu Dam</b> (<a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">Guarani</a>: <span lang="gn" xml:lang="gn"><i>Presa Itaipu</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><i>Barragem de Itaipu</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>Represa de Itaipú</i></span>; <small>Portuguese pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[itɐjˈpu]</a></span>, <small>locally:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[ita.iˈpu]</a></span>, <small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[itaiˈpu]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_dam" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric dam">hydroelectric dam</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_River" title="Paraná River">Paraná River</a> located on the border between Brazil and Paraguay. The name "Itaipu" was taken from an isle that existed near the construction site. In the <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">Guarani language</a>, <span lang="gn" xml:lang="gn"><i>Itaipu</i></span> means "the sounding stone".<sup id="cite_ref-energy_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Completed in 1984, it is a binational undertaking run by Brazil and Paraguay at the border between the two countries, 15&#160;km (9.3&#160;mi) north of the <a href="/wiki/Friendship_Bridge_(Paraguay%E2%80%93Brazil)" class="mw-redirect" title="Friendship Bridge (Paraguay–Brazil)">Friendship Bridge</a>. The project ranges from <a href="/wiki/Foz_do_Igua%C3%A7u" title="Foz do Iguaçu">Foz do Iguaçu</a>, in Brazil, and <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_del_Este" title="Ciudad del Este">Ciudad del Este</a> in Paraguay, in the south to <a href="/wiki/Gua%C3%ADra,_Paran%C3%A1" title="Guaíra, Paraná">Guaíra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salto_del_Guair%C3%A1" title="Salto del Guairá">Salto del Guairá</a> in the north. The installed generation capacity of the plant is 14&#160;GW, with 20 generating units providing 700 MW each with a hydraulic design head of 118 metres (387&#160;ft). In 2016 the plant generated a record 103.1&#160;TWh,<sup id="cite_ref-brc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brc-5">[5]</a></sup> supplying approximately 75% of the electricity consumed by Paraguay and 17% of that consumed by Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-energy_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energy-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2016, the plant employed 3038 workers. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the twenty generator units currently installed, ten generate at 50&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hz</a> for Paraguay and ten generate at 60&#160;Hz for Brazil. Since the output capacity of the Paraguayan generators far exceeds the load in Paraguay, most of their production is exported directly to the Brazilian side, from where two <a href="/wiki/HVDC_Itaipu" title="HVDC Itaipu">600&#160;kV HVDC</a> lines, each approximately 800 kilometres (500&#160;mi) long, carry the majority of the energy to the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>/<a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> region where the terminal equipment converts the power to 60&#160;Hz.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations_between_Brazil_and_Paraguay">Negotiations between Brazil and Paraguay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Negotiations between Brazil and Paraguay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The concept behind the Itaipu Power Plant was the result of serious negotiations between the two countries during the 1960s. The "Ata do Iguaçu" (Iguaçu Act) was signed on July 22, 1966, by the Brazilian and Paraguayan Ministers of Foreign Affairs, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juracy_Magalh%C3%A3es&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Juracy Magalhães (page does not exist)">Juracy Magalhães</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ra%C3%BAl_Sapena_Pastor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Raúl Sapena Pastor (page does not exist)">Raúl Sapena Pastor</a>, respectively. This was a joint declaration of the mutual interest in studying the exploitation of the hydro resources that the two countries shared in the section of the Paraná River starting from, and including, the <i>Salto de Sete Quedas</i>, to the <i>Iguaçu River</i> watershed. The Treaty that gave origin to the power plant was signed in 1973.</p>
<p>The terms of the treaty, which expires in 2023, have been the subject of widespread discontent in Paraguay. The government of President Lugo vowed to renegotiate the terms of the treaty with Brazil, which long remained hostile to any renegotiation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, Brazil agreed to a fairer payment of electricity to Paraguay and also allowed Paraguay to sell excess power directly to Brazilian companies instead of solely through the Brazilian electricity monopoly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_starts">Construction starts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction starts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1970, the <a href="/wiki/Consortium" title="Consortium">consortium</a> formed by the companies <a href="/w/index.php?title=IECO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="IECO (page does not exist)">IECO</a> (from the United States),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/ELC_Electroconsult_S.p.A." class="mw-redirect" title="ELC Electroconsult S.p.A.">ELC Electroconsult S.p.A.</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>) won the international competition for the realization of the viability studies and for the elaboration of the construction project. Design studies began in February 1971. On April 26, 1973, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> signed the Itaipu Treaty, the legal instrument for the hydroelectric exploitation of the Paraná River by the two countries. On May 17, 1974, the Itaipu Binacional entity was created to administer the plant's construction. The construction began in January of the following year. Brazil's (and Latin America's) first electric car was introduced in late 1974; it received the name <a href="/wiki/Gurgel_Itaipu" title="Gurgel Itaipu"><i>Itaipu</i></a> in honor of the project.<sup id="cite_ref-QRweb_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QRweb-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paran.C3.A1_River_rerouted">Paraná River rerouted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paraná River rerouted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 14, 1978, the Paraná River had its route changed, which allowed a section of the riverbed to dry so the dam could be built there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agreement_by_Brazil.2C_Paraguay.2C_and_Argentina">Agreement by Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agreement by Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important diplomatic settlement was reached with the signing of the <i>Acordo Tripartite</i> by <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, on October 19, 1979. This agreement established the allowed river levels and how much they could change as a result of the various hydroelectrical undertakings in the watershed that was shared by the three countries. At that time, the three countries were ruled by military dictatorships. Argentina was concerned that, in the event of a conflict, Brazil could open the floodgates, raising the water level in the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">Río de la Plata</a> and consequently flood the capital city of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_lake">Formation of the lake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation of the lake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> began its formation on October 13, 1982, when the dam works were completed and the side canal's gates were closed. Throughout this period, heavy rains and flooding accelerated the filling of the reservoir as the water rose 100 meters (330 feet) and reached the gates of the <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">spillway</a> at 10:00 on October 27.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_of_operations">Start of operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Start of operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 5, 1984, the first generation unit started running in Itaipu. The first 18 units were installed at the rate of two to three a year; the last two of these started running in the year 1991.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capacity_expansion_in_2007">Capacity expansion in 2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Capacity expansion in 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Itaipu_3285.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Itaipu_3285.jpg/180px-Itaipu_3285.jpg" width="180" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Itaipu_3285.jpg/270px-Itaipu_3285.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Itaipu_3285.jpg/360px-Itaipu_3285.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Itaipu_3285.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The dam undergoes expansion work.</div>

<p>The last two of the 20 electric generation units started operations in September 2006 and in March 2007, thus raising the installed capacity to 14 GW and completing the power plant. This increase in capacity allows 18 generation units to run permanently while two are shut down for maintenance. Due to a clause in the treaty signed between Brazil, Paraguay and Argentina, the maximum number of generating units allowed to operate simultaneously cannot exceed 18 (see the <a href="#Agreement_by_Brazil.2C_Paraguay.2C_and_Argentina">agreement</a> section for more information).</p>
<p>The rated nominal power of each generating unit (turbine and generator) is 700 MW. However, because the <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">head</a> (difference between reservoir level and the river level at the foot of the dam) that actually occurs is higher than the designed head (118 m), the power available exceeds 750 MW half of the time for each generator. Each turbine generates around 700 MW; by comparison, all the water from the <a href="/wiki/Igua%C3%A7u_Falls" class="mw-redirect" title="Iguaçu Falls">Iguaçu Falls</a> would have the capacity to feed only two generators.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="November_2009_power_failure">November 2009 power failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: November 2009 power failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2009_Brazil_and_Paraguay_blackout" title="2009 Brazil and Paraguay blackout">2009 Brazil and Paraguay blackout</a></div>
<p>On November 10, 2009, transmission from the plant was completely disrupted, possibly due to a storm damaging up to three high-voltage transmission lines.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Itaipu itself was not damaged. This caused massive power outages in Brazil and Paraguay, blacking out the entire country of Paraguay for 15 minutes, and plunging Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo into darkness for more than 2 hours. 50 million people were reportedly affected.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The blackout hit at 22:13 local time. It affected the southeast of Brazil most severely, leaving São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro and Espírito Santo completely without electricity. Blackouts also swept through the interior of Rio Grande do Sul, Santa Catarina, Mato Grosso do Sul, Mato Grosso, the interior of Bahia and parts of Pernambuco, energy officials said.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By 00:30 power had been restored to most areas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wonder_of_the_Modern_World">Wonder of the Modern World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wonder of the Modern World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_environmental_impacts">Social and environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Social and environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When construction of the dam began, approximately 10,000 families living beside the Paraná River were displaced, because of construction.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The world's largest waterfall by volume, the <a href="/wiki/Gua%C3%ADra_Falls" title="Guaíra Falls">Guaíra Falls</a>, was drowned by the newly formed Itaipu reservoir. The Brazilian government liquidated the Guaíra Falls National Park, and dynamited the submerged rock face where the falls had been, facilitating safer navigation, thus eliminating the possibility of restoring the falls in the future. A few months before the reservoir was filled, 80 people died when an overcrowded bridge overlooking the falls collapsed, as tourists sought a last glimpse of the falls.<sup id="cite_ref-intlrivers_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intlrivers-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Guaíra Falls was an effective barrier that separated freshwater species in the upper Paraná basin (with its many <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemics</a>) from species found below it, and the two are recognized as different <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JulioJunior_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JulioJunior-19">[19]</a></sup> After the falls disappeared, many species formerly restricted to one of these areas have been able to invade the other, causing problems typically associated with <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a>. For example, more than 30 fish species that formerly were restricted to the region below the falls have been able to invade the region above.<sup id="cite_ref-JulioJunior_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JulioJunior-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The American composer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">Philip Glass</a> has written a symphonic <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantata</a> named <i><a href="/wiki/Itaipu_(composition)" class="mw-redirect" title="Itaipu (composition)">Itaipu</a></i>, in honour of the structure.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Ecological_Corridor" title="Santa Maria Ecological Corridor">Santa Maria Ecological Corridor</a> now connects the <a href="/wiki/Igua%C3%A7u_National_Park" title="Iguaçu National Park">Iguaçu National Park</a> with the protected margins of Lake Itaipu, and via these margins with the <a href="/wiki/Ilha_Grande_National_Park" title="Ilha Grande National Park">Ilha Grande National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The course of the seventh biggest river in the world was shifted, as were 50 million tons of earth and rock.</li>
<li>The amount of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> used to build the Itaipu Power Plant would be enough to build 210 <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> stadiums the size of the <a href="/wiki/Est%C3%A1dio_do_Maracan%C3%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="Estádio do Maracanã">Estádio do Maracanã</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> used would allow for the construction of 380 <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Towers</a>.</li>
<li>The volume of excavation of earth and rock in Itaipu is 8.5 times greater than that of the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a> and the volume of concrete is 15 times greater.</li>
<li>Around forty thousand people worked in the construction.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Itaipu is one of the <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_most_expensive_items" title="Lists of most expensive items">most expensive objects</a> ever built.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Generating_station_and_dam">Generating station and dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Itaipu_Dam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Generating station and dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The total length of the dam is 7,235 metres (23,737&#160;ft). The crest elevation is 225 metres (738&#160;ft). Itaipu is actually four dams joined together&#160;— from the far left, an earth fill dam, a rock fill dam, a concrete <a href="/wiki/Buttress_dam" title="Buttress dam">buttress main dam</a>, and a concrete wing dam to the right.</li>
<li>The spillway has a length of 483 metres (1,585&#160;ft).</li>
<li>The maximum flow of Itaipu's fourteen segmented <a href="/wiki/Spillway" title="Spillway">spillways</a> is 62.2&#160;thousand cubic metres per second (2.20<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;cu&#160;ft/s), into three skislope formed canals. It is equivalent to 40 times the average flow of the nearby natural <a href="/wiki/Igua%C3%A7u_Falls" class="mw-redirect" title="Iguaçu Falls">Iguaçu Falls</a>.</li>
<li>The flow of two generators (700 cubic metres per second (25,000&#160;cu&#160;ft/s) each) is roughly equivalent to the average flow of the Iguaçu Falls (1,500 cubic metres per second (53,000&#160;cu&#160;ft/s)).</li>
<li>If Brazil were to use <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">Thermal Power Generation</a> to produce the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> of Itaipu, 434,000 barrels (69,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> would have to be burned every day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Itaipu_Dam#Equivalent_Fuel_Oil_Usage" title="Talk:Itaipu Dam">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The dam is 196 metres (643&#160;ft) high, equivalent to a 65-story building.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>Though it is the seventh largest reservoir in size in Brazil, the Itaipu's <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> has the best relation between electricity production and flooded area. For the 14,000&#160;MW installed power, 1,350 square kilometres (520&#160;sq&#160;mi) were flooded. The reservoirs for the hydroelectric power plants of <a href="/wiki/Sobradinho_Dam" title="Sobradinho Dam">Sobradinho Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tucuru%C3%AD_Dam" title="Tucuruí Dam">Tucuruí Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto_Primavera_Dam" class="mw-redirect" title="Porto Primavera Dam">Porto Primavera Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balbina_Dam" title="Balbina Dam">Balbina Dam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serra_da_Mesa_Dam" title="Serra da Mesa Dam">Serra da Mesa Dam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furnas_Dam" title="Furnas Dam">Furnas Dam</a> are all larger than the one for Itaipu, but have a smaller installed generating capacity. The one with the next largest hydroelectric production, Tucuruí, has an installed capacity of 8,000&#160;MW, while flooding 2,430&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (938&#160;sq&#160;mi) of land.</li>
<li>Electricity is 55% cheaper when made by the Itaipu Dam than the other types of power plants in the area.</li>
</ul>
