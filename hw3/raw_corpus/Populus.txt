<p><i><b>Populus</b></i> is a genus of 25–35 species of <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Salicaceae" title="Salicaceae">Salicaceae</a>, native to most of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> names variously applied to different species include <b>poplar</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <b><a href="/wiki/Aspen" title="Aspen">aspen</a></b>, and <b><a href="/wiki/Populus_sect._Aigeiros" title="Populus sect. Aigeiros">cottonwood</a></b>.</p>
<p>In the September 2006 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science Magazine</a></i>, the Joint Genome Institute announced that the western balsam poplar (<i><a href="/wiki/Populus_trichocarpa" title="Populus trichocarpa">P. trichocarpa</a></i>) was the first tree whose full DNA code had been determined by <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">DNA sequencing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus has a large genetic diversity, and can grow from 15–50&#160;m (49–164&#160;ft) tall, with trunks up to 2.5&#160;m (8&#160;ft 2&#160;in) in diameter.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_(Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg/170px-Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg" width="170" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg/255px-Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg/340px-Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_%28Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1356" data-file-height="2016" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Populier_mannelijke_bloeiwijze_(Populus_canadensis_male_inflorescences).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Male catkins of <a href="/wiki/Populus_%C3%97_canadensis" title="Populus × canadensis"><i>Populus</i> × <i>canadensis</i></a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a> on young trees is smooth, white to greenish or dark grey, and often has conspicuous <a href="/wiki/Lenticel" title="Lenticel">lenticels</a>; on old trees, it remains smooth in some species, but becomes rough and deeply fissured in others. The shoots are stout, with (unlike in the related <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willows</a>) the terminal bud present. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are spirally arranged, and vary in shape from triangular to circular or (rarely) lobed, and with a long <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petiole</a>; in species in the sections <i>Populus</i> and <i>Aigeiros</i>, the petioles are laterally flattened, so that breezes easily cause the leaves to wobble back and forth, giving the whole tree a "twinkling" appearance in a breeze. Leaf size is very variable even on a single tree, typically with small leaves on side shoots, and very large leaves on strong-growing lead shoots. The leaves often turn bright gold to yellow before they fall during autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-rdm_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rdm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rushforth_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are mostly <a href="/wiki/Plant_sexuality" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant sexuality">dioecious</a> (rarely <a href="/wiki/Plant_sexuality" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant sexuality">monoecious</a>) and appear in early spring before the leaves. They are borne in long, drooping, sessile or pedunculate <a href="/wiki/Catkin" title="Catkin">catkins</a> produced from buds formed in the axils of the leaves of the previous year. The flowers are each seated in a cup-shaped disk which is borne on the base of a scale which is itself attached to the rachis of the catkin. The scales are obovate, lobed, and fringed, membranous, hairy or smooth, and usually caducous. The male flowers are without <a href="/wiki/Calyx_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calyx (botany)">calyx</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corolla_(flower)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corolla (flower)">corolla</a>, and comprise a group of four to 60 <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a> inserted on a disk; filaments are short and pale yellow; <a href="/wiki/Anther" class="mw-redirect" title="Anther">anthers</a> are oblong, purple or red, introrse, and two-celled; the cells open longitudinally. The female flower also has no calyx or corolla, and comprises a single-celled ovary seated in a cup-shaped disk. The style is short, with two to four stigmata, variously lobed, and numerous ovules. Pollination is by wind, with the female catkins lengthening considerably between pollination and maturity. The <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> is a two- to four-valved <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)" title="Dehiscence (botany)">dehiscent</a> <a href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capsule (fruit)">capsule</a>, green to reddish-brown, mature in midsummer, containing numerous minute light brown <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> surrounded by tufts of long, soft, white hairs which aid wind dispersal.<sup id="cite_ref-rdm_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rdm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Keeler_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keeler-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Poplars of the cottonwood section are often wetlands or <a href="/wiki/Riparian" class="mw-redirect" title="Riparian">riparian</a> trees. The aspens are among the most important <a href="/wiki/Boreal_ecosystem" title="Boreal ecosystem">boreal</a> broadleaf trees.<sup id="cite_ref-rdm_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rdm-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Poplars and aspens are important <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lepidoptera_that_feed_on_poplars" title="List of Lepidoptera that feed on poplars">food plants</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of a large number of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> species. <i><a href="/wiki/Pleurotus_populinus" title="Pleurotus populinus">Pleurotus populinus</a></i>, the aspen oyster mushroom, is found exclusively on dead wood of <i>Populus</i> trees in North America.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG/220px-Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG/330px-Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG/440px-Four_Poplars_in_four_seasons.JPG 2x" data-file-width="821" data-file-height="617" /></a>

<p>The genus <i>Populus</i> has traditionally been divided into six sections on the basis of leaf and flower characters;<sup id="cite_ref-rushforth_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rushforth-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> this classification is followed below. Recent genetic studies have largely supported this, confirming some previously suspected reticulate evolution due to past <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> and introgression events between the groups. Some species (noted below) had differing relationships indicated by their nuclear DNA (paternally inherited) and chloroplast DNA sequences (maternally inherited), a clear indication of likely hybrid origin.<sup id="cite_ref-hamzeh_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamzeh-7">[7]</a></sup> Hybridisation continues to be common in the genus, with several hybrids between species in different sections known.<sup id="cite_ref-rdm_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rdm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Many poplars are grown as <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornamental tree">ornamental trees</a>, with numerous <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> used. They have the advantage of growing to a very large size at a rapid pace. Almost all poplars take root readily from cuttings or where broken branches lie on the ground (they also often have remarkable suckering abilities, and can form huge colonies from a single original tree, such as the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Pando_(tree)" title="Pando (tree)">Pando</a></i> forest made of thousands of <i><a href="/wiki/Populus_tremuloides" title="Populus tremuloides">Populus tremuloides</a></i> clones).</p>
<p>Trees with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fastigiate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fastigiate (page does not exist)">fastigiate</a> (erect, columnar) branching are particularly popular, and are widely grown across Europe and southwest Asia. However, like <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willows</a>, poplars have very vigorous and invasive root systems stretching up to 40&#160;m from the trees; planting close to houses or ceramic water pipes may result in damaged foundations and cracked walls and pipes due to their search for moisture.</p>
<p>A simple, reproducible, high-frequency micropropagation protocol in eastern cottonwood <i>Populus deltoides</i> has been reported by Yadav et al. 2009<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>In India, the poplar is grown commercially by farmers, mainly in the Punjab region. Common poplar varieties are:</p>
<ul>
<li>G48 (grown in the plains of Punjab, Haryana, UP)</li>
<li>w22 (grown in mountainous regions, e.g., Himachal Pradesh, Pathankot, Jammu)</li>
</ul>
<p>The trees are grown from <i>kalam</i> or cuttings, harvested annually in January and February, and commercially available up to 15<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>November.</p>
<p>Most commonly used to make <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamuna_Nagar" class="mw-redirect" title="Yamuna Nagar">Yamuna Nagar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a> state has a large plywood industry reliant upon poplar. It is graded according to sizes known as "over" (over 24 inches (610&#160;mm)), "under" (18–24 inches (460–610&#160;mm)), and "sokta" (less than 18 inches (460&#160;mm)).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pamirihouse.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/Pamirihouse.JPG/220px-Pamirihouse.JPG" width="220" height="81" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/Pamirihouse.JPG/330px-Pamirihouse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b6/Pamirihouse.JPG/440px-Pamirihouse.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="379" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pamirihouse.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Traditional <a href="/wiki/Pamiris" title="Pamiris">Pamiris</a> house</div>

<p>Although the wood from <i>Populus</i> is known as poplar wood, a common high-quality hardwood "poplar" with a greenish colour is actually from an unrelated genus <i><a href="/wiki/Liriodendron" title="Liriodendron">Liriodendron</a></i>. <i>Populus</i> wood is a lighter, more porous material.</p>
<p>Its flexibility and close grain make it suitable for a number of applications, similar to those for willow. The Greeks and Etruscans made shields of poplar, and Pliny also recommended poplar for this purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Poplar continued to be used for shield construction through the Middle Ages and was renowned for a durability similar to that of oak, but at a substantial reduction in weight.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In many areas, fast-growing <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> poplars are grown on <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pulpwood" title="Pulpwood">pulpwood</a></li>
<li>Poplar is widely used for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>It is also sold as inexpensive hardwood <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>, used for <a href="/wiki/Pallet" title="Pallet">pallets</a> and cheap <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a>; more specialised uses include <a href="/wiki/Match" title="Match">matches</a> and the boxes in which <a href="/wiki/Camembert_(cheese)" class="mw-redirect" title="Camembert (cheese)">Camembert cheese</a> is sold.</li>
<li>Poplar wood is also widely used in the <a href="/wiki/Snowboard" title="Snowboard">snowboard</a> industry for the snowboard core, because it has exceptional flexibility, and is sometimes used in the bodies of <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drum" title="Drum">drums</a>.</li>
<li>Poplar wood, particularly when seasoned, makes a good hearth for a <a href="/wiki/Bow_drill" title="Bow drill">bow drill</a>.</li>
<li>Due to its high <a href="/wiki/Tannic_acid" title="Tannic acid">tannic acid</a> content, the bark has been used in Europe for tanning leather.<sup id="cite_ref-Keeler_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keeler-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Poplar wood can be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Chopsticks" title="Chopsticks">chopsticks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wooden_shoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Wooden shoes">wooden shoes</a>.</li>
<li>Baking <a href="/wiki/Mold_(cooking_implement)" title="Mold (cooking implement)">moulds</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wood_veneer" title="Wood veneer">peeled</a> poplar may be used in the freezer, oven, or microwave oven.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interest exists in using poplar as an <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crop</a> for <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Energy_forestry" title="Energy forestry">energy forestry</a> systems, particularly in light of its high energy-in to energy-out ratio, large carbon mitigation potential, and fast growth.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, poplar (as with fellow energy crop willow) is typically grown in a <a href="/wiki/Short_rotation_coppice" title="Short rotation coppice">short rotation coppice</a> system for two to five years (with single or multiple stems), then harvested and burned - the yield of some varieties can be as high as 12 oven-dry tonnes per hectare every year<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_and_literature">Art and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Art and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Poplar was the most common wood used in Italy for <a href="/wiki/Panel_painting" title="Panel painting">panel paintings</a>; the <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a></i> and most famous early renaissance Italian paintings are on poplar. The wood is generally white, often with a slightly yellowish colour.</p>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Stringed_instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Stringed instrument">stringed instruments</a> are made with one-piece poplar backs; <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">violas</a> made in this fashion are said<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to have a particularly resonant tone. Similarly, though typically it is considered to have a less attractive grain than the traditional <a href="/wiki/Sitka_spruce" class="mw-redirect" title="Sitka spruce">sitka spruce</a>, poplar is beginning to be targeted by some <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a> <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthiers</a> as a sustainable and even superior alternative for their <a href="/wiki/Sound_board_(music)" title="Sound board (music)">sound boards</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in these cases another hardwood veneer is sometimes applied to the resonant poplar base both for cosmetic reasons, and supposedly to fine-tune the acoustic properties.</p>
<p>Two notable poems in English lament the cutting down of poplars, <a href="/wiki/William_Cowper" title="William Cowper">William Cowper</a>'s "The Poplar Field" and <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Manley_Hopkins" title="Gerard Manley Hopkins">Gerard Manley Hopkins</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Binsey_Poplars" title="Binsey Poplars">Binsey Poplars</a> felled 1879".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Abel_Meeropol" title="Abel Meeropol">Abel Meeropol</a>'s poem "<a href="/wiki/Strange_Fruit" title="Strange Fruit">Strange Fruit</a>: mentions poplar trees in the context of <a href="/wiki/Lynching" title="Lynching">lynching</a>.</p>
<p>The Odd Poplers Alley, in <a href="/wiki/Ia%C8%99i" title="Iași">Iași</a>, Romania, is one of the spots where <a href="/wiki/Mihai_Eminescu" title="Mihai Eminescu">Mihai Eminescu</a> sought inspiration in his works (the poem "Down Where the Lonely Poplars Grow"). In 1973, the 15 white poplars still left (with age ranges between 233 and 371 years) were declared natural monuments.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_against_termites">Resistance against termites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Resistance against termites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Pakistan, poplar is grown on commercial level by farmers in Punjab, Sindh, and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Provinces. However, all varieties are seriously susceptible to termite attack, causing significant losses to poplar every year. Logs of poplar are therefore also used in termite traps (termaps) for biocontrol of termites in crops.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_management">Land management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Land management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lombardy_poplar" class="mw-redirect" title="Lombardy poplar">Lombardy poplars</a> are frequently used as a <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">windbreak</a> around agricultural fields to protect against wind erosion.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Populus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Logs from the poplar provide a growing medium for <a href="/wiki/Shiitake" title="Shiitake">shiitake</a> mushrooms.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
