<p><b>Camarillo</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">KAM</span></span></b>-ə-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">REE</span></span></b>-oh</i></a></span>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County,_California" title="Ventura County, California">Ventura County</a> in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The population was 65,201 at the 2010 census, up from 57,084 at the 2000 census. The <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Freeway" title="Ventura Freeway">Ventura Freeway</a> (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_101_(California)" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Route 101 (California)">U.S. Route 101</a>) is the city's primary thoroughfare. Camarillo is named for <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Camarillo" title="Adolfo Camarillo">Adolfo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Camarillo,_Jr." title="Juan Camarillo, Jr.">Juan Camarillo</a>, two of the few <i><a href="/wiki/Californio" title="Californio">Californios</a></i> (pre-1848 California natives of Hispanic ancestry) to preserve the city's heritage after the arrival of Anglo settlers. The <a href="/wiki/Coast_Line_(UP)" title="Coast Line (UP)">railroad coast route</a> came through in 1898 and built a station here. Adolfo Camarillo eventually employed 700 workers growing mainly lima beans. Walnuts and citrus were also grown on the ranch. Adolfo bred <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_White_Horse" title="Camarillo White Horse">Camarillo White Horses</a> in the 1920s through the 1960s and was well known for riding them, dressed in colorful Spanish attire, in parades such as the Fiesta of Santa Barbara.</p>
<p>The city grew slowly prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> but the war effort saw the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Air_Force_Base" title="Oxnard Air Force Base">Oxnard Army Air Field</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Air_Force_Base" title="Oxnard Air Force Base">Oxnard Air Force Base</a> in 1951, now <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_Airport" title="Camarillo Airport">Camarillo Airport</a>) to the west of town. The community also grew as the new base along with nearby <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Point_Mugu" title="Naval Air Station Point Mugu">Naval Air Station Point Mugu</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Naval_Construction_Battalion_Center_Port_Hueneme" title="Naval Construction Battalion Center Port Hueneme">Seabee base at Port Hueneme</a> brought many workers and their families to the area. The grounds of Camarillo State Hospital, which opened in 1936 south of town, are now the campus of <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Channel_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="California State University, Channel Islands">California State University, Channel Islands</a>.</p>
<p>As with most cities in Ventura County, it is noted for its resistance to new development.<sup id="cite_ref-conejocreek.org_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conejocreek.org-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2014, the council voted against an 895-acre project that would have extended development on agricultural lands east towards the <a href="/wiki/Conejo_Grade" title="Conejo Grade">Conejo Grade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg/220px-CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg" width="220" height="51" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg/330px-CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg/440px-CamarilloCaliforniaPanorama.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4353" data-file-height="1012" /></a>

<p>Camarillo is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Camarillo%2C_California&amp;params=34_14_N_119_2_W_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">34°14′N</span> <span class="longitude">119°2′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">34.233°N 119.033°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">34.233; -119.033</span></span></span></a></span> (34.2256, −119.0322).<sup id="cite_ref-GR1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-12">[12]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the city has a total area of 19.5 square miles (51&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). 0.015 square miles (0.039&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of the area (0.08%) is water.</p>
<p>Camarillo is located in Pleasant Valley<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> at the eastern end of the <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Plain" title="Oxnard Plain">Oxnard Plain</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susana_Mountains" title="Santa Susana Mountains">Santa Susana Mountains</a> to the north, the Camarillo Hills to the northwest, the <a href="/wiki/Conejo_Valley" title="Conejo Valley">Conejo Valley</a> to the east, and the western reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Mountains" title="Santa Monica Mountains">Santa Monica Mountains</a> to the south.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This region experiences warm (but not hot) and dry summers, with no average monthly temperatures above 71.6&#160;°F. According to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> system, Camarillo has a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate#Warm-summer_Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">warm-summer Mediterranean climate</a>, abbreviated "Csb" on climate maps.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> reported that Camarillo had a population of 65,201. The population density was 3,336.3 people per square mile (1,288.1/km²). The racial makeup of Camarillo was 48,947 (75.1%) <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 1,216 (1.9%) <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 397 (0.6%) <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 6,633 (10.2%) <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 116 (0.2%) <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 4,774 (7.3%) from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 3,118 (4.8%) from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 14,958 persons (22.9%).</p>
<p>The Census reported that 64,705 people (99.2% of the population) lived in households, 155 (0.2%) lived in non-institutionalized group quarters, and 341 (0.5%) were institutionalized.</p>
<p>There were 24,504 households, out of which 8,103 (33.1%) had children under the age of 18 living in them, 13,565 (55.4%) were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">opposite-sex married couples</a> living together, 2,386 (9.7%) had a female householder with no husband present, 1,078 (4.4%) had a male householder with no wife present. There were 1,099 (4.5%) <a href="/wiki/POSSLQ" title="POSSLQ">unmarried opposite-sex partnerships</a>, and 158 (0.6%) <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_partnerships" class="mw-redirect" title="Same-sex partnerships">same-sex married couples or partnerships</a>. 5,986 households (24.4%) were made up of individuals and 3,231 (13.2%) had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.64. There were 17,029 <a href="/wiki/Family_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family (U.S. Census)">families</a> (69.5% of all households); the average family size was 3.14.</p>
<p>The population was spread out with 15,115 people (23.2%) under the age of 18, 5,164 people (7.9%) aged 18 to 24, 15,895 people (24.4%) aged 25 to 44, 17,825 people (27.3%) aged 45 to 64, and 11,202 people (17.2%) who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 40.8 years. For every 100 females there were 93.7 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 90.7 males.</p>
<p>There were 25,702 housing units at an average density of 1,315.1 per square mile (507.8/km²), of which 17,059 (69.6%) were owner-occupied, and 7,445 (30.4%) were occupied by renters. The homeowner vacancy rate was 1.4%; the rental vacancy rate was 5.2%. 45,522 people (69.8% of the population) lived in owner-occupied housing units and 19,183 people (29.4%) lived in rental housing units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000">2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the census<sup id="cite_ref-GR2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-20">[20]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 57,084 people, 24,376 households, and 15,242 families residing in the city. The population density was 3,015.3 inhabitants per square mile (1,164.2/km²). There were 24,376 housing units at an average density of 1,159.4 per square mile (447.6/km²). The racial makeup of the city was 70.90% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 1.90% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.52% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 9.40% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.60% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 13.20% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 3.90% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 23.10% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 24,376 households out of which 33.0% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 59.7% were married couples living together, 8.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 28.9% were non-families. 24.1% of all households were made up of individuals and 13.6% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.62 and the average family size was 3.12.</p>
<p>In the city, the population was spread out with 25.3% under the age of 18, 6.5% from 18 to 24, 28.5% from 25 to 44, 22.7% from 45 to 64, and 17.0% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 39 years. For every 100 females there were 93.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 90.2 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the city was $62,457, and the median income for a family was $72,676 (these figures had risen to $78,677 and $92,683 respectively as of a 2007 estimate<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>). Males had a median income of $51,507 versus $36,240 for females. The per capita income for the city was $28,635. About 3.6% of families and 5.3% of the population were below the poverty line, including 5.9% of those under age 18 and 4.6% of those age 65 or older.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 13, 2010, the Camarillo City Council voted 5–0 to withdraw from the <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County_Library" title="Ventura County Library">Ventura County Library</a> System, and enter into a public-private contract with <a href="/wiki/Library_Systems_%26_Services" title="Library Systems &amp; Services">Library Systems &amp; Services</a> (LSSI) of Germantown, Maryland, a private company that administers several libraries throughout the United States, to provide locally hired staffing and to manage the day-to-day operations of the City of Camarillo Public Library. Under the partnership agreement, the library will remain in the public trust, managed by the City of Camarillo and operated by LSSI.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 1, 2011, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Camarillo_Public_Library" title="City of Camarillo Public Library">City of Camarillo Public Library</a> opened as municipal public library.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_life">Quality of life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Quality of life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Camarillo_April_2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Camarillo_April_2010.jpg/250px-Camarillo_April_2010.jpg" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Camarillo_April_2010.jpg/375px-Camarillo_April_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Camarillo_April_2010.jpg/500px-Camarillo_April_2010.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Camarillo_April_2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A view of Camarillo from nearby hills on a cloudy day in April. Such days are not typical of the area.</div>

<p>Camarillo and the surrounding area has a temperate, <i><a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a></i>-type climate. Its location in a coastal valley brings mild ocean breezes and temperatures in the 70s throughout most of the year. An average rainfall of 13 inches (330&#160;mm) occurs primarily from November to February. The city has over 300 days of sunshine a year and an average humidity of 62%.</p>
<p>Snow has only fallen about 3 times in the last thirty years and is seldom more than a dusting. Snow is often visible during the winter months above the 4,000-foot (1,200&#160;m) level in the mountains to the north. The proximity of the ocean sometimes causes morning fog in the spring and early summer.</p>
<p>Camarillo is primarily a <a href="/wiki/Bedroom_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedroom community">bedroom community</a> made up of large housing tracts, with elementary schools and small <a href="/wiki/Strip_malls" class="mw-redirect" title="Strip malls">strip malls</a> serving the neighborhood. The primary public high schools serving Camarillo are <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Camarillo_High_School" title="Adolfo Camarillo High School">Adolfo Camarillo High School</a> in Mission Oaks, and <a href="/wiki/Rio_Mesa_High_School" title="Rio Mesa High School">Rio Mesa High School</a> in Strickland between Oxnard and Camarillo. Both are part of the <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Union_High_School_District" title="Oxnard Union High School District">Oxnard Union High School District</a> which is building <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Campana_High_School" title="Rancho Campana High School">Rancho Campana High School</a> near the intersection of Lewis Road and Las Posas Road and scheduled to be completed by the fall semester of 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Leung_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leung-26">[26]</a></sup> The private <a href="/wiki/Cornerstone_Christian_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornerstone Christian School">Cornerstone Christian School</a> is also located in Camarillo.</p>
<p>The Boys and Girls Club of Camarillo has been open since 1967. The Club serves close to 400 kids per day and is primarily funded by donations from the Community. The YMCA has a facility on Village at the Park Drive, and a new library was constructed and opened on March 31, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Many sports leagues, including adult leagues, such as baseball, basketball, football, and the largest AYSO soccer league west of the Mississippi are located in Camarillo. An outdoor in-line hockey rink is in Freedom Park, near the Camarillo Airport.</p>
<p>The incidence of all types of crime committed in the city is far below the national average.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleasant_Valley_Recreation_and_Park_District">Pleasant Valley Recreation and Park District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pleasant Valley Recreation and Park District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Pleasant Valley Recreation &amp; Park District (PVRPD, www.pvrpd.org)<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> is an independent <a href="/wiki/Special-purpose_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Special-purpose district">Special-purpose district</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County,_California" title="Ventura County, California">Ventura County, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The District is located in and around the City of <a href="/wiki/Camarillo" class="mw-redirect" title="Camarillo">Camarillo</a>, serves a population of over 70,000 and covers an area approximately 45 square miles.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It has grown from one park and 30 acres to 27 parks and over 300 acres since its inception in 1962. PVRPD was incorporated prior to the City of <a href="/wiki/Camarillo" class="mw-redirect" title="Camarillo">Camarillo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the District, a variety of recreational facilities exist including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Senior Center</li>
<li>Only Public Indoor Aquatic Center in <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventura County">Ventura County</a></li>
<li>Community Center</li>
<li>Dog Parks</li>
<li>Lighted Ball Fields</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">Tennis</a> Courts</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Running" title="Running">Running</a> Track</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">Walking</a> Paths</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soccer" class="mw-redirect" title="Soccer">Soccer</a> Fields</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">Hiking</a> Trails <sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Picnic Shelters</li>
<li>Children's Play Equipment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Skatepark" title="Skatepark">Skatepark</a></li>
<li>Barbecue Areas</li>
</ul>
<p>The District is a separate government agency from the City of <a href="/wiki/Camarillo" class="mw-redirect" title="Camarillo">Camarillo</a>. The boundaries of the District also varies from the City of Camarillo. The District is governed by publicly elected Board of Directors and is managed by a General Manager.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The Pleasant Valley Recreation &amp; Park District is funded by local property taxes and fees to provide and maintain parks and outdoor recreational facilities for the enjoyment of all Camarillo citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the city's incorporation in 1964, a council-manager form of government was created. The five member city council is elected at large for four-year terms. The council is responsible for establishing policy, enacting laws and making legal and financial decisions for the city. A city manager, hired by the council and answerable to it, is responsible for the day-to-day operation of the city. He is charged with overall management of the five city departments and 97 full-time employees. Services such as water, sewer, trash collection, street maintenance and traffic engineering are provided by a combination of contractors and city employees.</p>
<p>As of January 2017, the mayor of Camarillo is Jan McDonald.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Police services are provided by the <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County_Sheriff%27s_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventura County Sheriff's Department">Ventura County Sheriff's Department</a> under contract to the city, headquartered in a police station owned by the city. The Sheriff's department helicopter fleet is hangared at <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_Airport" title="Camarillo Airport">Camarillo Airport</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County_Fire_Department" title="Ventura County Fire Department">Ventura County Fire Department</a> provides fire protection, with five stations within the city limits.</p>
<p>The major source of city funding is sales tax revenue. The mix of retail and commercial businesses in the city provides a stable tax base. The addition of a Factory Outlet Center and a new shopping center added significantly to the sales tax revenues.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Columbian">Pre-Columbian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pre-Columbian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chumash_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chumash (tribe)">Chumash</a> Indians were the first known settlers in what is now known as Ventura County. Fishermen built their villages along the Pacific Coast near the mouths of the Calleguas Creek and Santa Clara River. Artifacts from their settlements are on display in the Ventura County Historical Museum and their paintings are still visible on canyon walls and in caves in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_exploration">European exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: European exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Spanish navigator <a href="/wiki/Juan_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Cabrillo" title="Juan Rodríguez Cabrillo">Juan Rodríguez Cabrillo</a>, while exploring the Pacific coast for the king of Spain, came upon the <a href="/wiki/Chumash_people" title="Chumash people">Chumash</a> in an area near <a href="/wiki/Point_Mugu" class="mw-redirect" title="Point Mugu">Point Mugu</a>. He explored the surrounding region and claimed it in the name of Spain in 1542. Cabrillo was followed in 1602 by Sebastián Vizcaíno on a mapping expedition for the King of Spain. The Chumash continued to inhabit the coast until 1768 when Russians, having established a settlement 800 miles (1,300&#160;km) to the north, launched expeditions challenging the Spanish land claims. In the 18th century, the Spanish began settling California and built the first of what would become a chain of 21 <a href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission (Christian)">missions</a> in San Diego. <a href="/wiki/Father_Jun%C3%ADpero_Serra" class="mw-redirect" title="Father Junípero Serra">Father Junípero Serra</a> establish the ninth mission in <a href="/wiki/Ventura,_California" title="Ventura, California">Ventura</a> in 1782 bringing more settlers to the area and exposing the Indians who had settled around the mission to many European diseases to which they had no immunity. Their numbers diminished until the Chumash, once the largest Indian nation in California, had largely vanished by 1839.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexican_independence">Mexican independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mexican independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the early 1820s, Mexico had gained independence from Spain, and shortly afterward California allied itself with Mexico. The Mexican land grant system was liberalized in 1824 resulting in many large grants in California and the proliferation of Ranchos north of the border. One grant to José Pedro Ruiz created <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Calleguas" title="Rancho Calleguas">Rancho Calleguas</a> in 1837, in the area that is now Camarillo. The grant was later sold to Juan Camarillo, who had arrived in 1834 as a member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hijar-Jos%C3%A9_Colony&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hijar-José Colony (page does not exist)">Hijar-Padres Expedition</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Lees_pastoral_region_insert_page_4_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lees_pastoral_region_insert_page_4-56">[56]</a></sup> it was his sons Adolfo and Juan that are credited with the founding of the town that was to bear their name. The earlier proposed name of Calleguas was rejected as too difficult to pronounce.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Springville">Springville</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Springville">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At about same time, the town of Springville had begun to form just to the west of the emerging town of Camarillo on the main road to Ventura which is now the intersection of Ventura Boulevard and Wood Road.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The Springville Post Office, which was open from 1875 to 1903,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> was one of seventeen post offices operating in the county in 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-Storke_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Storke-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(p194)</sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[note 1]</a></sup> The Springville School District was established in 1887<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> and the grammar school was south of the settlement on the main road (now Pleasant Valley Road)<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Hueneme" title="Port of Hueneme">new wharf</a> at Hueneme where schooners brought lumber from the north and carried grain, lima beans and sheep to markets in San Francisco. Springville's existence was threatened though when the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Pacific_Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern Pacific Railroad">Southern Pacific Railroad</a> Coast Route was routed farther south through Pleasant Valley<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> and a depot was established in nearby Camarillo. The settlement disappeared but in 2012 a new road was named <i>Springville Drive</i> to honor the little town and is now marked on the 101 freeway with an overpass and ramps at the western end of Camarillo. The original settlement would have been nearby on the south side of the freeway.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St._John.27s_Seminary">St. John's Seminary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: St. John's Seminary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1927 Don Juan Camarillo, brother of Adolfo, donated 100 acres (0.40&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) to be used as a seminary to be named in honor of <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_the_Evangelist" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint John the Evangelist">Saint John the Evangelist</a>. The Roman Catholic seminary was opened in 1939 as <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Seminary_(California)" title="St. John's Seminary (California)">St. John's Seminary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stjohn_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stjohn-64">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_growth">Early growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Early growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Camarillo's growth was slow from founding through World War II. In the late 1940s, building lots on <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Boulevard" title="Ventura Boulevard">Ventura Boulevard</a>, the main downtown street, were being offered for $450 and home lots on the adjoining streets were $250, with few buyers. Travel to and from Los Angeles was difficult, owing to the narrow, tortuous road climbing the Conejo Grade to the east of the city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Camarillo_farms.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Camarillo_farms.JPG/300px-Camarillo_farms.JPG" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Camarillo_farms.JPG/450px-Camarillo_farms.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Camarillo_farms.JPG/600px-Camarillo_farms.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Camarillo_farms.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the farm fields in southern Camarillo.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The main industry during this period was agriculture, and the area surrounding the small town was blanketed with orange, lemon and walnut groves. The State Mental hospital south of the town was the largest employer. A few houses had sprung up to the north and south of town center. The Oxnard Army Air Field, built during World War II to the west of town, the Naval Air Facility at <a href="/wiki/Point_Mugu" class="mw-redirect" title="Point Mugu">Point Mugu</a> and the Seabee base at <a href="/wiki/Port_Hueneme" class="mw-redirect" title="Port Hueneme">Port Hueneme</a> brought many military personnel to the area, but there was little private industry or other source of non-agricultural employment.</p>
<p>Oxnard AAF closed at the end of World War II, but the Navy facilities remained open, with the airfield upgraded to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Point_Mugu" title="Naval Air Station Point Mugu">Naval Air Station Point Mugu</a> and the Seabee base becoming <a href="/wiki/Naval_Base_Ventura_County" title="Naval Base Ventura County">Naval Construction Battalion Center Port Hueneme and Naval Surface Warfare Center Port Hueneme</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> tensions, the former Oxnard AAF was reactivated in 1951 as <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Air_Force_Base" title="Oxnard Air Force Base">Oxnard Air Force Base</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Air_Defense_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Defense Command">Air Defense Command</a> / <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="Aerospace Defense Command">Aerospace Defense Command</a> fighter-interceptor base, that closed again in 1970 and became the present-day <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_Airport" title="Camarillo Airport">Camarillo Airport</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventura_Freeway_.28US_Route_101.29">Ventura Freeway (US Route 101)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ventura Freeway (US Route 101)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-1950s, the <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Freeway" title="Ventura Freeway">Ventura Freeway</a>, which bisects the town, was completed from Los Angeles to points north, making it an easy one-hour trip to Camarillo. The freeway was originally planned to follow the path of Potrero Road, south of Camarillo, which would have completely by-passed the soon-to-be city. However, after much debate, city officials persuaded <a href="/wiki/Caltrans" class="mw-redirect" title="Caltrans">Caltrans</a> to lay the freeway parallel to Ventura Boulevard, creating the infamously steep descent from the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Mountains" title="Santa Monica Mountains">Santa Monica Mountains</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Conejo_Grade" title="Conejo Grade">Conejo Grade</a>. The grade is about 2.7 miles (4.3&#160;km) and posted as a 7% grade—which translates as about one thousand feet of elevation change in less than three miles (70 meters per kilometer). There is a <a href="/wiki/California_Highway_Patrol" title="California Highway Patrol">California Highway Patrol</a> brake inspection station at the top of the grade and a stop is mandatory for all 18-wheel trucks. The completion of the freeway facilitated the growth that followed. In 1962, the population was 7,500 and <a href="/wiki/3M" title="3M">3M</a> began construction for the <a href="/wiki/3M" title="3M">Mincom</a> and Magnetic Tape Divisions, which would ultimately employ 900 people, becoming the largest local employer. That plant briefly housed a factory for 3M <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spinoff</a> <a href="/wiki/Imation" class="mw-redirect" title="Imation">Imation</a> before being closed in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> Housing tracts were built where orchards once stood. House prices were $14,000 to $65,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incorporation_in_1964">Incorporation in 1964</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Incorporation in 1964">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At this time plans were made for the incorporation of the city to control the rapid expansion. Camarillo became a city in 1964 and soon put into place a General Plan and building codes that were to lead to an attractive city environment. In 1964 the closest traffic signal was 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) from the City center on the road to Point Mugu, and the first shopping center and supermarket were under construction. Because of the late date of city incorporation, the local telephone exchange is still listed as part of <a href="/wiki/Oxnard" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxnard">Oxnard</a>. Much of the city was expected to be developed to the south of Ventura Blvd, however it was to the north that the new city grew, and the land south of Ventura Blvd remains reserved for agricultural use to this day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many of the home buyers during the 1960s were military veterans, who had been stationed at one of the local bases during their service. The temperate climate and the living conditions lured them back. With the establishment of both the Pacific Missile Range at NAS Point Mugu and the Naval Civil Engineering Laboratory at Port Hueneme many found employment that utilized their military training. Other newcomers were those who worked and lived in the San Fernando Valley and were willing to endure the commute for the opportunity to raise their families in a smog-free, semirural environment. Still others relocated here with their employers, like 3M, and <a href="/wiki/Harbor_Freight_Tools" title="Harbor Freight Tools">Harbor Freight Tools</a> who built facilities in and around the city to take advantage of the large workforce. <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">Technicolor</a> Video Services Inc. is the largest DVD duplicator in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> The members of the original city council (1964) were Ned Chatfield, Stan Daily, Earl Joseph, Tweedy Rouce &amp; Guy Turner.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camarillo_State_Hospital_.26_CSUCI">Camarillo State Hospital &amp; CSUCI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Camarillo State Hospital &amp; CSUCI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_State_Mental_Hospital" title="Camarillo State Mental Hospital">Camarillo State Mental Hospital</a> and <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Channel_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="California State University, Channel Islands">California State University, Channel Islands</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Camarillo_State_Mental_Hospital" title="Camarillo State Mental Hospital">Camarillo State Mental Hospital</a> was established near the city in the 1930s so that persons suffering from mental illnesses or <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> could recover in Ventura County's balmy climate. Jazzman <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>'s "Relaxin' at Camarillo," written while he was detoxing from heroin addiction, is a tribute to the facility. The song "Camarillo" by punk outfit <a href="/wiki/Fear_(band)" title="Fear (band)">Fear</a> is also written about the facility. The band <a href="/wiki/Ambrosia_(band)" title="Ambrosia (band)">Ambrosia</a> released a song called "Ready for Camarillo" on their 1978 <i>Life Beyond L.A.</i> album. "Ready for Camarillo" also appeared as the single B side of their hit "How Much I Feel." The former hospital is the now the site of <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Channel_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="California State University, Channel Islands">California State University, Channel Islands</a>. The University has retained the distinctive <a href="/wiki/Mission_Revival_Style_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission Revival Style architecture">Mission Revival Style architecture</a> bell tower in the South quad. The band <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville_(US_band)" title="Brazzaville (US band)">Brazzaville</a> released a video called "Camarillo" in 2007, with mental hospital-like imagery and lyrics concerning lead singer David Brown's relatives' stay in the institution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Camarillo State Hospital was closed in the 1990s and remained vacant until the site was converted into California State University, Channel Islands (CSUCI). CSUCI officially opened in August 2002 and is now accredited by the WASC.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_Oaks">Mission Oaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Mission Oaks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Mission Oaks</i> is the name given by developer <a href="/wiki/Pardee_Homes" title="Pardee Homes">Pardee Homes</a> to a 1,312 acres (531&#160;ha) parcel of land located in the north-eastern portion of the city. This parcel was developed as a <a href="/wiki/Planned_community" title="Planned community">planned community</a> over the span of 35 years, and was completed in October 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup> The area developed by Pardee Homes makes up approximately 15% of Camarillo's total land. Due to the decades-long timescale of the project, many residents are unaware of Mission Oaks' proprietary nature, and the area east of Lewis Road (<a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_34" title="California State Route 34">State Route 34</a>), south of Somis and north of the <a href="/wiki/US_101" class="mw-redirect" title="US 101">101 Freeway</a> is generally thought of as Mission Oaks regardless of which company built the buildings in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-growth_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-growth-68">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camarillo_Premium_Outlets">Camarillo Premium Outlets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Camarillo Premium Outlets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-1990s, multiple large retail centers, including one of California's largest <a href="/wiki/Outlet_mall" class="mw-redirect" title="Outlet mall">outlet malls</a><sup id="cite_ref-Pacific_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pacific-69">[68]</a></sup> and movie theater were built south of US 101 and west of Carmen Drive. These new retail centers have provided a large influx of cash to the city; from 1993 to 1998 sales tax revenues nearly doubled from approximately $3.5 million to approximately $6.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup> On April 23, 2009, several new shops and restaurants opened at the Camarillo Premium Outlets, designated "The Promenade". The Promenade is 220,000 square feet (20,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>), while the Premium Outlets is 454,000. The new center has 45 stores and restaurants, bringing the total to about 160<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camarillo_Springs_Fire">Camarillo Springs Fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Camarillo Springs Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Springs_Fire" title="Springs Fire">Springs Fire</a></div>
<p>Beginning 7:02 am. on Thursday, May 2, 2013, a major brush fire began in the Camarillo Springs area and burned throughout the area. The community of Dos Vientos and CSUCI were evacuated due to the proximity of the fire. About 15 houses were damaged, but none burned down. 28,000 acres of land was burned by the fire. Finally, on Sunday, May 5, 2013, rain in the area during the night helped firefighters bring the fire under full control.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camarillo_Airport">Camarillo Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Camarillo Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_Airport" title="Camarillo Airport">Camarillo Airport</a></div>
<p>Camarillo Airport (<a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>KCMA</b>,&#160;<a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">LID</a>: <b>CMA</b>) is a public airport located 3 miles (4.8&#160;km) west of the central business district of Camarillo. The airport has one <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a> and exclusively serves privately operated <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Executive_jet" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive jet">executive</a> aircraft with no <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_air_carriers" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled air carriers">scheduled commercial service</a>.</p>
<p>The airport was originally established in 1942 when the California State Highway Department constructed an auxiliary landing field with a 5,000&#160;ft (1,500&#160;m) runway, which was later extended to 8,000&#160;ft (2,400&#160;m) in 1951 to accommodate what by then had developed into <a href="/wiki/Oxnard_Air_Force_Base" title="Oxnard Air Force Base">Oxnard Air Force Base</a>. The airport runway was further extended in 1959 to accommodate jet fighter aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/F-89_Scorpion" class="mw-redirect" title="F-89 Scorpion">Northrop F-89</a> and <a href="/wiki/F-101_VooDoo" class="mw-redirect" title="F-101 VooDoo">McDonnell F-101B</a>, used as part of the Los Angeles area Air Defense network.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the mid-1960s the base received 17 new <a href="/wiki/F-106_Delta_Dart" class="mw-redirect" title="F-106 Delta Dart">F-106 Delta Darts</a>.</p>
<p>On January 1, 1970, Oxnard AFB was deactivated and the base became surplus property. Oxnard had 99 Officers and 990 enlisted personnel assigned prior to its closing in 1970. The last commander of the 414th Fighter Group was Colonel Paul D. Cofer.</p>
<p>The transfer of the airport to Ventura County was approved by the Department of Defense in 1976. Camarillo Airport also serves as the base of operations of the <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County_Sheriff%27s_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventura County Sheriff's Department">Ventura County Sheriff's Department</a> Aviation Unit and the home of the VCSD's Training Facility and Academy, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventura_County_Criminal_Justice_Training_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ventura County Criminal Justice Training Center (page does not exist)">Ventura County Criminal Justice Training Center</a>. The Camarillo Airport also serves as the base of operations for the <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County_Fire_Department" title="Ventura County Fire Department">Ventura County Fire Department</a>, the Ventura County Fire Department Regional Fire/EMS Communications Center (or FCC, which handles all 911 calls for service for fire and medical emergencies in Ventura County w/ the exception of Oxnard City Fire), and facilitates the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxnard_College_Regional_Fire_Academy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Oxnard College Regional Fire Academy (page does not exist)">Oxnard College Regional Fire Academy</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ventura_County_Reserve_Officers_Training_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ventura County Reserve Officers Training Center (page does not exist)">Ventura County Reserve Officers Training Center</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Camarillo_(Amtrak_station)" class="mw-redirect" title="Camarillo (Amtrak station)">Camarillo (Amtrak station)</a></div>
<p>Camarillo has one train station, served by both Amtrak's <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Surfliner" title="Pacific Surfliner">Pacific Surfliner</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Obispo,_California" title="San Luis Obispo, California">San Luis Obispo</a> to San Diego and Metrolink's <a href="/wiki/Metrolink_Ventura_County_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Metrolink Ventura County Line">Ventura County Line</a> from Los Angeles <a href="/wiki/Union_Station_(Los_Angeles)" title="Union Station (Los Angeles)">Union Station</a> to <a href="/wiki/Montalvo,_Ventura,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Montalvo, Ventura, California">Montalvo</a>. Nine <i>Pacific Surfliner</i> trains serve the station daily and six Metrolink trains serve the station each weekday. This limited Metrolink service runs only at peak hours in the peak direction of travel (i.e. three morning departures to Los Angeles and three evening arrivals from Los Angeles).<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buses">Buses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Buses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ci.camarillo.ca.us/i3.aspx?p=96">CAT</a> operates one scheduled bus line on Monday through Friday within Camarillo, and Dial-A-Ride services for the disabled on Monday through Saturday. <a href="/wiki/Ventura_Intercity_Service_Transit_Authority" title="Ventura Intercity Service Transit Authority">VISTA</a> operates buses between Camarillo and several nearby cities, including the Conejo Express to the Warner Center area in western Los Angeles County.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Camrosa.2C_or_Santa_Rosa.2C_area">Camrosa, or Santa Rosa, area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Camrosa, or Santa Rosa, area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A rural region northeast of <a href="/wiki/Camarillo" class="mw-redirect" title="Camarillo">Camarillo</a>, California may be referred to as <i>Santa Rosa</i> or <i>Camrosa</i>. Camrosa is believed to be a contraction of <b>Cam</b>arillo and Santa <b>Rosa</b>. The area includes just over a five-mile (8&#160;km) distance along Santa Rosa Road from the city limit east to Moorpark Road. The area is unincorporated as of 2007. Wired telephone service to the area comes from the Camarillo <a href="/wiki/Telephone_exchange" title="Telephone exchange">telephone exchange</a>, while homes on the hillside overlooking the valley (from the south) are from <a href="/wiki/Thousand_Oaks" class="mw-redirect" title="Thousand Oaks">Thousand Oaks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup> Geographic features supporting these names include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_Valley" title="Santa Rosa Valley">Santa Rosa Valley</a>, USGS feature ID 249122.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Geological_Survey_1998_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Geological_Survey_1998-74">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>Camrosa Water Treatment Plane, part of the Calleguas Water District, 7385 Santa Rosa Road.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li>Santa Rosa Elementary, 13282 Santa Rosa Rd., Pleasant Valley Elementary School Dist., USGS feature ID 249119.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Geological_Survey_1998_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Geological_Survey_1998-74">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>Arroyo Santa Rosa, a stream with USGS feature ID 238765.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Geological_Survey_1998_74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Geological_Survey_1998-74">[73]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>This Santa Rosa is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa,_California" title="Santa Rosa, California">the Sonoma County city</a> of the same name.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Camarillo,_California&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The motion pictures <i><a href="/wiki/Coming_Home_(1978_film)" title="Coming Home (1978 film)">Coming Home</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor_(film)" title="Pearl Harbor (film)">Pearl Harbor</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Jay_and_Silent_Bob_Strike_Back" title="Jay and Silent Bob Strike Back">Jay and Silent Bob Strike Back</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Eagle" title="Iron Eagle">Iron Eagle</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Romy_and_Michele%27s_High_School_Reunion" title="Romy and Michele's High School Reunion">Romy and Michele's High School Reunion</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Friday_the_13th:_A_New_Beginning" title="Friday the 13th: A New Beginning">Friday the 13th: A New Beginning</a></i> were filmed in Camarillo. The well used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ring_(2002_film)" title="The Ring (2002 film)">The Ring</a></i> is located in Camarillo</p>
<p>Camarillo is sometimes mentioned in literature because of the mental hospital once located there. In <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dharma_Bums" title="The Dharma Bums">The Dharma Bums</a></i>, he mentions a place "...somewhere near Camarillo where <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>'d been mad and relaxed back to normal health" (<i>The Dharma Bums</i>, 1).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a> referred to the city in his 1973 song "<a href="/wiki/Camarillo_Brillo" title="Camarillo Brillo">Camarillo Brillo</a>". The city's name is mispronounced so as to rhyme with the second word in the song's title.</p>
