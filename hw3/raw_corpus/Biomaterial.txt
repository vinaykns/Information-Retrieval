			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Biomaterial</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">"Biomaterials" redirects here. For the journal, see <a href="/wiki/Biomaterials_(journal)" title="Biomaterials (journal)">Biomaterials (journal)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>biomaterial</b> is any substance that has been engineered to interact with biological systems for a medical purpose - either a therapeutic (treat, augment, repair or replace a tissue function of the body) or a diagnostic one. As a science, <b>biomaterials</b> is about fifty years old. The study of biomaterials is called <b>biomaterials science</b> or <b>biomaterials engineering</b>. It has experienced steady and strong growth over its history, with many companies investing large amounts of money into the development of new products. Biomaterials science encompasses elements of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">tissue engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>.</p>
<p>Note that a biomaterial is different from a biological material, such as <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, that is produced by a <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological system</a>. Additionally, care should be exercised in defining a biomaterial as <a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">biocompatible</a>, since it is application-specific. A biomaterial that is biocompatible or suitable for one application may not be biocompatible in another.<sup id="cite_ref-SchmalzBio08_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchmalzBio08-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biomaterials can be derived either from nature or synthesized in the laboratory using a variety of chemical approaches utilizing metallic components, <a href="/wiki/Polymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Polymers">polymers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioceramic" title="Bioceramic">ceramics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Composite_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Composite materials">composite materials</a>. They are often used and/or adapted for a medical application, and thus comprises whole or part of a living structure or biomedical device which performs, augments, or replaces a natural function. Such functions may be relatively passive, like being used for a <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">heart valve</a>, or may be <a href="/wiki/Bioactive" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioactive">bioactive</a> with a more interactive functionality such as <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylapatite" title="Hydroxylapatite">hydroxy-apatite</a> coated <a href="/wiki/Hip_implant" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip implant">hip implants</a>. Biomaterials are also used every day in dental applications, surgery, and drug delivery. For example, a construct with impregnated pharmaceutical products can be placed into the body, which permits the prolonged release of a drug over an extended period of time. A biomaterial may also be an <a href="/wiki/Autograft" class="mw-redirect" title="Autograft">autograft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allograft" class="mw-redirect" title="Allograft">allograft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xenograft" class="mw-redirect" title="Xenograft">xenograft</a> used as a <a href="/wiki/Organ_transplant" class="mw-redirect" title="Organ transplant">transplant</a> material.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomineralization">Biomineralization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biomineralization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biomineralization" title="Biomineralization">Biomineralization</a></div>
<p>Biomineralization is the process by which living organisms produce <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[4]</a></sup> often to harden or stiffen existing tissues. Such tissues are called <a href="/wiki/Mineralized_tissues" title="Mineralized tissues">mineralized tissues</a>. It is an extremely widespread phenomenon; all six taxonomic kingdoms contain members that are able to form minerals, and over 60 different minerals have been identified in organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weiner1989_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner1989-9">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[7]</a></sup> Examples include <a href="/wiki/Silicates" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicates">silicates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diatoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Invertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphates" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcium phosphates">calcium phosphates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">carbonates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>. These minerals often form structural features such as sea <a href="/wiki/Animal_shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal shell">shells</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammals">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds">birds</a>. <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">Organisms</a> have been producing mineralised <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeletons</a> for the past 550 million years. Other examples include <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> deposits involving bacteria. Biologically-formed minerals often have special uses such as magnetic sensors in <a href="/wiki/Magnetotactic_bacteria" title="Magnetotactic bacteria">magnetotactic bacteria</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), gravity sensing devices (CaCO<sub>3</sub>, CaSO<sub>4</sub>, BaSO<sub>4</sub>) and iron storage and mobilization (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>•H<sub>2</sub>O in the protein <a href="/wiki/Ferritin" title="Ferritin">ferritin</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-assembly">Self-assembly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Self-assembly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Self-assembly" title="Self-assembly">Self-assembly</a> is the most common term in use in the modern scientific community to describe the spontaneous aggregation of particles (atoms, molecules, <a href="/wiki/Colloids" class="mw-redirect" title="Colloids">colloids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Micelles" class="mw-redirect" title="Micelles">micelles</a>, etc.) without the influence of any external forces. Large groups of such particles are known to assemble themselves into <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermodynamic">thermodynamically</a> stable, structurally well-defined arrays, quite reminiscent of one of the 7 crystal systems found in <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> (e.g. face-centered cubic, body-centered cubic, etc.). The fundamental difference in equilibrium structure is in the spatial scale of the <a href="/wiki/Unit_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit cell">unit cell</a> (or lattice parameter) in each particular case.</p>
<p>Molecular self-assembly is found widely in biological systems and provides the basis of a wide variety of complex biological structures. This includes an emerging class of mechanically superior biomaterials based on microstructural features and designs found in nature. Thus, self-assembly is also emerging as a new strategy in chemical synthesis and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>. Molecular crystals, liquid crystals, colloids, micelles, <a href="/wiki/Emulsions" class="mw-redirect" title="Emulsions">emulsions</a>, phase-separated polymers, thin films and self-assembled monolayers all represent examples of the types of highly ordered structures which are obtained using these techniques. The distinguishing feature of these methods is self-organization.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_hierarchy">Structural hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structural hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nearly all materials could be seen as hierarchically structured, especially since the changes in spatial scale bring about different mechanisms of deformation and damage. However, in biological materials this hierarchical organization is inherent to the microstructure. One of the first examples of this, in the history of structural biology, is the early <a href="/wiki/X-ray_scattering_techniques" title="X-ray scattering techniques">X-ray scattering</a> work on the hierarchical structure of <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> by Astbury and Woods.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[11]</a></sup> In bone, for example, <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> is the building block of the <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(biology)" title="Matrix (biology)">organic matrix</a> — a triple helix with diameter of 1.5&#160;nm. These <a href="/wiki/Tropocollagen" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropocollagen">tropocollagen</a> molecules are <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(chemistry)" title="Intercalation (chemistry)">intercalated</a> with the mineral phase (<a href="/wiki/Hydroxyapatite" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyapatite">hydroxyapatite</a>, a calcium phosphate) forming <a href="/wiki/Fibril" title="Fibril">fibrils</a> that curl into <a href="/wiki/Helicoid" title="Helicoid">helicoids</a> of alternating directions. These "<a href="/wiki/Osteon" title="Osteon">osteons</a>" are the basic building blocks of bones, with the volume fraction distribution between organic and mineral phase being about 60/40.</p>
<p>In another level of complexity, the hydroxyapatite crystals are mineral platelets that have a diameter of approximately 70–100&#160;nm and thickness of 1&#160;nm. They originally nucleate at the gaps between collagen fibrils.</p>
<p>Similarly, the hierarchy of <a href="/wiki/Abalone" title="Abalone">abalone</a> shell begins at the nanolevel, with an organic layer having a thickness of 20–30&#160;nm. This layer proceeds with single crystals of <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> (a polymorph of CaCO<sub>3</sub>) consisting of "bricks" with dimensions of 0.5 and finishing with layers approximately 0.3&#160;mm (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Mesostructure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mesostructure (page does not exist)">mesostructure</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">Crabs</a> are arthropods whose carapace is made of a mineralized hard component (which exhibits brittle fracture) and a softer organic component composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>. The brittle component is arranged in a helical pattern. Each of these mineral ‘rods’ (1 μm diameter) contains chitin–protein fibrils with approximately 60&#160;nm diameter. These fibrils are made of 3&#160;nm diameter canals which link the interior and exterior of the shell.</p>

</ul>
<p>Biomaterials must be compatible with the body, and there are often issues of <a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">biocompatibility</a> which must be resolved before a product can be placed on the market and used in a <a href="/wiki/Clinic" title="Clinic">clinical</a> setting. Because of this, biomaterials are usually subjected to the same requirements as those undergone by new <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drug</a> therapies.<sup id="cite_ref-Meyers08_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meyers08-20">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Espinosa09_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Espinosa09-21">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>All manufacturing companies are also required to ensure traceability of all of their products so that if a defective product is discovered, others in the same batch may be traced.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart_valves">Heart valves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Heart valves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, 45% of the 250,000 valve replacement procedures performed annually involve a mechanical valve implant. The most widely used valve is a bileaflet disc heart valve, or St. Jude valve. The mechanics involve two semicircular discs moving back and forth, with both allowing the flow of blood as well as the ability to form a seal against backflow. The valve is coated with pyrolytic carbon, and secured to the surrounding tissue with a mesh of woven fabric called Dacron<sup>TM</sup> (du Pont's trade name for <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a>). The mesh allows for the body's tissue to grow while incorporating the valve.<sup id="cite_ref-Chemistry_Textbook_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_Textbook-22">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skin_repair">Skin repair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Skin repair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tissue_engineering" title="Tissue engineering">Tissue engineering</a></div>
<p>Most of the time, ‘artificial’ tissue is grown from the patient’s own cells. However, when the damage is so extreme that it is impossible to use the patient's own cells, artificial tissue cells are grown. The difficulty is in finding a scaffold that the cells can grow and organize on. The characteristics of the scaffold must be that it is biocompatible, cells can adhere to the scaffold, mechanically strong and <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodegradable">biodegradable</a>. One successful scaffold is a <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycolic_acid" title="Glycolic acid">glycolic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chemistry_Textbook_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_Textbook-22">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compatibility">Compatibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Compatibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">Biocompatibility</a> is related to the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Biomaterials" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomaterials">biomaterials</a> in various environments under various chemical and physical conditions. The term may refer to specific properties of a material without specifying where or how the material is to be used. For example, a material may elicit little or no <a href="/wiki/Immune_response" title="Immune response">immune response</a> in a given organism, and may or may not able to integrate with a particular cell type or <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a>. The ambiguity of the term reflects the ongoing development of insights into how biomaterials interact with the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> and eventually how those interactions determine the clinical success of a <a href="/wiki/Medical_device" title="Medical device">medical device</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Pacemaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacemaker">pacemaker</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hip_replacement" title="Hip replacement">hip replacement</a>). Modern medical devices and <a href="/wiki/Prostheses" class="mw-redirect" title="Prostheses">prostheses</a> are often made of more than one material—so it might not always be sufficient to talk about the biocompatibility of a specific material. <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biopolymers">Biopolymers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomaterial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biopolymers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biopolymer" title="Biopolymer">Biopolymer</a></div>
<p><b>Biopolymers</b> are <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> produced by living organisms. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">Cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> are all examples of biopolymers, in which the <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomeric</a> units, respectively, are <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> Cellulose is both the most common biopolymer and the most common organic compound on Earth. About 33% of all plant matter is cellulose.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup></p>
