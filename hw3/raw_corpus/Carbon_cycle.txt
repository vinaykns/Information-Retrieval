			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon cycle</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>carbon cycle</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycle</a> by which <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is exchanged among the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> of the Earth. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, the carbon cycle comprises a sequence of events that are key to make the Earth capable of sustaining life; it describes the movement of carbon as it is recycled and reused throughout the biosphere, including <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sinks" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon sinks">carbon sinks</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>global carbon budget</i> is the balance of the exchanges (incomes and losses) of carbon between the carbon reservoirs or between one specific loop (e.g., atmosphere &lt;-&gt; biosphere) of the carbon cycle. An examination of the carbon budget of a pool or reservoir can provide information about whether the pool or reservoir is functioning as a source or sink for carbon dioxide. The carbon cycle was initially discovered by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, and popularized by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AOW_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOW-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_climate">Global climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Global climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon-based molecules are crucial for life on Earth, because it is the main component of biological compounds. Carbon is also a major component of many minerals. Carbon also exists in various forms in the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) is partly responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> and is one of the most important human-contributed <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the past two centuries, human activities have altered the global carbon cycle, most significantly in the atmosphere. Although carbon dioxide levels have changed naturally over the past several thousand years, human emissions of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere contribute to natural fluctuations.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> Some people think that changes in the amount of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> are considerably altering weather patterns and indirectly influencing oceanic chemistry but this conclusion is controversial. Current carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere exceed measurements from the last 420,000 years but are a small fraction of what they were in throughout most of the evolution of life on the planet and levels are rising quickly,<sup id="cite_ref-Crowley2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crowley2000-3">[3]</a></sup> making it of critical importance to better understand how the carbon cycle works and what its effects are on the global climate.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The global carbon cycle is now usually divided into the following major reservoirs of carbon interconnected by pathways of exchange:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a></li>
<li>The terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">dissolved inorganic carbon</a> and living and non-living marine biota</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, fresh water systems and non-living organic material.</li>
<li>The Earth's interior, carbon from the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. These carbon stores interact with the other components through geological processes</li>
</ul>
<p>The carbon exchanges between reservoirs occur as the result of various chemical, physical, geological, and biological processes. The ocean contains the largest active pool of carbon near the surface of the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> The natural flows of carbon between the atmosphere, ocean, terrestrial ecosystems, and sediments is fairly balanced, so that carbon levels would be roughly stable without human influence.<sup id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-U_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_cycle" title="Atmospheric carbon cycle">Atmospheric carbon cycle</a></div>

<p>Carbon in the Earth's atmosphere exists in two main forms: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Both of these gases absorb and retain heat in the atmosphere and are partially responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>. Methane produces a larger greenhouse effect per volume as compared to carbon dioxide, but it exists in much lower concentrations and is more short-lived than carbon dioxide, making carbon dioxide the more important greenhouse gas of the two.<sup id="cite_ref-Forster2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forster2007-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carbon dioxide leaves the atmosphere through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, thus entering the terrestrial and oceanic biospheres. Carbon dioxide also dissolves directly from the atmosphere into bodies of water (oceans, lakes, etc.), as well as dissolving in precipitation as raindrops fall through the atmosphere. When dissolved in water, carbon dioxide reacts with water molecules and forms <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, which contributes to ocean acidity. It can then be absorbed by rocks through weathering. It also can acidify other surfaces it touches or be washed into the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-Planet_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Human activities over the past two centuries have significantly increased the amount of carbon in the atmosphere, mainly in the form of carbon dioxide, both by modifying ecosystems' ability to extract carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and by emitting it directly, e.g., by burning fossil fuels and manufacturing concrete.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">Carbon sink</a></div>
<p>The terrestrial biosphere includes the organic carbon in all land-living organisms, both alive and dead, as well as carbon stored in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>. About 500 gigatons of carbon are stored above ground in plants and other living organisms,<sup id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> while soil holds approximately 1,500 gigatons of carbon.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most carbon in the terrestrial biosphere is organic carbon,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> while about a third of soil carbon is stored in inorganic forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lal-2008_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lal-2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Organic carbon is a major component of all organisms living on earth. <a href="/wiki/Autotrophs" class="mw-redirect" title="Autotrophs">Autotrophs</a> extract it from the air in the form of carbon dioxide, converting it into organic carbon, while <a href="/wiki/Heterotrophs" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterotrophs">heterotrophs</a> receive carbon by consuming other organisms.</p>
<p>Because carbon uptake in the terrestrial biosphere is dependent on biotic factors, it follows a diurnal and seasonal cycle. In CO<sub>2</sub> measurements, this feature is apparent in the <a href="/wiki/Keeling_curve" class="mw-redirect" title="Keeling curve">Keeling curve</a>. It is strongest in the northern <a href="/wiki/Hemisphere_of_the_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemisphere of the Earth">hemisphere</a>, because this hemisphere has more land mass than the southern hemisphere and thus more room for ecosystems to absorb and emit carbon.</p>
<p>Carbon leaves the terrestrial biosphere in several ways and on different time scales. The <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a> of organic carbon releases it rapidly into the atmosphere. It can also be exported into the oceans through rivers or remain sequestered in soils in the form of inert carbon. Carbon stored in soil can remain there for up to thousands of years before being washed into rivers by <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> or released into the atmosphere through <a href="/wiki/Soil_respiration" title="Soil respiration">soil respiration</a>. Between 1989 and 2008 soil respiration increased by about 0.1% per year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2008, the global total of CO<sub>2</sub> released from the soil reached roughly 98 billion tonnes, about 10 times more carbon than humans are now putting into the atmosphere each year by burning fossil fuel. There are a few plausible explanations for this trend, but the most likely explanation is that increasing temperatures have increased rates of decomposition of soil organic matter, which has increased the flow of CO<sub>2</sub>. The length of carbon sequestering in soil is dependent on local climatic conditions and thus changes in the course of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. From pre-industrial era to 2010, the terrestrial biosphere represented a net source of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> prior to 1940, switching subsequently to a net sink.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_carbon_cycle" title="Oceanic carbon cycle">Oceanic carbon cycle</a></div>
<p>Oceans contain the greatest quantity of actively cycled carbon in this world and are second only to the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> in the amount of carbon they store.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> The oceans' <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a> holds large amounts of dissolved inorganic carbon that is exchanged rapidly with the atmosphere. The deep layer's concentration of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) is about 15% higher than that of the surface layer.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarmiento_and_Gruber_2006_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarmiento_and_Gruber_2006-13">[13]</a></sup> DIC is stored in the deep layer for much longer periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Thermohaline circulation</a> exchanges carbon between these two layers.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carbon enters the ocean mainly through the dissolution of atmospheric carbon dioxide, which is converted into <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a>. It can also enter the oceans through rivers as <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_organic_carbon" title="Dissolved organic carbon">dissolved organic carbon</a>. It is converted by organisms into organic carbon through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and can either be exchanged throughout the food chain or precipitated into the ocean's deeper, more carbon rich layers as dead soft tissue or in shells as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>. It circulates in this layer for long periods of time before either being deposited as sediment or, eventually, returned to the surface waters through thermohaline circulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oceanic absorption of CO<sub>2</sub> is one of the most important forms of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestering" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon sequestering">carbon sequestering</a> limiting the human-caused rise of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. However, this process is limited by a number of factors. Because the rate of CO<sub>2</sub> dissolution in the ocean is dependent on the weathering of rocks and this process takes place slower than current rates of human greenhouse gas emissions, ocean CO<sub>2</sub> uptake will decrease in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> absorption also makes water more acidic, which affects ocean biosystems. The projected rate of increasing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">oceanic acidity</a> could slow the biological precipitation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonates</a>, thus decreasing the ocean's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Klyepas1999_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klyepas1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Langdon2000_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langdon2000-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_carbon_cycle">Geological carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geological carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The geologic component of the carbon cycle operates slowly in comparison to the other parts of the global carbon cycle. It is one of the most important determinants of the amount of carbon in the atmosphere, and thus of global temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the earth's carbon is stored inertly in the earth's <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> Much of the carbon stored in the earth's mantle was stored there when the earth formed.<sup id="cite_ref-DiVenere2012_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiVenere2012-17">[17]</a></sup> Some of it was deposited in the form of organic carbon from the biosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Berner1999_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berner1999-18">[18]</a></sup> Of the carbon stored in the geosphere, about 80% is <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and its derivatives, which form from the sedimentation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> stored in the shells of marine organisms. The remaining 20% is stored as <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">kerogens</a> formed through the sedimentation and burial of terrestrial organisms under high heat and pressure. Organic carbon stored in the geosphere can remain there for millions of years.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carbon can leave the geosphere in several ways. Carbon dioxide is released during the <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Metamorphism (geology)">metamorphosis</a> of carbonate rocks when they are <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> into the earth's mantle. This carbon dioxide can be released into the atmosphere and ocean through <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DiVenere2012_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiVenere2012-17">[17]</a></sup> It can also be removed by humans through the direct extraction of kerogens in the form of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. After extraction, fossil fuels are burned to release energy, thus emitting the carbon they store into the atmosphere.</p>

<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, human activity has modified the carbon cycle by changing its components' functions and directly adding carbon to the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> This interaction between humans and the carbon cycle can be described by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_rift" title="Carbon rift">carbon rift</a> theory, which attributes the non-stop growth of carbon emissions to the capitalism.</p>
<p>The largest human impact on the carbon cycle is through direct emissions from burning <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>, which transfers carbon from the geosphere into the atmosphere. The rest of this increase is caused mostly by changes in land-use, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_and_climate_change" title="Deforestation and climate change">particularly deforestation</a>.</p>
<p>Another direct human impact on the carbon cycle is the chemical process of <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> for <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> production, which releases CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Clinker is an industrial <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>.</p>
<p>Humans also influence the carbon cycle indirectly by changing the terrestrial and oceanic biosphere. Over the past several centuries, direct and indirect human-caused <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and land cover change (LUCC) has led to the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>, which lowers ecosystems' resilience to environmental stresses and decreases their ability to remove carbon from the atmosphere. More directly, it often leads to the release of carbon from terrestrial ecosystems into the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> for agricultural purposes removes forests, which hold large amounts of carbon, and replaces them, generally with agricultural or urban areas. Both of these replacement land cover types store comparatively small amounts of carbon, so that the net product of the process is that more carbon stays in the atmosphere.</p>
<p>Other human-caused changes to the environment change ecosystems' productivity and their ability to remove carbon from the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a>, for example, damages plants and soils, while many agricultural and land use practices lead to higher <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> rates, washing carbon out of soils and decreasing plant productivity.</p>
<p>Humans also affect the oceanic carbon cycle. Current trends in climate change lead to higher ocean temperatures, thus modifying ecosystems. Also, acid rain and polluted runoff from agriculture and industry change the ocean's chemical composition. Such changes can have dramatic effects on highly sensitive ecosystems such as <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>, thus limiting the ocean's ability to absorb carbon from the atmosphere on a regional scale and reducing oceanic biodiversity globally.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_emissions" title="Arctic methane emissions">Arctic methane emissions</a> indirectly caused by anthropogenic global warming also affect the carbon cycle, and contribute to further warming in what is known as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a>.</p>
<p>On 12 November 2015, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA scientists</a> reported that human-made carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">continues to increase</a> above levels not seen in hundreds of thousands of years: currently, about half of the carbon dioxide released from the burning of fossil fuels remains in the atmosphere and is not absorbed by vegetation and the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20151112-ab_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20151112-ab-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20151112b_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20151112b-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20151110_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151110-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AP-20151109_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151109-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
