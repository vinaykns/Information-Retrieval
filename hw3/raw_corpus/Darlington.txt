<p><b>Darlington</b> is a large <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>, in <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, and part of the <a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley" title="Tees Valley">Tees Valley</a>. With a population of 106,000 in 2011, the town lies on the <a href="/wiki/River_Skerne" title="River Skerne">River Skerne</a>, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">River Tees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The town owes much of its development to the influence of local <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> families in the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_era" title="Georgian era">Georgian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, and who provided much of the finance and vision in creating the [Stockton and Darlington Railway]], the world's first steam locomotive powered, permanent passenger railway. The town is often colloquially referred to as 'Darlo'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darlington started life as an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> settlement. The name Darlington derives from the Anglo-Saxon <i>Dearthington</i>, which seemingly meant 'the settlement of Deornoth's people', but by Norman times the name had changed to Derlinton. During the 17th and 18th centuries the town was generally known by the name of Darnton.<sup id="cite_ref-englandsnortheast_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-englandsnortheast-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darlington has a historic market area in the town centre. Built in 1183, the <a href="/wiki/Grade_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade I">Grade I</a> listed <a href="/wiki/Cuthbert_of_Lindisfarne" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuthbert of Lindisfarne">St Cuthbert</a>'s Church is one of the most important early English churches in the north of England.<sup id="cite_ref-visitdarlington_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visitdarlington-6">[6]</a></sup> However the oldest church in the town happens to be that of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_the_Apostle" title="Andrew the Apostle">St Andrew</a>'s Church built around 1125 and presides in the <a href="/wiki/Haughton-le-Skerne" title="Haughton-le-Skerne">Haughton</a> area of Darlington.</p>
<p>Visiting during the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> noted that the town was eminent for "good bleaching of linen, so that I have known cloth brought from Scotland to be bleached here". However he also disparaged the town, writing that it had "nothing remarkable but dirt" (the roads would typically be unpaved at the time).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Durham_Ox" title="Durham Ox">Durham Ox</a> came from Darlington.<sup id="cite_ref-englandsnortheast_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-englandsnortheast-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th-century_industry">19th-century industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19th-century industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the early 19th century, Darlington remained a small market town.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As the century progressed, powerful <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> families such as the <a href="/wiki/Pease_family_(Darlington)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pease family (Darlington)">Pease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backhouse%27s_Bank" title="Backhouse's Bank">Backhouse</a> families were prominent employers and philanthropists in the area. Darlington's most famous landmark, the clock tower, was a gift to the town by the industrialist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pease_(railway_pioneer)" title="Joseph Pease (railway pioneer)">Joseph Pease</a> in 1864.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The clock's face was produced by <a href="/wiki/T._Cooke_%26_Sons" title="T. Cooke &amp; Sons">T. Cooke &amp; Sons</a> of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, and the tower bells were cast by <a href="/wiki/John_Warner_%26_Sons" title="John Warner &amp; Sons">John Warner &amp; Sons</a> of nearby <a href="/wiki/Norton,_County_Durham" title="Norton, County Durham">Norton-on-Tees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-visitdarlington_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visitdarlington-6">[6]</a></sup> These bells were in fact the sister bells to those which are inside the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Tower">Elizabeth Tower</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Houses_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Houses of Parliament">Houses of Parliament</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, the most famous of which is called <a href="/wiki/Big_Ben" title="Big Ben">Big Ben</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Mechanics_Institute" title="Darlington Mechanics Institute">Darlington Mechanics Institute</a> was opened in 1854 by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Pease_Nichol" title="Elizabeth Pease Nichol">Elizabeth Pease Nichol</a>, who had made the largest donation towards its building costs.<sup id="cite_ref-rude_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rude-10">[10]</a></sup> The 91-acre South Park was redeveloped into its current form in 1853, with financial backing from the Backhouse family.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Waterhouse" title="Alfred Waterhouse">Alfred Waterhouse</a>, responsible for London's <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">Natural History Museum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Town_Hall" title="Manchester Town Hall">Manchester Town Hall</a>, designed the <a href="/wiki/Grade_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade II">Grade II</a> listed Victorian Market Hall in 1860, and also the Backhouse's Bank building, now a branch of <a href="/wiki/Barclays" title="Barclays">Barclays</a>, in 1864, the latter taking three years to complete.<sup id="cite_ref-visitdarlington_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visitdarlington-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Gordon_Hoskins" title="George Gordon Hoskins">George Gordon Hoskins</a> was responsible for much of the town's architecture in this period, such as <i>The King's Hotel</i>. The <i>Darlington Free Library</i> was built with funding from <a href="/wiki/Edward_Pease_(railway_pioneer)" title="Edward Pease (railway pioneer)">Edward Pease</a>, and opened in 1885 when the Council accepted the gift of the purpose built, fully stocked library and agreed to run it in perpetuity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Darlington is known for its associations with the birth of the modern railway. This is celebrated in the town at <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Railway_Centre_and_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington Railway Centre and Museum">Darlington Railway Centre and Museum</a>. On the 27th of September 1825 <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a>'s engine <a href="/w/index.php?title=Locomotion_No.1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Locomotion No.1 (page does not exist)">Locomotion No.1</a> ushered in the modern railway age when it it travelled between <a href="/wiki/Shildon" title="Shildon">Shildon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stockton-on-Tees" title="Stockton-on-Tees">Stockton-on-Tees</a> via Darlington, on the <a href="/wiki/Stockton_and_Darlington_Railway" title="Stockton and Darlington Railway">Stockton and Darlington Railway</a>, which from its outset was designed for passengers and goods, to a standard gauge on a permanent main line with branches and powered by steam locomotives.</p>
<p>The town later became an important centre for railway manufacturing. An early railway works was the <a href="/wiki/Hopetown_Carriage_Works" title="Hopetown Carriage Works">Hopetown Carriage Works</a> (est.1853) which supplied carriages and locomotives to the Stockton and Darlington Railway. The engineering firm of <a href="/wiki/William_and_Alfred_Kitching" class="mw-redirect" title="William and Alfred Kitching">William and Alfred Kitching</a> also manufactured locomotives in the 19th century. The town developed to have three significant works; the largest of these was the main line <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Works" title="Darlington Works">Darlington Works</a>, whose main works were known as the North Road Shops which opened in 1863 and closed in 1966. Another was <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson_and_Company" title="Robert Stephenson and Company">Robert Stephenson &amp; Co.</a> (colloquially: "Stivvies"), who moved to Darlington from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in 1902, became Robert Stephensons &amp; Hawthorns in 1937, were absorbed by <a href="/wiki/English_Electric" title="English Electric">English Electric</a> around 1960, and closed by 1964. The third was <a href="/wiki/Faverdale_Wagon_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="Faverdale Wagon Works">Faverdale Wagon Works</a>, established in 1923 and closed in 1962, which in the 1950s was a UK pioneer in the application of mass-production techniques to the manufacture of railway goods wagons.</p>
<p>To commemorate the town's contribution to the railways, <a href="/wiki/David_Mach" title="David Mach">David Mach</a>'s 1997 work "Train" is located alongside the A66, close to the original Stockton–Darlington railway. It is a life-size brick sculpture of a steaming locomotive emerging from a tunnel, made from 185,000 "Accrington Nori" bricks. The work had a budget of £760,000.</p>
<p>For 19 years, the <a href="/wiki/A1_Steam_Locomotive_Trust" title="A1 Steam Locomotive Trust">A1 Steam Locomotive Trust</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> built a 50th member of the long withdrawn <a href="/wiki/LNER_Peppercorn_Class_A1" title="LNER Peppercorn Class A1">LNER Peppercorn Class A1</a> engine, called <a href="/wiki/LNER_Peppercorn_Class_A1_60163_Tornado" title="LNER Peppercorn Class A1 60163 Tornado">'Tornado'</a> and numbered 60163, from scratch in the 1853 former <a href="/wiki/Stockton_and_Darlington_Railway_Carriage_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockton and Darlington Railway Carriage Works">Stockton and Darlington Railway Carriage Works</a> at Hopetown. Many of the original fleet had been built at <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Works" title="Darlington Works">Darlington locomotive works</a> in the late 1940s.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering">Engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Darlington has long been a centre for engineering, particularly <a href="/wiki/Bridge_building" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridge building">bridge building</a>. Bridges built in Darlington are found as far away as the <a href="/wiki/River_Nile" class="mw-redirect" title="River Nile">River Nile</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Amazon" class="mw-redirect" title="River Amazon">River Amazon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-englandsnortheast_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-englandsnortheast-5">[5]</a></sup> The large engineering firm <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Bridge_%26_Engineering_Company" title="Cleveland Bridge &amp; Engineering Company">Cleveland Bridge &amp; Engineering Company</a> still has its headquarters in the town. The firm built the <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Bridge" title="Tyne Bridge">Tyne Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough_Transporter_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Middlesbrough Transporter Bridge">Middlesbrough Transporter Bridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humber_Bridge" title="Humber Bridge">Humber Bridge</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Harbour_Bridge" title="Sydney Harbour Bridge">Sydney Harbour Bridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> One of the leading engine building firms, <a href="/wiki/Cummins" title="Cummins">Cummins</a>, has major premises in Darlington, and it houses the industrial headquarters of <a href="/wiki/AMEC" class="mw-redirect" title="AMEC">AMEC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The engineering companies Darlington Forge Company (cl.1967)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Whessoe" title="Whessoe">Whessoe</a> also originated in Darlington.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1870, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Northern_Echo" title="The Northern Echo">The Northern Echo</a></i> newspaper was launched. It is based in Priestgate and is a long-standing part of life in the North East. Although a regional paper, it is a full-bodied newspaper in its own right and includes national and international news in its scope, as well as having a well-maintained website and active social media presence. <a href="/wiki/William_Thomas_Stead" class="mw-redirect" title="William Thomas Stead">William Thomas Stead</a> was a notable editor of <i>The Northern Echo</i> who died in 1912 on the <a href="/wiki/Titanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Titanic">Titanic</a>. Opposite The Northern Echo building is The William Stead <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public house</a>, restaurant and <a href="/wiki/Beer_garden" title="Beer garden">beer garden</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1939, Darlington had the most cinema seats per head of population in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-visitdarlington_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visitdarlington-6">[6]</a></sup> The town centre has undergone a full refurbishment entitled <i>The Pedestrian Heart</i>, which has seen the majority of the town centre pedestrianised.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Initially, the project received criticism surrounding changes to public transport, and removal of Victorian features along High Row.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There is now growing evidence, however, that the now-completed changes are meeting with local approval.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Then in 2014, the town saw the revamp of one of its old cinemas, The Majestic, into a soft play centre and theatre.</p>
<p>In August 2008 the King's Hotel in the town centre was devastated by fire, severely damaging the roof and 100 bedrooms. Several shops, including <a href="/wiki/Woolworths_Group" title="Woolworths Group">Woolworths</a>, were damaged and had to close for weeks afterwards. No one was killed in the blaze. The hotel was carefully restored to its former glory and re-opened in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Darlington_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Darlington (UK Parliament constituency)">Darlington (UK Parliament constituency)</a></div>
<p>On 1 April 1997, the Borough of Darlington became a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority area</a> with the formation of <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Borough_Council" title="Darlington Borough Council">Darlington Borough Council</a>, which separated it from the <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan county</a> of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a> for administrative purposes only, as the town is still within <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> for ceremonial purposes. Currently a <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> seat, the current <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> is <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour's</a> <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Chapman" title="Jenny Chapman">Jenny Chapman</a>. Former members of parliament for the town include <a href="/wiki/Alan_Milburn" title="Alan Milburn">Alan Milburn</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Health" title="Secretary of State for Health">Secretary of State for Health</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> Labour Government and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fallon" title="Michael Fallon">Michael Fallon</a>, the current <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Defence" title="Secretary of State for Defence">Secretary of State for Defence</a> under the <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> Coalition Government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Darlington is located in south <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a> close to the River Tees which acts as the border between Durham and Yorkshire. Both the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">River Tees</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Skerne" title="River Skerne">River Skerne</a> pass through the town, the River Skerne later joining the River Tees which then flows through Teesside and into the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Darlington_railway_station" title="Darlington railway station">Darlington railway station</a> lies on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a>. There are also local services from the historic <a href="/wiki/North_Road_railway_station" title="North Road railway station">North Road railway station</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Railway_Centre_and_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington Railway Centre and Museum">Darlington Railway Centre and Museum</a>.</p>
<p>Neighbouring towns include <a href="/wiki/Newton_Aycliffe" title="Newton Aycliffe">Newton Aycliffe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockton-on-Tees" title="Stockton-on-Tees">Stockton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Auckland" title="Bishop Auckland">Bishop Auckland</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suburbs">Suburbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Suburbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several suburbs of Darlington. In the north are <a href="/wiki/Harrowgate_Hill" title="Harrowgate Hill">Harrowgate Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harrowgate_Village" title="Harrowgate Village">Harrowgate Village</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coatham_Mundeville" title="Coatham Mundeville">Coatham Mundeville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beaumont_Hill" title="Beaumont Hill">Beaumont Hill</a> and to the north-east are <a href="/wiki/Whinfield" title="Whinfield">Whinfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haughton_Le_Skerne" class="mw-redirect" title="Haughton Le Skerne">Haughton Le Skerne</a>. To the east is the suburb of Eastbourne and Red Hall with <a href="/wiki/Firthmoor" class="mw-redirect" title="Firthmoor">Firthmoor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skerne_Park" title="Skerne Park">Skerne Park</a> to the south. Situated in the west end are <a href="/wiki/Hummersknott" title="Hummersknott">Hummersknott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mowden,_County_Durham" title="Mowden, County Durham">Mowden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blackwell,_County_Durham" title="Blackwell, County Durham">Blackwell</a>. Finally, to the north-west are <a href="/wiki/Branksome,_County_Durham" title="Branksome, County Durham">Branksome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cockerton" title="Cockerton">Cockerton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faverdale" title="Faverdale">Faverdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Denes" title="The Denes">The Denes</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Park,_Darlington" title="West Park, Darlington">West Park</a>, High Grange and Pierremont which is associated with the notable <a href="/wiki/Henry_Pease_(MP)" title="Henry Pease (MP)">Henry Pease (MP)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_towns">Twin towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Twin towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Darlington is <a href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twinned</a> with:</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/EE_(telecommunications_company)" class="mw-redirect" title="EE (telecommunications company)">EE</a> are the largest private sector employers in the town, hiring 2,500 people. Another major employer in the area is the English division of the <a href="/wiki/Student_Loans_Company" title="Student Loans Company">Student Loans Company</a>, Student Finance England, who are based at Lingfield Point and employ over 1,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Nevertheless, there are major engineering sites, with both <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Bridge" title="Cleveland Bridge">Cleveland Bridge</a> and the industrial arm of <a href="/wiki/AMEC" class="mw-redirect" title="AMEC">AMEC</a> headquartered in the town. Another major firm within the engineering industry based in the town is <a href="/wiki/Cummins" title="Cummins">Cummins</a> which has a large scale engine building facility in the Morton Park area of Darlington. UK retail company <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a> have their largest warehouse distribution centre in the North of England located in Darlington, within Faverdale Industrial Estate to the North West of the town. Other large service sector companies with offices in the town include Darlington Building Society, Bannatyne Fitness Ltd and the national vehicle rental company Northgate, which is headquartered in the town; also within the Morton Park area.</p>
<p>As well as the large engineering sites based in the town, Darlington is also home to many businesses within the modern and developing industries including Information Technology. With large firms such as Infoserve Ltd having offices within the Morton Park area to the east of Darlington, located next to the A66 Darlington Eastern Bypass.</p>
<p>Close to the Darlington A66 Bypass is also the Link66 redevelopment scheme, which is a large plot of land close to the Morton Park site. It is hoped developers will see potential in the site due to its excellent transport links with fast access to the town centre and central park regeneration zone via the new Darlington Eastern Transport Corridor constructed in 2008. While also being in close proximity to the <a href="/wiki/A1(M)" class="mw-redirect" title="A1(M)">A1(M)</a> Motorway which heads south to London and north to Edinburgh.</p>
<p>In November 2012, a deal was signed between Darlington Borough Council and developer Terrace Hill for a £30 million re-development of the site of the former Feethams bus depot. The development includes a new multiplex cinema run by <a href="/wiki/Vue_Cinemas" title="Vue Cinemas">Vue Cinemas</a> to serve Darlington and the wider South Durham area, as the area currently has no multiplex cinema. Residents currently have to travel to Teesside for the nearest multiplex cinema. The development will also house a new 80 bedroom <a href="/wiki/Premier_Inn" title="Premier Inn">Premier Inn</a> hotel, and various food and drink venues including <a href="/wiki/Prezzo" title="Prezzo">Prezzo</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bella_Pasta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bella Pasta (page does not exist)">Bella Pasta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungry_Horse" title="Hungry Horse">Hungry Horse</a>. The proposal had an expected completion date of late 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> though this did overrun with completion early 2016.</p>
<p>Darlington Borough Council also announced the neighbouring site, previously a car park for the town hall, was also to be redeveloped to house riverside office space for the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Education" title="Department for Education">Department for Education</a> in an effort to safeguard Darlington jobs. This was officially opened on 19 March 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The international telecommunications company <a href="/wiki/BT_Group" title="BT Group">BT Group</a> recently announced Darlington as one of the economically important locations in England to have BT fibre-optic cables installed underground as part of the company's <a href="/wiki/BT_Infinity" title="BT Infinity">BT Infinity</a> superfast broadband rollout project. BT Group cites their decision to include Darlington in the national rollour of multi-provider fibre optic (cable) broadband as necessary due to the towns relatively large amount of IT demanding firms and future plans for developments including space for high-tech firms. Darlington Borough Council, residents and local businesses praised the decision by BT Group and it is hoped the investment will attract enterprise to the town, potentially creating employment for residents and boosting the economy.</p>
<p>The Morton Park area of Darlington is currently undergoing a partial redevelopment, with areas of unused waste land being redeveloped into modern industrial and office space, the developments add to the plans for redevelopment of the current Council depot on Central Park which is also to be redeveloped into commercial space and the Link66 scheme also close to Morton Park. Other commercial spaces in Darlington include North Road Industrial Estate, which includes a <a href="/wiki/Morrisons" title="Morrisons">Morrisons</a> superstore supermarket; Cleveland Trading Estate and Faverdale Industrial Estate.</p>

<p>Darlington is historically a market town with a well established weekly outdoor market and an indoor market located underneath the town clock on Prebend Row. Also located on Prebend Row is the Cornmill Shopping centre which is the main retail area of Darlington. The market square is one of the biggest in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darlington attracts people from a wide area. <a href="/wiki/House_of_Fraser" title="House of Fraser">House of Fraser</a> trading as <a href="/wiki/Binns_(department_store)" title="Binns (department store)">Binns (department store)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marks_%26_Spencer" title="Marks &amp; Spencer">Marks &amp; Spencer</a> both have stores in the town centre.</p>

<p>Darlington has been subject to increasing amounts of inward investment in recent years with the completion of large scale local council sponsored schemes such as the 2007 Town Centre Pedestrian Heart Project worth 10 million pounds, which saw most of Darlington Town Centre modernised with an emphasis on fewer vehicles in the centre and roads pedestrianised completely. The town is also received further investment worth 170 million pounds with the creation of Central Park, a new industrial, residential, cultural and education park which includes hundreds of new affordable homes, Darlington College Campus, Teesside University Darlington Campus, luxury office space, retail units and a new local centre. The Central Park construction site stretches from the current Darlington College campus on Haughton road to the Darlington Railway station entrance at Yarm Road.</p>
<p>Furthermore, the local council, regional development agency and government departments are consulting about investing in an upgrade of Darlington's A66 Eastern Bypass to dual carriageway to improve congestion and journey times, as well as to prevent the tees valley region from suffering due to the road currently being single track passing Darlington which is essentially the gateway to the tees valley region.</p>
<p>All of the investment in recent years and planned investment with large scale public sector support makes Darlington the most invested in Borough in the Tees Valley region, with Stockton a close second. The Civic Theatre is a popular arts venue in the town, hosting a mix of musicals, dramas, plays and pantomimes. The smaller but well-used Arts Centre, founded in 1982, featured smaller events, film screenings and more experimental material; however this closed in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Darlington is well known within the North of England as being tolerant of diverse lifestyles as it was the first town in England to allow same-sex civil ceremonies in 2001. The town thus has a small gay scene developed over a number of years by the local LGBT community, hosting a weekly Gay Night held every Monday night supported by over 7 venues in Darlington town centre. The scene has grown to become a beacon of LGBT entertainment in the region, and particularly the Tees Valley with the support of the local LGBT support charity Gay Advice Darlington/Durham (GADD), Darlington Gay Scene Collective (DGSC) as well as a large selection of local bars and clubs. The town hosts an annual Gay Pride Festival which comprises a series of celebrations of local LGBT culture and acceptance held at venues across the town.</p>
<p>The Rhythm'n'Brews festival is a music and real ale festival normally held in early autumn, with many rock, blues and jazz acts playing at various venues around Darlington.</p>
<p>The Forum Music Centre, opened in 2004, hosts regular live music events, from Ska and Punk to Indie and Classic Rock. It also runs a popular comedy club. As well as live music, the facility houses a state of the art recording studio and several rehearsal rooms. The Carmel Rhythm Club, at Carmel College in the Hummersknott end of town, is another music venue. A charitable organisation for the Carmel PTA (Parents and Teachers Association) attracts many large bands in the genre of rhythm and blues.</p>
<p>Darlington town centre has built a strong focus on independent shopping, and offers a more varied shopping experience than in many other UK towns and cities which have a higher concentration of national and international chains. Grange Road has a number of "designer" stores, Duke Street houses art galleries and restaurants and between the two is Skinnergate, which holds the greatest variety of original stores.</p>
<p>Darlington Dog Show was a championship event from 1969. It was usually held in September on the showground in South Park; but it has now moved to Ripon.</p>
<p>Football teams in the town are Darlington, a team in the Football Conference, and Darlington Railway Athletic, a team in the Northern League. The rugby teams are Darlington Mowden Park R.F.C., who were promoted as champions from National League Three in 2011-12 and played in National League 2 in 2012-13, and Darlington RFC. Cricket clubs are Darlington Cricket Club and Darlington Railway Athletic Cricket Club. The Darlington 10K road run is held every August, and attracts several thousand competitors. The Dolphin Centre, which provides a wide range of sporting facilities, was opened by Roger Bannister in 1982. It received a £5m refurbishment in 2006 and was later officially opened by Redcar athlete Tanni Grey-Thompson.</p>
<p>Darlington Library, an impressive Grade II listed building situated in Crown Street,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> was a gift to the town from Edward Pease (1834–1880), a leading Darlington Quaker. He left £10,000 in his will to build a free library in Darlington or for other educational purposes. The people of Darlington voted to adopt the Free Libraries Act, and the town's first free library was officially opened on 23 October 1885 by his daughter, Lady Lymington. The building now houses the Central Lending Department, Reference Library and Centre for Local Studies.</p>
<p>The Jamia Mosque and Islamic Society of Darlington is located in the North Lodge Terrace area of the town, an area with a high proportion of ethnic minority residents.</p>
<p>Darlington Memorial Hospital is on Hollyhurst Road,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> in the corridor between Woodland Road and The Denes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Darlington has excellent road transport links with the North East's major trunk route the <a href="/wiki/A1(M)_motorway" title="A1(M) motorway">A1(M)</a>, which bypasses the town to the west. It was completed in 1965 replacing the Great North Road route which is now known as the A167. The town is served by 3 close junctions of the <a href="/wiki/A1(M)_motorway" title="A1(M) motorway">A1(M)</a>: Junction 57 A66(M), Junction 58 A68, Junction 59 A167. Junction 59 is the access exit for Darlington Motorway Services (Newton Park), with an onsite filling station, Hotel and a 24-hour restaurant with a drive through. Darlington is also close to other major trunk routes, including the A66 transpennine route connecting Darlington to <a href="/wiki/Stockton-on-Tees" title="Stockton-on-Tees">Stockton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/A19_road" title="A19 road">A19</a>. The £5.9&#160;million five-mile (8&#160;km) <a href="/wiki/A66_road" title="A66 road">A66</a> Darlington Eastern Bypass opened on 25 November 1985. The Darlington Eastern Transport Corridor, linking the Central Park regeneration zone (Haughton Road) and Darlington town-centre to a new roundabout on the A66, was opened in the summer of 2008.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Darlington_railway_station" title="Darlington railway station">Darlington railway station</a> is an important interchange stop on the East Coast Main Line and has regular services to <a href="/wiki/London_King%27s_Cross_railway_station" title="London King's Cross railway station">London Kings Cross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds City station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh Waverley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Piccadilly_station" title="Manchester Piccadilly station">Manchester Piccadilly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airport railway station">Manchester Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_railway_station" title="Newcastle railway station">Newcastle</a>. Darlington railway station also serves as the mainline interchange for <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough_railway_station" title="Middlesbrough railway station">Middlesbrough railway station</a>, which itself has few intercity services. Darlington railway station boasts a large <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> clock tower which, in the relatively low rise town centre, can be seen throughout large areas of the town. Darlington also has access to the <a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Tees Valley Line">Tees Valley Line</a> connecting all the main settlements of the <a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley" title="Tees Valley">Tees Valley</a> and runs from Bishop Auckland to Saltburn via Darlington, Stockton-on-Tees and Middlesbrough among many other smaller settlements.</p>

<p>Bus transport in the town is provided by <a href="/wiki/Arriva_North_East" title="Arriva North East">Arriva North East</a>. Darlington lost out on considerable public receipts when the municipal bus operator <i>Darlingon Transport Company</i> was placed into administration during an attempted <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatisation</a>, due to continuing financial difficulties and the <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Bus_War" title="Darlington Bus War">Darlington Bus War</a>.</p>
<p>Arriva run most of the bus services in the town.</p>
<p>Stagecoach used to operate in the town (since the Bus War) until 2007, when they sold their operations to Arriva. Arriva therefore became the main bus operator.</p>
<p>Darlington was chosen by the Department for Transport as one of three national Sustainable Travel Demonstration Towns (together with Peterborough and Worcester) in 2004, and has successfully delivered a three-year research and marketing programme to promote sustainable travel choices under the brand name 'Local Motion'. It was also chosen as one of six cycling demonstration towns in October 2005, receiving £3 million worth of funding from the government and local council money.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The money has been spent over the course of three years on improving cycling facilities and routes, and linking the town to the national cycle route network. Darlington is the only place to win both sustainable travel and cycling demonstration town status.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport">Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Durham_Tees_Valley_Airport" title="Durham Tees Valley Airport">Durham Tees Valley Airport</a> is 5 miles (8&#160;km) east of Darlington town centre and serves <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a>. The airport was known as Teesside Airport until 2004. It has flights to a few domestic locations across the UK and international flights to locations across Europe. Many private or General Aviation Flights use the airport. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Weston_Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Weston Aviation (page does not exist)">Weston Aviation</a> are there for that purpose. The airport has a Fire Training Centre that trains many airport firefighters from all over the UK. The nearest larger airports are <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Airport" title="Newcastle Airport">Newcastle Airport</a> (42.3 miles (68.1&#160;km)) and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Leeds Bradford International Airport</a> (62 miles (100&#160;km)).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_schools_in_Darlington" title="List of schools in Darlington">List of schools in Darlington</a></div>
<p>The town has the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_Sixth_Form_College" title="Queen Elizabeth Sixth Form College">Queen Elizabeth Sixth Form College</a> (former <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a>). There are many other schools including: <a href="/wiki/Carmel_RC_Technology_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Carmel RC Technology College">Carmel RC Technology College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darlington_School_of_Maths_and_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington School of Maths and Science">Darlington School of Maths and Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haughton_Academy" title="Haughton Academy">Haughton Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hummersknott_Academy" title="Hummersknott Academy">Hummersknott Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurworth_School" title="Hurworth School">Hurworth School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longfield_Academy,_Darlington" title="Longfield Academy, Darlington">Longfield Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polam_Hall_School" title="Polam Hall School">Polam Hall School</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Aidan%27s_Church_of_England_Academy" title="St Aidan's Church of England Academy">St Aidan's Church of England Academy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Darlington_College" title="Darlington College">Darlington College</a> is the newly built FE College. Teesside University opened a Darlington campus in 2011 offering higher education in the town to students and businesses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Darlington is home to the regional daily newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Northern_Echo" title="The Northern Echo">The Northern Echo</a></i> and its sister weekly newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Darlington_%26_Stockton_Times" title="Darlington &amp; Stockton Times">Darlington &amp; Stockton Times</a></i>. Local <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> radio station <a href="/wiki/Star_Radio_North_East" title="Star Radio North East">Star Radio North East</a> broadcasts from the town.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2009 the town appointed an official 'Twitterer in residence', the first of its kind in the UK. Mike McTimoney (known on Twitter as TheDarloBard) is a local regular Twitter user who has been officially charged with tweeting for and about Darlington,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and to help promote The Darlington Experiment 2.0, the town's <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> campaign.</p>
<p>In 1998 the <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> town of Darlington hit the national headlines after local man David James Harker murdered and beheaded his girlfriend Julie Paterson before eating parts of her body in the Harewood Grove area, close to the large Sainsbury's supermarket; followed by the dumping of her torso which was later discovered in bushes by residents and reported to Durham Police. Harker was later diagnosed by experts as having Antisocial Personality Disorder, and is remembered as the Darlington Cannibal Killer. He was jailed for life in February 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>The town is home to the <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team <a href="/wiki/Darlington_1883" title="Darlington 1883">Darlington 1883</a>, successor club to <a href="/wiki/Darlington_F.C." title="Darlington F.C.">Darlington F.C.</a>, known as The Quakers because of the contributions made to the town by men such as <a href="/wiki/Edward_Pease_(railway_pioneer)" title="Edward Pease (railway pioneer)">Edward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pease_(railway_pioneer)" title="Joseph Pease (railway pioneer)">Joseph Pease</a>, members of the <a href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Society of Friends">Religious Society of Friends</a>. The town's football club played at the 25,000 capacity <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington Arena">Darlington Arena</a> when it opened on Neasham Road in 2003, after 120 years at <a href="/wiki/Feethams" title="Feethams">Feethams</a>. In 2010 they were relegated from the <a href="/wiki/Football_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Football League">Football League</a>, 21 years after they suffered a similar fate when they were then promoted back from the <a href="/wiki/Football_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Football Conference">Football Conference</a> at the first attempt. In the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_FA_Trophy" title="2010–11 FA Trophy">2010–11 season</a> Darlington won the <a href="/wiki/FA_Trophy" title="FA Trophy">FA Trophy</a> but were relegated four divisions to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Football_League" title="Northern Football League">Northern Football League</a> Division One for season 2012–13. Darlington played home games at Heritage Park in <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Auckland" title="Bishop Auckland">Bishop Auckland</a> from the start of the 2012-13 season until moving back to Darlington in late 2016 to commence a new long term groundshare arrangement with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Darlington_Rugby_Football_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Darlington Rugby Football Club (page does not exist)">Darlington Rugby Football Club</a> at Blackwell Meadows. Darlington's first home game at Blackwell Meadows (a 3-2 home win against <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax Town">Halifax Town</a>) took place on 26 December 2016.</p>
<p>Darlingtons leading athletics club, the Darlington Harriers was formed in 1891 and has had a number of successful athletes wearing the club colours as well as GB vests. The club celebrated its 125th year in 2016 in style with anniversary games held at Eastbourne Sports Complex.</p>
<p>Darlington's leading <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Rugby Union">Rugby Union</a> club is <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Mowden_Park_R.F.C." title="Darlington Mowden Park R.F.C.">Darlington Mowden RFC</a>. Mowden were promoted as champions from <a href="/wiki/National_League_3_North" title="National League 3 North">National League 3 North</a> in 2011–12 and now own and play at the Darlington Arena, in the fourth tier of the English league system, the <a href="/wiki/National_League_2_North" title="National League 2 North">National League 2 North</a>. <a href="/wiki/Darlington_RFC" title="Darlington RFC">Darlington RFC</a> play at Blackwell Meadows in the seventh tier, <a href="/wiki/North_1_East" title="North 1 East">North 1 East</a>. The Northern Echo arena will also have a role to play in the 2015 Rugby World cup hosting the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_union_team" title="New Zealand national rugby union team">New Zealand</a> national team known as the "<a href="/wiki/All_Blacks" class="mw-redirect" title="All Blacks">All Blacks</a>".</p>
