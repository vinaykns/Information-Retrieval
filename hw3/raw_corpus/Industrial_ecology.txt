			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Industrial ecology</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Industrial ecology</b> (IE) is the study of <a href="/wiki/Material_flow" title="Material flow">material</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_flow_(ecology)" title="Energy flow (ecology)">energy flows</a> through industrial systems. The <a href="/wiki/Global_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Global economy">global</a> <a href="/wiki/Industrial_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial economy">industrial economy</a> can be modelled as a network of industrial processes that extract resources from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and transform those resources into <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodities</a> which can be bought and sold to meet the needs of humanity. Industrial ecology seeks to quantify the material flows and document the industrial processes that make modern society function. Industrial ecologists are often concerned with the impacts that industrial activities have on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, with use of the planet's supply of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, and with problems of <a href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Waste disposal">waste disposal</a>. Industrial ecology is a young but growing multidisciplinary field of research which combines aspects of <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toxicology" title="Toxicology">toxicology</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_natural_science" title="Outline of natural science">natural sciences</a>.</p>
<p>Industrial ecology has been defined as a "systems-based, multidisciplinary discourse that seeks to understand emergent behaviour of complex integrated human/natural systems".<sup id="cite_ref-OntologiesOfIE_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OntologiesOfIE-2">[2]</a></sup> The field approaches issues of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> by examining problems from multiple perspectives, usually involving aspects of sociology, the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>. The name comes from the idea that the analogy of natural systems should be used as an aid in understanding how to design sustainable industrial systems.<sup id="cite_ref-StrategiesForManufacturing_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StrategiesForManufacturing-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png/250px-IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png" width="250" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png/375px-IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png/500px-IndustrialSymbiosisWasteHeatExchange.png 2x" data-file-width="1285" data-file-height="1392" /></a>

<p>Industrial ecology is concerned with the shifting of industrial process from linear (open loop) systems, in which resource and capital investments move through the system to become waste, to a closed loop system where wastes can become inputs for new processes.</p>
<p>Much of the research focuses on the following areas:<sup id="cite_ref-is4ieHistory_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-is4ieHistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Material_flow_analysis" title="Material flow analysis">material</a> and energy flow studies ("<a href="/wiki/Industrial_metabolism" title="Industrial metabolism">industrial metabolism</a>")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dematerialization_(economics)" title="Dematerialization (economics)">dematerialization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">decarbonization</a></li>
<li>technological change and the environment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Life cycle assessment">life-cycle planning, design and assessment</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Design_for_Environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Design for Environment">design for the environment ("eco-design")</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extended_producer_responsibility" title="Extended producer responsibility">extended producer responsibility ("product stewardship")</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eco-industrial_park" title="Eco-industrial park">eco-industrial parks ("industrial symbiosis")</a></li>
<li>product-oriented environmental policy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eco-efficiency" title="Eco-efficiency">eco-efficiency</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Industrial ecology seeks to understand the way in which industrial systems (for example a factory, an <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>, or national or global economy) interact with the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Natural ecosystems provide a metaphor for understanding how different parts of industrial systems interact with one another, in an "ecosystem" based on resources and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructural_capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrastructural capital">infrastructural capital</a> rather than on <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>. It seeks to exploit the idea that natural systems do not have waste in them to inspire <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_design" title="Sustainable design">sustainable design</a>.</p>
<p>Along with more general <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> and material conservation goals, and redefining <a href="/wiki/Commodity_markets" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodity markets">commodity markets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Product_stewardship" title="Product stewardship">product stewardship</a> relations strictly as a <a href="/wiki/Service_economy" title="Service economy">service economy</a>, industrial ecology is one of the four objectives of <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism" title="Natural Capitalism">Natural Capitalism</a>. This strategy discourages forms of amoral purchasing arising from ignorance of what goes on at a distance and implies a <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> that values <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> highly and relies on more <a href="/wiki/Instructional_capital" title="Instructional capital">instructional capital</a> to design and maintain each unique industrial ecology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_industrial_ecology" title="History of industrial ecology">History of industrial ecology</a></div>
<p>Industrial ecology was popularized in 1989 in a <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> article by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frosch" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Frosch">Robert Frosch</a> and Nicholas E. Gallopoulos. Frosch and Gallopoulos' vision was "why would not our industrial system behave like an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, where the wastes of a species may be <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resource</a> to another species? Why would not the outputs of an industry be the inputs of another, thus reducing use of <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw materials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and saving on <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a> treatment?"<sup id="cite_ref-StrategiesForManufacturing_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StrategiesForManufacturing-3">[3]</a></sup> A notable example resides in a Danish industrial park in the city of <a href="/wiki/Kalundborg_Eco-industrial_Park" title="Kalundborg Eco-industrial Park">Kalundborg</a>. Here several linkages of <a href="/wiki/Byproduct" class="mw-redirect" title="Byproduct">byproducts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> can be found between numerous entities such as a large power plant, an oil refinery, a pharmaceutical plant, a plasterboard factory, an enzyme manufacturer, a waste company and the city itself.<sup id="cite_ref-KalundborgCentre_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KalundborgCentre-5">[5]</a></sup> Another example is the Rantasalmi EIP in Rantasalmi, Finland. While this country has had previous organically formed EIP's, the park at Rantasalmi is Finland's first planned EIP.</p>
<p>The scientific field Industrial Ecology has grown quickly in recent years. The Journal of Industrial Ecology (since 1997), the <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Industrial_Ecology" title="International Society for Industrial Ecology">International Society for Industrial Ecology</a> (since 2001), and the journal Progress in Industrial Ecology (since 2004) give Industrial Ecology a strong and dynamic position in the international scientific community. Industrial Ecology principles are also emerging in various policy realms such as the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Circular_Economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Circular Economy">Circular Economy</a> that is being promoted in China. Although the definition of the Circular Economy has yet to be formalized, generally the focus is on strategies such as creating a circular flow of materials, and cascading energy flows. An example of this would be using waste heat from one process to run another process that requires a lower temperature. The hope is that strategy such as this will create a more efficient economy with fewer pollutants and other unwanted by-products.<sup id="cite_ref-CircularEconomy_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CircularEconomy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the central principles of Industrial Ecology is the view that societal and technological systems are bounded within the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, and do not exist outside of it. <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a> is used as a <i>metaphor</i> due to the observation that natural systems reuse materials and have a largely closed loop cycling of nutrients. Industrial Ecology approaches problems with the hypothesis that by using similar principles as <i>natural systems, industrial systems</i> can be improved to reduce their impact on the natural environment as well. The table shows the general metaphor.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kalundborg" title="Kalundborg">Kalundborg</a> industrial park is located in Denmark. This industrial park is special because companies reuse each other's waste (which then becomes by-products). For example, the Energy E2 <a href="/wiki/Asn%C3%A6s_Power_Station" title="Asnæs Power Station">Asnæs Power Station</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> as a by-product of the electricity generation process; this gypsum becomes a resource for the BPB Gyproc A/S which produces plasterboards.<sup id="cite_ref-KalundborgCentre_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KalundborgCentre-5">[5]</a></sup> This is one example of a system inspired by the biosphere-technosphere metaphor: in ecosystems, the waste from one organism is used as inputs to other organisms; in industrial systems, waste from a company is used as a resource by others.</p>
<p>Apart from the direct benefit of incorporating waste into the loop, the use of an eco-industrial park can be a means of making renewable energy generating plants, like <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">Solar PV</a>, more economical and environmentally friendly. In essence, this assists the growth of the renewable energy industry and the environmental benefits that come with replacing fossil-fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-PearcePhotovoltaic_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PearcePhotovoltaic-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additional examples of industrial ecology include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Substituting the fly ash byproduct of coal burning practices for cement in concrete production<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Using second generation biofuels. An example of this is converting grease or cooking oil to biodiesels to fuel vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>IE examines societal issues and their relationship with both technical systems and the environment. Through this <i><a href="/wiki/Holistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic">holistic</a> view</i> , IE recognizes that solving problems must involve understanding the connections that exist between these systems, various aspects cannot be viewed in isolation. Often changes in one part of the overall <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> can propagate and cause changes in another part. Thus, you can only understand a problem if you look at its parts in relation to the whole. Based on this framework, IE looks at environmental issues with a <i><a href="/wiki/Systems_thinking" title="Systems thinking">systems thinking</a></i> approach. A good IE example with these societal impacts can be found at the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lagoon_(geothermal_spa)" title="Blue Lagoon (geothermal spa)">Blue Lagoon</a> in Iceland. The Lagoon uses super-heated water from a local geothermal power plant to fill mineral-rich basins that have become recreational healing centers. In this sense the industrial process of energy production uses its wastewater to provide a crucial resource for the dependent recreational industry.</p>
<p>South Africa's National Cleaner Production Center was created in order to make the region's industries more efficient in terms of materials. Results of the use of sustainable methods will include lowered energy costs and improved waste management. The program assesses existing companies to implement change. The region includes thousands of businesses that are able to benefit from the sustainable consulting.</p>
<p>Take a city for instance. A city can be divided into commercial areas, residential areas, offices, services, infrastructures, and so forth. These are all sub-systems of the 'big city’ system. Problems can emerge in one sub-system, but the solution has to be global. Let’s say the price of housing is rising dramatically because there is too high a demand for housing. One solution would be to build new houses, but this will lead to more people living in the city, leading to the need for more infrastructure like roads, schools, more supermarkets, etc. This system is a simplified interpretation of reality whose behaviors can be ‘predicted’.</p>
<p>In many cases, the systems IE deals with are complex systems. <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">Complexity</a> makes it difficult to understand the behavior of the system and may lead to rebound effects. Due to unforeseen behavioral change of users or consumers, a measure taken to improve environmental performance does not lead to any improvement or may even worsen the situation.</p>
<p>Moreover, <i>life cycle thinking</i> is also a very important principle in industrial ecology. It implies that all environmental impacts caused by a product, system, or project during its life cycle are taken into account. In this context life cycle includes</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">Raw material</a> extraction</li>
<li>Material processing</li>
<li>Manufacture</li>
<li>Use</li>
<li>Maintenance</li>
<li>Disposal</li>
</ul>
<p>The transport necessary between these stages is also taken into account as well as, if relevant, extra stages such as <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remanufacturing" title="Remanufacturing">remanufacture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Recycle">recycle</a>. Adopting a life cycle approach is essential to avoid shifting environmental impacts from one life cycle stage to another. This is commonly referred to as problem shifting. For instance, during the re-design of a product, one can choose to reduce its weight, thereby decreasing use of resources. However, it is possible that the lighter materials used in the new product will be more difficult to dispose of. The environmental impacts of the product gained during the extraction phase are shifted to the disposal phase. Overall environmental improvements are thus null.</p>
<p>A final and important principle of IE is its <i>integrated approach</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_approach" title="Multidisciplinary approach">multidisciplinarity</a></i>. IE takes into account three different disciplines: social sciences (including economics), technical sciences and environmental sciences. The challenge is to merge them into a single approach.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_directions">Future directions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future directions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> metaphor popularized by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frosch" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Frosch">Frosch</a> and Gallopoulos<sup id="cite_ref-StrategiesForManufacturing_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StrategiesForManufacturing-3">[3]</a></sup> has been a valuable creative tool for helping researchers look for novel solutions to difficult problems. Recently, it has been pointed out that this metaphor is based largely on a model of classical ecology, and that advancements in understanding ecology based on <a href="/wiki/Complexity_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Complexity science">complexity science</a> have been made by researchers such as <a href="/wiki/C._S._Holling" title="C. S. Holling">C. S. Holling</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_J._Kay" title="James J. Kay">James J. Kay</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-OnComplexityTheory_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OnComplexityTheory-10">[10]</a></sup> and further advanced in terms of contemporary ecology by others.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WhatHasModernEcosystemTheory_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WhatHasModernEcosystemTheory-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For industrial ecology, this may mean a shift from a more mechanistic view of systems, to one where <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> is viewed as an <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent</a> property of a complex system.<sup id="cite_ref-CanIndustrialEcologyBe_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CanIndustrialEcologyBe-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WouldIndustrialEcologyExist_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WouldIndustrialEcologyExist-16">[16]</a></sup> To explore this further, several researchers are working with <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">agent based modeling techniques</a> .<sup id="cite_ref-ABMAndIE_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABMAndIE-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ApplyingABMinIE_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ApplyingABMinIE-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">Exergy</a> analysis is performed in the field of industrial ecology to use energy more efficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-ExergyUsefulConcept_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ExergyUsefulConcept-19">[19]</a></sup> The term <i>exergy</i> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Zoran_Rant" title="Zoran Rant">Zoran Rant</a> in 1956, but the concept was developed by <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs" title="Josiah Willard Gibbs">J. Willard Gibbs</a>. In recent decades, utilization of exergy has spread outside of physics and engineering to the fields of industrial ecology, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_ecology" title="Systems ecology">systems ecology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Energetics" title="Energetics">energetics</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_examples">Other examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another great example of industrial ecology both in practice and in potential is the Burnside Cleaner Production Centre in <a href="/wiki/Burnside,_Nova_Scotia" title="Burnside, Nova Scotia">Burnside, Nova Scotia</a>. They play a role in facilitating the 'greening' of over 1200 businesses that are located in Burnside, Eastern Canada's largest industrial park. The creation of waste exchange is a big part of what they work towards, which will promote strong industrial ecology relationships. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
