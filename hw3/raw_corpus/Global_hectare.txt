			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Global hectare</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>The <b>global hectare</b> (gha) is a measurement unit for quantifying both the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of people or activities as well as the <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> or its regions. One <b>global hectare</b> represents the average productivity of all biologically productive areas (measured in <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a>) on earth in a given year. Examples of biologically productive areas include cropland, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, and fishing grounds; they do not include <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Open_ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Open ocean">open ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> "Global hectare per person" refers to the amount of biologically productive land and water available per person on the planet. The total number of global hectares is approximately 11.3 billion, averaging about 1.8 global hectares per person (2004).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A total of 13.4 billion hectares were used, or 2.2 global hectares per person, meaning about 20% more was consumed than produced. This is possible because there are natural reserves all around the globe that function as backup food, material and energy supplies, although only for a relatively short period of time. Due to rapid population growth, these reserves are being depleted at an ever increasing tempo. See <a href="/wiki/Earth_Overshoot_Day" title="Earth Overshoot Day">Earth Overshoot Day</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_hectare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global hectare is a useful measure of biocapacity as it can convert things like <a href="/wiki/Human#Diet" title="Human">human dietary requirements</a> into a physical area, which can show how many <a href="/wiki/People" title="People">people</a> a certain region on earth can sustain, assuming current <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technologies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> methods. It can be used as a way of determining the relative carrying capacity of the earth.</p>
<p>A given hectare of land may be measured in equivalent global hectares. For example, a hectare of lush area with high <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> would be scale higher in global hectares than would a hectare of desert.</p>
<p>It can also be used to show that consuming different foods may increase the earth's ability to support larger populations. To illustrate, producing meat generally requires more land and energy than what producing vegetables requires; sustaining a meat-based diet would require a less populated planet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hectare_equivalents">Hectare equivalents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_hectare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hectare equivalents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The average global hectare would occupy the area of a standard hectare. A <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; symbol <b>ha</b>) is a unit of area equal to 10,000 square metres (107,639&#160;sq&#160;ft) (a square 100 metres on each side, or, a square 328.08 feet on each side), 2.471 acre, 0.00386102 square miles, or one square <a href="/wiki/Hectometre" title="Hectometre">hectometre</a> (100 metres squared).</p>
