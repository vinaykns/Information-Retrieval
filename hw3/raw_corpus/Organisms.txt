<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, an <b>organism</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: οργανισμός, <i>organismos</i>) is any contiguous <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>, such as an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaeon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterium</a>. All known types of organisms are capable of some degree of response to <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">growth and development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. An organism consists of one or more <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>; when it has one cell it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular organism</a>; and when it has more than one it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular organism</a>. Most unicellular organisms are of <a href="/wiki/Microscopic_scale" title="Microscopic scale">microscopic scale</a> and are thus loosely described as <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>. Humans are multicellular organisms composed of many trillions of cells grouped into specialized <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>.</p>
<p>An organism may be either a <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a>. Prokaryotes are represented by two separate <a href="/wiki/Three-domain_system" title="Three-domain system">domains</a>—<a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. Eukaryotic organisms are characterized by the presence of a membrane-bound <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a> and contain additional membrane-bound compartments called <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> in animals and plants and <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a> in plants and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, all generally considered to be derived from <a href="/wiki/Symbiogenesis" title="Symbiogenesis">endosymbiotic</a> bacteria).<sup id="cite_ref-cavaliersmith1987_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cavaliersmith1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Fungi, animals and plants are examples of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdoms</a> of organisms within the eukaryotes.</p>
<p>Estimates on the number of Earth's current <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> range from 10 million to 14 million,<sup id="cite_ref-MillerSpoolman2012_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillerSpoolman2012-2">[2]</a></sup> of which only about 1.2 million have been documented.<sup id="cite_ref-PLoS-20110823_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PLoS-20110823-3">[3]</a></sup> More than 99% of all species, amounting to over five billion species,<sup id="cite_ref-Book-Biology_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book-Biology-4">[4]</a></sup> that ever lived are estimated to be <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StearnsStearns2000_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StearnsStearns2000-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20141108-MJN_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20141108-MJN-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2016, a set of 355 <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor" title="Last universal common ancestor">last universal common ancestor</a> (LUCA) of all living organisms living was identified.<sup id="cite_ref-Weissetal_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weissetal-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20160725_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20160725-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "organism" (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> ὀργανισμός, <i>organismos</i>, from ὄργανον, <i>organon</i>, i.e. "instrument, implement, tool, organ of sense or apprehension"<sup id="cite_ref-LSJ_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OnlineEtDict_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtDict-10">[10]</a></sup>) first appeared in the English language in 1703 and took on its current definition by 1834 (<a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a>). It is directly related to the term "organization". There is a long tradition of defining organisms as self-organizing beings, going back at least to <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>'s 1790 <i><a href="/wiki/Critique_of_Judgment" title="Critique of Judgment">Critique of Judgment</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An organism may be defined as an assembly of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> functioning as a more or less stable whole that exhibits the <a href="/wiki/Life#Biology" title="Life">properties of life</a>. Dictionary definitions can be broad, using phrases such as "any living structure, such as a plant, animal, fungus or bacterium, capable of growth and reproduction".<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-12">[12]</a></sup> Many definitions exclude <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> and possible man-made <a href="/wiki/Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry" title="Hypothetical types of biochemistry">non-organic life</a> forms, as viruses are dependent on the biochemical machinery of a host cell for reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-13">[13]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Superorganism" title="Superorganism">superorganism</a> is an organism consisting of many individuals working together as a single functional or <a href="/wiki/Social_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Social unit">social unit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There has been controversy about the best way to define the organism<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and indeed about whether or not such a definition is necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Several contributions<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> are responses to the suggestion that the category of "organism" may well not be adequate in biology.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (November 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-cellular_life">Non-cellular life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-cellular life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-cellular_life" title="Non-cellular life">Non-cellular life</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">Viruses</a> are not typically considered to be organisms because they are incapable of autonomous <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>, growth or <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. This controversy is problematic because some cellular organisms are also incapable of independent survival (but are capable of independent metabolism and procreation) and live as obligatory intracellular parasites. Although viruses have a few <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and molecules characteristic of living organisms, they have no metabolism of their own; they cannot synthesize and organize the organic compounds from which they are formed. Naturally, this rules out autonomous reproduction: they can only be passively replicated by the machinery of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">host cell</a>. In this sense, they are similar to inanimate matter. While viruses sustain no independent metabolism, and thus are usually not classified as organisms, they do have their own <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, and they do <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolve</a> by mechanisms similar to the evolutionary mechanisms of organisms.</p>
<p>The most common argument in support of viruses as living organisms is their ability to undergo evolution and replicate through self-assembly. Some scientists argue that viruses neither evolve, nor self- reproduce. In fact, viruses are evolved by their host cells, meaning that there was co-evolution of viruses and host cells. If host cells did not exist, viral evolution would be impossible. This is not true for cells. If viruses did not exist, the direction of cellular evolution could be different, but cells would nevertheless be able to evolve. As for the reproduction, viruses totally rely on hosts' machinery to replicate.<sup id="cite_ref-10reasons_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10reasons-28">[28]</a></sup> The discovery of viral megagenomes with genes coding for energy metabolism and protein synthesis fueled the debate about whether viruses belong in the <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_life" title="Tree of life">tree of life</a>. The presence of these genes suggested that viruses were once able to metabolize. However, it was found later that the genes coding for energy and protein metabolism have a cellular origin. Most likely, these genes were acquired through <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a> from viral hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-10reasons_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10reasons-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Organisms are complex chemical systems, organized in ways that promote reproduction and some measure of sustainability or survival. The same laws that govern non-living chemistry govern <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">the chemical processes of life</a>. It is generally the phenomena of entire organisms that determine their fitness to an environment and therefore the survivability of their <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>-based genes.</p>
<p>Organisms clearly owe their origin, metabolism, and many other internal functions to chemical phenomena, especially the chemistry of large organic molecules. Organisms are complex systems of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> that, through interaction and environment, play a wide variety of roles.</p>
<p>Organisms are semi-closed chemical systems. Although they are individual units of life (as the definition requires), they are not closed to the environment around them. To operate they constantly take in and release energy. <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">Autotrophs</a> produce usable energy (in the form of organic compounds) using light from the sun or inorganic compounds while <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a> take in organic compounds from the environment.</p>
<p>The primary <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> in these compounds is <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">chemical properties</a> of this element such as its great affinity for bonding with other small atoms, including other carbon atoms, and its small size making it capable of forming multiple bonds, make it ideal as the basis of organic life. It is able to form small three-atom compounds (such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>), as well as large chains of many thousands of atoms that can store data (<a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>), hold cells together, and transmit information (protein).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macromolecules">Macromolecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Macromolecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Compounds that make up organisms may be divided into <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a> and other, smaller molecules. The four groups of macromolecule are <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>. Nucleic acids (specifically deoxyribonucleic acid, or DNA) store genetic data as a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>. The particular sequence of the four different types of nucleotides (<a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>) dictate many characteristics that constitute the organism. The sequence is divided up into <a href="/wiki/Codon" class="mw-redirect" title="Codon">codons</a>, each of which is a particular sequence of three nucleotides and corresponds to a particular <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a>. Thus a sequence of DNA codes for a particular protein that, due to the chemical properties of the amino acids it is made from, <a href="/wiki/Protein_folding" title="Protein folding">folds</a> in a particular manner and so performs a particular function.</p>
<p>These protein functions have been recognized:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzymes</a>, which catalyze all of the reactions of metabolism</li>
<li>Structural proteins, such as <a href="/wiki/Tubulin" title="Tubulin">tubulin</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a></li>
<li>Regulatory proteins, such as <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">transcription factors</a> or cyclins that regulate the cell cycle</li>
<li>Signaling molecules or their receptors such as some <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a> and their receptors</li>
<li>Defensive proteins, which can include everything from <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibodies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, to toxins (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Dendrotoxin" title="Dendrotoxin">dendrotoxins</a> of snakes), to proteins that include unusual amino acids like <a href="/wiki/Canavanine" title="Canavanine">canavanine</a></li>
</ol>
<p>A bilayer of <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> makes up the <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid_membrane" class="mw-redirect" title="Phospholipid membrane">membrane</a> of cells that constitutes a barrier, containing everything within the cell and preventing compounds from freely passing into, and out of, the cell. Due to the selective permeability of the phospholipid membrane only specific compounds can pass through it. In some multicellular organisms they serve as a storage of energy and mediate communication between cells. Carbohydrates are more easily broken down than lipids and yield more energy to compare to lipids and proteins. In fact, carbohydrates are the number one source of energy for all living organisms.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All organisms consist of monomeric units called <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>; some contain a single cell (unicellular) and others contain many units (multicellular). Multicellular organisms are able to specialize cells to perform specific functions. A group of such cells is a <a href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological tissue">tissue</a>, and in animals these occur as four basic types, namely <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nervous_tissue" title="Nervous tissue">nervous tissue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle_tissue" title="Muscle tissue">muscle tissue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>. Several types of tissue work together in the form of an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> to produce a particular function (such as the pumping of the blood by the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, or as a barrier to the environment as the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>). This pattern continues to a higher level with several organs functioning as an <a href="/wiki/Organ_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Organ system">organ system</a> to allow for <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_system" title="Reproductive system">reproduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digestive_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Digestive system">digestion</a>, etc. Many multicellular organisms consist of several organ systems, which coordinate to allow for life.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell">Cell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">cell theory</a>, first developed in 1839 by <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Jakob_Schleiden" title="Matthias Jakob Schleiden">Schleiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Schwann</a>, states that all organisms are composed of one or more cells; all cells come from preexisting cells; and cells contain the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">hereditary information</a> necessary for regulating cell functions and for transmitting information to the next generation of cells.</p>
<p>There are two types of cells, eukaryotic and prokaryotic. Prokaryotic cells are usually singletons, while eukaryotic cells are usually found in multicellular organisms. Prokaryotic cells lack a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_membrane" title="Nuclear membrane">nuclear membrane</a> so <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is unbound within the cell; eukaryotic cells have nuclear membranes.</p>
<p>All cells, whether prokaryotic or eukaryotic, have a <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">membrane</a>, which envelops the cell, separates its interior from its environment, regulates what moves in and out, and maintains the <a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">electric potential of the cell</a>. Inside the membrane, a <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salty</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> takes up most of the cell volume. All cells possess DNA, the hereditary material of <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, containing the information necessary to <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">build</a> various <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, the cell's primary machinery. There are also other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a> in cells.</p>
<p>All cells share several similar characteristics of:<sup id="cite_ref-AlbertsCh1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlbertsCh1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Reproduction by <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a> (<a href="/wiki/Binary_fission" class="mw-redirect" title="Binary fission">binary fission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>).</li>
<li>Use of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and other proteins <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">coded</a> by DNA genes and made via <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">messenger RNA</a> intermediates and <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosomes</a>.</li>
<li>Metabolism, including taking in raw materials, building cell components, converting energy, <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and releasing <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a>. The functioning of a cell depends upon its ability to extract and use chemical energy stored in organic molecules. This energy is derived from <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a>.</li>
<li>Response to external and internal <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">stimuli</a> such as changes in temperature, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> or nutrient levels.</li>
<li>Cell contents are contained within a <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell surface membrane</a> that contains proteins and a <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">Common descent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">Origin of life</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_universal_common_ancestor">Last universal common ancestor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Last universal common ancestor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stromatolites.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/220px-Stromatolites.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/330px-Stromatolites.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/440px-Stromatolites.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="371" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">stromatolites</a> in the Siyeh Formation, <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a>. In 2002, a paper in the scientific journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> suggested that these 3.5 <a href="/wiki/Billion_years" title="Billion years">Gya</a> (billion years old) <a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">geological formations</a> contain fossilized <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> microbes. This suggests they are evidence of one of the earliest known life forms on Earth.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor" title="Last universal common ancestor">Last universal common ancestor</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_evolutionary_history_of_life" title="Timeline of the evolutionary history of life">Timeline of the evolutionary history of life</a></div>
<p>The last universal common ancestor (LUCA) is the most recent organism from which all organisms now living on Earth <a href="/wiki/Common_descent" title="Common descent">descend</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-theobald_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theobald-30">[30]</a></sup> Thus it is the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> of all current life on Earth. The LUCA is estimated to have lived some <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_evolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Timeline of evolution">3.5 to 3.8 billion years ago</a> (sometime in the <a href="/wiki/Paleoarchean" title="Paleoarchean">Paleoarchean</a> <a href="/wiki/Era" title="Era">era</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The earliest evidences for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> are <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> found to be <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic</a> in 3.7 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary rocks</a> discovered in <a href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a><sup id="cite_ref-NG-20131208_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20131208-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> <a href="/wiki/Fossils" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossils">fossils</a> found in 3.48 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> discovered in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AP-20131113_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20131113-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AST-20131108_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AST-20131108-35">[35]</a></sup> Although more than 99 percent of all species that ever lived on the planet are estimated to be extinct,<sup id="cite_ref-StearnsStearns2000_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StearnsStearns2000-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20141108-MJN_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20141108-MJN-6">[6]</a></sup> there are currently 10–14 million species of life on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-MillerSpoolman2012_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillerSpoolman2012-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Information about the early development of life includes input from many different fields, including <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>. These sciences provide information about the history of the Earth and the changes produced by life. However, a great deal of information about the early Earth has been destroyed by geological processes over the course of time.</p>
<p>All organisms are descended from a common ancestor or ancestral gene pool. Evidence for common descent may be found in traits shared between all living organisms. In Darwin's day, the evidence of shared traits was based solely on visible observation of morphologic similarities, such as the fact that all birds have wings, even those that do not fly.</p>
<p>There is strong evidence from genetics that all organisms have a common ancestor. For example, every living cell makes use of <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> as its genetic material, and uses the same twenty <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> as the building blocks for <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. All organisms use the same <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> (with some extremely rare and minor deviations) to <a href="/wiki/Translation_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Translation (genetics)">translate</a> nucleic acid sequences into proteins. The universality of these traits strongly suggests common ancestry, because the selection of many of these traits seems arbitrary. <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</a> makes it more difficult to study the last universal ancestor.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> However, the universal use of the same genetic code, same nucleotides, and same amino acids makes the existence of such an ancestor overwhelmingly likely.<sup id="cite_ref-theo_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theo-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">Reproduction</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">Sexual reproduction</a> is widespread among current eukaryotes, and was likely present in the last common ancestor.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This is suggested by the finding of a core set of genes for meiosis in the descendants of lineages that diverged early form the eukaryotic evolutionary tree.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and Malik et al.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> It is further supported by evidence that eukaryotes previously regarded as "ancient asexuals", such as <i><a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">Amoeba</a></i>, were likely sexual in the past, and that most present day asexual amoeboid lineages likely arose recently and independently.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In prokaryotes, <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)#Natural_transformation" title="Transformation (genetics)">natural bacterial transformation</a> involves the transfer of DNA from one bacterium to another and integration of the donor DNA into the recipient chromosome by recombination. Natural bacterial transformation is considered to be a primitive sexual process and occurs in both bacteria and archaea, although it has been studied mainly in bacteria. Transformation is clearly a bacterial adaptation and not an accidental occurrence, because it depends on numerous gene products that specifically interact with each other to enter a state of <a href="/wiki/Natural_competence" title="Natural competence">natural competence</a> to perform this complex process.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Transformation is a common mode of DNA transfer among prokaryotes.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizontal_gene_transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Horizontal gene transfer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">Horizontal gene transfer</a></div>
<p>The ancestry of living organisms has traditionally been reconstructed from morphology, but is increasingly supplemented with phylogenetics—the reconstruction of phylogenies by the comparison of genetic (DNA) sequence.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Sequence comparisons suggest recent <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal transfer</a> of many genes among diverse <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> including across the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> "domains". Thus determining the phylogenetic history of a species can not be done conclusively by determining evolutionary trees for single genes.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Biologist Gogarten suggests "the original metaphor of a tree no longer fits the data from recent genome research", therefore "biologists (should) use the metaphor of a mosaic to describe the different histories combined in individual genomes and use (the) metaphor of a net to visualize the rich exchange and cooperative effects of HGT among microbes."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_life_.28cloning_and_synthetic_organisms.29">Future of life (cloning and synthetic organisms)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Future of life (cloning and synthetic organisms)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern biotechnology is challenging traditional concepts of organism and species. <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">Cloning</a> is the process of creating a new multicellular organism, genetically identical to another, with the potential of creating entirely new species of organisms. Cloning is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_cloning" title="Ethics of cloning">much ethical debate</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/J._Craig_Venter_Institute" title="J. Craig Venter Institute">J. Craig Venter Institute</a> assembled a synthetic <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_genome_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Bacterial genome size">bacterial genome</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_genitalium" title="Mycoplasma genitalium">Mycoplasma genitalium</a></i>, by using recombination in yeast of 25 overlapping DNA fragments in a single step. <i>The use of yeast recombination greatly simplifies the assembly of large DNA molecules from both synthetic and natural fragments.</i><sup id="cite_ref-Venter_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Venter-46">[46]</a></sup> Other companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Genomics" title="Synthetic Genomics">Synthetic Genomics</a>, have already been formed to take advantage of the many commercial uses of custom designed genomes.</p>
