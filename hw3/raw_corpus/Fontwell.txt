<p><b>Walberton</b> is a village and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arun" title="Arun">Arun</a> District of <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>, England centred five miles (8&#160;km) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Littlehampton" title="Littlehampton">Littlehampton</a>, and south of the <a href="/wiki/A27_road" title="A27 road">A27 road</a>. The land rises from 10 metres above sea level to 35 metres, a quarter of the height of the nearest foothill, Nore Hill, of the <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">South Downs</a> which is outside of the parish to the north. The civil parish, administering local community buildings, fields and paths includes the smaller village of Binsted to the east and larger neighbourhood of Fontwell, less than a kilometre to the north-west. Walberton has a medieval church by its <a href="/wiki/Nucleated_village" title="Nucleated village">clustered village centre</a>; Binsted's medieval church retains its original landscape setting, with village houses dispersed over the farm fields.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walberton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Church,_Walberton" title="St Mary's Church, Walberton">St Mary's Church</a> in Walberton has a <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Baptismal_font" title="Baptismal font">font</a> and west wall. The <i>The Holly Tree</i> is recorded as a pub from 1845 and received its present name in 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Towards the end of the village there is a small duck pond. There are a few shops and a post office next to the modern village hall.</p>
<p>Binsted has a pub, the <i>Black Horse</i> and a Norman church built in AD 1140 by the monks of Tortington Priory to the immediate east.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Roman and medieval pottery and tile kilns have been excavated there.<sup id="cite_ref-bh_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bh-4">[4]</a></sup> Binsted also has a traditional summer festival, Strawberry Fair, where locally grown produce is sold for charity. Part of Binsted is within the <a href="/wiki/South_Downs_National_Park" title="South Downs National Park">South Downs National Park</a>. The whole of Binsted's countryside was originally assessed by the Countryside Agency as meeting the criteria for inclusion.</p>
<p>In 2015, the community and wildlife of Binsted were being threatened by "Option B" proposals for an Arundel bypass.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walberton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Wards_and_electoral_divisions_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Wards and electoral divisions of the United Kingdom">electoral ward</a> with the name Walberton exists. This stretches north-east to <a href="/wiki/Madehurst" title="Madehurst">Madehurst</a>, with a total population taken at the 2011 census of 2,889.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walberton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Anson,_3rd_Baronet" title="Sir William Anson, 3rd Baronet">Sir William Anson</a> (1843–1914), jurist, academic and politician, was born in Walberton.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Marquis,_1st_Earl_of_Woolton" title="Frederick Marquis, 1st Earl of Woolton">Frederick Marquis, 1st Earl of Woolton</a> (1883–1964), businessman and government minister, is buried in Walberton churchyard.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Sutcliff" title="Rosemary Sutcliff">Rosemary Sutcliff</a> (1920–1992), children's writer, spent the latter part of her life in Walberton.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Hilton Avisford Park Hotel,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with its golf course between Walberton and Binsted, was formerly Avisford Roman Catholic Preparatory School for Boys (1928–73),<sup id="cite_ref-bh_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bh-4">[4]</a></sup> where BBC journalist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stourton_(journalist)" title="Edward Stourton (journalist)">Edward Stourton</a> (born 1957) was head boy and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nairac" title="Robert Nairac">Robert Nairac</a> (1948–1977), an army officer abducted and murdered in Northern Ireland, spent a year teaching before university in the late 1960s.</li>
</ul>
