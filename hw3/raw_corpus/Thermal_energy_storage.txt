			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Thermal energy storage</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p><b>Thermal energy storage (TES)</b> is achieved with greatly differing technologies that collectively accommodate a wide range of needs. It allows excess <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> to be collected for later use, hours, days or many months later, at individual building, multiuser building, district, town, or even regional scale depending on the specific technology. As examples: energy demand can be balanced between daytime and nighttime; summer heat from <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar collectors</a> can be <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">stored interseasonally</a> for use in winter; and cold obtained from winter air can be provided for summer air conditioning. Storage media include: water or ice-slush tanks ranging from small to massive, masses of native earth or bedrock accessed with heat exchangers in clusters of small-diameter boreholes (sometimes quite deep); deep aquifers contained between impermeable strata; shallow, lined pits filled with gravel and water and top-insulated; and <a href="/wiki/Eutectic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase-change_material" title="Phase-change material">phase-change materials</a>.</p>
<p>Other sources of thermal energy for storage include heat or cold produced with <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pumps</a> from off-peak, lower cost electric power, a practice called <a href="/wiki/Peak_shaving" class="mw-redirect" title="Peak shaving">peak shaving</a>; heat from combined heat and power (CHP) power plants; heat produced by renewable electrical energy that exceeds grid demand and <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> from industrial processes. Heat storage, both seasonal and short term, is considered an important means for cheaply balancing high shares of variable <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable electricity</a> production and integration of electricity and heating sectors in energy systems almost or <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">completely fed</a> by renewable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy_storage">Solar energy storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solar energy storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water_storage_tank" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar hot water storage tank">Solar hot water storage tank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">Seasonal thermal energy storage</a></div>
<p>Most practical active solar heating systems provide storage from a few hours to a day's worth of energy collected. However, there are a growing number of facilities that use <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">seasonal thermal energy storage</a> (STES), enabling solar energy to be stored in summer for space heating use during winter.<sup id="cite_ref-drake_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drake-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">The Drake Landing Solar Community</a> in Alberta, Canada, has now achieved a year-round 97% solar heating fraction, a world record made possible only by incorporating STES.<sup id="cite_ref-drake_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drake-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of both <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> are also possible with high temperature solar thermal input. Various eutectic mixtures of metals, such as Aluminium and Silicon (AlSi12) offer a high melting point suited to efficient steam generation,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> while high alumina cement-based materials offer good thermal storage capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">Molten salt</a> is a means of storing heat at a high temperature. This is a current commercial technology used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> for later use in electricity generation, to allow solar power to provide electricity on a more continuous basis. These molten salts (<a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_nitrate" title="Calcium nitrate">calcium nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">sodium nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium_nitrate" title="Lithium nitrate">lithium nitrate</a>, etc.) have the property to absorb and store the heat energy that is released to the water, to transfer energy when needed. To improve the salt properties it must be mixed in a <a href="/wiki/Eutectic_system" title="Eutectic system">eutectic mixture</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molten_salt_technology">Molten salt technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Molten salt technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molten salt can be employed as a thermal energy storage method to retain thermal energy collected by a <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar tower</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solar_trough" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar trough">solar trough</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentrated solar power plant">concentrated solar power plant</a>, so that it can be used to generate electricity in bad weather or at night. It was demonstrated in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_Two" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar Two">Solar Two</a> project from 1995-1999. The system is predicted to have an annual efficiency of 99%, a reference to the energy retained by storing heat before turning it into electricity, versus converting heat directly into electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The molten salt mixtures vary. The most extended mixture contains <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">sodium nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_nitrate" title="Calcium nitrate">calcium nitrate</a>. It is non-flammable and nontoxic, and has already been used in the chemical and metals industries as a heat-transport fluid, so experience with such systems exists in non-solar applications.</p>
<p>The salt melts at 131&#160;°C (268&#160;°F). It is kept liquid at 288&#160;°C (550&#160;°F) in an insulated "cold" storage tank. The liquid salt is pumped through panels in a solar collector where the focused sun heats it to 566&#160;°C (1,051&#160;°F). It is then sent to a hot storage tank. This is so well insulated that the thermal energy can be usefully stored for up to a week.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>When electricity is needed, the hot salt is pumped to a conventional steam-generator to produce <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated steam</a> for a turbine/generator as used in any conventional coal, oil, or nuclear power plant. A 100-megawatt turbine would need a tank of about 9.1 metres (30&#160;ft) tall and 24 metres (79&#160;ft) in diameter to drive it for four hours by this design.</p>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic trough</a> power plants in Spain<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_tower" title="Solar power tower">solar power tower</a> developer <a href="/wiki/SolarReserve" title="SolarReserve">SolarReserve</a> use this thermal energy storage concept. The <a href="/wiki/Solana_Generating_Station" title="Solana Generating Station">Solana Generating Station</a> in the U.S. has 6 hours of storage by molten salt. During the summer of 2013 the <a href="/wiki/Gemasolar_Thermosolar_Plant" title="Gemasolar Thermosolar Plant">Gemasolar Thermosolar</a> solar power tower/molten salt plant in Spain achieved a first by continuously producing electricity 24 hours per day for 36 days.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_storage_in_tanks_or_rock_caverns">Heat storage in tanks or rock caverns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heat storage in tanks or rock caverns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Steam_accumulator" title="Steam accumulator">Steam accumulator</a></div>
<p>A steam accumulator consists of an insulated steel pressure tank containing hot water and steam under pressure. As a heat storage device, it is used to mediate heat production by a variable or steady source from a variable demand for heat. Steam accumulators may take on a significance for energy storage in solar thermal energy projects.</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hot_water_storage_tank" title="Hot water storage tank">Hot water storage tank</a></div>
<p>Large stores are widely used in Scandinavia to store heat for several days, to decouple heat and power production and to help meet peak demands. Interseasonal storage in caverns has been investigated and appears to be economical.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_storage_in_hot_rocks.2C_concrete.2C_pebbles_etc">Heat storage in hot rocks, concrete, pebbles etc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heat storage in hot rocks, concrete, pebbles etc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water has one of the highest thermal capacities <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">Heat capacity</a> - 4.2 J/(cm³·K) whereas concrete has about one third of that. On the other hand, concrete can be heated to much higher temperatures – 1200&#160;°C by e.g. electrical heating and therefore has a much higher overall volumetric capacity. Thus in the example below, an insulated cube of about 2.8 m would appear to provide sufficient storage for a single house to meet 50% of heating demand. This could, in principle, be used to store surplus wind or PV heat due to the ability of electrical heating to reach high temperatures. At the neighborhood level, the Wiggenhausen-Süd solar development at Friedrichshafen has received international attention. This features a 12,000 m³ (420,000 cu ft) reinforced concrete thermal store linked to 4,300 m² (46,000 sq ft) of solar collectors, which will supply the 570 houses with around 50% of their heating and hot water. Siemens builds a 36 MWh thermal storage near Hamburg with 600&#160;°C basalt and 1.5 MW electric output.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A similar system is scheduled for <a href="/wiki/Sor%C3%B8" title="Sorø">Sorø</a>, Denmark, with 41-58% of the stored 18 MWh heat returned for the town's <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>, and 30-41% returned as electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscibility_Gap_Alloy_technology_.28MGA.29">Miscibility Gap Alloy technology (MGA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Miscibility Gap Alloy technology (MGA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Miscibility gap alloys rely on the phase change of a metallic material (see: <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>) to store thermal energy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rather than pumping the liquid metal between tanks as in a molten salt system, the metal is encapsulated in another metallic material that it cannot alloy with (<a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">immiscible</a>). Depending on the two materials selected (the phase changing material and the encapsulating material) storage densities can be between 0.2 and 2 MJ/L.</p>
<p>A working fluid, typically water or steam, is used to transfer the heat into and out of the MGA. Thermal conductivity of MGAs is often higher (up to 400 W/m K) than competing technologies<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> which means quicker "charge" and "discharge" of the thermal storage is possible. The technology has not yet been implemented on a large scale.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_thermal_storage_heaters">Electric thermal storage heaters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Electric thermal storage heaters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Storage_heater" title="Storage heater">Storage heater</a></div>
<p>Storage heaters are commonplace in European homes with time of use metering. They consist of high-density ceramic bricks or <a href="/wiki/Feolite" title="Feolite">feolite</a> blocks heated to a high temperature with electricity, and may or may not have good insulation and controls to release heat over a number of hours.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice-based_technology">Ice-based technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ice-based technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ice_storage_air_conditioning" title="Ice storage air conditioning">Ice storage air conditioning</a></div>
<p>Air conditioning can be provided more economically by using cheaper electricity at night to freeze water into ice, then using the cool of the ice in the afternoon to reduce the electricity needed to handle air conditioning demands. Thermal energy storage using ice makes use of the large <a href="/wiki/Heat_of_fusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat of fusion">heat of fusion</a> of water. One <a href="/wiki/Metric_ton" class="mw-redirect" title="Metric ton">metric ton</a> of water, one cubic meter, can store 334 million <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> (MJ) or 317,000 <a href="/wiki/BTU" class="mw-redirect" title="BTU">BTUs</a> (93kWh or 26.4 ton-hours). In fact, ice was originally transported from mountains to cities for use as a coolant, and the original definition of a "ton" of <a href="/wiki/Cooling_capacity" title="Cooling capacity">cooling capacity</a> (heat flow) was the heat to melt one ton of ice every 24 hours. This is the heat flow one would expect in a 3,000-square-foot (280&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) house in Boston in the summer. This definition has since been replaced by less archaic units: one ton <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> capacity = 12,000 BTU/hour (~3.5&#160;kW). Either way, an agreeably small storage facility can hold enough ice to cool a large building for a day or a week, whether that ice is produced by <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous_ammonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhydrous ammonia">anhydrous ammonia</a> chillers or hauled in by horse-drawn carts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As such there are developing and developed applications where ice is produced during off-peak periods and used for cooling at later time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In addition to using ice in cooling applications it is also being used in heat pump based heating systems. In these applications the phase change energy provides a very significant layer of thermal capacity that is near the bottom range of temperature that water source heat pumps can operate in. This allows the system to ride out the heaviest heating load conditions and extends the timeframe by which the source energy elements can contribute heat back into the system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryogenic_energy_storage">Cryogenic energy storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cryogenic energy storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_energy_storage" title="Cryogenic energy storage">Cryogenic energy storage</a></div>
<p>This uses liquification of air or nitrogen as an energy store.</p>
<p>A pilot cryogenic energy system that uses liquid air as the energy store, and low-grade waste heat to drive the thermal re-expansion of the air, has been operating at a power station in Slough, UK since 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_silicon_technology">Hot silicon technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hot silicon technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid or molten <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> offers much higher storage temperatures than salts with consequent greater capacity and efficiency. It is being researched as a possible more energy efficient storage technology. Silicon is able to store more than 1MWh of energy per cubic metre at 1400&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pumped-heat_electricity_storage">Pumped-heat electricity storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pumped-heat electricity storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In pumped-heat electricity storage (PHES), a reversible heat-pump system is used to store energy as a temperature difference between two heat stores.<sup id="cite_ref-econ1406_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ1406-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IMEchE_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMEchE-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ruer_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruer-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isentropic">Isentropic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Isentropic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One system which was being developed by the now bankrupt UK company Isentropic operates as follows.<sup id="cite_ref-Isentropic_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Isentropic-29">[29]</a></sup> It comprises two insulated containers filled with crushed rock or gravel; a hot vessel storing thermal energy at high temperature and high pressure, and a cold vessel storing thermal energy at low temperature and low pressure. The vessels are connected at top and bottom by pipes and the whole system is filled with the inert gas <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>.</p>
<p>During the charging cycle the system uses off-peak electricity to work as a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump_and_refrigeration_cycle" title="Heat pump and refrigeration cycle">heat pump</a>. Argon at ambient temperature and pressure from the top of the cold store is compressed <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatically</a> to a pressure of 12 bar, heating it to around 500&#160;°C (900&#160;°F). The compressed gas is transferred to the top of the hot vessel where it percolates down through the gravel, transferring its heat to the rock and cooling to ambient temperature. The cooled, but still pressurized, gas emerging at the bottom of the vessel is then expanded (again adiabatically) back down to 1 bar, which lowers its temperature to -150&#160;°C. The cold gas is then passed up through the cold vessel where it cools the rock while being warmed back to its initial condition.</p>
<p>The energy is recovered as electricity by reversing the cycle. The hot gas from the hot vessel is expanded to drive a generator and then supplied to the cold store. The cooled gas retrieved from the bottom of the cold store is compressed which heats the gas to ambient temperature. The gas is then transferred to the bottom of the hot vessel to be reheated.</p>
<p>The compression and expansion processes are provided by a specially designed <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_compressor" title="Reciprocating compressor">reciprocating machine</a> using sliding valves. Surplus heat generated by inefficiencies in the process is shed to the environment through heat exchangers during the discharging cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-econ1406_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ1406-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Isentropic_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Isentropic-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The developer claims that a round trip efficiency of 72-80% is achievable.<sup id="cite_ref-econ1406_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ1406-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Isentropic_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Isentropic-29">[29]</a></sup> This compares to &gt;80% achievable with pumped hydro energy storage.<sup id="cite_ref-IMEchE_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMEchE-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another proposed system uses <a href="/wiki/Turbomachinery" title="Turbomachinery">turbomachinery</a> and is capable of operating at much higher power levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruer_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruer-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Endothermic.2Fexothermic_chemical_reaction">Endothermic/exothermic chemical reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Endothermic/exothermic chemical reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The system works by storing heat in a container containing 50 percent <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (NaOH) solution. Heat is stored by heating the solution, using a solar collector on a rooftop or any other hear source, and evaporating the water in an endothermic reaction. When water is added again heat is released in an exothermic reaction at 50&#160;°C (120&#160;°F). Current systems operate at 60% efficiency. Energy stored in such a matter can last from a few months to years. The solution can also be transported to other location.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013 the Dutch technology developer TNO launched the results of a project to store heat in a salt container. The heat, which can be derived from a solar collector on a rooftop, expels the water contained in the salt. When the water is added again, the heat is released, with almost no energy losses. A container with a few cubic meters of salt could store enough of this thermochemical energy to heat a house throughout the winter, in a temperate climate like that of the Netherlands. Currently researchers in several countries are conducting experiments to determine the best type of salt, or salt mixture. Low pressure within the container seems favourable for the energy transport.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Storing energy in molecular bonds is being investigated. Energy densities equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_batteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithium-ion batteries">lithium-ion batteries</a> have been achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
