<p>A <b>developing country</b>, also called a <b>less developed country</b> or an <b>underdeveloped country</b>, is a nation or a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> with a less developed <a href="/wiki/Industrial_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial base">industrial base</a> and a low <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI) relative to other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There are no universally agreed-upon criteria for what makes a country developing versus developed and which countries fit these two categories,<sup id="cite_ref-UN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN-2">[2]</a></sup> although there are general reference points such as a nation's <a href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP per capita">GDP per capita</a> compared to other nations. Also, the general term <i>less-developed country</i> should not be confused with the specific <a href="/wiki/Least_developed_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Least developed country">least developed country</a>. The term "developing" describes a currently observed situation and not a dynamic or expected direction of progress. Since the late 1990s developing countries tended to demonstrate higher growth rates than the developed ones.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is criticism of the use of the term <i>developing country</i>. The term implies inferiority of a <i>developing country</i> or <i>undeveloped country</i> compared to a <i><a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed country</a></i>, which many countries dislike. It assumes a desire to develop along the traditional Western model of <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> which a few countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, choose not to follow.<sup id="cite_ref-Karma_Ura_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karma_Ura-4">[4]</a></sup> An alternative measurement that has been suggested is that of <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_happiness" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross national happiness">gross national happiness</a>. Countries on the boundary between developed and developing are often categorized under the term <i><a href="/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country" title="Newly industrialized country">newly industrialized countries</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Globalization_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Globalization-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Limits_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Limits-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AIA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Principles_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Principles-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to authors such as <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman_Rostow" title="Walt Whitman Rostow">Walt Whitman Rostow</a>, developing countries are in transition from traditional lifestyles towards the modern lifestyle which began in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in the 18th and 19th centuries.</p>
<p>In the 2016 edition of its <a href="/wiki/World_Development_Indicators" title="World Development Indicators">World Development Indicators</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> made a decision to no longer distinguish between “developed” and “developing” countries in the presentation of its data. Nobody has ever agreed on a definition for these terms in the first place.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<div class="legend"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #858585; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Data unavailable</div>

<p>Various terms are used for whatever is not a <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed country</a>. Terms used include less developed country or less economically developed country, and for the more extreme, <a href="/wiki/Least_developed_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Least developed country">least developed country</a> or least economically developed country.</p>
<p>Criteria for what is not a developed country can be obtained by inverting the factors that define a developed country:</p>
<ul>
<li>people have lower <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a></li>
<li>people have less education and <a href="/wiki/Literacy_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Literacy rate">literacy rate</a></li>
<li>people have less money (income)</li>
<li>women have higher <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a> rate and <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, former Secretary General of the United Nations, defined a developed country as "one that allows all its citizens to enjoy a free and healthy life in a safe environment."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> But according to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Statistics_Division" title="United Nations Statistics Division">United Nations Statistics Division</a>,</p>
<dl>
<dd>There is no established convention for the designation of "developed" and "developing" countries or areas in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-UN_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>The designations "developed" and "developing" are intended for statistical convenience and do not necessarily express a judgment about the stage reached by a particular country or area in the development process.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The UN also notes,</p>
<dl>
<dd>In common practice, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in Asia, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in northern America, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> in Oceania and <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a> are considered "developed" regions or areas. In international trade statistics, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_African_Customs_Union" title="Southern African Customs Union">Southern African Customs Union</a> is also treated as a developed region and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> as a developed country; countries emerging from the <a href="/wiki/Former_Yugoslavia" class="mw-redirect" title="Former Yugoslavia">former Yugoslavia</a> are treated as developing countries; and countries of <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">Commonwealth of Independent States</a> (code 172) in Europe are not included under either developed or developing regions.<sup id="cite_ref-UN_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>On the other hand, according to the classification from <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> (IMF) before April 2004, all countries of <a href="/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe" title="Central and Eastern Europe">Central and Eastern Europe</a> (including Central European countries that still belongs to the "Eastern Europe Group" in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> institutions) as well as the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (USSR) countries in Central Asia (<a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, were not included under either developed or developing regions, but rather were referred to as "countries in transition"; however they are now widely regarded (in the international reports) as "developing countries".</p>
<p>The IMF uses a flexible classification system that considers "(1) per capita income level, (2) export diversification—so oil exporters that have high per capita GDP would not make the advanced classification because around 70% of its exports are oil, and (3) degree of integration into the global financial system."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The World Bank classifies countries into four income groups. These are set each year on July 1. Economies were divided according to 2016 GNI per capita using the following ranges of income:<sup id="cite_ref-WB_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WB-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Low income countries had GNI per capita of US$1,025 or less.</li>
<li>Lower middle income countries had GNI per capita between US$1,026 and US$4,035.</li>
<li>Upper middle income countries had GNI per capita between US$4,036 and US$12,475.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/High_income_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="High income countries">High income countries</a> had GNI per capita above US$12,476.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since 2016 the World Bank no longer divide countries into two groups according to the out-dated concept of developed and developing <sup id="cite_ref-WB_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WB-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with the current level of development, countries may be classified by how much this has changed over some amount of time.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This may be by absolute numbers or country ranking.</p>
<ul>
<li>countries that were more less-developed, and are less less-developed (also developing country)</li>
<li>countries that were less-developed, and are about the same (developing country)</li>
<li>countries that were less less-developed, and are more less-developed (developing country)</li>
</ul>

<p>The development of a country is measured with statistical indexes such as income <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a> (per person) (<a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>), <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>, the rate of literacy. The UN has developed the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI), a compound indicator of the above statistics, to gauge the level of human development for countries where data is available. The UN sets Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) from a blueprint developed by all of the world's countries and leading development institutions, in order to evaluate growth.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Developing countries are, in general, countries that have not achieved a significant degree of industrialization relative to their populations, and have, in most cases, a medium to low <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a>. There is a strong association between low income and high population growth.</p>
<p>The terms utilized when discussing developing countries refer to the intent and to the constructs of those who utilize these terms. Other terms sometimes used are less developed countries (LDCs), least economically developed countries (LEDCs), "underdeveloped nations" or <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> nations, and "non-industrialized nations". Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Developed_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Most_economically_developed_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Most economically developed countries">most economically developed countries</a> (MEDCs), <a href="/wiki/First_World" title="First World">First World</a> nations and "industrialized nations" are the opposite end of the spectrum.</p>
<p>To moderate the <a href="/wiki/Euphemistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphemistic">euphemistic</a> aspect of the word <i>developing</i>, <a href="/wiki/International_organization" title="International organization">international organizations</a> have started to use the term <a href="/wiki/Least_developed_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Least developed country">less economically developed country</a> (LEDCs) for the poorest nations—which can, in no sense, be regarded as developing. That is, LEDCs are the poorest <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subset</a> of LDCs. This may moderate against a belief that the standard of living across the entire developing world is the same.</p>
<p>The concept of the developing nation is found, under one term or another, in numerous theoretical systems having diverse orientations — for example, theories of <a href="/wiki/Decolonization" title="Decolonization">decolonization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">liberation theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-imperialism" title="Anti-imperialism">anti-imperialism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a>.</p>
<p>Another important indicator is the sectoral changes that have occurred since the stage of development of the country. On an average, countries with a 50% contribution from the Secondary sector of <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</a> have grown substantially. Similarly countries with a tertiary Sector stronghold also see greater rate of <a href="/wiki/Economic_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic Development">Economic Development</a>.</p>
<p>Some researchers in development economics, such as <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Schultz" title="Theodore Schultz">Theodore Schultz</a> who won a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in 1979, have found that literate farmers in developing countries are more productive than illiterate farmers. They therefore recommend investing in <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a> (education, health, etc.) as an effective tool for economic development. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Tamim" title="Mohammed Tamim">Mohammed Tamim</a>, believe that economic development is measurable in educational level from primary school to the university. They noticed that wherever the educational level is raised, the level of development is also raised. They conclude that the percentage of the schooled population is proportional to the economic growth rate and inversely proportional in the demographic growth rate. The <a href="/wiki/Rostow%27s_stages_of_growth" title="Rostow's stages of growth">Take-Off</a> of <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman_Rostow" title="Walt Whitman Rostow">Walt Whitman Rostow</a> can start in a country if its population is completely schooled. It is therefore necessary for the organization of a worldwide education program, itself conditioned by another worldwide program of birth control and the establishment of a worldwide organization for the implementation of this development strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>There is some criticism of the use of the term "developing country". The term implies inferiority of a "developing country" or "undeveloped country" compared to a "developed country", which many countries dislike. It is criticized for being <i>too positive</i> and <i>too negative</i>.</p>
<p>It assumes a desire to "develop" along the traditional Western model of economic development, which a few countries, such as Cuba and Bhutan, choose not to follow.<sup id="cite_ref-Karma_Ura_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karma_Ura-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of "<a href="/wiki/Social_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Social development">development</a>" rests on the assumption that <a href="/wiki/Modernization_theory" title="Modernization theory">Modernization theory</a> holds. Modernization theory, as the dominant <a href="/wiki/Development_theory" title="Development theory">development theory</a> of the late 19th and 20th centuries, has largely contributed to the definition of "development". In short, it argues that there is only one way to achieve "modernity" and "development" - that of "Western" nation-states. Largely challenged today, modernization theory still holds an important role in defining "development".</p>
<p>The term "developing" implies <a href="/wiki/Economic_mobility" title="Economic mobility">mobility</a> and does not acknowledge that development may be in decline or static in some countries, particularly in southern African states worst affected by HIV/AIDS. In such cases, the term "developing country" may be considered a <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemism</a>. The term implies homogeneity between such countries, which vary widely. The term also implies homogeneity within such countries when wealth (and health) of the most and least affluent groups varies widely. Similarly, the term "developed country" incorrectly implies a lack of continuing economic development/growth in more-developed countries.</p>
<p>In general, development entails a modern infrastructure (both physical and institutional), and a move away from low value added sectors such as agriculture and natural resource extraction. Developed countries, in comparison, usually have economic systems based on continuous, self-sustaining economic growth in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">tertiary sector of the economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Quaternary sector of the economy">quaternary sector of the economy</a> and high material standards of living. However, there are notable exceptions, as some countries considered developed have a significant component of <a href="/wiki/Primary_industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary industries">primary industries</a> in their national economies, e.g., Norway, Canada, Australia. The USA and Western Europe have a very important agricultural sector, and are major players in international agricultural markets. Also, natural resource extraction can be a very profitable industry (high value added), e.g., oil extraction.</p>
<p>An alternative measurement that has been suggested is that of <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_happiness" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross national happiness">gross national happiness</a>, measuring the actual satisfaction of people as opposed to how fiscally wealthy a country is.</p>
<p>During the late 20th century, and with the advance of <a href="/wiki/World-systems_theory" title="World-systems theory">World-systems theory</a>, the notions of "developed country" and "developing country" have started to slowly be replaced by the less-controversial, trade-based, notions of "<a href="/wiki/Core_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Core country">core country</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Semi-periphery_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Semi-periphery country">semi-periphery country</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Periphery_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Periphery country">periphery country</a>". The terms of "developing countries" and "developed countries", although obsolete, still continue to be dominant, as part of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Official narrative (page does not exist)">official narrative</a>.</p>
