<p><i><b>Jatropha curcas</b></i> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Euphorbia" title="Euphorbia">spurge</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a>, that is native to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, most likely <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Janick_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janick-3">[3]</a></sup> It is cultivated in tropical and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> regions <a href="/wiki/Pantropical" title="Pantropical">around the world</a>, becoming <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">naturalized</a> in some areas. The <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific name (botany)">specific epithet</a>, <i>"curcas"</i>, was first used by <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> doctor <a href="/wiki/Garcia_de_Orta" title="Garcia de Orta">Garcia de Orta</a> more than 400 years ago and is of uncertain origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Agroforest_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agroforest-4">[4]</a></sup> Common names include <b>Barbados nut</b>, <b>purging nut</b>, <b>physic nut</b>, or <b>JCL</b> (abbreviation of <i>Jatropha curcas</i> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>).</p>
<p><i>J. curcas</i> is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_poisonous_plants" title="List of poisonous plants">poisonous</a>, semi-<a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> or small <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>, reaching a height of 6&#160;m (20&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Janick_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janick-3">[3]</a></sup> It is resistant to a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">aridity</a>, allowing it to be grown in <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> contain 27-40% <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_fats_and_oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetable fats and oils">oil</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (average: 34.4%<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>) that can be processed to produce a high-quality <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> fuel, usable in a standard <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engine</a>. The seeds are also a source of the highly poisonous <a href="/wiki/Toxalbumin" title="Toxalbumin">toxalbumin</a> curcin or jatrophin.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_features">Botanical features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Botanical features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">Leaves</a>: The leaves have significant variability in their morphology. In general, the leaves are green to pale green, alternate to subopposite, and three- to five-lobed with a <a href="/wiki/Phyllotaxis" title="Phyllotaxis">spiral phyllotaxis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nahar_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nahar-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">Flowers</a>: male and female flowers are produced on the same <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a>, averaging 20 male flowers to each female flower,<sup id="cite_ref-Pesquisa_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pesquisa-10">[10]</a></sup> or 10 male flowers to each female flower.<sup id="cite_ref-Jatropha_journal_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jatropha_journal-11">[11]</a></sup> The inflorescence can be formed in the <a href="/wiki/Leaf_axil" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf axil">leaf axil</a>. Plants occasionally present hermaphroditic flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-Nahar_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nahar-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">Fruits</a>&#160;: fruits are produced in winter, or there may be several crops during the year if soil moisture is good and temperatures are sufficiently high. Most fruit production is concentrated from midsummer to late fall with variations in production peaks where some plants have two or three harvests and some produce continuously through the season.<sup id="cite_ref-Nahar_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nahar-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">Seeds</a>: the seeds are mature when the capsule changes from green to yellow. The seeds contain around 20% saturated fatty acids and 80% unsaturated fatty acids, and they yield 25%–40% oil by weight. In addition, the seeds contain other chemical compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Saccharose" class="mw-redirect" title="Saccharose">saccharose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raffinose" title="Raffinose">raffinose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stachyose" title="Stachyose">stachyose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>. The oil is largely made up of oleic and <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic</a> acids. Furthermore, the plant also contains curcasin, arachidic, <a href="/wiki/Myristic_acid" title="Myristic acid">myristic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">stearic</a> acids and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Curcin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Curcin (page does not exist)">curcin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nahar_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nahar-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">Genome</a>: the whole genome was sequenced by <i>Kazusa DNA Research Institute</i>, Chiba Japan in October 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg/250px-J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg" width="250" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg/375px-J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg/500px-J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="700" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J_curcas_seed_ies.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Jatropha curcas</i> seeds</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cultivation is uncomplicated. <i>Jatropha curcas</i> grows in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> regions.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The plant can grow in <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wasteland" class="extiw" title="wikt:wasteland">wastelands</a> and grows on almost any terrain, even on <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gravelly" class="extiw" title="wikt:gravelly">gravelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sandy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">saline</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>. It can thrive in poor and stony soils, although new research suggests that the plant's ability to adapt to these poor soils is not as extensive as had been previously stated. Complete <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a> is achieved within 9 days. Adding <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a> during the germination has negative effects during that phase, but is favorable if applied after germination is achieved. It can be propagated by cuttings, which yields faster results than multiplication by seeds.</p>
<p>The flowers only develop terminally (at the end of a stem), so a good ramification (plants presenting many branches) produces the greatest amount of fruits. The plants are <a href="/wiki/Self-incompatibility_in_plants#Self-compatibility_.28SC.29" title="Self-incompatibility in plants">self-compatible</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pesquisa_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pesquisa-10">[10]</a></sup> Another productivity factor is the ratio between female and male flowers within an inflorescence, more female flowers mean more fruits.<sup id="cite_ref-Jatropha_journal_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jatropha_journal-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>Jatropha curcas</i> thrives on a mere 250&#160;mm (10&#160;in) of rain a year, and only during its first two years does it need to be watered in the closing days of the dry season. <a href="/wiki/Ploughing" class="mw-redirect" title="Ploughing">Ploughing</a> and planting are not needed regularly, as this shrub has a life expectancy of approximately forty years. The use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> is not necessary, due to the pesticidal and <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicidal</a> properties of the plant. It is used in rural Bengal for <a href="/wiki/Tinea_cruris" title="Tinea cruris">dhobi itch</a> (a common fungal infection of the skin).</p>
<p>While <i>Jatropha curcas</i> starts yielding from 9-12 months time, the best yields are obtained only after 2–3 years time. The seed production is around 3.5 tons per hectare (seed production ranges from about 0.4 t/ha in the first year to over 5 t/ha after 3 years). If planted in <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">hedges</a>, the reported productivity of <i>Jatropha</i> is from 0.8 to 1.0&#160;kg of seed per meter of live fence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation">Propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Jatropha curcas</i> has limited natural vegetative propagation and is usually propagated by <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>. Propagation through seed (sexual propagation) leads to a lot of genetic variability in terms of growth, biomass, seed yield and oil content. Low seed viability and the recalcitrant nature of oil seeds also limit seed propagation. However, clonal techniques can help in overcoming these problems that hinder mass propagation of this tree-borne oilseed species. Vegetative propagation has been achieved by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stem_cuttings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stem cuttings (page does not exist)">stem cuttings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">grafting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a> as well as by <a href="/wiki/Air_layering" class="mw-redirect" title="Air layering">air layering</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The investigation leads to the recommendation that cuttings should be taken preferably from juvenile plants and treated with 200 micro gram per litre of <a href="/wiki/Indole-3-butyric_acid" title="Indole-3-butyric acid">IBA</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rooting_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Rooting hormone">rooting hormone</a>) to ensure the highest level of rooting in stem cuttings. These vegetative methods have potential for commercial propagation of these plants.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing">Processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seed extraction is made simple with the use of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Nut_Sheller" title="Universal Nut Sheller">Universal Nut Sheller</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a> designed by the <a href="/wiki/Full_Belly_Project" title="Full Belly Project">Full Belly Project</a>.</p>
<p>Oil content varies from 28% to 30% and 80% extraction, one <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> of plantation will give 400 to 600 litres of oil if the soil is average.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oily seeds are processed into <a href="/wiki/Plant_oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant oils">oil</a>, which may be used directly ("<a href="/wiki/Straight_Vegetable_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Straight Vegetable Oil">Straight Vegetable Oil</a>") to fuel combustion engines or may be subjected to <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Jatropha oil is not suitable for human consumption, as it induces strong <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuel">Biofuel <span id="Biodiesel"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biofuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg/220px-Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg" width="220" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg/330px-Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg/440px-Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg 2x" data-file-width="684" data-file-height="1000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jatropha_in_Paraguay_Chaco.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jatropha plantation in the dry center/west of the <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Chaco</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When jatropha seeds are crushed, the resulting jatropha oil can be processed to produce a high-quality <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> that can be used in a standard diesel car or further processed into jet fuel, while the residue (<a href="/wiki/Press_cake" title="Press cake">press cake</a>) can also be used as biomass feedstock to power electricity plants, used as fertilizer (it contains nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium), or as animal <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a>. The cake can also be used as feed in <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">digesters</a> and gasifiers to produce biogas.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg/220px-Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg" width="220" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg/330px-Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg/440px-Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1683" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedari_Menanam_Hingga_Memetik.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>"From planting to harvest. Treat the jatropha plant as well as possible to make the harvest as large as possible!"</i> (A reference to the compulsory planting of jatropha in Indonesia for the production of oil as machinery lubricant and fuel for the Japanese WWII war effort.)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are several forms of biofuel, often manufactured using <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a>. The fats and oils are turned into esters while separating the <a href="/wiki/Glycerin" class="mw-redirect" title="Glycerin">glycerin</a>. At the end of the process, the glycerin settles and the biofuel floats. The process through which the glycerin is separated from the biodiesel is known as <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a>. Glycerin is another by-product from Jatropha oil processing that can add value to the crop. Transesterification is a simple chemical reaction that neutralizes the free fatty acids present in any fatty substances in Jatropha. A chemical exchange takes place between the <a href="/wiki/Alkoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkoxy">alkoxy</a> groups of an ester compound by an alcohol. Usually, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> are used for the purpose. The reaction occurs by the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, usually sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or caustic soda and potassium hydroxide (KOH), which forms fatty esters (e.g., methyl or ethyl esters), commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. It takes approximately 10% of methyl alcohol by weight of the fatty substance to start the transesterification process.<sup id="cite_ref-Nahar_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nahar-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Estimates of <i>Jatropha</i> seed yield vary widely, due to a lack of research data, the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> diversity of the crop, the range of <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environments</a> in which it is grown, and <i>Jatropha'</i>s perennial life cycle. Seed yields under cultivation can range from 1,500 to 2,000 kilograms per <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a>, corresponding to extractable oil yields of 540 to 680 <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litres</a> per <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> (58 to 73 <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">US gallons</a> per <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acre</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2009 <i>Time</i> magazine cited the potential for as much as 1,600 gallons of diesel fuel per acre per year.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The plant may yield more than four times as much fuel per <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> as <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a>, and more than ten times that of <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> (corn), but at the same time it requires five times as much water per unit of energy produced as does corn (see below). A hectare of jatropha has been claimed to produce 1,892 litres of fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, as it has not yet been domesticated or improved by plant breeders, yields are variable.<sup id="cite_ref-Fairless_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairless-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Jatropha</i> can also be <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercropped</a> with other cash crops such as coffee, sugar, fruits and vegetables.<sup id="cite_ref-reuk_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuk-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> cited <i>Jatropha curcas</i> as one of the best candidates for future biodiesel production.<sup id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-22">[22]</a></sup> However, despite its abundance and use as an oil and <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">reclamation</a> plant, none of the <i>Jatropha</i> species has been properly <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> and, as a result, its productivity is variable, and the long-term impact of its large-scale use on soil quality and the environment is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Fairless2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairless2-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 researchers at Daimler Chrysler Research explored the use of jatropha oil for automotive use, concluding that although jatropha oil as fuel "has not yet reached optimal quality, ... it already fulfills the EU norm for biodiesel quality". <a href="/wiki/Archer_Daniels_Midland_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Archer Daniels Midland Company">Archer Daniels Midland Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bayer_CropScience" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayer CropScience">Bayer CropScience</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a> have a joint project to develop jatropha as a biofuel.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Three Mercedes cars powered by Jatropha diesel have already put some 30,000 kilometres behind them. The project is supported by <a href="/wiki/DaimlerChrysler" class="mw-redirect" title="DaimlerChrysler">DaimlerChrysler</a> and by the German Association for Investment and Development (Deutschen Investitions- und Entwicklungsgesellschaft, DEG).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_fuel">Jet fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jet fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aviation fuels may be more widely replaced by biofuels such as jatropha oil than fuels for other forms of transportation. There are fewer planes than cars or trucks and far fewer jet fueling stations to convert than gas stations.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes12-30_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes12-30-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 30, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">Air New Zealand</a> flew the first successful test flight from <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> running one of its four <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce</a> engines on a 50:50 blend of jatropha oil and <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet A-1 fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes12-30_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes12-30-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In the same press release, Air New Zealand announced plans to use the new fuel for 10% of its needs by 2013. At the time of this test, jatropha oil was much cheaper than crude oil, costing an estimated $43 a barrel or about one-third of the June 4, 2008 closing price of $122.30 for a <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(volume)#Oil_barrel" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrel (volume)">barrel</a> of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">crude oil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 7, 2009 Continental Airlines successfully completed a test flight from Houston, Texas using a 50/50 mixture of algae/jatropha-oil-derived biofuel and Jet A in one of the two CFM56 engines of a Boeing 737-800 Next Generation jet. The two-hour test flight could mark another promising step for the airline industry to find cheaper and more <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>.</p>
<p>On April 1, 2011 <a href="/wiki/Interjet" title="Interjet">Interjet</a> completed the first Mexican aviation biofuels test flight on an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a>. The fuel was a 70:30 traditional jet fuel biojet blend produced from Jatropha oil provided by three Mexican producers, Global Energías Renovables (a wholly owned subsidiary of U.S.-based <a href="/wiki/Global_Clean_Energy_Holdings" title="Global Clean Energy Holdings">Global Clean Energy Holdings</a>), Bencafser S.A. and Energy JH S.A. Honeywell's UOP processed the oil into Bio-SPK (Synthetic Paraffinic Kerosene).<sup id="cite_ref-BiodeiselsMexico_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BiodeiselsMexico-28">[28]</a></sup> Global Energías Renovables operates the largest Jatropha farm in the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-BiodeiselsMexico_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BiodeiselsMexico-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 28, 2011 <a href="/wiki/Air_China" title="Air China">Air China</a> completed the first successful demonstration flight by a Chinese airline that used jatropha-based biofuel. The mixture was a 50:50 mix of conventional jet fuel blended with jatropha oil from China National Petroleum Corp. The 747-400 powered one of its four engines on the fuel mixture during the 1-hour flight around Beijing airport.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_sequestration">Carbon dioxide sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2013 study published by the <a href="/wiki/European_Geosciences_Union" title="European Geosciences Union">European Geosciences Union</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the jatropha tree may have applications in the absorption of carbon dioxide, whose sequestration is important in combating climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This small tree is very resistant to aridity so it can be planted in hot and dry land in soil unsuitable for food production. The plant does need water to grow though, so coastal areas where desalinated seawater can be made available are ideal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_developing_world">Use in developing world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use in developing world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently the oil from <i>Jatropha curcas</i> <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> is used for making <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> fuel in <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, where it grows naturally and in plantations in the southeast, north, and northeast of Brazil. Likewise, jatropha oil is being promoted as an easily grown biofuel crop in hundreds of projects throughout India and other developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-WAC_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WAC-32">[32]</a></sup> Large plantings and nurseries have been undertaken in India by many research institutions, and by women's <a href="/wiki/Self-help" title="Self-help">self-help</a> groups who use a system of <a href="/wiki/Microcredit" title="Microcredit">microcredit</a> to ease poverty among semiliterate Indian women. The <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> is planted with <i>jatropha</i> and the train itself runs on 15-20% <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fairless_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairless-20">[20]</a></sup> In Africa, cultivation of <i>jatropha</i> is being promoted and it is grown successfully in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Gran Chaco</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, where a native variety (<i>Jatropha matacensis</i>) also grows, studies have shown the suitability of Jatropha cultivation<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and agro producers are starting to consider planting in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> is also actively pursuing the use of jatropha oil. On 15 December 2005, then-head of state, Senior General <a href="/wiki/Than_Shwe" title="Than Shwe">Than Shwe</a>, said “the States and Divisions concerned are to put 50,000 acres (200 km²) under the physic nut plants [Jatropha] each within three years totalling 700,000 acres (2,800 km²) during the period”. On the occasion of Burma’s Peasant Day 2006, Than Shwe described in his a message that “For energy sector which is an essential role in transforming industrial agriculture system, the Government is encouraging for cultivation of physic nut plants nationwide and the technical know how that can refine physic nuts to biodiesel has also identified.” He would like to urge peasants to cultivate physic nut plants on a commercial scale with major aims for emergence of industrial agriculture system, for fulfilling rural electricity supply and energy needs, for supporting rural areas development and import substitute</p>
<p>economy. (2005 from MRTV)</p>
<p>In 2006, the chief research officer at state-run <a href="/wiki/Myanma_Oil_and_Gas_Enterprise" title="Myanma Oil and Gas Enterprise">Myanma Oil and Gas Enterprise</a> said Burma hoped to completely replace the country's oil imports of 40,000 barrels a day with home-brewed, jatropha-derived biofuel. Other government officials declared Burma would soon start exporting jatropha oil. Despite the military's efforts, the jatropha campaign apparently has largely flopped in its goal of making Burma self-sufficient in fuel. (2006 from MyawaddyTV)</p>
<p>Z.G.S. Bioenergy has started Jatropha Plantation Projects in Northern <a href="/wiki/Shan_State" title="Shan State">Shan State</a>, the company has begun planting Jatropha plants during late June 2007 and will start producing seeds by 2010. (20 July 2007 from New Light of Myanmar)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2011 skepticism about the "miracle" properties of <i>Jatropha</i> has been voiced. For example: "The idea that jatropha can be grown on marginal land is a <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">red herring</a>", according to Harry Stourton, business development director of UK-based Sun Biofuels, which cultivates <i>Jatropha</i> in Mozambique and Tanzania. "It does grow on marginal land, but if you use marginal land you'll get marginal yields," he said.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>An August 2010 article warned about the actual utility and potential dangers of reliance on <i>Jatropha</i> in Kenya. Major concerns included its invasiveness, which could disrupt local biodiversity, as well as damage to water catchment areas.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Jatropha curcas</i> is lauded as being sustainable, and that its production would not compete with food production, but the jatropha plant needs water like every other crop to grow. This could create competition for water between the jatropha and other edible food crops. In fact, jatropha requires five times more water per unit of energy than sugarcane and corn.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jatropha_curcas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Flowers</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>The species is listed as a <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> plant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Hydrogen cyanide is present.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Nuts</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Can be construed for home cooking fuel in briquette form replacing charcoalized timber as in Haiti.<sup id="cite_ref-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3-43">[43]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>They can be burned like <a href="/wiki/Candlenut" class="mw-redirect" title="Candlenut">candlenuts</a> when strung on grass.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">HCN</a> is present.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Used as a <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">contraceptive</a> in South Sudan.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-45">[45]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Seeds</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Interest exists in producing animal feed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> from the bio-waste once the oil is expressed, as in the case with Haiti, where Jatropha curcas grows prolifically and animal feed is in very short supply.<sup id="cite_ref-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3-43">[43]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Similarly, Metsiyen in the Haitian culture dates back as a medicinal crop - thus the name "metsiyen"/"medsiyen". Some suggest it "calms the stomach".<sup id="cite_ref-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glenn_Brooks_Jachob_e3-43">[43]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Also used as a contraceptive in South Sudan.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-45">[45]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>The oil has been used for illumination, <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candle" title="Candle">candles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Adulterant" title="Adulterant">adulteration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, and making <a href="/wiki/Castor_oil" title="Castor oil">Turkey red oil</a>. <a href="/wiki/Castor_oil" title="Castor oil">Turkey red oil</a>, also called sulphonated (or sulfated) castor oil, is the only oil that completely disperses in water. It is made by adding <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to pure <i>Jatropha</i> oil.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It was the first synthetic <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergent</a> after ordinary <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, as this allows easy use for making bath oil products. It is used in formulating <a href="/wiki/Lubricant" title="Lubricant">lubricants</a>, softeners, and <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyeing assistants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>The seeds in the zone around Misantla, Veracruz are very appreciated by the population as food once they have been boiled and roasted. It is unclear if this is due to the existence of a non-toxic variety of Jatropha in Mexico and Central America, or if the seeds become edible once processed by cooking.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>It is also similarly reported that Jatropha seeds are edible once the embryo has been removed.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Again it may be so because of these seeds coming from a local non-toxic variety. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">HCN</a> is present.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Roots</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Their ashes are used as a salt substitute. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">HCN</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rotenone" title="Rotenone">rotenone</a> are present.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Bark</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Used as a fish poison. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">HCN</a> is present.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Igbinosa and colleagues (2009) demonstrated potential broad spectrum antimicrobial activity of <i>J. curcas</i> bark extract.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Latex</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Strongly inhibits the watermelon mosaic virus.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Sap</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>It stains linen. Sometimes used for marking.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Shrub</b></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>Used for <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> control,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bagan.C3.AD_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bagan.C3.AD-56">[56]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
