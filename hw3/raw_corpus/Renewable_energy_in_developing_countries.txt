			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Renewable energy in developing countries</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>Renewable energy technology has sometimes been seen as a costly luxury item by critics, and affordable only in the affluent developed world. This erroneous view has persisted for many years, but 2015 was the first year when investment in non-hydro renewables, was higher in developing countries, with $156 billion invested, mainly in China, India, and Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most developing countries have abundant <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> resources, including <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, as well as the ability to manufacture the relatively labor-intensive systems that harness these. By developing such energy sources developing countries can reduce their dependence on oil and natural gas, creating energy portfolios that are less vulnerable to price rises. In many circumstances, these investments can be less expensive than fossil fuel energy systems.<sup id="cite_ref-mdg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In isolated rural areas, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity grid">electricity grid</a> extensions are often not economical. Off‐grid renewable technologies provide a sustainable and cost‐effective alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">diesel generators</a> that would be otherwise be deployed in such areas. Renewable technologies can also help to displace other unsustainable energy sources such as <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">traditional biomass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-reeee_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reeee-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> is the world leader in the number of <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> systems installed per capita (but not the number of watts added). More than 30,000 small <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a>, each producing 12 to 30 watts, are sold in Kenya annually. Kenya was the first African country to use <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_Kenya" title="Geothermal power in Kenya">geothermal power</a>, and still has the largest installed capacity of geothermal power in Africa at 200 MW, with a potential of up to 10 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-en.ccchina.gov.cn_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en.ccchina.gov.cn-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale_for_renewable_energy">Rationale for renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rationale for renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009, about 1.4 billion people in the world lived without electricity, and 2.7 billion relied on wood, charcoal, and dung for home energy requirements. This lack of access to modern energy technology limits income generation, blunts efforts to escape poverty, affects people's health, and contributes to global deforestation and climate change. Small-scale renewable energy technologies and distributed energy options, such as onsite solar power and improved cookstoves, offer rural households modern energy services.<sup id="cite_ref-verlaw_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verlaw-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Renewable energy can be particularly suitable for developing countries. In rural and remote areas, transmission and distribution of energy generated from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> can be difficult and expensive. Producing renewable energy locally can offer a viable alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Renewable energy doesn't always have to come from a developing country. The Developing Areas Study Group session is a group of speakers from all over the energy businesses discuss the potential ideas to get developing countries the renewable energy that they need. Papers written by W. Morgan, R. Moss and P. Richard discuss the opportunities of renewable resources that lie within the developing country as well. Morgan and Richard claim firewood and agriculture could play a great role in an alternative energy solution in developing countries, while Richards claims that efficient use of agriculture could lead to renewable energy. Morgan also points out that green plants could play a great role in producing synthetic fuel alcohol, which would not only impact the developing country, but the world as a whole in providing an alternative fuel source.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Interest in renewable energies has increased in recent years due to environmental concern about <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, reduced costs of the technologies themselves, and improved efficiency and reliability.<sup id="cite_ref-aus_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aus-6">[6]</a></sup> In recent years, supportive programs from governments, businesses, nonprofit organizations, and community cooperatives have expanded access to these off-grid technologies and the energy services they provide. Program planners should select “low-hanging fruit” first, aiming for maximum access to modern energy services with the least effort.<sup id="cite_ref-verlaw_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verlaw-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_renewables">Use of renewables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use of renewables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Collectively, developing countries have more than half of global renewable power capacity. China and India are rapidly expanding markets for renewable energy. Brazil produces most of the world’s sugar-derived ethanol and has been adding new biomass and wind power plants. Many renewable markets are growing at rapid rates in countries such as Argentina, Costa Rica, Egypt, Indonesia, Kenya, Tanzania, Thailand, Tunisia, and Uruguay.<sup id="cite_ref-ren9_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ren9-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In isolated rural areas, electricity grid extensions are often not economical. Off‐grid renewable technologies provide a sustainable and cost‐effective alternative to the diesel generators that would be otherwise be deployed in such areas. Renewable technologies can also help to displace other unsustainable energy sources such as kerosene lamps and traditional biomass.<sup id="cite_ref-reeee_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reeee-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Technology advances are opening up a huge new market for solar power: the approximately 1.3 billion people around the world who don't have access to grid electricity. Even though they are typically very poor, these people have to pay far more for lighting than people in rich countries because they use inefficient kerosene lamps. Solar power costs half as much as lighting with kerosene.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> An estimated 3 million households get power from small solar PV systems.<sup id="cite_ref-re10_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-re10-10">[10]</a></sup> Kenya is the world leader in the number of solar power systems installed per capita. More than 30,000 very small solar panels, each producing 12 to 30 watts, are sold in Kenya annually.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Micro-hydro systems configured into village-scale or county-scale mini-grids serve many areas. More than 30 million rural households get lighting and cooking from biogas made in household-scale systems. These stoves are being manufactured in factories and workshops worldwide, and more than 160 million households now use them.<sup id="cite_ref-re10_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-re10-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty_alleviation">Poverty alleviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poverty alleviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Energy_poverty" title="Energy poverty">Energy poverty</a></div>
<p>Renewable energy projects in many developing countries have demonstrated that renewable energy can directly contribute to <a href="/wiki/Poverty_alleviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty alleviation">poverty alleviation</a> by providing the energy needed for creating businesses and employment. Renewable energy technologies can also make indirect contributions to alleviating poverty by providing energy for cooking, space heating, and lighting.<sup id="cite_ref-mdg_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Renewable energy can also contribute to education, by providing electricity to schools. Renewable energy for cooking and heating can reduce the time that children spend out of school collecting fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>2.4 billion people use only traditional biomass, such as wood, residues and dung, for cooking and heating. The constant use of these types of energy sources exposes them to indoor particulate and carbon monoxide concentrations many times higher than <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a> (WHO) standards. "Traditional stoves using dung and charcoal emit large amounts of carbon monoxide and other noxious gases. Women and children suffer most, because they are exposed for the longest periods of time. Acute respiratory illnesses affect as much as 6% of the world population. The WHO estimates that 2.5 million women and young children in developing countries die prematurely each year from breathing the fumes from indoor biomass stoves".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Renewable energy can improve this situation by reducing exposure to indoor pollutants.</p>
<p>Furthermore, renewable can also provide energy to refrigerate medicine and sterilize medical equipment in rural areas where the access to electricity is difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It can also provide power to supply the fresh water and sewerage services needed to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-mdg_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_policies">Government policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable energy policy">Renewable energy policy</a></div>
<p>More developing countries are implementing the public policies needed for the widespread development of renewable energy technologies and markets, which have traditionally been dominated by Europe, Japan, and North America. The exceptions include countries like Brazil, which has built the world’s leading <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> industry, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_China" title="Renewable energy in China">China</a>, India, which are leaders in developing decentralized renewable sources such as small hydro, small wind, biogas, and solar water heating.<sup id="cite_ref-mdg_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdg-2">[2]</a></sup> However, policies like <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> are applied. Besides, with the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, the program called the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> (CDM) that allows for industrialized nations to invest in projects that reduce emissions in developing countries as an alternative to more expensive emission reductions in their own countries.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Developing-country Governments need to steer resources mobilized for large-scale investments into new production sectors and new technologies. Some argue that policies should base on active industrial policies, combining large scale investments and active policy interventions. There is a need of subsidizing these type of energy services to make them affordable to the major part of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_in_the_Philippines" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable Energy in the Philippines">Renewable Energy in the Philippines</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG/220px-Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG/330px-Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG/440px-Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bangui_Wind_Mills.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bangui_Wind_Farm" title="Bangui Wind Farm">Bangui Wind Farm</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ilocos_Norte" title="Ilocos Norte">Ilocos Norte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippine</a> government sees the growth of the renewable energy sector essential for national energy security. The Philippines' fossil fuel sector is unsustainable, being dependent on the import of nonrenewable fuel, including petroleum, but has significant potential in the renewable energy sector. Based on a report of an Australian consulting firm, International Energy Consultants, the Philippines has the highest electricity rate in Asia, followed by <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Transmitting power and transporting fuel throughout the Philippine archipelago is problematic due to very high cost.<sup id="cite_ref-manila.b_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manila.b-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Philippines could be considered a world leader in renewable energy, with its 30 percent of its power generation being powered by the renewable energy sector. The Philippines is the world's second largest generator of geothermal energy and was the first <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asian</a> nation to invest in large-scale solar and wind technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-manila.b_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manila.b-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Promotion and support of renewable energy in the country was intensified with the passing of the Renewable Energy Act of 2008 which made a feed-in-tariff and a renewable portfolio standard. The Philippines aims to triple renewable energy supply by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-manila.b_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manila.b-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently the government has concluded agreements with private developers for extensive projects in Oriental Mindoro with an eventual output of 48 MW, with plans for even larger development in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite government efforts, some investors have criticized the government's lack of firmness in its feed-in-tariff policy, and the solar industry accused the government for hampering its progress in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Africa" title="Renewable energy in Africa">Renewable energy in Africa</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Algeria">Algeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Algeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On February 3, 2011, Algeria launched the National Development Programme for new and renewable energy and energy efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The program, which spans the period from 2011 to 2013, aims to produce 22,000 MW of electricity from solar and wind power which 10,000 MW for export.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenya">Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Kenya" title="Renewable energy in Kenya">Renewable energy in Kenya</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> is in charge of renewable energy policies. In March 2008, the country adopted the <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> policy. In January 2010, the policy was revised to urge private sectors to invest in electricity generation from renewable sources.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kenya was the first African country to use <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_Kenya" title="Geothermal power in Kenya">geothermal power</a>, and still has the largest installed capacity of geothermal power in Africa at 200 MW, with a potential of up to 10 GW.<sup id="cite_ref-en.ccchina.gov.cn_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en.ccchina.gov.cn-4">[4]</a></sup> The only other country in Africa utilising geothermal power is <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.</p>
<p>Kenya is the world leader in the number of solar power systems installed per capita (but not the number of watts added). More than 30,000 small solar panels, each producing 12 to 30 watts, are sold in Kenya annually. For an investment of as little as $100 for the panel and wiring, the PV system can be used to charge a car battery, which can then provide power to run a fluorescent lamp or a small television for a few hours a day. More Kenyans are turning to solar power every year rather than making connections to the country’s electric grid. This is due to the high connectivity costs and the fact that there is an abundance of solar power in Kenya.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America_and_the_Caribbean">Latin America and the Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Latin America and the Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Brazil" title="Renewable energy in Brazil">Renewable energy in Brazil</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a> accounted for more than 85.4% of the domestically produced electricity used in Brazil, according to preliminary data from the 2009 National Energy Balance, conducted by the Energy Research Corporation (EPE).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> After the oil shocks of the 1970s, Brazil started focusing on developing alternative sources of energy, mainly <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel_in_Brazil" title="Ethanol fuel in Brazil">sugarcane ethanol</a>. Its large sugarcane farms helped. In 1985, 91% of cars produced that year ran on sugarcane ethanol. The success of <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">flexible-fuel vehicles</a>, introduced in 2003, together with the mandatory E25 blend throughout the country, have allowed ethanol fuel consumption in the country to achieve a 50% market share of the gasoline-powered fleet by February 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-ANP07_2008_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ANP07_2008-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">Costa Rica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_in_Costa_Rica" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable Energy in Costa Rica">Renewable Energy in Costa Rica</a> accounts for over 90% of the total output of the nation's energy. The country is the world leader in renewable use with massive investment in windmill technologies. The government aim is to make the country the world's first carbon neutral country.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In March 2015 the whole country was running over 75 straight days on 100% renewable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
