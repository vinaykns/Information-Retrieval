			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Renewable resource</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>A <b>renewable resource</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> which replenishes to overcome <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a> caused by usage and consumption, either through biological reproduction or other naturally recurring processes in a finite amount of time in a human time scale. Renewable resources are a part of Earth's natural environment and the largest components of its <a href="/w/index.php?title=Outline_of_earth_science_Earth.27s_spheres&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Outline of earth science Earth.27s spheres (page does not exist)">ecosphere</a>. A positive life cycle assessment is a key indicator of a resource's sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Definitions of renewable resources may also include agricultural production, as in <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> and to an extent <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-What_are_Renewable_Resources_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_are_Renewable_Resources-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1962 <a href="/wiki/Paul_Alfred_Weiss" title="Paul Alfred Weiss">Paul Alfred Weiss</a> defined Renewable Resources as: "<i>The total range of living organisms providing man with food, fibres, drugs, etc...</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Another type of renewable resources is <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> resources. Common sources of renewable energy include solar, geothermal and wind power, which are all categorised as renewable resources.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air.2C_Food.2C_Water">Air, Food, Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Air, Food, Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_resources">Water resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Water resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">Water resources</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a> can be considered a <i>renewable</i> material when carefully controlled usage, treatment, and release are followed. If not, it would become a non-renewable resource at that location. For example, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> is usually removed from an <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> at a rate much greater than its very slow natural recharge, and so groundwater is considered non-renewable. Removal of water from the pore spaces may cause permanent compaction (<a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>) that cannot be renewed. 97.5% of the water on the Earth is salt water, and 3% is <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>; slightly over two thirds of this is frozen in <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_climate" title="Polar climate">polar</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_dist_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_dist-4">[4]</a></sup> The remaining unfrozen freshwater is found mainly as groundwater, with only a small fraction (0.008%) present above ground or in the air.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a> is one of the main concerns regarding water resources. It is estimated that 22% of worldwide water is used in industry.<sup id="cite_ref-WBCSD_Water_Facts_.26_Trends_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WBCSD_Water_Facts_.26_Trends-6">[6]</a></sup> Major industrial users include hydroelectric dams, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation#Other_generation_methods" title="Electricity generation">thermoelectric power plants</a>, which use water for cooling, <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> refineries, which use water in chemical processes, and manufacturing plants, which use water as a solvent.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non_agricultural_food">Non agricultural food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non agricultural food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alaska_wild_berries.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Alaska_wild_berries.jpg/220px-Alaska_wild_berries.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Alaska_wild_berries.jpg/330px-Alaska_wild_berries.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Alaska_wild_berries.jpg/440px-Alaska_wild_berries.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2401" data-file-height="1563" /></a>

<p>Food is any substance consumed to provide nutritional support for the body.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Most food has its origin in renewable resources. Food is obtained directly from plants and animals.</p>
<p>Wild berries and other fruits, mushrooms, plants, seeds and naturally growing edible resources, still represent a valuable source of nutrition in many countries, especially in rural areas. In fact many wild animals are dependent on wild plants and fruits as a source of food.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis375_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis375-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hunting may not be the first source of meat in the modernised world, but it is still an important and essential source for many rural and remote groups. It is also the sole source of feeding for wild carnivores.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sustainable agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</a></div>
<p>The phrase <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> was coined by Australian agricultural scientist <a href="/wiki/Gordon_McClymont" title="Gordon McClymont">Gordon McClymont</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It has been defined as "an integrated system of plant and animal production practices having a site-specific application that will last over the long term".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Expansion of agricultural land reduces <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and contributes to <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a> of the United Nations estimates that in coming decades, cropland will continue to be lost to industrial and urban development, along with reclamation of wetlands, and conversion of forest to cultivation, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a> and increased <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> are available everywhere on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a> also depend on <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> <a href="/wiki/Nutrients" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> and the availability of <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water</a>. <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">Monoculture</a> is a method of growing only one crop at a time in a given field, which can damage land and cause it to become either unusable or suffer from reduced <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yields</a>. Monoculture can also cause the build-up of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> and pests that target one specific species. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Irish_Famine" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Irish Famine">Great Irish Famine (1845–1849)</a> is a well-known example of the dangers of monoculture.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">Crop rotation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">long-term crop rotations</a> confer the replenishment of nitrogen through the use of <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a> in sequence with cereals and other crops, and can improve <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">soil structure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertility (soil)">fertility</a> by alternating deep-rooted and shallow-rooted plants. Other methods to combat lost soil nutrients are returning to natural cycles that annually flood cultivated lands (returning lost nutrients indefinitely) such as the <a href="/wiki/Flooding_of_the_Nile" title="Flooding of the Nile">Flooding of the Nile</a>, the long-term use of <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, and use of crop and livestock <a href="/wiki/Landrace" title="Landrace">landraces</a> that are adapted to less than ideal conditions such as pests, drought, or lack of nutrients.</p>
<p>Agricultural practices are the single greatest contributor to the global increase in <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">soil erosion</a> rates.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It is estimated that "more than a thousand million tonnes of southern Africa's soil are eroded every year. Experts predict that crop yields will be halved within thirty to fifty years if erosion continues at present rates."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> phenomenon in the 1930s was caused by severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> combined with farming methods that did not include crop rotation, fallow fields, <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">cover crops</a>, soil terracing and wind-breaking trees to prevent <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">wind erosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-drought_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drought-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a> of agricultural lands is one of the primary contributing factors to erosion, due to mechanised agricultural equipment that allows for deep plowing, which severely increases the amount of soil that is available for transport by <a href="/wiki/Water_erosion" class="mw-redirect" title="Water erosion">water erosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The phenomenon called <i>Peak Soil</i> describes how large-scale factory farming techniques are jeopardizing humanity's ability to grow food in the present and in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Without efforts to improve soil management practices, the availability of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable soil</a> will become increasingly problematic.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Methods to combat erosion include <a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">no-till farming</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Keyline_design" title="Keyline design">keyline design</a>, growing <a href="/wiki/Hedge" title="Hedge">wind breaks</a> to hold the soil, and widespread use of <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_fertiliser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical fertiliser">Chemical fertiliser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> can also have an effect of soil erosion, which can contribute to <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">soil salinity</a> and prevent other species from growing. <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">Phosphate</a> is a primary component in the chemical fertiliser applied most commonly in modern agricultural production. However, scientists estimate that rock phosphate reserves will be depleted in 50–100 years and that <i>Peak Phosphate</i> will occur in about 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Industrial_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial process">Industrial processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> also have an effect on agriculture's sustainability. The way and locations crops are <a href="/wiki/Selling" class="mw-redirect" title="Selling">sold</a> requires energy for transportation, as well as the energy cost for materials, <a href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour (economics)">labour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>. Food sold at a local location, such a <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_market" title="Farmers' market">farmers' market</a>, have reduced energy overheads.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air">Air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Air is a renewable resource. All <a href="/wiki/Living_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Living organisms">living organisms</a> need <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>(directly or indirectly), <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>(directly or indirectly) and many other gases in small <a href="/wiki/Quantities" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantities">quantities</a> for their <a href="/wiki/Survival_skills" title="Survival skills">survival</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-food_resources">Non-food resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-food resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">Energy crop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-food_crop" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-food crop">Non-food crop</a></div>
<p>An important renewable resource is <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> provided by means of <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, which is used for construction, housing and firewood since ancient times. <sup id="cite_ref-unece_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unece-21">[21]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-fao_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-22">[22]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-woodecon_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woodecon-23">[23]</a></sup> Plants provide the main sources for renewable resources, the main distinction is made between <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-food_crop" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-food crop">Non-food crops</a>. A large variety of <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">lubricants</a>, industrially used vegetable oils, textiles and fibre made e.g. of <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copra" title="Copra">copra</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> derived from <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">rags</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioplastic" title="Bioplastic">bioplastic</a> are based on plant renewable resources. A large variety of chemical base products like <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> can be provided with plant renewables. Animal based renewables include <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, technical <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> and lubricants and further derived products, as e.g. <a href="/wiki/Animal_glue" title="Animal glue">animal glue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casing_(sausage)" title="Casing (sausage)">casings</a> or in historical times <a href="/wiki/Ambergris" title="Ambergris">ambra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baleen" title="Baleen">baleen</a> provided by <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a>.</p>
<p>With regard to pharmacy ingredients and legal and illegal drugs, plants are important sources, however e.g. venom of snakes, frogs and insects has been a valuable renewable source of pharmacological ingredients. Before GMO production set in, <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> and important <a href="/wiki/Hormones" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormones">hormones</a> were based on animal sources. <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">Feathers</a>, an important byproduct of poultry farming for food, is still being used as filler and as base for <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a> in general. Same applies for the <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> produced in farming <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">Crustaceans</a> which may be used as base of <a href="/wiki/Chitosan" title="Chitosan">chitosan</a>. The most important part of the human body used for non-medical purposes is <a href="/wiki/Human_hair" class="mw-redirect" title="Human hair">human hair</a> as for <a href="/wiki/Artificial_hair_integrations" title="Artificial hair integrations">artificial hair integrations</a>, which is being traded worldwide.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_role">Historical role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Historically, renewable resources like firewood, <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood_ash" title="Wood ash">wood ash</a>, plant colors as <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a>, and whale products have been crucial for human needs but failed to supply demand in the beginning of the industrial era.<sup id="cite_ref-rad_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rad-24">[24]</a></sup> Early modern times faced large problems with overuse of renewable resources as in <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rad_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rad-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides fresh meat and milk, which is as a food item not topic of this section, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> farmers and artisans used further animal ingredients as <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendons</a>, horn, bones, bladders. Complex technical constructions as the <a href="/wiki/Composite_bow" title="Composite bow">composite bow</a> were based on combination of animal and plant based materials. The current distribution conflict between biofuel and food production is being described as <a href="/wiki/Food_vs._fuel" title="Food vs. fuel">Food vs. fuel</a>. Conflicts between food needs and usage, as supposed by <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a> obligations were in so far common in historical times as well.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> However, a significant percentage of (middle European) farmers yields went into <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, which provides as well organic fertiliser.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Oxen and horses were important for transportation purposes, drove engines as e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Treadmill" title="Treadmill">treadmills</a>.</p>
<p>Other regions solved the transportation problem with <a href="/wiki/Terrace_(agriculture)" title="Terrace (agriculture)">terracing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_agriculture" title="Urban agriculture">urban</a> and garden agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-rad_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rad-24">[24]</a></sup> Further conflicts as between forestry and herding, or (sheep) herders and cattle farmers led to various solutions. Some confined wool production and sheep to large state and nobility domains or outsourced to professional shepherds with larger wandering herds.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Agricultural_Revolution" title="British Agricultural Revolution">British Agricultural Revolution</a> was mainly based on a new system of <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, the four-field rotation. <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> agriculturist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Townshend,_2nd_Viscount_Townshend" title="Charles Townshend, 2nd Viscount Townshend">Charles Townshend</a> recognised the invention in Dutch <a href="/wiki/Waasland" title="Waasland">Waasland</a> and popularised it in the 18th century UK, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Carver" title="George Washington Carver">George Washington Carver</a> in the USA. The system used <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turnip" title="Turnip">turnips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> and introduced as well <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a>. Clover is able to fix nitrogen from air, a practically non exhaustive renewable resource, into fertilizing compounds to the soil and allowed to increase yields by large. Farmers opened up a fodder crop and grazing crop. Thus <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> could to be bred year-round and winter <a href="/wiki/Culling" title="Culling">culling</a> was avoided. The amount of manure rose and allowed more crops but to refrain from <a href="/wiki/Wood_pasture" title="Wood pasture">wood pasture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rad_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rad-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early modern times and the 19th century saw the previous resource base partially replaced respectively supplemented by large scale chemical synthesis and by the use of fossil and mineral resources respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-DCH_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCH-28">[28]</a></sup> Besides the still central role of wood, there is a sort of renaissance of renewable products based on modern agriculture, genetic research and extraction technology. Besides fears about an upcoming <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">global shortage of fossil fuels</a>, local shortages due to boycotts, war and blockades or just transportation problems in remote regions have contributed to different methods of replacing or substituting fossil resources based on renewables.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of certain basically renewable products as in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine#Animal_substances" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">TCM</a> <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangers various species</a>. Just the black market in <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros_horn" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhinoceros horn">rhinoceros horn</a> reduced the world's rhino population by more than 90 percent over the past 40 years.<sup id="cite_ref-RH_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RH-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FTCMRH_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTCMRH-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewables_used_for_autarky_approaches">Renewables used for autarky approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renewables used for autarky approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The success of the German chemical industry till World War I was based on the replacement of colonial products. The predecessors of <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben" title="IG Farben">IG Farben</a> dominated the world market for <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">synthetic dyes</a> at the beginning of the 20th century<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and had an important role in artificial <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">pharmaceuticals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agrichemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Agrichemical">agricultural chemicals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemicals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DCH_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCH-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>However the former <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">Plant breeding</a> research institutes took a different approach. After the loss of the <a href="/wiki/German_colonial_empire" title="German colonial empire">German colonial empire</a>, important players in the field as <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Baur" title="Erwin Baur">Erwin Baur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Meyer" title="Konrad Meyer">Konrad Meyer</a> switched to using local crops as base for economic <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-heim_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heim-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup> Meyer as a key agricultural scientist and spatial planner of the Nazi era managed and lead <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Forschungsgemeinschaft" title="Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft">Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft</a> resources and focused about a third of the complete research grants in Nazi Germany on agricultural and genetic research and especially on resources needed in case of a further German war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-heim_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heim-32">[32]</a></sup> A wide array of agrarian research institutes still existing today and having importance in the field was founded or enlarged in the time.</p>
<p>There were some major failures as trying to e.g. grow <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">frost resistant</a> olive species, but some success in the case of <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linum" title="Linum">Linum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a>, which are still of current importance.<sup id="cite_ref-heim_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heim-32">[32]</a></sup> During the war, German scientists tried to systematically exploit foreign research results in occupied countries. <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> personally supported a research project using Russian <a href="/wiki/Taraxacum" title="Taraxacum">Taraxacum</a> (dandelion) species to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">natural rubber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-heim_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heim-32">[32]</a></sup> The project was conducted using 150 female KZ prisoners and captured Russian scientists kept together as 'Kommando Pflanzenzucht' (Plant breeding command) in a <a href="/wiki/Subcamp_(SS)" title="Subcamp (SS)">subcamp (SS)</a> of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Konzentrationslager_Auschwitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Konzentrationslager Auschwitz (page does not exist)">Konzentrationslager Auschwitz</a> led by SS agrarian research officer Joachim Caesar. Rubber dandelions are still of interest, as scientists in the Fraunhofer Institute for Molecular Biology and Applied Ecology (IME) announced 2013 to have developed a cultivar that is suitable for commercial production of natural rubber.<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_situation_and_subsidies">Legal situation and subsidies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legal situation and subsidies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several legal and economic means have been used to enhance the market share of renewables. The UK uses <a href="/wiki/Non-Fossil_Fuel_Obligation" title="Non-Fossil Fuel Obligation">Non-Fossil Fuel Obligations</a> (NFFO), a collection of <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument" title="Statutory Instrument">orders</a> requiring the electricity <a href="/wiki/Distribution_Network_Operator" class="mw-redirect" title="Distribution Network Operator">Distribution Network Operators</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> to purchase electricity from the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sectors. Similar mechanisms operate in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> (the Scottish Renewable Orders under the Scottish Renewables Obligation) and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> (the Northern Ireland Non-Fossil Fuel Obligation). In the USA, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Certificates" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable Energy Certificates">Renewable Energy Certificates</a> (RECs), use a similar approach. German <a href="/wiki/Energy_transition" title="Energy transition">Energiewende</a> is using fed-in tariffs. An unexpected outcome of the subsidies was the quick increase of pellet byfiring in conventional fossil fuel plants (compare <a href="/wiki/Tilbury_power_stations" title="Tilbury power stations">Tilbury power stations</a>) and cement works, making wood respectively biomass accounting for about half of Europe’s renewable-energy consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-woodecon_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woodecon-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_industrial_use">Examples of industrial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Examples of industrial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioplastics">Bioplastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bioplastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioplastics" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioplastics">Bioplastics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

A packaging blister made from <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate" title="Cellulose acetate">cellulose acetate</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bioplastic" title="Bioplastic">bioplastic</a></div>

<p>Bioplastics are a form of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a> derived from renewable <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_fats_and_oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetable fats and oils">vegetable fats and oils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_starch" title="Corn starch">corn starch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">pea</a> <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Microbiota_(microbiology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbiota (microbiology)">microbiota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The most common form of bioplastic is <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a> starch. Other forms include <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">Cellulose</a> bioplastics, bio<a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polylactic_acid" title="Polylactic acid">Polylactic acid</a>, and bio-derived <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>.</p>
<p>The production and use of bioplastics is generally regarded as a more <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable activity</a> when compared to plastic production from petroleum (petroplastic); however, manufacturing of bioplastic materials is often still reliant upon petroleum as an energy and materials source. Because of the fragmentation in the market and ambiguous definitions it is difficult to describe the total market size for bioplastics, but the global production capacity is estimated at 327,000 tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-NNFCC_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NNFCC-37">[37]</a></sup> In contrast, global consumption of all flexible packaging is estimated at around 12.3 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioasphalt">Bioasphalt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bioasphalt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioasphalt" title="Bioasphalt">Bioasphalt</a></div>
<p>Bioasphalt is an <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> alternative made from non-petroleum based renewable resources. Manufacturing sources of bioasphalt include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/molasses" class="extiw" title="wikt:molasses">molasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a>, and vegetable oil based waste. Asphalt made with vegetable oil based binders was patented by Colas SA in France in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a> refers to the provision of energy via renewable resources which are naturally replenished fast enough as being used. It includes e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal heat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Renewable energy may replace or enhance fossil energy supply various distinct areas: <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar hot water">hot water</a>/<a href="/wiki/Space_heating" class="mw-redirect" title="Space heating">space heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_fuel" title="Motor fuel">motor fuels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stand-alone_power_system" title="Stand-alone power system">rural (off-grid)</a> energy services.<sup id="cite_ref-ren15_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ren15-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">Biomass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Biomass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a> is referring to <a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biological material</a> from living, or recently living organisms, most often referring to plants or plant-derived materials.</p>
<p>Sustainable harvesting and use of renewable resources (i.e., maintaining a positive renewal rate) can reduce <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Biomass energy is derived from six distinct energy sources: garbage, wood, plants, waste, <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfill gases">landfill gases</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcohol fuels">alcohol fuels</a>. Historically, humans have harnessed biomass-derived energy since the advent of burning wood to make fire, and wood remains the largest biomass energy source today.<sup id="cite_ref-online.wsj.com_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online.wsj.com-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, low tech use of biomass, which still amounts for more than 10% of world energy needs may induce <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_pollution_in_developing_nations" title="Indoor air pollution in developing nations">indoor air pollution in developing nations</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and results in between 1.5 million and 2 million deaths in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Ezzati_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ezzati-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biomass used for electricity generation varies by region.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-48">[48]</a></sup> Forest by-products, such as wood residues, are common in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-48">[48]</a></sup> Agricultural waste is common in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> (sugar cane residue) and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> (rice husks).<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-48">[48]</a></sup> Animal husbandry residues, such as poultry litter, are common in the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI1_48-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-48">[48]</a></sup> The biomass power generating industry in the United States, which consists of approximately 11,000 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a> of summer operating capacity actively supplying power to the grid, produces about 1.4 percent of the U.S. electricity supply.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuel">Biofuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biofuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Biofuels">Biofuels</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A biofuel is a type of <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> whose energy is derived from biological <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon fixation</a>. Biofuels include fuels derived from <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> conversion, as well as <a href="/wiki/Biofuel#Solid_biofuels" title="Biofuel">solid biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid fuels">liquid fuels</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bioethanol" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioethanol">Bioethanol</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">fermentation</a>, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> produced in <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> crops such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Switchgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a> is made from <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_fat" title="Animal fat">animal fats</a>. Biodiesel is produced from oils or fats using <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> and is the most common biofuel in Europe.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">Biogas</a> is <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> produced by the process of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic material">organic material</a> by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobe" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaerobe">anaerobes</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> etc. is also a renewable source of energy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas">Biogas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biogas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">Biogas</a> typically refers to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> produced by the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. Biogas is produced by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> with anaerobic bacteria or <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> of biodegradable materials such as <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_waste" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal waste">municipal waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_waste" title="Green waste">green waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant material">plant material</a>, and crops.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> It is primarily <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
4</span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and may have small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulphide" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen sulphide">hydrogen sulphide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>S</span>), moisture and <a href="/wiki/Siloxane" title="Siloxane">siloxanes</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_fibre">Natural fibre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Natural fibre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">Natural fiber</a></div>
<p>Natural fibres are a class of hair-like materials that are continuous filaments or are in discrete elongated pieces, similar to pieces of <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">thread</a>. They can be used as a component of <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a> materials. They can also be <a href="/wiki/Felted" title="Felted">matted</a> into sheets to make products such as <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Felt" title="Felt">felt</a>. Fibres are of two types: natural fibre which consists of animal and plant fibres, and man made fibre which consists of synthetic fibres and regenerated fibres.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_to_renewable_resources">Threats to renewable resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Threats to renewable resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Renewable resources are endangered by non-regulated industrial developments and growth.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> They must be carefully managed to avoid exceeding the natural world's capacity to replenish them.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> A life cycle assessment provides a systematic means of evaluating renewability. This is a matter of sustainability in the natural environment.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/200px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" width="200" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/300px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/400px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="942" data-file-height="738" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> stocks severely overfished leading to abrupt collapse</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> has described ocean over fishing as "simply the taking of wildlife from the sea at rates too high for fished species to replace themselves."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">Tuna</a> meat is driving overfishing as to endanger some species like the bluefin tuna. The European Community and other organisations are trying to regulate fishery as to protect species and to prevent their extinctions.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a> treaty deals with aspects of overfishing in articles 61, 62, and 65.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of overfishing exist in areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_in_the_North_Sea" title="Fishing in the North Sea">North Sea</a> of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Banks_of_Newfoundland" title="Grand Banks of Newfoundland">Grand Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_China_Sea" title="East China Sea">East China Sea</a> of Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The decline of <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguin</a> population is caused in part by overfishing, caused by human competition over the same renewable resources<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg/220px-Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg" width="220" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg/330px-Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg/440px-Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="542" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Deforestation_Carceres_Braasil_1996.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Deforestation in Brazil 1996</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Besides their role as a resource for fuel and building material, trees protect the environment by absorbing carbon dioxide and by creating oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The destruction of rain forests is one of the critical causes of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Deforestation causes carbon dioxide to linger in the atmosphere. As carbon dioxide accrues, it produces a layer in the atmosphere that traps radiation from the sun. The radiation converts to heat which causes <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which is better known as the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deforestation also affects the water cycle. It reduces the content of water in the soil and groundwater as well as atmospheric moisture.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Deforestation reduces soil cohesion, so that <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">erosion</a>, flooding and <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> ensue.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rain forests house many species and organisms providing people with food and other commodities. In this way biofuels may well be unsustainable if their production contributes to deforestation.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endangered_species">Endangered species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Endangered species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg/200px-Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg" width="200" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg/300px-Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg/400px-Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5689" data-file-height="4448" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bison_skull_pile_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Over-hunting of <a href="/wiki/American_Bison" class="mw-redirect" title="American Bison">American Bison</a>.</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">Endangered species</a></div>
<p>Some renewable resources, species and organisms are facing a very high risk of extinction caused by growing human population and over-consumption. It has been estimated that over 40% of all living species on Earth are at risk of going extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Many nations have laws to protect hunted species and to restrict the practice of hunting. Other conservation methods include restricting land development or creating preserves. The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</a> is the best-known worldwide conservation status listing and ranking system.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Internationally, 199 countries have signed an accord agreeing to create <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plans</a> to protect endangered and other threatened species.</p>
