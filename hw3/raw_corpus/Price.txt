<p>In ordinary usage, <b>price</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Payment" title="Payment">payment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Financial_compensation" title="Financial compensation">compensation</a> given by one <a href="/wiki/Party_(law)" title="Party (law)">party</a> to another in return for <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In modern <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economies</a>, prices are generally expressed in units of some form of <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>. (For <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodities</a>, they are expressed as currency per unit weight of the commodity, e.g. euros per kilogram.) Although prices could be <a href="/wiki/Sales_quote" title="Sales quote">quoted</a> as quantities of other goods or services this sort of <a href="/wiki/Barter_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Barter exchange">barter exchange</a> is rarely seen. Prices are sometimes quoted in terms of vouchers such as trading stamps and air miles. In some circumstances, cigarettes have been used as currency, for example in prisons, in times of <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation" title="Hyperinflation">hyperinflation</a>, and in some places during World War 2. In a <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> economy, <a href="/wiki/Barter" title="Barter">barter</a> is also relatively common.</p>
<p>In many financial transactions, it is customary to quote prices in other ways. The most obvious example is in pricing a loan, when the <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> will be expressed as the percentage rate of interest. The total amount of interest payable depends upon credit risk, the loan amount and the period of the loan. Other examples can be found in pricing financial derivatives and other financial assets. For instance the price of inflation-linked government securities in several countries is quoted as the actual price divided by a factor representing inflation since the security was issued.</p>
<p>Price sometimes refers to the quantity of payment <i>requested</i> by a seller of goods or services, rather than the eventual payment amount. This requested amount is often called the <a href="/wiki/Ask_price" title="Ask price">asking price</a> or <b>selling price</b>, while the actual payment may be called the <b>transaction price</b> or <b>traded price</b>. Likewise, the <a href="/wiki/Bid_price" title="Bid price">bid price</a> or <b>buying price</b> is the quantity of payment <i>offered</i> by a buyer of goods or services, although this meaning is more common in asset or financial markets than in consumer markets.</p>
<p>Economists sometimes define price more generally as the ratio of the quantities of goods that are exchanged for each other.</p>
<p>Price theory Economic theory asserts that in a free market economy the market price reflects interaction between <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">supply and demand</a>: the price is set so as to equate the quantity being supplied and that being demanded. In turn these quantities are determined by the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_utility" title="Marginal utility">marginal utility</a> of the asset to different buyers and to different sellers. In reality, the price may be distorted by other factors, such as tax and other government regulations.</p>
<p>When a <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> is for sale at multiple locations, the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_one_price" title="Law of one price">law of one price</a> is generally believed to hold. This essentially states that the cost difference between the locations cannot be greater than that representing shipping, taxes, other distribution costs and more. In the case of the majority of consumer goods and services, distribution costs are quite a high proportion of the overall price, so the law may not be very useful.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_and_value">Price and value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Price and value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paradox_of_value" title="Paradox of value">paradox of value</a> was observed and debated by <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economists</a>. <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> described what is now called the <i>diamond – water paradox</i>: diamonds command a higher price than water, yet water is essential for life and diamonds are merely ornamentation. <a href="/wiki/Use_value" title="Use value">Use value</a> was supposed to give some measure of usefulness, later refined as <a href="/wiki/Marginal_benefit" class="mw-redirect" title="Marginal benefit">marginal benefit</a> (which is marginal utility counted in common units of value) while <a href="/wiki/Exchange_value" title="Exchange value">exchange value</a> was the measure of how much one good was in terms of another, namely what is now called <a href="/wiki/Relative_price" title="Relative price">relative price</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Price#Dubious" title="Talk:Price">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Austrian_School_theory">Austrian School theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Austrian School theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One solution offered to the paradox of value is through the theory of marginal utility proposed by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Menger" title="Carl Menger">Carl Menger</a>, one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">Austrian School</a> of economics.</p>
<p>As William Barber put it, human volition, the human subject, was "brought to the centre of the stage" by marginalist economics, as a bargaining tool. Neoclassical economists sought to clarify choices open to producers and consumers in market situations, and thus "fears that cleavages in the economic structure might be unbridgeable could be suppressed".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Without denying the applicability of the Austrian theory of value as <i>subjective</i> only, within certain contexts of price behavior, the Polish economist <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Lange" class="mw-redirect" title="Oskar Lange">Oskar Lange</a> felt it was necessary to attempt a serious <i>integration</i> of the insights of classical political economy with neo-classical economics. This would then result in a much more realistic theory of price and of real behavior in response to prices. Marginalist theory lacked anything like a theory of the social framework of real market functioning, and criticism sparked off by the <a href="/wiki/Capital_controversy" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital controversy">capital controversy</a> initiated by <a href="/wiki/Piero_Sraffa" title="Piero Sraffa">Piero Sraffa</a> revealed that most of the foundational tenets of the marginalist theory of value either reduced to <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(logic)" title="Tautology (logic)">tautologies</a>, or that the theory was true only if counter-factual conditions applied.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One insight often ignored in the debates about price theory is something that businessmen are keenly aware of: in different markets, prices may not function according to the same principles except in some very abstract (and therefore not very useful) sense. From the classical political economists to <a href="/wiki/Michal_Kalecki" class="mw-redirect" title="Michal Kalecki">Michal Kalecki</a> it was known that prices for industrial goods behaved differently from prices for agricultural goods, but this idea could be extended further to other broad classes of goods and services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_as_productive_human_labour_time">Price as productive human labour time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Price as productive human labour time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marxists assert that <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_value_(economics)" title="Theory of value (economics)">value</a> derives from the volume of <a href="/wiki/Socially_necessary_labour_time" title="Socially necessary labour time">socially necessary labour time</a> exerted in the creation of an object. This value does not relate to price in a simple manner, and the difficulty of the conversion of the mass of values into the actual prices is known as the <a href="/wiki/Transformation_problem" title="Transformation problem">transformation problem</a>. However, many recent Marxists deny that any problem exists. Marx was not concerned with proving that prices derive from values. In fact, he admonished the other classical political economists (like Ricardo and Smith) for trying to make this proof. Rather, for Marx, price equals the cost of production (capital-cost and labor-costs) plus the average rate of profit. So if the average rate of profit (return on capital investment) is 22% then prices would reflect cost-of-production plus 22%. The perception that there is a transformation problem in Marx stems from the injection of <a href="/wiki/Competitive_equilibrium" title="Competitive equilibrium">Walrasian equilibrium theory</a> into Marxism where there is no such thing as equilibrium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confusion_between_prices_and_costs_of_production">Confusion between prices and costs of production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Confusion between prices and costs of production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Price is commonly confused with the notion of cost of production, as in “I paid a high <u>cost</u> for <u>buying</u> my new plasma television”; but technically these are different concepts. Price is what a buyer pays to acquire products from a seller. Cost of production concerns the seller’s investment (e.g., manufacturing expense) in the product being exchanged with a buyer. For <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> organizations seeking to make a profit, the hope is that price will exceed cost of production so that the organization can see financial gain from the transaction.</p>
<p>Finally, while pricing is a topic central to a company's profitability, pricing decisions are not limited to for-profit companies. The behavior of <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organizations">non-profit organizations</a>, such as charities, educational institutions and industry trade groups, also involve setting prices.<sup id="cite_ref-ewot2014_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ewot2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:160–165</sup> For instance, charities seeking to raise money may set different “target” levels for donations that reward donors with increases in status (e.g., name in newsletter), gifts or other benefits; likewise educational and cultural nonprofits often price seats for events in theatres, auditoriums and stadiums. Furthermore, while nonprofit organizations may not earn a "profit", by definition, it is the case that many nonprofits may desire to maximize <i>net revenue</i>—total revenue less total cost—for various programs and activities, such as selling seats to theatrical and cultural performances.<sup id="cite_ref-ewot2014_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ewot2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:183–194</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_point">Price point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Price point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The price of an item is also called the "<a href="/wiki/Price_point" title="Price point">price point</a>", especially where it refers to stores that set a limited number of price points. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dollar_General" title="Dollar General">Dollar General</a> is a <a href="/wiki/General_store" title="General store">general store</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Five_and_dime" class="mw-redirect" title="Five and dime">five and dime</a>" store that sets price points only at even amounts, such as exactly one, two, three, five, or ten <a href="/wiki/Dollar" title="Dollar">dollars</a> (among others). Other stores will have a policy of setting most of their prices ending in 99 cents or pence. Other stores (such as <a href="/wiki/Dollar_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar store">dollar stores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound</a> stores, <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> stores, 100-<a href="/wiki/Yen" class="mw-redirect" title="Yen">yen</a> stores, and so forth) only have a single price point ($1, £1, €1, ¥100), though in some cases this price may purchase more than one of some very small items.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_terms">Other terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Basic price is the price a seller gets after removing any taxes paid by a buyer and adding any subsidy the seller gets for selling.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Producer price is the amount the producer gets from a buyer for a unit of a good or service produced as output minus any tax, it excludes any transport charges invoiced separately by the producer.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Price_optimization" title="Price optimization">Price optimization</a> is the use of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">mathematical analysis</a> by a company to determine how customers will respond to different prices for its products and services through different channels.</p>
