<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <b>energy flow</b>, also called the <b>calorific flow</b>, refers to the flow of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>, and is the focus of study in <b>ecological energetics</b>. In an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecologist">ecologists</a> seek to <a href="/wiki/Quantification_(science)" title="Quantification (science)">quantify</a> the relative importance of different component <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and feeding relationships.</p>
<p>A general energy flow scenario follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> is fixed by the <a href="/wiki/Photoautotroph" class="mw-redirect" title="Photoautotroph">photoautotrophs</a>, called primary producers, like green <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>. <a href="/wiki/Primary_consumers" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary consumers">Primary consumers</a> absorb most of the stored energy in the plant through <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>, and transform it into the form of energy they need, such as <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP), through <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a>. A part of the energy received by primary consumers, <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a>, is converted to <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">body heat</a> (an effect of respiration), which is <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">radiated</a> away and lost from the system. The loss of energy through body heat is far greater in <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">warm-blooded</a> animals, which must eat much more frequently than those that are <a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">cold-blooded</a>. Energy loss also occurs in the expulsion of <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">undigested</a> food (egesta) by <a href="/wiki/Excrement" class="mw-redirect" title="Excrement">excretion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitation</a>.</li>
<li>Secondary consumers, <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a>, then consume the primary consumers, although <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a> also consume primary producers. Energy that had been used by the primary consumers for growth and storage is thus absorbed into the secondary consumers through the process of digestion. As with primary consumers, secondary consumers convert this energy into a more suitable form (ATP) during respiration. Again, some energy is lost from the system, since energy which the primary consumers had used for respiration and regulation of body temperature cannot be utilized by the secondary consumers.</li>
<li>Tertiary consumers, which may or may not be <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a>, then consume the secondary consumers, with some energy passed on and some lost, as with the lower levels of the food chain.</li>
<li>A final link in the food chain are <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposers</a> which break down the <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> of the tertiary consumers (or whichever consumer is at the top of the chain) and release <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>. They also break down plants, herbivores and carnivores that were not eaten by organisms higher on the food chain, as well as the undigested food that is excreted by herbivores and carnivores. <a href="/wiki/Saprotrophic_nutrition" title="Saprotrophic nutrition">Saprotrophic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a> are decomposers, and play a pivotal role in the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycles</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The energy is passed on from <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> to trophic level and each time about 90% of the energy is lost, with some being lost as heat into the environment (an effect of <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a>) and some being lost as incompletely digested food (egesta). Therefore, primary consumers get about 10% of the energy produced by autotrophs, while secondary consumers get 1% and tertiary consumers get 0.1%. This means the top consumer of a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> receives the least energy, as a lot of the food chain's energy has been lost between trophic levels. This loss of energy at each level limits typical food chains to only four to six links.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Ecological_energetics">History of Ecological energetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_flow_(ecology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Ecological energetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecological energetics appears to have grown out of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> and the concerns of the <a href="/wiki/Physiocrats" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiocrats">Physiocrats</a>. It began in the works of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sergei_Podolinksy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sergei Podolinksy (page does not exist)">Sergei Podolinksy</a> in the late 1800s, and subsequently was developed by the Soviet ecologist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladmir_Stanchinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vladmir Stanchinsky (page does not exist)">Vladmir Stanchinsky</a>, the Austro-American <a href="/wiki/Alfred_J._Lotka" title="Alfred J. Lotka">Alfred J. Lotka</a>, and American limnologists, <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Lindeman" title="Raymond Lindeman">Raymond Lindeman</a> and <a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a>. It underwent substantial development by <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Howard T. Odum</a> and was applied by <a href="/wiki/Systems_ecology" title="Systems ecology">systems ecologists</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Radiation ecology (page does not exist)">radiation ecologists</a>.</p>
