<p><b>Intensive farming</b> or <b>intensive agriculture</b> is any of various types of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> that involve higher levels of input and output per unit of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">agricultural land</a> area. It is characterised by a low <a href="/wiki/Ley_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Ley farming">fallow</a> ratio, higher use of inputs such as <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour (economics)">labour</a>, and higher <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop yields</a> per unit land area.<sup id="cite_ref-britannicaRef_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannicaRef-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbcFactSheet_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcFactSheet-2">[2]</a></sup> This is in contrast to <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">traditional agriculture</a> in which the inputs per unit land are lower. The term "intensive" has various <a href="/wiki/Word_sense" title="Word sense">senses</a>, some of which refer to <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> methods (such as <a href="/wiki/Biointensive_agriculture" title="Biointensive agriculture">biointensive agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_intensive_gardening" title="French intensive gardening">French intensive gardening</a>) and others of which refer to nonorganic and industrial methods. <a href="/wiki/Intensive_animal_farming" title="Intensive animal farming">Intensive animal farming</a> involves either large numbers of animals raised on limited land, usually <a href="/wiki/Confined_animal_feeding_operation" class="mw-redirect" title="Confined animal feeding operation">confined animal feeding operations</a> (CAFO) often referred to as factory farms,<sup id="cite_ref-britannicaRef_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannicaRef-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-britannicaRefFactoryFarm_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannicaRefFactoryFarm-4">[4]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Managed_intensive_rotational_grazing" title="Managed intensive rotational grazing">managed intensive rotational grazing</a> (MIRG), which has both organic and non-organic types. Both increase the yields of food and fiber per acre as compared to traditional animal husbandry. In CAFO, feed is brought to the seldom-moved animals, while in MIRG the animals are repeatedly moved to fresh forage.</p>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a> agriculture is intensive in one or more ways. Forms that rely especially heavily on <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">industrial methods</a> are often called industrial agriculture, which is characterised by <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovations</a> designed to increase yield. Techniques include planting multiple crops per year, reducing the frequency of fallow years, and improving <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>. It also involves increased use of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, plant growth regulators, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mechanised_agriculture" title="Mechanised agriculture">mechanised agriculture</a>, controlled by increased and more detailed analysis of growing conditions, including weather, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, water, weeds and pests. This system is supported by ongoing innovation in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_machinery" title="Agricultural machinery">agricultural machinery</a> and farming methods, <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic technology</a>, techniques for achieving <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Data_collection" title="Data collection">data collection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_analysis" title="Data analysis">analysis</a> technology. Intensive farms are widespread in <a href="/wiki/Developed_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed nation">developed nations</a> and increasingly prevalent worldwide. Most of the <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruits" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruits">fruits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetables" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetables">vegetables</a> available in <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">supermarkets</a> are produced by such farms.</p>
<p>Smaller intensive farms usually include higher inputs of labor and more often use sustainable intensive methods. The farming practices commonly found on such farms are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a>. These farms are less widespread in both developed countries and worldwide, but are growing more rapidly. Most of the food available in specialty markets such as <a href="/wiki/Farmers_markets" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmers markets">farmers markets</a> is produced by these smallholder farms.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Agriculture#Modern_developments" title="Agriculture">Agriculture §&#160;Modern developments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_Agricultural_Revolution" title="British Agricultural Revolution">British Agricultural Revolution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Agriculture_(Plowing)_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg/220px-Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg/330px-Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg/440px-Agriculture_%28Plowing%29_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1379" data-file-height="969" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Agriculture_(Plowing)_CNE-v1-p58-H.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Early 20th-century image of a tractor ploughing an <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> field</div>

</div>
<p>Agricultural development in Britain between the 16th century and the mid-19th century saw a massive increase in agricultural productivity and net output. This in turn supported unprecedented population growth, freeing up a significant percentage of the workforce, and thereby helped enable the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Historians cited <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">enclosure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanised_agriculture" title="Mechanised agriculture">mechanization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Four-field_crop_rotation" class="mw-redirect" title="Four-field crop rotation">four-field crop rotation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a> as the most important innovations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Industrial agriculture arose along with the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. By the early 19th century, agricultural techniques, implements, seed stocks and <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> had so improved that yield per land unit was many times that seen in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kingsbury_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kingsbury-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The industrialization phase involved a continuing process of mechanization. Horse-drawn machinery such as the <a href="/wiki/McCormick_reaper" class="mw-redirect" title="McCormick reaper">McCormick reaper</a> revolutionized harvesting, while inventions such as the <a href="/wiki/Cotton_gin" title="Cotton gin">cotton gin</a> reduced the cost of processing. During this same period, farmers began to use <a href="/wiki/Traction_engine" title="Traction engine">steam-powered</a> <a href="/wiki/Threshing_machine" title="Threshing machine">threshers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tractor" title="Tractor">tractors</a>, although they were expensive and dangerous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1892, the first <a href="/wiki/Gasoline_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasoline engine">gasoline-powered</a> tractor was successfully developed, and in 1923, the <a href="/wiki/International_Harvester" title="International Harvester">International Harvester</a> <a href="/wiki/Farmall" title="Farmall">Farmall</a> tractor became the first all-purpose tractor, marking an inflection point in the replacement of draft animals with machines. Mechanical harvesters (<a href="/wiki/Combine_harvester" title="Combine harvester">combines</a>), planters, transplanters and other equipment were then developed, further revolutionizing agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These inventions increased yields and allowed individual farmers to manage increasingly large farms.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The identification of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> (NPK) as critical factors in plant growth led to the manufacture of synthetic <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, further increasing <a href="/wiki/Crop_yields" class="mw-redirect" title="Crop yields">crop yields</a>. In 1909 the <a href="/wiki/Haber-Bosch" class="mw-redirect" title="Haber-Bosch">Haber-Bosch</a> method to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> was first demonstrated. NPK fertilizers stimulated the first concerns about industrial agriculture, due to concerns that they came with serious side effects such as <a href="/wiki/Soil_compaction_(agriculture)" title="Soil compaction (agriculture)">soil compaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and declines in overall <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">soil fertility</a>, along with health concerns about toxic chemicals <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">entering the food supply</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stinner2007_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stinner2007-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The identification of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> as a critical factor in plant growth and <a href="/wiki/Soil_health" title="Soil health">soil health</a>, particularly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>, led to so-called <i>sustainable agriculture</i>, alternative forms of intensive agriculture that also surpass traditional agriculture, without side effects or health issues. Farmers adopting this approach were initially referred to as <i>humus farmers</i>, later as <i>organic farmers</i>.</p>
<p>The discovery of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> and their role in <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, in the first two decades of the 20th century, led to vitamin supplements, which in the 1920s allowed some livestock to be raised indoors, reducing their exposure to adverse natural elements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Chemicals developed for use in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> gave rise to synthetic <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>.</p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, synthetic fertilizer use increased rapidly,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> while sustainable intensive farming advanced much more slowly. Most of the resources in developed nations went to improving industrial intensive farming, and very little went to improving <a href="/wiki/Organic_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic farm">organic farming</a>. Thus, particularly in the developed nations, industrial intensive farming grew to become the dominant form of agriculture.</p>
<p>The discovery of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a> facilitated raising livestock in CAFOs by reducing diseases caused by crowding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Developments in logistics and refrigeration as well as processing technology made long-distance distribution feasible.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Between 1700 and 1980, "the total area of cultivated land worldwide increased 466%" and yields increased dramatically, particularly because of <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selectively bred</a> high-yielding varieties, fertilizers, pesticides, irrigation and machinery.<sup id="cite_ref-Matson1997_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matson1997-11">[11]</a></sup> Global agricultural production doubled between 1820 1920; between 1920 and 1950; between 1950 and 1965; and again between 1965 and 1975 to feed a global population that grew from one billion in 1800 to 6.5 billion in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-dominion_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dominion-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup> The number of people involved in farming in industrial countries dropped, from 24 percent of the American population to 1.5 percent in 2002. In 1940, each farmworker supplied 11 consumers, whereas in 2002, each worker supplied 90 consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-dominion_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dominion-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup> The number of farms also decreased and their ownership became more concentrated. In 2000 in the U.S., four companies produced 81 percent of cows, 73 percent of sheep, 57 percent of pigs, and 50 percent of chickens, cited as an example of "<a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">vertical integration</a>" by the president of the U.S. National Farmers' Union.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Between 1967 and 2002 the one million pig farms in America consolidated into 114,000<sup id="cite_ref-dominion_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dominion-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup> with 80 million pigs (out of 95 million) produced each year on factory farms, according to the U.S. National Pork Producers Council.<sup id="cite_ref-dominion_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dominion-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:29</sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a>, 74 percent of the world's poultry, 43 percent of beef, and 68 percent of eggs are produced this way.<sup id="cite_ref-WW2006_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WW2006-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:26</sup></p>
<p>Concerns over the <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> of industrial agriculture, which has become associated with <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">decreased</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil_quality" title="Soil quality">soil quality</a>, and over the environmental effects of fertilizers and pesticides, have not subsided. Alternatives such as <a href="/wiki/Integrated_pest_management" title="Integrated pest management">integrated pest management</a> (IPM) have had little impact because policies encourage the use of pesticides and IPM is knowledge-intensive.<sup id="cite_ref-Matson1997_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matson1997-11">[11]</a></sup> These concerns sustained the <a href="/wiki/Organic_movement" title="Organic movement">organic movement</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and caused a resurgence in sustainable intensive farming and funding for the development of appropriate technology.</p>
<p>Famines continued throughout the 20th century. Through the effects of climactic events, government policy, war and crop failure, millions of people died in each of at least ten famines between the 1920s and the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Confined_animal_feeding_operations">Confined animal feeding operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Confined animal feeding operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Intensive livestock farming, also called "factory farming" is a term referring to the process of raising <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> in confinement at high stocking density.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kaufmann_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufmann-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mc1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc1-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Concentrated_animal_feeding_operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentrated animal feeding operations">Concentrated animal feeding operations</a>" (CAFO) or "intensive livestock operations", can hold large numbers (some up to hundreds of thousands) of cows, hogs, turkeys or chickens, often indoors. The essence of such farms is the concentration of livestock in a given space. The aim is to provide maximum output at the lowest possible cost and with the greatest level of food safety.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The term is often used pejoratively.<sup id="cite_ref-encyc-organic_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-organic-23">[23]</a></sup> However, CAFOs have dramatically increased the production of food from animal husbandry worldwide, both in terms of total food produced and efficiency.</p>
<p>Food and water is delivered to the animals, and therapeutic use of antimicrobial agents, vitamin supplements and growth hormones are often employed. Growth hormones are not used on chickens nor on any animal in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. Undesirable behaviours often related to the stress of confinement led to a search for docile breeds (e.g., with natural dominance behaviours bred out), physical restraints to stop interaction, such as individual cages for chickens, or physically modification such as the de-beaking of chickens to reduce the harm of fighting.</p>
<p>The CAFO designation resulted from the 1972 US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Clean_Water_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Clean Water Act">Federal Clean Water Act</a>, which was enacted to protect and restore lakes and rivers to a "fishable, swimmable" quality. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) identified certain animal feeding operations, along with many other types of industry, as "point source" <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> polluters. These operations were subjected to regulation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 17 states in the U.S., isolated cases of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_contamination" class="mw-redirect" title="Groundwater contamination">groundwater contamination</a> were linked to CAFOs.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> For example, the ten million hogs in North Carolina generate 19 million tons of waste per year.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The U.S. federal government acknowledges the <a href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Waste disposal">waste disposal</a> issue and requires that <a href="/wiki/Animal_waste" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal waste">animal waste</a> be stored in <a href="/wiki/Manure_lagoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Manure lagoon">lagoons</a>. These lagoons can be as large as 7.5 acres (30,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>). Lagoons not protected with an impermeable liner can leak into groundwater under some conditions, as can runoff from manure used as fertilizer. A lagoon that burst in 1995 released 25 million gallons of nitrous sludge in North Carolina's New River. The spill allegedly killed eight to ten million fish.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The large concentration of animals, animal waste and dead animals in a small space poses ethical issues to some consumers. <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">Animal rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> activists have charged that intensive animal rearing is cruel to animals.</p>
<p>Other concerns include persistent noxious odor, the effects on human health and the role of antibiotics use in the rise of resistant infectious bacteria.</p>
<p>According to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC), farms on which animals are intensively reared can cause adverse health reactions in farm workers. Workers may develop acute and/or chronic lung disease, musculoskeletal injuries and may catch ( <a href="/wiki/Zoonotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoonotic">zoonotic</a>) infections from the animals.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Managed_intensive_rotational_grazing">Managed intensive rotational grazing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Managed intensive rotational grazing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Managed_intensive_rotational_grazing" title="Managed intensive rotational grazing">Managed intensive rotational grazing</a></div>
<p><b>Managed Intensive Rotational Grazing</b> (<b>MIRG</b>), also known as cell grazing, mob grazing and <a href="/wiki/Holistic_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic management">holistic managed planned grazing</a>, is a variety of forage use in which herds/flocks are regularly and systematically moved to fresh, rested grazing areas to maximize the quality and quantity of <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> growth. MIRG can be used with cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, chickens, turkeys, ducks and other animals. The herds graze one portion of pasture, or a paddock, while allowing the others to recover. Resting grazed lands allows the vegetation to renew energy reserves, rebuild shoot systems, and deepen root systems, resulting in long-term maximum <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> production.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> MIRG is especially effective because grazers thrive on the more tender younger plant stems. MIRG also leave parasites behind to die off minimizing or eliminating the need for de-wormers. Pasture systems alone can allow grazers to meet their energy requirements, and with the increased productivity of MIRG systems, the animals obtain the majority of their nutritional needs, in some cases all, without the supplemental feed sources that are required in continuous grazing systems or CAFOs.<sup id="cite_ref-Undersander.2C_D._2002_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Undersander.2C_D._2002-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crops">Crops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intensive_crop_farming" title="Intensive crop farming">Intensive crop farming</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a> transformed farming in many developing countries. It spread technologies that had already existed, but had not been widely used outside of industrialized nations. These technologies included "miracle seeds", pesticides, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and synthetic nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brown_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seeds">Seeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Seeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 1970s scientists created strains of <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> that are generally referred to as high-yielding varieties (HYV). HYVs have an increased nitrogen-absorbing potential compared to other varieties. Since cereals that absorbed extra nitrogen would typically lodge (fall over) before harvest, semi-dwarfing genes were bred into their genomes. <a href="/wiki/Norin_10_wheat" title="Norin 10 wheat">Norin 10 wheat</a>, a variety developed by <a href="/wiki/Orville_Vogel" title="Orville Vogel">Orville Vogel</a> from Japanese <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_wheat" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwarf wheat">dwarf wheat</a> varieties, was instrumental in developing wheat cultivars. IR8, the first widely implemented HYV rice to be developed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Rice_Research_Institute" title="International Rice Research Institute">International Rice Research Institute</a>, was created through a cross between an Indonesian variety named “Peta” and a Chinese variety named “Dee Geo Woo Gen.”<sup id="cite_ref-ir8_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ir8-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the availability of molecular genetics in <a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis" title="Arabidopsis">Arabidopsis</a> and rice the mutant genes responsible (<i>reduced height (rht)</i>, <i>gibberellin insensitive (gai1)</i> and <i>slender rice (slr1)</i>) have been cloned and identified as cellular signalling components of <a href="/wiki/Gibberellic_acid" title="Gibberellic acid">gibberellic acid</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Phytohormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Phytohormone">phytohormone</a> involved in regulating stem growth via its effect on cell division. Photosynthetic investment in the stem is reduced dramatically as the shorter plants are inherently more mechanically stable. Nutrients become redirected to grain production, amplifying in particular the yield effect of chemical fertilisers.</p>
<p>HYVs significantly outperform traditional varieties in the presence of adequate irrigation, pesticides and fertilizers. In the absence of these inputs, traditional varieties may outperform HYVs. They were developed as <a href="/wiki/F1_hybrids" class="mw-redirect" title="F1 hybrids">F1 hybrids</a>, meaning seeds need to be purchased every season to obtain maximum benefit, thus increasing costs.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crop_rotation">Crop rotation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Crop rotation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">Crop rotation</a></div>

<p>Crop rotation or crop sequencing is the practice of growing a series of dissimilar types of <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a> in the same space in sequential seasons for benefits such as avoiding pathogen and pest buildup that occurs when one species is continuously cropped. Crop rotation also seeks to balance the nutrient demands of various crops to avoid <a href="/wiki/Soil_nutrient" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil nutrient">soil nutrient</a> depletion. A traditional component of crop rotation is the replenishment of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> through the use of <a href="/wiki/Legumes" class="mw-redirect" title="Legumes">legumes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a> in sequence with cereals and other crops. Crop rotation can also improve <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">soil structure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertility (soil)">fertility</a> by alternating deep-rooted and shallow-rooted plants. One technique is to plant multi-species <a href="/wiki/Cover_crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Cover crops">cover crops</a> between commercial crops. This combines the advantages of intensive farming with continuous cover and <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Irrigation">Irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">Irrigation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/220px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/330px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/440px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Crop <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> accounts for 70% of the world's fresh water use.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Surface_irrigation" title="Surface irrigation">Flood irrigation</a>, the oldest and most common type, is typically unevenly distributed, as parts of a field may receive excess water in order to deliver sufficient quantities to other parts. <a href="/wiki/Irrigation#Overhead_.28sprinkler.29_irrigation" title="Irrigation">Overhead irrigation</a>, using center-pivot or lateral-moving sprinklers, gives a much more equal and controlled distribution pattern. <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">Drip irrigation</a> is the most expensive and least-used type, but delivers water to plant roots with minimal losses.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_catchment" class="mw-redirect" title="Water catchment">Water catchment</a> management measures include recharge pits, which capture rainwater and runoff and use it to recharge groundwater supplies. This helps in the replenishment of groundwater wells and eventually reduces soil erosion. Dammed rivers creating <a href="/wiki/Reservoirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Reservoirs">Reservoirs</a> store water for irrigation and other uses over large areas. Smaller areas sometimes use irrigation ponds or groundwater.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Weed_control">Weed control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Weed control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weed_control" title="Weed control">Weed control</a></div>
<p>In agriculture, systematic weed management is usually required, often performed by machines such as cultivators or liquid herbicide sprayers. <a href="/wiki/Herbicides" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbicides">Herbicides</a> kill specific targets while leaving the crop relatively unharmed. Some of these act by interfering with the growth of the weed and are often based on plant <a href="/wiki/Hormones" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormones">hormones</a>. <a href="/wiki/Weed_control" title="Weed control">Weed control</a> through <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a> is made more difficult when the weeds become resistant to the herbicide. Solutions include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cover crops (especially those with <a href="/wiki/Allelopathic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allelopathic">allelopathic</a> properties) that out-compete weeds or inhibit their regeneration.</li>
<li>Multiple herbicides, in combination or in rotation</li>
<li>Strains genetically engineered for herbicide tolerance</li>
<li>Locally adapted strains that tolerate or out-compete weeds</li>
<li>Tilling</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ground_cover" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground cover">Ground cover</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a> or plastic</li>
<li>Manual removal</li>
<li>Mowing</li>
<li>Grazing</li>
<li>Burning</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Terracing">Terracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Terracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terrace_(agriculture)" title="Terrace (agriculture)">Terrace (agriculture)</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Terrace_(agriculture)" title="Terrace (agriculture)">terrace</a> is a leveled section of a <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hilly</a> cultivated area, designed as a method of <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a> to slow or prevent the rapid <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> water. Often such land is formed into multiple terraces, giving a stepped appearance. The human landscapes of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> cultivation in terraces that follow the natural contours of the escarpments like <a href="/wiki/Contour_ploughing" class="mw-redirect" title="Contour ploughing">contour ploughing</a> is a classic feature of the island of <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Banaue_Rice_Terraces" title="Banaue Rice Terraces">Banaue Rice Terraces</a> in <a href="/wiki/Banaue,_Ifugao" class="mw-redirect" title="Banaue, Ifugao">Banaue, Ifugao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tahuantinsuyu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tahuantinsuyu">Inca</a> made use of otherwise unusable slopes by <a href="/wiki/Drystone_wall" class="mw-redirect" title="Drystone wall">drystone walling</a> to create terraces.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rice_paddies">Rice paddies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rice paddies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">Paddy field</a></div>
<p>A <b>paddy field</b> is a flooded parcel of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> used for growing <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">semiaquatic crops</a>. Paddy fields are a typical feature of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>-growing countries of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">east</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a> including <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. They are also found in other rice-growing regions such as <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> (Italy), the <a href="/wiki/Camargue" title="Camargue">Camargue</a> (France) and the <a href="/wiki/Artibonite_Valley" title="Artibonite Valley">Artibonite Valley</a> (Haiti). They can occur naturally along <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marshes" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshes">marshes</a>, or can be constructed, even on hillsides. They require large water quantities for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, much of it from flooding. It gives an environment favourable to the strain of rice being grown, and is hostile to many <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a>. As the only <a href="/wiki/Draft_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Draft animal">draft animal</a> species which is comfortable in <a href="/wiki/Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Water_buffalo" title="Water buffalo">water buffalo</a> is in widespread use in Asian rice paddies.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Paddy-based rice-farming has been practiced in Korea since ancient times. A <a href="/wiki/Pit-house" title="Pit-house">pit-house</a> at the Daecheon-ni archaeological site yielded carbonized rice grains and radiocarbon dates indicating that rice cultivation may have begun as early as the Middle <a href="/wiki/Jeulmun_Pottery_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeulmun Pottery Period">Jeulmun Pottery Period</a> (c. 3500-2000 BC) in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The earliest rice cultivation there may have used dry-fields instead of paddies.</p>
<p>The earliest <a href="/wiki/Mumun" class="mw-redirect" title="Mumun">Mumun</a> features were usually located in naturally swampy, low-lying narrow gulleys and fed by local streams. Some Mumun paddies in flat areas were made of a series of squares and rectangles separated by bunds approximately 10&#160;cm in height, while terraced paddies consisted of long irregularly shapes that followed natural contours of the land at various levels.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like today's, Mumun period rice farmers used terracing, bunds, canals and small reservoirs. Some paddy-farming techniques of the Middle Mumun (c. 850-550 BC) can be interpreted from the well-preserved wooden tools excavated from archaeological rice paddies at the Majeon-ni Site. However, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> tools for paddy-farming were not introduced until sometime after 200 BC. The spatial scale of individual paddies, and thus entire paddy-fields, increased with the regular use of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">iron</a> tools in the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms of Korea</a> Period (c. AD 300/400-668).</p>
<p>A recent development in the intensive production of rice is <a href="/wiki/System_of_Rice_Intensification" title="System of Rice Intensification">System of Rice Intensification</a> (SRI). Developed in 1983 by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">Father</a> <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Laulanie" title="Henri de Laulanie">Henri de Laulanié</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-henri_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-henri-37">[37]</a></sup> by 2013 the number of smallholder farmers using SRI had grown to between 4 and 5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aquaculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</a></div>
<p>Aquaculture is the cultivation of the natural products of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> and other aquatic organisms). Intensive aquaculture takes place on land using tanks, ponds or other controlled systems or in the ocean, using cages.<sup id="cite_ref-AmericanHeritageDef_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanHeritageDef-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McGrawHillDef_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrawHillDef-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_intensive_farming">Sustainable intensive farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sustainable intensive farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable farming">Sustainable farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Multi-Trophic_Aquaculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture">Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste_agriculture" title="Zero waste agriculture">Zero waste agriculture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">Organic farming</a></div>
<p>Sustainable intensive farming practises have been developed to slow the deterioration of agricultural land and even regenerate <a href="/wiki/Soil_health" title="Soil health">soil health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, while still offering high yields. Most of these developments fall in the category of organic farming, or the integration of organic and conventional agriculture.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Organic systems and the practices that make them effective are being picked up more and more by conventional agriculture and will become the foundation for future farming systems. They won't be called organic, because they'll still use some chemicals and still use some fertilizers, but they'll function much more like today's organic systems than today's conventional systems."</p>
<p>Dr. Charles Benbrook Executive director US House Agriculture Subcommittee Director Agricultural Board - National Academy Sciences (FMR)</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_System_of_Crop_Intensification&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The System of Crop Intensification (page does not exist)">The System of Crop Intensification</a> (SCI) was born out of research primarily at Cornell University and smallholder farms in India on SRI. It uses the SRI concepts and methods for rice and applies them to crops like wheat, sugarcane, finger millet, and others. It can be 100% organic, or integrated with reduced conventional inputs.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Holistic_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic management">Holistic management</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Systems_thinking" title="Systems thinking">systems thinking</a> approach that was originally developed for reversing <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-greenbiz_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenbiz-43">[43]</a></sup> Holistic planned grazing is similar to rotational grazing but differs in that it more explicitly provides a framework for adapting to four basic ecosystem processes: the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-dx_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-44">[44]</a></sup> the mineral cycle including the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Energy_flow_(ecology)" title="Energy flow (ecology)">energy flow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological community">community</a> dynamics (the relationship between organisms in an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> as equal in importance to livestock production and social welfare. By intensively managing the behavior and movement of livestock, holistic planned grazing simultaneously increases stocking rates and restores grazing land.<sup id="cite_ref-dx_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pasture cropping plants grain crops directly into grassland without first applying herbicides. The perennial grasses form a living mulch understory to the grain crop, eliminating the need to plant cover crops after harvest. The pasture is intensively grazed both before and after grain production using holistic planned grazing. This intensive system yields equivalent farmer profits (partly from increased livestock forage) while building new <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestering</a> up to 33 tons of CO2/ha/year.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Twelve Aprils grazing program for dairy production, developed in partnership with <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a>-SARE, is similar to pasture cropping, but the crops planted into the perennial pasture are forage crops for dairy herds. This system improves milk production and is more sustainable than confinement dairy production.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Integrated_Multi-Trophic_Aquaculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture">Integrated Multi-Trophic Aquaculture</a> (IMTA) is an example of a holistic approach. IMTA is a practice in which the by-products (wastes) from one species are recycled to become inputs (<a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>) for another. Fed <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>) is combined with inorganic extractive (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>) and organic extractive (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>) aquaculture to create balanced systems for environmental sustainability (biomitigation), economic stability (product diversification and risk reduction) and social acceptability (better management practices).<sup id="cite_ref-Chopin_et_al._2001_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chopin_et_al._2001-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biointensive" class="mw-redirect" title="Biointensive">Biointensive</a> agriculture focuses on maximizing efficiency<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> such as per unit area, energy input and water input. <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">Agroforestry</a> combines agriculture and orchard/forestry technologies to create more integrated, diverse, productive, profitable, healthy and sustainable land-use systems.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">Intercropping</a> can increase yields or reduce inputs and thus represents (potentially sustainable) agricultural intensification. However, while total yield per acre is often increased dramatically, yields of any single crop often diminish. There are also challenges to farmers relying on farming equipment optimized for <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a>, often resulting in increased labor inputs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vertical_farming" title="Vertical farming">Vertical farming</a> is intensive crop production on a large scale in urban centers in multi-story, artificially-lit structures that uses far less inputs and produces fewer environmental impacts.</p>
<p>An integrated farming system is a progressive biologically integrated <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> system such as IMTA or <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste_agriculture" title="Zero waste agriculture">Zero waste agriculture</a> whose implementation requires exacting knowledge of the interactions of multiple species and whose benefits include sustainability and increased profitability. Elements of this integration can include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intentionally introducing flowering plants into agricultural ecosystems to increase pollen-and nectar-resources required by natural enemies of insect pests<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>Using crop rotation and cover crops to suppress nematodes in potatoes<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_policy" title="Agricultural policy">Agricultural policy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">Agribusiness</a></div>
<p>The challenges and issues of industrial agriculture for society, for the industrial agriculture sector, for the individual farm, and for <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> include the costs and benefits of both current practices and proposed changes to those practices.<sup id="cite_ref-challenges_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-challenges-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-information_management_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-information_management-59">[59]</a></sup> This is a continuation of thousands of years of invention in feeding ever growing populations.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>[W]hen hunter-gatherers with growing populations depleted the stocks of game and wild foods across the Near East, they were forced to introduce agriculture. But agriculture brought much longer hours of work and a less rich diet than hunter-gatherers enjoyed. Further population growth among shifting slash-and-burn farmers led to shorter fallow periods, falling yields and soil erosion. Plowing and fertilizers were introduced to deal with these problems - but once again involved longer hours of work and degradation of soil resources(Boserup, The Conditions of Agricultural Growth, Allen and Unwin, 1965, expanded and updated in Population and Technology, Blackwell, 1980.).</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>While the point of industrial agriculture is to profitably supply the world at the lowest cost, industrial methods have significant side effects. Further, industrial agriculture is not an indivisible whole, but instead is composed of multiple elements, each of which can be modified in response to market conditions, government regulation and further innovation and has its own side-effects. Various interest groups reach different conclusions on the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-challenges_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-challenges-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-information_management_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-information_management-59">[59]</a></sup></p>

<p>An example of industrial agriculture providing cheap and plentiful food is the U.S.'s "most successful program of agricultural development of any country in the world". Between 1930 and 2000 U.S. agricultural productivity (output divided by all inputs) rose by an average of about 2 percent annually causing food prices to decrease. "The percentage of U.S. disposable income spent on food prepared at home decreased, from 22 percent as late as 1950 to 7 percent by the end of the century."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intensive_farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_agriculture" title="Environmental impact of agriculture">Environmental impact of agriculture</a></div>
<p>Industrial agriculture uses huge amounts of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_energy_resources_and_consumption" class="mw-redirect" title="World energy resources and consumption">energy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">industrial chemicals</a>; increasing <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Land_pollution" class="mw-redirect" title="Land pollution">arable land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">usable water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">atmosphere</a>. <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">Herbicides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> are accumulating in <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">ground</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface waters</a>. "Many of the negative effects of industrial agriculture are remote from fields and farms. Nitrogen compounds from the Midwest, for example, travel down the Mississippi to degrade coastal fisheries in the Gulf of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> But other adverse effects are showing up within agricultural production systems -- for example, the rapidly developing resistance among pests is rendering our arsenal of herbicides and insecticides increasingly ineffective.".<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Agrochemical" title="Agrochemical">Agrochemicals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> have been implicated in <a href="/wiki/Colony_Collapse_Disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Colony Collapse Disorder">Colony Collapse Disorder</a>, in which the individual members of bee colonies disappear.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Agricultural production is highly dependent on bees to <a href="/wiki/Pollinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollinate">pollinate</a> many varieties of fruits and vegetables.</p>
