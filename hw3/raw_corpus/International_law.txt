			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">International law</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg/230px-Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg" width="230" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg/345px-Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg/460px-Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Original_Geneva_Conventions.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/First_Geneva_Convention" title="First Geneva Convention">First Geneva Convention</a> (1864) is one of the earliest formulations of international law</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>International law</b> is the set of rules generally regarded and accepted as binding in relations between <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">states</a> and between <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-definition_.E2.80.93_international_law_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-definition_.E2.80.93_international_law-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It serves as a framework for the practice of stable and organized international relations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> International law differs from state-based <a href="/wiki/Legal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal system">legal systems</a> in that it is primarily applicable to countries rather than to private citizens. National law may become international law when <a href="/wiki/Treaties" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaties">treaties</a> delegate national jurisdiction to <a href="/wiki/Supranational" class="mw-redirect" title="Supranational">supranational</a> tribunals such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> or the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a>. Treaties such as the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">Geneva Conventions</a> may require national law to conform to respective parts.</p>
<p>Much of international law is consent-based governance. This means that a state member is not obliged to abide by this type of international law, unless it has expressly consented to a particular course of conduct.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This is an issue of <a href="/wiki/State_sovereignty" class="mw-redirect" title="State sovereignty">state sovereignty</a>. However, other aspects of international law are not consent-based but still are obligatory upon state and <a href="/wiki/Non-state_actor" title="Non-state actor">non-state actors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a> and peremptory norms (<i><a href="/wiki/Peremptory_norm" title="Peremptory norm">jus cogens</a></i>).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "international law" can refer to three distinct legal disciplines:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" title="Public international law">Public international law</a>, which governs the relationship between states and international entities. It includes these legal fields: <a href="/wiki/Treaty_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty law">treaty law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_of_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of sea">law of sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_criminal_law" title="International criminal law">international criminal law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of war">laws of war</a> or <a href="/wiki/International_humanitarian_law" title="International humanitarian law">international humanitarian law</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_law" title="International human rights law">international human rights law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Refugee_law" title="Refugee law">refugee law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Private international law, or <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_laws" title="Conflict of laws">conflict of laws</a>, which addresses the questions of (1) which jurisdiction may hear a case, and (2) the law concerning which jurisdiction applies to the issues in the case.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Supranational_law" title="Supranational law">Supranational law</a> or the law of <a href="/wiki/Supranational" class="mw-redirect" title="Supranational">supranational</a> organizations, which concerns regional agreements where the laws of nation states may be held inapplicable when conflicting with a supranational legal system when that nation has a <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> obligation to a supranational <a href="/wiki/Collective" title="Collective">collective</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">Branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two traditional branches of international law are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Jus_gentium" title="Jus gentium">jus gentium</a></i> – law of nations</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Jus_inter_gentes" title="Jus inter gentes">jus inter gentes</a></i> – agreements between nations.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alberico_Gentili.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Alberico_Gentili.jpg/250px-Alberico_Gentili.jpg" width="250" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Alberico_Gentili.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="368" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alberico_Gentili.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> thinker <a href="/wiki/Alberico_Gentili" title="Alberico Gentili">Sir Alberico Gentili</a> is acknowledged to be the founder of the science of international law<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The modern study of international law starts in the early 19th century, but its origins go back at least to the 16th century, and <a href="/wiki/Alberico_Gentili" title="Alberico Gentili">Alberico Gentili</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Vitoria" title="Francisco de Vitoria">Francisco de Vitoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>, the "fathers of international law."<sup id="cite_ref-Jr.2012_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jr.2012-9">[9]</a></sup> Several legal systems developed in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including the codified systems of continental European states and <a href="/wiki/English_common_law" class="mw-redirect" title="English common law">English common law</a>, based on decisions by judges and not by written codes. Other areas developed differing legal systems, with the Chinese legal tradition dating back more than four thousand years, although at the end of the 19th century, there was still no written code for civil proceedings.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some doubt the effectiveness of international law, as they see the implementation of international law as a policy option among others to tackle global dilemmas.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> They say that international law must be evaluated with other, possibly more effective, international law options.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_international_law">Sources of international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sources of international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sources_of_international_law" title="Sources of international law">Sources of international law</a></div>
<p>International law is sourced from decision makers and researchers looking to verify the substantive legal rule governing a legal dispute or academic discourse. The <a href="/wiki/Sources_of_international_law" title="Sources of international law">sources of international law</a> applied by the community of nations to find the content of international law are listed under Article 38.1 of the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_the_International_Court_of_Justice" title="Statute of the International Court of Justice">Statute of the International Court of Justice</a>: <a href="/wiki/Treaties" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaties">Treaties</a>, customs, and general principles are stated as the three primary sources; and judicial decisions and scholarly writings are expressly designated as the subsidiary sources of international law. Many scholars agree that the fact that the sources are arranged sequentially in the Article 38 of the ICJ Statute suggests an implicit hierarchy of sources.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, there is no concrete evidence, in the decisions of the international courts and tribunals, to support such strict hierarchy, at least when it is about choosing international customs and treaties. In addition, unlike the Article 21 of the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, which clearly defines hierarchy of applicable law (or sources of international law), the language of the Article 38 do not explicitly support hierarchy of sources.</p>
<p>The sources have been influenced by a range of political and legal theories. During the 20th century, it was recognized by legal <a href="/w/index.php?title=Positivists&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Positivists (page does not exist)">positivists</a> that a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> could limit its authority to act by consenting to an agreement according to the principle <i><a href="/wiki/Pacta_sunt_servanda" title="Pacta sunt servanda">pacta sunt servanda</a></i>. This consensual view of international law was reflected in the 1920 Statute of the Permanent Court of International Justice, which was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a> and is preserved in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Article 7 of the 1946 Statute of the International Court of Justice.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_international_law">Types of international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types of international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_international_law">Public international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" title="Public international law">Public international law</a></div>
<p>Public international law (or international public law) concerns the <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> relationships between the nations and persons which are considered the subjects of international law. Norms of international law have their source in either:</p>
<ol>
<li>custom, or <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a> (consistent state practice accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Opinio_juris" class="mw-redirect" title="Opinio juris">opinio juris</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>globally accepted standards of behavior (peremptory norms known as <a href="/wiki/Jus_cogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Jus cogens">jus cogens</a> or ius cogens), or</li>
<li>codifications contained in conventional agreements, generally termed <a href="/wiki/Treaties" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaties">treaties</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>Article 13 of the United Nations Charter obligates the <a href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="UN General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> to initiate studies and make recommendations which encourage the progressive development of international law and its codification. Evidence of consensus or state practice can sometimes be derived from intergovernmental resolutions or academic and expert legal opinions (sometimes collectively termed <a href="/wiki/Soft_law" title="Soft law">soft law</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_international_law">Private international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Private international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_laws" title="Conflict of laws">Conflict of laws</a></div>
<p>Conflict of laws, often called "private international law" in <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> jurisdictions, is distinguished from public international law because it governs conflicts between private persons rather than states (or other international bodies with standing). It concerns the questions of which jurisdiction should be permitted to hear a legal dispute between private parties, and <a href="/wiki/Choice_of_law" title="Choice of law">which jurisdiction's law should be applied</a>, therefore raising issues of international law. Today <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a> are increasingly capable of shifting capital and labor <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chains</a> across borders, as well as trading with overseas corporations. This increases the number of disputes of an inter-state nature outside a unified legal framework, and raises issues of the enforceability of standard practices. Increasing numbers of businesses use commercial <a href="/wiki/Arbitration" title="Arbitration">arbitration</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Recognition_and_Enforcement_of_Foreign_Arbitral_Awards" title="Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards"><i>New York Convention 1958</i></a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supranational_law">Supranational law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Supranational law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supranational_law" title="Supranational law">Supranational law</a></div>
<p>Systems of "supranational law" arise when nations explicitly cede their right to make certain judicial decisions to a common tribunal.<sup id="cite_ref-Degan1997_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degan1997-16">[16]</a></sup> The decisions of the common tribunal are directly effective in each party nation, and have priority over decisions taken by national courts.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanpain2010_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanpain2010-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> is an example of an international treaty organization which implements a supranational legal framework, with the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> having supremacy over all member-nation courts in matter of <a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">European Union law</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_courts">International courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are numerous international bodies created by treaties adjudicating on legal issues where they may have jurisdiction. The only one claiming <a href="/wiki/Universal_jurisdiction" title="Universal jurisdiction">universal jurisdiction</a> is the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a>. Others are: the United Nations <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a> (when national systems have totally failed and the <a href="/wiki/Rome_Statute_of_the_International_Criminal_Court" title="Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court">Treaty of Rome</a> is applicable) and the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Arbitration_for_Sport" title="Court of Arbitration for Sport">Court of Arbitration for Sport</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Africa_Community">East Africa Community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: East Africa Community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/East_African_Community" title="East African Community">East African Community</a></div>
<p>There were ambitions to make the East African Community, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, a political federation with its own form of binding supranational law, but this effort has not materialized.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Union_of_South_American_Nations">Union of South American Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Union of South American Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_American_Nations" title="Union of South American Nations">Union of South American Nations</a></div>
<p>The Union of South American Nations serves the South American continent. It intends to establish a framework akin to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> by the end of 2019. It is envisaged to have its own passport and currency, and limit barriers to trade.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Andean_Community_of_Nations">Andean Community of Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Andean Community of Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Andean_Community_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Andean Community of Nations">Andean Community of Nations</a></div>
<p>The Andean Community of Nations is the first attempt to integrate the countries of the <a href="/wiki/Andes_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Andes Mountains">Andes Mountains</a> in South America. It started with the Cartagena Agreement of 26 May 1969, and consists of four countries: <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. The Andean Community follows supranational laws, called Agreements, which are mandatory for these countries.</p>
