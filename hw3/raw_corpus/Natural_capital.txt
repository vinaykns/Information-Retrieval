<p><b>Natural capital</b> is the world's stock of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, which includes geology, soils, air, water and all living organisms. Natural capital assets provide people with a wide range of free goods and services, often called <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, which underpin our economy and society and some of which even make human life possible.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is an extension of the economic notion of <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> (resources which enable the production of more resources) to goods and services provided by the natural environment. For example, a well-maintained forest or river may provide an indefinitely sustainable flow of new trees or fish, whereas over-use of those resources may lead to a permanent decline in timber availability or fish stocks. Natural capital also provides people with essential services, like water catchment, erosion control and crop pollination by insects, which in turn ensure the long-term viability of other natural resources. Since the continuous supply of services from the available natural capital assets is dependent upon a healthy, functioning environment, the structure and diversity of habitats and ecosystems are important components of natural capital.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Methods, called 'natural capital asset checks', help decision-makers understand how changes in the current and future performance of natural capital assets will impact on human well-being and the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural capital is one approach to <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">ecosystem valuation</a> which revolves around the idea, in contrast to traditional economics, that non-human life produces essential resources. Thus, ecological health is essential to the sustainability of the economy. In <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism:_Creating_the_Next_Industrial_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution">Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the author claims that the global economy is within a larger economy of natural resources and ecosystem services that sustain us. In order to continue to reap the benefits of our natural environment, we need to recognize the importance of natural capital within the economy. According to the authors, the "next industrial revolution" depends on the espousal of four central strategies: "the conservation of resources through more effective <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> processes, the reuse of materials as found in natural systems, a change in values from quantity to quality, and investing in natural capital, or restoring and sustaining <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-nat_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nat-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a traditional economic analysis of the factors of production, natural capital would usually be classified as "land" distinct from traditional "capital". The historical distinction between "land" and "capital" defined “land” as naturally occurring with a fixed supply, whereas “capital”, as originally defined referred only to man-made goods. (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Georgism" title="Georgism">Georgism</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>) It is however, misleading to view "land" as if its productive capacity is fixed, because natural capital can be improved or degraded by the actions of man over time (see <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>). Moreover, natural capital yields benefits and goods, such as timber or food, which can be harvested by humans. These benefits are similar to those realized by owners of infrastructural capital which yields more goods, such as a factory which produces automobiles just as an apple tree produces apples.</p>
<p>The term 'natural capital' was first used in 1973 by <a href="/wiki/E.F._Schumacher" class="mw-redirect" title="E.F. Schumacher">E.F. Schumacher</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and is closely identified with <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_2" title="Biosphere 2">Biosphere 2</a> project, and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism" title="Natural Capitalism">Natural Capitalism</a> economic model of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hawken" title="Paul Hawken">Paul Hawken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">Amory Lovins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Lovins" title="Hunter Lovins">Hunter Lovins</a>. Recently, it has begun to be used by politicians, notably Ralph Nader, Paul Martin Jr., and agencies of the UK government, including its <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capital_Committee" title="Natural Capital Committee">Natural Capital Committee</a> and the London Health Observatory. All users of the term currently differentiate natural from man-made or infrastructural capital in some way. Indicators adopted by United Nations Environment Programme's <a href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Monitoring_Centre" title="World Conservation Monitoring Centre">World Conservation Monitoring Centre</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD) to measure natural biodiversity use the term in a slightly more specific way. According to the OECD, natural capital is “natural assets in their role of providing natural resource inputs and environmental services for economic production” and is “generally considered to comprise three principal categories: natural resources stocks, land, and ecosystems.”</p>
<p>Within the international community the basic principle is not controversial, although much uncertainty exists over how best to value different aspects of ecological health, natural capital and ecosystem services. <a href="/wiki/Environmental_full-cost_accounting" title="Environmental full-cost accounting">Full-cost accounting</a>, triple bottom line, measuring well-being and other proposals for accounting reform often include suggestions to measure an "ecological deficit" or "natural deficit" alongside a social and financial deficit. It is difficult to measure such a deficit without some agreement on methods of valuation and auditing of at least the global forms of natural capital (e.g. value of air, water, soil).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Ecologists are teaming up with economists to measure and express values of the wealth of ecosystems as a way of finding solutions to the biodiversity crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards98_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards98-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Naidoo09_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Naidoo09-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zhoua09_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhoua09-14">[14]</a></sup> Some researchers have attempted to place a dollar figure on ecosystem services such as the value that the Canadian boreal forest's contribution to global ecosystem services. If ecologically intact, the boreal forest has an estimated value of US$3.7 trillion. The boreal forest ecosystem is one of the planet's great atmospheric regulators and it stores more carbon than any other biome on the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-Jonsson09_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jonsson09-15">[15]</a></sup> The annual value for ecological services of the Boreal Forest is estimated at US$93.2 billion, or 2.5 greater than the annual value of resource extraction. The economic value of 17 ecosystem services for the entire biosphere (calculated in 1997) has an estimated average value of US$33 trillion per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Costanza97_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costanza97-16">[16]</a></sup> These ecological economic values are not currently included in calculations of national income accounts, the GDP and they have no price attributes because they exist mostly outside of the global markets.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson06_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson06-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Anielski05_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anielski05-18">[18]</a></sup> The loss of natural capital continues to accelerate and goes undetected or ignored by mainstream monetary analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-Wakernagel97_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wakernagel97-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_capital_declaration">Natural capital declaration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural capital declaration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 2012 a 'national capital declaration' (NCD) was launched at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Sustainable_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development">Rio+20</a> summit held in Brazil. An initiative of the global finance sector, it was signed by 40 <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEOs</a> to 'integrate natural capital considerations into loans, equity, fixed income and insurance products, as well as in accounting, disclosure and reporting frameworks.' They worked with supporting organisations to develop tools and metrics to integrate natural capital factors into existing business structures.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In summary, its four key aims are to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Increase understanding of business dependency on natural capital assets;</li>
<li>Support development of tools to integrate natural capital considerations into the decision-making process of all financial products and services;</li>
<li>Help build a global consensus on integrating natural capital into private sector accounting and decision-making;</li>
<li>Encourage a consensus on <a href="/wiki/Integrated_reporting" title="Integrated reporting">integrated reporting</a> to include natural capital as one of the key components of an organisation's success.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_Capital_Protocol">Natural Capital Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural Capital Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2016, the Natural Capital Coalition <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> released the Natural Capital Protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Protocol provides a standardised framework for organisations to identify, measure and value their direct and indirect impacts and dependencies on natural capital. The Protocol harmonises existing tools and methodologies, and guides organisations towards the information they need to make strategic and operational decisions that include impacts and dependencies on natural capital.</p>
<p>The Protocol was developed in a unique collaboration between 38 organisations who signed voluntary, pre-competitive contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Protocol is available on a creative commons license and is free for organisations to apply.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internationally_agreed_standard">Internationally agreed standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Internationally agreed standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental-economic accounts provide the conceptual framework for integrated statistics on the environment and its relationship with the economy, including the impacts of the economy on the environment and the contribution of the environment to the economy. A coherent set of indicators and descriptive statistics can be derived from the accounts that inform a wide range of policies, including, but not limited to, <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a>/<a href="/wiki/Green_growth" title="Green growth">green growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>. The <b>System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA)</b> contains the internationally agreed standard concepts, definitions, classifications, accounting rules and tables for producing internationally comparable statistics on the environment and its relationship with the economy. The SEEA is a flexible system in the sense that its implementation can be adapted to countries' specific situations and priorities. Coordination of the implementation of the SEEA and on-going work on new methodological developments is managed and supervised by the UN Committee of Experts on Environmental-Economic Accounting (UNCEEA). The final, official version of the SEEA Central Framework was published in February 2014.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whilst measuring the components of natural capital in any region is a relatively straightforward process, both the task and the rationale of putting a monetary valuation on them, or on the value of the goods and services they freely give us, has proved more contentious.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Within the UK, <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a> columnist, <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a>, has been critical of the work of the government's <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capital_Committee" title="Natural Capital Committee">Natural Capital Committee</a> and of other attempts to place any sort of monetary value on natural capital assets, or on the free <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> they provide us with. In a speech referring to a report to government which suggested that better protection of the UK's freshwater ecosystems would yield an enhancement in aesthetic value of £700m, he derided attempts 'to compare things which cannot be directly compared'.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He went on to say:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>These figures, ladies and gentlemen, are marmalade. They are finely shredded, boiled to a pulp, heavily sweetened ... and still indigestible. In other words they are total gibberish.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— G. Monbiot</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Others have defended efforts to integrate the valuation of natural capital into local and national economic decision-making, arguing that it puts the environment on a more balanced footing when weighed against other commercial pressures, and that 'valuation' of those assets is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Monetisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Monetisation">monetisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
