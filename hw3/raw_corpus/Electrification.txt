			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Electrification</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">This article is about the system to generate, transmit, distribute and use electricity. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Electrification_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Electrification (disambiguation)">Electrification (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Electrification</b> is the process of powering by <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and, in many contexts, the introduction of such power by changing over from an earlier power source. The broad meaning of the term, such as in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_technology" title="History of technology">history of technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">economic history</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>, usually applies to a region or national economy. Broadly speaking, electrification was the build-out of the <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">electric power distribution</a> systems that occurred in Britain, the United States, and other now-<a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed</a> countries from the mid-1880s until around 1950 and is still in progress in rural areas in some <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>. This included the change over from <a href="/wiki/Line_shaft" title="Line shaft">line shaft</a> and belt drive using <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">water power</a> to <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Devine83_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devine83-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nye_1990_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nye_1990-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The electrification of particular sectors of the economy is called by terms such as <a href="/wiki/Mass_production#Factory_electrification" title="Mass production"><i>factory electrification</i></a>, <i>household electrification</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">rural electrification</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">railway electrification</a></i>. It may also apply to changing industrial processes such as smelting, melting, separating or refining from coal or coke heating, or chemical processes to some type of electric process such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_induction" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric induction">electric induction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">resistance</a> heating, or <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> or electrolytic separating.</p>
<p>Electrification was called "the greatest engineering achievement of the 20th Century" by the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Constable1999_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constable1999-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_electrification">History of electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">Electricity</a></div>
<p>The earliest commercial uses of electricity were <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">telegraph</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_magnetos.2C_dynamos_and_generators">Development of magnetos, dynamos and generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of magnetos, dynamos and generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">Electric generator</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Faraday_disk_generator.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/220px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/330px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/440px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg 2x" data-file-width="487" data-file-height="382" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Faraday_disk_generator.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Faraday disk, the first electric generator. The horseshoe-shaped magnet <i>(A)</i> created a magnetic field through the disk <i>(D)</i>. When the disk was turned, this induced an electric current radially outward from the center toward the rim. The current flowed out through the sliding spring contact <i>m</i>, through the external circuit, and back into the center of the disk through the axle.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the years of 1831–1832, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> discovered the operating principle of electromagnetic generators. The principle, later called <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">Faraday's law</a>, is that an <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">electromotive force</a> is generated in an electrical conductor that is subjected to a varying <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a>, as for example, a wire moving through a magnetic field. He also built the first electromagnetic generator, called the <a href="/wiki/Faraday_disk" class="mw-redirect" title="Faraday disk">Faraday disk</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Homopolar_generator" title="Homopolar generator">homopolar generator</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> disc rotating between the poles of a horseshoe <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a>. It produced a small DC voltage.</p>
<p>Around 1832, <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Pixii" title="Hippolyte Pixii">Hippolyte Pixii</a> improved the magneto by using a wire wound horseshoe, with the extra coils of conductor generating more current, but it was AC. <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="André-Marie Ampère">André-Marie Ampère</a> suggested a means of converting current from Pixii's magneto to DC using a rocking switch. Later segmented commutators were used to produce direct current.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Fothergill_Cooke" title="William Fothergill Cooke">William Fothergill Cooke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">Charles Wheatstone</a> developed a telegraph around 1838-40. In 1840 Wheatstone was using a magneto that he developed to power the telegraph. Wheatstone and Cooke made an important improvement in electrical generation by using a battery-powered electromagnet in place of a permanent magnet, which they patented in 1845.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The self-excited magnetic field dynamo did away with the battery to power electromagnets. This type of dynamo was made by several people in 1866.</p>
<p>The first practical generator, the <a href="/wiki/Gramme_machine" title="Gramme machine">Gramme machine</a> was made by Z.T Gramme, who sold many of these machines in the 1870s. British engineer <a href="/wiki/R._E._B._Crompton" title="R. E. B. Crompton">R. E. B. Crompton</a> improved the generator to allow better air cooling and made other mechanical improvements. Compound winding, which gave more stable voltage with load, improved operating characteristics of generators.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The improvements in electrical generation technology increased the efficiency and reliability greatly in the 19th century. The first magnetos only converted a few percent of mechanical energy to electricity. By the end of the 19th century the highest efficiencies were over 90%.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_lighting">Electric lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electric lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arc_lighting">Arc lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arc lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yablochkov_candles_illuminating_Avenue_de_l%27Opera_ca1878.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Yablochkov's demonstration of his brilliant arc lights at the 1878 Paris Exposition along the <a href="/wiki/Avenue_de_l%27Op%C3%A9ra" title="Avenue de l'Opéra">Avenue de l'Opéra</a> triggered a steep sell off of gas utility stocks.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_arc_lamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon arc lamp">Carbon arc lamp</a></div>
<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_arc_lamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon arc lamp">carbon arc lamp</a> in 1802 upon discovering that electricity could produce a light <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">arc</a> with carbon electrodes. However, it was not used to any great extent until a practical means of generating electricity was developed.</p>
<p>Carbon arc lamps were started by making contact between two carbon electrodes, which were then separated to within a narrow gap. Because the carbon burned away, the gap had to be constantly readjusted. Several mechanisms were developed to regulate the arc. A common approach was to feed a carbon electrode by gravity and maintain the gap with a pair of electromagnets, one of which retracted the upper carbon after the arc was started and the second controlled a brake on the gravity feed.<sup id="cite_ref-HB_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HB-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Arc lamps of the time had very intense light output – in the range of 4000 <a href="/wiki/Candlepower" title="Candlepower">candlepower</a> (candelas) – and released a lot of heat, and they were a fire hazard, all of which made them inappropriate for lighting homes.<sup id="cite_ref-McNeil1990_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil1990-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1850s, many of these problems were solved by the arc lamp invented by <a href="/wiki/William_Petrie_(electrical_engineer)" title="William Petrie (electrical engineer)">William Petrie</a> and William Staite. The lamp used a magneto-electric generator and had a self-regulating mechanism to control the gap between the two carbon rods. Their light was used to light up the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery" title="National Gallery">National Gallery</a> in London and was a great novelty at the time. These arc lamps and designs similar to it, powered by large magnetos, were first installed on English lighthouses in the mid 1850s, but the power limitations prevented these models from being a proper success.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first successful <a href="/wiki/Yablochkov_candle" title="Yablochkov candle">arc lamp</a> was developed by Russian engineer <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Yablochkov" title="Pavel Yablochkov">Pavel Yablochkov</a>, and used the <a href="/wiki/Gramme_machine" title="Gramme machine">Gramme generator</a>. Its advantage lay in the fact that it didn't require the use of a mechanical regulator like its predecessors. It was first exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1878)" title="Exposition Universelle (1878)">Paris Exposition of 1878</a> and was heavily promoted by Gramme.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc light</a> was installed along the half mile length of <a href="/wiki/Avenue_de_l%27Op%C3%A9ra" title="Avenue de l'Opéra">Avenue de l'Opéra</a>, Place du Theatre Francais and around the <a href="/wiki/Place_de_l%27Op%C3%A9ra" title="Place de l'Opéra">Place de l'Opéra</a> in 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>British engineer <a href="/wiki/R._E._B._Crompton" title="R. E. B. Crompton">R. E. B. Crompton</a> developed a more sophisticated design in 1878 which gave a much brighter and steadier light than the Yablochkov candle In 1878, he formed Crompton &amp; Co. and began to manufacture, sell and install the Crompton lamp. His concern was one of the first electrical engineering firms in the world.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Incandescent_light_bulbs">Incandescent light bulbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Incandescent light bulbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Various forms of <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Incandescent light bulbs">incandescent light bulbs</a> had numerous inventors; however, the most successful early bulbs were those that used a carbon filament sealed in a high vacuum. These were invented by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Swan" title="Joseph Swan">Joseph Swan</a> in 1878 in Britain and by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> in 1879 in the US. Edison’s lamp was more successful than Swan’s because Edison used a thinner filament, giving it higher resistance and thus conducting much less current. Edison began commercial production of carbon filament bulbs in 1880. Swan's light began commercial production in 1881.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Swan's house, in <a href="/wiki/Low_Fell" title="Low Fell">Low Fell</a>, Gateshead, was the world's first to have working light bulbs installed. The Lit &amp; Phil Library in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, was the first public room lit by electric light,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Savoy_Theatre" title="Savoy Theatre">Savoy Theatre</a> was the first public building in the world lit entirely by electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-Burgess_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_power_stations_and_isolated_systems">Central power stations and isolated systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Central power stations and isolated systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first central station providing public power is believed to be one at <a href="/wiki/Godalming#Public_electricity_supply" title="Godalming">Godalming</a>, Surrey, U.K. autumn 1881. The system was proposed after the town failed to reach an agreement on the rate charged by the gas company, so the town council decided to use electricity. The system lit up arc lamps on the main streets and incandescent lamps on a few side streets with hydroelectric power. By 1882 between 8 and 10 households were connected, with a total of 57 lights. The system was not a commercial success and the town reverted to gas.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first large scale central distribution supply plant was opened at <a href="/wiki/Holborn_Viaduct" title="Holborn Viaduct">Holborn Viaduct</a> in London in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Equipped with 1000 incandescent lightbulbs that replaced the older gas lighting, the station lit up Holborn Circus including the offices of the <a href="/wiki/General_Post_Office" title="General Post Office">General Post Office</a> and the famous <a href="/wiki/City_Temple_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="City Temple (London)">City Temple church</a>. The supply was a direct current at 110V; due to power loss in the copper wires, this amounted to 100V for the customer.</p>
<p>Within weeks, a parliamentary committee recommended passage of the landmark 1882 Electric Lighting Act, which allowed the licensing of persons, companies or local authorities to supply electricity for any public or private purposes.</p>
<p>The first large scale central power station in America was Edison's <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_Station" title="Pearl Street Station">Pearl Street Station</a> in New York, which began operating in September 1882. The station had six 200 horsepower Edison dynamos, each powered by a separate steam engine. It was located in a business and commercial district and supplied 110 volt direct current to 85 customers with 400 lamps. By 1884 Pearl Street was supplying 508 customers with 10,164 lamps.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHunterBryant1991191_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHunterBryant1991191-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the mid-1880s, other electric companies were establishing central power stations and distributing electricity, including <a href="/wiki/R._E._B._Crompton#Crompton_.26_Co." title="R. E. B. Crompton">Crompton &amp; Co.</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Edison_and_Swan_Electric_Light_Company" title="Edison and Swan Electric Light Company">Swan Electric Light Company</a> in the UK, <a href="/wiki/Thomson-Houston_Electric_Company" title="Thomson-Houston Electric Company">Thomson-Houston Electric Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" class="mw-redirect" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">Westinghouse</a> in the US and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. By 1890 there were 1000 central stations in operation.<sup id="cite_ref-HB_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HB-7">[7]</a></sup> The 1902 census listed 3,620 central stations. By 1925 half of power was provided by central stations.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Load_factor_.26_isolated_systems">Load factor &amp; isolated systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Load factor &amp; isolated systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the biggest problems facing the early power companies was the hourly variable demand. When lighting was practically the only use of electricity, demand was high during the first hours before the workday and the evening hours when demand peaked.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_276.E2.80.939_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_276.E2.80.939-20">[20]</a></sup> As a consequence, most early electric companies did not provide daytime service, with two-thirds providing no daytime service in 1897.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ratio of the average load to the peak load of a central station is called the load factor.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> For electric companies to increase profitability and lower rates, it was necessary to increase the load factor. The way this was eventually accomplished was through motor load.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_276.E2.80.939_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_276.E2.80.939-20">[20]</a></sup> Motors are used more during daytime and many run continuously. (See: <a href="/wiki/Continuous_production" title="Continuous production">Continuous production</a>.) Electric street railways were ideal for load balancing. Many electric railways generated their own power and also sold power and operated distribution systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Nye_1990_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nye_1990-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The load factor adjusted upward by the turn of the 20th century—at <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_Station" title="Pearl Street Station">Pearl Street</a> the load factor increased from 19.3% in 1884 to 29.4% in 1908. By 1929, the load factor around the world was greater than 50%, mainly due to motor load.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before widespread power distribution from central stations, many factories, large hotels, apartment and office buildings had their own power generation. Often this was economically attractive because the exhaust steam could be used for building and industrial process heat,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which today is known as <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> or combined heat and power (CHP). Most self-generated power became uneconomical as power prices fell. As late as the early 20th century, isolated power systems greatly outnumbered central stations.<sup id="cite_ref-HB_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HB-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Cogeneration</a> is still commonly practiced in many industries that use large amounts of both steam and power, such as pulp and paper, chemicals and refining. The continued use of private electric generators is called <a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">microgeneration</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_current_electric_motors">Direct current electric motors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Direct current electric motors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">Electric motor</a> and <a href="/wiki/DC_motor" title="DC motor">DC motor</a></div>
<p>The first commutator DC electric motor capable of turning machinery was invented by the British scientist <a href="/wiki/William_Sturgeon" title="William Sturgeon">William Sturgeon</a> in 1832.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The crucial advance that this represented over the motor demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> was the incorporation of a <a href="/wiki/Commutator" title="Commutator">commutator</a>. This allowed Sturgeon's motor to be the first capable of providing continuous rotary motion.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_J._Sprague" title="Frank J. Sprague">Frank J. Sprague</a> improved on the <a href="/wiki/DC_motor" title="DC motor">DC motor</a> in 1884 by solving the problem of maintaining a constant speed with varying load and reducing sparking from the brushes. Sprague sold his motor through Edison Co.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It is easy to vary speed with DC motors, which made them suited for a number of applications such as electric street railways, machine tools and certain other industrial applications where speed control was desirable.<sup id="cite_ref-HB_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HB-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternating_current">Alternating current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alternating current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">Alternating current</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">Transformer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">Electric power transmission</a>, and <a href="/wiki/War_of_currents" class="mw-redirect" title="War of currents">War of currents</a></div>
<p>Although the first power stations supplied <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a>, the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> soon became the most favored option. The main advantages of AC were that it could be transformed to high voltage to reduce transmission losses and that AC motors could easily run at constant speeds.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">Alternating current</a> technology was rooted in <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> 1830-31 discovery that a changing <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> can induce an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Electrical_circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical circuit">circuit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first person to conceive of a rotating magnetic field was Walter Baily who gave a workable demonstration of his battery-operated <a href="/wiki/Polyphase_system" title="Polyphase system">polyphase</a> motor aided by a <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutator</a> on June 28, 1879 to the Physical Society of London.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-29">[29]</a></sup> Nearly identical to Baily’s apparatus, French electrical engineer <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Deprez" title="Marcel Deprez">Marcel Deprez</a> in 1880 published a paper that identified the rotating magnetic field principle and that of a two-phase AC system of currents to produce it.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1886, English engineer <a href="/wiki/Elihu_Thomson" title="Elihu Thomson">Elihu Thomson</a> built an AC motor by expanding upon the induction-repulsion principle and his <a href="/wiki/Wattmeter" title="Wattmeter">wattmeter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was in the 1880s that the technology was commercially developed for large scale electricity generation and transmission. In 1882 the British inventor and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical engineer">electrical engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_de_Ferranti" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastian de Ferranti">Sebastian de Ferranti</a>, working for the company <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> collaborated with the distinguished physicist <a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">Lord Kelvin</a> to pioneer AC power technology including an early transformer.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">power transformer</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Gaulard" title="Lucien Gaulard">Lucien Gaulard</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Dixon_Gibbs" title="John Dixon Gibbs">John Dixon Gibbs</a> was demonstrated in London in 1881, and attracted the interest of <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" class="mw-redirect" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">Westinghouse</a>. They also exhibited the invention in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a> in 1884, where it was adopted for an electric lighting system. Many of their designs were adapted to the particular laws governing electrical distribution in the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Ziani_de_Ferranti" title="Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti">Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti</a> went into this business in 1882 when he set up a shop in London designing various electrical devices. Ferranti believed in the success of alternating current power distribution early on, and was one of the few experts in this system in the UK. With the help of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Kelvin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Kelvin">Lord Kelvin</a>, Ferranti pioneered the first AC power generator and <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformer</a> in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Hopkinson" title="John Hopkinson">John Hopkinson</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, invented the three-wire (<a href="/wiki/Three-phase" title="Three-phase">three-phase</a>) system for the distribution of electrical power, for which he was granted a <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Italian inventor <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Ferraris" title="Galileo Ferraris">Galileo Ferraris</a> invented a polyphase AC <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">induction motor</a> in 1885. The idea was that two out-of-phase, but synchronized, currents might be used to produce two magnetic fields that could be combined to produce a rotating field without any need for switching or for moving parts. Other inventors were the American engineers Charles S. Bradley and <a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">Nikola Tesla</a>, and the German technician <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friedrich_August_Haselwander&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Friedrich August Haselwander (page does not exist)">Friedrich August Haselwander</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.com_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-35">[35]</a></sup> They were able to overcome the problem of starting up the AC motor by using a rotating magnetic field produced by a poly-phase current.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_248_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_248-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Dolivo-Dobrovolsky" title="Mikhail Dolivo-Dobrovolsky">Mikhail Dolivo-Dobrovolsky</a> introduced the first three-phase induction motor in 1890, a much more capable design that became the prototype used in Europe and the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> By 1895 GE and Westinghouse both had AC motors on the market.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> With single phase current either a capacitor or coil (creating inductance) can be used on part of the circuit inside the motor to create a rotating magnetic field.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Multi-speed AC motors that have separately wired poles have long been available, the most common being two speed. Speed of these motors is changed by switching sets of poles on or off, which was done with a special motor starter for larger motors, or a simple multiple speed switch for fractional horsepower motors.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="AC_power_stations">AC power stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: AC power stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first AC power station in the world was built by the English <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical engineer">electrical engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_de_Ferranti" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastian de Ferranti">Sebastian de Ferranti</a>. In 1887 the London Electric Supply Corporation hired Ferranti for the design of their power station at <a href="/wiki/Deptford" title="Deptford">Deptford</a>. He designed the building, the generating plant and the distribution system. It was built at the Stowage, a site to the west of the mouth of <a href="/wiki/Deptford_Creek" class="mw-redirect" title="Deptford Creek">Deptford Creek</a> once used by the <a href="/wiki/Honourable_East_India_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Honourable East India Company">East India Company</a>. Built on an unprecedented scale and pioneering the use of high voltage (10,000V) AC current, it generated 800 kilowatts and supplied central London. On its completion in 1891 it was the first truly modern power station, supplying high-voltage AC power that was then "stepped down" with transformers for consumer use on each street. This basic system remains in use today around the world.</p>
<p>In America, <a href="/wiki/George_Westinghouse" title="George Westinghouse">George Westinghouse</a> who had become interested in the power transformer developed by Gaulard and Gibbs, began to develop his AC lighting system, using a transmission system with a 20:1 step up voltage with step-down. In 1890 Westinghouse and Stanley built a system to transmit power several miles to a mine in Colorado. A decision was taken to use AC for power transmission from the Niagara Power Project to Buffalo, New York. Proposals submitted by vendors in 1890 included DC and compressed air systems. A combination DC and compressed air system remained under consideration until late in the schedule. Despite the protestations of the Niagara commissioner <a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">William Thomson</a> (Lord Kelvin) the decision was taken to build an AC system, which had been proposed by both Westinghouse and General Electric. In October 1893 Westinghouse was awarded the contract to provide the first three 5,000&#160;hp, 250 rpm, 25&#160;Hz, two phase generators.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the 1890s, single and poly-phase AC was undergoing rapid introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In the U.S. by 1902, 61% of generating capacity was AC, increasing to 95% in 1917.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Despite the superiority of alternating current for most applications, a few existing DC systems continued to operate for several decades after AC became the standard for new systems.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_turbines">Steam turbines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Steam turbines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">Steam turbine</a></div>
<p>The efficiency of steam prime movers in converting the heat energy of fuel into mechanical work was a critical factor in the economic operation of steam central generating stations. Early projects used reciprocating <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a>, operating at relatively low speeds. The introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> fundamentally changed the economics of central station operations. Steam turbines could be made in larger ratings than reciprocating engines, and generally had higher efficiency. The speed of steam turbines did not fluctuate cyclically during each revolution; making parallel operation of AC generators feasible, and improved the stability of rotary converters for production of direct current for traction and industrial uses. Steam turbines ran at higher speed than reciprocating engines, not being limited by the allowable speed of a piston in a cylinder. This made them more compatible with AC generators with only two or four poles; no gearbox or belted speed increaser was needed between the engine and the generator. It was costly and ultimately impossible to provide a belt-drive between a low-speed engine and a high-speed generator in the very large ratings required for central station service.</p>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> was invented in 1884 by the British <a href="/wiki/Charles_Algernon_Parsons" title="Charles Algernon Parsons">Sir Charles Parsons</a>, whose first model was connected to a <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> that generated 7.5&#160;kW (10&#160;hp) of electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-birrcastle.com_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birrcastle.com-43">[43]</a></sup> The invention of Parson's steam turbine made cheap and plentiful electricity possible. <a href="/wiki/C._A._Parsons_and_Company" title="C. A. Parsons and Company">Parsons turbines</a> were widely introduced in English central stations by 1894; the first electric supply company in the world to generate electricity using <a href="/wiki/Turbo_generator" title="Turbo generator">turbo generators</a> was Parsons' own electricity supply company <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_and_District_Electric_Lighting_Company" title="Newcastle and District Electric Lighting Company">Newcastle and District Electric Lighting Company</a>, set up in 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-siemens_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siemens-44">[44]</a></sup> Within Parson's lifetime, the generating capacity of a unit was scaled up by about 10,000 times.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png/220px-PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png/330px-PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png/440px-PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png 2x" data-file-width="3170" data-file-height="1762" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PSM_V56_D0717_Parsons_steam_turbine_linked_directly_to_a_dynamo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The first U.S. turbines were two De Leval units at Edison Co. in New York in 1895. The first U.S. Parsons turbine was at <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Air_Brake_Company" title="Westinghouse Air Brake Company">Westinghouse Air Brake Co.</a> near <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Steam turbines also had capital cost and operating advantages over reciprocating engines. The condensate from steam engines was contaminated with oil and could not be reused, while condensate from a turbine is clean and typically reused. Steam turbines were a fraction of the size and weight of comparably rated reciprocating steam engine. Steam turbines can operate for years with almost no wear. Reciprocating steam engines required high maintenance. Steam turbines can be manufactured with capacities far larger than any steam engines ever made, giving important <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Economy of scale">economies of scale</a>.</p>
<p>Steam turbines could be built to operate on higher pressure and temperature steam. A fundamental principle of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> is that the higher the temperature of the steam entering an engine, the higher the efficiency. The introduction of steam turbines motivated a series of improvements in temperatures and pressures. The resulting increased conversion efficiency lowered electricity prices.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The power density of boilers was increased by using forced combustion air and by using compressed air to feed pulverized coal. Also, coal handling was mechanized and automated.<sup id="cite_ref-Jerome_1934_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jerome_1934-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_grid">Electrical grid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electrical grid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">Electrical grid</a></div>
<p>With the realization of long distance power transmission it was possible to interconnect different central stations to balance loads and improve load factors. Interconnection became increasingly desirable as electrification grew rapidly in the early years of the 20th century.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Merz" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Merz">Charles Merz</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Merz_%26_McLellan" title="Merz &amp; McLellan">Merz &amp; McLellan</a> consulting partnership, built the <a href="/wiki/Neptune_Bank_Power_Station" title="Neptune Bank Power Station">Neptune Bank Power Station</a> near <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in 1901,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and by 1912 had developed into the largest integrated power system in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In 1905 he tried to influence Parliament to unify the variety of voltages and frequencies in the country's electricity supply industry, but it was not until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> that Parliament began to take this idea seriously, appointing him head of a Parliamentary Committee to address the problem. In 1916 Merz pointed out that the UK could use its small size to its advantage, by creating a dense distribution grid to feed its industries efficiently. His findings led to the <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Williamson,_1st_Baron_Forres" title="Archibald Williamson, 1st Baron Forres">Williamson Report</a> of 1918, which in turn created the Electricity Supply Bill of 1919. The bill was the first step towards an integrated electricity system in the UK.</p>
<p>The more significant Electricity (Supply) Act of 1926, lead to the setting up of the National Grid.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Central_Electricity_Board" title="Central Electricity Board">Central Electricity Board</a> standardised the nation's <a href="/wiki/Electrical_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical power">electricity supply</a> and established the first synchronised AC grid, running at 132 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">kilovolts</a> and 50 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">Hertz</a>. This started operating as a national system, the <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Grid (UK)">National Grid</a>, in 1938.</p>
<p>In the United States it became a national objective after the power crisis during the summer of 1918 in the midst of World War I to consolidate supply. In 1934 the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act">Public Utility Holding Company Act</a> recognized electric utilities as <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a> of importance along with gas, water, and telephone companies and thereby were given outlined restrictions and regulatory oversight of their operations.<sup id="cite_ref-Electric_Power_Planning_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Electric_Power_Planning-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most studies of electrification and electric grids focused on industrial core countries in Europe and the United States. Elsewhere, wired electricity was often carried on and through the circuits of colonial rule. Some historians and sociologists considered the interplay of colonial politics and the development of electric grids: in India, Rao <sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> showed that linguistics-based regional politics — not techno-geographical considerations — led to the creation of two separate grids; in colonial Zimbabwe (Rhodesia), Chikowero <sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> showed that electrification was racially based and served the white settler community while excluding Africans; and in Mandate Palestine, Shamir <sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> showed that British electric concessions to a Zionist-owned company deepened the economic disparities between Arabs and Jews.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_electrification">Household electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Household electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The electrification of households in Europe and North America began in the early 20th century in major cities and in areas served by electric railways and increased rapidly until about 1930 when 70% of households were electrified in the U.S.</p>
<p>Rural areas were electrified first in Europe, and in the US the <a href="/wiki/Rural_Electric_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural Electric Administration">Rural Electric Administration</a>, established in 1935 brought electrification to rural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_cost_of_electricity">Historical cost of electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Historical cost of electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Central station electric power generating provided power more efficiently and at lower cost than small generators. The capital and operating cost per unit of power were also cheaper with central stations.<sup id="cite_ref-Devine83_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devine83-1">[1]</a></sup> The cost of electricity fell dramatically in the first decades of the twentieth century due to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbines" class="mw-redirect" title="Steam turbines">steam turbines</a> and the improved load factor after the introduction of AC motors. As electricity prices fell, usage increased dramatically and central stations were scaled up to enormous sizes, creating significant economies of scale.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> For the historical cost see Ayres-Warr (2002) Fig. 7.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_electrification">Benefits of electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Benefits of electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_electric_lighting">Benefits of electric lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Benefits of electric lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Electric lighting was highly desirable. The light was much brighter than oil or gas lamps, and there was no soot. Although early electricity was very expensive compared to today, it was far cheaper and more convenient than oil or gas lighting. Electric lighting was so much safer than oil or gas that some companies were able to pay for the electricity with the insurance savings.<sup id="cite_ref-Nye_1990_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nye_1990-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-electric_power">Pre-electric power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pre-electric power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Line_shaft" title="Line shaft">Line shaft</a></div>
<blockquote>
<p>"One of the inventions most important to a class of highly skilled workers (engineers) would be a small motive power - ranging perhaps from the force of from half a man to that of two horses, which might commence as well as cease its action at a moment's notice, require no expense of time for its management and be of modest cost both in original cost and in daily expense." Charles Babbage, 1851<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>To be efficient steam engines needed to be several hundred horsepower. Steam engines and boilers also required operators and maintenance. For these reasons the smallest commercial steam engines were about 2 horsepower. This was above the need for many small shops. Also, a small steam engine and boiler cost about $7,000 while an old blind horse that could develop 1/2 horsepower cost $20 or less.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Machinery to use horses for power cost $300 or less.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Batteuse_1881.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Batteuse_1881.jpg/250px-Batteuse_1881.jpg" width="250" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Batteuse_1881.jpg/375px-Batteuse_1881.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Batteuse_1881.jpg/500px-Batteuse_1881.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1447" data-file-height="785" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Batteuse_1881.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Threshing machine in 1881.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many power requirements were less than that of a horse. Shop machines, such as woodworking lathes, were often powered with a one- or two-man crank. Household sewing machines were powered with a foot treadle; however, factory sewing machines were steam-powered from a <a href="/wiki/Line_shaft" title="Line shaft">line shaft</a>. Dogs were sometimes used on machines such as a treadmill, which could be adapted to churn butter.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 19th century specially designed <i>power buildings</i> leased space to small shops. These building supplied power to the tenants from a steam engine through <a href="/wiki/Line_shaft" title="Line shaft">line shafts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Electric motors were several times more efficient than small steam engines because central station generation was more efficient than small steam engines and because line shafts and belts had high friction losses.<sup id="cite_ref-Devine83_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devine83-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter_.26_Bryant_1991_pp-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Electric motors were more efficient than human or animal power. The conversion efficiency for animal feed to work is between 4 and 5% compared to over 30% for electricity generated using coal.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayers-Warr_2002_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayers-Warr_2002-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact_of_electrification">Economic impact of electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Economic impact of electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mass_production#history" title="Mass production">Mass production §&#160;history</a></div>
<p>From 1870-80 each man-hour was provided with .55&#160;hp. In 1950 each man-hour was provided with 5&#160;hp, or a 3% annual increase, declining to 1.5% from 1930-50.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>The period of electrification of factories and households from 1900 to 1940, was one of high <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> and economic growth. In economics, the efficiency of electrical generation has been shown to correlate with <i>technological progress</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayers-Warr_2002_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayers-Warr_2002-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_sources_for_generation_of_electricity">Power sources for generation of electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Power sources for generation of electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">Electricity generation</a></div>
<p>Most electricity is generated by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a> or steam plants, the majority of which are <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_station" title="Fossil fuel power station">fossil fuel power stations</a> that burn coal, natural gas, fuel oil or bio-fuels, such as wood waste and black liquor from chemical pulping.</p>
<p>The most efficient thermal system is <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> in which a <a href="/wiki/Combustion_turbine" class="mw-redirect" title="Combustion turbine">combustion turbine</a> powers a generator using the high temperature combustion gases and then exhausts the cooler combustion gases to generate low pressure steam for conventional steam cycle generation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</a> uses a <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbine</a> to generate power. In 1878 the world's first hydroelectric power scheme was developed at <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William George Armstrong</a>. It was used to power a single <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc lamp</a> in his art gallery.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The old <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses_Niagara_Hydroelectric_Power_Station#Origins" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Moses Niagara Hydroelectric Power Station">Schoelkopf Power Station No. 1</a> near <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a> in the U.S. side began to produce electricity in 1881. The first <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Alva_Edison" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Alva Edison">Edison</a> hydroelectric power plant, the <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Street_Plant" title="Vulcan Street Plant">Vulcan Street Plant</a>, began operating September 30, 1882, in <a href="/wiki/Appleton,_Wisconsin" title="Appleton, Wisconsin">Appleton, Wisconsin</a>, with an output of about 12.5 kilowatts.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbines">Wind turbines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Wind turbines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first electricity-generating <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbine</a> was a battery charging machine installed in July 1887 by Scottish academic <a href="/wiki/James_Blyth_(engineer)" title="James Blyth (engineer)">James Blyth</a> to light his holiday home in <a href="/wiki/Marykirk" title="Marykirk">Marykirk</a>, Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-70">[70]</a></sup> Some months later American inventor <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Brush" title="Charles F. Brush">Charles F Brush</a> built the first automatically operated wind turbine for electricity production in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-70">[70]</a></sup> Advances in recent decades greatly lowered the cost of wind power making it one of the most competitive alternate energies and competitive with higher priced natural gas (before shale gas). Wind energy's main problem is that it is intermittent and therefore needs grid extensions and energy storage to be a reliable main energy source.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_energy">Geothermal energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Geothermal energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prince Piero Ginori Conti tested the first <a href="/wiki/Geothermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Geothermal">geothermal</a> power generator on 4 July 1904 in <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a>, Italy. It successfully lit four light bulbs.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Later, in 1911, the world's first commercial geothermal power plant was built there. Italy was the world's only industrial producer of geothermal electricity until 1958. Geothermal requires very hot underground temperatures near the surface to generate steam which is used in a low temperature steam plant. Geothermal power is only used in a few areas. Italy supplies all of the electrified rail network with geothermal power.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy">Solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">Photovoltaics</a></div>
<p>Electricity production from solar energy either directly through <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaic cells</a> or indirectly such as by producing steam to drive a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> generator.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_extent_of_electrification">Current extent of electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Current extent of electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While electrification of cities and homes has existed since the late 19th century even today about 1.3 billion people lack electricity, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. One estimate (2010) suggests that as many as half of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s households lack electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most recent progress in electrification took place between the 1950s and 1980s. Vast gains were seen in the 1970s and 1980s - from 49 percent of the world's population in 1970 to 76 percent in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Recent gains have been more modest - by the early 2010s 81 to 83 percent of the world's population had access to electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_resilience">Energy resilience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Energy resilience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_resilience" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy resilience">Energy resilience</a></div>
<p>Electricity is:</p>
<ul>
<li>the ‘<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stickiest" class="extiw" title="wikt:stickiest">stickiest</a>’ form of energy: it stays in the continent where it is produced.</li>
<li>multi-sourced. If one source suffers a shortage, electricity can be produced from another, including <a href="/wiki/Renewable_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable source">renewable sources</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>As a result, it gives the greatest degree of energy resilience and the energy system is going to electrification.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
