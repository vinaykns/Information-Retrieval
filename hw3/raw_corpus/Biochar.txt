			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Biochar</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p><b>Biochar</b> is <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> used as a <a href="/wiki/Soil_conditioner" title="Soil conditioner">soil amendment</a>. Biochar is a stable solid, rich in <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, and can endure in soil for thousands of years.<sup id="cite_ref-indep1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indep1-1">[1]</a></sup> Like most charcoal, biochar is made from <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> via <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a>. Biochar is under investigation as an approach to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-indep1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indep1-1">[1]</a></sup> Biochar thus has the potential to help mitigate <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> via <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOI10.1038.2Fncomms1053_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOI10.1038.2Fncomms1053-3">[3]</a></sup> Independently, biochar can increase <a href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertility (soil)">soil fertility</a> of <a href="/wiki/Acidic_soil" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidic soil">acidic soils</a> (low pH soils), increase agricultural productivity, and provide protection against some foliar and soil-borne diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its potential benefits have at times been oversold.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">Pre-Columbian</a> <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazonians</a> are believed to have used biochar to enhance soil productivity. They seem to have produced it by <a href="/wiki/Smolder" class="mw-redirect" title="Smolder">smoldering</a> agricultural waste (i.e., covering burning biomass with soil)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in pits or trenches.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> European settlers called it <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta de Indio</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following observations and experiments, a research team working in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> hypothesized that the Amazonian <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworm</a> <i>Pontoscolex corethrurus</i> was the main agent of fine powdering and incorporation of charcoal debris to the mineral soil.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term “biochar” was coined by Peter Read to describe charcoal used as a soil improvement.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biochar is a high-carbon, fine-grained residue that today is produced through modern <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> processes; it is the direct <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a> of biomass in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (preventing <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>), which produces a mixture of solids (the biochar proper), liquid (<a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis_oil" title="Pyrolysis oil">bio-oil</a>), and gas (<a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>) products. The specific yield from the pyrolysis is dependent on process conditions. such as temperature, and can be optimized to produce either energy or biochar.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Temperatures of 400–500&#160;°C (752–932&#160;°F) produce more <a href="/wiki/Char" title="Char">char</a>, while temperatures above 700&#160;°C (1,292&#160;°F) favor the yield of liquid and gas fuel components.<sup id="cite_ref-Winsley1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winsley1-12">[12]</a></sup> Pyrolysis occurs more quickly at the higher temperatures, typically requiring seconds instead of hours. High temperature pyrolysis is also known as <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a>, and produces primarily <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, which has been used as vehicle fuel in some times and places.<sup id="cite_ref-Winsley1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winsley1-12">[12]</a></sup> Typical yields are 60% <a href="/wiki/Bio-oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Bio-oil">bio-oil</a>, 20% biochar, and 20% syngas. By comparison, slow pyrolysis can produce substantially more char (~50%); it is this which contributes to the observed soil fertility of <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta</a>. Once initialized, both processes produce net energy. For typical inputs, the energy required to run a “fast” pyrolyzer is approximately 15% of the energy that it outputs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Modern pyrolysis plants can use the syngas created by the pyrolysis process and output 3–9 times the amount of energy required to run.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Amazonian pit/trench method<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup> harvests neither bio-oil nor syngas, and releases a large amount of CO<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> (GHG)s (and potentially, <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">toxins</a>) into the air. Commercial-scale systems process agricultural waste, paper byproducts, and even municipal waste and typically eliminate these side effects by capturing and using the liquid and gas products. The production of biochar as an output is not a priority in most cases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Centralized.2C_decentralized.2C_and_mobile_systems">Centralized, decentralized, and mobile systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Centralized, decentralized, and mobile systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a centralized system, all biomass in a region is brought to a central plant for processing. Alternatively, each farmer or group of farmers can operate a lower-tech <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a>. Finally, a truck equipped with a pyrolyzer can move from place to place to pyrolyze biomass. Vehicle power comes from the <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> stream, while the biochar remains on the farm. The <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> is sent to a refinery or storage site. Factors that influence the choice of system type include the cost of transportation of the liquid and solid byproducts, the amount of material to be processed, and the ability to feed directly into the power grid.</p>
<p>For crops that are not exclusively for biochar production, the residue-to-product ratio (RPR) and the collection factor (CF) the percent of the residue not used for other things, measure the approximate amount of feedstock that can be obtained for pyrolysis after harvesting the primary product. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> harvests approximately 460 million tons (MT) of <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> annually,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> with an RPR of 0.30, and a CF of 0.70 for the sugarcane tops, which normally are burned in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This translates into approximately 100 MT of residue annually, which could be pyrolyzed to create energy and soil additives. Adding in the <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a> (sugarcane waste) (RPR=0.29 CF=1.0), which is otherwise burned (inefficiently) in boilers, raises the total to 230 MT of pyrolysis feedstock. Some plant residue, however, must remain on the soil to avoid increased costs and emissions from nitrogen fertilizers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pyrolysis technologies for processing loose and leafy biomass produce both biochar and syngas.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermo-catalytic_depolymerization">Thermo-catalytic depolymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermo-catalytic depolymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternatively, "thermo-catalytic depolymerization", which utilizes <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_heating" title="Dielectric heating">microwaves</a>, has recently been used to efficiently convert organic matter to biochar on an industrial scale, producing ~50% char.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_sink">Carbon sink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon sink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Climate engineering</a></div>
<p>The burning and natural decomposition of biomass and in particular agricultural waste adds large amounts of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> to the atmosphere. Biochar that is stable, fixed, and 'recalcitrant' carbon can store large amounts of greenhouse gases in the ground for centuries, potentially reducing or stalling the growth in atmospheric greenhouse gas levels; at the same time its presence in the earth can improve water quality, increase soil fertility, raise agricultural productivity, and reduce pressure on <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">old-growth forests</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biochar can sequester carbon in the soil for hundreds to thousands of years, like <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Terra_Preta_de_Indio_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terra_Preta_de_Indio-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Such a carbon-negative technology would lead to a net withdrawal of CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere, while producing and consuming energy. This technique is advocated by prominent scientists such as <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a>, head of the <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Institute_for_Space_Studies" title="Goddard Institute for Space Studies">NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a>, creator of the <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia hypothesis</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation of global warming</a> by <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_remediation" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas remediation">greenhouse gas remediation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Researchers have estimated that sustainable use of biocharring could reduce the global net emissions of carbon dioxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>), methane, and nitrous oxide by up to 1.8 Pg <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>-C equivalent</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>-C<sub>e</sub>) per year (12% of current anthropogenic <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>-Ce emissions; 1 Pg=1 Gt), and total net emissions over the course of the next century by 130 Pg <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>-C<sub>e</sub>, without endangering food security, habitat, or soil conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_biochar_to_mitigate_global_climate_change_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_biochar_to_mitigate_global_climate_change-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_amendment">Soil amendment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Soil amendment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biochar is recognised as offering a number of benefits for soil health. Many benefits are related to the extremely porous nature of biochar. This structure is found to be very effective at retaining both water and water-soluble nutrients. Soil biologist <a href="/wiki/Elaine_Ingham" title="Elaine Ingham">Elaine Ingham</a> indicates<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> the extreme suitability of biochar as a habitat for many beneficial soil <a href="/wiki/Soil_biology" title="Soil biology">micro organisms</a>. She points out that when pre charged with these beneficial organisms biochar becomes an extremely effective soil amendment promoting good soil, and in turn plant, health.</p>
<p>Biochar has also been shown to reduce leaching of <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E-coli</a></i> through sandy soils depending on application rate, feedstock, pyrolysis temperature, soil moisture content, soil texture, and surface properties of the bacteria.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>For plants that require high <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a> and elevated <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> biochar can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Soil_conditioner" title="Soil conditioner">soil amendment</a> to improve yield.</p>
<p>Biochar can improve water quality, reduce soil emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>, reduce <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">nutrient leaching</a>, reduce <a href="/wiki/Soil_acidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil acidity">soil acidity</a>, and reduce <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Biochar was also found under certain circumstances to induce plant systemic responses to foliar fungal diseases and to improve plant responses to diseases caused by soilborne pathogens.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jaiswal.2C_A.K._2014_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaiswal.2C_A.K._2014-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The various impacts of biochar can be dependent on the properties of the biochar,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> as well as the amount applied,<sup id="cite_ref-Jaiswal.2C_A.K._2014_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaiswal.2C_A.K._2014-37">[37]</a></sup> and there is still a lack of knowledge about the important mechanisms and properties.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Biochar impact may depend on regional conditions including soil type, soil condition (depleted or healthy), temperature, and humidity.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Modest additions of biochar to soil reduce <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>O</span> emissions by up to 80% and eliminate <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> emissions, which are both more potent greenhouse gases than CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies have reported positive effects from biochar on crop production in degraded and nutrient–poor soils.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Biochar can be designed with specific qualities to target distinct properties of soils.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In an Columbian savanna soil, biochar reduced leaching of critical nutrients, created a higher crop uptake of nutrients, and provided greater soil availability of nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> At 10% levels biochar reduced contaminant levels in plants by up to 80%, while reducing total <a href="/wiki/Chlordane" title="Chlordane">chlordane</a> and DDX content in the plants by 68 and 79%, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On the other hand, because of its high adsorption capacity, biochar may reduce the efficacy of soil applied pesticides that are needed for weed and pest control.<sup id="cite_ref-Graber.2C_E.R._2011_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graber.2C_E.R._2011-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> High-surface-area biochars may be particularly problematic in this regard; more research into the long-term effects of biochar addition to soil is needed.<sup id="cite_ref-Graber.2C_E.R._2011_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graber.2C_E.R._2011-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slash-and-char">Slash-and-char</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Slash-and-char">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Switching from <i><a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Slash-and-char" title="Slash-and-char">slash-and-char</a></i> farming techniques in Brazil can decrease both deforestation of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> and carbon dioxide emission, as well as increase crop yields. Slash-and-burn leaves only 3% of the carbon from the organic material in the soil.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Slash-and-char can keep up to 50% of the carbon in a highly stable form.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Returning the biochar into the soil rather than removing it all for energy production reduces the need for nitrogen fertilizers, thereby reducing cost and emissions from fertilizer production and transport.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Additionally, by improving the soil's ability to be tilled, fertility, and productivity, biochar–enhanced soils can indefinitely sustain agricultural production, whereas non-enriched soils quickly become depleted of nutrients, forcing farmers to abandon the fields, producing a continuous slash and burn cycle and the continued loss of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a>. Using pyrolysis to produce bio-energy also has the added benefit of not requiring infrastructure changes the way processing biomass for <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> does. Additionally, the biochar produced can be applied by the currently used machinery for tilling the soil or equipment used to apply fertilizer.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_retention">Water retention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Water retention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biochar is a desirable soil material in many locations due to its ability to attract and retain water. This is possible because of its porous structure and high surface area.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> As a result, nutrients, phosphorus, and agrochemicals are retained for the plants benefit. Plants are therefore healthier, and less fertilizer leaches into surface or groundwater.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_production:_Bio-oil_and_Syngas">Energy production: Bio-oil and Syngas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Energy production: Bio-oil and Syngas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a></div>
<p>Mobile pyrolysis units can be used to lower the costs of transportation of the biomass if the biochar is returned to the soil and the syngas stream is used to power the process.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Bio-oil contains organic acids that are corrosive to steel containers, has a high water vapor content that is detrimental to ignition, and, unless carefully cleaned, contains some biochar particles which can block injectors.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Currently, it is less suitable for use as a kind of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> than other sources.</p>
<p>If biochar is used for the production of energy rather than as a soil amendment, it can be directly substituted for any application that uses coal. Pyrolysis also may be the most cost-effective way of <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> from biomaterial.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_and_indirect_benefits">Direct and indirect benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Direct and indirect benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The pyrolysis of forest- or agriculture-derived biomass residue generates a biofuel without competition with crop production.</li>
<li>Biochar is a pyrolysis byproduct that may be ploughed into soils in crop fields to enhance their fertility and stability, and for medium- to long-term carbon sequestration in these soils.</li>
<li>Biochar enhances the natural process: the biosphere captures <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>, especially through plant production, but only a small portion is stably sequestered for a relatively long time (soil, wood, etc.).</li>
<li>Biomass production to obtain biofuels and biochar for carbon sequestration in the soil is a carbon-negative process, i.e. more <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> is removed from the atmosphere than released, thus enabling long-term sequestration.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Intensive research into manifold aspects involving the pyrolysis/biochar platform is underway around the world. From 2005 to 2012, there were 1,038 articles that included the word “biochar” or “bio-char” in the topic that had been indexed in the ISI Web of Science.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Further research is in progress by such diverse institutions around the world as <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, which has a dedicated research unit.,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Volcani_Institute_of_Agricultural_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcani Institute of Agricultural Research">Agricultural Research Organization (ARO) of Israel, Volcani Center</a>, where a network of researchers involved in biochar research (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://sites.google.com/site/ibrnisraelbiocharnetwork/">iBRN, Israel Biochar Researchers Network</a>) was established as early as 2009, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Delaware" title="University of Delaware">University of Delaware</a>.</p>
<p>Students at <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Institute_of_Technology" title="Stevens Institute of Technology">Stevens Institute of Technology</a> in New Jersey are developing <a href="/wiki/Supercapacitor" title="Supercapacitor">supercapacitors</a> that use electrodes made of biochar.<sup id="cite_ref-Supercapacitors_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Supercapacitors-60">[60]</a></sup> A process developed by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a> researchers that removes <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> from water, also yields <a href="/wiki/Methane_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane gas">methane gas</a> usable as fuel and phosphate-laden carbon suitable for enriching soil.<sup id="cite_ref-Removing_Phosphate_from_Water_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Removing_Phosphate_from_Water-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_commercial_sector">Possible commercial sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biochar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Possible commercial sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If biomass is burned to biochar and put back into the soil, rather than being completely burned, this may reduce carbon emissions. Potentially, the bioenergy industry might even be made to sequester net carbon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELehmann2007b143.2C_144_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELehmann2007b143.2C_144-62">[62]</a></sup> Pyrolysis might be cost-effective for a combination of sequestration and energy production when the cost of a <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> ton reaches $37.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELehmann2007b143.2C_144_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELehmann2007b143.2C_144-62">[62]</a></sup> As of mid-February 2010, <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> was trading</a> at $16.82/ton on the <a href="/wiki/European_Climate_Exchange" title="European Climate Exchange">European Climate Exchange</a> (ECX), so using pyrolysis for bioenergy production may be feasible even if it is more expensive than fossil fuel. This awaits the development of carbon trading credits for biochar, not available as of 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Current biochar projects make no significant impact on the overall global carbon budget, although expansion of this technique has been advocated as a <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a> approach.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In May 2009, the Biochar Fund received a grant from the Congo Basin Forest Fund for a project in <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a> to simultaneously slow down <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, increase the <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> of rural communities, provide <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and sequester carbon. Though some farmers did report better maize crops, the project ended early without significant results and with promises to the farmers not kept.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>Application rates of 2.5–20 tonnes per hectare (1.0–8.1&#160;t/acre) appear to be required to produce significant improvements in plant yields. Biochar costs in developed countries vary from $300–7000/tonne, generally too high for the farmer/horticulturalist and prohibitive for low-input field crops. In developing countries, constraints on agricultural biochar relate more to biomass availability and production time. An alternative is to use small amounts of biochar in lower cost biochar-fertilizer complexes.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various companies in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> sell biochar or biochar production units.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="need secondary source, not commercial web sites (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In Sweden the 'Stockholm Solution' is an urban tree planting system that uses 30% biochar to support healthy growth of the urban forest. The Qatar Aspire Park now uses biochar to help trees cope with the intense heat of their summers.</p>
<p>At the 2009 International Biochar Conference, a mobile pyrolysis unit with a specified intake of 1,000 pounds (450&#160;kg) was introduced for agricultural applications. The unit had a length of 12 feet and height of 7 feet (3.6 m by 2.1m).<sup id="cite_ref-biomassmag.com.2Farticle_id.3D3091_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biomassmag.com.2Farticle_id.3D3091-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>A production unit in <a href="/wiki/Dunlap,_Tennessee" title="Dunlap, Tennessee">Dunlap, Tennessee</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mantria_Corporation_Ponzi_scheme" title="Mantria Corporation Ponzi scheme">Mantria Corporation</a> opened in August 2009 after testing and an initial run, was later shut down as part of a <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a> investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>

To date, scientists have been unable to completely reproduce the beneficial growth properties of <i>terra preta</i>. It is hypothesized that part of the alleged benefits of <i>terra preta</i> require the biochar to be aged so that it increases the cation exchange capacity of the soil, among other possible effects. In fact, there is no evidence natives made biochar for soil treatment, but rather for transportable fuel charcoal; there is little evidence for any hypothesis accounting for the frequency and location of terra preta patches in Amazonia. Abandoned or forgotten charcoal pits left for centuries were eventually reclaimed by the forest. In that time, the initially harsh negative effects of the char (high pH, extreme ash content, salinity) wore off and turned positive as the forest soil ecosystem saturated the charcoals with nutrients. <i>supra</i> note 2 at 386 ("Only aged biochar shows high cation retention, as in Amazonian Dark Earths.
