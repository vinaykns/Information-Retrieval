<p><b>Geopolitics</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> γῆ <i>ge</i> "earth, land" and πολιτική <i>politikē</i> "politics") is the study of the effects of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> (human and physical) on international <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Geopolitics is a method of studying <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a> to understand, explain and predict international political behavior through geographical variables. These include <a href="/wiki/Area_studies" title="Area studies">area studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> being evaluated.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geopolitics focuses on <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">political power</a> in relation to geographic space. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial waters</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_sovereign_states" title="List of sovereign states">land territory</a> in correlation with <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_history" title="Diplomatic history">diplomatic history</a>. Academically, geopolitics analyses history and social science with reference to <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> in relation to <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>. Outside of academia, a variety of groups offer a geopolitical prognosis, including non-profit groups and for-profit private institutions (such as brokerage houses<sup id="cite_ref-financeExample_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-financeExample-3">[3]</a></sup> and consulting companies<sup id="cite_ref-mckinseyExample_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mckinseyExample-4">[4]</a></sup>).Topics of geopolitics include relations between the interests of international political actors, interests focused to an area, space, geographical element or ways, relations which create a geopolitical system.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Critical_geopolitics" title="Critical geopolitics">Critical geopolitics</a>" deconstructs classical geopolitical theories, by showing their political/ideological functions for <a href="/wiki/Great_powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Great powers">great powers</a> during and after the age of imperialism.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term has been used to describe a broad spectrum of ideas, from "a synonym for international relations, social, political and historical phenomena" to various <a href="/wiki/Pseudo_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudo science">pseudo-scientific</a> theories of <a href="/wiki/Historical_determinism" title="Historical determinism">historical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geographic_determinism" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographic determinism">geographic determinism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gogwilt_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gogwilt-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alfred_Thayer_Mahan_and_sea_power">Alfred Thayer Mahan and sea power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alfred Thayer Mahan and sea power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Thayer_Mahan" title="Alfred Thayer Mahan">Alfred Thayer Mahan</a> (1840–1914), a frequent commentator on world naval strategic and diplomatic affairs, believed that national greatness was inextricably associated with the sea—and particularly with its commercial use in peace and its control in war. Mahan's theoretical framework came from <a href="/wiki/Antoine-Henri_Jomini" title="Antoine-Henri Jomini">Antoine-Henri Jomini</a>, and emphasized that strategic locations (such as <a href="/wiki/Choke_point" title="Choke point">chokepoints</a>, canals, and coaling stations), as well as quantifiable levels of fighting power in a fleet, were conducive to control over the sea. He proposed six conditions required for a nation to have <a href="/wiki/Sea_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea power">sea power</a>:</p>
<ol>
<li>Advantageous geographical position;</li>
<li>Serviceable coastlines, abundant natural resources, and favorable climate;</li>
<li>Extent of territory</li>
<li>Population large enough to defend its territory;</li>
<li>Society with an aptitude for the sea and commercial enterprise; and</li>
<li>Government with the influence and inclination to dominate the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Mahan distinguished a key region of the world in the Eurasian context, namely, the Central Zone of Asia lying between 30° and 40° north and stretching from Asia Minor to Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In this zone independent countries still survived – Turkey, Persia, Afghanistan, China, and Japan. Mahan regarded those countries, located between Britain and Russia, as if between "Scylla and Charybdis". Of the two monsters – Britain and Russia – it was the later that Mahan considered more threatening to the fate of Central Asia. Mahan was impressed by Russia's transcontinental size and strategically favorable position for southward expansion. Therefore, he found it necessary for the Anglo-Saxon "sea power" to resist Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emil_Reich">Emil Reich</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Emil Reich">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Austro-Hungarian historian <a href="/wiki/Emil_Reich" title="Emil Reich">Emil Reich</a> (1854–1910) is considered to be the first having coined the acceptance in English<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> as early as 1902 and later in 1904 in his book <i>Foundations of Modern Europe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homer_Lea">Homer Lea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Homer Lea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Homer_Lea" title="Homer Lea">Homer Lea</a> in <i>The Day of the Saxon</i> (1912) described that the entire Anglo-Saxon race faced a threat from German (Teuton), Russian (Slav), and Japanese expansionism: The "fatal" relationship of Russia, Japan, and Germany "has now assumed through the urgency of natural forces a coalition directed against the survival of Saxon supremacy." It is "a dreadful Dreibund".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Lea believed that while Japan moved against Far East and Russia against India, the Germans would strike at England, the center of the British Empire. He thought the Anglo-Saxons faced certain disaster from their militant opponents.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mackinder_and_the_Heartland_theory">Mackinder and the Heartland theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mackinder and the Heartland theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pivot_area.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Pivot_area.png/450px-Pivot_area.png" width="450" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Pivot_area.png/675px-Pivot_area.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/Pivot_area.png 2x" data-file-width="715" data-file-height="358" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pivot_area.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sir Halford Mackinder's Heartland concept showing the situation of the "pivot area" established in the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_the_Heartland" class="mw-redirect" title="Theory of the Heartland">Theory of the Heartland</a>. He later revised it to mark Northern Eurasia as a pivot while keeping area marked above as Heartland.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sir_Halford_Mackinder" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Halford Mackinder">Sir Halford Mackinder</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Geographical_Pivot_of_History" title="The Geographical Pivot of History">Heartland Theory</a> initially received little attention outside geography, but some thinkers would claim that it subsequently influenced the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policies</a> of world powers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Those scholars who look to MacKinder through critical lenses accept him as an organic strategist who tried to build a foreign policy vision for Britain with his Eurocentric analysis of historical geography.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> His formulation of the Heartland Theory was set out in his article entitled "<a href="/wiki/The_Geographical_Pivot_of_History" title="The Geographical Pivot of History">The Geographical Pivot of History</a>", published in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1904. Mackinder's doctrine of geopolitics involved concepts diametrically opposed to the notion of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Thayer_Mahan" title="Alfred Thayer Mahan">Alfred Thayer Mahan</a> about the significance of navies (he coined the term <i>sea power</i>) in world conflict. He saw navy as a basis of Colombian era empire (roughly from 1492 to the 19th century), and predicted the 20th century to be domain of land power. The Heartland theory hypothesized a huge empire being brought into existence in the Heartland—which wouldn't need to use coastal or transoceanic transport to remain coherent. The basic notions of Mackinder's doctrine involve considering the geography of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> as being divided into two sections: the <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">World Island</a> or Core, comprising <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>; and the Peripheral "islands", including the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>. Not only was the Periphery noticeably smaller than the World Island, it necessarily required much sea transport to function at the technological level of the World Island—which contained sufficient natural resources for a developed economy.</p>
<p>Mackinder posited that the industrial centers of the Periphery were necessarily located in widely separated locations. The World Island could send its navy to destroy each one of them in turn, and could locate its own industries in a region further inland than the Periphery (so they would have a longer struggle reaching them, and would face a well-stocked industrial bastion). Mackinder called this region the <i>Heartland</i>. It essentially comprised <a href="/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe" title="Central and Eastern Europe">Central and Eastern Europe</a>: <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, Western <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mitteleuropa" title="Mitteleuropa">Mitteleuropa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Heartland contained the grain reserves of Ukraine, and many other natural resources. Mackinder's notion of geopolitics was summed up when he said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Who rules Central and Eastern Europe commands the Heartland. Who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island. Who rules the World-Island commands the World.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_J._Spykman" title="Nicholas J. Spykman">Nicholas J. Spykman</a> is both a follower and critic of geostrategists <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Mahan" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfred Mahan">Alfred Mahan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halford_Mackinder" title="Halford Mackinder">Halford Mackinder</a>. His work is based on assumptions similar to Mackinder's,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> including the unity of world politics and the world sea. He extends this to include the unity of the air. Spykman adopts Mackinder's divisions of the world, renaming some:</p>
<ol>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_the_Heartland" class="mw-redirect" title="Theory of the Heartland">Heartland</a>;</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Rimland" title="Rimland">Rimland</a> (analogous to Mackinder's "inner or marginal crescent" also an intermediate region, lying between the Heartland and the marginal sea powers); and</li>
<li>The Offshore Islands &amp; Continents (Mackinder's "outer or insular crescent").<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>Under Spykman's theory, a Rimland separates the Heartland from ports that are usable throughout the year (that is, not frozen up during winter). Spykman suggested this required that attempts by Heartland nations (particularly <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>) to conquer ports in the Rimland must be prevented. Spykman modified Mackinder's formula on the relationship between the Heartand and the Rimland (or the inner crescent), claiming that "Who controls the rimland rules Eurasia. Who rules Eurasia controls the destinies of the world." This theory can be traced in the origins of <a href="/wiki/Containment" title="Containment">Containment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> policy on preventing the spread of Soviet influence after World War II (see also <a href="/wiki/Truman_Doctrine" title="Truman Doctrine">Truman Doctrine</a>).</p>
<p>Another famous follower of Mackinder was <a href="/wiki/Karl_Haushofer" title="Karl Haushofer">Karl Haushofer</a> who called Mackinder's <a href="/wiki/Geographical_Pivot_of_History" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographical Pivot of History">Geographical Pivot of History</a> a "genius' scientific tractate."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He commented on it: "Never have I seen anything greater than those few pages of geopolitical masterwork."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Mackinder located his Pivot, in the words of Haushofer, on "one of the first solid, geopolitically and geographically irreproachable maps, presented to one of the earliest scientific forums of the planet – the Royal Geographic Society in London"<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Haushofer adopted both Mackinder's Heartland thesis and his view of the Russian-German alliance – powers that Mackinder saw as the major contenders for control of Eurasia in the twentieth century. Following Mackinder he suggested an alliance with the Soviet Union and, advancing a step beyond Mackinder, added Japan to his design of the <i>Eurasian Bloc.</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, at the centenary of <a href="/wiki/The_Geographical_Pivot_of_History" title="The Geographical Pivot of History">The Geographical Pivot of History</a>, famous Historian <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kennedy" title="Paul Kennedy">Paul Kennedy</a> wrote: "Right now with hundreds of thousands of US troops in the Eurasian rimlands and with administration constantly explaining why it has to stay the course, it looks as if Washington is taking seriously Mackinder's injunction to ensure control of the geographical pivot of history."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kissinger.2C_Brzezinski_and_the_Grand_Chessboard">Kissinger, Brzezinski and the Grand Chessboard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kissinger, Brzezinski and the Grand Chessboard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg/300px-%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg" width="300" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg/450px-%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg/600px-%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1102" data-file-height="749" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%D0%A5%D0%B0%D1%80%D1%82%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D0%B8_%D0%A0%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Two famous <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Advisor_(United_States)" title="National Security Advisor (United States)">Security Advisers</a> from the Cold War period, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew_Brzezinski" title="Zbigniew Brzezinski">Zbigniew Brzezinski</a>, cared lest the United States lose its geopolitical focus on Eurasia and, first and foremost, on Russia despite the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Communism" class="mw-redirect" title="Fall of Communism">fall of Communism</a> and the end of the ideological struggle. Ideological <a href="/wiki/Cold_warrior" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold warrior">Cold warriors</a>, both turned into convinced geopoliticians after the end of the Cold War,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and each wrote an influential book on the subject in the 1990s—<a href="/wiki/Diplomacy_(book)" title="Diplomacy (book)">Diplomacy (Kissinger 1994)</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Chessboard:_American_Primacy_and_Its_Geostrategic_Imperatives" class="mw-redirect" title="The Grand Chessboard: American Primacy and Its Geostrategic Imperatives">The Grand Chessboard: American Primacy and Its Geostrategic Imperatives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Anglo-American classical geopolitical theories were revived.</p>
<p>Students of geopolitics and history, Kissinger wrote in Diplomacy, are uneasy about the approach that hostile intentions have disappeared and traditional foreign policy considerations no longer apply. "They would argue … that Russia, regardless of who govern it, sits astride the territory Halford Mackinder called the geopolitical heartland, and is the heir to one of the most potent imperial traditions." The United States must "maintain the global balance of power vis-à-vis the country with a long history of expansionism."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Russia, the second geopolitical threat traditionally remained Germany and, as Mackinder had feared ninety years ago, its partnership with Russia. During the Cold War, Kissinger argues, both sides of the Atlantic recognized that, "unless America is organically involved in Europe, it would be obliged to involve itself later under circumstances far less favorable to both sides of the Atlantic. That is even more true today. Germany has become so strong that existing European institutions cannot by themselves strike a balance between Germany and its European partners. Nor can Europe, even with Germany, manage by itself … Russia…" It is in no country's interest that Germany and Russia should fixate on each other as principal partner. They would raise fears of condominium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Without America, Britain and France cannot cope with Germany and Russia; and "without Europe, America could turn … into an island off the shores of Eurasia."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spykman's vision of Eurasia was strongly confirmed: "Geopolitically, America is an island off the shores of the large landmass of Euraisa, whose resources and population far exceed those of the United States. The domination by a single power of either of Eurasia's two principal spheres—Europe and Asia—remains a good definition of strategic danger for America. Cold War or no Cold War. For such a grouping would have the capacity to outstrip America economically and, in the end, militarily. That danger would have to be resisted even were the dominant power apparently benevolent, for if the intentions ever changed, America would find itself with a grossly diminished capacity for effective resistance and a growing inability to shape events."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The main interest of the American leaders is maintaining the balance of power in Eurasia<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having converted from ideologist into geopolitician, Kissinger in retrospect interpreted the Cold War in geopolitical terms—an approach not characteristic for his works during the Cold War. Now, however, he stressed on the beginning of the Cold War: "The objective of moral opposition to Communism had merged with the geopolitical task of containing the Soviet expansion."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Nixon, he added, was geopolitical rather than ideological cold warrior.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three years after Kissinger's Dilpomacy, <a href="/wiki/Brzezinski" class="mw-redirect" title="Brzezinski">Brzezinski</a> followed suit, launching <a href="/wiki/The_Grand_Chessboard:_American_Primacy_and_Its_Geostrategic_Imperatives" class="mw-redirect" title="The Grand Chessboard: American Primacy and Its Geostrategic Imperatives">The Grand Chessboard: American Primacy and Its Geostrategic Imperatives</a> and, after three more years, The Geostrategic Triad: Living with China, Europe, and Russia. The Grand Chessboard described the American triumph in the Cold War in terms of control over Eurasia: for the first time ever, a "non-Eurasian" power had emerged as a key arbiter of "Eurasian" power relations.<sup id="cite_ref-Zbignew_Brzezinski_1997_p._31_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zbignew_Brzezinski_1997_p._31-33">[33]</a></sup> The book states its purpose: "The formulation of a comprehensive and integrated Eurasian geostrategy is therefore the purpose of this book."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Although the power configuration underwent a revolutionary change, Brzezinski confirmed three years later, Eurasia was still a megacontinent.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Like Spykman, Brzezinski acknowledges that: "Cumulatively, Eurasia's power vastly overshadows America's."<sup id="cite_ref-Zbignew_Brzezinski_1997_p._31_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zbignew_Brzezinski_1997_p._31-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In classical Spykman terms, Brzezinski formulized his geostrategic "chessboard" doctrine of Eurasia, which aims to prevent the unification of this megacontinent.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Europe and Asia are politically and economically powerful…. It follows that… American foreign policy must…employ its influence in Eurasia in a manner that creates a stable continental equilibrium, with the United States as the political arbiter.… Eurasia is thus the chessboard on which the struggle for global primacy continues to be played, and that struggle involves geo- strategy – the strategic management of geopolitical interests…. But in the meantime it is imperative that no Eurasian challenger emerges, capable of dominating Eurasia and thus also of challenging America… For America the chief geopolitical prize is Eurasia…and America's global primacy is directly dependent on how long and how effectively its preponderance on the Eurasian continent is sustained."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>German <i><a href="/wiki/Geopolitik" title="Geopolitik">Geopolitik</a></i> is characterized by the belief that life of States—being similar to those of human beings and animals—is shaped by scientific <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">determinism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a>. German geopolitics develops the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Lebensraum" title="Lebensraum">Lebensraum</a></i> (living space) that is thought to be necessary to the development of a nation like a favorable natural environment would be for animals.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Friedrich_Ratzel">Friedrich Ratzel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Friedrich Ratzel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ratzel" title="Friedrich Ratzel">Friedrich Ratzel</a> (1844–1904), influenced by thinkers such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Heinrich_Haeckel" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernst Heinrich Haeckel">Ernst Heinrich Haeckel</a>, contributed to 'Geopolitik' by the expansion on the biological conception of geography, without a static conception of borders. Positing that states are organic and growing, with borders representing only a temporary stop in their movement, he held that the expanse of a state's borders is a reflection of the health of the nation—meaning that static countries are in decline. Ratzel published several papers, among which was the essay "Lebensraum" (1901) concerning <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>. Ratzel created a foundation for the German variant of geopolitics, <i>geopolitik</i>. Influenced by the American <a href="/wiki/Geostrategy" title="Geostrategy">geostrategist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Thayer_Mahan" title="Alfred Thayer Mahan">Alfred Thayer Mahan</a>, Ratzel wrote of aspirations for German naval reach, agreeing that sea power was self-sustaining, as the profit from trade would pay for the merchant marine, unlike land power.</p>
<p>The geopolitical theory of Ratzel has been criticized as being too sweeping, and his interpretation of human history and geography being too simple and mechanistic. Critically, he also underestimated the importance of social organization in the development of power.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_association_of_German_Geopolitik_with_Nazism">The association of German Geopolitik with Nazism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The association of German Geopolitik with Nazism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the thoughts of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Kjell%C3%A9n" title="Rudolf Kjellén">Rudolf Kjellén</a> and Ratzel were picked up and extended by a number of German authors such as <a href="/wiki/Karl_Haushofer" title="Karl Haushofer">Karl Haushofer</a> (1869–1946), <a href="/wiki/Erich_Obst" title="Erich Obst">Erich Obst</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Lautensach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hermann Lautensach (page does not exist)">Hermann Lautensach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_Maull" title="Otto Maull">Otto Maull</a>. In 1923, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Haushofer" title="Karl Haushofer">Karl Haushofer</a> founded the <i>Zeitschrift für Geopolitik</i> (Journal for Geopolitics), which was later used in the <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. The key concepts of Haushofer's Geopolitik were Lebensraum, <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan-region" title="Pan-region">pan-regions</a>, and organic borders. States have, Haushofer argued, an undeniable right to seek <a href="/wiki/Natural_border" title="Natural border">natural borders</a> which would guarantee <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/autarky" class="extiw" title="wikt:autarky">autarky</a>.</p>
<p>Haushofer's influence within the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> has recently been challenged,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> given that Haushofer failed to incorporate the Nazis' racial ideology into his work. Popular views of the role of geopolitics in the Nazi Third Reich suggest a fundamental significance on the part of the geo-politicians in the ideological orientation of the Nazi state. Bassin (1987) reveals that these popular views are in important ways misleading and incorrect.</p>
<p>Despite the numerous similarities and affinities between the two doctrines, geopolitics was always held suspect by the National Socialist ideologists. This was understandable, for the underlying philosophical orientation of geopolitics did not comply with that of National Socialism. Geopolitics shared Ratzel's <a href="/wiki/Scientific_materialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific materialism">scientific materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geographic_determinism" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographic determinism">geographic determinism</a>, and held that human society was determined by external influences—in the face of which qualities held innately by individuals or groups were of reduced or no significance. National Socialism rejected in principle both materialism and determinism and also elevated innate human qualities, in the form of a hypothesized 'racial character,' to the factor of greatest significance in the constitution of human society. These differences led after 1933 to friction and ultimately to open <a href="/wiki/Denunciation" title="Denunciation">denunciation</a> of geopolitics by Nazi ideologues.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Nevertheless, German Geopolitik was discredited by its (mis)use in Nazi expansionist policy of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and has never achieved standing comparable to the pre-war period.</p>
<p>The resultant negative association, particularly in U.S. academic circles, between classical geopolitics and Nazi or <a href="/wiki/Imperialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperialist">imperialist</a> ideology, is based on loose justifications. This has been observed in particular by critics of contemporary academic geography, and proponents of a "neo"-classical geopolitics in particular. These include Haverluk et al., who argue that the stigmatization of geopolitics in academia is unhelpful as geopolitics as a field of positivist inquiry maintains potential in researching and resolving topical, often politicized issues such as <a href="/wiki/Conflict_resolution" title="Conflict resolution">conflict resolution</a> and prevention, and mitigating <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disciplinary_differences_in_perspectives">Disciplinary differences in perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Disciplinary differences in perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Negative associations with the term "geopolitics" and its practical application stemming from its association with <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and pre-World War II German scholars and students of Geopolitics are largely specific to the field of academic <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geography</a>, and subdisciplines of <a href="/wiki/Human_Geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Human Geography">Human Geography</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Political_Geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Geography">Political Geography</a> in particular. However, this negative association does not exist, or at least not to the same extent, in disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">History</a> or <a href="/wiki/Political_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Science">Political Science</a> that make use of geopolitical concepts. Classical Geopolitics forms an important element of analysis for <a href="/wiki/Military_History" class="mw-redirect" title="Military History">Military History</a> as well as for subdisciplines of <a href="/wiki/Political_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Science">Political Science</a> such as <a href="/wiki/International_Relations" class="mw-redirect" title="International Relations">International Relations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Security_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Security Studies">Security Studies</a>. This difference in disciplinary perspectives is addressed by Bert Chapman in <i>Geopolitics: A Guide To the Issues</i>, in which Chapman makes note that academic and professional <a href="/wiki/International_Relations" class="mw-redirect" title="International Relations">International Relations</a> journals are more amenable to the study and analysis of Geopolitics, and in particular Classical Geopolitics, than contemporary academic journals in the field of <a href="/wiki/Political_Geography" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Geography">Political Geography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In disciplines outside of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geography</a>, Geopolitics is not negatively viewed (as it often is among academic geographers such as <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Gallaher" title="Carolyn Gallaher">Carolyn Gallaher</a> or <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Dodds" title="Klaus Dodds">Klaus Dodds</a>) as a tool of <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">Imperialism</a> or associated with <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>, but rather viewed as a valid and consistent manner of assessing major international geopolitical circumstances and events, not necessarily related to armed conflict or military operations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">

<p>French geopolitical doctrines lie broadly in opposition to German <a href="/wiki/Geopolitik" title="Geopolitik">Geopolitik</a> and reject the idea of a fixed geography. French geography is focused on the evolution of polymorphic territories being the result of mankind's actions. It also relies on the consideration of long time periods through a refusal to take specific events into account. This method has been theorized by Professor Lacoste according to three principles: <a href="/wiki/Social_representation" title="Social representation">Representation</a>; <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diachronie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Diachronie (page does not exist)">Diachronie</a>; and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diatopie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Diatopie (page does not exist)">Diatopie</a>.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_the_Laws" title="The Spirit of the Laws">The Spirit of the Laws</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Montesquieu" title="Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a> outlined the view that man and societies are influenced by climate. He believed that hotter climates create hot-tempered people and colder climates aloof people, whereas the mild climate of France is ideal for political systems. Considered as one of the founders of French geopolitics, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lis%C3%A9e_Reclus" title="Élisée Reclus">Élisée Reclus</a>, is the author of a book considered as a reference in modern geography (Nouvelle Géographie universelle). Alike Ratzel, he considers geography through a global vision. However, in complete opposition to Ratzel's vision, Reclus considers geography not to be unchanging; it is supposed to evolve commensurately to the development of human society. His marginal political views resulted in his rejection by academia.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographer</a> and geopolitician <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Ancel" title="Jacques Ancel">Jacques Ancel</a> is considered to be the first theoretician of geopolitics in France, and gave a notable series of lectures at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnegie_foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carnegie foundation (page does not exist)">Carnegie foundation</a> and published "Géopolitique" in 1936. Like Reclus, Ancel rejects German determinist views on geopolitics (including Haushofer's doctrines).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Braudel" class="mw-redirect" title="Braudel">Braudel</a>'s broad view used insights from other social sciences, employed the concept of the <i>longue durée</i>, and downplayed the importance of specific events. This method was inspired by the French <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Vidal_de_la_Blache" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Vidal de la Blache">Paul Vidal de la Blache</a> (who in turn was influenced by German thought, particularly that of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ratzel" title="Friedrich Ratzel">Friedrich Ratzel</a> whom he had met in Germany). Braudel's method was to analyse the interdependence between individuals and their environment.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Vidalian geopolitics is based on varied forms of cartography and on <i><a href="/wiki/Possibilism_(geography)" title="Possibilism (geography)">possibilism</a></i> (founded on a societal approach of geography—i.e. on the principle of spaces polymorphic faces depending from many factors among them mankind, culture, and ideas) as opposed to determinism.</p>
<p>Due to the influence of German <i>Geopolitik</i> on French geopolitics, the latter were for a long time banished from academic works. In the mid-1970s, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Lacoste" title="Yves Lacoste">Yves Lacoste</a>—a French <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographer</a> who was directly inspired by Ancel, Braudel and Vidal de la Blache—wrote <i>La géographie, ça sert d'abord à faire la guerre</i> (Geography first use is war)in 1976. This book—which is very famous in France—symbolizes the birth of this new school of geopolitics (if not so far the first French school of geopolitics as Ancel was very isolated in the 1930s–40s). Initially linked with communist party evolved to a less liberal approach. At the end of the 1980s he founded the Institut Français de Géopolitique (French Institute for Geopolitics) that publishes the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9rodote" title="Hérodote">Hérodote</a> revue. While rejecting the generalizations and broad abstractions employed by the German and Anglo-American traditions (and the <i>new geographers</i>), this school does focus on spatial dimension of geopolitics affairs on different levels of analysis. This approach emphazises the importance of multi-level (or multi-scales) analysis and maps at the opposite of critical geopolitics which avoid such tools. Lacoste proposed that every conflict (both local or global) can be considered from a perspective grounded in three assumptions:</p>
<ol>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Social_representation" title="Social representation">Representation</a></b>: Each group or individuals is the product of an education and is characterized by specific representations of the world or others groups or individuals. Thus, basic societal beliefs are grounded in their ethnicity or specific location. The study of representation is a common point with the more contemporary critical geopolitics. Both are connected with the work of Henri Lefebvre (La production de l'espace, first published in 1974)</li>
<li><b>Diachronie.</b> Conducting an historical analysis confronting "long periods" and short periods as the prominent French historian Fernand Braudel suggested.</li>
<li><b>Diatopie</b>: Conducting a cartographic survey through a multiscale mapping.</li>
</ol>
<p>Connected with this stream, and former member of Hérodote editorial board, the French geographer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michel_Foucher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michel Foucher (page does not exist)">Michel Foucher</a> developed a long term analysis of international borders. He coined various neologism among them: <i>Horogenesis</i>: Neologism that describes the concept of studying the <i>birth of borders</i>, <i>Dyade</i>: border shared by two neighbouring states (for instance US territory has two terrestrial dyades&#160;: one with Canada and one with Mexico). The main book of this searcher "Fronts et frontières" (Fronts and borders) first published in 1991, without equivalent remains as of yet untranslated in English. Michel Foucher is an expert of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> for borders affairs.</p>
<p>More or less connected with this school, Stéphane Rosière can be quoted as the editor in Chief of the online journal <i>L'Espace politique</i>, <sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> this journal created in 2007 became the most prominent French journal of political geography and Geopolitics with Hérodote.</p>
<p>A much more conservative stream is personified by François Thual. Thual was a French expert in geopolitics, and a former official of the Ministry of Civil Defence. Thual taught geopolitics of the religions at the French War College, and has written thirty books devoted mainly to geopolitical method and its application to various parts of the world. He is particularly interested in the Orthodox, Shiite, and Buddhist religions, and in troubled regions like the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>. Connected with F. Thual, Aymeric Chauprade, former professor of geopolitics at the French War College and now member of the extreme-right party "Front national", subscribes to a supposed "new" French school of geopolitics which advocates above all a return to <i>realpolitik</i> and "clash of civilization" (Huntington). The thought of this school is expressed through the French Review of Geopolitics (headed by Chauprade) and the International Academy of Geopolitics. Chauprade is a supporter of a Europe of nations, he advocates a European Union excluding Turkey, and a policy of compromise with Russia (in the frame of a Eurasian alliance which is <i>en vogue</i> among European extreme-right politists) and supports the idea of a multipolar world—including a balanced relationship between China and the U.S.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1990s a senior researcher at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Philosophy,_Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Institute of Philosophy, Russian Academy of Sciences">Institute of Philosophy, Russian Academy of Sciences</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vadim_Tsymbursky&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vadim Tsymbursky (page does not exist)">Vadim Tsymbursky</a> <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;( <a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A6%D1%8B%D0%BC%D0%B1%D1%83%D1%80%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9,_%D0%92%D0%B0%D0%B4%D0%B8%D0%BC_%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B4%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" class="extiw" title="ru:Цымбурский, Вадим Леонидович">ru</a> )</span> (1957-2009), coined the term "island-Russia" and developed the "Great Limitrophe" concept.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Colonel-General <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Ivashov" title="Leonid Ivashov">Leonid Ivashov</a> (retired), a Russian geopolitics specialist of the early 21st century, headed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Academy_of_Geopolitical_Problems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Academy of Geopolitical Problems (page does not exist)">Academy of Geopolitical Problems</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Академия геополитических проблем</span>), which analyses the international and domestic situations and develops geopolitical doctrine. Earlier, Colonel-General Leonid Ivashov headed the Main Directorate for International Military Cooperation of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_of_the_Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Defence of the Russian Federation">Ministry of Defence of the Russian Federation</a>.</p>
<p>Vladimir Karyakin, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leading_Researcher_(Russia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Leading Researcher (Russia) (page does not exist)">Leading Researcher</a> at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_Institute_of_Strategic_Studies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Russian Institute of Strategic Studies (page does not exist)">Russian Institute of Strategic Studies</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A0%D0%BE%D1%81%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%B9%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9_%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B8%D1%82%D1%83%D1%82_%D1%81%D1%82%D1%80%D0%B0%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%B8%D1%87%D0%B5%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D1%85_%D0%B8%D1%81%D1%81%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%B4%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B9" class="extiw" title="ru:Российский институт стратегических исследований">ru</a>)</span>, has proposed the term "geopolitics of the third wave".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Summary needed, rather than the gobbledygook I removed (February 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meta-geopolitics">Meta-geopolitics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Meta-geopolitics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The framework of <i>Meta</i>-geopolitics, proposed by <a href="/wiki/Nayef_Al-Rodhan" title="Nayef Al-Rodhan">Nayef Al-Rodhan</a> combines traditional and new dimensions of geopolitics to offer a multidimensional view of power and power relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In this framework, the importance of <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> is superseded by the combination of <a href="/wiki/Hard_power" title="Hard power">hard-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">soft- power</a> tools that states can employ to preserve and obtain power. <i>Meta</i>-geopolitics defines seven key dimensions of state power that include social and health issues, domestic politics, economics, environment, science and human potential, military and security issues, and international diplomacy.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The <i>Meta</i>-geopolitics framework allows for the assessment of relative strengths and weaknesses as well as predictions about future trends. Furthermore, while this analytical grid is relevant for states, it also applies to private and transnational entities, which are playing an increasingly important role in contemporary geopolitics.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-interventionism">Non-interventionism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geopolitics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Non-interventionism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Non-interventionism" title="Non-interventionism">Non-interventionism</a>, the geopolitical policy whereby a nation seeks to avoid alliances with other nations in order to avoid being drawn into wars not related to direct territorial self-defense. An original more formal definition is that Non-intervention is a policy characterized by the absence of interference by a state or states in the external affairs of another state without its consent, or in its internal affairs with or without its consent. This is based on the grounds that a state should not interfere in the internal politics of another state, based upon the principles of <a href="/wiki/State_sovereignty" class="mw-redirect" title="State sovereignty">state sovereignty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>. A similar phrase is "strategic independence".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Historical examples of supporters of non-interventionism are US Presidents <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, who both favored nonintervention in European Wars while maintaining free trade. Other proponents include United States Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">Robert A. Taft</a> and United States Congressman <a href="/wiki/Ron_Paul" title="Ron Paul">Ron Paul</a>.</p>
