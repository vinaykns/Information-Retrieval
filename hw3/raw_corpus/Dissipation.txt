			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dissipation</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Dissipation</b> is the result of an <a href="/wiki/Irreversible_process" title="Irreversible process">irreversible process</a> that takes place in inhomogeneous <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> systems. A dissipative process is a process in which energy (internal, bulk flow kinetic, or system potential) is transformed from some initial form to some final form; the capacity of the final form to do mechanical work is less than that of the initial form. For example, <a href="/wiki/Heat_Transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat Transfer">heat transfer</a> is dissipative because it is a transfer of internal energy from a hotter body to a colder one. Following the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> varies with temperature (reduces the capacity of the combination of the two bodies to do mechanical work), but never decreases in an isolated system.</p>
<p>These processes produce entropy (see <a href="/wiki/Entropy_production" title="Entropy production">entropy production</a>) at a certain rate. The entropy production rate times ambient temperature gives the dissipated power. Important examples of irreversible processes are: heat flow through a thermal resistance, fluid flow through a flow resistance, diffusion (mixing), chemical reactions, and electrical current flow through an electrical resistance (Joule heating).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thermodynamic dissipative processes are essentially irreversible. They <a href="/wiki/Entropy_production" title="Entropy production">produce entropy</a> at a finite rate. In a process in which the temperature is locally continuously defined, the local density of rate of entropy production times local temperature gives the local density of dissipated power.[Definition needed!]</p>
<p>A particular occasion of occurrence of a dissipative process cannot be described by a single individual <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics" title="Hamiltonian mechanics">Hamiltonian</a> formalism. A dissipative process requires a collection of admissible individual Hamiltonian descriptions, exactly which one describes the actual particular occurrence of the process of interest being unknown. This includes friction, and all similar forces that result in decoherency of energy—that is, conversion of <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">coherent</a> or directed energy flow into an indirected or more <a href="/wiki/Isotropic" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> distribution of energy.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"The conversion of mechanical energy into heat is called energy dissipation." – <i>François Roddier</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term is also applied to the loss of energy due to generation of unwanted heat in electric and electronic circuits.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_physics">Computational physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Computational physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computational_physics" title="Computational physics">computational physics</a>, numerical dissipation (also known as "numerical diffusion") refers to certain side-effects that may occur as a result of a numerical solution to a differential equation. When the pure <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a> equation, which is free of dissipation, is solved by a numerical approximation method, the energy of the initial wave may be reduced in a way analogous to a diffusional process. Such a method is said to contain 'dissipation'. In some cases, "artificial dissipation" is intentionally added to improve the <a href="/wiki/Numerical_stability" title="Numerical stability">numerical stability</a> characteristics of the solution.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A formal, mathematical definition of dissipation, as commonly used in the mathematical study of <a href="/wiki/Measure-preserving_dynamical_system" title="Measure-preserving dynamical system">measure-preserving dynamical systems</a>, is given in the article <i><a href="/wiki/Wandering_set" title="Wandering set">wandering set</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_hydraulic_engineering">In hydraulic engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In hydraulic engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dissipation is the process of converting mechanical energy of downward-flowing water into thermal and acoustical energy. Various devices are designed in stream beds to reduce the kinetic energy of flowing waters to reduce their <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosive potential</a> on banks and <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">river bottoms</a>. Very often these devices look like small <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfalls</a> or <a href="/wiki/Waterfall#Types" title="Waterfall">cascades</a>, where water flows vertically or over <a href="/wiki/Riprap" title="Riprap">riprap</a> to lose some of its <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversible_processes">Irreversible processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Irreversible processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Important examples of irreversible processes are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Heat flow through a thermal resistance</li>
<li>Fluid flow through a flow resistance</li>
<li>Diffusion (mixing)</li>
<li>Chemical reactions<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Electrical current flow through an electrical resistance (<a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">Joule heating</a>).</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waves_or_oscillations">Waves or oscillations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Waves or oscillations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">Waves</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a>, lose <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> over <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, typically from <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>. In many cases the "lost" energy raises the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the system. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a> that loses <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> is said to dissipate. The precise nature of the effects depends on the nature of the wave: an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_wave" title="Atmospheric wave">atmospheric wave</a>, for instance, may dissipate close to the surface due to <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> with the land mass, and at higher levels due to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">radiative cooling</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dissipation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_thermodynamics" title="Timeline of thermodynamics">Timeline of thermodynamics</a></div>
<p>The concept of dissipation was introduced in the field of thermodynamics by <a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">William Thomson</a> (Lord Kelvin) in 1852.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Lord Kelvin deduced that a subset of the above-mentioned irreversible dissapative processes will occur unless a process is governed by a "perfect thermodynamic engine". The processes that Lord Kelvin identified were friction, diffusion, conduction of heat and the absorption of light.</p>
