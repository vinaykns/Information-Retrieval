<p><b>Georg Ludwig Hartig</b> (September 2, 1764 – February 2, 1837) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forester</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ludwig_Hartig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hartig was born at <a href="/wiki/Gladenbach" title="Gladenbach">Gladenbach</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>. After obtaining a practical knowledge of forestry from his uncle at <a href="/wiki/Harzburg" title="Harzburg">Harzburg</a>, he studied from 1781 to 1783 at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Giessen" title="University of Giessen">University of Giessen</a>, which had commenced a course of instruction in forestry just a few years earlier, in 1778.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ludwig_Hartig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1786, Hartwig was appointed as Manager of Forests for the Prince of <a href="/wiki/Solms-Braunfels" title="Solms-Braunfels">Solms-Braunfels</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hungen" title="Hungen">Hungen</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Wetterau" title="Wetterau">Wetterau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>. While in this position, he founded a school for the teaching of <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, one of the first dedicated schools of forestry in Europe.</p>
<p>After a decade in Hungen, in 1797, he received an appointment as Inspector of Forests to the Prince of <a href="/wiki/House_of_Orange-Nassau" title="House of Orange-Nassau">Orange-Nassau</a> and moved to <a href="/wiki/Dillenburg" title="Dillenburg">Dillenburg</a>, continuing his school of forestry there. Attendance increased considerably in Dillenburg. On the dissolution of the principality by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon I of France</a> in 1805 he lost his position.</p>
<p>In 1806, Hartig went to <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> as Chief Inspector of Forests. Five years later, in 1811, he was called to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> in a similar capacity. There he reestablished his school once again, succeeding in connecting it with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>.</p>
<p>Hartwig received an appointment as Honorary Professor at the University of Berlin in 1830. He died at Berlin on 2 February 1837.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sons">Sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Ludwig_Hartig&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His son <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Hartig" title="Theodor Hartig">Theodor Hartig</a> (1805–1880) and grandson <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hartig" title="Robert Hartig">Robert Hartig</a> (1839–1901) also were distinguished for their contributions to the study of forestry.</p>
