<p>A <b>population</b> is the number of all the <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> of the same group or <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, which live in a particular <a href="/wiki/Geographical_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographical area">geographical area</a>, and have the capability of interbreeding.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area that is used to define a <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">sexual</a> population is defined as the area where <a href="/wiki/Interbreeding" class="mw-redirect" title="Interbreeding">inter-breeding</a> is potentially possible between any pair within the area, and where the probability of interbreeding is greater than the probability of <a href="/wiki/Crossbreed" title="Crossbreed">cross-breeding</a> with individuals from other areas.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, population refers to a collection of <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>. <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">Demography</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> which entails the statistical study of human populations. This article refers mainly to human population.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_genetics_.28ecology.29">Population genetics (ecology)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population genetics (ecology)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a> a sexual population is a set of organisms in which any pair of members can <a href="/wiki/Breeding_in_the_wild" title="Breeding in the wild">breed</a> together. This means that they can regularly exchange gametes to produce normally-fertile offspring, and such a breeding group is also known therefore as a <i>gamodeme</i>. This also implies that all members belong to the same species.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> If the gamodeme is very large (theoretically, approaching infinity), and all gene alleles are uniformly distributed by the gametes within it, the gamodeme is said to be panmictic. Under this state, <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">allele</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gamete</a>) frequencies can be converted to genotype (<a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>) frequencies by expanding an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Quadratic_equation" title="Quadratic equation">quadratic equation</a>, as shown by Sir <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a> in his establishment of quantitative genetics.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>This seldom occurs in nature: localisation of gamete exchange – through dispersal limitations, preferential mating, cataclysm, or other cause – may lead to small actual gamodemes which exchange gametes reasonably uniformly within themselves, but are virtually separated from their neighbouring gamodemes. However, there may be low frequencies of exchange with these neighbours. This may be viewed as the breaking up of a large sexual population (panmictic) into smaller overlapping sexual populations. This failure of <a href="/wiki/Panmixia" title="Panmixia">panmixia</a> leads to two important changes in overall population structure: (1) the component gamodemes vary (through gamete sampling) in their allele frequencies when compared with each other and with the theoretical panmictic original (this is known as <i>dispersion</i>, and its details can be estimated using expansion of an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Binomial_equation" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial equation">binomial equation</a>); and (2) the level of homozygosity rises in the entire collection of gamodemes. The overall rise in homozygosity is quantified by the <i>inbreeding coefficient</i> (<i>f</i> or <i>φ</i>). Note that <i>all homozygotes</i> are increased in frequency – both the deleterious and the desirable. The mean phenotype of the gamodemes collection is lower than that of the panmictic "original" – which is known as <i>inbreeding depression</i>. It is most important to note, however, that some dispersion lines will be superior to the panmictic original, while some will be about the same, and some will be inferior. The probabilities of each can be estimated from those binomial equations. In <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_breeding" title="Animal breeding">animal breeding</a>, procedures have been developed which deliberately utilise the effects of dispersion (such as line breeding, pure-line breeding, back-crossing). It can be shown that <i>dispersion-assisted selection</i> leads to the greatest <i>genetic advance</i> (<i>ΔG</i>=change in the phenotypic mean), and is much more powerful than selection acting without attendant dispersion. This is so for both allogamous (random fertilization)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and autogamous (self-fertilization) gamodemes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In ecology, the population of a certain species in a certain area can be estimated using the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln Index">Lincoln Index</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_human_population">World human population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World human population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">World population</a></div>
<p>As of today's date, the world population is <a href="/wiki/Guesstimate" title="Guesstimate">estimated</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> to be 7.491 <a href="/wiki/1,000,000,000" title="1,000,000,000">billion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The US Census Bureau estimates the 7 billion number was surpassed on 12 March 2012. According to a separate estimate by the United Nations, Earth’s population exceeded seven billion in October 2011, a milestone that offers unprecedented challenges and opportunities to all of humanity, according to <a href="/wiki/UNFPA" class="mw-redirect" title="UNFPA">UNFPA</a>, the United Nations Population Fund.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to papers published by the United States Census Bureau, the world population hit 6.5 billion on 24 February 2006. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> designated 12 October 1999 as the approximate day on which world population reached 6 billion. This was about 12 years after world population reached 5 billion in 1987, and 6 years after world population reached 5.5 billion in 1993. The population of countries such as <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, is not even known to the nearest million,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> so there is a considerable margin of error in such estimates.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Researcher Carl Haub calculated that a total of over 100 billion people have probably been born in the last 2000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predicted_growth_and_decline">Predicted growth and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Predicted growth and decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">Population growth</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">Population growth</a> increased significantly as the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> gathered pace from 1700 onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-historic_population_graphs_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historic_population_graphs-13">[13]</a></sup> The last 50 years have seen a yet more rapid increase in the <a href="/wiki/Population_growth#Population_growth_rate" title="Population growth">rate</a> of population growth<sup id="cite_ref-historic_population_graphs_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historic_population_graphs-13">[13]</a></sup> due to <a href="/wiki/History_of_medicine#Modern_medicine" title="History of medicine">medical advances</a> and substantial increases in agricultural productivity, particularly beginning in the 1960s,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> made by the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Population Division">United Nations Population Division</a> projected that the world's population will likely surpass 10 billion in 2055.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the future, the world's population is expected to peak,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> after which it will decline due to economic reasons, health concerns, land exhaustion and environmental hazards. According to one report, it is very likely that the world's population will stop growing before the end of the 21st century. Further, there is some likelihood that population will actually decline before 2100.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Population has already declined in the last decade or two in Eastern Europe, the Baltics and in the Commonwealth of Independent States.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The population pattern of less-developed regions of the world in recent years has been marked by gradually declining birth rates. These followed an earlier sharp reduction in death rates.<sup id="cite_ref-users.rcn.com_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-users.rcn.com-21">[21]</a></sup> This transition from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates is often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic transition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-users.rcn.com_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-users.rcn.com-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_population_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population control">Human population control</a></div>
<p>Human population control is the practice of altering the rate of growth of a human population. Historically, human population control has been implemented with the goal of increasing the rate of population growth. In the period from the 1950s to the 1980s, concerns about global population growth and its effects on poverty, environmental degradation and political stability led to efforts to reduce population growth rates. While population control can involve measures that improve people's lives by giving them greater control of their reproduction, a few programmes, most notably the Chinese government's one-child per family policy, have resorted to coercive measures.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, tension grew between population control advocates and women's health activists who advanced women's <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_rights" title="Reproductive rights">reproductive rights</a> as part of a <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>-based approach.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Growing opposition to the narrow population control focus led to a significant change in population control policies in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
