<p>The <b>National Renewable Energy Laboratory</b> (<b>NREL</b>), located in <a href="/wiki/Golden,_Colorado" title="Golden, Colorado">Golden, Colorado</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' primary laboratory for <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>. The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) is a government-owned, contractor-operated facility, and is funded through the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> (DOE). This arrangement allows a private entity to operate the lab on behalf of the federal government under a prime contract. NREL receives funding from Congress to be applied toward research and development projects. NREL also performs research on <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> (PV) under the National Center for Photovoltaics. NREL has a number of PV research capabilities including research and development, testing, and deployment. NREL's campus houses several facilities dedicated to PV research.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Established in 1974,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[Note 1]</a></sup> NREL began operating in 1977 as the <b>Solar Energy Research Institute</b>. Under the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Jimmy_Carter" title="Presidency of Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter administration</a>, it was the recipient of a large budget and its activities went beyond research and development in solar energy as it tried to popularize knowledge about already existing technologies, like passive solar, amongst the population. During the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Ronald_Reagan" title="Presidency of Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan administration</a>, the institute's budget was cut by some 90%; many employees 'reduced in force' and the laboratory's activities were reduced to R&amp;D. In later years, renewed interest in the energy problem improved the institute's position, but funding has fluctuated. In 2006 funding had dropped to the point that NREL was forced to lay off 32 workers,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> and in 2011 anticipated congressional budget shortfalls led to a voluntary buyout program for 100 to 150 staff reductions.<sup id="cite_ref-DenverPost-2011.10.04_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DenverPost-2011.10.04-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since its inception in 1977 as the Solar Energy Research Institute, it has been operated under contract by <a href="/wiki/MRIGlobal" title="MRIGlobal">MRIGlobal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> In September 1991, the NREL was designated a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy_National_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Department of Energy National Laboratories">national laboratory of the U.S. Department of Energy</a> (DOE) by President George H.W. Bush and its name was changed to NREL. Currently, NREL is managed for the DOE by the Alliance for Sustainable Energy, LLC. The Alliance was formed in 2008 as a joint venture between <a href="/wiki/MRIGlobal" title="MRIGlobal">MRIGlobal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battelle_Memorial_Institute" title="Battelle Memorial Institute">Battelle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EERE:_Golden_Field_Office_Public_Reading_Room_-_FOIA_Proactive_Disclosures_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EERE:_Golden_Field_Office_Public_Reading_Room_-_FOIA_Proactive_Disclosures-7">[6]</a></sup> Dr. Martin Keller became NREL's ninth director in October 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-directorappointed_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-directorappointed-8">[7]</a></sup> and currently serves as both the director of the lab and the president of the Alliance.<sup id="cite_ref-director_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-director-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL's areas of research and development expertise are <a href="/wiki/Renewable_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable electricity">renewable electricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_fuels" title="Renewable fuels">renewable fuels</a>, integrated energy systems, and strategic energy analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-over_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-over-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, NREL became the co-secretariat of the <a href="/wiki/Low_Emission_Development_Strategies_Global_Partnership_(LEDS_GP)" title="Low Emission Development Strategies Global Partnership (LEDS GP)">Low Emission Development Strategies Global Partnership (LEDS GP)</a> alongside the <a href="/wiki/Climate_%26_Development_Knowledge_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate &amp; Development Knowledge Network">Climate &amp; Development Knowledge Network</a> (CDKN).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercialization_and_technology_transfer">Commercialization and technology transfer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercialization and technology transfer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NREL works closely with a number of private partners to transfer technological developments in renewable energy and energy efficiency technologies to the marketplace and social arena.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL's innovative technologies have been recognized with 57 R&amp;D 100 Awards. The engineering and science behind these technology transfer successes and awards demonstrates NREL's commitment to a sustainable energy future.<sup id="cite_ref-over_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-over-10">[9]</a></sup> The idea of technology transfer was added to the mission of NREL as a means of enhancing commercial impact and societal benefit, ultimately justifying the use of tax dollars to in part fund the projects in the lab.<sup id="cite_ref-Ampluse_Corp_Case_Study_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ampluse_Corp_Case_Study-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As many of these technologies are young and often just emerging, NREL aims to reduce the risk of private sector investment and adoption of their developments.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> Three key pieces of federal legislation laid the policy framework to enact technology transfer: The Stevenson-Wydler Technology Innovation Act of 1980, The Bayh-Dole Act or The University and Small Business Patent Procedures Act of 1980, and The Federal Technology Transfer Act of 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Ampluse_Corp_Case_Study_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ampluse_Corp_Case_Study-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ultimately, many of the deployed technologies help mitigate the oil dependence of the United States, reduce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a> from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> use, and maintain U.S. industry competitiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> Deployment of technologies is accomplished by developing technology partnerships with private industry.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> NREL serves as a reduced-risk platform for research, and through partnerships those advances can effectively be translated into serving the interest of both the private sector and the public sector.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> The energy goals set by the DOE are at the forefront of the research done in the laboratory, and the research reflects the energy goals, which are designed with the interest of "U.S. industry competitiveness" in mind.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> The challenge to achieving these goals is investment security.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Part of the technology transfer process is to form partnerships that not only focus on financial security, but also to consider partners who have demonstrated core values that reflect the integrity to manage the introduction and assimilation of the technological developments.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup> NREL focuses on the core values of the partnering entity, the willingness to set and meet timely goals, dedication to transparency, and a reciprocating intent to further development.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_About_Technology_Transfer-13">[12]</a></sup> Under these partnership agreements, NREL does not fund projects conducted by their private partners.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup> NREL does provide funding opportunities through their competitively placed contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup> In order to form a Technology Partnership Agreement with NREL, there are essentially 11 steps:<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Discuss the project proposal with the appropriate NREL technical contact</li>
<li>Determine if the project meets qualifications (separate checklist of criteria)</li>
<li>Determine agreement type
<ol>
<li>Cooperative Research and Development Agreements (CRADAs)</li>
<li>Work for others</li>
</ol>
</li>
<li>Develop statement of work</li>
<li>Provide draft agreement</li>
<li>Review and/or negotiate</li>
<li>Sign agreement</li>
<li>Review and approve</li>
<li>Send funds</li>
<li>Start work</li>
<li>Manage commitment</li>
</ol>
<p>The process is estimated to require 45 business days<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup> subject to negotiations. Technology Partnership Agreements provide only the technical services of NREL.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_Technology_Partnership_Agreements-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moving further than Technology Partnership Agreements are Licensing Agreements and Nondisclosure Agreements. Licensing Agreements provide industry to market and commercialize technology developed by NREL. This type of agreement is available to any organization, regardless of size.<sup id="cite_ref-Licensing_Agreements_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Licensing_Agreements-16">[15]</a></sup> A Nondisclosure Agreement is an agreement to implement confidentiality of information for a mutually beneficial collaboration in research and development.<sup id="cite_ref-Licensing_Agreements_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Licensing_Agreements-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior successes include the NREL and SkyFuel partnership in the next generation of solar parabolic troughs, and also the partnership between NREL scientists, Ampulse Corporation, and <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> (ORNL) to create a less expensive alternative to wafer crystalline silicon solar cells.<sup id="cite_ref-Success_Stories_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Success_Stories-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooperative_Research_and_Development_Agreement">Cooperative Research and Development Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cooperative Research and Development Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Cooperative Research and Development Agreement (CRADA) is a partnership between NREL and an outside company. This type of agreement protects the intellectual property of both NREL and the outside company, and allows the investing company the right to exclusive field-of-use license for any inventions that come out of the CRADA. There are two types of CRADA's. A Shared-Resource CRADA is used to "develop, advance, or commercialize an NREL project, without money changing hands."<sup id="cite_ref-CRADA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRADA-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The development and progress of a Shared-Resource CRADA must fit within the scope of an existing NREL project already funded by the DOE. The second type of agreement is called a "Funds-In" CRADA. It allows for the partner to pay for some or all of NREL expenses under a project, but NREL does not supply funds to the partner.<sup id="cite_ref-CRADA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRADA-18">[17]</a></sup> A specific example of a Cooperative Research and Development Agreement (CRADA) is between NREL, Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) and the Ampulse Corporation to develop a less expensive silicon solar cell. Funding came from both parties, and the resulting processes and investigations were carried out in cooperation between the three entities (two federal and one non-federal).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_for_others">Work for others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Work for others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NREL offers technical services to partners who require resources that are not available to them through the form of a Work-for-Others agreement. This agreement differs from CRADA's in that they are not for the purpose of performing joint research. The partner covers the entire cost of the project.<sup id="cite_ref-WFO_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFO-20">[19]</a></sup> There are four types of Work-for-Others agreements:</p>
<ol>
<li>Interagency Agreement-Government (IAG): A partnership formed between the DOE and a second U.S. federal agency. The outside agency funds the project directly.<sup id="cite_ref-WFO_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFO-20">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Funds-In Agreement (FIA): A non-federal entity funds the project and research conducted by NREL. In some occasions, the partner can obtain title to inventions.<sup id="cite_ref-WFO_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFO-20">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Technical Services Agreement (TSA): A non-federal entity pays NREL for services related to an analytical problem. NREL provides consultation and basic technical assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-WFO_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFO-20">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Analytical Services Agreement (ASA): This is a narrower, more specified version of a Technical Services Agreement. Non-federal sponsors pay for a specific analytical service performed by the lab in one of 13 different categories of testing/characterization services. It is a small-scale agreement, often dubbed a "shrink-wrap" agreement, has a maximum duration of 3 months and a maximum value of $25,000.<sup id="cite_ref-WFO_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFO-20">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_licensing">Technology licensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technology licensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NREL offers licensing for many of its technologies related to energy efficiency and renewable energy development. Licensing of NREL's intellectual property is available to businesses of any size, from start-up to <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>. The available technologies fall under the categories of: renewable electricity conversion and delivery systems, renewable fuels formulation and delivery, efficient and integrated energy systems, and <a href="/wiki/Strategic" class="mw-redirect" title="Strategic">strategic</a> energy analysis. "NREL-developed technologies include vehicles and fuels, basic sciences, biomass, concentrating solar power, electric infrastructure systems, geothermal, hydrogen and fuel cells, photovoltaics, and wind energy."<sup id="cite_ref-Tech_Available_for_Licensing_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tech_Available_for_Licensing-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL has a list of 33 market summaries available for licensing, and the list includes information about the descriptions of the technologies, their benefits, potential applications, and their current stage in development.<sup id="cite_ref-Tech_Available_for_Licensing_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tech_Available_for_Licensing-21">[20]</a></sup> In addition to market summaries, NREL and the DOE hold 457 patents under the heading of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Tech_Available_for_Licensing_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tech_Available_for_Licensing-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Center_for_Photovoltaics">National Center for Photovoltaics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: National Center for Photovoltaics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The goals of the <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> (PV) research done at NREL are to decrease the "nation's reliance on <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil-fuel</a> generated electricity by lowering the cost of delivered electricity and improving the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell_efficiency" title="Solar cell efficiency">efficiency</a> of PV <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">modules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">systems</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Research_Home_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Research_Home-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Photovoltaic research at NREL is performed under the National Center for Photovoltaics (NCPV). NREL, along with <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a>, helps to coordinate work on PV for the NCPV with other research institutions including <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Institute of Technology</a>, DOE's Regional Experiment Stations; Southeast Regional Experiment Station and the Southwest Technology Development Institute. The NCPV also partners with many universities and other industry partners.<sup id="cite_ref-NCPV_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCPV-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The National Center for Photovoltaics is part of the DOE's Solar Energy Technologies Program (STEP). STEP’s main goal is to “develop cost-competitive solar energy systems for America.” <sup id="cite_ref-STEP_About_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STEP_About-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The STEP program focuses funding on PV solar and concentrating solar because they feel that they have the greatest potential to be cost-competitive by 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-STEP_About_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STEP_About-24">[23]</a></sup> In 2010, STEP spent over $128 million on PV research and development.<sup id="cite_ref-STEP_Budget_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STEP_Budget-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lab maintains a number of research partnerships for PV research. Some examples include partnerships with the <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA) and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Governors_Association" title="Western Governors Association">Western Governors' Association</a> Solar Energy Task Force.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_PV_Partnerships_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_PV_Partnerships-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capabilities">Capabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Capabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NREL uses its expertise in the areas of research and development, testing and evaluation, and deployment to achieve its goals when it comes to PV research.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Research_Home_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Research_Home-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_development">Research and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some of the areas of PV R&amp;D include the physical properties of PV panels, performance and reliability of PV, junction formation, and research into photo-electrochemical materials. NREL partners with both industry and academia to "investigate properties and operating mechanisms of cell materials and devices."<sup id="cite_ref-PV_R.26D_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_R.26D-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through this research, NREL hopes to surpass current technologies in efficiency and cost-competitiveness and reach the overall goal of generating electricity at $0.06/<a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kWh</a> for <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">grid</a>-tied PV systems.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_R.26D_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_R.26D-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL identifies the following as cornerstones to its PV R&amp;D program:the High-Performance Photovoltaics Project, the Thin-Film Partnership, and the PV Manufacturing R&amp;D Project.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Projects_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Projects-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The High-Performance Photovoltaics Project aims to double the conversion efficiency of solar PV panels by analyzing a broad set of complex technical issues. In doing so, they hope to create working prototypes of these high-efficiency PV panels.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_PV_High-performance_project_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_PV_High-performance_project-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Thin Film Partnership Program at NREL coordinates national research teams of manufacturers, academics, and NREL scientists on a variety of subjects relating to thin-film PV. The research areas of the Thin Film Partnership Program include amorphous silicon (a-Si), copper indium diselenide (CuInSe2 or CIS) and, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a> (CdTe), and module reliability.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_PV_TFPP_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_PV_TFPP-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL's PV Manufacturing Research and Development Project is an ongoing partnership between NREL and private sector solar manufacturing companies. It started in 1991 as the Photovoltaic Manufacturing Technology (PVMaT) project and was extended and renamed in 2001 due to its success as a project. The overall goal of research done under the PV Manufacturing R&amp;D Project is to help maintain a strong market position for US solar companies by researching ways to reduce costs to manufacturers and customers and improving the manufacturing process. It is estimated that the project has helped to reduce manufacturing cost for PV panels by 50%.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Man._R.26D_Project_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Man._R.26D_Project-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of achievements under the PV Manufacturing Research and Development Project include the development of a manufacturing process that increase the production of silicon solar modules by 8% without increasing costs and the development of a new boron coating process that reduces solar costs over traditional processes.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Man._R.26D_Project_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Man._R.26D_Project-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>NREL is capable of providing an array of testing and evaluation capabilities to the PV industry with indoor, outdoor, and field testing facilities. NREL is able to provide testing on long-term performance, reliability, and component failure for PV systems. NREL also has accelerated testing capabilities from both PV cells and system components to identify areas of potential long-term degradation and failure. NREL is accredited by the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) for primary reference cell and secondary module calibration. It is also certified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> (IEC) in accordance standard for calibrating terrestrial primary reference PV cells. The Device Performance group at NREL is able to measure the performance of PV cells and modules with regard to a standard or customized reference set. This allows NREL to serve as independent facility for verifying device performance. NREL allows industry members to test and evaluate potential products, with the hope that it will lead to more cost effective and reliable technology. The overall goal is to help improve the reliability in the PV industry.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Test_.26_Eval_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Test_.26_Eval-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment">Deployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>NREL also seeks to raise public awareness of PV technologies through its deployment services. NREL provides a number of technical and non-technical publications intended to help raise consumer awareness and understanding of solar PV. Scientists at NREL perform research into energy markets and how to develop the solar energy market. They also perform research and outreach in the area of <a href="/wiki/Building-integrated_photovoltaics" title="Building-integrated photovoltaics">building-integrated PV</a>. NREL is also an active organizer and sponsor in the DOE’s <a href="/wiki/Solar_Decathlon" title="Solar Decathlon">Solar Decathlon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PV_Dep._33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PV_Dep.-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL provides a wealth of information on solar energy, beyond the scientific papers on research done at the lab. The lab provides a great number of publications on solar resources and manuals on different applications of solar technology, as well as a number of different solar resource models and tools. The lab also makes available a number of different solar resource data sets in its Renewable Resource Data Center.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_RReDC_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_RReDC-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>NREL’s <a href="/wiki/Golden,_Colorado" title="Golden, Colorado">Golden, Colorado</a> campus houses several facilities dedicated to PV and biomass research. In the recently opened Science and Technology Facility, research is conducted on solar cells, thin films, and nanostructure research. NREL's Outdoor Test Facility allows researchers to test and evaluate PV technologies under a range of conditions, both indoor and outdoor. Scientists at NREL work at the Outdoor Test Facility to develop standards for testing PV technologies. At the Outdoor Test Facility NREL researchers calibrate primary reference cells for use in a range of applications. One of the main buildings for PV research at NREL is the Solar Energy Research Facility (SERF). Examples of research conducted at the SERF include semiconductor material research, prototype solar cell production, and measurement and characterization of solar cell and module performance. Additionally, the roof at the SERF is able to house ten PV panels to evaluate and analyze the performance of commercial building-integrated PV systems. Additionally, R&amp;D in PV materials and devices, measurement and characterization, reliability testing are also conducted at the SERF.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_PV_Facilities_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_PV_Facilities-35">[34]</a></sup> At the Solar Radiation Research Laboratory, NREL has been measuring solar radiation and meteorological data since 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-NREL_PV_Facilities_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_PV_Facilities-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Bioenergy_Center">National Bioenergy Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: National Bioenergy Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Bioenergy Center (NBC) was established in October 2000. "The National Bioenergy Center is composed of four technical groups and a technical lead for partnership development with industry. Partnership development includes work performed at NREL under Cooperative Research and Development Agreements (CRADA), Technical Service Agreements (TSA), Analytical Service Agreements (ASA), and Work for Others (WFO) contract research for DOE's industry partners."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main focus of the research is to convert biomass into biofuels/biochemical intermediates via both biochemical and thermochemical processes.</p>
<p>The mission statement as of November 2013, states:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>"The National Bioenergy Center's mission is to foster capability to catalyze the replacement of petroleum with transportation fuels from biomass by delivering innovative, cost-effective biofuels solutions."</i></dd>
</dl>
<p>T<i>he National Bioenergy Center is currently divided into certain technology and research areas:<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></i></p>
<ul>
<li>Applied Science</li>
<li>Catalysis and Thermochemical Sciences and Engineering R&amp;D</li>
<li>Biochemical Process R&amp;D</li>
<li>Biorefinery Analysis</li>
</ul>
<p>Some of the current projects are in the following areas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Biomass characteristics</li>
<li>Biochemical conversion</li>
<li>Thermochemical conversion</li>
<li>Chemical and catalyst science</li>
<li>Integrated biorefinery processes</li>
<li>Microalgal biofuels</li>
<li>Biomass process and sustainability analysis</li>
</ul>
<p>NREL biomass researchers have strong capabilities in many facets of biomass technology that support the cost-effective conversion of biomass to biofuels. At NREL's state-of-the-art biomass research facilities, researchers design and optimize processes to convert renewable biomass feedstocks into transportation fuels and chemicals. The Integrated Biorefinery Research Facility (IBRF) houses multiple pilot-scale process trains for converting biomass to various liquid fuels at a rate of 450–900&#160;kg (0.5–1 ton) per day of dry biomass. Unit operations include feedstock washing and milling, pretreatment, enzymatic hydrolysis, fermentation, distillation, and solid-liquid separation. The heart of the Thermochemical Users Facility (TCUF) is the 0.5-metric-ton-per-day Thermochemical Process Development Unit (TCPDU), which can be operated in either a pyrolysis or gasification mode.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Wind_Technology_Center">National Wind Technology Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: National Wind Technology Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NREL_Windmills.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/NREL_Windmills.jpg/220px-NREL_Windmills.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/NREL_Windmills.jpg/330px-NREL_Windmills.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/NREL_Windmills.jpg/440px-NREL_Windmills.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NREL_Windmills.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The main research <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> at NREL</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>NREL's National Wind Technology Center (NWTC) is the nation's premier <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/wind">wind energy technology research facility</a>. The NWTC advances the development of innovative land-based and offshore wind energy technologies through its research and testing facilities. Researchers draw on years of experience and their wealth of expertise in fluid dynamics and structural testing to also <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/water/">advance marine and hydrokinetic water power technologies</a>. At the NWTC researchers work side-by-side with industry partners to develop new technologies that can compete in the global market and to increase system reliability and reduce costs.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>NREL has produced many technologies that impact the wind industry at a global level. The center is home of 20 patents and has created software such as (FAST), simulation software that is used to model wind turbines.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Located at the base of the foothills just south of <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder</a>, Colorado, the center's test sites experience diverse and robust wind patterns that are ideal for the development of advanced wind energy technologies. The NWTC's 305-acre site comprises field test sites, test laboratories, industrial high-bay work areas, machine shops, electronics and instrumentation laboratories, and office areas.</p>
<p>The NWTC is also home to NREL's Distributed Energy Resources Test Facility (DERTF). The DERTF is a working laboratory for interconnection and systems integration testing. This state-of-the-art facility includes generation, storage, and interconnection technologies as well as electric power system equipment capable of simulating a real-world electric system.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The center is the first facility in the United States with a controllable grid interface test system] that has fault simulation capabilities and allows manufacturers and system operators to conduct the tests required for certification in a controlled laboratory environment.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> It is the only system in the world that is fully integrated with two dynamometers and has the capacity to extend that integration to turbines in the field and to a matrix of electronic and mechanical storage devices, all of which are located within close proximity on the same site.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_and_hydrogen_systems_research">Transportation and hydrogen systems research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transportation and hydrogen systems research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NREL spearheads <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/docs/fy15osti/63161.pdf">sustainable transportation research, development, and deployment</a> (RD&amp;D) to accelerate the widespread adoption of high-performance, low-emission, energy-efficient passenger and freight vehicles. As the only national laboratory dedicated 100% to renewable energy and energy efficiency, NREL collaborates with industry, government, and research partners to create better:</p>
<ul>
<li>Electric, hybrid, fuel cell, and conventional vehicle technologies</li>
<li>Biofuels, hydrogen, natural gas, propane, and petroleum-based fuels</li>
<li>Charging and fueling infrastructure.</li>
</ul>
<p>NREL <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/success_stories.html">research successes</a> have led to more effective <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/energystorage/">batteries</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/peem.html">drivetrains</a>, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/combustion.html">engines</a>; thermal management, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/energystorage/">energy storage</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/peem.html">power electronic</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/vtm.html">climate control</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/combustion.html">combustion</a>, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/emissions.html">emission systems</a>; and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/alternative_fuels.html">biofuel formulas</a>.</p>
<p>Sustainable transportation efforts are not limited to vehicles and fuels. NREL also explores ways to fast-track progress toward energy savings and greenhouse gas reduction goals by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/project_ev_grid_integration.html">integrating transportation technology advancements</a> with renewable energy generation, charging and fueling systems, power grids and building systems, urban planning and policy, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/fleettest.html">fleet operations</a>.</p>
<p>Transportation researchers tap the expertise of NREL's <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/hydrogen/">hydrogen and fuel cell</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/bioenergy/">biomass</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/analysis/tech_vehic_analysis.html">energy analysis</a>, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/esi/">energy systems integration</a> teams to formulate solutions that bridge technologies. Explore NREL's transportation RD&amp;D <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/capabilities.html">capabilities</a> and learn about the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/sustainable-mobility-initiative.html">Sustainable Mobility Initiative</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nrel.gov/transportation/sustainable_transportation_basics.html">sustainable transportation basics</a>.</p>
