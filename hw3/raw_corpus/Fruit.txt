<p>In <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, a <b>fruit</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>-bearing structure in <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> (also known as angiosperms) formed from the <a href="/wiki/Ovary_(plants)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovary (plants)">ovary</a> after <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering</a>.</p>
<p>Fruits are the means by which angiosperms disseminate <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>. Edible fruits, in particular, have propagated with the movements of humans and animals in a <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic relationship</a> as a means for <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal" title="Seed dispersal">seed dispersal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>; in fact, humans and many animals have become dependent on fruits as a source of food.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis375_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis375-1">[1]</a></sup> Accordingly, fruits account for a substantial fraction of the world's <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> output, and some (such as the <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a>) have acquired extensive cultural and symbolic meanings.</p>
<p>In common language usage, "fruit" normally means the fleshy seed-associated structures of a plant that are sweet or sour, and edible in the raw state, such as <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grapes" class="mw-redirect" title="Grapes">grapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberries</a>. On the other hand, in botanical usage, "fruit" includes many structures that are not commonly called "fruits", such as <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">bean</a> pods, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">kernels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> grains.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mauseth271_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mauseth271-3">[3]</a></sup> The section of a <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> that produces <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a> is also called a fruiting body.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanic_fruit_and_culinary_fruit">Botanic fruit and culinary fruit</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/220px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/330px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png/440px-Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png 2x" data-file-width="1458" data-file-height="1068" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Botanical_Fruit_and_Culinary_Vegetables.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Venn_diagram" title="Venn diagram">Venn diagram</a> representing the relationship between (culinary) vegetables and botanical fruits<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many common terms for seeds and fruit do not correspond to the botanical classifications. In culinary terminology, a <i>fruit</i> is usually any sweet-tasting plant part, especially a botanical fruit; a <i>nut</i> is any hard, oily, and shelled plant product; and a <i><a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetable</a></i> is any <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">savory</a> or less sweet plant product.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, in botany, a <i>fruit</i> is the ripened ovary or carpel that contains seeds, a <i><a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a></i> is a type of fruit and not a seed, and a <i>seed</i> is a ripened ovule.<sup id="cite_ref-McGee247_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGee247-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of culinary "vegetables" and nuts that are botanically fruit include <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cucurbitaceae" title="Cucurbitaceae">cucurbits</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cucumber" title="Cucumber">cucumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (plant)">squash</a>), <a href="/wiki/Eggplant" title="Eggplant">eggplant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>), sweet <a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">pepper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomato</a>. In addition, some <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Allspice" title="Allspice">allspice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili pepper</a>, are fruits, botanically speaking.<sup id="cite_ref-McGee247_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGee247-6">[6]</a></sup> In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Rhubarb" title="Rhubarb">rhubarb</a> is often referred to as a fruit, because it is used to make sweet desserts such as pies, though only the <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petiole</a> (leaf stalk) of the rhubarb plant is edible,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and edible <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperm</a> seeds are often given fruit names, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba" title="Ginkgo biloba">ginkgo</a> nuts and <a href="/wiki/Pine_nut" title="Pine nut">pine nuts</a>.</p>
<p>Botanically, a <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> grain, such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, is also a kind of fruit, termed a <a href="/wiki/Caryopsis" title="Caryopsis">caryopsis</a>. However, the fruit wall is very thin and is fused to the seed coat, so almost all of the edible grain is actually a seed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fruit_anatomy" title="Fruit anatomy">Fruit anatomy</a></div>
<p>The outer, often edible layer, is the <i>pericarp</i>, formed from the ovary and surrounding the seeds, although in some species other tissues contribute to or form the edible portion. The pericarp may be described in three layers from outer to inner, the <i>epicarp</i>, <i>mesocarp</i> and <i>endocarp</i>.</p>
<p>Fruit that bears a prominent pointed terminal projection is said to be <i>beaked</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

The development sequence of a typical <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nectarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Nectarine">nectarine</a> (<i>Prunus persica</i>) over a 7.5 month period, from bud formation in early winter to fruit <a href="/wiki/Ripening" title="Ripening">ripening</a> in midsummer (see <a href="/wiki/File:Nectarine_Fruit_Development.jpg" title="File:Nectarine Fruit Development.jpg">image page</a> for further information)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A fruit results from maturation of one or more flowers, and the <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium" title="Gynoecium">gynoecium</a> of the flower(s) forms all or part of the fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Inside the ovary/ovaries are one or more <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovules</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Megagametophyte" class="mw-redirect" title="Megagametophyte">megagametophyte</a> contains the egg cell.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Double_fertilization" title="Double fertilization">double fertilization</a>, these ovules will become seeds. The ovules are fertilized in a process that starts with <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, which involves the movement of pollen from the stamens to the stigma of flowers. After pollination, a tube grows from the pollen through the stigma into the ovary to the ovule and two sperm are transferred from the pollen to the megagametophyte. Within the megagametophyte one of the two sperm unites with the egg, forming a <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>, and the second sperm enters the central cell forming the endosperm mother cell, which completes the double fertilization process.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Later the zygote will give rise to the embryo of the seed, and the endosperm mother cell will give rise to <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>, a nutritive tissue used by the embryo.</p>
<p>As the ovules develop into seeds, the ovary begins to ripen and the ovary wall, the <i>pericarp</i>, may become fleshy (as in berries or <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupes</a>), or form a hard outer covering (as in nuts). In some multiseeded fruits, the extent to which the flesh develops is proportional to the number of fertilized ovules.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The pericarp is often differentiated into two or three distinct layers called the <i>exocarp</i> (outer layer, also called epicarp), <i>mesocarp</i> (middle layer), and <i>endocarp</i> (inner layer). In some fruits, especially simple fruits derived from an <a href="/wiki/Inferior_ovary" class="mw-redirect" title="Inferior ovary">inferior ovary</a>, other parts of the flower (such as the floral tube, including the <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">sepals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a>), fuse with the ovary and ripen with it. In other cases, the sepals, petals and/or stamens and <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium" title="Gynoecium">style</a> of the flower fall off. When such other floral parts are a significant part of the fruit, it is called an <i><a href="/wiki/Accessory_fruit" title="Accessory fruit">accessory fruit</a></i>. Since other parts of the flower may contribute to the structure of the fruit, it is important to study flower structure to understand how a particular fruit forms.<sup id="cite_ref-Mauseth271_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mauseth271-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are three general modes of fruit development:</p>
<ul>
<li>Apocarpous fruits develop from a single flower having one or more separate carpels, and they are the simplest fruits.</li>
<li>Syncarpous fruits develop from a single gynoecium having two or more carpels fused together.</li>
<li>Multiple fruits form from many different flowers.</li>
</ul>
<p>Plant scientists have grouped fruits into three main groups, simple fruits, aggregate fruits, and composite or multiple fruits.<sup id="cite_ref-plants_systematics_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plants_systematics-15">[15]</a></sup> The groupings are not evolutionarily relevant, since many diverse plant <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> may be in the same group, but reflect how the flower organs are arranged and how the fruits develop.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_fruit">Simple fruit</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vaccinium.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Vaccinium.jpg/220px-Vaccinium.jpg" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Vaccinium.jpg/330px-Vaccinium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Vaccinium.jpg/440px-Vaccinium.jpg 2x" data-file-width="732" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vaccinium.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Epigynous berries are simple fleshy fruit. Clockwise from top right: <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lingonberries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lingonberries">lingonberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blueberries" class="mw-redirect" title="Blueberries">blueberries</a>, red <a href="/wiki/Huckleberry" title="Huckleberry">huckleberries</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Simple fruits can be either dry or fleshy, and result from the ripening of a simple or compound ovary in a flower with only one <a href="/wiki/Carpel" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpel">pistil</a>. Dry fruits may be either <a href="/wiki/Dehiscent" class="mw-redirect" title="Dehiscent">dehiscent</a> (they open to discharge seeds), or indehiscent (they do not open to discharge seeds).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Types of dry, simple fruits, and examples of each, include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Achene" title="Achene">achene</a> – Most commonly seen in aggregate fruits (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Capsule_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capsule (fruit)">capsule</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Brazil_nut" title="Brazil nut">Brazil nut</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caryopsis" title="Caryopsis">caryopsis</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Achene" title="Achene">cypsela</a> – an achene-like fruit derived from the individual florets in a <a href="/wiki/Head_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Head (botany)">capitulum</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">fibrous drupe</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Follicle_(fruit)" title="Follicle (fruit)">follicle</a> – is formed from a single carpel, opens by one suture (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Milkweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Milkweed">milkweed</a>), commonly seen in aggregate fruits (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">magnolia</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">pea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Loment" title="Loment">loment</a> – a type of <a href="/wiki/Indehiscent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indehiscent">indehiscent</a> legume</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnut</a>, oak <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorn</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samara_(fruit)" title="Samara (fruit)">samara</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Ash_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ash tree">ash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">elm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> key)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schizocarp" title="Schizocarp">schizocarp</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrot</a> seed)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silique" title="Silique">silique</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radish</a> seed)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicle">silicle</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Shepherd%27s_purse" class="mw-redirect" title="Shepherd's purse">shepherd's purse</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Utricle_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Utricle (fruit)">utricle</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Beet" class="mw-redirect" title="Beet">beet</a>)</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lilyfruit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lilyfruit.jpg/170px-Lilyfruit.jpg" width="170" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lilyfruit.jpg/255px-Lilyfruit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Lilyfruit.jpg/340px-Lilyfruit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="749" data-file-height="900" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lilyfruit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Lilium" title="Lilium">Lilium</a></i> unripe capsule fruit</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Fruits in which part or all of the <i>pericarp</i> (fruit wall) is fleshy at maturity are <i>simple fleshy fruits</i>. Types of simple, fleshy, fruits (with examples) include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Berry_(botany)" title="Berry (botany)">berry</a> – (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gooseberry" title="Gooseberry">gooseberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redcurrant" title="Redcurrant">redcurrant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomato</a>)</li>
<li>stone fruit or <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Apricot" title="Apricot">apricot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plum" title="Plum">plum</a>)</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DewberriesWeb.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/DewberriesWeb.jpg/170px-DewberriesWeb.jpg" width="170" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/DewberriesWeb.jpg/255px-DewberriesWeb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/DewberriesWeb.jpg/340px-DewberriesWeb.jpg 2x" data-file-width="543" data-file-height="704" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DewberriesWeb.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Dewberry" title="Dewberry">Dewberry</a> flowers. Note the multiple <a href="/wiki/Pistil" class="mw-redirect" title="Pistil">pistils</a>, each of which will produce a <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupelet</a>. Each flower will become a blackberry-like <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_fruit" title="Aggregate fruit">aggregate fruit</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An aggregate fruit, or <i>etaerio</i>, develops from a single flower with numerous simple pistils.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">Magnolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peony" title="Peony">peony</a>, collection of follicles developing from one flower.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sweet_gum" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweet gum">Sweet gum</a>, collection of capsules.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sycamore" title="Sycamore">Sycamore</a>, collection of achenes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Teasel" class="mw-redirect" title="Teasel">Teasel</a>, collection of cypsellas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tuliptree" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuliptree">Tuliptree</a>, collection of samaras.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pome" title="Pome">pome</a> fruits of the family <a href="/wiki/Rosaceae" title="Rosaceae">Rosaceae</a>, (including <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosehip" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosehip">rosehips</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon_berry" class="mw-redirect" title="Saskatoon berry">saskatoon berry</a>) are a syncarpous fleshy fruit, a simple fruit, developing from a half-inferior ovary.<sup id="cite_ref-evolutionary_trends_in_flowering_plants_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evolutionary_trends_in_flowering_plants-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Schizocarp" title="Schizocarp">Schizocarp</a> fruits form from a syncarpous ovary and do not really <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)" title="Dehiscence (botany)">dehisce</a>, but rather split into segments with one or more seeds; they include a number of different forms from a wide range of families.<sup id="cite_ref-plants_systematics_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plants_systematics-15">[15]</a></sup> Carrot seed is an example.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_fruit">Aggregate fruit</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_fruit" title="Aggregate fruit">Aggregate fruit</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif/220px-Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif/330px-Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="209" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Longitudinal_section_of_raspberry_flower.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Detail of raspberry <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Aggregate fruits form from single flowers that have multiple carpels which are not joined together, i.e. each pistil contains one carpel. Each pistil forms a fruitlet, and collectively the fruitlets are called an etaerio. Four types of aggregate fruits include etaerios of achenes, follicles, drupelets, and berries. Ranunculaceae species, including <i><a href="/wiki/Clematis" title="Clematis">Clematis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ranunculus" title="Ranunculus">Ranunculus</a></i> have an etaerio of achenes, <i><a href="/wiki/Calotropis" title="Calotropis">Calotropis</a></i> has an etaerio of follicles, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">Rubus</a></i> species like raspberry, have an etaerio of drupelets. <i><a href="/wiki/Annona" title="Annona">Annona</a></i> have an etaerio of berries.<sup id="cite_ref-Gupta_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberry</a>, whose pistils are termed <i>drupelets</i> because each is like a small <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a> attached to the receptacle. In some <a href="/wiki/Bramble" title="Bramble">bramble</a> fruits (such as <a href="/wiki/Blackberry" title="Blackberry">blackberry</a>) the receptacle is elongated and part of the ripe fruit, making the blackberry an <i>aggregate-accessory</i> fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a> is also an aggregate-accessory fruit, only one in which the seeds are contained in <a href="/wiki/Achene" title="Achene">achenes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In all these examples, the fruit develops from a single flower with numerous pistils.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_fruits">Multiple fruits</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Multiple_fruit" title="Multiple fruit">Multiple fruit</a></div>
<p>A multiple fruit is one formed from a cluster of flowers (called an <i><a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a></i>). Each flower produces a fruit, but these mature into a single mass.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mulberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry">mulberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osage-orange" class="mw-redirect" title="Osage-orange">osage-orange</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Breadfruit" title="Breadfruit">breadfruit</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Noni_fruit_dev.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Noni_fruit_dev.jpg/220px-Noni_fruit_dev.jpg" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Noni_fruit_dev.jpg/330px-Noni_fruit_dev.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Noni_fruit_dev.jpg/440px-Noni_fruit_dev.jpg 2x" data-file-width="464" data-file-height="285" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Noni_fruit_dev.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In some plants, such as this <a href="/wiki/Noni" class="mw-redirect" title="Noni">noni</a>, flowers are produced regularly along the stem and it is possible to see together examples of flowering, fruit development, and fruit ripening.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the photograph on the right, stages of flowering and fruit development in the <a href="/wiki/Noni" class="mw-redirect" title="Noni">noni</a> or Indian mulberry (<i>Morinda citrifolia</i>) can be observed on a single branch. First an inflorescence of white flowers called a head is produced. After <a href="/wiki/Fertilization#Fertilisation_in_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a>, each flower develops into a drupe, and as the drupes expand, they become <i>connate</i> (merge) into a <i>multiple fleshy fruit</i> called a <i>syncarp</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berries">Berries</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Berry" title="Berry">Berry</a></div>
<p>Berries are another type of fleshy fruit; they are simple fruit created from a single ovary. The ovary may be compound, with several carpels. Types include (examples follow in the table below):</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pepo_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pepo (botany)">Pepo</a> – berries whose skin is hardened, <a href="/wiki/Cucurbitaceae" title="Cucurbitaceae">cucurbits</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hesperidium" title="Hesperidium">Hesperidium</a> – berries with a rind and a juicy interior, like most <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> fruit</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessory_fruit">Accessory fruit</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg/220px-Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg/330px-Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg/440px-Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1772" data-file-height="1488" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pineapple_and_cross_section.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The fruit of a pineapple includes tissue from the <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">sepals</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Pistil" class="mw-redirect" title="Pistil">pistils</a> of many flowers. It is an <a href="/wiki/Accessory_fruit" title="Accessory fruit">accessory fruit</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Multiple_fruit" title="Multiple fruit">multiple fruit</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accessory_fruit" title="Accessory fruit">Accessory fruit</a></div>
<p>Some or all of the edible part of accessory fruit is not generated by the ovary. Accessory fruit can be simple, aggregate, or multiple, i.e., they can include one or more pistils and other parts from the same flower, or the pistils and other parts of many flowers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seedless_fruits">Seedless fruits</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FruitArrangement.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/FruitArrangement.jpg/220px-FruitArrangement.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/FruitArrangement.jpg/330px-FruitArrangement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/FruitArrangement.jpg/440px-FruitArrangement.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FruitArrangement.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An arrangement of fruits commonly thought of as vegetables, including <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Squash_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (fruit)">squash</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Seedlessness is an important feature of some fruits of commerce. Commercial <a href="/wiki/Cultivars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultivars">cultivars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pineapples" class="mw-redirect" title="Pineapples">pineapples</a> are examples of <a href="/wiki/Seedless_fruit" title="Seedless fruit">seedless fruits</a>. Some cultivars of <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> fruits (especially <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin oranges</a>, navel <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mikan" class="mw-redirect" title="Mikan">satsumas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Table_grape" title="Table grape">table grapes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Watermelon" title="Watermelon">watermelons</a> are valued for their seedlessness. In some species, seedlessness is the result of <i><a href="/wiki/Parthenocarpy" title="Parthenocarpy">parthenocarpy</a></i>, where fruits set without fertilization. Parthenocarpic fruit set may or may not require pollination, but most seedless citrus fruits require a stimulus from pollination to produce fruit.</p>
<p>Seedless bananas and grapes are <a href="/wiki/Triploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Triploid">triploids</a>, and seedlessness results from the abortion of the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryonic</a> plant that is produced by fertilization, a phenomenon known as <i><a href="/wiki/Stenospermocarpy" title="Stenospermocarpy">stenospermocarpy</a></i>, which requires normal pollination and fertilization.<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegel87_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel87-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_dissemination">Seed dissemination</span></h2>
<p>Variations in fruit structures largely depend on their seeds' <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">mode of dispersal</a>. This dispersal can be achieved by animals, <a href="/wiki/Explosive_dehiscence" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive dehiscence">explosive dehiscence</a>, water, or wind.<sup id="cite_ref-Capon198_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capon198-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG/220px-Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG/330px-Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG/440px-Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Grape_Mango_Rezowan.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grapes and Mangoes</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Some fruits have coats covered with spikes or hooked burrs, either to prevent themselves from being eaten by <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, or to stick to the feathers, hairs, or legs of animals, using them as dispersal agents. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Cocklebur" class="mw-redirect" title="Cocklebur">cocklebur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unicorn_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Unicorn plant">unicorn plant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sweet flesh of many fruits is "deliberately" appealing to animals, so that the seeds held within are eaten and "unwittingly" carried away and deposited (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecated</a>) at a distance from the parent. Likewise, the nutritious, oily kernels of <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a> are appealing to rodents (such as <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">squirrels</a>), which <a href="/wiki/Hoarding" title="Hoarding">hoard</a> them in the soil to avoid starving during the winter, thus giving those seeds that remain uneaten the chance to <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germinate</a> and grow into a new plant away from their parent.<sup id="cite_ref-McGee247_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGee247-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other fruits are elongated and flattened out naturally, and so become thin, like <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> or <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> blades, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">elm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuliptree" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuliptree">tuliptree</a>. This is an <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolutionary</a> mechanism to increase dispersal distance away from the parent, via wind. Other wind-dispersed fruit have tiny "<a href="/wiki/Pappus_(flower_structure)" title="Pappus (flower structure)">parachutes</a>", e.g., <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asclepias" title="Asclepias">milkweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tragopogon" title="Tragopogon">salsify</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Capon198_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capon198-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">Coconut</a> fruits can float thousands of miles in the ocean to spread seeds. Some other fruits that can disperse via water are <a href="/wiki/Nipa_palm" class="mw-redirect" title="Nipa palm">nipa palm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screw_pine" class="mw-redirect" title="Screw pine">screw pine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Capon198_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capon198-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some fruits fling seeds substantial distances (up to 100&#160;m in <a href="/wiki/Sandbox_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandbox tree">sandbox tree</a>) via <a href="/wiki/Explosive_dehiscence" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive dehiscence">explosive dehiscence</a> or other mechanisms, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Impatiens" title="Impatiens">impatiens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squirting_cucumber" class="mw-redirect" title="Squirting cucumber">squirting cucumber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stewed_nectarines.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Stewed_nectarines.JPG/220px-Stewed_nectarines.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Stewed_nectarines.JPG/330px-Stewed_nectarines.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Stewed_nectarines.JPG/440px-Stewed_nectarines.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2280" data-file-height="1709" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Nectarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Nectarine">Nectarines</a> are one of many fruits that can be easily stewed.</div>

</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Misc_fruit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Misc_fruit.jpg/220px-Misc_fruit.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Misc_fruit.jpg/330px-Misc_fruit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Misc_fruit.jpg/440px-Misc_fruit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>

<p>Many hundreds of fruits, including fleshy fruits (like <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiwifruit" title="Kiwifruit">kiwifruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mango" title="Mango">mango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pear</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Watermelon" title="Watermelon">watermelon</a>) are commercially valuable as <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> food, eaten both fresh and as jams, marmalade and other <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">preserves</a>. Fruits are also used in manufactured foods (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cookie" title="Cookie">cookies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muffin" title="Muffin">muffins</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>) or beverages, such as fruit juices (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Apple_juice" title="Apple juice">apple juice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grape_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Grape juice">grape juice</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Orange_juice" title="Orange juice">orange juice</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverages" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverages">alcoholic beverages</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_beer" title="Fruit beer">fruit beer</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Fruits are also used for gift giving, e.g., in the form of <a href="/wiki/Fruit_Basket" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit Basket">Fruit Baskets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fruit_Bouquet" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit Bouquet">Fruit Bouquets</a>.</p>
<p>Many "vegetables" in culinary <i>parlance</i> are botanical fruits, including <a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">bell pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cucumber" title="Cucumber">cucumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eggplant" title="Eggplant">eggplant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_bean" title="Green bean">green bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Okra" title="Okra">okra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squash_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (fruit)">squash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomato</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zucchini" title="Zucchini">zucchini</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">Olive</a> fruit is pressed for <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>. Spices like <a href="/wiki/Allspice" title="Allspice">allspice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paprika" title="Paprika">paprika</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a> are derived from berries.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Fresh fruits are generally high in <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hulme1970_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hulme1970-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regular consumption of fruit is generally associated with reduced risks of several diseases and functional declines associated with aging.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonfood_uses">Nonfood uses</span></h3>
<p>Because fruits have been such a major part of the human diet, various cultures have developed many different uses for fruits they do not depend on for food. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bayberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayberry">Bayberry</a> fruits provide a wax often used to make candles;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Many dry fruits are used as decorations or in dried flower arrangements (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Annual_honesty" class="mw-redirect" title="Annual honesty">annual honesty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotoneaster" title="Cotoneaster">cotoneaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nelumbo" title="Nelumbo">lotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milkweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Milkweed">milkweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unicorn_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Unicorn plant">unicorn plant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>). <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornamental tree">Ornamental trees</a> and shrubs are often cultivated for their colorful fruits, including <a href="/wiki/Beautyberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Beautyberry">beautyberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotoneaster" title="Cotoneaster">cotoneaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyracantha" title="Pyracantha">pyracantha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skimmia" title="Skimmia">skimmia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Viburnum" title="Viburnum">viburnum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Fruits of <a href="/wiki/Opium_poppy" class="mw-redirect" title="Opium poppy">opium poppy</a> are the source of <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, which contains the drugs <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, as well as the biologically inactive chemical theabaine from which the drug <a href="/wiki/Oxycodone" title="Oxycodone">oxycodone</a> is synthesized.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Osage_orange" class="mw-redirect" title="Osage orange">Osage orange</a> fruits are used to repel <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Many fruits provide <a href="/wiki/Natural_dye" title="Natural dye">natural dyes</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mulberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry">mulberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumac" title="Sumac">sumac</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>Dried <a href="/wiki/Gourd" title="Gourd">gourds</a> are used as bird houses, cups, decorations, dishes, musical instruments, and water jugs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">Pumpkins</a> are carved into <a href="/wiki/Jack-o%27-lantern" title="Jack-o'-lantern">Jack-o'-lanterns</a> for <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>.</li>
<li>The spiny fruit of <a href="/wiki/Burdock" class="mw-redirect" title="Burdock">burdock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cocklebur" class="mw-redirect" title="Cocklebur">cocklebur</a> inspired the invention of <a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">Velcro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coir" title="Coir">Coir</a> fiber from <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a> shells is used for brushes, doormats, floor tiles, insulation, mattresses, sacking, and as a growing medium for container plants. The shell of the coconut fruit is used to make bird houses, bowls, cups, musical instruments, and souvenir heads.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Fruit is often a subject of <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> paintings.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">food safety</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">CDC</a> recommends proper fruit handling and preparation to reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Food_contamination" class="mw-redirect" title="Food contamination">food contamination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne illness</a>. Fresh fruits and vegetables should be carefully selected; at the store, they should not be damaged or bruised; and precut pieces should be refrigerated or surrounded by ice.</p>
<p>All fruits and vegetables should be rinsed before eating. This recommendation also applies to produce with rinds or skins that are not eaten. It should be done just before preparing or eating to avoid premature spoilage.</p>
<p>Fruits and vegetables should be kept separate from raw foods like meat, poultry, and seafood, as well as from utensils that have come in contact with raw foods. Fruits and vegetables that are not going to be cooked should be thrown away if they have touched raw meat, poultry, seafood, or eggs.</p>
<p>All cut, peeled, or cooked fruits and vegetables should be refrigerated within two hours. After a certain time, harmful bacteria may grow on them and increase the risk of foodborne illness.<sup id="cite_ref-cdc_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdc-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allergies">Allergies</span></h2>
<p>Fruit allergies make up about 10 percent of all food related allergies.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span></h2>
<p>All fruits benefit from proper post harvest care, and in many fruits, the plant hormone <a href="/wiki/Ethylene-ripened_fruits" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethylene-ripened fruits">ethylene</a> causes <a href="/wiki/Ripening" title="Ripening">ripening</a>. Therefore, maintaining most fruits in an efficient <a href="/wiki/Cold_chain" title="Cold chain">cold chain</a> is optimal for post harvest storage, with the aim of extending and ensuring shelf life.<sup id="cite_ref-pxkf_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pxkf-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
