			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecosystem</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>An <b>ecosystem</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">community</a> of living organisms in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Abiotic_component" title="Abiotic component">nonliving components</a> of their environment (things like air, water and mineral soil), interacting as a system.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These biotic and <a href="/wiki/Abiotic_component" title="Abiotic component">abiotic components</a> are regarded as linked together through nutrient cycles and energy flows.<sup id="cite_ref-Odum1971_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Odum1971-3">[3]</a></sup> As ecosystems are defined by the network of interactions among organisms, and between organisms and their environment,<sup id="cite_ref-Schulze400_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze400-4">[4]</a></sup> they can be of any size but usually encompass specific, limited spaces<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (although some scientists say that the entire planet is an ecosystem).<sup id="cite_ref-planet_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Energy, water, nitrogen and soil minerals are other essential abiotic components of an ecosystem. The energy that flows through ecosystems is obtained primarily from the sun. It generally enters the system through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, a process that also captures <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> from the atmosphere. By feeding on plants and on one another, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> play an important role in the movement of matter and energy through the system. They also influence the quantity of plant and <a href="/wiki/Microbe" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbe">microbial</a> <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> present. By breaking down dead organic matter, <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposers</a> release carbon back to the atmosphere and facilitate <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a> by converting nutrients stored in dead biomass back to a form that can be readily used by plants and other microbes.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin10_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin10-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecosystems are controlled both by external and internal factors. External factors such as <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parent_material" title="Parent material">parent material</a> that forms the soil, and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> control the overall structure of an ecosystem and the way things work within it, but are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> Other external factors include time and potential <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>. Ecosystems are dynamic entities—invariably, they are subject to periodic disturbances and are in the process of recovering from some past disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> Ecosystems in similar environments that are located in different parts of the world can have very different characteristics simply because they contain different species.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduction of non-native species</a> can cause substantial shifts in ecosystem function. Internal factors not only control ecosystem processes but are also controlled by them and are often subject to <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback loops</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> While the <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resource</a> inputs are generally controlled by external processes like climate and parent material, the availability of these resources within the ecosystem is controlled by internal factors like decomposition, root competition or shading.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> Other internal factors include disturbance, succession and the types of species present. Although humans exist and operate within ecosystems, their cumulative effects are large enough to influence external factors like climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a> affects ecosystem function, as do the processes of <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>. Ecosystems provide a variety of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">goods and services</a> upon which people depend; the principles of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_management" title="Ecosystem management">ecosystem management</a> suggest that rather than managing individual species, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> should be managed at the level of the ecosystem itself. Classifying ecosystems into ecologically homogeneous units is an important step towards effective ecosystem management, but there is no single, agreed-upon way to do this.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "ecosystem" was first used in a publication by British ecologist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tansley" title="Arthur Tansley">Arthur Tansley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[fn 1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Willis1997_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willis1997-11">[10]</a></sup> Tansley devised the concept to draw attention to the importance of transfers of materials between organisms and their environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin7_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin7-12">[11]</a></sup> He later refined the term, describing it as "The whole system, ... including not only the organism-complex, but also the whole complex of physical factors forming what we call the environment".<sup id="cite_ref-Tansley1935_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1935-13">[12]</a></sup> Tansley regarded ecosystems not simply as natural units, but as mental isolates.<sup id="cite_ref-Tansley1935_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1935-13">[12]</a></sup> Tansley later<sup id="cite_ref-Tansley1939_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1939-14">[13]</a></sup> defined the spatial extent of ecosystems using the term <a href="/wiki/Ecotope" title="Ecotope">ecotope</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a>, a pioneering <a href="/wiki/Limnologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Limnologist">limnologist</a> who was a contemporary of Tansley's, combined <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton</a>'s ideas about <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/trophic" class="extiw" title="wikt:Special:Search/trophic">trophic</a> ecology with those of Russian geochemist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Vernadsky" title="Vladimir Vernadsky">Vladimir Vernadsky</a> to suggest that mineral nutrient availability in a lake limited algal <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">production</a> which would, in turn, limit the abundance of animals that feed on algae. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Lindeman" title="Raymond Lindeman">Raymond Lindeman</a> took these ideas one step further to suggest that the flow of energy through a lake was the primary driver of the ecosystem. Hutchinson's students, brothers <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Howard T. Odum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugene_P._Odum" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene P. Odum">Eugene P. Odum</a>, further developed a "systems approach" to the study of ecosystems, allowing them to study the flow of energy and material through ecological systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin7_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin7-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_processes">Ecosystem processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecosystem processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> enter ecosystems through photosynthesis, are incorporated into living tissue, transferred to other organisms that feed on the living and dead plant matter, and eventually released through respiration.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin123_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin123-15">[14]</a></sup> Most mineral nutrients, on the other hand, are recycled within ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecosystems are controlled both by external and internal factors. External factors, also called state factors, control the overall structure of an ecosystem and the way things work within it, but are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem. The most important of these is <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> Climate determines the <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a> in which the ecosystem is embedded. Rainfall patterns and temperature seasonality determine the amount of water available to the ecosystem and the supply of energy available (by influencing photosynthesis).<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parent_material" title="Parent material">Parent material</a>, the underlying geological material that gives rise to soils, determines the nature of the soils present, and influences the supply of mineral nutrients. <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">Topography</a> also controls ecosystem processes by affecting things like <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a>, soil development and the movement of water through a system. This may be the difference between the ecosystem present in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> situated in a small depression on the landscape, and one present on an adjacent steep hillside.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other external factors that play an important role in ecosystem functioning include time and potential <a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>. Ecosystems are dynamic entities—invariably, they are subject to periodic disturbances and are in the process of recovering from some past disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> Time plays a role in the development of soil from bare rock and the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">recovery of a community from disturbance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> Similarly, the set of organisms that can potentially be present in an area can also have a major impact on ecosystems. Ecosystems in similar environments that are located in different parts of the world can end up doing things very differently simply because they have different pools of species present.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduction of non-native species</a> can cause substantial shifts in ecosystem function.</p>
<p>Unlike external factors, internal factors in ecosystems not only control ecosystem processes, but are also controlled by them. Consequently, they are often subject to <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback loops</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> While the <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resource</a> inputs are generally controlled by external processes like climate and parent material, the availability of these resources within the ecosystem is controlled by internal factors like decomposition, root competition or shading.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup> Other factors like disturbance, succession or the types of species present are also internal factors. Human activities are important in almost all ecosystems. Although humans exist and operate within ecosystems, their cumulative effects are large enough to influence external factors like climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin11_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_production">Primary production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Primary production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">Primary production</a></div>
<p>Primary production is the production of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> from inorganic carbon sources. Overwhelmingly, this occurs through photosynthesis. The energy incorporated through this process supports life on earth, while the carbon makes up much of the organic matter in living and dead biomass, <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. It also drives the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, which influences global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.</p>
<p>Through the process of photosynthesis, plants capture energy from light and use it to combine <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The photosynthesis carried out by all the plants in an ecosystem is called the gross primary production (GPP).<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin97_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin97-17">[16]</a></sup> About 48–60% of the GPP is consumed in plant respiration. The remainder, that portion of GPP that is not used up by respiration, is known as the net primary production (NPP).<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin123_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin123-15">[14]</a></sup> Total photosynthesis is limited by a range of environmental factors. These include the amount of light available, the amount of <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaf</a> area a plant has to capture light (shading by other plants is a major limitation of photosynthesis), rate at which carbon dioxide can be supplied to the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> to support photosynthesis, the availability of water, and the availability of suitable temperatures for carrying out <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin97_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin97-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_flow">Energy flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">Food web</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">Trophic level</a></div>
<p>The carbon and energy incorporated into plant tissues (net primary production) is either consumed by animals while the plant is alive, or it remains uneaten when the plant tissue dies and becomes <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem" title="Terrestrial ecosystem">terrestrial ecosystems</a>, roughly 90% of the NPP ends up being broken down by <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposers</a>. The remainder is either consumed by animals while still alive and enters the plant-based trophic system, or it is consumed after it has died, and enters the detritus-based trophic system. In <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic systems</a>, the proportion of plant biomass that gets consumed by <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> is much higher.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin244_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-19">[18]</a></sup> In trophic systems photosynthetic organisms are the primary producers. The organisms that consume their tissues are called primary consumers or <a href="/wiki/Secondary_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary production">secondary producers</a>—<a href="/wiki/Herbivores" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a>. Organisms which feed on <a href="/wiki/Microbe" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbe">microbes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>) are termed microbivores. Animals that feed on primary consumers—<a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a>—are secondary consumers. Each of these constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin244_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-19">[18]</a></sup> The sequence of consumption—from plant to herbivore, to carnivore—forms a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. Real systems are much more complex than this—organisms will generally feed on more than one form of food, and may feed at more than one trophic level. Carnivores may capture some prey which are part of a plant-based trophic system and others that are part of a detritus-based trophic system (a bird that feeds both on herbivorous grasshoppers and earthworms, which consume detritus). Real systems, with all these complexities, form <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a> rather than food chains.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin244_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition">Decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">Decomposition</a></div>
<p>The carbon and nutrients in <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">dead organic matter</a> are broken down by a group of processes known as decomposition. This releases nutrients that can then be re-used for plant and microbial production, and returns carbon dioxide to the atmosphere (or water) where it can be used for photosynthesis. In the absence of decomposition, dead organic matter would accumulate in an ecosystem and nutrients and atmospheric carbon dioxide would be depleted.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup> Approximately 90% of terrestrial NPP goes directly from plant to decomposer.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin244_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decomposition processes can be separated into three categories—<a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a>, fragmentation and chemical alteration of dead material. As water moves through dead organic matter, it dissolves and carries with it the water-soluble components. These are then taken up by organisms in the soil, react with mineral soil, or are transported beyond the confines of the ecosystem (and are considered "lost" to it).<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup> Newly shed leaves and newly dead animals have high concentrations of water-soluble components, and include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> and mineral nutrients. Leaching is more important in wet environments, and much less important in dry ones.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fragmentation processes break organic material into smaller pieces, exposing new surfaces for colonization by microbes. Freshly shed <a href="/wiki/Leaf_litter" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf litter">leaf litter</a> may be inaccessible due to an outer layer of <a href="/wiki/Plant_cuticle" title="Plant cuticle">cuticle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bark" title="Bark">bark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protoplasm" title="Protoplasm">cell contents</a> are protected by a <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a>. Newly dead animals may be covered by an <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>. Fragmentation processes, which break through these protective layers, accelerate the rate of microbial decomposition.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup> Animals fragment detritus as they hunt for food, as does passage through the gut. <a href="/wiki/Freeze-thaw_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Freeze-thaw cycle">Freeze-thaw cycles</a> and cycles of wetting and drying also fragment dead material.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The chemical alteration of dead organic matter is primarily achieved through bacterial and fungal action. Fungal <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphae</a> produce enzymes which can break through the tough outer structures surrounding dead plant material. They also produce enzymes which break down <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, which allows to them access to both cell contents and to the nitrogen in the lignin. Fungi can transfer carbon and nitrogen through their hyphal networks and thus, unlike bacteria, are not dependent solely on locally available resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin151_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decomposition rates vary among ecosystems. The rate of decomposition is governed by three sets of factors—the physical environment (temperature, moisture and soil properties), the quantity and quality of the dead material available to decomposers, and the nature of the microbial community itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> Temperature controls the rate of microbial respiration; the higher the temperature, the faster microbial decomposition occurs. It also affects soil moisture, which slows microbial growth and reduces leaching. Freeze-thaw cycles also affect decomposition—freezing temperatures kill soil microorganisms, which allows leaching to play a more important role in moving nutrients around. This can be especially important as the soil thaws in the Spring, creating a pulse of nutrients which become available.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Decomposition rates are low under very wet or very dry conditions. Decomposition rates are highest in wet, moist conditions with adequate levels of oxygen. Wet soils tend to become deficient in oxygen (this is especially true in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>), which slows microbial growth. In dry soils, decomposition slows as well, but bacteria continue to grow (albeit at a slower rate) even after soils become too dry to support plant growth. When the rains return and soils become wet, the <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_gradient" class="mw-redirect" title="Osmotic gradient">osmotic gradient</a> between the bacterial cells and the soil water causes the cells to gain water quickly. Under these conditions, many bacterial cells burst, releasing a pulse of nutrients.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> Decomposition rates also tend to be slower in acidic soils.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> Soils which are rich in <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a> tend to have lower decomposition rates, and thus, higher levels of organic matter.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> The smaller particles of clay result in a larger surface area that can hold water. The higher the water content of a soil, the lower the oxygen content<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin61_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin61-22">[21]</a></sup> and consequently, the lower the rate of decomposition. Clay minerals also bind particles of organic material to their surface, making them less accessibly to microbes.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> Soil disturbance like <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tilling</a> increase decomposition by increasing the amount of oxygen in the soil and by exposing new organic matter to soil microbes.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The quality and quantity of the material available to decomposers is another major factor that influences the rate of decomposition. Substances like sugars and amino acids decompose readily and are considered "labile". <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">Cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, which are broken down more slowly, are "moderately labile". Compounds which are more resistant to decay, like lignin or <a href="/wiki/Cutin" title="Cutin">cutin</a>, are considered "recalcitrant".<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> Litter with a higher proportion of labile compounds decomposes much more rapidly than does litter with a higher proportion of recalcitrant material. Consequently, dead animals decompose more rapidly than dead leaves, which themselves decompose more rapidly than fallen branches.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup> As organic material in the soil ages, its quality decreases. The more labile compounds decompose quickly, leaving an increasing proportion of recalcitrant material. Microbial cell walls also contain recalcitrant materials like <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>, and these also accumulate as the microbes die, further reducing the quality of older <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin159_21-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_cycling">Nutrient cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nutrient cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycle" title="Nutrient cycle">Nutrient cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">Biogeochemical cycle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">Nitrogen cycle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg/400px-Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg" width="400" height="312" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="475" data-file-height="370" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Biological nitrogen cycling</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ecosystems continually exchange energy and carbon with the wider <a href="/wiki/Environment_(systems)" title="Environment (systems)">environment</a>; mineral nutrients, on the other hand, are mostly cycled back and forth between plants, animals, microbes and the soil. Most nitrogen enters ecosystems through biological <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>, is deposited through precipitation, dust, gases or is applied as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup> Since most terrestrial ecosystems are nitrogen-limited, nitrogen cycling is an important control on ecosystem production.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Until modern times, nitrogen fixation was the major source of nitrogen for ecosystems. Nitrogen fixing bacteria either live <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotically</a> with plants, or live freely in the soil. The energetic cost is high for plants which support nitrogen-fixing symbionts—as much as 25% of GPP when measured in controlled conditions. Many members of the <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> plant family support nitrogen-fixing symbionts. Some <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> are also capable of nitrogen fixation. These are <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophs</a>, which carry out photosynthesis. Like other nitrogen-fixing bacteria, they can either be free-living or have symbiotic relationships with plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup> Other sources of nitrogen include <a href="/wiki/Acid_deposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Acid deposition">acid deposition</a> produced through the combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> gas which evaporates from agricultural fields which have had fertilizers applied to them, and dust.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup> Anthropogenic nitrogen inputs account for about 80% of all nitrogen fluxes in ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>When plant tissues are shed or are eaten, the nitrogen in those tissues becomes available to animals and microbes. Microbial decomposition releases nitrogen compounds from dead organic matter in the soil, where plants, fungi and bacteria compete for it. Some soil bacteria use organic nitrogen-containing compounds as a source of carbon, and release <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ions into the soil. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Ammonification" class="mw-redirect" title="Ammonification">nitrogen mineralization</a>. Others convert ammonium to <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> ions, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Nitrification" title="Nitrification">nitrification</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">Nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> are also produced during nitrification.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup> Under nitrogen-rich and oxygen-poor conditions, nitrates and nitrites are converted to <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen gas</a>, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin197_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other important nutrients include <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin215_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-23">[22]</a></sup> Phosphorus enters ecosystems through <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. As ecosystems age this supply diminishes, making phosphorus-limitation more common in older landscapes (especially in the tropics).<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin215_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-23">[22]</a></sup> Calcium and sulfur are also produced by weathering, but acid deposition is an important source of sulfur in many ecosystems. Although magnesium and manganese are produced by weathering, exchanges between soil organic matter and living cells account for a significant portion of ecosystem fluxes. Potassium is primarily cycled between living cells and soil organic matter.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin215_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-23">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ecosystem processes are broad generalizations that actually take place through the actions of individual organisms. The nature of the organisms—the species, <a href="/wiki/Functional_group_(ecology)" title="Functional group (ecology)">functional groups</a> and trophic levels to which they belong—dictates the sorts of actions these individuals are capable of carrying out, and the relative efficiency with which they do so. Thus, ecosystem processes are driven by the number of species in an ecosystem, the exact nature of each individual species, and the relative abundance organisms within these species.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin265_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-25">[24]</a></sup> Biodiversity plays an important role in ecosystem functioning.<sup id="cite_ref-Schulze449_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze449-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecological theory suggests that in order to coexist, species must have some level of <a href="/wiki/Limiting_similarity" title="Limiting similarity">limiting similarity</a>—they must be different from one another in some fundamental way, otherwise one species would <a href="/wiki/Competitive_exclusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Competitive exclusion">competitively exclude</a> the other.<sup id="cite_ref-niche_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-niche-27">[26]</a></sup> Despite this, the cumulative effect of additional species in an ecosystem is not linear—additional species may enhance nitrogen retention, for example, but beyond some level of species richness, additional species may have little additive effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin265_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-25">[24]</a></sup> The addition (or loss) of species which are ecologically similar to those already present in an ecosystem tends to only have a small effect on ecosystem function. Ecologically distinct species, on the other hand, have a much larger effect. Similarly, dominant species have a large impact on ecosystem function, while rare species tend to have a small effect. <a href="/wiki/Keystone_species" title="Keystone species">Keystone species</a> tend to have an effect on ecosystem function that is disproportionate to their abundance in an ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin265_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_goods_and_services">Ecosystem goods and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ecosystem goods and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_goods_and_services" title="Ecological goods and services">Ecological goods and services</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">Ecosystem valuation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_yield" title="Ecological yield">Ecological yield</a></div>
<p>Ecosystems provide a variety of goods and services upon which people depend.<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> Ecosystem goods include the "tangible, material products"<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-29">[28]</a></sup> of ecosystem processes—food, construction material, medicinal plants—in addition to less tangible items like tourism and recreation, and genes from wild plants and animals that can be used to improve domestic species.<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> Ecosystem services, on the other hand, are generally "improvements in the condition or location of things of value".<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-29">[28]</a></sup> These include things like the maintenance of hydrological cycles, cleaning air and water, the maintenance of oxygen in the atmosphere, crop pollination and even things like beauty, inspiration and opportunities for research.<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> While ecosystem goods have traditionally been recognized as being the basis for things of economic value, ecosystem services tend to be taken for granted.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-29">[28]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Gretchen_Daily" title="Gretchen Daily">Gretchen Daily</a>'s original definition distinguished between ecosystem goods and ecosystem services, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Costanza" title="Robert Costanza">Robert Costanza</a> and colleagues' later work and that of the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> lumped all of these together as ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_management">Ecosystem management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ecosystem management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_management" title="Ecosystem management">Ecosystem management</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">Sustainability</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a></div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a> is applied to whole ecosystems, rather than single species, it is termed ecosystem management.<sup id="cite_ref-Grumbine1994_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grumbine1994-30">[29]</a></sup> A variety of definitions exist: <a href="/wiki/F._Stuart_Chapin_III" title="F. Stuart Chapin III">F. Stuart Chapin</a> and coauthors define it as "the application of ecological science to resource management to promote long-term sustainability of ecosystems and the delivery of essential ecosystem goods and services",<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin362_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin362-31">[30]</a></sup> while Norman Christensen and coauthors defined it as "management driven by explicit goals, executed by policies, protocols, and practices, and made adaptable by monitoring and research based on our best understanding of the ecological interactions and processes necessary to sustain ecosystem structure and function"<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> and Peter Brussard and colleagues defined it as "managing areas at various scales in such a way that ecosystem services and biological resources are preserved while appropriate human use and options for livelihood are sustained".<sup id="cite_ref-Brussard1998_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brussard1998-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although definitions of ecosystem management abound, there is a common set of principles which underlie these definitions.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin362_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin362-31">[30]</a></sup> A fundamental principle is the long-term sustainability of the production of goods and services by the ecosystem;<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin362_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin362-31">[30]</a></sup> "intergenerational sustainability [is] a precondition for management, not an afterthought".<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> It also requires clear goals with respect to future trajectories and behaviors of the system being managed. Other important requirements include a sound ecological understanding of the system, including connectedness, ecological dynamics and the context in which the system is embedded. Other important principles include an understanding of the role of humans as components of the ecosystems and the use of <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">adaptive management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> While ecosystem management can be used as part of a plan for <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> conservation, it can also be used in intensively managed ecosystems<sup id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_28-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-28">[27]</a></sup> (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/Agroecosystem" title="Agroecosystem">agroecosystem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Close_to_nature_forestry" title="Close to nature forestry">close to nature forestry</a>).</p>

<p>Ecosystems are dynamic entities—invariably, they are subject to periodic disturbances and are in the process of recovering from some past disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> When an ecosystem is subject to some sort of <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Perturbation (biology)">perturbation</a>, it responds by moving away from its initial state. The tendency of a system to remain close to its equilibrium state, despite that disturbance, is termed its <a href="/wiki/Resistance_(ecology)" title="Resistance (ecology)">resistance</a>. On the other hand, the speed with which it returns to its initial state after disturbance is called its <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>From one year to another, ecosystems experience variation in their biotic and abiotic environments. A drought, an especially cold winter and a pest outbreak all constitute short-term variability in environmental conditions. Animal populations vary from year to year, building up during resource-rich periods and crashing as they overshoot their food supply. These changes play out in changes in NPP, decomposition rates, and other ecosystem processes.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> Longer-term changes also shape ecosystem processes—the forests of eastern North America still show legacies of cultivation which ceased 200 years ago, while methane production in eastern Siberian lakes is controlled by organic matter which accumulated during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">Disturbance</a> also plays an important role in ecological processes. <a href="/wiki/F._Stuart_Chapin_III" title="F. Stuart Chapin III">F. Stuart Chapin</a> and coauthors define disturbance as "a relatively discrete event in time and space that alters the structure of populations, communities and ecosystems and causes changes in resources availability or the physical environment".<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin_285_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin_285-33">[32]</a></sup> This can range from tree falls and insect outbreaks to hurricanes and wildfires to volcanic eruptions and can cause large changes in plant, animal and microbe populations, as well soil organic matter content.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> Disturbance is followed by <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>, a "directional change in ecosystem structure and functioning resulting from biotically driven changes in resources supply."<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin_285_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin_285-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The frequency and severity of disturbance determines the way it impacts ecosystem function. Major disturbance like a volcanic eruption or glacial advance and retreat leave behind soils that lack plants, animals or organic matter. Ecosystems that experience disturbances that undergo <a href="/wiki/Primary_succession" title="Primary succession">primary succession</a>. Less severe disturbance like forest fires, hurricanes or cultivation result in <a href="/wiki/Secondary_succession" title="Secondary succession">secondary succession</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup> More severe disturbance and more frequent disturbance result in longer recovery times. Ecosystems recover more quickly from less severe disturbance events.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The early stages of primary succession are dominated by species with small propagules (seed and spores) which can be dispersed long distances. The early colonizers—often <a href="/wiki/Alga" class="mw-redirect" title="Alga">algae</a>, cyanobacteria and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>—stabilize the substrate. Nitrogen supplies are limited in new soils, and nitrogen-fixing species tend to play an important role early in primary succession. Unlike in primary succession, the species that dominate secondary succession, are usually present from the start of the process, often in the <a href="/wiki/Soil_seed_bank" title="Soil seed bank">soil seed bank</a>. In some systems the successional pathways are fairly consistent, and thus, are easy to predict. In others, there are many possible pathways—for example, the introduced nitrogen-fixing legume, <i><a href="/wiki/Myrica_faya" title="Myrica faya">Myrica faya</a></i>, alter successional trajectories in Hawaiian forests.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapin281_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_ecology" title="Theoretical ecology">theoretical ecologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ulanowicz" title="Robert Ulanowicz">Robert Ulanowicz</a> has used <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a> tools to describe the structure of ecosystems, emphasizing <a href="/wiki/Mutual_information" title="Mutual information">mutual information</a> (correlations) in studied systems. Drawing on this methodology and prior observations of complex ecosystems, Ulanowicz depicts approaches to determining the stress levels on ecosystems and predicting system reactions to defined types of alteration in their settings (such as increased or reduced energy flow, and <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_ecology">Ecosystem ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecosystem ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Ecosystem ecology studies "the flow of energy and materials through organisms and the physical environment". It seeks to understand the processes which govern the stocks of material and energy in ecosystems, and the flow of matter and energy through them. The study of ecosystems can cover 10 <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a>, from the surface layers of rocks to the surface of the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is no single definition of what constitutes an ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-Sagoff2003_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sagoff2003-36">[35]</a></sup> German ecologist Ernst-Detlef Schulze and coauthors defined an ecosystem as an area which is "uniform regarding the biological turnover, and contains all the fluxes above and below the ground area under consideration." They explicitly reject <a href="/wiki/Gene_Likens" title="Gene Likens">Gene Likens</a>' use of entire river catchments as "too wide a demarcation" to be a single ecosystem, given the level of heterogeneity within such an area.<sup id="cite_ref-Schulze399_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze399-37">[36]</a></sup> Other authors have suggested that an ecosystem can encompass a much larger area, even the whole planet.<sup id="cite_ref-planet_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planet-6">[6]</a></sup> Schulze and coauthors also rejected the idea that a single rotting log could be studied as an ecosystem because the size of the flows between the log and its surroundings are too large, relative to the proportion cycles within the log.<sup id="cite_ref-Schulze399_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze399-37">[36]</a></sup> Philosopher of science Mark Sagoff considers the failure to define "the kind of object it studies" to be an obstacle to the development of theory in ecosystem ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-Sagoff2003_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sagoff2003-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ecosystems can be studied through a variety of approaches—theoretical studies, studies monitoring specific ecosystems over long periods of time, those that look at differences between ecosystems to elucidate how they work and direct manipulative experimentation.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter1998_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter1998-38">[37]</a></sup> Studies can be carried out at a variety of scales, from <a href="/wiki/Microcosm:_Model_/_experimental_ecosystem" class="mw-redirect" title="Microcosm: Model / experimental ecosystem">microcosms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesocosm" title="Mesocosm">mesocosms</a> which serve as simplified representations of ecosystems, through whole-ecosystem studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Schindler1998_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schindler1998-39">[38]</a></sup> American ecologist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_R._Carpenter" title="Stephen R. Carpenter">Stephen R. Carpenter</a> has argued that microcosm experiments can be "irrelevant and diversionary" if they are not carried out in conjunction with field studies carried out at the ecosystem scale, because microcosm experiments often fail to accurately predict ecosystem-level dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter1996_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter1996-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hubbard_Brook_Ecosystem_Study" class="mw-redirect" title="Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study">Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study</a>, established in the <a href="/wiki/White_Mountains_(New_Hampshire)" title="White Mountains (New Hampshire)">White Mountains, New Hampshire</a> in 1963, was the first successful attempt to study an entire watershed as an ecosystem. The study used stream chemistry as a means of monitoring ecosystem properties, and developed a detailed <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemistry" title="Biogeochemistry">biogeochemical model</a> of the ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-Lindenmayer2010_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lindenmayer2010-41">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Long_Term_Ecological_Research_Network" title="Long Term Ecological Research Network">Long-term research</a> at the site led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> in North America in 1972, and was able to document the consequent depletion of soil <a href="/wiki/Cations" class="mw-redirect" title="Cations">cations</a> (especially calcium) over the next several decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Likens2004_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Likens2004-42">[41]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Classifying ecosystems into ecologically homogeneous units is an important step towards effective ecosystem management.<sup id="cite_ref-Lugo1999_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo1999-43">[42]</a></sup> A variety of systems exist, based on vegetation cover, remote sensing, and <a href="/wiki/Holdridge_life_zones" title="Holdridge life zones">bioclimatic classification</a> systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Lugo1999_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo1999-43">[42]</a></sup> American geographer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bailey_(geographer)" title="Robert Bailey (geographer)">Robert Bailey</a> defines a hierarchy of ecosystem units ranging from microecosystems (individual homogeneous sites, on the order of 10 square kilometres (4&#160;sq&#160;mi) in area), through mesoecosystems (<a href="/wiki/Landscape_ecology" title="Landscape ecology">landscape mosaics</a>, on the order of 1,000 square kilometres (400&#160;sq&#160;mi)) to macroecosystems (<a href="/wiki/Ecoregions" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecoregions">ecoregions</a>, on the order of 100,000 square kilometres (40,000&#160;sq&#160;mi)).<sup id="cite_ref-BaileyCh2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BaileyCh2-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bailey outlined five different methods for identifying ecosystems: <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gestalt" class="extiw" title="wikt:gestalt">gestalt</a></i> ("a whole that is not derived through considerable of its parts"), in which regions are recognized and boundaries drawn intuitively; a map overlay system where different layers like <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landforms</a> and soil types are overlain to identify ecosystems; <a href="/wiki/Cluster_analysis" title="Cluster analysis">multivariate clustering</a> of site attributes; <a href="/wiki/Digital_image_processing" title="Digital image processing">digital image processing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remotely sensed</a> data grouping areas based on their appearance or other <a href="/wiki/Multispectral_image" title="Multispectral image">spectral</a> properties; or by a "controlling factors method" where a subset of factors (like soils, climate, vegetation <a href="/wiki/Physiognomy" title="Physiognomy">physiognomy</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Species_distribution" title="Species distribution">distribution</a> of plant or animal species) are selected from a large array of possible ones are used to delineate ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-BaileyCh3_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BaileyCh3-45">[44]</a></sup> In contrast with Bailey's methodology, Puerto Rico ecologist <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Lugo" title="Ariel Lugo">Ariel Lugo</a> and coauthors identified ten characteristics of an effective classification system: that it be based on <a href="/wiki/Georeference" class="mw-redirect" title="Georeference">georeferenced</a>, quantitative data; that it should minimize subjectivity and explicitly identify criteria and assumptions; that it should be structured around the factors that drive ecosystem processes; that it should reflect the hierarchical nature of ecosystems; that it should be flexible enough to conform to the various scales at which ecosystem management operates; that it should be tied to reliable measures of climate so that it can "anticipat[e] global climate change; that it be applicable worldwide; that it should be validated against independent data; that it take into account the sometimes complex relationship between climate, vegetation and ecosystem functioning; and that it should be able to adapt and improve as new data become available".<sup id="cite_ref-Lugo1999_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo1999-43">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_threats">Anthropogenic threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecosystem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">Planetary boundaries</a></div>
<p>As human populations and per capita consumption grow, so do the resource demands imposed on ecosystems and the impacts of the human <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>. Natural resources are not invulnerable and infinitely available. The environmental impacts of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> actions, which are processes or materials derived from human activities, are becoming more apparent—air and water quality are increasingly compromised, oceans are being <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfished</a>, pests and diseases are extending beyond their historical boundaries, and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> is exacerbating flooding downstream. It has been reported that approximately 40–50% of Earth's ice-free land surface has been heavily transformed or degraded by anthropogenic activities, 66% of marine <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> are either <a href="/wiki/Overexploited" class="mw-redirect" title="Overexploited">overexploited</a> or at their limit, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_CO2" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric CO2">atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub></a> has increased more than 30% since the advent of <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, and nearly 25% of Earth's bird species have gone <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a> in the last two thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Society is increasingly becoming aware that <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> are not only limited, but also that they are threatened by human activities. The need to better consider long-term <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_health" title="Ecosystem health">ecosystem health</a> and its role in enabling human habitation and economic activity is urgent. To help inform decision-makers, many ecosystem services are being assigned economic values, often based on the cost of replacement with anthropogenic alternatives. The ongoing challenge of prescribing economic value to nature, for example through <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_banking" title="Biodiversity banking">biodiversity banking</a>, is prompting transdisciplinary shifts in how we recognize and manage the environment, <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a>, business opportunities, and our future as a species.</p>
