<p><b>Deep ecology</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_philosophy" title="Environmental philosophy">environmental philosophy</a> promoting the inherent worth of living beings regardless of their instrumental utility to human needs, plus a radical restructuring of modern human societies in accordance with such ideas. Deep ecology argues that the natural world is a subtle balance of complex inter-relationships in which the existence of organisms is dependent on the existence of others within <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Human interference with or destruction of the natural world poses a threat therefore not only to humans but to all organisms constituting the natural order.</p>
<p>Deep ecology's core principle is the belief that the living environment as a whole should be respected and regarded as having certain inalienable legal <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a> to live and flourish, independent of its <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarian instrumental benefits</a> for human use. Deep ecology is often framed in terms of the idea of a much broader sociality; it recognizes diverse communities of life on Earth that are composed not only through biotic factors but also, where applicable, through ethical relations, that is, the valuing of other beings as more than just resources. It describes itself as "deep" because it regards itself as looking more deeply into the actual reality of humanity's relationship with the natural world arriving at philosophically more profound conclusions than that of the prevailing view of ecology as a branch of biology.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The movement does not subscribe to <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentric</a> environmentalism (which is concerned with conservation of the environment only for exploitation by and for human purposes) since deep ecology is grounded in a quite different set of philosophical assumptions. Deep ecology takes a more <a href="/wiki/Holistic_view" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic view">holistic view</a> of the world human beings live in and seeks to apply to life the understanding that the separate parts of the ecosystem (including humans) function as a whole. This philosophy provides a foundation for the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecology movement">ecology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Green_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Green movement">green movements</a> and has fostered a new system of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">environmental ethics</a> advocating <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> preservation, <a href="/wiki/Human_population_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population control">human population control</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Simple_living" title="Simple living">simple living</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-barry_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barry-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his original 1972/73 deep ecology paper, <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Naess</a> claims the deep ecology movement arose from scientists – ecologists who were out in the field studying the biodiversity and wild ecosystems throughout the world. They were also doing the work of philosophers, laying the foundations for the Age of Ecology and a new ecological worldview to replace the anthropocentric, mastery of Nature, and modernist worldview arising in the 17th and 18th centuries. Three of the most influential ecological spokespersons of the 1960s were Rachel Carson, David Brower, and Paul Ehrlich.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Some consider the publication of <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s book <a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a> (1962) as the beginning of the contemporary, long-range deep ecology movement. When her book appeared there was a long-standing movement for conservation of land and resources, as well as support for creating parks and other areas devoted to preserving wilderness and spectacular nature. Carson's writings were especially influential because they clearly showed how human well-being depends on the condition of whole biotic communities. She explained in practical terms how living beings are interrelated within ecosystems. She explained how pesticides used to control mosquitoes and other insects led to declines in some bird populations. “Silent Spring” helped show how complex food webs and networks of biotic relationships function. Since humans are at the top of many food chains, exposure to chemicals becomes more concentrated as these move up the chains. The chemicals also can be stored in human tissues and gradually accumulate over time, adversely affecting health. Carson showed the need for deep changes in human practices and ways of living.</p>
<p>The 1960s was a decade of vigorous social <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activism</a> in the United States, Canada, Western Europe, and Australia. Some activism focused on war and peace and the issue of nuclear weapons. A well-known early environmental organization started with a focus on nuclear tests and their environmental hazards. Some people in British Columbia, Canada, were opposed to the test of a nuclear weapon by the US government on Amchitka Island. They hired a fishing vessel and sailed towards the nuclear test site in protest. This action led to the founding of "<a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>", which became more identified with environmental issues as time went by. These great movements were further catalyzed by the now iconic images of the whole Earth floating in space taken during the return of the Apollo space missions from their journey to the moon. Among the astronauts that witnessed seeing the whole Earth firsthand was <a href="/wiki/Edgar_D._Mitchell" class="mw-redirect" title="Edgar D. Mitchell">Edgar D. Mitchell</a>, who in 1971, during the return mission of Apollo 14, had an epiphany that what is needed to solve the eco-crisis "is a transformation of consciousness".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proponents of deep ecology believe that the world does not exist as a <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resource</a> to be freely <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_natural_resources" title="Exploitation of natural resources">exploited</a> by humans. If material goods do not guarantee happiness beyond a very moderate level, and over-consumption is endangering the biosphere, defining a new non-consumptive paradigm of well-being seems primordial, such a paradigm would be non-acquisitive/non-consumerist and non-hierarchical in relation to our place on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The ethics of deep ecology hold that the survival of any part is dependent upon the well-being of the whole. Proponents of deep ecology offer an eight-tier platform to elucidate their claims:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<ol>
<li>The well-being and flourishing of human and nonhuman life on Earth have value in themselves (synonyms: intrinsic value, inherent value). These values are independent of the usefulness of the nonhuman world for human purposes.</li>
<li>Richness and <a href="/wiki/Diversity_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Diversity of life">diversity of life</a> forms contribute to the realization of these values and are also values in themselves.</li>
<li>Humans have no right to reduce this richness and diversity except to satisfy vital human needs.</li>
<li>The flourishing of human life and cultures is compatible with a substantial decrease of the <a href="/wiki/Human_population" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population">human population</a>. The flourishing of nonhuman life requires such a decrease.</li>
<li>Present human interference with the nonhuman world is excessive, and the situation is rapidly worsening.</li>
<li>Policies must therefore be changed. These policies affect basic economic, technological, and ideological structures. The resulting state of affairs will be deeply different from the present.</li>
<li>The ideological change is mainly that of appreciating <a href="/wiki/Life_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Life quality">life quality</a> (dwelling in situations of inherent value) rather than adhering to an increasingly higher standard of living. There will be a profound <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a> of the difference between big and great.</li>
<li>Those who subscribe to the foregoing points have an obligation directly or indirectly to try to implement the necessary changes.</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
<p>These principles can be reduced to three simple propositions:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">Wilderness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> preservation;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Human_population_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population control">Human population control</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simple_living" title="Simple living">Simple living</a> (or treading lightly on the planet).<sup id="cite_ref-barry_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barry-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase "deep ecology" was coined by the Norwegian philosopher <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a> in 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Næss rejected the idea that beings can be ranked according to their relative value. For example, judgments on whether an animal has an eternal <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a>, whether it uses <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> or whether it has <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> (or indeed <a href="/wiki/Higher_consciousness" title="Higher consciousness">higher consciousness</a>) have all been used to justify the ranking of the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human animal</a> as superior to other animals. Næss states that from an ecological point of view "the right of all forms [of life] to live is a universal right which cannot be quantified. No single species of living being has more of this particular right to live and unfold than any other species."</p>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> idea is elucidated in <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Fox" title="Warwick Fox">Warwick Fox</a>'s claim that humanity and all other beings are "aspects of a single unfolding reality".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As such deep ecology would support the view of <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Sand_County_Almanac" title="A Sand County Almanac">A Sand County Almanac</a></i> that humans are "plain members of the biotic community". They also would support <a href="/wiki/Land_ethic#Aldo_Leopold.27s_land_ethic" title="Land ethic">Leopold's land ethic</a>: "a thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability and beauty of the <a href="/wiki/Biocoenosis" title="Biocoenosis">biotic community</a>. It is wrong when it tends otherwise." Daniel Quinn, in his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ishmael_(novel)" title="Ishmael (novel)">Ishmael</a></i>, showed that an anthropocentric myth underlies our current view of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ecological problems faced by the world today are partly due to the loss of traditional knowledge, values, and ethics of behavior that celebrate the intrinsic value and sacredness of the natural world and that give the preservation of Nature prime importance. Correspondingly, the assumption of human superiority to other life forms, as if we were granted royalty status over Nature; the idea that Nature is mainly here to serve human will and purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Deep ecology had a response to the anthropocentric view and several different actors played a role in its development. Prominent among them is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_W._Meeker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joseph W. Meeker (page does not exist)">Joseph W. Meeker</a>, who in 1973 told <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Sessions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="George Sessions (page does not exist)">George Sessions</a> about Arne Næss, whom Meeker knew personally.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As Warwick Fox related, "One of the things that initially interested Sessions about Næss was Næss's strong interest in, and innovative approach to, the work of Spinoza. Sessions says that he had himself 'arrived at Spinoza as the answer to the process of teaching history of philosophy by about 1972 and independently of being in contact with Næss.'"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sessions therefore wrote to Næss at this time, beginning a lifelong association. Meeker's (1972, 1997) book <i>The Comedy of Survival: Studies in Literary Ecology</i> emerged through the work of scholars seeking an environmental ethic. That book represents Meeker's founding work in literary ecology and <a href="/wiki/Ecocriticism" title="Ecocriticism">ecocriticism</a>, which demonstrates the relationship between the literary arts and scientific ecology, especially humankind's consideration of comedy and tragedy. It reminds readers that adaptive behaviors (comedy) promote survival, whereas tragedy estranges from other life forms. This thesis rests on Meeker's study of comparative literature, his work with biologist <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Lorenz" title="Konrad Lorenz">Konrad Lorenz</a>, and his work as a field ecologist in the National Park service in Alaska, Oregon, and California.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deep ecology offers a philosophical basis for environmental advocacy which may, in turn, guide human activity against perceived self-destruction. Deep ecology and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> hold that the science of ecology <a href="/wiki/History_of_ecology" title="History of ecology">shows</a> that <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> can absorb only limited change by humans or other dissonant influences. Further, both hold that the actions of modern civilization threaten global ecological well-being. Ecologists have described change and stability in ecological systems in various ways, including <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">dynamic equilibrium</a>, and "flux of nature".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Regardless of which model is most accurate, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> contend that massive human economic activity has pushed the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> far from its "natural" state through reduction of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity#Ecological_role_of_biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, and other influences. As a consequence, civilization is causing <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinction</a> at a rate between 100 species a day and possibly 140,000 species a year, which is 10,000 times the background rate of extinction. Deep ecologists hope to influence social and political change through their philosophy. Næss has proposed, as <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Goodrick-Clarke" title="Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke">Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke</a> writes, "that the earth’s human population should be reduced to about 100 million."<sup id="cite_ref-Goodrick-Clarke1998_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodrick-Clarke1998-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_education">Environmental education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecology in the narrow sense refers to the biological science of ecology. However, ecological paradigms and principles are being developed and applied in almost all disciplines, and these paradigms have to do with the way we approach understanding the relationships and inter-connections within and between living beings which give to each its special place and identity. Human ecology, e.g., must certainly take account of the role of our subjective lives and spiritual needs, as well as our biological ones, in terms of their ecological effects. Ecology in this sense is not a reductionist undertaking, but a movement toward a more whole (or holistic) vision and understanding of world processes.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Deep ecology seeks to look into all levels of existence and might be viewed as radical by some; for them a more anthropocentric view is appropriated because it put humans at the center. Learning how to live in harmony with our surroundings is beneficial because stopping the global extinction crisis and achieving true ecological sustainability will require rethinking our values as a society. In that way education seems to be the best way to start. Sustainability education aims to help learners understand their interconnectedness with all life, to become creative problem solvers and active citizens, and to engage personally and intellectually in shaping our common future. Experiential learning and critical pedagogy are central to providing opportunities for learners to engage in transformative sustainability learning. The “Environment” broadly defined, remains somewhat neglected within development studies, despite a substantial increase in contributions to the field over the fifteen years since 2000. Undergraduate and postgraduate courses (with some notable exceptions) often “add on” environmental issues as special lectures or modules, and there remains a tendency for those who are grounded in the material and discursive struggles that define the discipline to consider the environment as an exotic special interest, a problem that manifests itself in societies that have the leisure to care about the natural world. Development of a modern education model promoting patriotism and civic responsibility, active social position and healthy lifestyle is closely linked to the development of environmental responsibility in the younger generation. Development of environmentally responsible personality in individual is of particular importance for graduates of educational institutions. Environmental education could be integrated in different curriculum in most fields: education for sustainable development in the context of ecopedagogy.</p>
<p>Ecopedagogy calls for the remaking of capitalist practices and seeks to re-engage democracy to include multispecies interests in the face of our current global ecological crisis. It does so by using different ideas that challenge the way we see education. In Critical Pedagogy, Ecoliteracy and Planetary Crisis: The Ecopedagogy Movement, Richard Kahn (2010) reformulates Herbert Marcuse’s critical theories of society, and supports the kind of education that seizes the power of radical environmental activists and supports the earth democracy in which multispecies interests are represented. The destruction of habitats and threats to biodiversity resulting from expansion of human population and consumption is rarely addressed in a way that confronts students with the necessity to consider moral implications of such destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">[15]</a></sup> Pedagogically, a return to education associated with significant life experiences, such as hiking in wilderness areas as a youth; as well as strategically significant education, action competence, social learning, and variations and combinations of those and many other pedagogical approaches developed in the past 40 years. Some of these pedagogical approaches have been disputed—for example, the belief that experiencing environment first hand is an essential component of engaging people in conservation has been disputed by arguments that these education efforts have been informed by behaviorist socio-psychology models that assumed a linear causality between education experience and pro-environmental behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Rather, the critics have argued that people’s environmental behaviors are too complex and contextually dependent to be captured by a simple casual model. The process of environmental education of schoolchildren has the following methodological characteristics:</p>
<p>· Goal-setting as the projected results reflects a model of environmentally responsible personality, taking into account trends in the development of key elements of education system; all natural sciences are involved in the development of basic ecological concepts.</p>
<p>· The introduction of interactive training methods takes place at the high school level in teaching self-reflection, hypothesizing, predicting; school natural science education is rebuilt on the basis of system approach in accordance with the planned ecologization results. Implementation of relevant methodology will promote successful development of environmentally responsible personality in high school graduates.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In higher education, the analysis of students’ individual writing assignments after viewing films/documentaries presents an interesting case of using radical ‘messages’ within the aims of environmental education in order to trigger both student’s engagement and critical thinking. The case study “If a Tree Falls and Everybody Hears the Sound” provides an example of how environmental advocacy and the objective of pluralistic education can be combined as mutually supportive means of achieving both democratic learning in which students’ individual opinions are seen as extremely valuable, and simultaneously provide an example of the type of ecopedagogy that supports learning for environmental sustainability. The role of environmental advocacy can be crucially important if the interests of all planetary citizens—and not just one species—are to be taken seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Næss and Fox do not claim to use <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">induction</a> to derive the philosophy directly from scientific ecology<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> but rather hold that scientific ecology directly implies the metaphysics of deep ecology, including its ideas about the self and further, that deep ecology finds scientific underpinnings in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">system dynamics</a>.</p>
<p>In their 1985 book <i>Deep Ecology</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Bill Devall and George Sessions describe a series of sources of deep ecology. They include the science of ecology itself, and cite its major contribution as the rediscovery in a modern context that "everything is connected to everything else." They point out that some ecologists and natural historians, in addition to their scientific viewpoint, have developed a deep ecological consciousness—for some a <a href="/wiki/Political_consciousness" title="Political consciousness">political consciousness</a> and at times a <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_consciousness" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiritual consciousness">spiritual consciousness</a>. This is a perspective beyond the strictly human viewpoint, beyond <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a>. Among the scientists they mention specifically are <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Livingston_(naturalist)" title="John Livingston (naturalist)">John Livingston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barry_Commoner" title="Barry Commoner">Barry Commoner</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Frank_Fraser_Darling" title="Frank Fraser Darling">Frank Fraser Darling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Sutherland Elton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Odum" title="Eugene Odum">Eugene Odum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sears" title="Paul Sears">Paul Sears</a>.</p>
<p>A further scientific source for deep ecology adduced by Devall and Sessions is the "new physics", which they describe as shattering <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">Descartes</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>'s vision of the universe as a machine explainable in terms of simple <a href="/wiki/Linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear">linear</a> cause and effect. They propose that Nature is in a state of constant flux and reject the idea of observers as existing independent of their environment. They refer to <a href="/wiki/Fritjof_Capra" title="Fritjof Capra">Fritjof Capra</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tao_of_Physics" title="The Tao of Physics">The Tao of Physics</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Turning_Point_(book)" title="The Turning Point (book)">The Turning Point</a></i> for their characterisation of how the new physics leads to metaphysical and ecological views of interrelatedness, which, according to Capra, should make deep ecology a framework for future human societies. Devall and Sessions also credit the American poet and social critic <a href="/wiki/Gary_Snyder" title="Gary Snyder">Gary Snyder</a>—with his devotion to <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> studies, the outdoors, and alternative social movements—as a major voice of wisdom in the evolution of their ideas.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia hypothesis</a> was also an influence on the development of deep ecology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spiritual">Spiritual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spiritual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The central spiritual tenet of deep ecology is that the human species is a part of the Earth, not separate from it, and as such human existence is dependent on the diverse organisms within the natural world each playing a role in the natural economy of the biosphere. Coming to an awareness of this reality involves a transformation of an outlook that presupposes humanity's superiority over the natural world. This self-realisation or "re-earthing"<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> is used for an individual to intuitively gain an ecocentric perspective. The notion is based on the idea that the more we <i>expand the self</i> to identify with "others" (people, animals, ecosystems), the more we realize ourselves. <a href="/wiki/Transpersonal_psychology" title="Transpersonal psychology">Transpersonal psychology</a> has been used by <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Fox" title="Warwick Fox">Warwick Fox</a> to support this idea. Deep ecology has influenced the development of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Ecospirituality" title="Ecospirituality">Ecospirituality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of spiritual and philosophical traditions including <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a>, Buddhist and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jain</a> are drawn upon in a continuing critique of the philosophical assumptions of the modern European mind which has enabled and led to what is seen as an increasingly unsustainable level of disregard to towards the rights and needs of the natural world and its ability to continue to support human life. In relation to the <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> tradition, Næss offers the following criticism: "The arrogance of stewardship [as found in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>] consists in the idea of superiority which underlies the thought that we exist to watch over nature like a highly respected middleman between the <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">Creator</a> and Creation."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This theme had been expounded in <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Townsend_White,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Lynn Townsend White, Jr.">Lynn Townsend White, Jr.</a>'s 1967 article "The Historical Roots of Our Ecological Crisis",<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> in which however he also offered as an alternative Christian view of man's relation to nature that of <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">Saint Francis of Assisi</a>, who he says spoke for the equality of all creatures, in place of the idea of man's domination over creation. Næss' further criticizes the reformation's view of creation as property to be put into maximum productive use: a view used frequently in the past to exploit and dispossess native populations. Many Protestant sects today regard the Bible's call for man to have stewardship of the earth as a call for the care for creation, rather than for exploitation.</p>
<p>The original Christian teachings on property support the Franciscan/stewardship interpretation of the Bible. Against this view, Martin Luther condemned church ownership of lands because "they did not want to use that property in an economically productive fashion. At best they used it to produce prayers. Luther, and other Reformation leaders insisted that it should be used, not to relieve men from the necessity of working, but as a tool for making more goods. The attitude of the Reformation was practically, "not prayers, but production." And production, not for consumption, but for more production." This justification was offered to support secular takings of church endowments and properties.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_roots">Philosophical roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philosophical roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinoza">Spinoza</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spinoza">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a>, who first wrote about the idea of deep ecology, from the early days of developing this outlook conceived <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a> as a philosophical source.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others have followed Naess' inquiry, including Eccy de Jonge, in <i>Spinoza and Deep Ecology: Challenging Traditional Approaches to Environmentalism</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and Brenden MacDonald, in <i>Spinoza, Deep Ecology, and Human Diversity—Realization of Eco-Literacies</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>One of the topical centres of inquiry connecting Spinoza to Deep Ecology is "self-realization." See Arne Næss in <i>The Shallow and the Deep, Long-Range Ecology movement</i> and <i>Spinoza and the Deep Ecology Movement</i> for discussion on the role of Spinoza's conception of self-realization and its link to deep ecology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism.2C_debate.2C_and_response">Criticism, debate, and response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism, debate, and response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Knowledge_of_non-human_interests">Knowledge of non-human interests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Knowledge of non-human interests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Animal rights activists state that for an entity to require rights and protection <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_and_extrinsic_properties" title="Intrinsic and extrinsic properties">intrinsically</a>, it must have interests.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Deep ecology is criticised for assuming that living things such as plants, for example, have their own interests as they are manifested by the plant's behavior—for instance, self-preservation being considered an expression of a will to live. Deep ecologists claim to <i>identify</i> with non-human nature, and in doing so, deny those who claim that non-human (or non-sentient) lifeforms' needs or interests are nonexistent or unknowable. The criticism is that the interests that a deep ecologist attributes to non-human organisms such as survival, reproduction, growth, and prosperity are really human interests. This is sometimes construed as a <a href="/wiki/Pathetic_fallacy" title="Pathetic fallacy">pathetic fallacy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphism</a>, in which "the earth is endowed with 'wisdom', wilderness equates with 'freedom', and life forms are said to emit 'moral' qualities."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Deepness.22">"Deepness"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: &quot;Deepness&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deep ecology is criticised for its claim to being <i>deeper</i> than alternative theories, which by implication are <i>shallow</i>. When <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a> coined the term <i>deep ecology</i>, he compared it favourably with <i>shallow environmentalism</i> which he criticized for its <a href="/wiki/Utilitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentric" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropocentric">anthropocentric</a> attitude to nature and for its <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumer-oriented</a> outlook.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Against this is <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a>'s own view that the "depth" of deep ecology resides in the persistence of its penetrative questioning, particularly in asking "Why?" when faced with initial answers.</p>
<p>Writer William D. Grey believes that developing a non-anthropocentric set of values is "a hopeless quest". He seeks an improved "shallow" view, writing, "What's wrong with shallow views is not their concern about the well-being of humans, but that they do not really consider enough in what that well-being consists. We need to develop an enriched, fortified anthropocentric notion of human interest to replace the dominant short-term, sectional and self-regarding conception."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bookchin.27s_criticisms">Bookchin's criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bookchin's criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some critics, particularly <a href="/wiki/Social_ecology" title="Social ecology">social ecologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a>, have interpreted deep ecology as being <a href="/wiki/Misanthropic" class="mw-redirect" title="Misanthropic">hateful toward humanity</a>, due in part to the characterization of humanity by some deep ecologists, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Foreman" title="David Foreman">David Foreman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a>, as a pathological infestation on the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodrick-Clarke1998_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodrick-Clarke1998-13">[13]</a></sup> Bookchin<sup id="cite_ref-Bookchin_1987_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bookchin_1987-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AtKisson_1989_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AtKisson_1989-33">[33]</a></sup> therefore asserts that "deep ecology, formulated largely by privileged male white academics, has managed to bring sincere naturalists like Paul Shepard into the same company as patently antihumanist and macho mountain men like David Foreman who preach a gospel that humanity is some kind of cancer in the world of life."<sup id="cite_ref-Bookchin_1987_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bookchin_1987-32">[32]</a></sup> Bookchin mentions that some, like Foreman, defend seemingly anti-human measures, such as severe population control and the claim regarding the Third World that "the best thing would be to just let nature seek its own balance, to let the people there just starve".<sup id="cite_ref-Bookchin_1987_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bookchin_1987-32">[32]</a></sup> However, Bookchin himself later admitted that "statements made by Earth First! activists are not to be confused with those made by deep ecology theorists".<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmerman_1993_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerman_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> Ecophilosopher <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Fox" title="Warwick Fox">Warwick Fox</a> similarly "warns critics not to commit the fallacy of 'misplaced misanthropy.' That is, just because deep ecology criticizes an arrogant anthropocentrism does <i>not</i> mean that deep ecology is misanthropic."<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmerman_1993_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmerman_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> Likewise, <i>The Deep Ecology Movement: An Introductory Anthology</i> attempts to clarify that "deep ecologists have been the strongest critics of <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a>, so much so that they have often been accused of a mean-spirited misanthropy"; however, "deep ecology is actually vitally concerned with humans realizing their best potential" and "is explicit in offering a vision of an alternative way of living that is joyous and enlivening."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bookchin's second major criticism is that deep ecology fails to link environmental crises with <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>. Social ecologists like him believe that environmental problems are firmly rooted in the manner of human social interaction, and suggest that deep ecologists fail to recognise the potential for human beings to solve environmental issues through a change of cultural attitudes. According to Bookchin, it is a social reconstruction alone that "can spare the biosphere from virtual destruction."<sup id="cite_ref-Bookchin_1987_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bookchin_1987-32">[32]</a></sup> Though some deep ecologists may reject the argument that ecological behavior is rooted in the social paradigm (which, according to their view, would be an anthropocentric fallacy), others in fact embrace this argument, such as the adherents to the deep ecologist movement <a href="/wiki/Deep_Green_Resistance" title="Deep Green Resistance">Deep Green Resistance</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Botkin.27s_criticism">Botkin's criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Botkin's criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Daniel Botkin<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> has likened deep ecology to its antithesis, the <a href="/wiki/Wise_use" class="mw-redirect" title="Wise use">wise use</a> movement, when he says that they both "misunderstand scientific information and then arrive at conclusions based on their misunderstanding, which are in turn used as justification for their ideologies. Both begin with an ideology and are political and social in focus." Elsewhere, though, he asserts that deep ecology must be taken seriously in the debate about the relationship between humans and nature because it challenges the fundamental assumptions of <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">Western philosophy</a>. Botkin has also criticized Næss's restatement and reliance upon the balance of nature idea and the perceived contradiction between his argument that all species are morally equal and his disparaging description of <a href="/wiki/Pioneering_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Pioneering species">pioneering species</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response">Response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some writers have misunderstood Naess, taking his Ecosophy T, with its Self-realization norm, as something meant to characterize the whole deep ecology movement as part of a single philosophy called "deep ecology." Naess was not doing either of these. He emphasized that movements cannot be precisely defined, but only roughly characterized by very general statements. They are often united internationally by means of such principles as found in the United Nations (UN) Earth Charter (1980), and in UN documents about basic human rights. Naess was doing something more subtle than many thought. He was not putting forth a single worldview and philosophy of life that everyone should adhere to in support of the international ecology movement. Instead, he was making an empirical claim based on overwhelming evidence that global social movements, from the grass roots up, consist of people with very diverse religious, philosophical, cultural, and personal orientations. Nonetheless, they can agree on certain courses of action and certain broad principles, especially at the international level. As supporters of a given movement, they can treat one another with mutual respect. Because of these misunderstandings Naess introduced an Apron Diagram to clearly illustrate his subtle distinctions.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-37">[37]</a></sup> The Apron Diagram is meant to illustrate logical, as distinct from genetic, relations between views and their connection with social movements, policies and practical actions. By "logical relations" this means verbally articulated relations between the premises and conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-37">[37]</a></sup> There is collective cooperation on global concerns, and yet a great variety of ultimate premises from which each person or group acts locally. Within global movements there is diversity at the local level because each place and community is different and must adapt to its unique setting. Thus, Naess stressed that his Ecosophy T is not meant to hold for everyone, since it is tailored to his very modest lifestyle suitable to a place such as Tvergastein. The ultimate premises for his whole view might be conceptually incompatible with those in someone else's whole views. But even if this is true, they could both support the Platform Principles of the deep ecology movement and other social-political global movements, such as for peace and social justice. In recognizing the principle that all living beings have intrinsic worth, one acknowledges they are good for their own sake. This does not commit one to biocentric equality or egalitarianism between species. Within the vast diversity of living beings, there are complex relationships the range of which is predation, competition, cooperation, and symbiosis. Many think that symbiosis and complementarity are important values to embrace as they are consistent with global cooperation, community life, and support for the deep ecology movement Platform.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecofeminist_response">Ecofeminist response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ecofeminist response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecofeminism and deep ecology have been in dialogue for some time now, and while the debate between them has been very fruitful over the years, the exploration of their relationship remains important. As valuable as our individually reconnecting with the natural world may be, and as much as such experiences should be encouraged, some have questioned whether this approach is sufficient, given the magnitude of the threat that human encroachment poses to the nonhuman world. With this in mind, the call has been made for a broader challenge to the dominant culture than deep ecological experience may offer. This call has come most forcefully from another school of environmental ethics: ecofeminism. While sharing with deep ecologists a general concern for biocentrism and an appreciation for personal interaction with nonhuman reality, ecofeminists have also offered some harsh criticism of Leopold, Callicott, and advocates of the deep ecological approach, as well as extensionists like Singer and Regan.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-38">[38]</a></sup> Like deep ecology, ecofeminism is not a singular theory, and covers a broad range of thought; in rough terms, its criticism of other forms of nature ethics is grounded in an attempt to synthesize the insights of environmental ethics and animal advocacy with a feminist analysis of Western ethics and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The resulting attempt to rethink our relationship to the animal and nature casts speciesism and anthropocentrism as symptoms of a deeper patriarchy in the Western tradition that needs to be deconstructed before a successful animal ethic can be produced. As Josephine Donovan details, there is evidence to suggest a strong emotional and philosophical affinity among Anglo-American antivivisectionists and suffragettes, who viewed their causes as common responses to Enlightenment rationalism and scientism, and jointly sought a “feminizing” of culture that would liberate human and animal alike.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-38">[38]</a></sup> Ecological feminism began as a critique and rejection of the western cultural worldview with its overemphasis on rationality, and linearity.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> It argued against a Cartesian science which elevated the material and objective above the spiritual and the subjective as appropriate ways of knowing the world. Like deep ecology, ecological feminism stresses the importance of experience, and personal experience at that. However, the ecofeminists seem to be talking about experience in a sense more related to bioregionalism than deep ecology.</p>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a> and deep ecology put forward a new conceptualization of the self. Some ecofeminists, such as <a href="/wiki/Marti_Kheel" title="Marti Kheel">Marti Kheel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> argue that self-realization and identification with all nature places too much emphasis on the whole, at the expense of the independent being. Similarly, some ecofeminists place more emphasis on the problem of <a href="/wiki/Androcentrism" title="Androcentrism">androcentrism</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a>. To others, like <a href="/wiki/Karen_J._Warren" title="Karen J. Warren">Karen J. Warren</a>, the domination of women is tethered conceptually and historically to the domination of nature. Ecofeminism denies abstract <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a> and embraces the interconnectedness of the living world; relationships, including our relationship with non-human nature, are not extrinsic to our identity and are essential in defining what it means to be human. Warren argues that hierarchical classifications in general, such as <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Speciesism" title="Speciesism">speciesism</a>, are all forms of <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> and are no different from <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexism</a>. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a> is simply another form of discrimination as a result of our flawed <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">value structure</a> and should be abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experiential Deep Ecologist <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Macy" title="Joanna Macy">Joanna Macy</a> has attempted to avoid these conflicts and criticisms through her <a href="/w/index.php?title=Work_that_Reconnects&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Work that Reconnects (page does not exist)">Work that Reconnects</a>. By focussing Deep Ecology on the experience of the consciousness of personal depth within the participant, she speaks of "The Greening of the Self", which is part of the epochal journey of our times from an <a href="/wiki/Id,_ego_and_super-ego#Ego" title="Id, ego and super-ego">Egoic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egotistical" class="mw-redirect" title="Egotistical">Egotistical</a> Self to an Ecological Self.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Links_with_other_philosophies">Links with other philosophies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Links with other philosophies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parallels have been drawn between deep ecology and other philosophies, in particular those of the <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> movement, <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deep_Green_Resistance" title="Deep Green Resistance">Deep Green Resistance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">anarcho-primitivism</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a>'s 1975 book <i><a href="/wiki/Animal_Liberation_(book)" title="Animal Liberation (book)">Animal Liberation</a></i> critiqued anthropocentrism and put the case for animals to be given moral consideration. This can be seen as a part of a process of expanding the prevailing system of ethics to wider groupings. However, Singer has disagreed with deep ecology's belief in the intrinsic value of nature separate from questions of suffering, taking a more utilitarian stance.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights movements</a> also brought about expansion of the ethical system for their particular domains. Likewise deep ecology brought the whole of nature under moral consideration.<sup id="cite_ref-Zimmermann_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zimmermann-44">[44]</a></sup> The links with <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> are perhaps the strongest, as "proponents of such ideas argue that 'All life has intrinsic value'".<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many in the radical environmental direct-action movement <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a> claim to follow deep ecology, as indicated by one of their slogans <i>No compromise in defence of mother earth</i>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/David_Foreman" title="David Foreman">David Foreman</a>, the co-founder of the movement, has also been a strong advocate for deep ecology, and engaged in a public debate with <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a> on the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Judi_Bari" title="Judi Bari">Judi Bari</a> was another prominent Earth Firster who espoused deep ecology. Many Earth First! actions have a distinct deep ecological theme; often these actions will be to save an area of <a href="/wiki/Old_growth_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Old growth forest">old growth forest</a>, the habitat of a snail or an owl, even individual trees. Actions are often symbolic or have other political aims. At one point Arne Næss also engaged in environmental direct action, though not under the Earth First! banner, when he chained himself to rocks in front of <a href="/wiki/Mardalsfossen" title="Mardalsfossen">Mardalsfossen</a>, a waterfall in a Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a>, in a successful protest against the building of a dam.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> currents in the movement, especially in the United Kingdom. For example, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a>, pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">forest gardening</a> in temperate climates, wrote the essay "Can Life Survive?" in <i>Deep Ecology &amp; Anarchism</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Greenway (page does not exist)">Robert Greenway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roszak_(scholar)" title="Theodore Roszak (scholar)">Theodore Roszak</a> have employed the deep ecology platform as a means to argue for <a href="/wiki/Ecopsychology" title="Ecopsychology">ecopsychology</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although ecopsychology is a highly differentiated umbrella that encompasses many practices and perspectives, its ethos is generally consistent with deep ecology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As this now almost forty-year-old "field" expands and continues to be reinterpreted by a variety of practitioners, social and natural scientists, and humanists, "ecopsychology" may change to include these novel perspectives.</p>
<p>Heidegger’s critique of technology<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has certainly inspired environmentalist and postmodernist of our time. Deep ecologists, like Heidegger<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, allege that certain metaphysical presuppositions are responsible for ecological destruction, and also contend that any transformation can be brought about only through a renewed awareness about the world. Then the key to environmental crisis, require an ontological shift: from an anthropocentric and utilitarian understanding of world to an understanding which lets things be. A non-anthropocentric humanity would probably initiate attitudes, practices, and institutions that would exhibit respect and care for all beings.</p>
