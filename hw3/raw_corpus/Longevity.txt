<p>The word "<b>longevity</b>" is sometimes used as a synonym for "<a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a> - however, the term "longevity" is sometimes meant to refer only to especially long-lived members of a population, whereas "life expectancy" is always defined statistically as the average number of years remaining at a given age. For example, a population's life expectancy at birth is the same as the average age at death for all people born in the same year (in the case of cohorts). Longevity is best thought of as a term for general audiences meaning 'typical length of life' and specific statistical definitions should be clarified when necessary.</p>
<p>Reflections on longevity have usually gone beyond acknowledging the brevity of human life and have included thinking about methods to extend life. Longevity has been a topic not only for the scientific community but also for writers of <a href="/wiki/Hyperborei" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyperborei">travel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopian</a> novels.</p>
<p>There are many difficulties in authenticating the longest human <a href="/wiki/Maximum_life_span" title="Maximum life span">life span</a> ever by modern verification standards, owing to inaccurate or incomplete birth statistics. Fiction, legend, and folklore have proposed or claimed life spans in the past or future vastly longer than those verified by modern standards, and <a href="/wiki/Longevity_narratives" class="mw-redirect" title="Longevity narratives">longevity narratives</a> and unverified <a href="/wiki/Longevity_claims" title="Longevity claims">longevity claims</a> frequently speak of their existence in the present.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Life_annuity" title="Life annuity">life annuity</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Longevity_insurance" title="Longevity insurance">longevity insurance</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_expectancy.2C_as_of_2010">Life expectancy, as of 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life expectancy, as of 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_life_expectancy" title="List of countries by life expectancy">List of countries by life expectancy</a></div>
<p>Various factors contribute to an individual's longevity. Significant factors in life expectancy include <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, access to <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a>, diet and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exercise" class="mw-redirect" title="Exercise">exercise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crime_rates" class="mw-redirect" title="Crime rates">crime rates</a>. Below is a list of life expectancies in different types of countries:<sup id="cite_ref-ciawfb2010_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ciawfb2010-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">Developed countries</a>: 77–90 years (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>: 81.29 years, 2010 est.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">Developing countries</a>: 32–80 years (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>: 41.37 years, 2010 est.)</li>
</ul>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gerontology_Research_Group" title="Gerontology Research Group">Gerontology Research Group</a> validates current longevity records by modern standards, and maintains a <a href="/wiki/List_of_supercentenarians" class="mw-redirect" title="List of supercentenarians">list of supercentenarians</a>; many other unvalidated <a href="/wiki/Longevity_claims" title="Longevity claims">longevity claims</a> exist. Record-holding individuals include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Eilif Philipsen (1682-1785, 102 years, 333 days): first person to reach the ages of 100, 101, and 102 (on July 21, 1782) and whose age could be validated.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geert_Adriaans_Boomgaard" title="Geert Adriaans Boomgaard">Geert Adriaans Boomgaard</a> (1788–1899, 110 years, 135 days): first person to reach the age of 110 (on September 21, 1898) and whose age could be validated<br />
<a href="/wiki/Margaret_Ann_Neve" title="Margaret Ann Neve">Margaret Ann Neve</a>, (18 May 1792 - 4 April 1903, 110 years, 346 days) the first validated female supercentenarian (on 18 May 1902)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Calment" title="Jeanne Calment">Jeanne Calment</a> (1875–1997, 122 years, 164 days): the oldest person in history whose age has been verified by modern documentation. This defines the modern human <a href="/wiki/Maximum_life_span" title="Maximum life span">life span</a>, which is set by the oldest documented individual who ever lived.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sarah_Knauss" title="Sarah Knauss">Sarah Knauss</a> (1880–1999, 119 years, 97 days): the second oldest documented person in modern times and the oldest American.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jiroemon_Kimura" title="Jiroemon Kimura">Jiroemon Kimura</a> (1897–2013, 116 years, 54 days): the oldest man in history whose age has been verified by modern documentation.</li>
</ul>

<p>Evidence-based studies indicate that longevity is based on two major factors, genetics and lifestyle choices.<sup id="cite_ref-USC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USC-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twin studies have estimated that approximately 20-30% the variation in human lifespan can be related to genetics, with the rest due to individual behaviors and environmental factors which can be modified.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although over 200 gene variants have been associated with longevity according to a US-Belgian-UK research database of human genetic variants,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> these explain only a small fraction of the heritability.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A 2012 study found that even modest amounts of leisure time physical exercise can extend life expectancy by as much as 4.5 years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lymphoblastoid" class="mw-redirect" title="Lymphoblastoid">Lymphoblastoid</a> cell lines established from blood samples of <a href="/wiki/Centenarians" class="mw-redirect" title="Centenarians">centenarians</a> have significantly higher activity of the DNA repair protein PARP (<a href="/wiki/Poly_ADP_ribose_polymerase" title="Poly ADP ribose polymerase">Poly ADP ribose polymerase</a>) than cell lines from younger (20 to 70 year old) individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9587069_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9587069-10">[10]</a></sup> The lymphocytic cells of centenarians have characteristics typical of cells from young people, both in their capability of priming the mechanism of repair after <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> sublethal <a href="/wiki/DNA_oxidation" title="DNA oxidation">oxidative DNA damage</a> and in their PARP gene expression.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17518695_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17518695-11">[11]</a></sup> These findings suggest that elevated PARP gene expression contributes to the longevity of centenarians, consistent with the <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_theory_of_aging" title="DNA damage theory of aging">DNA damage theory of aging</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_factors">Environmental factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study of the regions of the world known as <a href="/wiki/Blue_Zone" title="Blue Zone">blue zones</a>, where people commonly live active lives past 100 years of age, speculated that longevity is related to a healthy social and family life, not smoking, eating a plant-based diet, frequent consumption of legumes and nuts, and engaging in regular physical activity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In a cohort study, the combination of a plant based diet, normal BMI, and not smoking accounted for differences up to 15 years in life expectancy.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Korean court records going back to 1392 indicate that the average lifespan of eunuchs was 70.0 ± 1.76 years, which was 14.4–19.1 years longer than the lifespan of non-castrated men of similar socio-economic status.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Alameda_County_Study" title="Alameda County Study">Alameda County Study</a> hypothesized three additional lifestyle characteristics that promote longevity: limiting alcohol consumption, sleeping 7 to 8 hours per night, and not <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/snack" class="extiw" title="wikt:snack">snacking</a> (eating between meals), although the study found the association between these characteristics and mortality is "weak at best".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There are however many other possible factors potentially affecting longevity, including the impact of high peer competition, which is typically experienced in large cities.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_over_time">Change over time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Change over time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In preindustrial times, deaths at young and middle age were more common than they are today. This is not due to genetics, but because of environmental factors such as disease, accidents, and malnutrition, especially since the former were not generally treatable with pre-20th century medicine. Deaths from childbirth were common for women, and many children did not live past infancy. In addition, most people who did attain old age were likely to die quickly from the above-mentioned untreatable health problems. Despite this, we do find many examples of pre-20th century individuals attaining lifespans of 72 years or greater, including <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cato_the_Elder" title="Cato the Elder">Cato the Elder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_of_Pomerania" title="Eric of Pomerania">Eric of Pomerania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Polhem" title="Christopher Polhem">Christopher Polhem</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. This was also true for poorer people like <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Laborer" title="Laborer">laborers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">Genealogists</a> will almost certainly find ancestors living to their 70s, 80s and even 90s several hundred years ago.</p>
<p>For example, an 1871 census in the UK (the first of its kind, but personal data from other censuses dates back to 1841 and numerical data back to 1801) found the average male life expectancy as being 44, but if infant mortality is subtracted, males who lived to adulthood averaged 75 years. The present male life expectancy in the UK is 77 years for males and 81 for females, while the United States averages 74 for males and 80 for females.</p>
<p>Studies have shown that black American males have the shortest lifespans of any group of people in the US, averaging only 69 years (Asian-American females average the longest).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This reflects overall poorer health and greater prevalence of heart disease, obesity, diabetes, and cancer among black American men.</p>
<p>Women normally outlive men, and this was as true in pre-industrial times as today. Theories for this include smaller bodies (and thus less stress on the heart), a stronger immune system (since testosterone acts as an immunosuppressant), and less tendency to engage in physically dangerous activities.</p>
<p>There is a current debate as to whether or not the pursuit of longevity is a worthwhile health care goal for the United States. Bioethicist <a href="/wiki/Ezekiel_Emanuel" title="Ezekiel Emanuel">Ezekiel Emanuel</a>, who is also one of the architects of <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">ObamaCare</a>, has stated that the pursuit of longevity via the <a href="/wiki/Compression_of_morbidity" title="Compression of morbidity">compression of morbidity</a> explanation is a "fantasy" and that life is not worth living after age 75; therefore longevity should not be a goal of health care policy.<sup id="cite_ref-Emanuel_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel-19">[19]</a></sup> This has been refuted by neurosurgeon <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Faria" class="mw-redirect" title="Miguel Faria">Miguel Faria</a>, who states that life can be worthwhile in healthy old age; that the compression of morbidity is a real phenomenon; that longevity should be pursued in association with quality of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Faria_75_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faria_75-20">[20]</a></sup> Faria has discussed how longevity in association with leading healthy lifestyles can lead to the postponement of <a href="/wiki/Senescence" title="Senescence">senescence</a> as well as happiness and wisdom in old age.<sup id="cite_ref-Faria_longevity_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faria_longevity-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_longevity">Limited longevity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Limited longevity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All of the biological organisms have a limited longevity, and different species of animals and plants have different potentials of longevity. Misrepair-accumulation aging theory <sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> suggests that the potential of longevity of an organism is related to its structural complexity.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Limited longevity is due to the limited structural complexity of the organism. If a species of organisms has too high structural complexity, most of its individuals would die before the reproduction age, and the species could not survive. This theory suggests that limited structural complexity and limited longevity are essential for the survival of a species.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Longevity_traditions">Longevity traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Longevity traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Longevity_traditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Longevity traditions">Longevity traditions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longevity_claims" title="Longevity claims">Longevity claims</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Longevity_traditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Longevity traditions">Longevity traditions</a> are traditions about long-lived people (generally <a href="/wiki/Supercentenarian" title="Supercentenarian">supercentenarians</a>), and practices that have been believed to confer longevity.<sup id="cite_ref-trad1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trad1-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-trad2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trad2-26">[26]</a></sup> A comparison and contrast of "longevity in antiquity" (such as the <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">Sumerian King List</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Genealogies_of_Genesis" title="Genealogies of Genesis">genealogies of Genesis</a>, and the Persian <a href="/wiki/Shahnameh" title="Shahnameh">Shahnameh</a>) with "longevity in historical times" (common-era cases through twentieth-century news reports) is elaborated in detail in <a href="/wiki/Lucian_Boia" title="Lucian Boia">Lucian Boia</a>'s 2004 book <i>Forever Young: A Cultural History of Longevity from Antiquity to the Present</i> and other sources.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fountain_of_Youth" title="Fountain of Youth">Fountain of Youth</a> reputedly restores the youth of anyone who drinks of its waters. The <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, following older Jewish tradition, attributes healing to the <a href="/wiki/Pool_of_Bethesda" title="Pool of Bethesda">Pool of Bethesda</a> when the waters are "stirred" by an angel.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> After the death of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Juan Ponce de León</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Oviedo_y_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo y Valdés">Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo y Valdés</a> wrote in <i>Historia General y Natural de las Indias</i> (1535) that Ponce de León was looking for the waters of <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a> to cure his aging.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Traditions that have been believed to confer greater human longevity also include <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-kohn_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kohn-30">[30]</a></sup> such as that attributed to <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Flamel" title="Nicolas Flamel">Nicolas Flamel</a>. In the modern era, the <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_diet" title="Okinawa diet">Okinawa diet</a> has some reputation of linkage to exceptionally high ages.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recent <a href="/wiki/Longevity_claims" title="Longevity claims">longevity claims</a> are subcategorized by many editions of <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> into four groups: "In late life, very old people often tend to advance their ages at the rate of about 17 years per decade .... Several celebrated super-centenarians (over 110 years) are believed to have been double lives (father and son, relations with the same names or successive bearers of a title) .... A number of instances have been commercially sponsored, while a fourth category of recent claims are those made for political ends ...."<sup id="cite_ref-g_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g-32">[32]</a></sup> The estimate of 17 years per decade was corroborated by the 1901 and 1911 British censuses.<sup id="cite_ref-g_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g-32">[32]</a></sup> Mazess and Forman also discovered in 1978 that inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Vilcabamba,_Ecuador" title="Vilcabamba, Ecuador">Vilcabamba, Ecuador</a>, claimed excessive longevity by using their fathers' and grandfathers' baptismal entries.<sup id="cite_ref-g_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ng_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng-33">[33]</a></sup> <i>Time</i> magazine considered that, by the Soviet Union, longevity had been elevated to a state-supported "Methuselah cult".<sup id="cite_ref-time_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ripley" title="Robert Ripley">Robert Ripley</a> regularly reported supercentenarian claims in <a href="/wiki/Ripley%27s_Believe_It_or_Not!" title="Ripley's Believe It or Not!">Ripley's Believe It or Not!</a>, usually citing his own reputation as a fact-checker to claim reliability.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a> view on the future of longevity is that life expectancy in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> will be in the mid-80s by 2050 (up from 77.85 in 2006) and will top out eventually in the low 90s, barring major scientific advances that can change the rate of human aging itself, as opposed to merely treating the effects of aging as is done today. The Census Bureau also predicted that the United States would have 5.3 million people aged over 100 in 2100. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> has also made projections far out into the future, up to 2300, at which point it projects that life expectancies in most developed countries will be between 100 and 106 years and still rising, though more and more slowly than before. These projections also suggest that life expectancies in poor countries will still be less than those in rich countries in 2300, in some cases by as much as 20 years. The UN itself mentioned that gaps in life expectancy so far in the future may well not exist, especially since the exchange of technology between rich and poor countries and the <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a> and development of poor countries may cause their life expectancies to converge fully with those of rich countries long before that point, similarly to the way life expectancies between rich and poor countries have already been converging over the last 60 years as better medicine, technology, and living conditions became accessible to many people in poor countries. The UN has warned that these projections are uncertain, and cautions that any change or advancement in medical technology could invalidate such projections.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent increases in the rates of <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_diseases" class="mw-redirect" title="Lifestyle diseases">lifestyle diseases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_2" title="Diabetes mellitus type 2">diabetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, may eventually slow or reverse this trend toward increasing life expectancy in the developed world, but have not yet done so. The average age of the US population is getting higher<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and these diseases show up in older people.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jennifer Couzin-Frankel examined how much mortality from various causes would have to drop in order to boost life expectancy and concluded that most of the past increases in life expectancy occurred because of improved survival rates for young people. She states that it seems unlikely that life expectancy at birth will ever exceed 85 years.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michio_Kaku" title="Michio Kaku">Michio Kaku</a> argues that <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> and future breakthroughs will accelerate the rate of life expectancy increase indefinitely.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Already genetic engineering has allowed the life expectancy of certain primates to be doubled, and for human skin cells in labs to divide and live indefinitely without becoming cancerous.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, since 1840, record life expectancy has risen <a href="/wiki/Linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear">linearly</a> for men and women, albeit more slowly for men. For women the increase has been almost three months per year, for men almost 2.7 months per year. In light of steady increase, without any sign of limitation, the suggestion that life expectancy will top out must be treated with caution. Scientists Oeppen and Vaupel observe that experts who assert that "life expectancy is approaching a ceiling ... have repeatedly been proven wrong." It is thought that life expectancy for women has increased more dramatically owing to the considerable advances in medicine related to childbirth.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mice have been genetically engineered to live twice as long as ordinary mice. Drugs such as deprenyl are a part of the prescribing pharmacopia of veterinarians specifically to increase mammal lifespan. A large plurality of research chemicals have been described at the scientific literature that increase the lifespan of a number of species.</p>
<p>Some argue that <a href="/wiki/Molecular_nanotechnology" title="Molecular nanotechnology">molecular nanotechnology</a> will greatly extend human life spans. If the rate of increase of life span can be raised with these technologies to a level of twelve months increase per year, this is defined as effective <a href="/wiki/Biological_immortality" title="Biological immortality">biological immortality</a> and is the goal of radical life extension.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_biological_longevity">Non-human biological longevity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non-human biological longevity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_long-living_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="List of long-living organisms">List of long-living organisms</a></div>
<p><b>Currently living</b>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Methuselah_(tree)" title="Methuselah (tree)">Methuselah</a>: 4,800-year-old <a href="/wiki/Bristlecone_pine" title="Bristlecone pine">bristlecone pine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/White_Mountains_(California)" title="White Mountains (California)">White Mountains of California</a>, the oldest currently living organism known.</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Non-living</b>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Possibly 250-million year-old bacteria, <i>Bacillus permians</i>, were revived from stasis after being found in sodium chloride crystals in a cavern in New Mexico. Russell Vreeland, and colleagues from <a href="/wiki/West_Chester_University" title="West Chester University">West Chester University</a> in Pennsylvania, reported on October 18, 2000 that they had revived the <a href="/wiki/Halobacteria" class="mw-redirect" title="Halobacteria">halobacteria</a> after bathing them with a nutrient solution. If they had survived for 250 million years, they would be the oldest living organisms ever recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> However, their findings date the crystal surrounding the bacteria, and DNA analysis suggests the bacteria themselves are likely to be less ancient.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Bristlecone_pine" title="Bristlecone pine">bristlecone pine</a> nicknamed <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(tree)" title="Prometheus (tree)">"Prometheus"</a>, felled by a climate dynamics researcher in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin_National_Park" title="Great Basin National Park">Great Basin National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> in 1964, found to be about 4,900 years old, is the longest-lived single organism known.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Arctica_islandica" title="Arctica islandica">quahog clam</a> (<i>Arctica islandica</i>) is exceptionally long-lived, with a maximum recorded age of 507 years, the longest of any animal.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Other clams of the species have been recorded as living up to 374 years.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lamellibrachia" title="Lamellibrachia">Lamellibrachia</a> luymesi</i>, a deep-sea cold-seep tubeworm, is estimated to reach ages of over 250 years based on a model of its growth rates.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanako_(koi_fish)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hanako (koi fish) (page does not exist)">Hanako</a> was the longest-lived vertebrate ever recorded at 226 years.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Bowhead_whale" title="Bowhead whale">bowhead whale</a> killed in a hunt was found to be approximately 211 years old (possibly up to 245 years old), the longest-lived mammal known.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tu%27i_Malila" title="Tu'i Malila">Tu'i Malila</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Radiated_tortoise" title="Radiated tortoise">radiated tortoise</a> presented to the <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tongan</a> royal family by <a href="/wiki/Captain_Cook" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain Cook">Captain Cook</a>, lived for over 185 years. It is the oldest documented reptile. <a href="/wiki/Adwaitya" class="mw-redirect" title="Adwaitya">Adwaitya</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Aldabra_Giant_Tortoise" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldabra Giant Tortoise">Aldabra Giant Tortoise</a>, may have lived for up to 250 years.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_immortality">Biological immortality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Longevity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biological immortality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biological_immortality" title="Biological immortality">Biological immortality</a></div>
<p>Certain exotic organisms do not seem to be subject to aging and can live indefinitely. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Tardigrades" class="mw-redirect" title="Tardigrades">Tardigrades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(genus)" title="Hydra (genus)">Hydras</a>. That is not to say that these organisms cannot die, merely that they only die as a result of disease or injury rather than age-related deterioration (and that they are not subject to the <a href="/wiki/Hayflick_limit" title="Hayflick limit">Hayflick limit</a>).</p>
