			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ethical consumerism</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Ethical consumerism</b> (alternatively called <b>ethical consumption</b>, <b>ethical purchasing</b>, <b>moral purchasing</b>, <b>ethical sourcing</b>, <b>ethical shopping</b> or <b>green consumerism</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Consumer_activism" title="Consumer activism">consumer activism</a> that is based on the concept of <a href="/wiki/Dollar_voting" title="Dollar voting">dollar voting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is practiced through 'positive buying' in that ethical products are favoured, or 'moral <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a>', that is negative purchasing and company-based purchasing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "ethical consumer", now used generically, was first popularised by the UK magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Ethical_Consumer" title="Ethical Consumer">Ethical Consumer</a></i>, first published in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Ethical Consumer</i> magazine's key innovation was to produce 'ratings tables', inspired by the criteria-based approach of the then emerging <a href="/wiki/Ethical_investment" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethical investment">ethical investment</a> movement. <i>Ethical Consumer'</i>s ratings tables awarded companies negative marks (and from 2005 overall scores) across a range of ethical and environmental categories such as 'animal rights', 'human rights' and 'pollution and toxics', empowering consumers to make ethically informed consumption choices and providing campaigners with reliable information on corporate behaviour. Such criteria-based ethical and environmental ratings have subsequently become commonplace both in providing consumer information and in business-to-business <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> ratings such as those provided by Innovest, <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Investments#The_Calvert_Foundation" title="Calvert Investments">Calvert Foundation</a>, Domini, IRRC, <a href="/wiki/TIAA%E2%80%93CREF" class="mw-redirect" title="TIAA–CREF">TIAA–CREF</a> and KLD Analytics. Today, <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P." title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> provide "environmental, social and governance" ratings direct to the financial data screens of hundreds of thousands of stock market traders.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The not-for-profit Ethical Consumer Research Association continues to publish <i>Ethical Consumer</i> and its associated website, which provides free access to ethical ratings tables.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basis">Basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_morality">Global morality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Global morality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/350px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg" width="350" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/525px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/700px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2352" data-file-height="1568" /></a>

<p>In <i>Unequal Freedoms: The Global Market As An Ethical System</i> (1998), <a href="/wiki/John_McMurtry" title="John McMurtry">John McMurtry</a> argues that no purchasing decision exists that does not itself imply some moral choice, and that there is no purchasing that is not ultimately moral in nature. This mirrors older arguments, especially by the <a href="/wiki/Anabaptists" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptists">Anabaptists</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">Mennonites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, that one must accept all personal moral and spiritual liability of all harms done at any distance in space or time to anyone by one's own choices. It is often suggested that <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> scriptures further direct followers towards practising good stewardship of the Earth, under an obligation to a God who is believed to have created the planet for us to share with other creatures. A similar argument presented from a <a href="/wiki/Secular" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular">secular</a> <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> point of view is that it is simply better for human beings to acknowledge that the planet supports <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> only because of a delicate balance of many different factors.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spending_as_morality">Spending as morality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spending as morality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some trust criteria, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Creditworthiness" class="mw-redirect" title="Creditworthiness">creditworthiness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Implied_warranty" title="Implied warranty">implied warranty</a>, are considered to be part of any purchasing or sourcing decision. However, these terms refer to broader systems of guidance that would, ideally, cause any purchasing decision to disqualify offered products or services based on non-price criteria that affect the moral rather than the functional liabilities of the entire production process. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hawken" title="Paul Hawken">Paul Hawken</a>, a proponent of <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism" title="Natural Capitalism">Natural Capitalism</a>, refers to "comprehensive outcomes" of production services as opposed to the "culminative outcomes" of using the product of such services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Often, moral criteria are part of a much broader shift away from <a href="/wiki/Commodity_markets" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodity markets">commodity markets</a> towards a deeper <a href="/wiki/Service_economy" title="Service economy">service economy</a> where all activities, from growing to harvesting to processing to delivery, are considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Value_chain" title="Value chain">value chain</a> and for which consumers are "responsible".</p>
<p>Andrew Wilson, Director of the UK's Ashridge Centre for Business and Society, argues that "Shopping is more important than voting", and that the disposition of <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> is the most basic role we play in any system of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some theorists believe that it is the clearest way that we express our actual moral choices, i.e., if we say we care about something but continue to buy from parties that have a high probability of risk of harm or destruction of that thing, we don't really care about it, we are practicing a form of simple <a href="/wiki/Hypocrisy" title="Hypocrisy">hypocrisy</a>.</p>
<p>In an effort by churches to advocate moral and ethical consumerism, many have become involved in the <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">Fair Trade</a> movement:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ten_Thousand_Villages" title="Ten Thousand Villages">Ten Thousand Villages</a> is affiliated with the Mennonite Central Committee<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SERRV_International" title="SERRV International">SERRV International</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is partnered with Catholic Relief Services<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Lutheran World Relief<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Village Markets of Africa sells Fair Trade gifts from the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran Church</a> in Kenya<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Catholic_Relief_Services" title="Catholic Relief Services">Catholic Relief Services</a> has their own Fair Trade mission in CRS Fair Trade<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_diverse_use_of_the_term">Growing diverse use of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growing diverse use of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As large corporations have tried to position themselves as moral, principled or ethical organisations, the definition has become wider and means different things to different groups of people. For example, <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> started to sell salads (a more healthy choice) and has a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a> blog. <b>Ethical Consumerism</b> can be seen as a movement in <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a>, which may or may not reflect actual changes in the practices of businesses. Particular areas of interest for large businesses are <b>environmental impact</b> and the <b>treatment of workers</b> at the bottom of the organisational hierarchy. This change reflects an increasing awareness of ethical issues and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_identity" title="Corporate identity">corporate identity</a> amongst mainstream <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a>.</p>

<p>Along with disclosure of ingredients, some <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_labelling" title="Mandatory labelling">mandatory labelling</a> of origins of clothing or food is required in all <a href="/wiki/Developed_nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed nations">developed nations</a>. This practice has been extended in some <a href="/wiki/Developing_nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing nations">developing nations</a>, e.g., where every item carries the name, phone number and fax number of the factory where it was made so a buyer can inspect its conditions. And, more importantly, to prove that the item was not made by "<a href="/wiki/Prison_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Prison labor">prison labor</a>", use of which to produce export goods is banned in most developed nations. Such labels have also been used for boycotts, as when the merchandise mark <a href="/wiki/Made_in_Germany" title="Made in Germany">Made in Germany</a> was introduced in 1887.</p>
<p>These labels serve as tokens of some reliable validation process, some <a href="/wiki/Instructional_capital" title="Instructional capital">instructional capital</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> much as does a brand name or a nation's flag. They also signal some <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">social capital</a>, or trust, in some community of auditors that must follow those instructions to validate those labels.</p>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Verus_Carbon_Neutral_Sign.JPG" class="image"><img alt="Verus Carbon Neutral Sign.JPG" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Verus_Carbon_Neutral_Sign.JPG" width="150" height="160" data-file-width="150" data-file-height="160" /></a></div>
<p>Some companies in the United States, though currently not required to reduce their <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>, are doing so voluntarily by changing their energy use practices, as well as by directly funding (through <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offsets</a>), businesses that are already sustainable—or are developing or improving <a href="/wiki/Environmental_technology" title="Environmental technology">green technologies</a> for the future.</p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Virginia-Highland" class="mw-redirect" title="Virginia-Highland">Virginia-Highland</a> neighborhood became the first Carbon-Neutral Zone in the United States. Seventeen merchants in Virginia-Highland allowed their carbon footprint to be audited. Now, they are partnered with the Valley Wood Carbon Sequestration Project—thousands of acres of forest in rural Georgia—through the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Climate_Exchange" title="Chicago Climate Exchange">Chicago Climate Exchange</a> (CCX).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The businesses involved in the partnership display the Verus Carbon Neutral seal in each store front and posted a sign prominently declaring the area's Carbon Neutral status. (CCX ceased trading carbon credits at the end of 2010 due to inactivity in the U.S. carbon markets,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> although carbon exchanges were intended to still be facilitated.)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over time, some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> theorists suggest, the amount of <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">social capital</a> or trust invested in nation-states (or "flags") will continue to decrease, and that placed in corporations (or "brands") will increase. This can only be offset by retrenched <a href="/wiki/National_sovereignty" class="mw-redirect" title="National sovereignty">national sovereignty</a> to reinforce shared national standards in <a href="/wiki/Tax,_trade,_and_tariff" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax, trade, and tariff">tax, trade, and tariff</a> laws, and by placing the trust in civil society in such "moral labels". These arguments have been a major focus of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">anti-globalization movement</a>, which includes many broader arguments against the amoral nature of markets as such. However, the economic school of <a href="/wiki/Public_Choice_Theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Choice Theory">Public Choice Theory</a> pioneered by <a href="/wiki/James_M._Buchanan" title="James M. Buchanan">James M. Buchanan</a> has offered counter-arguments based on economic demonstration to this theory of 'amoral markets' versus 'moral governments'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_of_concern">Areas of concern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Areas of concern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ethical_Consumer" title="Ethical Consumer">Ethical Consumer Research Association</a>, the alternative consumer organisation, collects and categorises information of more than 30,000 companies according to their performance in five main areas, composing the Ethiscore:</p>
<ul>
<li>Environment: Environmental Reporting, Nuclear Power, Climate Change, Pollution &amp; Toxics, Habitats &amp; Resources</li>
<li>People: Human Rights, Workers' Rights, Supply Chain Policy, Irresponsible Marketing, Armaments</li>
<li>Animals: Animal Testing, Factory Farming, Other Animal Rights</li>
<li>Politics: Political Activity, Boycott Call, Genetic Engineering, Anti-Social Finance, Company Ethos</li>
<li>Product Sustainability: Organic, Fairtrade, Positive Environmental Features, Other Sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=GfK_NOP&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="GfK NOP (page does not exist)">GfK NOP</a>, the market research group, has made a five-country study of consumer beliefs about the ethics of large companies. The report was described in a <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> article published on February 20, 2007 entitled "Ethical consumption makes mark on branding",<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and was followed up by an online debate/discussion hosted by FT.com.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The countries surveyed were Germany, the United States, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, France and Spain. More than half of respondents in Germany and the US believed there is a serious deterioration in standards of corporate practice. Almost half of those surveyed in Britain, France and Spain held similar beliefs.</p>
<p>About a third of respondents told researchers they would pay higher prices for ethical brands though perception of various companies' ethical or unethical status varied considerably from country to country.</p>
<p>The most ethically perceived brands were The <a href="/wiki/The_Co-operative_Group" title="The Co-operative Group">Co-op</a> (in the UK), <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> (in the US), <a href="/wiki/Danone" title="Danone">Danone</a> (in France), <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> (in Germany) and <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a> (in Spain). Coca-Cola, Danone, Adidas and Nestlé did not appear anywhere in the UK's list of 15 most ethical companies. <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a> appeared in the lists of the other four countries but not in the UK's list.</p>
<p>In the UK, <a href="/wiki/The_Co-operative_Bank" title="The Co-operative Bank">The Co-operative Bank</a> has produced an Ethical Consumerism Report<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (formerly the Ethical Purchasing Index) since 2001. The report measures the market size and growth of a basket of 'ethical' products and services, and valued UK ethical consumerism at <a href="/wiki/GBP" class="mw-redirect" title="GBP">GBP</a>36.0 billion (~<a href="/wiki/USD" class="mw-redirect" title="USD">USD</a>54.4 billion) in 2008, and <a href="/wiki/GBP" class="mw-redirect" title="GBP">GBP</a>47.2 billion (<a href="/wiki/USD" class="mw-redirect" title="USD">USD</a>72.5 billion) in 2012.</p>
<p>A number of organisations provide research-based evaluations of the behavior of companies around the world, assessing them along ethical dimensions such as <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Green_America" title="Green America">Green America</a> is a not-for-profit membership organization founded in 1982 that provides the <i>Green American Seal of Approval</i> and produces a "Responsible Shopper" guide to "alert consumers and investors to problems with companies that they may shop with or invest in."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Ethical Consumer Research Association is a not-for-profit workers' co-operative founded in the UK in 1988 to "provide information on the companies behind the brand names and to promote the ethical use of consumer power"<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which provides an online searchable database under the name Corporate Critic<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> or Ethiscore.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Ethiscore is a weightable numerical rating designed as a quick guide to the ethical status of companies, or brands in a particular area, and is linked to a more detailed ethical assessment. "alonovo" is an online shopping portal that provides similar weightable ethical ratings termed the "Corporate Social Behavior Index".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conscientious_consumption">Conscientious consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conscientious consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The consumer rationalizes unnecessary and even unwanted consumption by saying that "it's for a good cause".<sup id="cite_ref-Sulik_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sulik-28">[28]</a></sup> As a result, the consumer buys <a href="/wiki/Pink_ribbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Pink ribbons">pink ribbons</a> during <a href="/wiki/National_Breast_Cancer_Awareness_Month" title="National Breast Cancer Awareness Month">National Breast Cancer Awareness Month</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">green products</a> to support the environment, candy and popcorn from school children, greeting cards and gift wrap from charities, and many other, often unwanted objects. The consumer avoids considering whether the price offered is fair, whether a small cash donation would be more effective with far less work, or even whether selling the item is consistent with the ostensible mission, such as when sports teams sell candy.</p>
<p>Some of these efforts are based on <a href="/wiki/Concept_brand" class="mw-redirect" title="Concept brand">concept brands</a>: the consumer is buying an association with women's health or environmental concerns as much as she or he is buying a tangible product.<sup id="cite_ref-Sulik_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sulik-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_giving">Alternative giving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Alternative giving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alternative_giving" title="Alternative giving">Alternative giving</a></div>
<p>In response to an increasing demand for ethical consumerism surrounding gift giving occasions, charities have promoted an alternative gift market, in which charitable contributions are made on behalf of the gift "recipient". The "recipient" receives a card explaining the selected gift, while the actual gift item (frequently agricultural supplies or domestic animals) is sent to a family in a poor community.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethical_consumerism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critics argue that the ability to affect structural change is limited in ethical consumerism. Some cite the preponderance of <a href="/wiki/Niche_marketing" class="mw-redirect" title="Niche marketing">niche markets</a> as the actual effect of ethical consumerism,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> while others argue that information is limited regarding the outcomes of a given purchase, preventing consumers from making informed ethical choices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Critics have also argued that the uneven distribution of wealth prevents consumerism, ethical or otherwise, from fulfilling its democratic potential.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>One study suggests that "Buying Green" serves as a license for unethical behavior – in their 2009 paper, "Do Green Products Make Us Better People?",<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Nina Mazar, Chen-Bo Zhong state the following:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In line with the halo associated with green consumerism, people act more altruistically after mere exposure to green than conventional products. However, people act less altruistically and are more likely to cheat and steal after purchasing green products as opposed to conventional products. Together, the studies show that consumption is more tightly connected to our social and ethical behaviors in directions and domains other than previously thought.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a 2010 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> article, British environmental writer and activist <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> argued that green consumers who do not articulate their values are part of "a catastrophic mistake" on the grounds that such consumerism "strengthens extrinsic values" (those that "concern status and self-advancement"), thereby "making future campaigns less likely to succeed".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
