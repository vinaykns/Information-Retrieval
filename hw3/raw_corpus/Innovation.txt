			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Innovation</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Innovation_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Innovation (disambiguation)">Innovation (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Innovation</b> can be defined simply as a "new idea, device or method".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, innovation is often also viewed as the application of better solutions that meet new requirements, unarticulated needs, or existing market needs.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This is accomplished through more-effective <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procedure_(term)" title="Procedure (term)">processes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Technologies">technologies</a>, or business models that are readily available to <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">markets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">governments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>. The term "innovation" can be defined as something original and more effective and, as a consequence, new, that "breaks into" the market or society.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is related to, but not the same as, <a href="/wiki/Invention" title="Invention">invention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Innovation is often manifested via the <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> process. The opposite of innovation is <a href="/wiki/Exnovation" title="Exnovation">exnovation</a>.</p>
<p>While a novel device is often described as an innovation, in economics, management science, and other fields of practice and analysis, innovation is generally considered to be the result of a process that brings together various novel ideas in a way that they affect society. In <a href="/wiki/Industrial_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial economics">industrial economics</a>, innovations are created and found empirically from services to meet the growing <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumer</a> <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2013 survey of literature on innovation found over 40 definitions. In an industrial survey of how the <a href="/wiki/Software_industry" title="Software industry">software industry</a> defined innovation, the following definition given by Crossan and Apaydin was considered to be the most complete, which builds on the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD)</a> manual's definition:<sup id="cite_ref-Henry2013_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry2013-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Innovation is: production or adoption, assimilation, and exploitation of a value-added novelty in economic and social spheres; renewal and enlargement of products, services, and markets; development of new methods of production; and establishment of new management systems. It is both a process and an outcome.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Two main dimensions of innovation were degree of <a href="/wiki/Novelty_(patent)" title="Novelty (patent)">novelty (patent)</a> i.e. whether an innovation is new to the firm, new to the market, new to the industry, and new to the world and type of innovation, whether it is process or <a href="/wiki/Product-service_system" title="Product-service system">product-service system</a> innovation.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry2013_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry2013-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inter-disciplinary_views">Inter-disciplinary views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inter-disciplinary views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_and_economics">Business and economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Business and economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Innovation_economics" title="Innovation economics">innovation economics</a></div>
<p>In business and economics, innovation can be a catalyst to growth. With rapid advancements in <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Communications">communications</a> over the past few decades, the old world concepts of <a href="/wiki/Factor_endowment" title="Factor endowment">factor endowments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comparative_advantage" title="Comparative advantage">comparative advantage</a> which focused on an area’s unique inputs are outmoded for today’s <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">global economy</a>. Economist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter" title="Joseph Schumpeter">Joseph Schumpeter</a>, who contributed greatly to the study of <a href="/wiki/Innovation_economics" title="Innovation economics">innovation economics</a>, argued that industries must incessantly revolutionize the economic structure from within, that is innovate with better or more effective processes and products, as well as market distribution, such as the connection from the craft shop to factory. He famously asserted that "<a href="/wiki/Creative_destruction" title="Creative destruction">creative destruction</a> is the essential fact about <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-capsocdem_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-capsocdem-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneurs</a> continuously look for better ways to satisfy their <a href="/wiki/Consumer_demand" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumer demand">consumer base</a> with improved quality, durability, service, and price which come to fruition in innovation with advanced technologies and organizational strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>One prime example was the explosive boom of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> startups out of the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Industrial_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford Industrial Park">Stanford Industrial Park</a>. In 1957, dissatisfied employees of <a href="/wiki/Shockley_Semiconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="Shockley Semiconductor">Shockley Semiconductor</a>, the company of <a href="/wiki/Nobel_laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel laureate">Nobel laureate</a> and co-inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Shockley" title="William Shockley">William Shockley</a>, left to form an independent firm, <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor" title="Fairchild Semiconductor">Fairchild Semiconductor</a>. After several years, Fairchild developed into a formidable presence in the sector. Eventually, these founders left to start their own companies based on their own, unique, latest ideas, and then leading employees started their own firms. Over the next 20 years, this snowball process launched the momentous <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startup company</a> explosion of <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> firms. Essentially, Silicon Valley began as 65 new enterprises born out of Shockley’s eight former employees.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Since then, hubs of innovation have sprung up globally with similar <a href="/wiki/Metonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Metonym">metonyms</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Alley" title="Silicon Alley">Silicon Alley</a> encompassing <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>
<p>Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Business_incubator" title="Business incubator">Business Incubators</a> - a phenomenon that is nurtured by governments around the world, close to knowledge clusters, mostly research based, like universities or other Government Excellence Centres, while the main goal is to channel the generated knowledge to applied innovation outcomes in order to stimulate regional or national <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the organizational context, innovation may be linked to positive changes in <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Competitiveness" title="Competitiveness">competitiveness</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a>. However, recent research findings highlight the complementary role of organizational culture in enabling organizations to translate innovative activity into tangible performance improvements.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Organizations can also improve profits and performance by providing work groups opportunities and resources to innovate, in addition to employee's core job tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Drucker" title="Peter Drucker">Peter Drucker</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Innovation is the specific function of entrepreneurship, whether in an existing business, a public service institution, or a new venture started by a lone individual in the family kitchen. It is the means by which the entrepreneur either creates new wealth-producing resources or endows existing resources with enhanced potential for creating wealth. –Drucker<sup id="cite_ref-Drucker_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drucker-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Clayton Christensen, <a href="/wiki/Disruptive_innovation" title="Disruptive innovation">disruptive innovation</a> is the key to future success in business.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The organisation requires a proper structure in order to retain competitive advantage. It is necessary to create and nurture an environment of innovation. Executives and managers need to break away from traditional ways of thinking and use change to their advantage. It is a time of risk but even greater opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The world of work is changing with the increase in the use of technology and both companies and businesses are becoming increasingly competitive. Companies will have to downsize and re-engineer their operations to remain competitive. This will affect employment as businesses will be forced to reduce the number of people employed while accomplishing the same amount of work if not more.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>While disruptive innovation will typically "attack a traditional business model with a lower-cost solution and overtake incumbent firms quickly,"<sup id="cite_ref-hbr201701_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbr201701-19">[19]</a></sup> foundational innovation is slower, and typically has the potential to create new foundations for global technology systems over the longer term. Foundational innovation tends to transform business <a href="/wiki/Operating_model" title="Operating model">operating models</a> as entirely new business models <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emerge</a> over many years, with gradual and steady adoption of the innovation leading to waves of <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutional</a> change that gain momentum more slowly.<sup id="cite_ref-hbr201701_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbr201701-19">[19]</a></sup> The advent of the <a href="/wiki/Packet-switched" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet-switched">packet-switched</a> communication protocol <a href="/wiki/TCP/IP" class="mw-redirect" title="TCP/IP">TCP/IP</a>—originally introduced in 1972 to support a single <a href="/wiki/Use_case" title="Use case">use case</a> for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> electronic communication (email), and which gainded widespread adoption only in the mid-1990s with the advent of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>—is a foundational technology.<sup id="cite_ref-hbr201701_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbr201701-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>All organizations can innovate, including for example hospitals, universities, and local governments.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For instance, former Mayor <a href="/wiki/Martin_O%E2%80%99Malley" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin O’Malley">Martin O’Malley</a> pushed the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Baltimore" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Baltimore">City of Baltimore</a> to use <a href="/wiki/CitiStat" class="mw-redirect" title="CitiStat">CitiStat</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Performance_measurement" title="Performance measurement">performance-measurement</a> data and management system that allows city officials to maintain statistics on crime trends to condition of <a href="/wiki/Pothole" title="Pothole">pothole</a>s. This system aids in better evaluation of policies and procedures with accountability and efficiency in terms of time and money. In its first year, CitiStat saved the city $13.2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Even <a href="/wiki/Mass_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass transit">mass transit</a> systems have innovated with <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">hybrid</a> bus fleets to <a href="/wiki/Real-time_locating_system" title="Real-time locating system">real-time tracking</a> at bus stands. In addition, the growing use of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_data_terminal" title="Mobile data terminal">mobile data terminals</a> in vehicles, that serve as communication hubs between vehicles and a control center, automatically send data on location, passenger counts, engine performance, mileage and other information. This tool helps to deliver and manage transportation systems.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Still other innovative strategies include <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a> digitizing medical information in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_medical_records" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic medical records">electronic medical records</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development" title="United States Department of Housing and Urban Development">U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development</a>'s <a href="/wiki/HOPE_VI" title="HOPE VI">HOPE VI</a> initiatives turned severely distressed <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">public housing</a> in urban areas into <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">revitalized</a>, mixed-income environments; the <a href="/wiki/Harlem_Children%E2%80%99s_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Harlem Children’s Zone">Harlem Children’s Zone</a> used a community-based approach to educate local area children; and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_regulation_and_development" title="Brownfield regulation and development">brownfield grants</a> facilitates turning over <a href="/wiki/Brownfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Brownfield">brownfields</a> for <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Open_space_reserve" title="Open space reserve">green spaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_development" title="Community development">community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial development</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several sources of innovation. It can occur as a result of a focus effort by a range of different agents, by chance, or as a result of a major system failure.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Peter_F._Drucker" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter F. Drucker">Peter F. Drucker</a>, the general sources of innovations are different changes in industry structure, in market structure, in local and global demographics, in human perception, mood and meaning, in the amount of already available scientific knowledge, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-Drucker_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drucker-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Technological_Change.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Technological_Change.jpg/300px-Technological_Change.jpg" width="300" height="52" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Technological_Change.jpg/450px-Technological_Change.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Technological_Change.jpg 2x" data-file-width="570" data-file-height="98" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Technological_Change.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Original model of three phases of the process of Technological Change</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the simplest <a href="/wiki/Linear_model_of_innovation" title="Linear model of innovation">linear model of innovation</a> the traditionally recognized source is <i>manufacturer innovation</i>. This is where an agent (person or business) innovates in order to sell the innovation. Specifically, R&amp;D measurement is the commonly used input for innovation, in particular in the business sector, named Business Expenditure on R&amp;D (BERD) that grew over the years on the expenses of the declining R&amp;D invested by the public sector.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another source of innovation, only now becoming widely recognized, is <i>end-user innovation</i>. This is where an agent (person or company) develops an innovation for their own (personal or in-house) use because existing products do not meet their needs. <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Eric_von_Hippel" title="Eric von Hippel">Eric von Hippel</a> has identified end-user innovation as, by far, the most important and critical in his classic book on the subject, <i>The Sources of Innovation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The robotics engineer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_F._Engelberger" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph F. Engelberger">Joseph F. Engelberger</a> asserts that innovations require only three things:</p>
<ol>
<li>A recognized need,</li>
<li>Competent people with relevant technology, and</li>
<li>Financial support.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>However, innovation processes usually involve: identifying customer needs, macro and meso trends, developing competences, and finding financial support.</p>
<p>The Kline <a href="/wiki/Chain-linked_model" title="Chain-linked model">chain-linked model</a> of innovation<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> places emphasis on potential market needs as drivers of the innovation process, and describes the complex and often iterative feedback loops between marketing, design, manufacturing, and R&amp;D.</p>
<p>Innovation by businesses is achieved in many ways, with much attention now given to formal <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> (R&amp;D) for "breakthrough innovations". R&amp;D help spur on patents and other scientific innovations that leads to productive growth in such areas as industry, medicine, engineering, and government.<sup id="cite_ref-MetroPolicy_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetroPolicy-27">[27]</a></sup> Yet, innovations can be developed by less formal on-the-job modifications of practice, through exchange and combination of professional experience and by many other routes. Investigation of relationship between the concepts of innovation and technology transfer revealed overlap.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The more radical and revolutionary innovations tend to emerge from R&amp;D, while more incremental innovations may emerge from practice – but there are many exceptions to each of these trends.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">Information technology</a> and changing business processes and management style can produce a work climate favorable to innovation.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> For example, the software tool company <a href="/wiki/Atlassian" title="Atlassian">Atlassian</a> conducts quarterly "ShipIt Days" in which employees may work on anything related to the company's products.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Google employees work on self-directed projects for 20% of their time (known as <a href="/wiki/Innovation_Time_Off" class="mw-redirect" title="Innovation Time Off">Innovation Time Off</a>). Both companies cite these bottom-up processes as major sources for new products and features.</p>
<p>An important innovation factor includes customers buying products or using services. As a result, firms may incorporate users in <a href="/wiki/Focus_group" title="Focus group">focus groups</a> (user centred approach), work closely with so called <a href="/wiki/Lead_users" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead users">lead users</a> (lead user approach) or users might adapt their products themselves. The lead user method focuses on idea generation based on leading users to develop breakthrough innovations. U-STIR, a project to innovate <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>’s surface <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a> system, employs such workshops.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Regarding this <a href="/wiki/User_innovation" title="User innovation">user innovation</a>, a great deal of innovation is done by those actually implementing and using technologies and products as part of their normal activities. In most of the times user innovators have some personal record motivating them. Sometimes user-innovators may become <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneurs</a>, selling their product, they may choose to trade their innovation in exchange for other innovations, or they may be adopted by their suppliers. Nowadays, they may also choose to freely reveal their innovations, using methods like <a href="/wiki/Open_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source">open source</a>. In such networks of innovation the users or communities of users can further develop technologies and reinvent their social meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_and_failures">Goals and failures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Goals and failures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Programs of organizational innovation are typically tightly linked to organizational goals and objectives, to the <a href="/wiki/Business_plan" title="Business plan">business plan</a>, and to <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> <a href="/wiki/Competitiveness" title="Competitiveness">competitive positioning</a>. One driver for innovation programs in corporations is to achieve growth objectives. As Davila et al. (2006) notes, "Companies cannot grow through cost reduction and reengineering alone... Innovation is the key element in providing aggressive top-line growth, and for increasing bottom-line results".<sup id="cite_ref-Making_Innovation_Work_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Making_Innovation_Work-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>One survey across a large number of manufacturing and services organizations found, ranked in decreasing order of popularity, that systematic programs of organizational innovation are most frequently driven by: Improved <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a>, Creation of new <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">markets</a>, Extension of the <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">product</a> range, Reduced <a href="/wiki/Labor_cost" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor cost">labor costs</a>, Improved <a href="/wiki/Production_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Production process">production processes</a>, Reduced materials, Reduced <a href="/wiki/Environmental_damage" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental damage">environmental damage</a>, Replacement of <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a>/<a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>, Reduced <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> consumption, Conformance to <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Making_Innovation_Work_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Making_Innovation_Work-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>These goals vary between improvements to products, processes and services and dispel a popular myth that innovation deals mainly with new product development. Most of the goals could apply to any organisation be it a manufacturing facility, marketing firm, hospital or local government. Whether innovation goals are successfully achieved or otherwise depends greatly on the environment prevailing in the firm.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conversely, failure can develop in programs of innovations. The causes of failure have been widely researched and can vary considerably. Some causes will be external to the organization and outside its influence of control. Others will be internal and ultimately within the control of the organization. Internal causes of failure can be divided into causes associated with the cultural infrastructure and causes associated with the innovation process itself. Common causes of failure within the innovation process in most organizations can be distilled into five types: Poor goal definition, Poor alignment of actions to goals, Poor participation in teams, Poor monitoring of results, Poor communication and access to information.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diffusion">Diffusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diffusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:InnovationLifeCycle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="InnovationLifeCycle.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/InnovationLifeCycle.jpg/220px-InnovationLifeCycle.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/InnovationLifeCycle.jpg/330px-InnovationLifeCycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/InnovationLifeCycle.jpg/440px-InnovationLifeCycle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="448" data-file-height="287" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:InnovationLifeCycle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_of_innovations" title="Diffusion of innovations">Diffusion of innovations</a></div>
<p>Diffusion of innovation research was first started in 1903 by seminal researcher <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Tarde" title="Gabriel Tarde">Gabriel Tarde</a>, who first plotted the S-shaped <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_curve" title="Diffusion curve">diffusion curve</a>. Tarde defined the innovation-decision process as a series of steps that includes:<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>First knowledge</li>
<li>Forming an attitude</li>
<li>A decision to adopt or reject</li>
<li>Implementation and use</li>
<li>Confirmation of the decision</li>
</ol>
<p>Once innovation occurs, innovations may be spread from the innovator to other individuals and groups. This process has been proposed that the life cycle of innovations can be described using the '<a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_function" title="Sigmoid function">s-curve</a>' or <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_of_innovations" title="Diffusion of innovations">diffusion curve</a>. The s-curve maps growth of revenue or productivity against time. In the early stage of a particular innovation, growth is relatively slow as the new product establishes itself. At some point customers begin to demand and the product growth increases more rapidly. New incremental innovations or changes to the product allow growth to continue. Towards the end of its lifecycle, growth slows and may even begin to decline. In the later stages, no amount of new investment in that product will yield a normal rate of return</p>
<p>The s-curve derives from an assumption that new products are likely to have "product life" – i.e., a start-up phase, a rapid increase in revenue and eventual decline. In fact the great majority of innovations never get off the bottom of the curve, and never produce normal returns.</p>
<p>Innovative companies will typically be working on new innovations that will eventually replace older ones. Successive s-curves will come along to replace older ones and continue to drive growth upwards. In the figure above the first curve shows a current technology. The second shows an <a href="/wiki/Emerging_technologies" title="Emerging technologies">emerging technology</a> that currently yields lower growth but will eventually overtake current technology and lead to even greater levels of growth. The length of life will depend on many factors.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures">Measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edison et al.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry2013_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry2013-8">[8]</a></sup> in their review of literature on <a href="/wiki/Innovation_management" title="Innovation management">innovation management</a> found 232 innovation metrics. They categorized these measures along five dimensions i.e. inputs to the innovation process, output from the innovation process, effect of the innovation output, measures to access the activities in an innovation process and availability of factors that facilitate such a process.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry2013_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry2013-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are two different types of measures for innovation: the organizational level and the political level.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizational_level">Organizational level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organizational level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The measure of innovation at the organizational level relates to individuals, team-level assessments, and private companies from the smallest to the largest company. Measure of innovation for organizations can be conducted by surveys, workshops, consultants, or internal benchmarking. There is today no established general way to measure organizational innovation. Corporate measurements are generally structured around <a href="/wiki/Balanced_scorecard" title="Balanced scorecard">balanced scorecards</a> which cover several aspects of innovation such as business measures related to finances, innovation process efficiency, employees' contribution and motivation, as well benefits for customers. Measured values will vary widely between businesses, covering for example new product revenue, spending in R&amp;D, time to market, customer and employee perception &amp; satisfaction, number of patents, additional sales resulting from past innovations.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_level">Political level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Political level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the political level, measures of innovation are more focused on a country or region <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a> through innovation. In this context, organizational capabilities can be evaluated through various evaluation frameworks, such as those of the European Foundation for Quality Management. The <a href="/wiki/OECD" class="mw-redirect" title="OECD">OECD</a> Oslo Manual (1995) suggests standard guidelines on measuring technological product and process innovation. Some people consider the <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Manual" title="Oslo Manual">Oslo Manual</a> complementary to the <a href="/wiki/Frascati_Manual" title="Frascati Manual">Frascati Manual</a> from 1963. The new Oslo manual from 2005 takes a wider perspective to innovation, and includes marketing and organizational innovation. These standards are used for example in the European <a href="/wiki/Community_Innovation_Survey" title="Community Innovation Survey">Community Innovation Surveys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other ways of measuring innovation have traditionally been expenditure, for example, investment in R&amp;D (Research and Development) as percentage of GNP (Gross National Product). Whether this is a good measurement of innovation has been widely discussed and the Oslo Manual has incorporated some of the critique against earlier methods of measuring. The traditional methods of measuring still inform many policy decisions. The EU <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Strategy" title="Lisbon Strategy">Lisbon Strategy</a> has set as a goal that their average expenditure on R&amp;D should be 3% of GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indicators">Indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many scholars claim that there is a great bias towards the "science and technology mode" (S&amp;T-mode or STI-mode), while the "learning by doing, using and interacting mode" (DUI-mode) is ignored and measurements and research about it rarely done. For example, an institution may be high tech with the latest equipment, but lacks crucial doing, using and interacting tasks important for innovation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A common industry view (unsupported by empirical evidence) is that comparative <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness" class="mw-redirect" title="Cost-effectiveness">cost-effectiveness</a> research is a form of <a href="/wiki/Price_controls" title="Price controls">price control</a> which reduces returns to industry, and thus limits R&amp;D expenditure, stifles future innovation and compromises new products access to markets.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Some academics claim cost-effectiveness research is a valuable value-based measure of innovation which accords "truly significant" therapeutic advances (ie providing "health gain") higher prices than free market mechanisms.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Value-based_pricing" title="Value-based pricing">value-based pricing</a> has been viewed as a means of indicating to industry the type of innovation that should be rewarded from the public purse.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Research_and_Development_Expenditure_Credit" title="Research and Development Expenditure Credit">Research and Development Expenditure Credit</a></p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian">Australian</a> academic developed the case that national comparative <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">cost-effectiveness analysis</a> systems should be viewed as measuring "health innovation" as an <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_policy" title="Evidence-based policy">evidence-based policy</a> concept for valuing innovation distinct from valuing through competitive markets, a method which requires strong <a href="/wiki/Anti-trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-trust">anti-trust</a> laws to be effective, on the basis that both methods of assessing <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_innovations" title="Pharmaceutical innovations">pharmaceutical innovations</a> are mentioned in annex 2C.1 of the <a href="/wiki/AUSFTA" class="mw-redirect" title="AUSFTA">Australia-United States Free Trade Agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Need the year this event took place (March 2017)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate">Rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indices">Indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several indices attempt to measure innovation and rank entities based on these measures, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Innovation_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloomberg Innovation Index">Bloomberg Innovation Index</a></li>

(February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The "Creative Class" developed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Florida" title="Richard Florida">Richard Florida</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li>The Innovation Index, developed by the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Business_Research_Center" title="Indiana Business Research Center">Indiana Business Research Center</a>, to measure innovation capacity at the county or regional level in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>

<li>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovationsindikator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Innovationsindikator (page does not exist)">innovationsindikator</a> for Germany, developed by the <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundesverband_der_Deutschen_Industrie" class="extiw" title="de:Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie">Federation of German Industries</a> (Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie) in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/INSEAD" title="INSEAD">INSEAD</a> Innovation Efficacy Index<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/International_Innovation_Index" title="International Innovation Index">International Innovation Index</a>, produced jointly by <a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Consulting_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="The Boston Consulting Group">The Boston Consulting Group</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Manufacturers" title="National Association of Manufacturers">National Association of Manufacturers</a> and its nonpartisan research affiliate The Manufacturing Institute, is a worldwide index measuring the level of innovation in a country. NAM describes it as the "largest and most comprehensive global index of its kind".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The Management Innovation Index - Model for Managing Intangibility of Organizational Creativity: Management Innovation Index<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>The NYCEDC Innovation Index, by the New York City Economic Development Corporation, tracks New York City’s "transformation into a center for high-tech innovation. It measures innovation in the City’s growing science and technology industries and is designed to capture the effect of innovation on the City’s economy."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Manual" title="Oslo Manual">Oslo Manual</a> is focused on North America, Europe, and other rich economies.</li>
<li>The State Technology and Science Index, developed by the <a href="/wiki/Milken_Institute" title="Milken Institute">Milken Institute</a>, is a U.S.-wide benchmark to measure the science and technology capabilities that furnish high paying jobs based around key components.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many research studies try to rank countries based on measures of innovation. Common areas of focus include: <a href="/wiki/High-tech" class="mw-redirect" title="High-tech">high-tech</a> companies, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post_secondary_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Post secondary education">post secondary education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>, and research personnel. The left ranking of the top 10 countries below is based on the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Innovation_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloomberg Innovation Index">Bloomberg Innovation Index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> However, studies may vary widely; for example the <a href="/wiki/Global_Innovation_Index" title="Global Innovation Index">Global Innovation Index</a> 2016 ranks <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> as number one wherein countries like <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> do not even make the top ten.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Huebner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonathan Huebner (page does not exist)">Jonathan Huebner</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> working at the <a href="/wiki/Pentagon_Building" class="mw-redirect" title="Pentagon Building">Pentagon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Weapons_Station_China_Lake" title="Naval Air Weapons Station China Lake">Naval Air Warfare Center</a>, argued on the basis of both U.S. <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> and world technological breakthroughs, per capita, that the rate of human technological innovation peaked in 1873 and has been slowing ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-Huebner_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huebner-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In his article, he asked "Will the level of technology reach a maximum and then decline as in the Dark Ages?"<sup id="cite_ref-Huebner_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huebner-60">[60]</a></sup> In later comments to <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> magazine, Huebner clarified that while he believed that we will reach a rate of innovation in 2024 equivalent to that of the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>, he was not predicting the reoccurrence of the Dark Ages themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Smart" title="John Smart">John Smart</a> criticized the claim and asserted that <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">technological singularity</a> researcher <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kurzweil" title="Ray Kurzweil">Ray Kurzweil</a> and others showed a "clear trend of acceleration, not deceleration" when it came to innovations.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The foundation replied to Huebner the journal his article was published in, citing <a href="/wiki/Second_Life" title="Second Life">Second Life</a> and <a href="/wiki/EHarmony" title="EHarmony">eHarmony</a> as proof of accelerating innovation; to which Huebner replied.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> However, Huebner's findings were confirmed in 2010 with <a href="/wiki/U.S._Patent_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Patent Office">U.S. Patent Office</a> data.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and in a 2012 paper.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovation_and_international_development">Innovation and international development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Innovation and international development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The theme of innovation as a tool to disrupting patterns of poverty has gained momentum since the mid-2000s among major <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a> actors such as <a href="/wiki/DFID" class="mw-redirect" title="DFID">DFID</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gates_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Gates Foundation">Gates Foundation</a>'s use of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Challenge" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Challenge">Grand Challenge</a> funding model,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/USAID" class="mw-redirect" title="USAID">USAID</a>'s Global Development Lab.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Networks have been established to support innovation in development, such as D-Lab at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Investment funds have been established to identify and catalyze innovations in <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>, such as DFID's Global Innovation Fund,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Innovation_Fund" title="Human Development Innovation Fund">Human Development Innovation Fund</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and (in partnership with USAID) the Global Development Innovation Ventures.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_policies">Government policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Government policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Given the noticeable effects on <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productive growth</a>, innovation is a key factor in society and economy. Consequently, policymakers have long worked to develop environments that will foster innovation and its resulting positive benefits, from funding <a href="/wiki/Research_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Research and Development">Research and Development</a> to supporting regulatory change, funding the development of innovation clusters, and using public purchasing and standardisation to 'pull' innovation through.</p>
<p>For instance, experts are advocating that the U.S. federal government launch a National Infrastructure Foundation, a nimble, collaborative strategic intervention organization that will house innovations programs from fragmented silos under one entity, inform federal officials on innovation <a href="/wiki/Performance_measurement" title="Performance measurement">performance metrics</a>, strengthen industry-university partnerships, and support innovation <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> initiatives, especially to strengthen <a href="/wiki/Business_cluster" title="Business cluster">regional clusters</a>. Because clusters are the geographic incubators of innovative products and processes, a cluster development grant program would also be targeted for implementation. By focusing on innovating in such areas as precision <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean energy">clean energy</a>, other areas of national concern would be tackled including <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">government debt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oil_dependence" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil dependence">oil dependence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MetroPolicy_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetroPolicy-27">[27]</a></sup> The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Economic_Development_Administration" title="Economic Development Administration">Economic Development Administration</a> understand this reality in their continued Regional Innovation Clusters initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In addition, federal grants in R&amp;D, a crucial driver of innovation and productive growth, should be expanded to levels similar to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> in order to stay globally competitive. Also, such grants should be better procured to <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_economy" title="Metropolitan economy">metropolitan areas</a>, the essential engines of the American economy.<sup id="cite_ref-MetroPolicy_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetroPolicy-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many countries recognize the importance of research and development as well as innovation including Japan's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education,_Culture,_Sports,_Science_and_Technology" title="Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology">Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology</a> (MEXT);<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Germany’s <a href="/wiki/Federal_Ministry_of_Education_and_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Ministry of Education and Research">Federal Ministry of Education and Research</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science_and_Technology_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Science and Technology of the People's Republic of China">Ministry of Science and Technology</a> in the People's Republic of China. Furthermore, Russia's innovation programme is the <a href="/wiki/Medvedev_modernisation_programme" title="Medvedev modernisation programme">Medvedev modernisation programme</a> which aims at creating a diversified economy based on high technology and innovation. Also, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Western_Australia" title="Government of Western Australia">Government of Western Australia</a> has established a number of innovation incentives for government departments. <a href="/wiki/Landgate" title="Landgate">Landgate</a> was the first Western Australian government agency to establish its Innovation Program.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cairns_Region" title="Cairns Region">Cairns Region</a> established the Tropical Innovation Awards in 2010 open to all businesses in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The 2011 Awards were extended to include participants from all Tropical Zone Countries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovators">Innovators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innovation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Innovators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An innovator, in a general sense, is a person or an organization, who is one of the first to introduce into reality, something better than before. Something that opens up a new area for others, and achieves an innovation.</p>
