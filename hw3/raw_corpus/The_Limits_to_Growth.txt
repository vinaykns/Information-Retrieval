<p><i><b>The Limits to Growth</b></i> is a 1972 book about the <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulation</a> of exponential <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> with finite resource supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Funded by the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Foundation" title="Volkswagen Foundation">Volkswagen Foundation</a><sup id="cite_ref-Jens_Rehl.C3.A4nder.2C_Barbara_Riegler.2C_german_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jens_Rehl.C3.A4nder.2C_Barbara_Riegler.2C_german-2">[2]</a></sup> and commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a>, it was first presented at the <a href="/wiki/St._Gallen_Symposium" title="St. Gallen Symposium">St. Gallen Symposium</a>. Its authors were <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella H. Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Meadows" title="Dennis Meadows">Dennis L. Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rgen_Randers" title="Jørgen Randers">Jørgen Randers</a>, and William W. Behrens III. The book used the <a href="/wiki/World3" title="World3">World3</a> model to simulate<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the consequence of interactions between the Earth's and human systems.</p>
<p>The original version presented a model based on five variables: <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a>, industrialisation, pollution, food production and <a href="/wiki/Resources_depletion" class="mw-redirect" title="Resources depletion">resources depletion</a>. These variables are considered to grow <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponentially</a>, while the ability of technology to increase resources availability is only <a href="/wiki/Linear_function" title="Linear function">linear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The authors intended to explore the possibility of a sustainable feedback pattern that would be achieved by altering growth trends among the five variables under three scenarios. They noted that their projections for the values of the variables in each scenario were predictions "only in the most limited sense of the word," and were only indications of the system's behavioral tendencies.<sup id="cite_ref-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93-6">[6]</a></sup> Two of the scenarios saw "overshoot and collapse" of the global system by the mid to latter part of the 21st century, while a third scenario resulted in a "stabilized world."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book continues to generate fervent debate and has been the subject of several subsequent publications. The most recent updated version was published on June 1, 2004 by Chelsea Green Publishing Company and <a href="/wiki/Earthscan" title="Earthscan">Earthscan</a> under the name <i>Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update</i>. Donella H. Meadows, Jørgen Randers, and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Meadows" title="Dennis Meadows">Dennis Meadows</a> have updated and expanded the original version.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The purpose of <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was not to make specific predictions, but to explore how exponential growth interacts with finite resources. Because the size of resources is not known, only the general behavior can be explored.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exponential_reserve_index">Exponential reserve index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exponential reserve index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>One key idea within <i>The Limits to Growth</i> is the notion that if the rate of resource use is increasing, the amount of reserves cannot be calculated by simply taking the current known reserves and dividing by the current yearly usage, as is typically done to obtain a static index. For example, in 1972, the amount of chromium reserves was 775 million metric tons, of which 1.85 million metric tons were mined annually (see <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a>). The static index is 775/1.85=418 years, but the rate of chromium consumption was growing at 2.6% annually (<i>Limits to Growth</i>, pp.&#160;54–71). If instead of assuming a constant rate of usage, the assumption of a constant rate of growth of 2.6% annually is made, the resource will instead last</p>

<p>The static reserve numbers assume that the usage is constant, and the exponential reserve assumes that the growth rate is constant.</p>
<p>The exponential index has been interpreted as a prediction of the number of years until the world would "run out" of various resources, both by environmentalist groups calling for greater conservation and restrictions on use, and by skeptics criticizing the index when supplies failed to run out.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> What <i>The Limits to Growth</i> actually has is the above table, which has the <i>current reserves</i> (that is no new sources of oil are found) for oil running out in 1992 assuming constant exponential growth. In <i>Limits to Growth: The Thirty Year Update</i> there are several pages explaining that new resources are found over time and that the <i>current reserves</i> therefore change but that ultimately resources are finite. (Earlier editions did explain this as well, but not in as much detail.) The standard model includes a resource base of double that of what they have calculated, but the book includes model runs where the assumed resources are infinite, but those model runs still result in overshoot and collapse from other factors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_reviews">Negative reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Negative reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The report has been criticized by academics, economists and businessmen.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Critics claimed that history proved the projections to be incorrect, which was specifically based on the popular belief that <i>The Limits to Growth</i> predicted resource depletion and associated economic collapse by the end of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>The Limits to Growth</i> faced ridicule as early as the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Attacks were made on the methodology, the computer, the conclusions, the rhetoric and the people behind the project.<sup id="cite_ref-alan201013_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alan201013-22">[22]</a></sup> Yale economist <a href="/wiki/Henry_C._Wallich" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry C. Wallich">Henry C. Wallich</a> agreed that growth could not continue indefinitely, but that a natural end to growth was preferable to intervention. Wallich stated that technology could solve all the problems the report was concerned about, but only if growth continued apace. By stopping growth too soon, Wallich warned, the world would be "consigning billions to permanent poverty".<sup id="cite_ref-alan201013_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alan201013-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a> from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> argued that prediction in <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was based on a weak foundation of data (<i>Newsweek</i>, March 13, 1972, p.&#160;103). Allen Kneese and Ronald Riker of <a href="/wiki/Resources_for_the_Future" title="Resources for the Future">Resources for the Future</a> (RFF) stated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The authors load their case by letting some things grow exponentially and others not. Population, capital and pollution grow exponentially in all models, but technologies for expanding resources and controlling pollution are permitted to grow, if at all, only in discrete increments.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Critics also argue that the authors of the report claimed to accept that the then-known resources of minerals and energy could, and would, grow in the future, and consumption growth rates could also decline. The theoretical expiry time for each resource would therefore need to be updated as new discoveries, technologies and trends came to light.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_reviews">Positive reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Positive reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:25%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #f8f8f8;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>With few exceptions, economics as a discipline has been dominated by a perception of living in an unlimited world, where resource and pollution problems in one area were solved by moving resources or people to other parts. The very hint of any global limitation as suggested in the report <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was met with disbelief and rejection by businesses and most economists. However, this conclusion was mostly based on false premises.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">– <a href="#CITEREFMeyerN.C3.B8rg.C3.A5rd2010">Meyer &amp; Nørgård (2010)</a>.</cite></div>
<p>In 2008, Graham Turner from the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) published "A comparison of <i>The Limits to Growth</i> with thirty years of reality".<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_2008_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2008-24">[24]</a></sup> Turner found that the observed historical data from 1970 to 2000 closely matches the simulated results of the "standard run" limits of growth model for almost all the outputs reported. "The comparison is well within uncertainty bounds of nearly all the data in terms of both magnitude and the trends over time."<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_2008_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2008-24">[24]</a></sup> Turner also examined a number of reports, particularly by economists, which over the years have purported to discredit the limits-to-growth model. Turner says these reports are flawed, and reflect misunderstandings about the model.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_2008_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2008-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2010, Nørgård, Peet and Ragnarsdóttir called the book a "pioneering report", and said that it "has withstood the test of time and, indeed, has only become more relevant."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, <i>The Guardian</i> published an article about a new Graham Turner review – 40 years later of "The Limits to Growth" according to which the actual data and the 1972 forecasts still match.<sup id="cite_ref-turner2014_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner2014-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Club of Rome has persisted after <i>The Limits of Growth</i> and has generally provided comprehensive updates to the book on a 5-year basis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977:_5_years">1977: 5 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1977: 5 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An independent retrospective on the public debate over <i>The Limits to Growth</i> concluded in 1978 that optimistic attitudes had won out, causing a general loss of momentum in the environmentalist movement. While summarizing a large number of opposing arguments, the article concluded that "scientific arguments for and against each position ... have, it would seem, played only a small part in the general acceptance of alternative perspectives."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1987:_15_years">1987: 15 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1987: 15 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, a symposium was held in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, entitled "Beyond the Limits to Growth: Global Industrial Society, Vision or Nightmare?"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1992:_20_years">1992: 20 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1992: 20 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Limits" title="Beyond the Limits">Beyond the Limits</a></i> was published as a 20-year update on the original material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002:_30_years">2002: 30 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2002: 30 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update</i> was published in 2004.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007:_35_years">2007: 35 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2007: 35 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008 Graham Turner at the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) in Australia published a paper called "A Comparison of `The Limits to Growth` with Thirty Years of Reality".<sup id="cite_ref-CSIRO_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-29">[29]</a></sup> It examined the past thirty years of reality with the predictions made in 1972 and found that changes in industrial production, food production and pollution are all in line with one of the book's three scenarios so far – that of 'business as usual'. This set of predictions in LtG goes on to forecast <a href="/wiki/Economic_collapse" title="Economic collapse">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">societal collapse</a> in the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2010, Peet, Nørgård, and Ragnarsdóttir called the book a "pioneering report". They said that, "its approach remains useful and that its conclusions are still surprisingly valid... unfortunately the report has been largely dismissed by critics as a doomsday prophecy that has not held up to scrutiny."<sup id="cite_ref-Solutions_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Solutions-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2009 article published in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Scientist" title="American Scientist">American Scientist</a></i> titled "Revisiting the Limits to Growth After Peak Oil," Hall and Day noted that "the values predicted by the limits-to-growth model and actual data for 2008 are very close."<sup id="cite_ref-American_Scientist_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Scientist-32">[32]</a></sup> These findings are consistent with the above-mentioned 2008 CSIRO study which concluded: "The analysis shows that 30 years of historical data compares favorably with key features… [of the Limits to Growth] ‘standard run’ scenario, which results in collapse of the global system midway through the 21st Century."<sup id="cite_ref-CSIRO_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 researcher Peter A. Victor wrote, that even though D.H. Meadows et al. probably underestimated price-mechanism's role in adjusting, their critics have overestimated it. He states that <i>Limits to Growth</i> has had a significant impact on the conception of environmental issues and notes that the models in the book were meant to be taken as predictions "only in the most limited sense of the word" as they wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012:_40_years">2012: 40 years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2012: 40 years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2011 <a href="/wiki/Ugo_Bardi" title="Ugo Bardi">Ugo Bardi</a> published a book-length academic study of <i>The Limits to Growth</i>, its methods and historical reception and concluded that "The warnings that we received in 1972 ... are becoming increasingly more worrisome as reality seems to be following closely the curves that the ... scenario had generated."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> A popular analysis of the accuracy of the report by science writer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Heinberg" title="Richard Heinberg">Richard Heinberg</a> was also published.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012 the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> held a symposium entitled "Perspectives on <i>Limits to Growth</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Another symposium was held in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> entitled "Already Beyond?"<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Limits to Growth</i> did not receive an official update in 2012, but one of its coauthors, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rgen_Randers" title="Jørgen Randers">Jørgen Randers</a>, published a book, <i><a href="/wiki/2052:_A_Global_Forecast_for_the_Next_Forty_Years" title="2052: A Global Forecast for the Next Forty Years">2052: A Global Forecast for the Next Forty Years</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2012 writing in <i>American Scientist</i>, Brian Hayes stated that the model is "more a polemical tool than a scientific instrument". He went on to say that the graphs generated by the computer program should not, as the authors note, be used as predictions.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014 Graham Turner concluded that "preparing for a collapsing global system could be even more important than trying to avoid collapse."<sup id="cite_ref-turner2014_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turner2014-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016 a report published by the UK <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://limits2growth.org.uk/">All-Party Parliamentary Group on Limits to Growth</a> concluded that "there is unsettling evidence that society is still following the ‘standard run’ of the original study – in which overshoot leads to an eventual collapse of production and living standards".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The report also highlights that some issues not well covered in the original 1972 report, such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, present additional challenges for human development.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_books">Related books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Related books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Precursors to <i>Limits to Growth</i> included <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus" title="Thomas Robert Malthus">Thomas Malthus's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_on_the_Principle_of_Population" title="An Essay on the Principle of Population">An Essay on the Principle of Population</a></i> (1798), <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Brown" title="Harrison Brown">Harrison Brown</a>'s <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Challenge_of_Man%27s_Future&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Challenge of Man's Future (page does not exist)">The Challenge of Man's Future</a></i> (1956), <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i> (1962) and <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i> (1968).<sup id="cite_ref-alan2010_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alan2010-40">[40]</a></sup> Some of the notable books published after 1972 include the <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_World_(book_series)" title="State of the World (book series)">State of the World</a></i> reports issued by the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a> (produced annually since 1984); the influential <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Common_Future" title="Our Common Future">Our Common Future</a></i>, published by the UN’s <a href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Environment_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Commission on Environment and Development">World Commission on Environment and Development</a> (1987); <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_in_the_Balance" title="Earth in the Balance">Earth in the Balance</a></i>, written by then-US senator <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> (1992); and <i>Earth Odyssey</i> (<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780767900591" class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn">ISBN 978-0767900591</a>) by journalist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hertsgaard" title="Mark Hertsgaard">Mark Hertsgaard</a> (1999).<sup id="cite_ref-alan2010_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alan2010-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
