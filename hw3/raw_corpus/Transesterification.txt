			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Transesterification</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, <b>transesterification</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/ 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the process of exchanging the organic group R″ of an <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> with the organic group R′ of an alcohol. These reactions are often <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyzed</a> by the addition of an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> catalyst. The reaction can also be accomplished with the help of enzymes (biocatalysts) particularly lipases (E.C.3.1.1.3).</p>

<center><b>Transesterification</b>: alcohol + ester → different alcohol + different ester</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Strong acids catalyse the reaction by donating a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group, thus making it a more potent <a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophile</a>, whereas bases catalyse the reaction by removing a proton from the alcohol, thus making it more <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophilic</a>. Esters with larger alkoxy groups can be made from methyl or ethyl esters in high purity by heating the mixture of ester, acid/base, and large alcohol and evaporating the small alcohol to drive equilibrium.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transesterification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the transesterification mechanism, the carbonyl carbon of the starting ester (RCOOR<sup>1</sup>) undergoes nucleophilic attack by the incoming alkoxide (R<sup>2</sup>O<sup>−</sup>) to give a tetrahedral intermediate, which either reverts to the starting material, or proceeds to the transesterified product (RCOOR<sup>2</sup>). The various species exist in equilibrium, and the product distribution depends on the relative energies of the reactant and product.</p>

</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transesterification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyester_production">Polyester production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transesterification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Polyester production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest scale application of transesterification is in the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyesters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup> In this application diesters undergo transesterification with diols to form macromolecules. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate" title="Dimethyl terephthalate">dimethyl terephthalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> react to form <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, which is evaporated to drive the reaction forward.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methanolysis_and_biodiesel_production">Methanolysis and biodiesel production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transesterification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methanolysis and biodiesel production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The reverse reaction, methanolysis, is also an example of transesterification. This process has been used to recycle polyesters into individual monomers (see <a href="/wiki/Plastic_recycling" title="Plastic recycling">plastic recycling</a>). It is also used to convert fats (<a href="/wiki/Triglycerides" class="mw-redirect" title="Triglycerides">triglycerides</a>) into <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. This conversion was one of the first uses. Transesterified <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>) was used to power heavy-duty vehicles in South Africa before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>It was <a href="/wiki/Patented" class="mw-redirect" title="Patented">patented</a> in the US in the 1950s by <a href="/wiki/Colgate-Palmolive" title="Colgate-Palmolive">Colgate</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Biolipid" class="mw-redirect" title="Biolipid">biolipid</a> transesterification may have been discovered much earlier. In the 1940s, researchers were looking for a method to more readily produce <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, which was used to produce <a href="/wiki/Explosives" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosives">explosives</a> for World War II. Many of the methods used today by producers and home brewers have their origin in the original 1940s research.</p>
<p>Biolipid transesterification has also been recently shown by Japanese researchers to be possible using a super-critical methanol methodology, whereby high temperature, high-pressure vessels are used to physically catalyze the biolipid/methanol reaction into fatty-acid methyl esters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-pressure_transesterification">High-pressure transesterification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transesterification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: High-pressure transesterification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Base-catalyzed transesterification is characterized by a negative activation volume (approx. −12&#160;cm<sup>3</sup>/mol) and therefore it proceeds faster under high-pressure conditions. It has been shown that amine-catalyzed alcoholysis of sterically hindered esters (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Protecting_group" title="Protecting group">protecting groups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiral_auxiliary" title="Chiral auxiliary">chiral auxiliaries</a>) proceeds rapidly at room temperature under 10&#160;kbar pressure, giving quantitative yields.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
