For software (in computing) whose source code is kept secret, see <a href="/wiki/Closed_source_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Closed source software">Closed source software</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>closed system</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Physical_system" title="Physical system">physical system</a> that does not allow certain types of transfers (such as <a href="/wiki/Mass_transfer" title="Mass transfer">transfer of mass</a>) in or out of the system. The specification of what types of transfers are excluded varies in the closed systems of physics, chemistry or engineering.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_physics">In physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Closed_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_classical_mechanics">In classical mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Closed_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In classical mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">nonrelativistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, a closed system is a <a href="/wiki/Physical_system" title="Physical system">physical system</a> that doesn't exchange any matter with its surroundings, and isn't subject to any <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> whose source is external to the system.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A closed system in classical mechanics would be considered an <a href="/wiki/Isolated_system" title="Isolated system">isolated system</a> in thermodynamics. Closed systems are often used to limit the factors that can affect the results of specific problem or experiment.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_thermodynamics">In thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Closed_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">Thermodynamic system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_Systems.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Diagram_Systems.png/220px-Diagram_Systems.png" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Diagram_Systems.png/330px-Diagram_Systems.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Diagram_Systems.png 2x" data-file-width="387" data-file-height="218" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diagram_Systems.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Properties of Isolated, closed, and open systems in exchanging energy and matter.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, a closed system can exchange energy (as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical work">work</a>) but not <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>, with its surroundings. An <a href="/wiki/Isolated_system" title="Isolated system">isolated system</a> cannot exchange any heat, work, or matter with the surroundings, while an <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system#Open_system" title="Thermodynamic system">open system</a> can exchange energy and matter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (This scheme of definition of terms is not uniformly used, though it is convenient for some purposes. In particular, some writers use 'closed system' where 'isolated system' is here used.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) For a simple system, with only one type of particle (atom or molecule), a closed system amounts to a constant number of particles. However, for systems which are undergoing a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical reaction</a>, there may be all sorts of molecules being generated and destroyed by the reaction process. In this case, the fact that the system is closed is expressed by stating that the total number of each elemental atom is conserved, no matter what kind of molecule it may be a part of. <p>In thermodynamics, a closed system is important for solving complicated thermodynamic problems. It allows the elimination of some external factors that could alter the results of the experiment or problem thus simplifying it. A closed system can also be used in situations where <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> is required to simplify the situation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_chemistry">In chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Closed_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In chemistry, a closed system is where no reactants or products can escape, only heat can be exchanged freely (ex. Ice cooler). A closed system can be used when conducting chemical experiments where temperature is not a factor (i.e. reaching <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">thermal equilibrium</a>)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_engineering">In engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Closed_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> context, a closed system is a bound system, i.e. defined, in which every input is known and every resultant is known (or can be known) within a specific time.</p>
