			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Resource depletion</h1>

<p><b>Resource depletion</b> is the consumption of a resource faster than it can be replenished. <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">Natural resources</a> are commonly divided between <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resources</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Mineral_resource_classification" title="Mineral resource classification">mineral resource classification</a>). Use of either of these forms of resources beyond their rate of replacement is considered to be resource depletion.</p>
<p>Resource depletion is most commonly used in reference to <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> usage, and consumption of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minerals">Minerals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Minerals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Peak_minerals" title="Peak minerals">Peak minerals</a></div>
<p>Minerals are needed to provide food, clothing, and housing. A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (USGS) study found a significant long-term trend over the 20th century for non-renewable resources such as minerals to supply a greater proportion of the raw material inputs to the non-fuel, non-food sector of the economy; an example is the greater consumption of crushed stone, sand, and gravel used in construction.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large-scale exploitation of minerals began in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> around 1760 in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and has grown rapidly ever since. Technological improvements have allowed humans to dig deeper and access lower grades and different types of ore over that time.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> Virtually all basic industrial metals (copper, iron, bauxite, etc.), as well as <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_mineral" title="Rare earth mineral">rare earth minerals</a>, face production output limitations from time to time,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> because supply involves large up-front investments and is therefore slow to respond to rapid increases in demand.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Minerals projected by some to enter production decline during the next 20 years:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gas (2023)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Copper (2024).<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-8">[8]</a></sup> Data from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) suggest that it is very unlikely that copper production will peak before 2040.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Zinc<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Developments in hydrometallurgy have transformed non-sulphide zinc deposits (largely ignored until now) into large low cost reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Minerals projected by some to enter production decline during the present century:</p>
<ul>
<li>Aluminium (2057)<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Coal (2060)<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Iron (2068)<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Such projections may change, as new discoveries are made<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-8">[8]</a></sup> and typically misinterpret available data on Mineral Resources and Mineral Reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil">Oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Oil_depletion" title="Oil depletion">Oil depletion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">Peak oil</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">Peak oil</a> is the period when the maximum rate of global <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a href="/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum" title="Extraction of petroleum">extraction</a> is reached, after which the rate of production enters terminal decline. It relates to a long-term decline in the available supply of petroleum. This, combined with increasing demand, will significantly increase the worldwide prices of petroleum derived products. Most significant will be the availability and price of liquid fuel for transportation.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hirsch_report" title="Hirsch report">Hirsch report</a> indicates that “The peaking of world oil production presents the U. S. and the world with an unprecedented risk management problem. As peaking is approached, liquid fuel prices and price volatility will increase dramatically, and, without timely mitigation, the economic, social, and political costs will be unprecedented. Viable mitigation options exist on both the supply and demand sides, but to have substantial impact, they must be initiated more than a decade in advance of peaking.”<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> is the clearing of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> by <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> or burning of trees and plants in a forested area. As a result of deforestation, presently about one half of the forests that once covered Earth have been destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It occurs for many different reasons, and it has several negative implications on the atmosphere and the quality of the land in and surrounding the forest.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_2">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the main causes of deforestation is clearing forests for <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> reasons. As the population of developing areas, especially near <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, increases, the need for land for farming becomes more and more important.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For most people, a forest has no value when its resources aren’t being used, so the incentives to deforest these areas outweigh the incentives to preserve the forests. For this reason, the economic value of the forests is very important for the <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because deforestation is so extensive, it has made several significant impacts on the environment, including <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the atmosphere, changing the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, an increase in <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, and a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_and_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Deforestation and global warming">Deforestation is often cited as a cause of global warming</a>. Because trees and plants remove carbon dioxide and emit oxygen into the atmosphere, the reduction of forests contribute to about 12% of <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> One of the most pressing issues that deforestation creates is soil erosion. The removal of trees causes higher rates of erosion, increasing risks of <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, which is a direct threat to many people living close to deforested areas. As forests get destroyed, so does the <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat</a> for millions of animals. It is estimated that 80% of the world’s known biodiversity lives in the rainforests, and the destruction of these rainforests is accelerating <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> at an alarming rate.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controlling_deforestation">Controlling deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource_depletion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controlling deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> created programs such as <a href="/wiki/Reducing_Emissions_from_Deforestation_and_Forest_Degradation" class="mw-redirect" title="Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation">Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation</a> (REDD), which works especially with developing countries to use subsidies or other incentives to encourage citizens to use the forest in a more sustainable way.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In addition to making sure that emissions from deforestation are kept to a minimum, an effort to educate people on sustainability and helping them to focus on the long-term risks is key to the success of these programs.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Declaration_on_Forests" title="New York Declaration on Forests">New York Declaration on Forests</a> and its associated actions promotes reforestation, which is being encouraged in many countries in an attempt to repair the damage that deforestation has done.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">Wetlands</a> are <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> that are often saturated by enough <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface</a> or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> to sustain <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> that is usually adapted to saturated soil conditions, such as <a href="/wiki/Cattails" class="mw-redirect" title="Cattails">cattails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulrush" title="Bulrush">bulrushes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_maple" class="mw-redirect" title="Red maple">red maples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_rice" title="Wild rice">wild rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blackberries" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackberries">blackberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cranberry" title="Cranberry">cranberries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peat_moss" class="mw-redirect" title="Peat moss">peat moss</a>. Because some varieties of wetlands are rich in minerals and nutrients and provide many of the advantages of both land and water environments they contain diverse species and provide a distinct basis for the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>.</p>
<p>Traditionally, wetlands were assumed to be useless so it was not a large concern when they were being dug up for settlements, agricultural use etc.. It is now believed that the wetland habitats contribute to a <a href="/wiki/Environmental_health" title="Environmental health">environmental health</a> and biodiversity.</p>
<p>In the USA, less than half of the wetlands that still existed in the 1600s have vanished.<sup id="cite_ref-ncsu_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncsu-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wetlands provide <a href="/wiki/Environmental_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental services">environmental services</a> for:</p>
<ol>
<li>Food and habitat</li>
<li>Improving <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Floodwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Floodwater">Floodwater</a> reduction</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shoreline" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoreline">Shoreline</a> stabilization</li>
<li>Recreation</li>
</ol>
<p>Some loss of wetlands resulted from natural causes such as erosion, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a> (the buildup of soil by the settling of fine particles over a long period of time), <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> (the sinking of land because of diminishing underground water supplies), and a rise in the sea level.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
