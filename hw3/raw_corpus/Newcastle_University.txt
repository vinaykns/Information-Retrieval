<p><b>Newcastle University</b> (Officially, the <b>University of Newcastle upon Tyne</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North-East</a> of England. The university can trace its origins to a School of Medicine and Surgery (later the College of Medicine), established in 1834, and to the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Fenwick_Boyd#College_of_Physical_Science" title="Edward Fenwick Boyd">College of Physical Science</a> (later renamed Armstrong College), founded in 1871. These two colleges came to form one division of the federal <a href="/wiki/University_of_Durham" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Durham">University of Durham</a>, with the Durham Colleges forming the other. The Newcastle colleges merged to form King's College in 1937. In 1963, following an Act of Parliament, King's College became the University of Newcastle upon Tyne.</p>
<p>Newcastle University is a <a href="/wiki/Red_brick_university" title="Red brick university">red brick university</a> and is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Russell_Group" title="Russell Group">Russell Group</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> an association of prestigious research-intensive UK universities. The university has one of the largest EU research portfolios in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Newcastle attracts over 20,000 students from more than 120 different countries.</p>
<p>Teaching and research are delivered in 24 academic schools and 40 research institutes and research centres, spread across three Faculties: the Faculty of Humanities and Social Sciences; the Faculty of Medical Sciences; and the Faculty of Science, Agriculture and Engineering. The university offers around 175 full-time undergraduate degree programmes in a wide range of subject areas spanning arts, sciences, engineering and medicine, together with approximately 340 postgraduate taught and research programmes across a range of disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The university has its origins in the School of Medicine and Surgery, which was established in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in October 1834, when it provided basic lectures and practical demonstrations to around 26 students. In June 1851, following a dispute among the teaching staff, the School split into two rival institutions. The majority formed the Newcastle College of Medicine, and the others established themselves as the Newcastle upon Tyne College of Medicine and Practical Science. By 1852, the majority college was formally linked to the <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">University of Durham</a>. It awarded its first 'Licence in Medicine' (Lic.Med) in 1856, and its teaching certificates were recognised by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> for graduation in medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The two colleges amalgamated in 1857 and renamed the <i>University of Durham College of Medicine</i> in 1870.</p>
<p>Attempts to realise a place for the teaching of sciences in the city were finally met with the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Fenwick_Boyd#College_of_Physical_Science" title="Edward Fenwick Boyd">College of Physical Science</a> in 1871. The college offered instruction in mathematics, physics, chemistry and geology to meet the growing needs of the mining industry, becoming the <i>Durham College of Physical Science</i> in 1883 and then renamed after <a href="/wiki/William_George_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" class="mw-redirect" title="William George Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William George Armstrong</a> as <a href="/wiki/Defunct_colleges_of_Durham_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Defunct colleges of Durham University">Armstrong College</a> in 1904. Both these separate and independent institutions later became part of the University of Durham, whose 1908 Act formally recognised that the university consisted of two Divisions, Durham and Newcastle, on two different sites. By 1908, the Newcastle Division was teaching a full range of subjects in the Faculties of Medicine, Arts, and Science, which also included agriculture and engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the early 20th century, the medical and science colleges vastly outpaced the growth of their Durham counterparts and a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" title="Royal Commission">Royal Commission</a> in 1934 recommended the merger of the two colleges to form King's College, Durham. Growth of the Newcastle Division of the federal Durham University led to tensions within the structure and on 1 August 1963 an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> separated the two, creating the University of Newcastle upon Tyne.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the successor of King's College, Durham, the university at its founding in 1963, adopted the coat of arms originally granted to the Council of King's College in 1937. In the Letters Patent authorising the transfer, the arms are blazoned <i>Azure, a Cross of St Cuthbert Argent and in chief of the last a lion passant guardant Gules</i> (On a blue shield, a silver square cross with flared ends, and on the top third of the shield, which is silver, a red lion walking and looking towards the viewer).</p>
<p>Above the portico of the Students' Union building are bas-relief carvings of the arms and mottoes of the University of Durham, Armstrong College and Durham University College of Medicine, the predecessor parts of Newcastle University. While a Latin motto - "mens agitat molem" (<i>mind moves matter</i>) appears in the Students' Union building, the university itself does not have an official motto.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_and_location">Campus and location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Campus and location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The university occupies a campus site close to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Haymarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle Haymarket">Haymarket</a> in central Newcastle upon Tyne. It is located to the northwest of the city centre between the open spaces of <a href="/wiki/Leazes_Park" title="Leazes Park">Leazes Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Town_Moor,_Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Town Moor, Newcastle upon Tyne">Town Moor</a>.</p>
<p>The Armstrong building is the oldest building on the campus and is the site of the original Armstrong College. The building was constructed in three stages;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the north east wing was completed first at a cost of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>18,000 and opened by <a href="/wiki/Princess_Louise,_Duchess_of_Argyll" title="Princess Louise, Duchess of Argyll">HRH Princess Louise</a> on 5 November 1888. The south-east wing, which includes the Jubilee Tower, and south-west wings were opened in 1894. The Jubilee Tower was built with surplus funds raised from an Exhibition to mark <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Jubilee">Jubilee</a> in 1887. The north-west front, forming the main entrance, was completed in 1906 and features two stone figures to represent science and the arts. Much of the later construction work was financed by Sir Isaac <a href="/wiki/Lowthian_Bell" title="Lowthian Bell">Lowthian Bell</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgist</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor" title="Lord Mayor">Lord Mayor</a> of Newcastle, after whom the main tower is named. In 1906 it was opened by <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VII">King Edward VII</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-royalopen_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalopen-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The building contains the <i>King's Hall</i>, which serves as the university's chief hall for ceremonial purposes where <a href="/wiki/Graduation" title="Graduation">Congregation</a> ceremonies are held. It can contain 500 seats.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> King Edward VII gave permission to call the Great Hall, King's Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-royalopen_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalopen-12">[12]</a></sup> Graduation photographs are often taken in the University Quadrangle, next to the Armstrong building. In 1949 the Quadrangle was turned into a formal garden in memory of members of Newcastle University who gave their lives in the two World Wars.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bruce Building is a former brewery, constructed between 1896 and 1900 on the site of the Hotspur Hotel, and designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Oswald" title="Joseph Oswald">Joseph Oswald</a><sup id="cite_ref-Pearson1999_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson1999-15">[15]</a></sup> as the new premises of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Breweries" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle Breweries">Newcastle Breweries</a> Limited.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As of 2014 the building had been empty for some time and the university had sold it on a long lease with the intention that it would be converted into student accommodation and commercial/office space. According to a spokesman for the university "It was not flexible enough for academic use. The proposed use is complementary to the university."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Devonshire Building, opened in 2004, incorporates in an energy efficient design. It uses <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic cell">photovoltaic cells</a> to help to power motorised shades that control the temperature of the building and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a> coils. Its architects won awards in the Hadrian awards and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Chartered_Surveyors" title="Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors">RICS</a> Building of the Year Award 2004. The university won a Green Gown award for its construction.<sup id="cite_ref-Devonshire_Building_-_The_Hadrian_Awards_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devonshire_Building_-_The_Hadrian_Awards-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Accolade_for_Devonshire_Building_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Accolade_for_Devonshire_Building-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Devonshire_Building_praised_by_HEFCE_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devonshire_Building_praised_by_HEFCE-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plans for additions and improvements to the campus were made public in March 2008 and completed in 2010 at a cost of £200&#160;million. They included a redevelopment of the south-east (<a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Haymarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle Haymarket">Haymarket</a>) façade with a five-storey King's Gate building and sculpture as well as new student accommodation. Two additional buildings for the school of medicine were also built.<sup id="cite_ref-2010plans_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010plans-22">[22]</a></sup> September 2012 saw the completion of the new buildings and facilities for <a href="/wiki/INTO_University_Partnerships" title="INTO University Partnerships">INTO Newcastle University</a> on the university campus. The main building provides 18 new teaching rooms, a Learning Resource Centre, a lecture theatre, science lab, administrative and academic offices and restaurant.</p>
<p>In addition to the city centre campus there are buildings such as the <a href="/wiki/Dove_Marine_Laboratory" title="Dove Marine Laboratory">Dove Marine Laboratory</a> located on <a href="/wiki/Cullercoats" title="Cullercoats">Cullercoats</a> Bay.</p>
<p>Newcastle's Library Service is the only one in the UK to be awarded the government's <a href="/wiki/Charter_Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter Mark">Charter Mark</a> for excellent customer services five times in a row, together with a Customer Services Excellence award.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It consists of three main facilities. The Robinson Library is the main university library. It is named after Philip Robinson, a bookseller in the city and benefactor to the library. The Walton Library specialises in services for the Faculty of Medical Sciences in the Medical School. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Baron_Walton_of_Detchant" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron Walton of Detchant">Lord Walton of Detchant</a>, former Dean of the Faculty of Medicine and Professor of Neurology. The library has a relationship with the Northern region of the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">NHS</a> allowing their staff to use the library for research and study. The Law Library specialises in resources relating to law. Together, these house over one million books and 500,000 electronic resources. Some schools within the university, such as the School of Modern Languages, also have their own smaller libraries with smaller highly specialised collections.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2008, the university's first overseas branch was opened in Singapore, a Marine International campus called, NUMI Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 2011, the Medical School opened a campus in Malaysia, Newcastle University Medicine Malaysia (NUMed).<sup id="cite_ref-numed_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-numed-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg/200px-Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg/300px-Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg/400px-Sir_Liam_Donaldson.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>

<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor</a> is Sir <a href="/wiki/Liam_Donaldson" title="Liam Donaldson">Liam Donaldson</a>. Donaldson was also the former <a href="/wiki/Chief_Medical_Officer_(United_Kingdom)" title="Chief Medical Officer (United Kingdom)">Chief Medical Officer</a> for England (1998 – May 2010). Donaldson assumed the position of Chancellor on 1 August 2009. The vice-chancellor is <a href="/wiki/Chris_Day_(clinician)" title="Chris Day (clinician)">Chris Day</a>, a hepatologist and former pro-vice-chancellor of the Faculty of Medical Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university has an enrolment of almost 16,000 undergraduate and 5,600 postgraduate students from more than 120 countries. Teaching and research are delivered in 24 academic schools and 40 research institutes and research centres, spread across three Faculties: the Faculty of Humanities and Social Sciences; the Faculty of Medical Sciences; and the Faculty of Science, Agriculture and Engineering. The university offers around 175 full-time undergraduate degree programmes in a wide range of subject areas spanning arts, sciences, engineering and medicine, together with approximately 340 postgraduate taught and research programmes across a range of disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-structure_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-structure-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university enjoys a friendly sporting rivalry with local universities. The <a href="/wiki/Stan_Calvert_Cup" title="Stan Calvert Cup">Stan Calvert Cup</a><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> is contested by major sports teams from Newcastle and <a href="/wiki/Northumbria_University" title="Northumbria University">Northumbria University</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_Water_University_Boat_Race" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbrian Water University Boat Race">Northumbrian Water University Boat Race</a> has also taken place between the <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a> clubs of Newcastle and <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>It holds a series of public lectures called 'Insights' each year in the Curtis Auditorium in the Herschel Building. Many of the university's partnerships with companies, like <a href="/wiki/Red_Hat" title="Red Hat">Red Hat</a>, are housed in the Herschel Annex.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civic_responsibility">Civic responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Civic responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The university describes itself as a civic university,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> with a role to play in society by bringing its research to bear on issues faced by communities (local, national or international).</p>
<p>It is focusing a major part of its research into tackling three profound challenges facing global society; namely Ageing, Sustainability and Social Renewal.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the university opened the Newcastle Institute for Social Renewal<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> to address issues of social and economic change, representing the research-led academic schools across the Faculty of Humanities and Social Sciences<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and the Business School.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Shucksmith" title="Mark Shucksmith">Mark Shucksmith</a> is Director of the Newcastle Institute for Social Renewal (NISR) at Newcastle University, where he is also Professor of Planning.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newcastle City Council has worked with the university to tackle local issues. One such is the issue to make public service cuts fair in times of austerity. Chris Brink, former Vice-Chancellor<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> at the university chaired the Newcastle Fairness Commission<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> to decide how the concept of 'fairness'<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> could be implemented with the full support of the community.</p>
<p>Newcastle was the only UK university to formally back the <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_Debt_Coalition" title="Jubilee Debt Coalition">Jubilee Debt Campaign</a> for the cancellation of debt in developing countries, and it has a strong ongoing commitment to the <a href="/wiki/Make_Poverty_History" title="Make Poverty History">Make Poverty History</a> campaign. At a high-profile honorary degree ceremony in January 2007, the university awarded honorary degrees to <a href="/wiki/Bob_Geldof" title="Bob Geldof">Bob Geldof</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> MP, and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Mkapa" title="Benjamin Mkapa">Benjamin Mkapa</a> (former president of <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>) among others, at an event that promoted debate on current debt-relief issues.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the university was granted <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">Fair trade</a> status and from January 2007 it became a smoke-free campus.</p>
<p>The university has also been actively involved with several of the region's museums for many years. The <a href="/wiki/Great_North_Museum:_Hancock" title="Great North Museum: Hancock">Great North Museum: Hancock</a> originally opened in 1884 and is often a venue for the university's events programme.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_School">Business School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Business School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Newcastle University Business School established the Business Accounting and Finance or 'Flying Start' degree in association with the <a href="/wiki/ICAEW" class="mw-redirect" title="ICAEW">ICAEW</a> and <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a>. The course offers an accelerated route towards the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Chartered_Accountants_in_England_%26_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Chartered Accountants in England &amp; Wales">ACA Chartered Accountancy qualification</a> and is the Business School's Flagship programme.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Its success has since resulted in <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_University" title="Lancaster University">Lancaster University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_%26_Young" title="Ernst &amp; Young">Ernst &amp; Young</a> collaborating to establish a competing degree programme in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Business School was ranked 43rd in <i>The Guardian</i> League tables in 2011. In 2011 the Business School opened their new £50&#160;million building built on the former <a href="/wiki/Scottish_and_Newcastle" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish and Newcastle">Scottish and Newcastle</a> brewery site next to <a href="/wiki/St_James%27_Park" title="St James' Park">St James' Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This building was officially opened on 19 March 2012 by <a href="/wiki/Terence_Burns,_Baron_Burns" title="Terence Burns, Baron Burns">Lord Burns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_School">Medical School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medical School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University_Medical_School" title="Newcastle University Medical School">Newcastle University Medical School</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University_Medical_School" title="Newcastle University Medical School">Medical School</a> gained 143 out of a possible 144 points in its six subject areas in the Teaching Quality Assessment, and was also the first institution in Europe, second in the world, to receive permission to pursue <a href="/wiki/Stem-cell_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Stem-cell research">stem-cell research</a> in human embryos. According to <a href="/wiki/UCAS" title="UCAS">UCAS</a>, Cambridge, Oxford and Newcastle are the most academically selective universities for entry to study medicine in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/BMC_journals" class="mw-redirect" title="BMC journals">BMC Medicine journal</a> reported medical graduates from Oxford, Cambridge and Newcastle performed better in postgraduate tests than any other medical school in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 the Medical School announced that they were expanding their campus to <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-numed_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-numed-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2009 the university won a <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Anniversary_Prize" title="Queen's Anniversary Prize">Queen's Anniversary Prize</a> for their research into <a href="/wiki/Gerontology" title="Gerontology">ageing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Victoria_Infirmary" title="Royal Victoria Infirmary">Royal Victoria Infirmary</a> has always had close links with the Faculty of Medical Sciences as a major teaching hospital.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="School_of_Modern_Languages">School of Modern Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: School of Modern Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The School of Modern Languages consists of five sections: East Asian (which includes Japanese and Chinese); French; German; Spanish, Portuguese &amp; Latin American Studies; and Translating &amp; Interpreting Studies.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Six languages are taught from beginner's level to full degree level ‒ Chinese, Japanese, French, German, Spanish and Portuguese ‒ and beginner's courses in Catalan, Dutch, Italian and Quechua are also available. Beyond the learning of the languages themselves, Newcastle also places a great deal of emphasis on study and experience of the cultures of the countries where the languages taught are spoken. The School of Modern Languages hosts <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>'s only branches of two internationally important institutes: the <a href="/wiki/Cam%C3%B5es_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Camões Institute">Camões Institute</a>, a language institute for Portuguese, and the <a href="/wiki/Confucius_Institute" title="Confucius Institute">Confucius Institute</a>, a language and cultural institute for Chinese.</p>
<p>The teaching of modern foreign languages at Newcastle predates the <a href="#History">creation of Newcastle University</a> itself, as in 1911 Armstrong College in Newcastle installed <a href="/wiki/Albert_George_Latham" title="Albert George Latham">Albert George Latham</a>, its first professor of modern languages.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Often in the School's history, the high quality of its teaching, research and student experience have been recognised. In the 2015 edition of <i>the Guardian University Guide</i>, the school was ranked 14 out of 63. In the 2014 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Rankings_of_universities_in_the_United_Kingdom#The_Sunday_Times" title="Rankings of universities in the United Kingdom">Sunday Times Good University Guide</a></i>, Iberian Studies, French and German in the School were each placed in the top ten for their subject across the UK. (East Asian Studies does not feature in this Guide.) In the 2013 <a href="/wiki/National_Student_Survey" title="National Student Survey">National Student Survey</a>, Spanish in the School came 6th in the UK for overall student satisfaction, and German came 7th.</p>
<p>The School of Modern Languages at Newcastle is also very active in outreach work. It is the Lead Institution in the North East <a href="/wiki/Routes_into_Languages" title="Routes into Languages">Routes into Languages</a> Consortium<sup id="cite_ref-routeslang_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-routeslang-51">[51]</a></sup> and together with the <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumbria_University" title="Northumbria University">Northumbria University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sunderland" title="University of Sunderland">University of Sunderland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Teesside_University" title="Teesside University">Teesside University</a> and a network of schools, undertakes work activities of discovery of languages for the 9 to 13 years pupils.<sup id="cite_ref-routeslang_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-routeslang-51">[51]</a></sup> This implies having festivals, Q&amp;A sessions, language tasters, or quizzes organised, as well as a web learning work aiming at constructing a web portal to link language learners across the region. There is a range of on-campus activities like the <i>Linguacast On-Campus</i>: 10 and 12 years pupils come and learn more about the practice and teaching of language; they visit Newcastle campus to produce Modern Foreign languages (MFL) web material and study it back in school. The SML provides structures from their linguistic communities so as to set up taster or revisions sessions.</p>
<p>The Language Days provide the 11 and 13 Years pupils the opportunity to immerse in core skill area in preparation for examinations. It also hosts, together with the School of Education, Communication and Language Sciences,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> the North East Network for Languages regional centre.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Furthermore, the SML is the provider of the only German Extension Courses in the UK;<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> these courses are funded by the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Education" title="Department for Education">Department for Education</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newcastle_Law_School">Newcastle Law School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Newcastle Law School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newcastle Law School is the longest established law school in the north-east of England as law has been continuously taught at the university and its predecessor college some three decades before it became independent from Durham University.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nuls_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nuls-56">[56]</a></sup> It boasts a number of recognised international and national experts in a variety of areas of legal scholarship ranging from Common and Chancery law, to International and European law, as well as contextual, socio-legal and theoretical legal studies.<sup id="cite_ref-nuls_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nuls-56">[56]</a></sup> It has been recognised for excellence in teaching and research by external bodies. In 2002–03, the <a href="/wiki/Higher_Education_Funding_Council_for_England" title="Higher Education Funding Council for England">Higher Education Funding Council for England</a> (HEFCE) Teaching Quality Audit, 'warmly commended' the quality of the taught programmes and self-critical and reflective practice of the staff. The latest national <a href="/wiki/Research_Assessment_Exercise" title="Research Assessment Exercise">Research Assessment Exercise</a> (RAE) published in December 2008 showed 85% of Newcastle Law School's research reached international standards of quality. In the previous RAE in 2001, the School was awarded a grade 5 for its research.<sup id="cite_ref-nuls_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nuls-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Law School occupies four specially adapted late-Victorian town houses. The Staff Offices, the Alumni Lecture Theatre and seminar rooms as well as the Law Library are all located within the School buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Undergraduate_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Undergraduate-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Law School is regarded as being one of the top institutions in the UK. It is ranked 3rd nationally by the <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Times">Sunday Times</a></i> University Guide 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> 11th by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> Good University Guide 2011<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and 8th by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> University Guide 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 2011, the university announced that it receives over 1000 applications for 170 places available each year.<sup id="cite_ref-how_to_apply_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-how_to_apply-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newcastle has been ranked the number one UK law school in the prestigious <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Times">Sunday Times</a></i> University Guide 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-news_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cavitation_tunnel">Cavitation tunnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cavitation tunnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newcastle University has the second largest <a href="/wiki/Cavitation_tunnel" class="mw-redirect" title="Cavitation tunnel">cavitation tunnel</a> in the UK. Founded in 1950, and based in the Marine Science and Technology Department, the <a href="/wiki/Emerson_Cavitation_Tunnel" title="Emerson Cavitation Tunnel">Emerson Cavitation Tunnel</a> is used as a test basin for propellers, water turbines, underwater coatings and interaction of propellers with ice.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Emerson_Cavitation_Tunnel" title="Emerson Cavitation Tunnel">Emerson Cavitation Tunnel</a> was recently relocated to a new facility in <a href="/wiki/Blyth,_Northumberland" title="Blyth, Northumberland">Blyth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>

<p>The university is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Russell_Group" title="Russell Group">Russell Group</a> of the UK's research-intensive universities. It is ranked in the top 200 of most world rankings, and in the top 20 of most UK rankings. It is ranked 129th by QS, 121st by <a href="/wiki/CWTS_Leiden_Ranking" title="CWTS Leiden Ranking">Leiden</a> and 198th by <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education</a> globally in 2013, placing it in the top 1% of universities. While nationally, it is ranked 18th by the Times/Sunday Times Good University Guide, 22nd by the Complete University Guide and 27th by the Guardian for 2014. The majority of its undergraduate courses are ranked in the top 20 by the Complete University Guide in the country with a number of subjects ranked in the top 10 such as Dentistry (6th), Medicine (8th), Architecture (5th), Art and Design (3rd) and Civil Engineering (8th). It was awarded 5 QS stars in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Newcastle won the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">Sunday Times</a></i> University of the Year award in 2000 and is ranked 12th best in the UK by the <a href="/wiki/Webometrics_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Webometrics Ranking of World Universities">Webometrics Ranking of World Universities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> According to the university, its <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University_Library" title="Newcastle University Library">library</a> is the only one in the UK to have been awarded the government's <a href="/wiki/Charter_Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter Mark">Charter Mark</a> for excellent customer services five times in a row.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Also, the university said it has a good track record for graduate employment in the country, and the Careers Service has won seven national careers awards in recent years by the Institute of Career Guidance.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university's Cochrane Park sports facility was a training venue for the teams playing football games at <a href="/wiki/St_James%27_Park" title="St James' Park">St James' Park</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2012_London_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="2012 London Olympics">2012 London Olympics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In 2013 the university entered into a two-year agreement with sports wear producer <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> that includes scholarships and sportswear for two of the university's most talented athletes, and some funding for the Athletics' Union.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, Newcastle University became one of 16 universities in the world, and only two in the UK, to achieve Five Plus Stars from <a href="/wiki/Quacquarelli_Symonds" title="Quacquarelli Symonds">Quacquarelli Symonds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> The university was awarded the perfect score of five Stars in all of the standardised assessment fields of Research, Internationalization, Facilities, Innovation, Inclusiveness, Specialist criteria and Student Employability (Newcastle University also made it to the Top 100 of QS Worldwide Employability Rankings in 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>

<p>In terms of average <a href="/wiki/UCAS" title="UCAS">UCAS</a> points of entrants, Newcastle ranked joint 19th in Britain in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> According to the 2017 <i>Times</i> and <i>Sunday Times</i> Good University Guide, approximately 25% of Newcastle's undergraduates come from independent schools.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The university gives offers of admission to 92.1% of its applicants, the highest amongst the <a href="/wiki/Russell_Group" title="Russell Group">Russell Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg/220px-Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg/330px-Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg/440px-Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herschelbuildingnewcastle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Herschel Building, home to the nanoLab, the School of Mathematics and Statistics, and the School of Chemical Engineering and Advanced Materials.</div>
</div>

<p>As a member of the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Russell_Group" title="Russell Group">Russell Group</a> of 24 research-intensive universities, Newcastle University has an outstanding reputation across a wide range of disciplines. All 38 of its submitted research areas were identified as "world leading" or "internationally recognised" by the latest 2008 Research Assessment Exercise (RAE).</p>
<p>It is ranked 17th in the UK for research power, according to the influential publication, Research Fortnight, and is in the UK's top twelve for research power in science and engineering. In the latest 2008 Research Assessment Exercise (RAE), Newcastle Biomedicine was ranked 8th in the UK overall for all "Medical" Units of Assessment combined in terms of research power. Newcastle is in the top 5 for both hospital- and laboratory-based clinical subjects, with two-thirds of its output classified as world leading (4*) or internationally excellent (3*) in the broad thematic areas of ageing, chronic disease, genetics and stem cells.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Students.27_Union">Students' Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Students' Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kings_Gate_building.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Kings_Gate_building.jpg/220px-Kings_Gate_building.jpg" width="220" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Kings_Gate_building.jpg/330px-Kings_Gate_building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Kings_Gate_building.jpg/440px-Kings_Gate_building.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="1630" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kings_Gate_building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The King's Gate building hosts student and administrative services and was built in 2009.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The university has many student organisations. Newcastle University Students' Union (known as the Union Society until a 2012 rebranding) includes student-run sports clubs and societies.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ncle_Union_2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ncle_Union_2012.jpg/200px-Ncle_Union_2012.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ncle_Union_2012.jpg/300px-Ncle_Union_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ncle_Union_2012.jpg/400px-Ncle_Union_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ncle_Union_2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Students' Union following refurbishment, 2012</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unlike the majority of other students' unions in the UK, Newcastle University Students' Union (NUSU) owns the building where it is housed. The Union building was built in 1924 following a generous gift from an anonymous donor, who is now believed to have been <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Algernon_Cochrane" class="mw-redirect" title="Cecil Algernon Cochrane">Sir Cecil Cochrane</a>, a major benefactor to the university.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> It is built in the neo-<a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">Jacobean</a> style and was designed by the local architect Robert Burns Dick whose firm designed the <a href="/wiki/Laing_Art_Gallery" title="Laing Art Gallery">Laing Art Gallery</a>, the towers of the <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Bridge" title="Tyne Bridge">Tyne Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Spanish_City" class="mw-redirect" title="The Spanish City">The Spanish City</a> in <a href="/wiki/Whitley_Bay" title="Whitley Bay">Whitley Bay</a>. It was opened on 22 October 1925 by the Rt. Hon. <a href="/wiki/Eustace_Percy,_1st_Baron_Percy_of_Newcastle" title="Eustace Percy, 1st Baron Percy of Newcastle">Lord Eustace Percy</a>, who later served as <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">Rector</a> of King's College from 1937 to 1952. It is a Grade II <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. In 2010 the university donated £8&#160;million towards a redevelopment project for the Union Building. This work won the Education Interior Design category of the National Mixology Interior Design Awards 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Students' Union is run by six sabbatical officers and fifteen part-time unpaid officer positions. The current leader of the Liberal Democrats <a href="/wiki/Tim_Farron" title="Tim Farron">Tim Farron</a> was President of NUSU in 1991–1992. The Students' Union also employs around 300 people in ancillary roles including bar staff and entertainment organisers. Artists that have performed at Students' Union include <a href="/wiki/Max%C3%AFmo_Park" title="Maxïmo Park">Maxïmo Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow_Patrol" title="Snow Patrol">Snow Patrol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kosheen" title="Kosheen">Kosheen</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fratellis" title="The Fratellis">The Fratellis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low_(band)" title="Low (band)">Low</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Rev" title="Mercury Rev">Mercury Rev</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goldie_Lookin%27_Chain" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldie Lookin' Chain">Goldie Lookin' Chain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicane_(recording_artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicane (recording artist)">Chicane</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Hoosiers" title="The Hoosiers">The Hoosiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damien_Rice" title="Damien Rice">Damien Rice</a>.</p>
<p><i>The Courier</i> is a weekly <a href="/wiki/Student_newspaper" class="mw-redirect" title="Student newspaper">student newspaper</a>. Established in 1948, the current weekly readership is around 12,000, most of whom are students at the university. It is published every week during term time, usually on a Monday unless a major news event warrants a delay.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> <i>The Courier</i> has won <i>The Guardian'</i>s <i>Student Publication of the Year</i> award twice in a row, in 2012 and 2013. In 2008, <i>The Courier</i> design editor Kerry Hyndman came runner up in the <i>Guardian</i> Student media award for Design for her work on the newspaper's entertainment pull-out, <i>Pulp</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> As part of a re-design of the paper in 2009/2010 <i>Pulp</i>, originally a separate publication was replaced by a lifestyle section in the main body of <i>The Courier</i>.</p>
<p>Newcastle Student Radio is a <a href="/wiki/Student_radio_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Student radio station">student radio station</a> based in the university.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The station aims to cater for a wide range of musical tastes, anything from Metal and Punk to R&amp;B and swing music.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_accommodation">Student accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Student accommodation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Leazes Terrace, a Grade I listed building, designed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Oliver_(architect)" title="Thomas Oliver (architect)">Thomas Oliver</a> and built by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Grainger" title="Richard Grainger">Richard Grainger</a>, in 1829–34; now student accommodation.</div>

<p>Newcastle University has many catered and non-catered <a href="/wiki/Dormitory" title="Dormitory">halls of residence</a> available to first-year students, located around the city of Newcastle.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> Popular Newcastle areas for private student houses and flats off campus include <a href="/wiki/Jesmond" title="Jesmond">Jesmond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heaton,_Newcastle" title="Heaton, Newcastle">Heaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandyford,_Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Sandyford, Newcastle upon Tyne">Sandyford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shieldfield" title="Shieldfield">Shieldfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spital_Tongues" title="Spital Tongues">Spital Tongues</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008 a survey conducted by an independent website ranked Newcastle as the number one student city in the UK, with a score of 63% across the categories of going out, shops, transport, community and facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Newcastle is also considered one of the world's friendliest cities.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In another 2008 survey, by <a href="/wiki/Bing_Travel" class="mw-redirect" title="Bing Travel">MSN Travel</a>, Newcastle was named as the number one university.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p>St Mary's College in <a href="/wiki/Fenham" title="Fenham">Fenham</a>, one of the halls of residence, was formerly St Mary's College of Education, a teacher training college.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newcastle_University&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newcastle is one of the leading universities for sport in the UK and is consistently ranked within the top 12 out of 152 higher education institutions in the British Universities and Colleges Sport (BUCS) rankings. More than 50 student-led sports clubs are supported through a team of professional staff and a network of indoor and outdoor sports facilities based over four sites.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
