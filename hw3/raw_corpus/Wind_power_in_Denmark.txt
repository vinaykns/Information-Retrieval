			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Wind power in Denmark</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>Denmark was a pioneer in developing commercial <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> during the 1970s, and today a substantial share of the <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> around the world are produced by <a href="/wiki/List_of_wind_turbine_manufacturers" title="List of wind turbine manufacturers">Danish manufacturers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Wind_Power" title="Siemens Wind Power">Siemens Wind Power</a> along with many component suppliers. <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">Wind power</a> produced the equivalent of 42.1% of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>'s total electricity consumption in 2015,<sup id="cite_ref-euractiv1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euractiv1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-enet42_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-2">[2]</a></sup> increased from 33% in 2013, and 39% in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-en2014_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-en2013_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2013-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2012 the Danish government adopted a plan to increase the share of electricity production from wind to 50% by 2020,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and to 84% in 2035.<sup id="cite_ref-Lindboe.2C_page_3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lindboe.2C_page_3-7">[7]</a></sup> Denmark had the 6th best <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a> in the world in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Danish inventor <a href="/wiki/Poul_la_Cour" title="Poul la Cour">Poul la Cour</a> experimented, taught and constructed wind power subjects around the year 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As concerns over <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> grew in the 1980s, Denmark found itself with relatively high <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a> per capita, primarily due to the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuel power plant">coal-fired electrical power plants</a> that had become the norm after the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973</a> and <a href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">1979 energy crises</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DWEA020222_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DWEA020222-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a> became the natural choice for Denmark, decreasing both dependence on other countries for energy and global warming pollution.</p>
<p>Many countries tried to subsidize green technology such as wind power, and most failed to make a viable industry. The Danish system was an exception, providing 30% of initial capital cost in the early years which was gradually reduced to zero, but still maintaining a feed-in tariff.<sup id="cite_ref-bsRE_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bsRE-12">[12]</a></sup> The capital cost subsidy was reduced to 20% in June 1985, when wind turbines received DKK 50 million per year. Other renewable energy forms received 37 million.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The research institution <a href="/w/index.php?title=Teknologisk_Institut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Teknologisk Institut (page does not exist)">Teknologisk Institut</a> identified many specific improvement needs, pushing development from <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a> to systemized solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 29 March 1985, one year before the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>, the Danes passed a law forbidding the construction of nuclear power plants. In the process the Danish grassroots movement had a substantial role. The Danish <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-nuclear Movement">Anti-nuclear Movement</a>'s (OOA) smiling-sun logo "Atomkraft, Nej Tak" ("Nuclear Power, No Thanks") spread worldwide, and the renewable alternatives were promoted by the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Organisation_for_Renewable_Energy" title="Danish Organisation for Renewable Energy">Danish Organisation for Renewable Energy</a> (OVE).</p>
<p>Denmark adopted a target of cutting <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a> by 22% from 1988 levels by 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-DWEA020222_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DWEA020222-11">[11]</a></sup> Planning of wind power was deliberately streamlined by authorities in order to minimize hurdles.<sup id="cite_ref-emDK_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emDK-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Denmark has relatively modest average wind speeds in the range of 4.9–5.6&#160;m/s measured at 10&#160;m height. Onshore wind resources are highest in the western part of the country, and on the eastern islands with coastlines facing south or west. The country has very large offshore wind resources, and large areas of sea territory with a shallow water depth of 5–15&#160;m, where siting is most feasible. These sites offer higher wind speeds, in the range of roughly 8.5–9.0&#160;m/s at 50&#160;m height.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There have been no major problems from wind variability, although there is a temporary problem resulting from the connection of a large bloc of wind power from offshore wind farms to a single point on a weak section of the transmission network.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The wind resource over Denmark was mapped in 1999 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=EMD_International_A/S&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EMD International A/S (page does not exist)">EMD International A/S</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ris%C3%B8" class="mw-redirect" title="Risø">Risø</a> National Laboratory. The mapping was made using a 200&#160;m grid resolution using the models in <a href="/w/index.php?title=WindPRO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="WindPRO (page does not exist)">WindPRO</a> and WAsP. The results were validated on more than 1200 wind turbines nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Denmark is connected by transmission line to other European countries (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cross-Skagerrak" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-Skagerrak">Cross-Skagerrak</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-hvdk_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hvdk-19">[19]</a></sup> at an <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_grid_of_Continental_Europe#List_of_electricity_interconnection_level" title="Synchronous grid of Continental Europe">electricity interconnection level</a> (transmission capacity relative to production capacity) of 44%.<sup id="cite_ref-eil_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eil-20">[20]</a></sup> Denmark has also retained its traditional power plants, therefore it does not need to install additional peak-load plants to balance its wind power. Instead, it purchases additional power from its neighbours when necessary. With some strengthening of the grid, Denmark plans to increase wind's share even further<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> to 50% of consumption in 2020,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and up to 84% in 2035.<sup id="cite_ref-Lindboe.2C_page_3_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lindboe.2C_page_3-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Analysts expect the cost of wind power to be 30 øre/kWh and its handling cost to be 15 øre/kWh, being lower than coal and natural gas at 55 øre/kWh minimum.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Due to their inability to <a href="/wiki/Load_following_power_plant" title="Load following power plant">follow load</a> wind power gets a lower price.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_related_to_wind_power">Consumption related to wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consumption related to wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Danish <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> plants use 100 <a href="/wiki/Petajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Petajoule">Petajoule</a>/year,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but little of this consumption is from 180MW of <a href="/wiki/Electrode_boiler" title="Electrode boiler">electrode boilers</a> installed in powerplants or 37MW of large <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pumps</a>. The boilers are only used to soak up the powerplant's own electricity when prices are negative, to avoid paying tax.<sup id="cite_ref-wittboil_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wittboil-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Expansion of wind powered district heating is calculated to be <a href="/wiki/Economic_efficiency" title="Economic efficiency">economically efficient</a> without taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-witHeatPump_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witHeatPump-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number of household heat pumps has stalled at 70,000 in 2015 due to tax-free <a href="/wiki/Wood_pellet" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood pellet">wood pellets</a>, and the goal of 300,000 small heat pumps in 2035 is unlikely to be reached, reducing the value of more wind power unless electricity tax is reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the end of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Denmark's capacity stood at 5,070&#160;MW.</p>
<p>Denmark has the highest proportion of wind power in the world. In 2015, Denmark produced 42% of electricity from wind,<sup id="cite_ref-euractiv1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euractiv1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-enet42_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-2">[2]</a></sup> up from the 2014 record of 39% of total power consumption. For the month of January 2014, that share was over 61%. The month of lowest wind power share was July at 23%.<sup id="cite_ref-en2014_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2014-3">[3]</a></sup> Denmark also had 548&#160;MW of <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Denmark" title="Solar power in Denmark">solar power</a><sup id="cite_ref-epia-2014-p34_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epia-2014-p34-35">[35]</a></sup> (790 MW in late 2015).<sup id="cite_ref-ing790_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing790-36">[36]</a></sup> A peak generation period occurred on 21 December 2013 when the wind share was 102%, and for 1 hour the share was 135%.<sup id="cite_ref-en2013_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2013-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A report on Denmark's wind power noted that their wind power network provided less than 1% of average demand on 54 days during 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-Denmark2002_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Denmark2002-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, Denmark had installed wind capacity of 3,127&#160;MW, which produced 23,810&#160;<a href="/wiki/Terajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Terajoule">TJ</a> (6.6&#160;<a href="/wiki/TW%C2%B7h" class="mw-redirect" title="TW·h">TW·h</a>) of energy, giving an actual average production of 755&#160;MW at a <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a> of 24%.<sup id="cite_ref-DK_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DK-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2009, Denmark's capacity grew to 3,482&#160;MW; most of the increase came from the 209&#160;MW <a href="/wiki/Horns_Rev_2" title="Horns Rev 2">Horns Rev 2</a> offshore wind farm, which was inaugurated on September&#160;17, 2009 by <a href="/wiki/Frederik,_Crown_Prince_of_Denmark" title="Frederik, Crown Prince of Denmark">Crown Prince Frederik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2010, capacity grew to 3,752&#160;MW, and most of the year's increase came from the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8dsand_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Rødsand II">Rødsand-2</a> off-shore wind farm. In 2014, the largest increase came from the 400&#160;MW <a href="/wiki/Anholt_wind_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Anholt wind farm">Anholt wind farm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-en2014_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2014-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wind power output reduces spot market prices in general via the <a href="/wiki/Merit_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Merit Order">merit order</a> effect; in 2008 this caused a net reduction of pre-tax electricity prices (balancing the increase from the feed-in law).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Denmark continues to install additional capacity, they continue to set new production records. This is a natural consequence of capacity growth. On July 9, 2015, in the evening, unusually strong wind conditions resulted in 116% of national electricity consumption being produced by wind farms and at 3AM the next morning at low demand, wind production exceeded 140% of current demand.<sup id="cite_ref-enet42_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_parks_in_Denmark">Future parks in Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Future parks in Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Offshore">Offshore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Offshore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_offshore_wind_farms_in_Denmark" title="List of offshore wind farms in Denmark">List of offshore wind farms in Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_offshore_wind_farms_in_the_North_Sea" title="List of offshore wind farms in the North Sea">List of offshore wind farms in the North Sea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_offshore_wind_farms_in_the_Baltic_Sea" title="List of offshore wind farms in the Baltic Sea">List of offshore wind farms in the Baltic Sea</a></div>
<p>On 22 March 2012 a coalition of parties representing 95% of all members of the Danish parliament agreed that the Danish state would increase the country's offshore wind capacity by 1,500&#160;MW.<sup id="cite_ref-ING_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ING-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ENS_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS-50">[50]</a></sup> The 1,500&#160;MW extra capacity will be achieved by constructing the future offshore wind farms <a href="/wiki/Horns_Rev" title="Horns Rev">Horns Rev</a> 3 with a capacity of 400&#160;MW in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a><sup id="cite_ref-ENS_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS-50">[50]</a></sup> at 77 øre/kWh<sup id="cite_ref-wit5_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wit5-51">[51]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kriegers_Flak_(wind_farm)" title="Kriegers Flak (wind farm)">Kriegers Flak</a> at 37.2 øre/kWh<sup id="cite_ref-steel1_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-steel1-43">[43]</a></sup> with a capacity of 600&#160;MW in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> close to the borders of Germany and Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-ENS_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS-50">[50]</a></sup> Kriegers Flak will also be used to connect Denmark and Germany with a 400MW cable, through the German <a href="/wiki/Baltic_1_Offshore_Wind_Farm" title="Baltic 1 Offshore Wind Farm">Baltic 2 Offshore Wind Farm</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and Energinet ordered electrical equipment in early 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Horns Rev 3 is set to be operational in 2019<sup id="cite_ref-NET_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NET-55">[55]</a></sup> while Kriegers Flak and all the nearshore wind farms are set to be fully operational<sup id="cite_ref-ENS_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NET2_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NET2-56">[56]</a></sup> around 2021. Eight groups applied for pre-qualification for Kriegers Flak,<sup id="cite_ref-witKF2_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witKF2-57">[57]</a></sup> of which 7 were approved<sup id="cite_ref-witKF3_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witKF3-58">[58]</a></sup> - 3 more than the 4 companies approved to compete for Horns Rev 3,<sup id="cite_ref-wit16feb_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wit16feb-59">[59]</a></sup> both having more bidders than the single bidder for Anholt.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nearshore">Nearshore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nearshore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;text-align: left"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/16px-Searchtool.svg.png" width="16" height="16" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/24px-Searchtool.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/32px-Searchtool.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="512" /> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://ing.dk/sites/ing/files/soendervig_strand_vattenfalls_visualisering.jpg">Visualization of North Sea nearshore turbines</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Hvide_Sande" title="Hvide Sande">Hvide Sande</a></td>

<p>In addition 6 nearshore wind farms with a total capacity of up to 450&#160;MW will be constructed along with 50&#160;MW of experimental offshore wind farms. The nearshore differ from conventional offshore in having the being close enough to the coast to have the transformer on land, decreasing cost.<sup id="cite_ref-ENS_50-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS-50">[50]</a></sup> The first 350&#160;MW were called for tenders in 2015, with a maximum price of 70 øre/kWh.<sup id="cite_ref-wit3_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wit3-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Vattenfall bid the lowest price for the 350 MW nearshore farms at 47,5 øre/kWh in September 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> but the politial situation was unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Onshore">Onshore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Onshore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In addition to the offshore projects, a further 500&#160;MW additional net capacity of onshore windfarms is expected to be constructed until 2020. The 500&#160;MW of additional net capacity is the expected result of the scrapping of 1,300&#160;MW capacity from obsolete wind turbines combined with the simultaneous building of 1,800&#160;MW capacity of modern wind turbines – a process also known as <a href="/wiki/Repowering" title="Repowering">repowering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Klima_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klima-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_exports_from_Denmark">Electricity exports from Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electricity exports from Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Annual wind power production is currently (2014) equal to about 39% of electricity consumed in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-enet42_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-en2014_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-en2014-3">[3]</a></sup> The proportion of this that is actually consumed in Denmark has been disputed, as the considerable <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> resources of Norway (and to some extent, Sweden) is used as <a href="/wiki/Grid_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Grid storage">grid storage</a> with low loss. Hydropower can rapidly reduce generation whenever wind farms are generating power, saving water for later, and can export electricity to Denmark when wind power output drops. Short term, Denmark imports electricity from Norway during daytime and exports in nighttime. Long term, Denmark imports electricity in summer and exports in winter. Wind is higher in autumn and winter, when consumption is also high.<sup id="cite_ref-holt_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holt-66">[66]</a></sup> This service of timeshifting production and consumption is also found around the world in <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a> balancing coal and nuclear plants.</p>
<p>For timeshifting trade with Norway, Denmark exports at DKK 157/MWh and imports at DKK 212/MWh.<sup id="cite_ref-witHeatPump_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witHeatPump-32">[32]</a></sup> The correlation is low between wind power in Norway and Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-holt_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holt-66">[66]</a></sup> Market price sometimes falls to near or below zero, particularly in high winds and low consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In 2014, there were 46 hours with negative prices, costing DKK 37.7 million.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In 2015, negative prices occurred in 65 hours in West Denmark and 36 hours in East Denmark - less than 1% of the time. Danish prices are mainly negative when German prices are even more negative. The whole date of September 2 (2015) was the first time that no central power plants were running in West Denmark, and grid stability was maintained by compensators.<sup id="cite_ref-enet42_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Denmark is generally a transit country for electricity trade between the much larger markets in Norway, Sweden and Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and plans to add cables to the Netherlands (<a href="/wiki/COBRAcable" title="COBRAcable">COBRAcable</a>) and England (<a href="/wiki/Viking_Link" title="Viking Link">Viking Link</a>) as well, further increasing the function of being a <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(road)" title="Intersection (road)">crossroads</a> for electricity.</p>
<p>Claims of up to 40% of wind power being exported have been made,<sup id="cite_ref-REO_2005_20066_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REO_2005_20066-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> countered by claims that only 1% was exported.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the first argument, power in excess of immediate demand is exported to neighbouring countries at lower prices. Part of the benefit of this goes to Denmark's northern neighbours: when Denmark exports power, it is sold at the <a href="/wiki/Spot_market" title="Spot market">spot market</a> price which must be lower than at the importing market in order to be transmitted.</p>
<p>According to the second argument, the correlation between exports and wind power is weak, and a similar correlation exists with conventional thermal plants running partly for district heating; meanwhile, causal analysis shows that export from Denmark typically occurs as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Merit_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Merit Order">merit order</a> effect, when large thermal plants have reserve capacities at times the spot market price of electricity is high.</p>
<p>In any case, the export price is the intermediate between the prices of the two areas, so the exporting TSO (Energinet) uses the profit to relieve tariffs<sup id="cite_ref-npbott_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npbott-74">[74]</a></sup> at around DKK 500 million per year.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Wind power organizations state that Denmark exports power at a higher price than it imports at.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_conditions">Economic conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economic conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbine_industry">Wind turbine industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wind turbine industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vestas_Turbine.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Vestas_Turbine.JPG/200px-Vestas_Turbine.JPG" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Vestas_Turbine.JPG/300px-Vestas_Turbine.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Vestas_Turbine.JPG/400px-Vestas_Turbine.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vestas_Turbine.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Vestas wind turbine</div>

<p>The Danish wind turbine industry is the world's largest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Around 90% of the national output is exported, and Danish companies accounted for 38% of the world turbine <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market</a> in 2003, when the industry employed some 20,000 people and had a turnover of around 3&#160;billion <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Scand2004_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scand2004-77">[77]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Return_of_investment" class="mw-redirect" title="Return of investment">return of investment</a> dropped from near 20% before the financial crisis, to 10% some years later.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The Danish wind turbine industry had a turnover of DKK 84 billion in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<p>The biggest wind turbine manufacturers with production facilities in Denmark are <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Wind_Power" title="Siemens Wind Power">Siemens Wind Power</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The development of wind power in Denmark has been characterized by a close collaboration between publicly financed research and industry in key areas such as <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>, <a href="/wiki/IEC_61400" title="IEC 61400">certification</a>, testing, and the preparation of standards.<sup id="cite_ref-risoe_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-risoe-81">[81]</a></sup> For example, in the 1980s, a large number of small Danish companies were developing wind turbines to sell to California, and the Danish Risø laboratory provided test facilities and certification procedures. These resulted in reliable products and the rapid expansion of the Danish turbine manufacturing industry.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Components are tested at <a href="/wiki/Odense_Steel_Shipyard#After_yard_closure" title="Odense Steel Shipyard">LORC</a> in <a href="/wiki/Odense" title="Odense">Odense</a>, and new large prototype turbines between 4-8 MW (including some non-Danish ones) are being tested at <a href="/wiki/%C3%98sterild_Wind_Turbine_Test_Field" title="Østerild Wind Turbine Test Field">Østerild</a>. Limited production turbines (four Siemens 7MW with 66kV cabling) are to be supported at <a href="/wiki/Lemvig_Municipality" title="Lemvig Municipality">Nissum Bredning</a> at a cost of DKK 300m, partially financed by local people.<sup id="cite_ref-Nissum_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nissum-83">[83]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>

<p>Denmark's electricity <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">costs</a> (including PSO; costs for cleaner energy) are average in the EU,<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-esIndu_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esIndu-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> increase the <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a> to the highest in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> The tax money is a considerable income for the state, and changing the composition of the taxes towards a "greener" mix is difficult. According to a government official, the majority of taxes are not based on environment concerns,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> in contrast to the DKK 5 billion per year in <a href="/wiki/Public_service_obligation" title="Public service obligation">PSO-money for cleaner energy</a>, paid by electricity consumers to producers of clean electricity. These tolls are not available for government consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-psofut_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psofut-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actual consumer-paid incentives (PSO) to new wind turbines depend on year of commission, but is generally around 25 øre (3.4 eurocent) per kWh for a limited amount of hours, although support is discounted if combined price exceeds 58 øre/kWh. PSO is also used for biomass, solar, and district heating; total PSO was DKK 5.8 billion in 2013, of which DKK 3.2 billion went to wind power.<sup id="cite_ref-psofut_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psofut-92">[92]</a></sup> In 2015, the cost of power was only 32% of the price, while PSO was 9%, and tolls and VAT the remaining 59%.<sup id="cite_ref-csrPrice_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csrPrice-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wind power displaces coal, oil and gas to some degree, reducing running cost for fossil fuels. <a href="/wiki/Nordic_energy_market#Coal_power_in_Denmark" title="Nordic energy market">Consumption of coal</a> was more than halved over 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Wind power reduces price variability slightly.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Criticism</dt>
</dl>
<p>In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Energy_Research" title="Institute for Energy Research">Institute for Energy Research</a> commissioned the Danish think-tank <a href="/wiki/CEPOS" title="CEPOS">CEPOS (Centre for Political Studies)</a> to report on electricity exports from Denmark and the economic impact of the Danish wind industry. This heavily criticized report<sup id="cite_ref-cepos_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cepos-95">[95]</a></sup> states that Danes pay the highest residential electricity rates in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (mostly for government revenue, but partly to subsidize wind power), and that the cost of saving a ton of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> between 2001 and 2008 has averaged 647 DKK (€87, US$124). It also estimated that 90% of <a href="/wiki/Guns_versus_butter_model" title="Guns versus butter model">wind industry jobs were transferred from other technology industries</a>, and states that as a result Danish GDP is 1.8&#160;billion DKK (US$270&#160;million) lower than it would have been without wind industry subsidies of 1.7-2.6 billion DKK (roughly $320M - $480M) yearly in 2001-2005. The report was later heavily criticised. Firstly Danish engineering magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Ingeni%C3%B8ren" title="Ingeniøren">Ingeniøren</a></i> claimed that the report was ordered and paid for by the American oil and <a href="/wiki/Coal_lobby" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal lobby">coal lobby</a> through IER.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> Later, several Danish researchers and professors from all technical universities in Denmark, wrote a joint response to the report, refuting it.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The report from CEPOS was even brought to Government level, where minister of Climate and Energy Lykke Friis discredited the work done by CEPOS and the report.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Nuclear_Association" title="World Nuclear Association">World Nuclear Association</a> estimates that the report was IER's response to <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Barack_Obama#Energy_and_the_environment" title="Presidency of Barack Obama">US president Obama</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_Obama_administration#Reactions_and_analysis_of_energy_and_climate_security" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy policy of the Obama administration">2009 Earth Day speech</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Iowa#.22Newton.27s_future.22_and_life_after_Maytag" title="Newton, Iowa">Newton, Iowa</a> claiming that USA could generate 20% of its electricity from wind by 2030, as Denmark already was.<sup id="cite_ref-Nuclear_Energy_in_Denmark_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nuclear_Energy_in_Denmark-99">[99]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_turbine_cooperatives">Wind turbine cooperatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Wind turbine cooperatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_cooperative" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbine cooperative">Wind turbine cooperative</a></div>
<p>To encourage investment in wind power, families were offered a tax exemption for generating their own electricity within their own or an adjoining <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Denmark" title="Municipalities of Denmark">municipality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WW1996_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WW1996-101">[101]</a></sup> While this could involve purchasing a turbine outright, more often families purchased shares in <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine_cooperative" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbine cooperative">wind turbine cooperatives</a> which in turn invested in community wind turbines. By 1996 there were around 2,100 such cooperatives in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-WW1996_101-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WW1996-101">[101]</a></sup> Opinion polls show that this direct involvement has helped the popularity of wind turbines, with some 86% of Danes supporting wind energy when compared with existing fuel sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Scand2004_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scand2004-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>The role of wind turbine cooperatives is not limited to single turbines. The <a href="/wiki/Middelgrunden" title="Middelgrunden">Middelgrunden</a> offshore <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farm</a> – with 20&#160;turbines the world's largest offshore farm at the time it was built in 2000 – is 50% owned by the 10,000 investors in the Middelgrunden Wind Turbine Cooperative, and 50% by the municipal utility company,<sup id="cite_ref-middelgrunden2_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-middelgrunden2-102">[102]</a></sup> as is the Avedøre near-shore turbines.</p>
<p>By 2001 over 100,000 families belonged to wind turbine cooperatives, which had installed 86% of all the wind turbines in Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-middelgrunden_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-middelgrunden-103">[103]</a></sup> By 2004 over 150,000 were either members or owned turbines, and about 5,500 turbines had been installed, although with greater private sector involvement the proportion owned by cooperatives had fallen to 75%.<sup id="cite_ref-Scand2004_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scand2004-77">[77]</a></sup> The cooperative model has also spread to Germany and the Netherlands.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sams.C3.B8_Island">Samsø Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_power_in_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Samsø Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The island of <a href="/wiki/Sams%C3%B8" title="Samsø">Samsø</a> erected 11 one-megawatt, land-based wind turbines in 2000, followed by ten offshore 2.3&#160;MW wind turbines completed in 2003. Together with other renewable energy measures, this community of 4,200 achieved fame,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> claiming that it is the largest carbon-neutral settlement on the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2015)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> This claim exploits the general discourse, that one can neglect carbon-dioxide and other pollution from fossil fuel consumption (cars, imported electricity, heating for houses...), if the yearly average electricity production from sustained sources is higher than the total energy consumed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
