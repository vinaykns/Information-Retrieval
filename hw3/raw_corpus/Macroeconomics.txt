<p><b>Macroeconomics</b> (from the Greek prefix <i>makro-</i> meaning "large" and economics) is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> dealing with the performance, structure, behavior, and decision-making of an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> as a whole. This includes national, regional, and global economies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Macroeconomics and <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomics</a>, a pair of terms coined by <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Frisch" title="Ragnar Frisch">Ragnar Frisch</a>, are the two most general fields in economics.<sup id="cite_ref-blaug1985_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blaug1985-2">[2]</a></sup> In contrast to macroeconomics, microeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the behavior of individuals and firms in making decisions and the interactions among these individuals and firms in narrowly-defined markets.</p>
<p><b>Macroeconomists</b> study aggregated indicators such as <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unemployment#Measurement" title="Unemployment">unemployment rates</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_income" class="mw-redirect" title="National income">national income</a>, <a href="/wiki/Price_index" title="Price index">price indices</a>, and the interrelations among the different sectors of the economy to better understand how the whole economy functions. Macroeconomists develop models that explain the relationship between such factors as <a href="/wiki/National_income" class="mw-redirect" title="National income">national income</a>, <a href="/wiki/Output_(economics)" title="Output (economics)">output</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savings" class="mw-redirect" title="Savings">savings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Investment_(macroeconomics)" title="Investment (macroeconomics)">investment</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_finance" title="International finance">international finance</a>.</p>
<p>While macroeconomics is a broad field of study, there are two areas of research that are emblematic of the discipline: the attempt to understand the causes and consequences of short-run fluctuations in national income (the <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle</a>), and the attempt to <a href="/wiki/Understanding_by_Design" title="Understanding by Design">understand</a> the determinants of long-run <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> (increases in national income). <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomic_model" title="Macroeconomic model">Macroeconomic models</a> and their forecasts are used by governments to assist in the development and evaluation of <a href="/wiki/Economic_policy" title="Economic policy">economic policy</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_macroeconomic_concepts">Basic macroeconomic concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic macroeconomic concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Macroeconomics encompasses a variety of concepts and variables, but there are three central topics for macroeconomic research.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Macroeconomic theories usually relate the phenomena of output, unemployment, and inflation. Outside of macroeconomic theory, these topics are also important to all economic agents including workers, consumers, and producers.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Circulation_in_macroeconomics.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Circulation in macroeconomics.</div>
</div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Output_and_income">Output and income</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Output and income">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>National <a href="/wiki/Output_(economics)" title="Output (economics)">output</a> is the total amount of everything a country produces in a given period of time. Everything that is produced and sold generates an equal amount of income. Therefore, output and income are usually considered equivalent and the two terms are often used interchangeably. Output can be measured as total income, or it can be viewed from the production side and measured as the total value of <a href="/wiki/Final_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Final goods">final goods</a> and services or the sum of all <a href="/wiki/Value_added" title="Value added">value added</a> in the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macroeconomic output is usually measured by <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) or one of the other <a href="/wiki/National_accounts" title="National accounts">national accounts</a>. Economists interest in long-run increases in output study economic growth. Advances in technology, accumulation of machinery and other <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a>, and better education and <a href="/wiki/Human_capital" title="Human capital">human capital</a> all lead to increased economic output over time. However, output does not always increase consistently. <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">Business cycles</a> can cause short-term drops in output called <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recessions</a>. Economists look for <a href="#Macroeconomic_policy">macroeconomic policies</a> that prevent economies from slipping into recessions and that lead to faster long-term growth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unemployment">Unemployment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unemployment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">Unemployment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png/220px-Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png/330px-Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png/440px-Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png 2x" data-file-width="1103" data-file-height="801" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Okuns_law_differences_1948_to_mid_2011.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A chart using US data showing the relationship between economic growth and unemployment expressed by <a href="/wiki/Okun%27s_law" title="Okun's law">Okun's law</a>. The relationship demonstrates cyclical unemployment. Economic growth leads to a lower unemployment rate.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The amount of <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> in an economy is measured by the unemployment rate, i.e. the percentage of workers without jobs in the <a href="/wiki/Labor_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor force">labor force</a>. The unemployment rate in the labor force only includes workers actively looking for jobs. People who are retired, pursuing education, or <a href="/wiki/Discouraged_worker" title="Discouraged worker">discouraged from seeking work</a> by a lack of job prospects are excluded.</p>
<p>Unemployment can be generally broken down into several types that are related to different causes.</p>
<ul>
<li>Classical unemployment theory suggests that unemployment occurs when wages are too high for employers to be willing to hire more workers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other more modern economic theories<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2016)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> suggest that increased wages actually decrease unemployment by creating more consumer demand. According to these more recent theories, unemployment results from reduced demand for the goods and services produced through labor and suggest that only in markets where profit margins are very low, and in which the market will not bear a price increase of product or service, will higher wages result in unemployment.</li>
<li>Consistent with classical unemployment theory, <a href="/wiki/Frictional_unemployment" title="Frictional unemployment">frictional unemployment</a> occurs when appropriate job vacancies exist for a worker, but the length of time needed to search for and find the job leads to a period of unemployment.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Structural_unemployment" title="Structural unemployment">Structural unemployment</a> covers a variety of possible causes of unemployment including a mismatch between workers' skills and the skills required for open jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Large amounts of structural unemployment can occur when an economy is transitioning industries and workers find their previous set of skills are no longer in demand. Structural unemployment is similar to frictional unemployment as both reflect the problem of matching workers with job vacancies, but structural unemployment also covers the time needed to acquire new skills in addition to the short term search process.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>While some types of unemployment may occur regardless of the condition of the economy, cyclical unemployment occurs when growth stagnates. <a href="/wiki/Okun%27s_law" title="Okun's law">Okun's law</a> represents the empirical relationship between unemployment and economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The original version of Okun's law states that a 3% increase in output would lead to a 1% decrease in unemployment.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflation_and_deflation">Inflation and deflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inflation and deflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:M2andInflation.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/M2andInflation.png/220px-M2andInflation.png" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/M2andInflation.png/330px-M2andInflation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/M2andInflation.png/440px-M2andInflation.png 2x" data-file-width="1103" data-file-height="801" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:M2andInflation.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The ten-year moving averages of changes in price level and growth in money supply (using the measure of M2, the supply of hard currency and money held in most types of bank accounts) in the US from 1875 to 2011. Over the long run, the two series show a close relationship.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A general price increase across the entire economy is called <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>. When prices decrease, there is <a href="/wiki/Deflation" title="Deflation">deflation</a>. Economists measure these changes in prices with <a href="/wiki/Price_index" title="Price index">price indexes</a>. Inflation can occur when an economy becomes overheated and grows too quickly. Similarly, a declining economy can lead to deflation.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">Central bankers</a>, who manage a country's money supply, try to avoid changes in price level by using <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a>. Raising interest rates or reducing the supply of money in an economy will reduce inflation. Inflation can lead to increased uncertainty and other negative consequences. Deflation can lower economic output. Central bankers try to stabilize prices to protect economies from the negative consequences of price changes.</p>
<p>Changes in price level may be the result of several factors. The <a href="/wiki/Quantity_theory_of_money" title="Quantity theory of money">quantity theory of money</a> holds that changes in price level are directly related to changes in the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a>. Most economists believe that this relationship explains long-run changes in the price level.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMankiw2014634_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMankiw2014634-10">[10]</a></sup> Short-run fluctuations may also be related to monetary factors, but changes in aggregate demand and aggregate supply can also influence price level. For example, a decrease in demand due to a recession can lead to lower price levels and deflation. A negative supply shock, such as an oil crisis, lowers aggregate supply and can cause inflation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Macroeconomic_models">Macroeconomic models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Macroeconomic models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_demand.E2.80.93aggregate_supply">Aggregate demand–aggregate supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aggregate demand–aggregate supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg/220px-AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg.png" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg/330px-AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg/440px-AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="368" data-file-height="288" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AS_%2B_AD_graph.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A traditional AS–AD diagram showing a shift in AD and the AS curve becoming inelastic beyond potential output.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/AD-AS_model" class="mw-redirect" title="AD-AS model">AD-AS model</a> has become the standard textbook model for explaining the macroeconomy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealey200212_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealey200212-11">[11]</a></sup> This model shows the price level and level of real output given the equilibrium in <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_supply" title="Aggregate supply">aggregate supply</a>. The aggregate demand curve's downward slope means that more output is demanded at lower price levels.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealey200213_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealey200213-12">[12]</a></sup> The downward slope is the result of three effects: the <a href="/wiki/Pigou_effect" title="Pigou effect">Pigou or real balance effect</a>, which states that as real prices fall, real wealth increases, resulting in higher consumer demand of goods; the <a href="/wiki/Keynes_effect" title="Keynes effect">Keynes or interest rate effect</a>, which states that as prices fall, the demand for money decreases, causing interest rates to decline and borrowing for investment and consumption to increase; and the net export effect, which states that as prices rise, domestic goods become comparatively more expensive to foreign consumers, leading to a decline in exports.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealey200213_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealey200213-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the conventional Keynesian use of the AS-AD model, the aggregate supply curve is horizontal at low levels of output and becomes inelastic near the point of <a href="/wiki/Potential_output" title="Potential output">potential output</a>, which corresponds with full employment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealey200212_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealey200212-11">[11]</a></sup> Since the economy cannot produce beyond the potential output, any AD expansion will lead to higher price levels instead of higher output.</p>
<p>The AD–AS diagram can model a variety of macroeconomic phenomena, including inflation. Changes in the non-price level factors or determinants cause changes in aggregate demand and shifts of the entire aggregate demand (AD) curve. When demand for goods exceeds supply there is an inflationary gap where <a href="/wiki/Demand-pull_inflation" title="Demand-pull inflation">demand-pull inflation</a> occurs and the AD curve shifts upward to a higher price level. When the economy faces higher costs, <a href="/wiki/Cost-push_inflation" title="Cost-push inflation">cost-push inflation</a> occurs and the AS curve shifts upward to higher price levels.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealey200214_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealey200214-13">[13]</a></sup> The AS–AD diagram is also widely used as a pedagogical tool to model the effects of various macroeconomic policies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColander1995173_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColander1995173-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IS.E2.80.93LM">IS–LM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: IS–LM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Islm.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Islm.svg/220px-Islm.svg.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Islm.svg/330px-Islm.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Islm.svg/440px-Islm.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Islm.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In this example of an IS/LM chart, the IS curve moves to the right, causing higher interest rates (i) and expansion in the "real" economy (real GDP, or Y).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IS%E2%80%93LM" class="mw-redirect" title="IS–LM">IS–LM</a> model represents all the combinations of interest rates and output that ensure the equilibrium in the goods and money markets.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008-15">[15]</a></sup> The goods market is represented by the equilibrium in investment and saving (IS), and the money market is represented by the equilibrium between the money supply and <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_preference" title="Liquidity preference">liquidity preference</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeston2002386-387_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeston2002386-387-16">[16]</a></sup> The IS curve consists of the points where investment, given the interest rate, is equal to savings, given output.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IS curve is downward sloping because output and interest rate have an inverse relationship in the goods market: as output increases, more money is saved, which means interest rates must be lower to spur enough investment to match savings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387-17">[17]</a></sup> The LM curve is upward sloping because interest rate and output have a positive relationship in the money market: as output increases, the demand for money increases, resulting in a rise in interest rate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387-388_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeston2002387-388-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The IS/LM model is often used to demonstrate the effects of monetary and fiscal policy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008-15">[15]</a></sup> Textbooks frequently use the IS/LM model, but it does not feature the complexities of most modern macroeconomic models.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008-15">[15]</a></sup> Nevertheless, these models still feature similar relationships to those in IS/LM.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurlaufHester2008-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_models">Growth models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Growth models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_growth_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Neoclassical growth model">neoclassical growth model</a> of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a> has become a common textbook model for explaining economic growth in the long-run.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The model begins with a <a href="/wiki/Production_function" title="Production function">production function</a> where national output is the product of two inputs: capital and labor. The Solow model assumes that labor and capital are used at constant rates without the fluctuations in unemployment and capital utilization commonly seen in business cycles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESolow2002518-519_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESolow2002518-519-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>An increase in output, or economic growth, can only occur because of an increase in the capital stock, a larger population, or technological advancements that lead to higher productivity (<a href="/wiki/Total_factor_productivity" title="Total factor productivity">total factor productivity</a>). An increase in the savings rate leads to a temporary increase as the economy creates more capital, which adds to output. However, eventually the depreciation rate will limit the expansion of capital: savings will be used up replacing depreciated capital, and no savings will remain to pay for an additional expansion in capital. Solow's model suggests that economic growth in terms of output per capita depends solely on technological advances that enhance productivity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESolow2002519_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESolow2002519-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s and 1990s <a href="/wiki/Endogenous_growth_theory" title="Endogenous growth theory">endogenous growth theory</a> arose to challenge neoclassical growth theory. This group of models explains economic growth through other factors, such as increasing returns to scale for capital and <a href="/wiki/Learning-by-doing_(economics)" title="Learning-by-doing (economics)">learning-by-doing</a>, that are endogenously determined instead of the exogenous technological improvement used to explain growth in Solow's model.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlaug2002202-203_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlaug2002202-203-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Macroeconomic_policy">Macroeconomic policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Macroeconomic policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Macroeconomic policy is usually implemented through two sets of tools: fiscal and monetary policy. Both forms of policy are used to <a href="/wiki/Stabilization_policy" title="Stabilization policy">stabilize the economy</a>, which can mean boosting the economy to the level of GDP consistent with full employment.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayer.2C_495_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayer.2C_495-22">[22]</a></sup> Macroeconomic policy focuses on limiting the effects of the business cycle to achieve the economic goals of price stability, full employment, and growth. <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetary_policy">Monetary policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Monetary policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">Monetary policy</a></div>
<p>Central banks implement monetary policy by controlling the money supply through several mechanisms. Typically, central banks take action by issuing money to buy bonds (or other assets), which boosts the supply of money and lowers interest rates, or, in the case of contractionary monetary policy, banks sell bonds and take money out of circulation. Usually policy is not implemented by directly targeting the supply of money.</p>
<p>Central banks continuously shift the money supply to maintain a targeted fixed interest rate. Some of them allow the interest rate to fluctuate and focus on <a href="/wiki/Inflation_targeting" title="Inflation targeting">targeting inflation rates</a> instead. Central banks generally try to achieve high output without letting loose monetary policy that create large amounts of inflation.</p>
<p>Conventional monetary policy can be ineffective in situations such as a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_trap" title="Liquidity trap">liquidity trap</a>. When interest rates and inflation are near zero, the central bank cannot loosen monetary policy through conventional means.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png/350px-Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png" width="350" height="263" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png/525px-Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png/700px-Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Economic_Policy_-_Intervention_Strategy_Matrix.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of intervention strategy under different conditions</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Central banks can use unconventional monetary policy such as <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_easing" title="Quantitative easing">quantitative easing</a> to help increase output. Instead of buying government bonds, central banks can implement quantitative easing by buying not only government bonds, but also other assets such as corporate bonds, stocks, and other securities. This allows lower interest rates for a broader class of assets beyond government bonds. In another example of unconventional monetary policy, the United States Federal Reserve recently made an attempt at such a policy with <a href="/wiki/Operation_Twist" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Twist">Operation Twist</a>. Unable to lower current interest rates, the Federal Reserve lowered long-term interest rates by buying long-term bonds and selling short-term bonds to create a flat <a href="/wiki/Yield_curve" title="Yield curve">yield curve</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiscal_policy">Fiscal policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fiscal policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_policy" title="Fiscal policy">Fiscal policy</a></div>
<p>Fiscal policy is the use of government's revenue and expenditure as instruments to influence the economy. Examples of such tools are <a href="/wiki/Government_spending" title="Government spending">expenditure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>.</p>
<p>For example, if the economy is producing less than potential output, government spending can be used to employ idle resources and boost output. Government spending does not have to make up for the entire output gap. There is a <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_multiplier" title="Fiscal multiplier">multiplier effect</a> that boosts the impact of government spending. For instance, when the government pays for a bridge, the project not only adds the value of the bridge to output, but also allows the bridge workers to increase their consumption and investment, which helps to close the output gap.</p>
<p>The effects of fiscal policy can be limited by <a href="/wiki/Crowding_out_(economics)" title="Crowding out (economics)">crowding out</a>. When the government takes on spending projects, it limits the amount of resources available for the private sector to use. Crowding out occurs when government spending simply replaces private sector output instead of adding additional output to the economy. Crowding out also occurs when government spending raises interest rates, which limits investment. Defenders of fiscal stimulus argue that crowding out is not a concern when the economy is depressed, plenty of resources are left idle, and interest rates are low.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Fiscal policy can be implemented through <a href="/wiki/Automatic_stabilizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Automatic stabilizers">automatic stabilizers</a>. Automatic stabilizers do not suffer from the policy lags of discretionary fiscal policy. Automatic stabilizers use conventional fiscal mechanisms but take effect as soon as the economy takes a downturn: spending on unemployment benefits automatically increases when unemployment rises and, in a progressive income tax system, the effective tax rate automatically falls when incomes decline.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison">Comparison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Comparison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Economists usually favor monetary over fiscal policy because it has two major advantages. First, monetary policy is generally implemented by independent central banks instead of the political institutions that control fiscal policy. Independent central banks are less likely to make decisions based on political motives.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayer.2C_495_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayer.2C_495-22">[22]</a></sup> Second, monetary policy suffers shorter <a href="/wiki/Inside_lag" title="Inside lag">inside lags</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outside_lag" title="Outside lag">outside lags</a> than fiscal policy. Central banks can quickly make and implement decisions while discretionary fiscal policy may take time to pass and even longer to carry out.<sup id="cite_ref-Mayer.2C_495_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mayer.2C_495-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_macroeconomic_thought" title="History of macroeconomic thought">History of macroeconomic thought</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Macroeconomics descended from the once divided fields of <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monetary_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Monetary theory">monetary theory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dimand_2008_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dimand_2008-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quantity_theory_of_money" title="Quantity theory of money">quantity theory of money</a> was particularly influential prior to World War II. It took many forms, including the version based on the work of <a href="/wiki/Irving_Fisher" title="Irving Fisher">Irving Fisher</a>:</p>

<p>In the typical view of the quantity theory, <a href="/wiki/Money_velocity" class="mw-redirect" title="Money velocity">money velocity</a> (V) and the quantity of goods produced (Q) would be constant, so any increase in <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> (M) would lead to a direct increase in price level (P). The quantity theory of money was a central part of the classical theory of the economy that prevailed in the early twentieth century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austrian_School">Austrian School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Austrian School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Von_Mises" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig Von Mises">Ludwig Von Mises</a>'s work <i><a href="/wiki/Theory_of_Money_and_Credit" class="mw-redirect" title="Theory of Money and Credit">Theory of Money and Credit</a></i>, published in 1912, was one of the first books from the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">Austrian School</a> to deal with macroeconomic topics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keynes_and_his_followers">Keynes and his followers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Keynes and his followers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Macroeconomics, at least in its modern form,<sup id="cite_ref-Blanchard_2011.2C_580_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_2011.2C_580-25">[25]</a></sup> began with the publication of <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" class="mw-redirect" title="General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dimand_2008_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dimand_2008-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Modern_macroeconomics_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_macroeconomics-26">[26]</a></sup> When the Great Depression struck, classical economists had difficulty explaining how goods could go unsold and workers could be left unemployed. In classical theory, prices and wages would drop until the market cleared, and all goods and labor were sold. Keynes offered a new theory of economics that explained why markets might not clear, which would evolve (later in the 20th century) into a group of macroeconomic schools of thought known as <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a> – also called Keynesianism or Keynesian theory.</p>
<p>In Keynes's theory, the quantity theory broke down because people and businesses tend to hold on to their cash in tough economic times–a phenomenon he described in terms of <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_preference" title="Liquidity preference">liquidity preferences</a>. Keynes also explained how the <a href="/wiki/Multiplier_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiplier effect">multiplier effect</a> would magnify a small decrease in consumption or investment and cause declines throughout the economy. Keynes also noted the role uncertainty and <a href="/wiki/Animal_spirits_(Keynes)" title="Animal spirits (Keynes)">animal spirits</a> can play in the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanchard_2011.2C_580_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_2011.2C_580-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The generation following Keynes combined the macroeconomics of the <i>General Theory</i> with neoclassical microeconomics to create the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_synthesis" title="Neoclassical synthesis">neoclassical synthesis</a>. By the 1950s, most economists had accepted the synthesis view of the macroeconomy.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanchard_2011.2C_580_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_2011.2C_580-25">[25]</a></sup> Economists like <a href="/wiki/Paul_Samuelson" title="Paul Samuelson">Paul Samuelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franco_Modigliani" title="Franco Modigliani">Franco Modigliani</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Tobin" title="James Tobin">James Tobin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a> developed formal Keynesian models and contributed formal theories of consumption, investment, and money demand that fleshed out the Keynesian framework.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetarism">Monetarism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Monetarism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a> updated the quantity theory of money to include a role for money demand. He argued that the role of money in the economy was sufficient to explain the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, and that aggregate demand oriented explanations were not necessary. Friedman also argued that monetary policy was more effective than fiscal policy; however, Friedman doubted the government's ability to "fine-tune" the economy with monetary policy. He generally favored a policy of steady growth in money supply instead of frequent intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Friedman also challenged the <a href="/wiki/Phillips_curve" title="Phillips curve">Phillips curve</a> relationship between inflation and unemployment. Friedman and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Phelps" title="Edmund Phelps">Edmund Phelps</a> (who was not a monetarist) proposed an "augmented" version of the Phillips curve that excluded the possibility of a stable, long-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When the <a href="/wiki/1970s_energy_crisis" title="1970s energy crisis">oil shocks</a> of the 1970s created a high unemployment and high inflation, Friedman and Phelps were vindicated. Monetarism was particularly influential in the early 1980s. Monetarism fell out of favor when central banks found it difficult to target money supply instead of interest rates as monetarists recommended. Monetarism also became politically unpopular when the central banks created recessions in order to slow inflation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_classical">New classical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: New classical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_classical_macroeconomics" title="New classical macroeconomics">New classical macroeconomics</a> further challenged the Keynesian school. A central development in new classical thought came when <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lucas,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Lucas, Jr.">Robert Lucas</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Rational_expectations" title="Rational expectations">rational expectations</a> to macroeconomics. Prior to Lucas, economists had generally used <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_expectations" title="Adaptive expectations">adaptive expectations</a> where agents were assumed to look at the recent past to make expectations about the future. Under rational expectations, agents are assumed to be more sophisticated. A consumer will not simply assume a 2% inflation rate just because that has been the average the past few years; she will look at current monetary policy and economic conditions to make an informed forecast. When new classical economists introduced rational expectations into their models, they showed that monetary policy could only have a limited impact.</p>
<p>Lucas also made an <a href="/wiki/Lucas_critique" title="Lucas critique">influential critique</a> of Keynesian empirical models. He argued that forecasting models based on empirical relationships would keep producing the same predictions even as the underlying model generating the data changed. He advocated models based on fundamental economic theory that would, in principle, be structurally accurate as economies changed. Following Lucas's critique, new classical economists, led by <a href="/wiki/Edward_C._Prescott" title="Edward C. Prescott">Edward C. Prescott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finn_E._Kydland" title="Finn E. Kydland">Finn E. Kydland</a>, created <a href="/wiki/Real_business_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Real business cycle">real business cycle</a> (RBC) models of the macroeconomy.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanchard_2011.2C_587_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_2011.2C_587-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>RBC models were created by combining fundamental equations from neo-classical microeconomics. In order to generate macroeconomic fluctuations, RBC models explained recessions and unemployment with changes in technology instead of changes in the markets for goods or money. Critics of RBC models argue that money clearly plays an important role in the economy, and the idea that technological regress can explain recent recessions is implausible.<sup id="cite_ref-Blanchard_2011.2C_587_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_2011.2C_587-29">[29]</a></sup> However, technological shocks are only the more prominent of a myriad of possible shocks to the system that can be modeled. Despite questions about the theory behind RBC models, they have clearly been influential in economic methodology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Keynesian_response">New Keynesian response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macroeconomics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: New Keynesian response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_Keynesian" class="mw-redirect" title="New Keynesian">New Keynesian</a> economists responded to the new classical school by adopting rational expectations and focusing on developing micro-founded models that are immune to the Lucas critique. <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Fischer" title="Stanley Fischer">Stanley Fischer</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_B._Taylor" title="John B. Taylor">John B. Taylor</a> produced early work in this area by showing that monetary policy could be effective even in models with rational expectations when contracts locked in wages for workers. Other new Keynesian economists, including <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Blanchard" title="Olivier Blanchard">Olivier Blanchard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julio_Rotemberg" title="Julio Rotemberg">Julio Rotemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Mankiw" title="Greg Mankiw">Greg Mankiw</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Romer" title="David Romer">David Romer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Woodford_(economist)" title="Michael Woodford (economist)">Michael Woodford</a>, expanded on this work and demonstrated other cases where inflexible prices and wages led to monetary and fiscal policy having real effects.</p>
<p>Like classical models, new classical models had assumed that prices would be able to adjust perfectly and monetary policy would only lead to price changes. New Keynesian models investigated sources of <a href="/wiki/Sticky_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sticky (economics)">sticky prices and wages</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Imperfect_competition" title="Imperfect competition">imperfect competition</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which would not adjust, allowing monetary policy to impact quantities instead of prices.</p>
<p>By the late 1990s economists had reached a rough consensus. The nominal rigidity of new Keynesian theory was combined with rational expectations and the RBC methodology to produce <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_stochastic_general_equilibrium" title="Dynamic stochastic general equilibrium">dynamic stochastic general equilibrium</a> (DSGE) models. The fusion of elements from different schools of thought has been dubbed the <a href="/wiki/New_neoclassical_synthesis" title="New neoclassical synthesis">new neoclassical synthesis</a>. These models are now used by many central banks and are a core part of contemporary macroeconomics.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>New Keynesian economics, which developed partly in response to new classical economics, strives to provide microeconomic foundations to Keynesian economics by showing how imperfect markets can justify demand management.</p>
