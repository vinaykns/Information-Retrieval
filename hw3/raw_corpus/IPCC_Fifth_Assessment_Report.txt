<p>The <b>Fifth Assessment Report</b> (<b>AR5</b>) of the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) is the fifth in <a href="/wiki/IPCC#Assessment_reports" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">a series of such reports</a>. The IPCC was established in 1988 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) to assess <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific</a>, technical and <a href="/wiki/Socio-economic" class="mw-redirect" title="Socio-economic">socio-economic</a> information concerning <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, its potential effects and options for adaptation and mitigation.</p>
<p>The Fifth Assessment Report was finalized in 2014. As had been the case in the past, the outline of the AR5 was developed through a scoping process which involved climate change experts from all relevant disciplines and users of IPCC reports; in particular representatives from governments. Governments and organizations involved in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Report</a> were asked to submit comments and observations in writing with the submissions analysed by the panel.<sup id="cite_ref-Website_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Website-1">[1]</a></sup> The report was delivered in stages, starting with Working Group I's report on the physical science basis, based on 9,200 peer-reviewed studies.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guard_numbers_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guard_numbers-3">[3]</a></sup> The summaries for policy makers were released on 27 September 2013 for the first report,<sup id="cite_ref-SfP2013_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SfP2013-4">[4]</a></sup> on 31 March 2014 for the second report entitled "Impacts, Adaptation, and Vulnerability",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and on 14 April 2014 for the third report entitled "Mitigation of Climate Change".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Synthesis Report was released on 2 November 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in time to pave the way for negotiations on reducing carbon emissions at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">UN Climate Change Conference</a> <a href="/wiki/COP21" class="mw-redirect" title="COP21">in Paris during late 2015</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Fifth Assessment Report (AR5) consists of three Working Group (WG) Reports and a Synthesis Report. The first Working Group Report was published in 2013 and the rest were completed in 2014.</p>
<ul>
<li>WG I: The Physical Science Basis – 30 September 2013, <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Summary_for_Policymakers" title="IPCC Summary for Policymakers">Summary for Policymakers</a> published 27 September 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-SfP2013_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SfP2013-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>WG II: Impacts, Adaptation and Vulnerability – 31 March 2014</li>
<li>WG III: Mitigation of Climate Change – 15 April 2014</li>
<li>AR5 Synthesis Report (SYR) – 2 November 2014</li>
</ul>
<p>The AR5 provides an update of knowledge on the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific</a>, technical and <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming" title="Economics of global warming">socio-economic</a> aspects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a>.</p>
<p>More than 800 authors, selected from around 3,000 nominations, were involved in writing the report. Lead authors' meetings and a number of workshops and expert meetings, in support of the assessment process, were held. A schedule of AR5 related meetings, review periods, and other important dates was published.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 14 December 2012, drafts of the Working Group 1 (WG1) report were leaked and posted on the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-revkin_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revkin-9">[9]</a></sup> The release of the summary for policymakers occurred on 27 September 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-SfP2013_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SfP2013-4">[4]</a></sup> Halldór Thorgeirsson, a UN official, warned that, because big companies are known to fund <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">the undermining of climate science</a>, scientists should be prepared for an increase in negative publicity at the time. "Vested interests are paying for the discrediting of scientists all the time. We need to be ready for that," he said.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marking the finalization of the <i>Physical Science Basis</i> UN Secretary General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> addressed the IPCC at Stockholm on 27 September 2013. He stated that "the heat is on. We must act". Jennifer Morgan, from the <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a>, said "Hopefully the IPCC will inspire leadership, from the Mom to the business leader, to the mayor to the head of state."<sup id="cite_ref-doyle-2013_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doyle-2013-11">[11]</a></sup> US Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> responded to the report saying "This is yet another wakeup call: those who deny the science or choose excuses over action are playing with fire."<sup id="cite_ref-Guard-BanKiMoon_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guard-BanKiMoon-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Authors_and_editors">Authors and editors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Authors and editors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2010, the IPCC received approximately 3,000 author nominations from experts around the world. At the bureau session held in Geneva, 19–20 May 2010, the three working groups presented their selected authors and review editors for the AR5. Each of the selected scientists, specialists and experts was nominated in accordance with IPCC procedures, by respective national IPCC focal-points, by approved observer organizations, or by the bureau. The IPCC received 50% more nominations of experts to participate in AR5 than it did for <a href="/wiki/AR4" class="mw-redirect" title="AR4">AR4</a>. A total of 559 authors and review editors had been selected for AR4 from 2,000 proposed nominees. On 23 June 2010 the IPCC announced the release of the final list of selected coordinating lead authors, comprising 831 experts who were drawn from fields including <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, physics, oceanography, statistics, engineering, ecology, social sciences and economics. In comparison to the Fourth Assessment Report (AR4), participation from developing countries was increased, reflecting the ongoing efforts to improve regional coverage in the AR5. About 30% of authors came from developing countries or economies in transition. More than 60% of the experts chosen were new to the IPCC process, bringing fresh knowledge and perspectives.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_2013:_report_overview">Climate change 2013: report overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate change 2013: report overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 23 June 2010, the IPCC announced the release of the final list of selected coordinating lead authors, comprising 831 experts. The working group reports would be published during 2013 and 2014. These experts would also provide contributions to the Synthesis Report published in late 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Website_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Website-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Fifth Assessment Report (<i>Climate Change 2013</i>) would be released in four distinct sections:</p>
<ul>
<li>Working Group I Report (WGI): Focusing on the physical science basis and including 258 experts.</li>
<li>Working Group II Report (WGII): Assessing the impacts, adaptation strategies and vulnerability related to climate change and involving 302 experts.</li>
<li>Working Group III Report (WGIII): Covering mitigation response strategies in an integrated risk and uncertainty framework and its assessments carried out by 271 experts.</li>
<li>The Synthesis Report (SYR): Final summary and overview.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_group_I_contribution">Working group I contribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Working group I contribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The full text of <i>Climate Change 2013: The Physical Science Basis</i> was released in an unedited form on Monday, 30 September 2013. It was over 2000 pages long and cited 9200 scientific publications.<sup id="cite_ref-Guard_numbers_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guard_numbers-3">[3]</a></sup> The full, edited report was released online in January 2014 and published in physical form by <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a> later in the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Press_release_1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Press_release_1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_for_Policymakers">Summary for Policymakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Summary for Policymakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A concise overview of Working Group I's findings was published as the <i>Summary for Policymakers</i> on 27 September 2013. The level of confidence in each finding was rated on a confidence scale, qualitatively from <i>very low</i> to <i>very high</i> and, where possible, quantitatively from <i>exceptionally unlikely</i> to <i>virtually certain</i> (determined based on statistical analysis and expert judgement).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The principal findings were:<sup id="cite_ref-SfP2013_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SfP2013-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Warming of the atmosphere and ocean system is <i>unequivocal</i>. Many of the associated impacts such as sea level change (among other metrics) have occurred since 1950 at rates unprecedented in the historical record.</li>
<li>There is a clear human influence on the climate</li>
<li>It is <i>extremely likely</i> that human influence has been the dominant cause of observed warming since 1950, with the level of confidence having increased since the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">fourth report</a>.</li>
<li>IPCC pointed out that the longer we wait to reduce our emissions, the more expensive it will become.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_climate_metrics">Historical climate metrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historical climate metrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li>It is <i>likely</i> (with medium confidence) that 1983–2013 was the warmest 30-year period for 1400 years.</li>
<li>It is <i>virtually certain</i> the upper ocean warmed from 1971 to 2010. This ocean warming accounts, with <i>high confidence</i>, for 90% of the energy accumulation between 1971 and 2010.</li>
<li>It can be said with <i>high confidence</i> that the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic</a> ice sheets have been losing mass in the last two decades and that <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice</a> and Northern Hemisphere spring snow cover have continued to decrease in extent.</li>
<li>There is <i>high confidence</i> that the sea level rise since the middle of the 19th century has been larger than the mean sea level rise of the prior two millennia.</li>
<li>Concentration of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> in the atmosphere has increased to levels unprecedented on earth in 800,000 years.</li>
<li>Total <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> of the earth system, relative to 1750, is positive and the most significant driver is the increase in CO<sub>2</sub>'s atmospheric concentration.</li>
</ul>

<p>AR5 relies on the <a href="/wiki/Coupled_model_intercomparison_project" title="Coupled model intercomparison project">Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 5</a> (CMIP5), an international effort among the <a href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Global climate model">climate modeling</a> community to coordinate climate change experiments.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Most of the CMIP5 and Earth System Model (ESM) simulations for AR5 WRI were performed with prescribed CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations reaching 421 ppm (<a href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">RCP</a>2.6), 538 ppm (RCP4.5), 670 ppm (RCP6.0), and 936 ppm (RCP 8.5) by the year 2100. (IPCC AR5 WGI, page 22).</p>
<ul>
<li>Climate models have improved since the prior report.</li>
<li>Model results, along with observations, provide confidence in the magnitude of global warming in response to past and future forcing.</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections">Projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li>Further warming will continue if emissions of greenhouse gases continue.</li>
<li>The global surface temperature increase by the end of the 21st century is <i>likely</i> to exceed 1.5&#160;°C relative to the 1850 to 1900 period for most scenarios, and is <i>likely</i> to exceed 2.0&#160;°C for many scenarios</li>
<li>The global water cycle will change, with increases in disparity between wet and dry regions, as well as wet and dry seasons, with some regional exceptions.</li>
<li>The oceans will continue to warm, with heat extending to the deep ocean, affecting circulation patterns.</li>
<li>Decreases are <i>very likely</i> in Arctic sea ice cover, Northern Hemisphere spring snow cover, and global glacier volume</li>
<li>Global mean sea level will continue to rise at a rate <i>very likely</i> to exceed the rate of the past four decades</li>
<li>Changes in climate will cause an increase in the rate of CO<sub>2</sub> production. Increased uptake by the oceans will increase the acidification of the oceans.</li>
<li>Future surface temperatures will be largely determined by cumulative CO<sub>2</sub>, which means climate change will continue even if CO<sub>2</sub> emissions are stopped.</li>
</ul>
<p>The summary also detailed the range of forecasts for warming, and climate impacts with different emission scenarios. Compared to the previous report, the lower bounds for the sensitivity of the climate system to emissions were slightly lowered, though the projections for global mean temperature rise (compared to pre-industrial levels) by 2100 exceeded 1.5&#160;°C in all scenarios.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_27Sep_1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_27Sep_1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 'Next Generation Earth System Models' (e.g. <a href="/wiki/HadGEM2" class="mw-redirect" title="HadGEM2">HadGEM2</a>) can produce hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Terabyte" title="Terabyte">terabytes</a> to perhaps tens of <a href="/wiki/Petabyte" title="Petabyte">petabytes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate model</a> data for analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Climate model simulations in support of AR5 use a different approach to account for increasing greenhouse gas concentrations than in the previous report. Instead of the scenarios from the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">Special Report on Emissions Scenarios</a> the models are performing simulations for various <a href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">Representative Concentration Pathways</a>.</p>
<p>Public debate after the publication of AR4 in 2009 put the IPCC under scrutiny, with controversies over alleged bias and inaccuracy in its reports. In 2010, this prompted U.N. Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> and IPCC chair <a href="/wiki/Rajendra_K._Pachauri" title="Rajendra K. Pachauri">Rajendra K. Pachauri</a> to request that the <a href="/wiki/InterAcademy_Panel" title="InterAcademy Panel">InterAcademy Council</a> (IAC) review the IPCC and recommend ways to strengthen its processes and procedures for the preparation of AR5. The IAC report made recommendations to fortify IPCC's management structure, to further develop its conflict-of-interest policy, to strengthen the review process, to clarify the guidelines on the use of so-called <a href="/wiki/Grey_literature" title="Grey literature">gray literature</a>, to ensure consistency in the use of probabilities for the likelihood of outcomes, and to improve its communications strategy especially regarding transparency and rapidity of response.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
