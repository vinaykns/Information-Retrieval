			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Resource</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Resources&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Resources">Resources</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Resource_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Resource (disambiguation)">Resource (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>resource</b> is a source or supply from which benefit is produced. Resources are two types based upon their availability they are renewable and non renewable resources. Typically resources are materials, energy, services, staff, knowledge, or other assets that are transformed to produce benefit and in the process may be consumed or made unavailable. Benefits of resource utilization may include increased wealth or wants, proper functioning of a system, or enhanced well being. From a <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> perspective a <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> is anything obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> to satisfy human needs and wants.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-1">[1]</a></sup> From a broader biological or ecological perspective a resource satisfies the needs of a living organism (see <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">biological resource</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ricklefs_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ricklefs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The concept of resources has been applied in diverse realms, with respect to <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a>, and is linked to the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a>. In application within human <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>, commercial or non-commercial factors require <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">resource allocation</a> through <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">resource management</a>.</p>
<p>Resources have three main characteristics: <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">limited availability</a>, and potential for <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depletion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>. Resources have been variously categorized as <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic</a> versus <a href="/wiki/Abiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable</a> versus <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable</a>, and potential versus actual, along with more elaborate classification.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic">Economic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Economic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> a resource is defined as a service or other asset used to produce goods and services that meet human needs and wants.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Economics itself has been defined as the study of how society manages its scarce resources.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">Classical economics</a> recognizes three categories of resources, also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Factors_of_production" title="Factors of production">factors of production</a>: land, labour, and capital.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-5">[5]</a></sup> Land includes all <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> and is viewed as both the site of production and the source of <a href="/wiki/Raw_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Raw materials">raw materials</a>. Labour or <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a> consists of human effort provided in the creation of products, paid in <a href="/wiki/Wage" title="Wage">wage</a>. Capital consists of human-made goods or means of production (machinery, buildings, and other <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>) used in the production of other goods and services, paid in <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological">Biological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In biology and ecology a resource is defined as a substance that is required by a living organism for normal <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maintenance_of_an_organism" title="Maintenance of an organism">maintenance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">biological resource</a>). The main essential resources for animals are food, water, and territory. For plants key resources include light, nutrients, water, and a place to grow.<sup id="cite_ref-Ricklefs_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ricklefs-2">[2]</a></sup> Resources,can be consumed by an organism and, as a result, become unavailable to other organisms. Competition for resources vary from <a href="/wiki/Size-asymmetric_competition" title="Size-asymmetric competition">complete symmetric</a> (all individuals receive the same amount of resources, irrespective of their size) to <a href="/wiki/Size-asymmetric_competition" title="Size-asymmetric competition">perfectly size symmetric</a> (all individuals exploit the same amount of resource per unit biomass) to absolutely <a href="/wiki/Size-asymmetric_competition" title="Size-asymmetric competition">size-asymmetric</a> (the largest individuals exploit all the available resource). The degree of size asymmetry has major effects on the structure and diversity of ecological communities, e.g. in plant communities size-asymmetric competition for light has stronger effects on diversity compared with competition for soil resources.The degree of size asymmetry has major effects on the structure and diversity of ecological communities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_versus_biological">Economic versus biological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic versus biological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three fundamental differences between economic versus ecological views: 1) the economic resource definition is human-centered (<a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentric</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Biological_resource" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological resource">biological or ecological resource</a> definition is nature-centered (<a href="/wiki/Biocentrism_(ethics)" title="Biocentrism (ethics)">biocentric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ecocentric" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecocentric">ecocentric</a>); 2) the economic view includes desire along with necessity, whereas the biological view is about basic biological needs; and 3) economic systems are based on markets of currency exchanged for goods and services, whereas biological systems are based on natural processes of growth, maintenance, and reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_resources">Computer resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Computer resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resource_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource (computer science)">Resource (computer science)</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Resource_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource (computer science)">computer resource</a> is any physically or virtual component of limited availability within a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Information_management" title="Information management">information management</a> system. Computer resources include means for input, processing, output, communication, and storage.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural">Natural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">Natural resources</a> are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. Many natural resources are essential for human survival, while others are used for satisfying human desire. Conservation is the management of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> with the goal of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. Natural resources may be further classified in different ways.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Resources can be categorized on the basis of origin:</p>
<ul>
<li>Abiotic resources comprise non-living things (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Land_(economics)" title="Land (economics)">land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">minerals</a> such as gold, iron, copper, silver).</li>
<li>Biotic resources are obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Forests and their products, animals, birds and their products, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and other marine organisms are important examples. <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">Minerals</a> such as coal and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> are sometimes included in this category because they were formed from fossilized organic matter, though over long periods of time.</li>
</ul>
<p>Natural resources are also categorized based on the stage of development:</p>
<ul>
<li>Potential resources are known to exist and may be used in the future. For example, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> may exist in many parts of India and Kuwait that have sedimentary rocks, but until the time it is actually drilled out and put into use, it remains a potential resource.</li>
<li>Actual resources are those that have been surveyed, their quantity and quality determined, and are being used in present times. For example, petroleum and natural gas is actively being obtained from the Mumbai High Fields. The development of an actual resource, such as <a href="/wiki/Wood_processing" title="Wood processing">wood processing</a> depends upon the technology available and the <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> involved. That part of the actual resource that can be developed profitably with available technology is called a reserve resource, while that part that can not be developed profitably because of lack of technology is called a stock resource.</li>
</ul>
<p>Natural resources can be categorized on the basis of renewability:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-renewable resources">Non-renewable resources</a> are formed over very long <a href="/wiki/Geological_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Geological period">geological periods</a>. Minerals and fossils are included in this category. Since their rate of formation is extremely slow, they cannot be replenished, once they are <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depleted</a>. Out of these, the metallic minerals can be re-used by recycling them, but coal and petroleum cannot be recycled.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Renewable_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable resources">Renewable resources</a>, such as forests and fisheries, can be replenished or reproduced relatively quickly. The highest rate at which a resource can be used sustainably is the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_yield" title="Sustainable yield">sustainable yield</a>. Some resources, like sunlight, air, and wind, are called perpetual resources because they are available continuously, though at a limited rate. Their quantity is not affected by human consumption. Many renewable resources can be depleted by human use, but may also be replenished, thus maintaining a flow. Some of these, like agricultural crops, take a short time for renewal; others, like water, take a comparatively longer time, while still others, like forests, take even longer.</li>
</ul>
<p>Dependent upon the speed and quantity of consumption, overconsumption can lead to depletion or total and everlasting destruction of a resource. Important examples are agricultural areas, fish and other animals, forests, healthy water and soil, cultivated and natural landscapes. Such conditionally renewable resources are sometimes classified as a third kind of resource, or as a subtype of renewable resources. Conditionally renewable resources are presently subject to excess human consumption and the only sustainable long term use of such resources is within the so-called <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">zero ecological footprint</a>, wherein human use less than the Earth's ecological capacity to regenerate.</p>
<p>Natural resources are also categorized based on distribution:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ubiquitous resources are found everywhere (e.g., air, light, water).</li>
<li>Localized resources are found only in certain parts of the world (e.g., copper and iron ore, geothermal power).</li>
</ul>
<p>Actual vs. potential natural resources are distinguished as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Actual resources are those resources whose location and quantity are known and we have the technology to exploit and use them.</li>
<li>Potential resources are the ones of which we have insufficient knowledge or we do not have the technology to exploit them at present.</li>
</ul>
<p>On the basis of ownership, resources can be classified as individual, community, national, and international.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_or_human_resources">Labour or human resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Labour or human resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In economics, labour or <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a> refers to the human effort in production of goods and rendering of services. Human resources can be defined in terms of skills, energy, talent, abilities, or knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a> context, human resources are those employees responsible for undertaking the activities defined in the project plan.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_or_infrastructure">Capital or infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Capital or infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Social_studies" title="Social studies">social studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> refers to already-produced <a href="/wiki/Durable_good" title="Durable good">durable goods</a> used in production of <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a>. In essence, capital refers to human-made resources created using knowledge and expertise based on utility or perceived value. Common examples of capital include buildings, machinery, railways, roads, and ships. As resources, capital goods may or may not be significantly consumed, though they may <a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">depreciate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Production_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Production process">production process</a> and they are typically of limited capacity or unavailable for use by others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tangible_versus_intangible">Tangible versus intangible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tangible versus intangible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whereas, tangible resources such as equipment have actual physical existence, intangible resources such as corporate images, brands and patents, and other intellectual property exist in abstraction.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Generally the economic value of a resource is controlled by supply and demand. Some view this as a narrow perspective on resources because there are many intangibles that cannot be measured in money. Natural resources such as forests and mountains have aesthetic value. Resources also have an ethical value.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_sustainable_development">Use and sustainable development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use and sustainable development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typically resources cannot be consumed in their original form, but rather through <b>resource development</b> they must be processed into more usable commodities and usable things. With increasing population, the demand for resources is increasing. There are marked differences in <a href="/wiki/Resource_distribution" title="Resource distribution">resource distribution</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">economic inequality</a> between regions or countries, with developed countries using more natural resources than developing countries. <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a> is a pattern of resource use, that aims to meet human needs while preserving the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-1">[1]</a></sup> Sustainable development means that we should exploit our resources carefully to meet our present requirement without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. The practice of the three R's - reduce, reuse and recycle must be followed in order to save and extend the availability of resources.</p>
<p>Various problems relate to the usage of resources:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">Environmental degradation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Over-consumption" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-consumption">Over-consumption</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Resource_curse" title="Resource curse">Resource curse</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">Resource depletion</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">Tragedy of the commons</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Myth_of_superabundance" title="Myth of superabundance">Myth of superabundance</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Various benefits can result from the wise usage of resources:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">Economic growth</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">Ethical consumerism</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prosperity" title="Prosperity">Prosperity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">Quality of life</a></li>

</ul>
