			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Electrolysis of water</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_electrolysis&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Water electrolysis">Water electrolysis</a>)</span></div>

Simple setup for demonstration of electrolysis of water at home</div>

<p><b>Electrolysis of water</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">decomposition of water</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O) into <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas (H<sub>2</sub>) due to an <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electric current</a> being passed through the water. The reaction has a standard potential of −1.23 V, meaning it ideally requires a potential difference of 1.23 volts to split water.</p>
<p>This technique can be used to make <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel" title="Hydrogen fuel">hydrogen fuel</a> (hydrogen gas) and breathable oxygen; though currently most industrial methods make hydrogen fuel from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> instead.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:164px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ritter_Wasserelektrolyse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/Ritter_Wasserelektrolyse.jpg" width="162" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="162" data-file-height="188" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ritter_Wasserelektrolyse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Device invented by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm_Ritter" title="Johann Wilhelm Ritter">Johann Wilhelm Ritter</a> to develop the electrolysis of water</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Jan Rudolph Deiman and Adriaan Paets van Troostwijk used, in 1789, an electrostatic machine to produce electricity which was discharged on gold electrodes in a <a href="/wiki/Leyden_jar" title="Leyden jar">Leyden jar</a> with water.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1800 <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Volta" title="Alessandro Volta">Alessandro Volta</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a>, and a few weeks later <a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(chemist)" title="William Nicholson (chemist)">William Nicholson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Carlisle" title="Anthony Carlisle">Anthony Carlisle</a> used it for the electrolysis of water. When <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%A9nobe_Gramme" title="Zénobe Gramme">Zénobe Gramme</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Gramme_machine" title="Gramme machine">Gramme machine</a> in 1869 electrolysis of water became a cheap method for the production of hydrogen. A method of industrial synthesis of hydrogen and oxygen through electrolysis was developed by <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Lachinov" title="Dmitry Lachinov">Dmitry Lachinov</a> in 1888.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle">Principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A DC electrical power source is connected to two <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>, or two plates (typically made from some inert metal such as <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>) which are placed in the water. Hydrogen will appear at the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> (where <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> enter the water), and oxygen will appear at the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Assuming ideal <a href="/wiki/Faradaic_efficiency" class="mw-redirect" title="Faradaic efficiency">faradaic efficiency</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount</a> of hydrogen generated is twice the amount of oxygen, and both are <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">proportional</a> to the total <a href="/wiki/Electrical_charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical charge">electrical charge</a> conducted by the solution.<sup id="cite_ref-carmo2013a_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carmo2013a-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in many cells <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis#Competing_half-reactions_in_solution_electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">competing side reactions</a> occur, resulting in different products and less than ideal faradaic efficiency.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">Electrolysis</a> of <i>pure</i> water requires excess energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> to overcome various activation barriers. Without the excess energy the electrolysis of <i>pure</i> water occurs very slowly or not at all. This is in part due to the limited <a href="/wiki/Self-ionization_of_water" title="Self-ionization of water">self-ionization of water</a>. Pure water has an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> about one millionth that of seawater. Many <a href="/wiki/Electrolytic_cell" title="Electrolytic cell">electrolytic cells</a> may also lack the requisite <a href="/wiki/Electrocatalyst" title="Electrocatalyst">electrocatalysts</a>. The efficiency of electrolysis is increased through the addition of an <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> (such as a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>) and the use of <a href="/wiki/Electrocatalyst" title="Electrocatalyst">electrocatalysts</a>.</p>
<p>Currently the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolytic process</a> is rarely used in industrial applications since hydrogen can currently be produced more affordably from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equations">Equations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Equations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Electrolysis_of_Water.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Electrolysis_of_Water.png/300px-Electrolysis_of_Water.png" width="300" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Electrolysis_of_Water.png/450px-Electrolysis_of_Water.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Electrolysis_of_Water.png/600px-Electrolysis_of_Water.png 2x" data-file-width="855" data-file-height="498" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Electrolysis_of_Water.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram showing the overall <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equation" title="Chemical equation">chemical equation</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In pure water at the negatively charged cathode, a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> reaction takes place, with electrons (e<sup>−</sup>) from the cathode being given to hydrogen cations to form hydrogen gas. The <a href="/wiki/Half_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Half reaction">half reaction</a>, balanced with acid, is:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Reduction at cathode: 2 H<sup>+</sup>(<i><a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aq</a></i>) + 2e<sup>−</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>(<i><a href="/wiki/Gaseous" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaseous">g</a></i>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>At the positively charged anode, an <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> reaction occurs, generating oxygen gas and giving electrons to the anode to complete the circuit:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Oxidation at anode: 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i><a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">l</a></i>) → O<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + 4 H<sup>+</sup>(<i>aq</i>) + 4e<sup>−</sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The same half reactions can also be balanced with base as listed below. Not all half reactions must be balanced with acid or base. Many do, like the oxidation or reduction of water listed here. To add half reactions they must both be balanced with either acid or base.</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>Cathode (reduction):</td>
<td>2 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i>l</i>) + 2e<sup>−</sup></td>
<td>→</td>
<td>H<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + 2 OH<sup>−</sup>(<i>aq</i>)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Anode (oxidation):</td>
<td>4 OH<sup>−</sup>(<i>aq</i>)</td>
<td>→</td>
<td>O<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i>l</i>) + 4 e<sup>−</sup></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Combining either half reaction pair yields the same overall decomposition of water into oxygen and hydrogen:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Overall reaction: 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i>l</i>) → 2 H<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + O<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>The number of hydrogen molecules produced is thus twice the number of oxygen molecules. Assuming equal temperature and pressure for both gases, the produced hydrogen gas has therefore twice the volume of the produced oxygen gas. The number of electrons pushed through the water is twice the number of generated hydrogen molecules and four times the number of generated oxygen molecules.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Decomposition of pure water into hydrogen and oxygen at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> is not favorable in <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermodynamic</a> terms.</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>Anode (oxidation):</td>
<td>2 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i>l</i>)</td>
<td>→</td>
<td>O<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + 4 H<sup>+</sup>(<i>aq</i>) + 4e<sup>−</sup></td>
<td>&#160;&#160;</td>
<td>E<sup>o</sup> = +1.23 V<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Cathode (reduction):</td>
<td>2 H<sup>+</sup>(<i>aq</i>) + 2e<sup>−</sup></td>
<td>→</td>
<td>H<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>)</td>
<td></td>
<td>E<sup>o</sup> = 0.00 V</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Thus, the standard potential of the water electrolysis cell (E<sup>o</sup><sub>cell</sub> = E<sup>o</sup><sub>cathode</sub> − E<sup>o</sup><sub>anode</sub>) is −1.23 V at 25&#160;°C at pH 0 ([H<sup>+</sup>] = 1.0 M). At 25&#160;°C with pH 7 ([H<sup>+</sup>] = 1.0<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−7</sup> M), the potential is unchanged based on the <a href="/wiki/Nernst_equation" title="Nernst equation">Nernst equation</a>. The thermodynamic standard cell potential can be obtained from standard-state free energy calculations to find ΔG° and then using the equation: ΔG°= -nFE°(where E° is the cell potential). In practice when an electrochemical cell is "driven" toward completion by applying reasonable potential, it is kinetically controlled. Therefore, activation energy, ion mobility (diffusion) and concentration, wire resistance, surface hindrance including bubble formation (causes electrode area blockage), and entropy, require a greater applied potential to overcome these factors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolyte_selection">Electrolyte selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electrolyte selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elektrolyse1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Elektrolyse1.jpg/220px-Elektrolyse1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Elektrolyse1.jpg/330px-Elektrolyse1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/78/Elektrolyse1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="270" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elektrolyse1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hoffman_voltameter" class="mw-redirect" title="Hoffman voltameter">Hoffman voltameter</a> connected to a <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> <a href="/wiki/Switched-mode_power_supply" title="Switched-mode power supply">power supply</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>If the above described processes occur in pure water, H<sup>+</sup> <a href="/wiki/Cation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cation">cations</a> will accumulate at the cathode and OH<sup>−</sup> <a href="/wiki/Anion" class="mw-redirect" title="Anion">anions</a> will accumulate at the anode. This can be verified by adding a <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">pH indicator</a> to the water: the water near the cathode is acidic while the water near the anode is basic. The negative hydroxide ions that approach the anode mostly combine with the positive hydronium ions (H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>) to form water. The positive hydronium ions that approach the cathode mostly combine with negative hydroxide ions to form water. Relatively few hydronium/hydroxide ions reach the cathode/anode. This can cause a concentration overpotential at both electrodes.</p>
<p>Pure water is a fairly good insulator since it has a low <a href="/wiki/Self-ionization_of_water" title="Self-ionization of water">autoionization</a>, K<sub>w</sub> = 1.0×10<sup>−14</sup> at room temperature and thus pure water conducts current poorly, 0.055 µS·cm<sup>−1</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Unless a very large potential is applied to cause an increase in the autoionization of water the electrolysis of pure water proceeds very slowly limited by the overall conductivity.</p>
<p>If a water-soluble electrolyte is added, the conductivity of the water rises considerably. The electrolyte disassociates into <a href="/wiki/Cation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cation">cations</a> and anions; the anions rush towards the anode and neutralize the buildup of positively charged H<sup>+</sup> there; similarly, the cations rush towards the cathode and neutralize the buildup of negatively charged OH<sup>−</sup> there. This allows the continued flow of electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Care must be taken in choosing an electrolyte, since an anion from the electrolyte is in competition with the hydroxide ions to give up an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. An electrolyte anion with less <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a> than hydroxide will be oxidized instead of the hydroxide, and no oxygen gas will be produced. A <a href="/wiki/Cation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cation">cation</a> with a greater <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a> than a hydrogen ion will be reduced in its stead, and no hydrogen gas will be produced.</p>
<p>The following <a href="/wiki/Cations" class="mw-redirect" title="Cations">cations</a> have lower <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">electrode potential</a> than H<sup>+</sup> and are therefore suitable for use as electrolyte cations: <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">Li</a><sup>+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">Rb</a><sup>+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K</a><sup>+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">Cs</a><sup>+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">Ba</a><sup>2+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">Sr</a><sup>2+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a><sup>2+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a><sup>+</sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><sup>2+</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> are frequently used, as they form inexpensive, soluble salts.</p>
<p>If an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> is used as the electrolyte, the cation is H<sup>+</sup>, and there is no competitor for the H<sup>+</sup> created by disassociating water. The most commonly used anion is <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">2−<br />
4</span></span>), as it is very difficult to oxidize, with the standard potential for oxidation of this ion to the <a href="/wiki/Peroxydisulfate" title="Peroxydisulfate">peroxydisulfate</a> ion being −2.05 volts.</p>
<p>Strong acids such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>), and strong bases such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> (KOH), and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (NaOH) are frequently used as electrolytes due to their strong conducting abilities.</p>
<p>A solid polymer electrolyte can also be used such as <a href="/wiki/Nafion" title="Nafion">Nafion</a> and when applied with a special catalyst on each side of the membrane can efficiently split the water molecule with as little as 1.5 Volts. There are also a number of other solid electrolyte systems that have been trialled and developed with a number of electrolysis systems now available commercially that use solid electrolytes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">Water splitting</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_demonstration">Fundamental demonstration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fundamental demonstration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Wire" title="Wire">leads</a>, running from the terminals of a battery, are placed in a cup of water with a quantity of electrolyte to establish conductivity in the solution. Using NaCl (table salt) in an electrolyte solution results in <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas rather than oxygen due to a <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis#Competing_half-reactions_in_solution_electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">competing half-reaction</a>. With the correct electrodes and correct electrolyte, such as baking soda (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>), hydrogen and oxygen gases will stream from the oppositely charged <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>. Oxygen will collect at the positively charged electrode (<a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>) and hydrogen will collect at the negatively charged electrode (<a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>). Note that hydrogen is positively charged in the H<sub>2</sub>O molecule, so it ends up at the negative electrode. (And vice versa for oxygen.)</p>
<p>Note that an aqueous solution of water with chloride ions, when electrolysed, will result in either OH<sup>−</sup> if the concentration of Cl<sup>−</sup> is low, or in chlorine gas being preferentially discharged if the concentration of Cl<sup>−</sup> is greater than 25% by mass in the solution.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hofmann_voltameter">Hofmann voltameter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hofmann voltameter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_voltameter" title="Hofmann voltameter">Hofmann voltameter</a></div>
<p>The Hofmann voltameter is often used as a small-scale electrolytic cell. It consists of three joined upright cylinders. The inner cylinder is open at the top to allow the addition of water and the electrolyte. A <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> electrode is placed at the bottom of each of the two side cylinders, connected to the positive and negative terminals of a source of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. When current is run through the Hofmann voltameter, gaseous oxygen forms at the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> (positive) and gaseous hydrogen at the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> (negative). Each gas displaces water and collects at the top of the two outer tubes, where it can be drawn off with a stopcock.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial">Industrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Industrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many industrial electrolysis cells are very similar to <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_voltameter" title="Hofmann voltameter">Hofmann voltameters</a>, with complex platinum plates or honeycombs as electrodes. Generally the only time hydrogen is intentionally produced from electrolysis is for specific point of use application such as is the case with <a href="/wiki/Oxyhydrogen" title="Oxyhydrogen">oxyhydrogen</a> torches or when extremely <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_purity" title="Hydrogen purity">high purity hydrogen</a> or oxygen is desired. The vast majority of hydrogen is produced from hydrocarbons and as a result contains trace amounts of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> among other impurities. The carbon monoxide impurity can be detrimental to various systems including many <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_pressure">High pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: High pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_electrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure electrolysis">High pressure electrolysis</a></div>
<p>High pressure electrolysis is the electrolysis of water with a <a href="/wiki/Compressed_hydrogen" title="Compressed hydrogen">compressed hydrogen</a> output around 120–200 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">Bar</a> (1740–2900 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By pressurising the hydrogen in the electrolyser, the need for an external <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_compressor" title="Hydrogen compressor">hydrogen compressor</a> is eliminated; the average energy consumption for internal compression is around 3%.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-temperature">High-temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: High-temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_electrolysis" title="High-temperature electrolysis">High-temperature electrolysis</a></div>
<p>High-temperature electrolysis (also HTE or steam electrolysis) is a method currently being investigated for water electrolysis with a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>. High temperature electrolysis may be preferable to traditional room-temperature electrolysis because some of the energy is supplied as heat, which is cheaper than electricity, and because the electrolysis reaction is more efficient at higher temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkaline_water">Alkaline water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alkaline water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_water_electrolysis" title="Alkaline water electrolysis">Alkaline water electrolysis</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_electrolyte_membrane">Polymer electrolyte membrane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Polymer electrolyte membrane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polymer_electrolyte_membrane_electrolysis" title="Polymer electrolyte membrane electrolysis">Polymer electrolyte membrane electrolysis</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickel.2Firon">Nickel/iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nickel/iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, researchers announced an electrolysis system made of inexpensive, abundant nickel and iron rather than precious metal catalysts, such as platinum or iridium. The nickel-metal/nickel-oxide structure is more active than pure nickel metal or pure nickel oxide alone. The catalyst significantly lowers the required <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-r1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About five percent of hydrogen gas produced worldwide is created by electrolysis. The majority of this hydrogen produced through electrolysis is a side product in the production of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine#Production" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caustic_soda" class="mw-redirect" title="Caustic soda">caustic soda</a>. This is a prime example of a <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis#Competing_half-reactions_in_solution_electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">competing side reaction</a>.</p>
<dl>
<dd>2NaCl + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → Cl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> + 2NaOH</dd>
</dl>
<p>The electrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> (saltwater), a water sodium chloride mixture, is only half the electrolysis of water since the <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> ions are oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> rather than water being oxidized to oxygen. The hydrogen produced from this process is either burned (converting it back to water), used for the production of <a href="/wiki/Specialty_chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Specialty chemicals">specialty chemicals</a>, or various other small-scale applications.</p>
<p>Water electrolysis is also used to generate oxygen for the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-asme_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asme-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nasa_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hydrogen may later be used in a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a> as a storage method of energy and water.<sup id="cite_ref-storage_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-storage-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">Efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_output">Industrial output</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Industrial output">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Efficiency of modern hydrogen generators is measured by <i>energy consumed per standard volume of hydrogen</i> (MJ/m<sup>3</sup>), assuming <a href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> of the H<sub>2</sub>. The lower the energy used by a generator, the higher would be its efficiency; a 100%-efficient electrolyser would consume 39.4 kilowatt-hours per kilogram (142&#160;MJ/kg) of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EU140207_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU140207-19">[19]</a></sup> 12,749 joules per litre (12.75&#160;MJ/m<sup>3</sup>). Practical electrolysis (using a rotating electrolyser at 15 bar pressure) may consume 50 kilowatt-hours per kilogram (180&#160;MJ/kg), and a further 15 kilowatt-hours (54&#160;MJ) if the hydrogen is compressed for use in hydrogen cars.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Electrolyser vendors provide efficiencies based on <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>. To assess the claimed efficiency of an electrolyser it is important to establish how it was defined by the vendor (i.e. what enthalpy value, what current density, etc.).</p>
<p>There are two main technologies available on the market, <i>alkaline</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">proton exchange membrane</a></i> (PEM) electrolysers. Alkaline electrolysers are cheaper in terms of investment (they generally use nickel catalysts), but less efficient; PEM electrolysers, conversely, are more expensive (they generally use expensive platinum-group metal catalysts) but are more efficient and can operate at higher current densities, and can therefore be possibly cheaper if the hydrogen production is large enough.</p>
<p>Reported working efficiencies were for alkaline in 1996 lying in the 50–60% range for the smaller electrolysers and around 65–70% for the larger plants.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Theorical efficiency for PEM electrolysers are predicted up to 94%.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Ranges in 2014 were 43–67% for the alkaline and 40–67% for the PEM, they should progress in 2030 to 53–70% for the alkaline and 62–74% for the PEM.<sup id="cite_ref-EU140207_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU140207-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overpotential">Overpotential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Overpotential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Real water electrolysers require higher voltages for the reaction to proceed. The part that exceeds 1.23 V<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> is called <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> or overvoltage, and represents any kind of loss and nonideality in the electrochemical process.</p>
<p>For a well designed cell the largest <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Overpotential#Reaction_overpotential" title="Overpotential">reaction overpotential</a> for the four-electron oxidation of water to oxygen at the anode; <a href="/wiki/Catalyst#Electrocatalysts" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">electrocatalysts</a> can facilitate this reaction, and <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> alloys are the state of the art for this oxidation. Developing a cheap, effective electrocatalyst for this reaction would be a great advance, and is a topic of current research; there are many approaches, among them a 30-year-old recipe for <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_sulfide" class="mw-redirect" title="Molybdenum sulfide">molybdenum sulfide</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-KibsgaardJaramillo2014_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KibsgaardJaramillo2014-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Potential_applications_of_graphene#Quantum_dots" title="Potential applications of graphene">graphene quantum dots</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FeiYe2014_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeiYe2014-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotubes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-r1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_(structure)" title="Perovskite (structure)">perovskite</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-LuoIm2014_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LuoIm2014-26">[26]</a></sup> and nickel/nickel-oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The simpler two-electron reaction to produce hydrogen at the cathode can be electrocatalyzed with almost no overpotential by platinum, or in theory a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenase" title="Hydrogenase">hydrogenase enzyme</a>. If other, less effective, materials are used for the cathode (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>), large overpotentials will appear.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics_2">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The electrolysis of water in standard conditions requires a theoretical minimum of 237 kJ of electrical energy input to dissociate each mole of water, which is the standard <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> of formation of water. It also requires energy to overcome the change in entropy of the reaction. Therefore, the process cannot proceed below 286 kJ per mol if no external heat/energy is added.</p>
<p>Since each mole of water requires two moles of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, and given that the <a href="/wiki/Faraday_constant" title="Faraday constant">Faraday constant</a> <i>F</i> represents the charge of a mole of electrons (96485 C/mol), it follows that the minimum voltage necessary for electrolysis is about 1.23 V.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> If electrolysis is carried out at high temperature, this voltage reduces. This effectively allows the electrolyser to operate at more than 100% electrical efficiency. In electrochemical systems this means that heat must be supplied to the reactor to sustain the reaction. In this way thermal energy can be used for part of the electrolysis energy requirement.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In a similar way the required voltage can be reduced (below 1 V) if fuels (such as carbon, alcohol, biomass) are reacted with water (PEM based electrolyzer in low temperature) or oxygen ions (solid oxide electrolyte based electrolyzer in high temperature). This results in some of the fuel's energy being used to "assist" the electrolysis process and can reduce the overall cost of hydrogen produced.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, observing the entropy component (and other losses), voltages over 1.48 V are required for the reaction to proceed at practical current densities (the <i>thermoneutral</i> voltage).</p>
<p>In the case of water electrolysis, Gibbs free energy represents the minimum <i>work</i> necessary for the reaction to proceed, and the reaction enthalpy is the amount of energy (both work and heat) that has to be provided so the reaction products are at the same temperature as the reactant (i.e. standard temperature for the values given above). Potentially, an electrolyser operating at 1.48 V would be 100% efficient.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
