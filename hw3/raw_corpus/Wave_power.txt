<p><b>Wave power</b> is the transport of energy by <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">wind waves</a>, and the capture of that energy to do useful <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical work">work</a> – for example, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_desalination" class="mw-redirect" title="Water desalination">water desalination</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pumping</a> of water (into reservoirs). A machine able to exploit wave <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> is generally known as a <b>wave energy converter</b> (WEC).</p>
<p>Wave power is distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal</a> flux of <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a> and the steady gyre of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean currents">ocean currents</a>. Wave-power generation is not currently a widely employed commercial technology, although there have been attempts to use it since at least 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2008, the first experimental <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a> was opened in Portugal, at the <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Aguçadoura Wave Park">Aguçadoura Wave Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Waves are generated by wind passing over the surface of the sea. As long as the waves propagate slower than the wind speed just above the waves, there is an energy transfer from the wind to the waves. Both air pressure differences between the upwind and the lee side of a wave <a href="/wiki/Crest_(physics)" title="Crest (physics)">crest</a>, as well as friction on the water surface by the wind, making the water to go into the <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear stress</a> causes the growth of the waves.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wave_height" title="Wave height">Wave height</a> is determined by wind speed, the duration of time the wind has been blowing, fetch (the distance over which the wind excites the waves) and by the depth and topography of the seafloor (which can focus or disperse the energy of the waves). A given wind speed has a matching practical limit over which time or distance will not produce larger waves. When this limit has been reached the sea is said to be "fully developed".</p>
<p>In general, larger waves are more powerful but wave power is also determined by wave speed, <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>, and water <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave#Science_of_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface wave">Oscillatory motion</a> is highest at the surface and diminishes exponentially with depth. However, for <a href="/wiki/Standing_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing waves">standing waves</a> (<a href="/wiki/Clapotis" title="Clapotis">clapotis</a>) near a reflecting coast, wave energy is also present as pressure oscillations at great depth, producing <a href="/wiki/Microseism" title="Microseism">microseisms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-4">[4]</a></sup> These pressure fluctuations at greater depth are too small to be interesting from the point of view of wave power.</p>
<p>The waves propagate on the ocean surface, and the wave energy is also transported horizontally with the <a href="/wiki/Group_velocity" title="Group velocity">group velocity</a>. The mean transport rate of the wave energy through a vertical <a href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> of unit width, parallel to a wave crest, is called the wave energy <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> (or wave power, which must not be confused with the actual power generated by a wave power device).</p>

<p>An effective wave power device captures as much as possible of the wave energy flux. As a result, the waves will be of lower height in the region behind the wave power device.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep-water_characteristics_and_opportunities">Deep-water characteristics and opportunities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deep-water characteristics and opportunities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deep water corresponds with a water depth larger than half the wavelength, which is the common situation in the sea and ocean. In deep water, longer-period waves propagate faster and transport their energy faster. The deep-water group velocity is half the <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">phase velocity</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Waves_and_shallow_water" title="Waves and shallow water">shallow water</a>, for wavelengths larger than about twenty times the water depth, as found quite often near the coast, the group velocity is equal to the phase velocity.<sup id="cite_ref-Dean_Dalrymple_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dean_Dalrymple-15">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first known patent to use energy from ocean waves dates back to 1799, and was filed in Paris by <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Girard" title="Pierre-Simon Girard">Girard</a> and his son.<sup id="cite_ref-cle2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cle2002-16">[13]</a></sup> An early application of wave power was a device constructed around 1910 by Bochaux-Praceique to light and power his house at <a href="/wiki/Royan" title="Royan">Royan</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> in France.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup> It appears that this was the first oscillating water-column type of wave-energy device.<sup id="cite_ref-morris2007_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morris2007-18">[15]</a></sup> From 1855 to 1973 there were already 340 patents filed in the UK alone.<sup id="cite_ref-cle2002_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cle2002-16">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern scientific pursuit of wave energy was pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Yoshio_Masuda" title="Yoshio Masuda">Yoshio Masuda</a>'s experiments in the 1940s.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup> He tested various concepts of wave-energy devices at sea, with several hundred units used to power navigation lights. Among these was the concept of extracting power from the angular motion at the joints of an articulated raft, which was proposed in the 1950s by Masuda.<sup id="cite_ref-ey2006_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ey2006-20">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A renewed interest in wave energy was motivated by the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil crisis in 1973</a>. A number of university researchers re-examined the potential to generate energy from ocean waves, among whom notably were <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Salter" title="Stephen Salter">Stephen Salter</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kjell_Budal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kjell Budal (page does not exist)">Kjell Budal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Falnes" title="Johannes Falnes">Johannes Falnes</a> from <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Institute_of_Technology" title="Norwegian Institute of Technology">Norwegian Institute of Technology</a> (now merged into <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_University_of_Science_and_Technology" title="Norwegian University of Science and Technology">Norwegian University of Science and Technology</a>), <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._McCormick" title="Michael E. McCormick">Michael E. McCormick</a> from <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Academy">U.S. Naval Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Evans_(mathematician)" title="David Evans (mathematician)">David Evans</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bristol_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol University">Bristol University</a>, Michael French from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Lancaster">University of Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Nicholas_Newman" class="mw-redirect" title="John Nicholas Newman">Nick Newman</a> and <a href="/wiki/C._C._Mei" class="mw-redirect" title="C. C. Mei">C. C. Mei</a> from <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>.</p>
<p>Stephen Salter's <a href="/wiki/1974_in_science" title="1974 in science">1974 invention</a> became known as <a href="/wiki/Salter%27s_duck" title="Salter's duck">Salter's duck</a> or <i>nodding duck</i>, although it was officially referred to as the Edinburgh Duck. In small scale controlled tests, the Duck's curved cam-like body can stop 90% of wave motion and can convert 90% of that to electricity giving 81% efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1980s, as the oil price went down, wave-energy funding was drastically reduced. Nevertheless, a few first-generation prototypes were tested at sea. More recently, following the issue of climate change, there is again a growing interest worldwide for renewable energy, including wave energy.<sup id="cite_ref-falnes2007_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-falnes2007-22">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The world's first marine energy test facility was established in 2003 to kick start the development of a wave and tidal energy industry in the UK. Based in Orkney, Scotland, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.emec.org.uk/">European Marine Energy Centre (EMEC)</a> has supported the deployment of more wave and tidal energy devices than at any other single site in the world. EMEC provides a variety of test sites in real sea conditions. Its grid-connected wave test site is situated at Billia Croo, on the western edge of the Orkney mainland, and is subject to the full force of the Atlantic Ocean with seas as high as 19 metres recorded at the site. Wave energy developers currently testing at the centre include <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.aquamarinepower.com/">Aquamarine Power</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pelamiswave.com/">Pelamis Wave Power</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.emec.org.uk/about-us/wave-clients/scottishpower-renewables/">ScottishPower Renewables</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.wello.eu/">Wello</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_technology">Modern technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wave power devices are generally categorized by the <b>method</b> used to capture the energy of the waves, by <b>location</b> and by the <b>power take-off system</b>. Locations are shoreline, nearshore and offshore. Types of power take-off include: <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_ram" title="Hydraulic ram">hydraulic ram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peristaltic_pump" title="Peristaltic pump">elastomeric hose pump</a>, pump-to-shore, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric turbine</a>, air turbine,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Linear_motor" title="Linear motor">linear electrical generator</a>. When evaluating <a href="/wiki/Wave_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Wave energy">wave energy</a> as a technology type, it is important to distinguish between the four most common approaches: point absorber buoys, surface attenuators, oscillating water columns, and overtopping devices.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Point_absorber_buoy">Point absorber buoy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Point absorber buoy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This device floats on the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, held in place by cables connected to the seabed. Buoys use the rise and fall of swells to drive hydraulic pumps and generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">EMF</a> generated by electrical transmission cables and acoustics of these devices may be a concern for marine organisms. The presence of the buoys may affect fish, marine mammals, and birds as potential minor collision risk and roosting sites. Potential also exists for entanglement in mooring lines. Energy removed from the waves may also affect the shoreline, resulting in a recommendation that sites remain a considerable distance from the shore.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_attenuator">Surface attenuator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Surface attenuator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These devices act similarly to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Point_absorber_buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Point absorber buoy (page does not exist)">point absorber buoys</a>, with multiple floating segments connected to one another and are oriented perpendicular to incoming waves. A flexing motion is created by swells that drive hydraulic pumps to generate electricity. Environmental effects are similar to those of point absorber buoys, with an additional concern that organisms could be pinched in the joints.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oscillating_wave_surge_converter">Oscillating wave surge converter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Oscillating wave surge converter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These devices typically have one end fixed to a structure or the seabed while the other end is free to move. <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> is collected from the relative motion of the body compared to the fixed point. Oscillating wave surge converters often come in the form of floats, flaps, or membranes. Environmental concerns include minor risk of collision, artificial reefing near the fixed point, <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">EMF</a> effects from subsea cables, and energy removal effecting sediment transport.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-25">[22]</a></sup> Some of these designs incorporate <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflectors</a> as a means of increasing the wave energy at the point of capture. These capture systems use the rise and fall motion of waves to capture energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Renewable_Sea_Power_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renewable_Sea_Power-26">[23]</a></sup> Once the wave energy is captured at a wave source, power must be carried to the point of use or to a connection to the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> by <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine_power_cable" title="Submarine power cable">power cables</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt20100316_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20100316-27">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oscillating_water_column">Oscillating water column</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Oscillating water column">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oscillating_Water_Column" title="Oscillating Water Column">Oscillating Water Column</a> devices can be located on shore or in deeper waters offshore. With an air chamber integrated into the device, swells compress air in the chambers forcing air through an air turbine to create <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cgrnflea_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cgrnflea-28">[25]</a></sup> Significant noise is produced as air is pushed through the turbines, potentially affecting <a href="/wiki/Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds">birds</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Marine_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine organisms">marine organisms</a> within the vicinity of the device. There is also concern about marine organisms getting trapped or entangled within the air chambers.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overtopping_device">Overtopping device</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Overtopping device">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Overtopping devices are long structures that use wave velocity to fill a reservoir to a greater water level than the surrounding ocean. The potential energy in the reservoir height is then captured with low-head turbines. Devices can be either on shore or floating offshore. Floating devices will have environmental concerns about the mooring system affecting <a href="/wiki/Benthic_organism" class="mw-redirect" title="Benthic organism">benthic organisms</a>, organisms becoming entangled, or EMF effects produced from <a href="/wiki/Subsea_cable" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsea cable">subsea cables</a>. There is also some concern regarding low levels of turbine noise and wave energy removal affecting the nearfield habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-Tethys_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tethys-25">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Submerged_Pressure_Differential">Submerged Pressure Differential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Submerged Pressure Differential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Submerged pressure differential based converters are a comparatively newer technology <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup> utilizing flexible (usually reinforced rubber) membranes to extract wave energy. These converters use the difference in pressure at different locations below a wave to produce a pressure difference within a closed power take-off fluid system. This pressure difference is usually used to produce flow, which drives a turbine and electrical generator. Submerged pressure differential converters frequently use flexible membranes as the working surface between the ocean and the power take-off system. Membranes offer the advantage over rigid structures of being compliant and low mass, which can produce more direct coupling with the wave’s energy. Their compliant nature also allows for large changes in the geometry of the working surface, which can be used to tune the response of the converter for specific wave conditions and to protect it from excessive loads in extreme conditions.</p>
<p>A submerged converter may be positioned either on the sea floor or in midwater. In both cases, the converter is protected from water impact loads which can occur at the <a href="/wiki/Free_surface" title="Free surface">free surface</a>. Wave loads also diminish in <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">non-linear</a> proportion to the distance below the free surface. This means that by optimizing the depth of submergence for such a converter, a compromise between protection from extreme loads and access to wave energy can be found. Submerged WECs also have the potential to reduce the impact on marine amenity and navigation, as they are not at the surface. Example's of submerged pressure differential converters include <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.m3wave.com/m3tech">M3 Wave</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bomborawavepower.com.au/">Bombora Wave Power</a>'s mWave.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Effects">Environmental Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_electricity_generation#Wave" title="Environmental impact of electricity generation">Environmental impact of electricity generation §&#160;Wave</a></div>
<p>Common environmental concerns associated with <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a> developments include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The risk of <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> being struck by tidal <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> blades;</li>
<li>The effects of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">EMF</a> and underwater noise emitted from operating marine energy devices;</li>
<li>The physical presence of <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a> projects and their potential to alter the behavior of marine mammals, fish, and <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> with attraction or avoidance;</li>
<li>The potential effect on nearfield and farfield marine environment and processes such as <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport" title="Sediment transport">sediment transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(database)" title="Tethys (database)">Tethys database</a> provides access to scientific literature and general information on the potential environmental effects of wave energy.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential">Potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The worldwide resource of wave energy has been estimated to be greater than 2 TW.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup> Locations with the most potential for wave power include the western seaboard of Europe, the northern coast of the UK, and the Pacific coastlines of North and South America, Southern Africa, Australia, and New Zealand. The north and south <a href="/wiki/Temperate_zones" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate zones">temperate zones</a> have the best sites for capturing wave power. The prevailing <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> in these zones blow strongest in winter.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a potential impact on the marine environment. Noise pollution, for example, could have negative impact if not monitored, although the noise and visible impact of each design vary greatly.<sup id="cite_ref-ocs_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocs-8">[7]</a></sup> Other biophysical impacts (flora and fauna, sediment regimes and water column structure and flows) of scaling up the technology is being studied.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup> In terms of socio-economic challenges, wave farms can result in the displacement of commercial and recreational fishermen from productive fishing grounds, can change the pattern of beach sand nourishment, and may represent hazards to safe navigation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[30]</a></sup> Waves generate about 2,700 gigawatts of power. Of those 2,700 gigawatts, only about 500 gigawatts can be captured with the current technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Renewable_Sea_Power_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renewable_Sea_Power-26">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_farms">Wave farms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Wave farms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">Wave farm</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a> was the world's first <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a>. It was located 5&#160;km (3&#160;mi) offshore near <b><a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">Póvoa de Varzim</a></b>, north of <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, Portugal. The farm was designed to use three <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_wave_energy_converter" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelamis wave energy converter">Pelamis</a> wave energy converters to convert the motion of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface waves</a> into electricity, totalling to <span class="nowrap">2.25 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a></span> in total installed capacity. The farm first generated electricity in July 2008<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup> and was officially opened on September 23, 2008, by the Portuguese Minister of Economy.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.pt_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.pt-35">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[33]</a></sup> The wave farm was shut down two months after the official opening in November 2008 as a result of the financial collapse of Babcock &amp; Brown due to the global economic crisis. The machines were off-site at this time due to technical problems, and although resolved have not returned to site and were subsequently scrapped in 2011 as the technology had moved on to the P2 variant as supplied to Eon and Scottish Power Renewables.<sup id="cite_ref-Pelamis_Sinks_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pelamis_Sinks-37">[34]</a></sup> A second phase of the project planned to increase the installed capacity to <span class="nowrap">21 MW</span> using a further 25 Pelamis machines<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup> is in doubt following Babcock's financial collapse.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Funding for a <span class="nowrap">3 MW</span> wave farm in <b>Scotland</b> was announced on February 20, 2007, by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Executive">Scottish Executive</a>, at a cost of over 4 million <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pounds</a>, as part of a £13 million funding package for <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Scotland#Wave_power" title="Renewable energy in Scotland">marine power in Scotland</a>. The first machine was launched in May 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>A facility known as <a href="/wiki/Wave_hub" class="mw-redirect" title="Wave hub">Wave hub</a> has been constructed off the north coast of Cornwall, England, to facilitate wave energy development. The Wave hub will act as giant extension cable, allowing arrays of wave energy generating devices to be connected to the electricity grid. The Wave hub will initially allow <span class="nowrap">20 MW</span> of capacity to be connected, with potential expansion to <span class="nowrap">40 MW</span>. Four device manufacturers have so far expressed interest in connecting to the Wave hub.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[38]</a></sup> The scientists have calculated that wave energy gathered at Wave Hub will be enough to power up to 7,500 households. The site has the potential to save greenhouse gas emissions of about 300,000 tons of carbon dioxide in the next 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bomborawavepower.com.au/">Bombora Wave Power</a> (Bombora Wave Power Pty Ltd) is based in <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth, Western Australia</a> and is currently developing the <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bomborawavepower.com.au/mwave/">mWave</a></i> flexible membrane converter. Bombora is currently preparing for a commercial pilot project in <a href="/wiki/Peniche,_Portugal" title="Peniche, Portugal">Peniche, Portugal</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/CETO_Wave_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="CETO Wave Power">CETO</a> wave farm off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> has been operating to prove commercial viability and, after preliminary environmental approval, underwent further development.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[41]</a></sup> In early 2015 a $100 million, multi megawatt system was connected to the grid, with all the electricity being bought to power <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Stirling" title="HMAS Stirling">HMAS Stirling</a> naval base. Two fully submerged buoys which are anchored to the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a>, transmit the energy from the ocean swell through <a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">hydraulic pressure</a> onshore; to drive a generator for electricity, and also to produce fresh water. As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> a third buoy is planned for installation.<sup id="cite_ref-abc.net.au.2F20150218_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc.net.au.2F20150218-45">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ocean_Power_Technologies" title="Ocean Power Technologies">Ocean Power Technologies</a> (<a href="/wiki/OPT_Australasia_Pty_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="OPT Australasia Pty Ltd">OPT Australasia Pty Ltd</a>) is developing a wave farm connected to the grid near <b><a href="/wiki/Portland,_Victoria" title="Portland, Victoria">Portland, Victoria</a></b> through a 19&#160;MW wave power station. The project has received an AU $66.46 million grant from the Federal Government of Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-Reeds12July2012_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reeds12July2012-47">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oceanlinx" title="Oceanlinx">Oceanlinx</a> will deploy a commercial scale demonstrator off the coast of South Australia at <a href="/wiki/Port_MacDonnell" class="mw-redirect" title="Port MacDonnell">Port MacDonnell</a> before the end of 2013. This device, the <i>greenWAVE</i>, has a rated electrical capacity of 1MW. This project has been supported by ARENA through the Emerging Renewables Program. The <i>greenWAVE</i> device is a bottom standing gravity structure, that does not require anchoring or seabed preparation and with no moving parts below the surface of the water.<sup id="cite_ref-ARENA_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARENA-48">[45]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wave_power_in_the_United_States" title="Wave power in the United States">Wave power in the United States</a></div>
<ul>
<li><b>Reedsport, Oregon</b> – a commercial wave park on the west coast of the United States located 2.5 miles offshore near <a href="/wiki/Reedsport,_Oregon" title="Reedsport, Oregon">Reedsport, Oregon</a>. The first phase of this project is for ten PB150 <a href="/wiki/PowerBuoy" title="PowerBuoy">PowerBuoys</a>, or 1.5 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-Reeds14Mar2012_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reeds14Mar2012-49">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reeds03Oct2012_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reeds03Oct2012-50">[47]</a></sup> The Reedsport wave farm was scheduled for installation spring 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Reeds04102012_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reeds04102012-51">[48]</a></sup> In 2013, the project had ground to a halt because of legal and technical problems.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kaneohe_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaneohe Bay">Kaneohe Bay</a> <a href="/wiki/Oahu" title="Oahu">Oahu</a>, Hawaii</b> - Navy’s Wave Energy Test Site (WETS) currently testing the <a href="/wiki/Azura_wave_power_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Azura wave power device">Azura wave power device</a><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[50]</a></sup> The Azura wave power device is 45-ton wave energy converter located at a depth of 30 meters in Kaneohe Bay.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[51]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
