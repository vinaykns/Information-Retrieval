			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Emerging technologies</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For specific emerging technologies, see the <a href="/wiki/List_of_emerging_technologies" title="List of emerging technologies">List of emerging technologies</a></div>
<p><b>Emerging technologies</b> are technologies that are perceived as capable of changing the status quo. These technologies are generally new but include older technologies that are still controversial and relatively undeveloped in potential, such as <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preimplantation_genetic_diagnosis" title="Preimplantation genetic diagnosis">preimplantation genetic diagnosis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">gene therapy</a> which date to 1981, 1989 and 1990 respectively.</p>
<p>Emerging technologies are characterized by radical novelty, relatively fast growth, coherence, prominent impact, and uncertainty and ambiguity. In other words, an emerging technology can be defined as "a radically novel and relatively fast growing technology characterised by a certain degree of coherence persisting over time and with the potential to exert a considerable impact on the socio-economic domain(s) which is observed in terms of the composition of actors, institutions and patterns of interactions among those, along with the associated knowledge production processes. Its most prominent impact, however, lies in the future and so in the emergence phase is still somewhat uncertain and ambiguous.".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emerging technologies include a variety of technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Educational_technology" title="Educational technology">educational technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychotechnology" title="Psychotechnology">psychotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>New technological fields may result from the <a href="/wiki/Technological_convergence" title="Technological convergence">technological convergence</a> of different systems evolving towards similar goals. Convergence brings previously separate technologies such as voice (and telephony features), data (and productivity applications) and video together so that they share resources and interact with each other, creating new efficiencies.</p>
<p>Emerging technologies are those technical innovations which represent progressive developments within a field for <a href="/wiki/Economic_competition" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic competition">competitive advantage</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> converging technologies represent previously distinct fields which are in some way moving towards stronger inter-connection and similar goals. However, the opinion on the degree of the impact, status and economic viability of several emerging and converging technologies vary.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_emerging_technologies">History of emerging technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of emerging technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_technology" title="History of technology">History of technology</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/History_of_technology" title="History of technology">history of technology</a>, emerging technologies<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> are contemporary advances and innovation in various fields of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>.</p>
<p>Over centuries, innovative methods and new technologies are developed and opened up. Some of these technologies are due to theoretical research, and others from commercial <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>.</p>
<p>Technological growth includes incremental developments and <a href="/wiki/Disruptive_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Disruptive technology">disruptive technologies</a>. An example of the former was the gradual roll-out of <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> (digital video disc) as a development intended to follow on from the previous optical technology <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a>. By contrast, disruptive technologies are those where a new method replaces the previous technology and makes it redundant, for example, the replacement of horse-drawn carriages by automobiles.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerging_technology_debates">Emerging technology debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emerging technology debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Technology_and_society" title="Technology and society">Technology and society</a></div>
<p>Many writers, including <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Joy" title="Bill Joy">Bill Joy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> have identified clusters of technologies that they consider critical to humanity's future. Joy warns that the technology could be used by elites for <a href="/wiki/Good_and_evil" title="Good and evil">good</a> or <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">evil</a>. They could use it as "good shepherds" for the rest of humanity, or decide everyone else is superfluous and push for mass extinction of those made unnecessary by technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Joy_2000_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joy_2000-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Advocates of the benefits of <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological change</a> typically see emerging and converging technologies as offering hope for the betterment of the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>. Cyberphilosophers <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Bard" title="Alexander Bard">Alexander Bard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_S%C3%B6derqvist" title="Jan Söderqvist">Jan Söderqvist</a> argue in <i>The Futurica Trilogy</i> that while Man himself is basically constant throughout human history (<a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genes</a> change very slowly), all relevant change is rather a direct or indirect result of technological innovation (<a href="/wiki/Memetics" title="Memetics">memes</a> change very fast) since new ideas always emanate from technology use and not the other way around.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Man should consequently be regarded as history's main constant and technology as its main variable. However, critics of the risks of technological change, and even some advocates such as <a href="/wiki/Transhumanist" class="mw-redirect" title="Transhumanist">transhumanist</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bostrom" title="Nick Bostrom">Nick Bostrom</a>, warn that some of these technologies could pose dangers, perhaps even contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Human_extinction" title="Human extinction">extinction of humanity</a> itself; i.e., some of them could involve <a href="/wiki/Existential_risk" class="mw-redirect" title="Existential risk">existential risks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bostrom_2002_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bostrom_2002-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_technology" title="Ethics of technology">ethical debate</a> centers on issues of <a href="/wiki/Distributive_justice" title="Distributive justice">distributive justice</a> in allocating access to beneficial forms of technology. Some thinkers, such as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">environmental ethicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a>, oppose the continuing development of advanced technology partly out of fear that its benefits will be distributed unequally in ways that could worsen the <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">plight of the poor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McKibben_2003_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKibben_2003-11">[11]</a></sup> By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Inventor" class="mw-redirect" title="Inventor">inventor</a> <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kurzweil" title="Ray Kurzweil">Ray Kurzweil</a> is among <a href="/wiki/Techno-utopianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Techno-utopianism">techno-utopians</a> who believe that emerging and converging technologies could and will <a href="/wiki/Post_scarcity" class="mw-redirect" title="Post scarcity">eliminate poverty</a> and abolish suffering.<sup id="cite_ref-Kurzweil_2005_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kurzweil_2005-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some analysts such as Martin Ford, author of <i>The Lights in the Tunnel: Automation, Accelerating Technology and the Economy of the Future</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Ford2009Lights_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ford2009Lights-13">[13]</a></sup> argue that as information technology advances, robots and other forms of automation will ultimately result in significant <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> as machines and software begin to match and exceed the capability of workers to perform most routine jobs.</p>
<p>As robotics and artificial intelligence develop further, even many skilled jobs may be threatened. Technologies such as machine learning<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> may ultimately allow computers to do many knowledge-based jobs that require significant education. This may result in substantial unemployment at all skill levels, stagnant or falling wages for most workers, and increased concentration of income and wealth as the owners of capital capture an ever larger fraction of the economy. This in turn could lead to depressed consumer spending and economic growth as the bulk of the population lacks sufficient discretionary income to purchase the products and services produced by the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_emerging_technologies" title="List of emerging technologies">List of emerging technologies</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_intelligence">Artificial intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Artificial intelligence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">Artificial intelligence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_artificial_intelligence" title="Outline of artificial intelligence">Outline of artificial intelligence</a></div>
<p><i>Artificial intelligence</i> (<i>AI</i>) is the intelligence exhibited by machines or software, and the branch of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> that develops machines and software with human-like intelligence. Major AI researchers and textbooks define the field as "the study and design of intelligent agents", where an <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">intelligent agent</a> is a system that perceives its environment and takes actions that maximize its chances of success. <a href="/wiki/John_McCarthy_(computer_scientist)" title="John McCarthy (computer scientist)">John McCarthy</a>, who coined the term in 1955, defines it as "the science and engineering of making intelligent machines".</p>
<p>The central problems (or goals) of AI research include reasoning, knowledge, planning, learning, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> (communication), <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> and the ability to move and manipulate objects. General intelligence (or "<a href="/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence" title="Artificial general intelligence">strong AI</a>") is still among the field's long-term goals. Currently popular approaches include deep learning, statistical methods, computational intelligence and traditional symbolic AI. There are an enormous number of tools used in AI, including versions of search and mathematical optimization, logic, methods based on probability and economics, and many others.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3D_Printing">3D Printing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 3D Printing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/3D_Printing" class="mw-redirect" title="3D Printing">3D Printing</a></div>
<p>3D printing, also known as additive manufacturing, has been posited by <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Rifkin" title="Jeremy Rifkin">Jeremy Rifkin</a> and others as part of the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Revolution" title="Digital Revolution">third industrial revolution</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Combined with Internet technology, 3D printing would allow for digital blueprints of virtually any material product to be sent instantly to another person to be produced on the spot, making purchasing a product online almost instantaneous.</p>
<p>Although this technology is still too crude to produce most products, it was controversially done with guns in 2013 and the technology is rapidly developing. <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gene_therapy">Gene therapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gene therapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">Gene therapy</a></div>
<p><i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering_timeline" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic engineering timeline">Genetic engineering timeline</a></i></p>
<p>Gene therapy was first successfully demonstrated in late 1990/early 1991 for <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_deaminase_deficiency" title="Adenosine deaminase deficiency">adenosine deaminase deficiency</a>, though the treatment was somatic - that is, did not affect the patient's germ line and thus was not heritable. This led the way to treatments for other genetic diseases and increased interest in <a href="/wiki/Germ_line_gene_therapy" class="mw-redirect" title="Germ line gene therapy">germ line gene therapy</a> - therapy affecting the gametes and descendants of patients.</p>
<p>Between September 1990 and January 2014 there were around 2,000 gene therapy trials conducted or approved. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer_vaccines">Cancer vaccines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cancer vaccines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cancer_vaccine" title="Cancer vaccine">Cancer vaccine</a></div>
<p>A <i>cancer vaccine</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccine</a> that treats existing <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> or prevents the development of cancer in certain high-risk individuals. Vaccines that treat existing cancer are known as <i>therapeutic</i> cancer vaccines. There are currently no vaccines able to prevent cancer in general.</p>
<p>On April 14, 2009, <a href="/wiki/Dendreon" title="Dendreon">Dendreon</a> Corporation announced that their Phase III clinical trial of <a href="/wiki/Provenge" class="mw-redirect" title="Provenge">Provenge</a>, a cancer vaccine designed to treat prostate cancer, had demonstrated an increase in survival. It received <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) approval for use in the treatment of advanced prostate cancer patients on April 29, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The approval of Provenge has stimulated interest in this type of therapy.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_vitro_meat"><i>In vitro</i> meat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In vitro meat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_meat" class="mw-redirect" title="In vitro meat">In vitro meat</a></div>
<p><i>In vitro meat</i>, also called <i>cultured meat</i>, <i>cruelty-free meat</i>, <i>shmeat</i>, and <i>test-tube meat</i>, is an animal-flesh product that has never been part of a living animal with exception of the <a href="/wiki/Fetal_calf_serum" class="mw-redirect" title="Fetal calf serum">fetal calf serum</a> taken from a slaughtered cow. In the 21st century, several research projects have worked on <i>in vitro</i> meat in the laboratory.<sup id="cite_ref-time_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="#First_public_trial">first in vitro beefburger</a>, created by a Dutch team, was eaten at a demonstration for the press in London in August 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC5Aug_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC5Aug-24">[24]</a></sup> There remain difficulties to be overcome before <i>in vitro</i> meat becomes commercially available.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Cultured meat is prohibitively expensive, but it is expected that the cost could be reduced to compete with that of conventionally obtained meat as technology improves.<sup id="cite_ref-no-kill_carnivore_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-no-kill_carnivore-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pharma_Concepts_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pharma_Concepts-27">[27]</a></sup> <i>In vitro</i> meat is also an ethical issue. Some argue that it is less objectionable than traditionally obtained meat because it doesn't involve killing and reduces the risk of animal cruelty, while others disagree with eating meat that has not developed naturally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nanotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_nanotechnology" title="Outline of nanotechnology">Outline of nanotechnology</a></div>
<p><i>Nanotechnology</i> (sometimes shortened to <i>nanotech</i>) is the manipulation of matter on an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular">molecular</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Supramolecular_chemistry" title="Supramolecular chemistry">supramolecular</a> scale. The earliest, widespread description of nanotechnology<sup id="cite_ref-Engines1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engines1-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nanotsystems_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nanotsystems-29">[29]</a></sup> referred to the particular technological goal of precisely manipulating atoms and molecules for fabrication of macroscale products, also now referred to as <a href="/wiki/Molecular_nanotechnology" title="Molecular nanotechnology">molecular nanotechnology</a>. A more generalized description of nanotechnology was subsequently established by the <a href="/wiki/National_Nanotechnology_Initiative" title="National Nanotechnology Initiative">National Nanotechnology Initiative</a>, which defines nanotechnology as the manipulation of matter with at least one dimension sized from 1 to 100 <a href="/wiki/Nanometers" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanometers">nanometers</a>. This definition reflects the fact that <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical</a> effects are important at this <a href="/wiki/Quantum_realm" title="Quantum realm">quantum-realm</a> scale, and so the definition shifted from a particular technological goal to a research category inclusive of all types of research and technologies that deal with the special properties of matter that occur below the given size threshold.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robotics">Robotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Robotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">Robotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_robotics" title="Outline of robotics">Outline of robotics</a></div>
<p><i>Robotics</i> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> that deals with the design, construction, operation, and application of <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-OED_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-30">[30]</a></sup> as well as computer systems for their control, sensory feedback, and information processing. These technologies deal with automated machines that can take the place of humans in dangerous environments or manufacturing processes, or resemble humans in appearance, behavior, and/or cognition. Many of today's robots are inspired by nature contributing to the field of <a href="/wiki/Bio-inspired_robotics" title="Bio-inspired robotics">bio-inspired robotics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stem_cell_therapy">Stem cell therapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Stem cell therapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell_therapy" class="mw-redirect" title="Stem cell therapy">Stem cell therapy</a></div>
<p><i>Stem cell therapy</i> is an intervention strategy that introduces new adult stem cells into damaged tissue in order to treat disease or injury. Many <a href="/wiki/Medical_research" title="Medical research">medical researchers</a> believe that stem cell treatments have the potential to change the face of human disease and alleviate suffering.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The ability of <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a> to self-renew and give rise to subsequent generations with variable degrees of differentiation capacities,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> offers significant potential for generation of tissues that can potentially replace diseased and damaged areas in the body, with minimal risk of rejection and side effects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_emerging_technologies">Development of emerging technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Development of emerging technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As innovation drives economic growth, and large economic rewards come from new inventions, a great deal of resources (funding and effort) go into the development of emerging technologies. Some of the sources of these resources are described below...</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_development">Research and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Research and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">Research and development</a> is directed towards the advancement of technology in general, and therefore includes development of emerging technologies. <i>See also <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_research_and_development_spending" title="List of countries by research and development spending">List of countries by research and development spending</a>.</i></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Applied_research" title="Applied research">Applied research</a> is a form of systematic inquiry involving the practical application of science. It accesses and uses some part of the research communities' (the academia's) accumulated theories, knowledge, methods, and techniques, for a specific, often state-, business-, or client-driven purpose.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Science_policy" title="Science policy">Science policy</a> is the area of public policy which is concerned with the policies that affect the conduct of the science and research enterprise, including the funding of science, often in pursuance of other national policy goals such as technological innovation to promote commercial product development, weapons development, health care and environmental monitoring.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DARPA">DARPA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: DARPA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA)</a> is an agency of the U.S. Department of Defense responsible for the development of emerging technologies for use by the military.</p>
<p>DARPA was created in 1958 as the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) by President Dwight D. Eisenhower. Its purpose was to formulate and execute research and development projects to expand the frontiers of technology and science, with the aim to reach beyond immediate military requirements.</p>
<p>Projects funded by DARPA have provided significant technologies that influenced many non-military fields, such as computer networking and graphical user interfaces in information technology.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_competitions_and_awards">Technology competitions and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Technology competitions and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are awards that provide incentive to push the limits of technology (generally synonymous with emerging technologies). Note that while some of these awards reward achievement after-the-fact via analysis of the merits of technological breakthroughs, others provide incentive via competitions for awards offered for goals yet to be achieved.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orteig_Prize" title="Orteig Prize">Orteig Prize</a> was a $25,000 award offered in 1919 by French hotelier <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Orteig" title="Raymond Orteig">Raymond Orteig</a> for the first nonstop flight between New York City and Paris. In 1927, underdog <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a> won the prize in a modified single-engine Ryan aircraft called the <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_St._Louis" title="Spirit of St. Louis">Spirit of St. Louis</a>. In total, nine teams spent $400,000 in pursuit of the Orteig Prize.</p>
<p>The XPRIZE series of awards, public competitions designed and managed by the non-profit organization called the <a href="/wiki/X_Prize_Foundation" title="X Prize Foundation">X Prize Foundation</a>, are intended to encourage technological development that could benefit mankind. The most high-profile XPRIZE to date was the $10,000,000 Ansari XPRIZE relating to spacecraft development, which was awarded in 2004 for the development of <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> is an annual prize given by the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> (ACM) to "an individual selected for contributions of a technical nature made to the computing community". It is stipulated that "The contributions should be of lasting and major technical importance to the computer field". The Turing Award is generally recognized as the highest distinction in computer science, and in 2014 grew to $1,000,000.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Technology_Prize" title="Millennium Technology Prize">Millennium Technology Prize</a> is awarded once every two years by <a href="/wiki/Technology_Academy_Finland" title="Technology Academy Finland">Technology Academy Finland</a>, an independent fund established by Finnish industry and the Finnish state in partnership. The first recipient was <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, inventor of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/David_Gobel" title="David Gobel">David Gobel</a> seed-funded the <a href="/wiki/Methuselah_Mouse_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Methuselah Mouse Prize">Methuselah Mouse Prize</a> (Mprize) to encourage the development of new life extension therapies in mice, which are genetically similar to humans. So far, three Mouse Prizes have been awarded: one for breaking longevity records to Dr. Andrzej Bartke of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois_University" title="Southern Illinois University">Southern Illinois University</a>; one for late-onset rejuvenation strategies to Dr. Stephen Spindler of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a>; and one to Dr. Z. Dave Sharp for his work with the pharmaceutical rapamycin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_science_fiction">Role of science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emerging_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Role of science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a> has criticized developing and future technologies, but also inspires <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> and new technology. This topic has been more often discussed in literary and sociological than in scientific forums. Cinema and media theorist <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Sobchack" title="Vivian Sobchack">Vivian Sobchack</a> examines the dialogue between science fiction films and technological imagination. Technology impacts artists and how they portray their fictionalized subjects, but the fictional world gives back to science by broadening imagination. <i><a href="/wiki/How_William_Shatner_Changed_the_World" title="How William Shatner Changed the World">How William Shatner Changed the World</a></i> is a documentary that gave a number of real-world examples of actualized technological imaginations. While more prevalent in the early years of science fiction with writers like <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>, new authors still find ways to make currently impossible technologies seem closer to being realized.</p>
