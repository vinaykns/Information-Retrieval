<p><b>Biogas</b> typically refers to a mixture of different <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> produced by the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. Biogas can be produced from raw materials such as agricultural waste, <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_waste" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal waste">municipal waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant material">plant material</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_waste" title="Green waste">green waste</a> or <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a>. Biogas is a renewable energy source and in many cases exerts a very small carbon footprint.</p>
<p>Biogas can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> with <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic organisms</a>, which digest material inside a closed system, or <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> of biodegradable materials.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biogas is primarily <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
4</span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and may have small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>S</span>), moisture and <a href="/wiki/Siloxane" title="Siloxane">siloxanes</a>. The gases <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO</span>) can be combusted or oxidized with oxygen. This energy release allows biogas to be used as a fuel; it can be used for any heating purpose, such as cooking. It can also be used in a gas engine to convert the energy in the gas into electricity and heat.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biogas can be compressed, the same way <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> is compressed to <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">CNG</a>, and used to power <a href="/wiki/Alternative_fuel_vehicle" title="Alternative fuel vehicle">motor vehicles</a>. In the UK, for example, biogas is estimated to have the potential to replace around 17% of vehicle fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-claverton-energy.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-claverton-energy.com-3">[3]</a></sup> It qualifies for renewable <a href="/wiki/Energy_subsidies" title="Energy subsidies">energy subsidies</a> in some parts of the world. Biogas can be cleaned and upgraded to natural gas standards, when it becomes bio-methane. Biogas is considered to be a renewable resource because its production-and-use cycle is continuous, and it generates no net carbon dioxide. Organic material grows, is converted and used and then regrows in a continually repeating cycle. From a carbon perspective, as much carbon dioxide is absorbed from the atmosphere in the growth of the primary bio-resource as is released when the material is ultimately converted to energy.</p>

<p>Biogas is produced as <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill gas</a> (LFG), which is produced by the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">Biodegradable waste</a> inside a landfill due to chemical reactions and microbes, or as digested gas, produced inside an <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digester</a>. A <i>biogas plant</i> is the name often given to an <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digester" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaerobic digester">anaerobic digester</a> that treats farm wastes or energy crops. It can be produced using anaerobic digesters (air-tight tanks with different configurations). These plants can be fed with energy crops such as maize silage or <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">biodegradable wastes</a> including sewage sludge and food waste. During the process, the microorganisms transform biomass waste into biogas (mainly methane and carbon dioxide) and <a href="/wiki/Digestate" title="Digestate">digestate</a>. The biogas is a renewable energy that can be used for heating, electricity, and many other operations that use a reciprocating internal combustion engine, such as <a href="/wiki/GE_Jenbacher" title="GE Jenbacher">GE Jenbacher</a> or <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">Caterpillar</a> gas engines.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other internal combustion engines such as gas turbines are suitable for the conversion of biogas into both electricity and heat. The digestate is the remaining inorganic matter that was not transformed into biogas. It can be used as an agricultural fertiliser.</p>
<p>There are two key processes: <a href="/wiki/Mesophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesophilic">mesophilic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermophilic">thermophilic</a> digestion which is dependent on temperature. In experimental work at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska_Fairbanks" title="University of Alaska Fairbanks">University of Alaska Fairbanks</a>, a 1000-litre digester using <a href="/wiki/Psychrophiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychrophiles">psychrophiles</a> harvested from "mud from a frozen lake in Alaska" has produced 200–300&#160;liters of methane per day, about 20%–30% of the output from digesters in warmer climates.<sup id="cite_ref-NewSc2785p14_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewSc2785p14-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">Dangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dangers of biogas are mostly similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, but with an additional risk from the toxicity of its <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> fraction. Biogas can be explosive when mixed in the ratio of one part biogas to 8-20 parts air. Special safety precautions have to be taken for entering an empty biogas digester for maintenance work.</p>
<p>It is important that a biogas system never has negative pressure as this could cause an explosion. Negative gas pressure can occur if too much gas is removed or leaked; Because of this biogas should not be used at pressures below one column inch of water, measured by a pressure gauge.</p>
<p><br />
Frequent smell checks must be performed on a biogas system. If biogas is smelled anywhere windows and doors should be opened immediately. If there is a fire the gas should be shut off at the gate valve of the biogas system.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landfill_gas">Landfill gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Landfill gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">Landfill gas</a></div>
<p>Landfill gas is produced by wet organic waste decomposing under anaerobic conditions in a biogas.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The waste is covered and mechanically compressed by the weight of the material that is deposited above. This material prevents oxygen exposure thus allowing anaerobic microbes to thrive. Biogas builds up and is slowly released into the atmosphere if the site has not been engineered to capture the gas. Landfill gas released in an uncontrolled way can be hazardous since it can become explosive when it escapes from the landfill and mixes with oxygen. The lower explosive limit is 5% methane and the upper is 15% methane.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The methane in biogas is 20 times more potent a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> than carbon dioxide. Therefore, uncontained landfill gas, which escapes into the atmosphere may significantly contribute to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs) in landfill gas contribute to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Smog#Photochemical_smog" title="Smog">photochemical smog</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical">Technical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biochemical_oxygen_demand" title="Biochemical oxygen demand">Biochemical oxygen demand</a> (BOD) is a measure of the amount of oxygen required by aerobic micro-organisms to decompose the organic matter in a sample of water. Knowing the energy density of the material being used in the biodigester as well as the BOD for the liquid discharge allows for the calculation of the daily energy output from a biodigester.</p>
<p>Another term related to biodigesters is effluent dirtiness, which tells how much organic material there is per unit of biogas source. Typical units for this measure are in mg BOD/litre. As an example, effluent dirtiness can range between 800–1200&#160;mg BOD/litre in Panama.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From 1&#160;kg of decommissioned kitchen bio-waste, 0.45 m³ of biogas can be obtained. The price for collecting biological waste from households is approximately €70 per ton.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The composition of biogas varies depending upon the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> process. <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">Landfill gas</a> typically has methane concentrations around 50%. Advanced waste treatment technologies can produce biogas with 55%–75% methane,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> which for reactors with free liquids can be increased to 80%-90% methane using <a href="/wiki/In-situ" class="mw-redirect" title="In-situ">in-situ</a> gas purification techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As produced, biogas contains water vapor. The fractional volume of water vapor is a function of biogas temperature; correction of measured gas volume for water vapor content and thermal expansion is easily done via simple mathematics<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which yields the standardized volume of dry biogas.</p>
<p>In some cases, biogas contains <a href="/wiki/Siloxanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Siloxanes">siloxanes</a>. They are formed from the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_decomposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaerobic decomposition">anaerobic decomposition</a> of materials commonly found in soaps and detergents. During combustion of biogas containing siloxanes, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> is released and can combine with free oxygen or other elements in the <a href="/wiki/Combustion_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Combustion gas">combustion gas</a>. Deposits are formed containing mostly <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span>) or <a href="/wiki/Silicates" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicates">silicates</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Si<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
<i>x</i></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
<i>y</i></span></span>) and can contain <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>. Such <i>white mineral</i> deposits accumulate to a surface thickness of several millimeters and must be removed by chemical or mechanical means.</p>
<p>Practical and cost-effective technologies to remove siloxanes and other biogas contaminants are available.<sup id="cite_ref-BiogasSiloxaneRemoval_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BiogasSiloxaneRemoval-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>For 1000&#160;kg (wet weight) of input to a typical biodigester, total solids may be 30% of the wet weight while <a href="/wiki/Volatile_suspended_solids" title="Volatile suspended solids">volatile suspended solids</a> may be 90% of the total solids. Protein would be 20% of the volatile solids, carbohydrates would be 70% of the volatile solids, and finally fats would be 10% of the volatile solids.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_manure_derived_biogas">Benefits of manure derived biogas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Benefits of manure derived biogas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>High levels of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> are produced when manure is stored under anaerobic conditions. During storage and when manure has been applied to the land, <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> is also produced as a byproduct of the denitrification process. Nitrous oxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">N2O</span>) is 320 times more aggressive as a greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and methane 25 times more than carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>By converting cow manure into methane biogas via <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a>, the millions of cattle in the United States would be able to produce 100 billion kilowatt hours of electricity, enough to power millions of homes across the United States. In fact, one cow can produce enough manure in one day to generate 3 kilowatt hours of electricity; only 2.4 kilowatt hours of electricity are needed to power a single 100-watt light bulb for one day.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Furthermore, by converting cattle manure into methane biogas instead of letting it decompose, global warming gases could be reduced by 99 million metric tons or 4%.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Biogas can be used for electricity production on sewage works,<sup id="cite_ref-Administrator_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Administrator-20">[20]</a></sup> in a <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">CHP</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engine</a>, where the waste heat from the engine is conveniently used for heating the digester; cooking; space heating; <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">water heating</a>; and process heating. If compressed, it can replace <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> for use in vehicles, where it can fuel an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> and is a much more effective displacer of carbon dioxide than the normal use in on-site CHP plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Administrator_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Administrator-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas_upgrading">Biogas upgrading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biogas upgrading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raw biogas produced from digestion is roughly 60% methane and 29% <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span></span> with trace elements of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>S</span>; it is not of high enough quality to be used as fuel gas for machinery. The corrosive nature of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><br />
2</span>S</span> alone is enough to destroy the internals of a plant.<sup id="cite_ref-AbatzoglouBoivin2009_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AbatzoglouBoivin2009-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuertasGiraldo2011_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuertasGiraldo2011-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Methane in biogas can be concentrated via a <a href="/wiki/Biogas_upgrader" title="Biogas upgrader">biogas upgrader</a> to the same standards as fossil <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, which itself has to go through a cleaning process, and becomes <i>biomethane</i>. If the local gas network allows, the producer of the biogas may use their distribution networks. Gas must be very clean to reach pipeline quality and must be of the correct composition for the distribution network to accept. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Particulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Particulate">particulates</a> must be removed if present.<sup id="cite_ref-AbatzoglouBoivin2009_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AbatzoglouBoivin2009-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four main methods of upgrading: water washing, pressure swing adsorption, selexol adsorption, and <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine gas treating</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition to these, the use of membrane separation technology for biogas upgrading is increasing, and there are already several plants operating in Europe and USA.<sup id="cite_ref-AbatzoglouBoivin2009_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AbatzoglouBoivin2009-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most prevalent method is water washing where high pressure gas flows into a column where the carbon dioxide and other trace elements are scrubbed by cascading water running counter-flow to the gas. This arrangement could deliver 98% methane with manufacturers guaranteeing maximum 2% methane loss in the system. It takes roughly between 3% and 6% of the total energy output in gas to run a biogas upgrading system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas_gas-grid_injection">Biogas gas-grid injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biogas gas-grid injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gas-grid injection is the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/injection" class="extiw" title="wikt:injection">injection</a> of biogas into the <a href="/wiki/Methane_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane grid">methane grid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural gas grid">natural gas grid</a>). Injections includes biogas<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> until the breakthrough of <a href="/wiki/Micro_combined_heat_and_power" title="Micro combined heat and power">micro combined heat and power</a> two-thirds of all the energy produced by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">biogas power plants</a> was lost (the heat), using the grid to transport the gas to customers, the electricity and the heat can be used for <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">on-site generation</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> resulting in a reduction of losses in the transportation of energy. Typical energy losses in natural gas transmission systems range from 1% to 2%. The current energy losses on a large electrical system range from 5% to 8%.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>If concentrated and compressed, it can be used in vehicle transportation. Compressed biogas is becoming widely used in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and Germany. A biogas-powered train, named Biogaståget Amanda (The Biogas Train Amanda), has been in service in Sweden since 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Biogas powers automobiles. In 1974, a British documentary film titled <i>Sweet as a Nut</i> detailed the biogas production process from pig manure and showed how it fueled a custom-adapted combustion engine.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2007, an estimated 12,000 vehicles were being fueled with upgraded biogas worldwide, mostly in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-What_is_biogas_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_is_biogas-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_in_biogas_environments">Measuring in biogas environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Measuring in biogas environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biogas is part of the wet gas and condensing gas (or air) category that includes mist or fog in the gas stream. The mist or fog is predominately water vapor that condenses on the sides of pipes or stacks throughout the gas flow. Biogas environments include wastewater digesters, landfills, and animal feeding operations (covered livestock lagoons).</p>
<p>Ultrasonic flow meters are one of the few devices capable of measuring in a biogas atmosphere. Most thermal flow meters are unable to provide reliable data because the moisture causes steady high flow readings and continuous flow spiking, although there are single-point insertion thermal mass flow meters capable of accurately monitoring biogas flows with minimal pressure drop. They can handle moisture variations that occur in the flow stream because of daily and seasonal temperature fluctuations, and account for the moisture in the flow stream to produce a dry gas value.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The European Union has legislation regarding waste management and landfill sites called the <a href="/wiki/Landfill_Directive" title="Landfill Directive">Landfill Directive</a>.</p>
<p>Countries such as the United Kingdom and Germany now have legislation in force that provides farmers with long-term revenue and energy security.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States legislates against landfill gas as it contains <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">VOCs</a>. The United States <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> and <a href="/wiki/Title_40_of_the_Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Title 40 of the Code of Federal Regulations">Title 40 of the Code of Federal Regulations</a> (CFR) requires landfill owners to estimate the quantity of non-methane organic compounds (NMOCs) emitted. If the estimated NMOC emissions exceeds 50 tonnes per year, the landfill owner is required to collect the gas and treat it to remove the entrained NMOCs. Treatment of the landfill gas is usually by combustion. Because of the remoteness of landfill sites, it is sometimes not economically feasible to produce electricity from the gas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_developments">Global developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Global developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the many benefits of biogas, it is starting to become a popular source of energy and is starting to be used in the United States more. In 2003, the United States consumed 147 trillion BTU of energy from "landfill gas", about 0.6% of the total U.S. natural gas consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-What_is_biogas_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_is_biogas-32">[32]</a></sup> Methane biogas derived from cow manure is being tested in the U.S. According to a 2008 study, collected by the <i>Science and Children</i> magazine, methane biogas from cow manure would be sufficient to produce 100 billion <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hours</a> enough to power millions of homes across America. Furthermore, methane biogas has been tested to prove that it can reduce 99 million metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions or about 4% of the greenhouse gases produced by the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Vermont, for example, biogas generated on dairy farms was included in the CVPS Cow Power program. The program was originally offered by Central Vermont Public Service Corporation as a voluntary tariff and now with a recent merger with Green Mountain Power is now the GMP Cow Power Program. Customers can elect to pay a premium on their electric bill, and that premium is passed directly to the farms in the program. In <a href="/wiki/Sheldon,_Vermont" title="Sheldon, Vermont">Sheldon, Vermont</a>, Green Mountain Dairy has provided renewable energy as part of the Cow Power program. It started when the brothers who own the farm, Bill and Brian Rowell, wanted to address some of the manure management challenges faced by dairy farms, including manure odor, and nutrient availability for the crops they need to grow to feed the animals. They installed an anaerobic digester to process the cow and milking center waste from their 950 cows to produce renewable energy, a bedding to replace sawdust, and a plant-friendly fertilizer. The energy and environmental attributes are sold to the GMP Cow Power program. On average, the system run by the Rowells produces enough electricity to power 300 to 350 other homes. The generator capacity is about 300 kilowatts.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hereford,_Texas" title="Hereford, Texas">Hereford, Texas</a>, cow manure is being used to power an <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol power</a> plant. By switching to methane biogas, the ethanol power plant has saved 1000 barrels of oil a day. Over all, the power plant has reduced transportation costs and will be opening many more jobs for future power plants that will rely on biogas.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Oakley,_Kansas" title="Oakley, Kansas">Oakley, Kansas</a>, an ethanol plant considered to be one of the largest biogas facilities in North America is using <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Manure_Utilization_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Integrated Manure Utilization System">Integrated Manure Utilization System</a> "IMUS" to produce heat for its boilers by utilizing feedlot manure, municipal organics and ethanol plant waste. At full capacity the plant is expected to replace 90% of the fossil fuel used in the manufacturing process of ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The level of development varies greatly in Europe. While countries such as Germany, Austria and Sweden are fairly advanced in their use of biogas, there is a vast potential for this renewable energy source in the rest of the continent, especially in Eastern Europe. Different legal frameworks, education schemes and the availability of technology are among the prime reasons behind this untapped potential.<sup id="cite_ref-SEBE_Website_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEBE_Website-39">[39]</a></sup> Another challenge for the further progression of biogas has been negative public perception. <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2009, the European Biogas Association (EBA) was founded in Brussels as a non-profit organisation to promote the deployment of sustainable biogas production and use in Europe. EBA's strategy defines three priorities: establish biogas as an important part of Europe’s energy mix, promote source separation of household waste to increase the gas potential, and support the production of biomethane as vehicle fuel. In July 2013, it had 60 members from 24 countries across Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK">UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of September 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there are about 130 non-sewage biogas plants in the UK. Most are on-farm, and some larger facilities exist off-farm, which are taking food and consumer wastes.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 October 2010, biogas was injected into the UK gas grid for the first time. Sewage from over 30,000 Oxfordshire homes is sent to Didcot <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> works, where it is treated in an anaerobic digestor to produce biogas, which is then cleaned to provide gas for approximately 200 homes.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 the Green-Energy company <a href="/wiki/Ecotricity" title="Ecotricity">Ecotricity</a> announced their plans to build three grid-injecting digesters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Germany is Europe's biggest biogas producer<sup id="cite_ref-Eurobserv.27er_2010_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurobserv.27er_2010-44">[44]</a></sup> and the market leader in biogas technology.<sup id="cite_ref-BMU_2011a_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMU_2011a-45">[45]</a></sup> In 2010 there were 5,905 biogas plants operating throughout the country: Lower Saxony, Bavaria, and the eastern federal states are the main regions.<sup id="cite_ref-Fachverband_Biogas_2011_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fachverband_Biogas_2011-46">[46]</a></sup> Most of these plants are employed as power plants. Usually the biogas plants are directly connected with a CHP which produces electric power by burning the bio methane. The electrical power is then fed into the public power grid.<sup id="cite_ref-IEA_2007_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_2007-47">[47]</a></sup> In 2010, the total installed electrical capacity of these power plants was 2,291 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-Fachverband_Biogas_2011_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fachverband_Biogas_2011-46">[46]</a></sup> The electricity supply was approximately 12.8 TWh, which is 12.6% of the total generated renewable electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-BMU_2011b_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMU_2011b-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biogas in Germany is primarily extracted by the co-fermentation of energy crops (called 'NawaRo', an abbreviation of <i>nachwachsende Rohstoffe</i>, German for renewable resources) mixed with manure. The main crop used is corn. Organic waste and industrial and agricultural residues such as waste from the food industry are also used for biogas generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Wieland.2C_P._2003_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wieland.2C_P._2003-49">[49]</a></sup> In this respect, biogas production in Germany differs significantly from the UK, where biogas generated from landfill sites is most common.<sup id="cite_ref-Eurobserv.27er_2010_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurobserv.27er_2010-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biogas production in Germany has developed rapidly over the last 20 years. The main reason is the legally created frameworks. Government support of renewable energy started in 1991 with the Electricity Feed-in Act (<i>StrEG</i>). This law guaranteed the producers of energy from renewable sources the feed into the public power grid, thus the power companies were forced to take all produced energy from independent private producers of green energy.<sup id="cite_ref-IfnE_et_al._2009_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IfnE_et_al._2009-50">[50]</a></sup> In 2000 the Electricity Feed-in Act was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/German_Renewable_Energy_Sources_Act" title="German Renewable Energy Sources Act">Renewable Energy Sources Act</a> (<i>EEG</i>). This law even guaranteed a fixed compensation for the produced electric power over 20 years. The amount of around 8<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>¢/kWh gave farmers the opportunity to become energy suppliers and gain a further source of income.<sup id="cite_ref-Wieland.2C_P._2003_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wieland.2C_P._2003-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The German agricultural biogas production was given a further push in 2004 by implementing the so-called NawaRo-Bonus. This is a special payment given for the use of renewable resources, that is, energy crops.<sup id="cite_ref-Wieland.2C_P._2006_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wieland.2C_P._2006-51">[51]</a></sup> In 2007 the German government stressed its intention to invest further effort and support in improving the renewable energy supply to provide an answer on growing climate challenges and increasing oil prices by the ‘Integrated Climate and Energy Programme’.</p>
<p>This continual trend of renewable energy promotion induces a number of challenges facing the management and organisation of renewable energy supply that has also several impacts on the biogas production.<sup id="cite_ref-Kanning_et_al._2009_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kanning_et_al._2009-52">[52]</a></sup> The first challenge to be noticed is the high area-consuming of the biogas electric power supply. In 2011 energy crops for biogas production consumed an area of circa 800,000 ha in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-FNR_2011_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FNR_2011-53">[53]</a></sup> This high demand of agricultural areas generates new competitions with the food industries that did not exist hitherto. Moreover, new industries and markets were created in predominately rural regions entailing different new players with an economic, political and civil background. Their influence and acting has to be governed to gain all advantages this new source of energy is offering. Finally biogas will furthermore play an important role in the German renewable energy supply if good governance is focused.<sup id="cite_ref-Kanning_et_al._2009_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kanning_et_al._2009-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Indian Subcontinent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biogas in India<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> has been traditionally based on dairy manure as feed stock and these "gobar" gas plants have been in operation for a long period of time, especially in rural India. In the last 2-3 decades, research organisations with a focus on rural energy security have enhanced the design of the systems resulting in newer efficient low cost designs such as the Deenabandhu model.</p>
<p>The Deenabandhu Model is a new biogas-production model popular in India. (<i>Deenabandhu</i> means "friend of the helpless.") The unit usually has a capacity of 2 to 3 cubic metres. It is constructed using bricks or by a <a href="/wiki/Ferrocement" title="Ferrocement">ferrocement</a> mixture. In India, the brick model costs slightly more than the ferrocement model; however, India's Ministry of New and Renewable Energy offers some subsidy per model constructed.</p>
<p>LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) is a key source of cooking fuel in urban India and its prices have been increasing along with the global fuel prices. Also the heavy subsidies provided by the successive governments in promoting LPG as a domestic cooking fuel has become a financial burden renewing the focus on biogas as a cooking fuel alternative in urban establishments. This has led to the development of prefabricated digester for modular deployments as compared to RCC and cement structures which take a longer duration to construct. Renewed focus on process technology like the Biourja process model<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> has enhanced the stature of medium and large scale anaerobic digester in India as a potential alternative to LPG as primary cooking fuel.</p>
<p>In India, Nepal, Pakistan and Bangladesh biogas produced from the anaerobic digestion of <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a> in small-scale digestion facilities is called <a href="/wiki/Biogas#Gobar_gas" title="Biogas">gobar gas</a>; it is estimated that such facilities exist in over 2 million households in India, 50,000 in Bangladesh and thousands in Pakistan, particularly North Punjab, due to the thriving population of livestock. The digester is an airtight circular pit made of concrete with a pipe connection. The manure is directed to the pit, usually straight from the cattle shed. The pit is filled with a required quantity of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>. The gas pipe is connected to the kitchen fireplace through control valves. The combustion of this biogas has very little odour or smoke. Owing to simplicity in implementation and use of cheap raw materials in villages, it is one of the most environmentally sound energy sources for rural needs. One type of these system is the Sintex Digester. Some designs use <a href="/wiki/Vermiculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Vermiculture">vermiculture</a> to further enhance the slurry produced by the biogas plant for use as compost.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>To create awareness and associate the people interested in biogas, the Indian Biogas Association<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> was formed. It aspires to be a unique blend of nationwide operators, manufacturers and planners of biogas plants, and representatives from science and research. The association was founded in 2010 and is now ready to start mushrooming. Its motto is "propagating Biogas in a sustainable way".</p>
<p>In Pakistan, the Rural Support Programmes Network is running the Pakistan Domestic Biogas Programme<sup id="cite_ref-pakenergysolution_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pakenergysolution-58">[58]</a></sup> which has installed 5,360 biogas plants<sup id="cite_ref-PakBioGasPlants_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PakBioGasPlants-59">[59]</a></sup> and has trained in excess of 200 masons on the technology and aims to develop the Biogas Sector in Pakistan.</p>
<p>In Nepal, the government provides subsidies to build biogas plant at home.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Chinese have experimented with the applications of biogas since 1958. Around 1970, China had installed 6,000,000 digesters in an effort to make <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> more efficient. During the last years the technology has met high growth rates. This seems to be the earliest developments in generating biogas from agricultural waste.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_developing_nations">In developing nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In developing nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Domestic biogas plants convert livestock manure and night soil into biogas and slurry, the fermented manure. This technology is feasible for small-holders with livestock producing 50&#160;kg manure per day, an equivalent of about 6 pigs or 3 cows. This manure has to be collectable to mix it with water and feed it into the plant. Toilets can be connected. Another precondition is the temperature that affects the fermentation process. With an optimum at 36 C° the technology especially applies for those living in a (sub) tropical climate. This makes the technology for small holders in developing countries often suitable.<sup id="cite_ref-Roub.C3.ADk_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roub.C3.ADk-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>Depending on size and location, a typical brick made fixed dome biogas plant can be installed at the yard of a rural household with the investment between US$300 to $500 in Asian countries and up to $1400 in the African context.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> A high quality biogas plant needs minimum maintenance costs and can produce gas for at least 15–20 years without major problems and re-investments. For the user, biogas provides clean cooking energy, reduces indoor air pollution, and reduces the time needed for traditional biomass collection, especially for women and children. The slurry is a clean organic fertilizer that potentially increases agricultural productivity.<sup id="cite_ref-Roub.C3.ADk_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roub.C3.ADk-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Domestic biogas technology is a proven and established technology in many parts of the world, especially Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Several countries in this region have embarked on large-scale programmes on domestic biogas, such as China<sup id="cite_ref-China_Biogas_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China_Biogas-64">[64]</a></sup> and India.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/SNV_Netherlands_Development_Organisation" title="SNV Netherlands Development Organisation">Netherlands Development Organisation</a>, SNV,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> supports national programmes on domestic biogas that aim to establish commercial-viable domestic biogas sectors in which local companies market, install and service biogas plants for households. In Asia, <a href="/wiki/SNV_Netherlands_Development_Organisation" title="SNV Netherlands Development Organisation">SNV</a> is working in Nepal,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Vietnam,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Bangladesh,<sup id="cite_ref-idcol.org_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idcol.org-68">[68]</a></sup> Bhutan, Cambodia,<sup id="cite_ref-idcol.org_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idcol.org-68">[68]</a></sup> Lao PDR,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Pakistan<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and Indonesia,<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> and in Africa; Rwanda,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Senegal, Burkina Faso, Ethiopia,<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Tanzania,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Uganda, Kenya,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Benin and Cameroon.</p>
<p>In South Africa a prebuilt Biogas system is manufactured and sold. One key feature is that installation requires less skill and is quicker to install as the digester tank is premade plastic.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biogas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1985 Australian film <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome" title="Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome">Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</a></i> the post-apocalyptic settlement Barter town is powered by a central biogas system based upon a piggery. As well as providing electricity, methane is used to power Barter's vehicles.</p>
<p>"Cow Town", written in the early 1940s, discuss the travails of a city vastly built on cow manure and the hardships brought upon by the resulting methane biogas. Carter McCormick, an engineer from a town outside the city, is sent in to figure out a way to utilize this gas to help power, rather than suffocate, the city.</p>
