<p>The <b>United States Department of Agriculture</b> (<b>USDA</b>), also known as the <b>Agriculture Department</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_executive_departments" title="United States federal executive departments">federal executive department</a> responsible for developing and executing federal laws related to farming, agriculture, forestry, and food. It aims to meet the needs of farmers and ranchers, promote agricultural trade and production, work to assure food safety, protect natural resources, foster rural communities and end hunger in the United States and internationally.</p>
<p>Approximately 80% of USDA's $140 billion budget goes to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Nutrition_Service" title="Food and Nutrition Service">Food and Nutrition Service</a> (FNS) program. The largest component of the FNS budget is the <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program</a> (formerly known as the Food Stamp program), which is the cornerstone of USDA's nutrition assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Vilsack" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Vilsack">Thomas Vilsack</a> on January 13, 2017 and the departure of President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> from office on January 20, 2017, the acting <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Agriculture" title="United States Secretary of Agriculture">Secretary of Agriculture</a> is Michael Young.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of the programs concerned with the distribution of food and nutrition to people of America and providing nourishment as well as <a href="/wiki/Nutrition_Education" title="Nutrition Education">nutrition education</a> to those in need are run and operated under the USDA Food and Nutrition Service. Activities in this program include the <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program</a>, which provides healthy food to over 40 million low-income and homeless people each month.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> USDA is a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Interagency_Council_on_Homelessness" title="United States Interagency Council on Homelessness">United States Interagency Council on Homelessness</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where it is committed to working with other agencies to ensure these mainstream benefits are accessed by those experiencing homelessness.</p>
<p>The USDA also is concerned with assisting farmers and food producers with the sale of crops and food on both the domestic and world markets. It plays a role in overseas aid programs by providing surplus foods to developing countries. This aid can go through <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">USAID</a>, foreign governments, international bodies such as <a href="/wiki/World_Food_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="World Food Program">World Food Program</a>, or approved nonprofits. The <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Act_of_1949" title="Agricultural Act of 1949">Agricultural Act of 1949</a>, section 416 (b) and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Trade_Development_and_Assistance_Act_of_1954" title="Agricultural Trade Development and Assistance Act of 1954">Agricultural Trade Development and Assistance Act of 1954</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Food_for_Peace" title="Food for Peace">Food for Peace</a>, provides the legal basis of such actions. The USDA is a partner of the <a href="/wiki/World_Cocoa_Foundation" title="World Cocoa Foundation">World Cocoa Foundation</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in its history, the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_States" title="Economy of the United States">economy of the United States</a> was largely <a href="/wiki/Agrarian_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Agrarian economy">agrarian</a>. Officials in the federal government had long sought new and improved varieties of seeds, plants and animals for import into the United States. In 1837 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Leavitt_Ellsworth" title="Henry Leavitt Ellsworth">Henry Leavitt Ellsworth</a>, a Yale-educated attorney interested in improving agriculture, became Commissioner of Patents, a position within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">Department of State</a>. He began collecting and distributing new varieties of seeds and plants through members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> and agricultural societies. In 1839, Congress established the Agricultural Division within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">Patent Office</a> and allotted $1,000 for "the collection of agricultural statistics and other agricultural purposes".</p>
<p>Ellsworth's interest in aiding agriculture was evident in his annual reports that called for a public depository to preserve and distribute the new seeds and plants, a clerk to collect agricultural statistics, statewide reports about crops in different regions, and the application of chemistry to agriculture. Ellsworth was called the "Father of the Department of Agriculture".</p>
<p>In 1849, the Patent Office was transferred to the newly created <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a>. In the ensuing years, agitation for a separate <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">bureau</a> of agriculture within the department or a separate department devoted to agriculture kept recurring.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_subsequent_history">Formation and subsequent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Formation and subsequent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>On May 15, 1862, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> established the independent Department of Agriculture to be headed by a commissioner without Cabinet status, and the agriculturalist <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton_(agriculturalist)" title="Isaac Newton (agriculturalist)">Isaac Newton</a> was appointed to be the first such commissioner.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Lincoln called it the "people's department". In the 1880s, varied <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">advocacy groups</a> were lobbying for Cabinet representation. Business interests sought a Department of Commerce and Industry, and farmers tried to raise the Department of Agriculture to Cabinet rank. In 1887, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> passed bills giving Cabinet status to the Department of Agriculture and Labor, but the bill was defeated in <a href="/wiki/Conference_committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Conference committee">conference committee</a> after farm interests objected to the addition of labor. Finally, on February 9, 1889, President <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> signed a bill into law elevating the Department of Agriculture to Cabinet level.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1887, the <a href="/wiki/Hatch_Act_of_1887" title="Hatch Act of 1887">Hatch Act</a> provided for the federal funding of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_experiment_station" title="Agricultural experiment station">agricultural experiment stations</a> in each state. The <a href="/wiki/Smith-Lever_Act_of_1914" class="mw-redirect" title="Smith-Lever Act of 1914">Smith-Lever Act of 1914</a> then funded <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_extension_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Cooperative extension service">cooperative extension services</a> in each state to teach agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a>, and other subjects to the public. With these and similar provisions, the USDA reached out to every county of every state.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, farming remained a common way of life for millions of Americans. The Department of Agriculture's <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Home_Economics" title="Bureau of Home Economics">Bureau of Home Economics</a>, established in 1923, published shopping advice and recipes to stretch family budgets and make food go farther.<sup id="cite_ref-Square_Meal_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Square_Meal-7">[7]</a></sup> USDA helped ensure that food continued to be produced and distributed to those who needed it, assisted with loans for small landowners, and contributed to the education of the rural youth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination">Discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allegations have been made that throughout the agency's history that its personnel have discriminated against farmers of various backgrounds, denying them loans and access to other programs well into the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The effect of this discrimination has been the reduction in the number of African-American farmers in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1999, the USDA settled a class action lawsuit, the <a href="/wiki/Pigford_v._Glickman" title="Pigford v. Glickman">Pigford Case</a>, alleging discrimination against African-American farmers in the late twentieth century. The government's settlement of nearly $1 billion with more than 13,300 farmers was reportedly the largest civil rights claim to date.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickert_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickert-13">[13]</a></sup> The 2008 Farm Bill provided for additional farmers to have their claims heard, as 70,000 had filed late in the original program.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickert_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickert-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2010 the federal government made another $1.2 billion settlement in what is called <i>Pigford II</i> for outstanding claims.<sup id="cite_ref-CRS_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many black farmers across the nation experienced discrimination in their dealings with in-state USDA agencies. Across the nation, black farmers alleged, and the USDA later agreed, they were denied access to loans and subsidies provided by the government.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1-15">[15]</a></sup> On a national level, farm subsidies that were afforded to white farmers were not afforded to black farmers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Since they were denied government loans, emergency or disaster assistance, and other aid, many black farmers lost their farms and homes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigford_v._Glickman"><i>Pigford v. Glickman</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pigford v. Glickman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pigford_v._Glickman" title="Pigford v. Glickman">Pigford v. Glickman</a></div>
<p>Following long-standing concerns, black farmers joined a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> discrimination suit against the USDA filed in federal court in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-nationalaglawcenter1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nationalaglawcenter1-18">[18]</a></sup> An attorney called it "the most organized, largest civil rights case in the history of the country".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1997, black farmers from at least five states held protests in front of the USDA headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, D.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Protests in front of the USDA were a strategy employed in later years as the black farmers sought to keep national attention focused on the plight of the black farmers. Representatives of the <a href="/wiki/National_Black_Farmers_Association" title="National Black Farmers Association">National Black Farmers Association</a> met with President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> and other administration officials at the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>. And NBFA's president testified before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Agriculture" title="United States House Committee on Agriculture">United States House Committee on Agriculture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Pigford_v._Glickman" title="Pigford v. Glickman">Pigford v. Glickman</a></i>, U.S. Federal District Court Judge <a href="/wiki/Paul_L._Friedman" title="Paul L. Friedman">Paul L. Friedman</a> approved the settlement and consent decree on April 14, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-nationalaglawcenter1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nationalaglawcenter1-18">[18]</a></sup> The settlement recognized discrimination against 22,363 black farmers but the NBFA would later call the agreement incomplete because more than 70,000 were excluded.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the settlement was deemed to be the largest-ever civil rights class action settlement in American history. Lawyers estimated the value of the settlement to be more than $2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1-15">[15]</a></sup> Some farmers would have their debts forgiven.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Judge Friedman appointed a monitor to oversee the settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Farmers in Alabama, Mississippi, Arkansas, and Georgia were among those affected by the settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reopening_of_case">Reopening of case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reopening of case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The NBFA's president was invited to testify before congress on this matter numerous times following the settlement including before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senate Committee on Agriculture">United States Senate Committee on Agriculture</a> on September 12, 2000, when he testified that many farmers had not yet received payments and others were left out of the settlement. NBFA asked Congress to pass legislation that would ensure a full resolution of the discrimination cases.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Working_Group" title="Environmental Working Group">Environmental Working Group</a> and NBFA issued a report in July 2004 accusing the USDA of withholding nearly three out of every four dollars in the multibillion-dollar settlement of discrimination cases.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The report says that the U.S. Department of Justice and the USDA pursued a path of "willful obstruction of justice" in blocking many of the cases. It was later revealed that one DoJ staff "general attorney" was unlicensed while she was handling black farmers' cases.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> NBFA called for all those cases to be reheard.</p>
<p>In 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> (GAO) issued a report highly critical of the USDA in its handling of the black farmers cases.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>NBFA continued to lobby Congress to provide relief. NBFA's Boyd secured congressional support for legislation that would provide $100 million in funds to settle late-filer cases. In 2006 a bill was introduced into the House of Representatives and later the Senate by Senator <a href="/wiki/George_Felix_Allen" class="mw-redirect" title="George Felix Allen">George Felix Allen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007 Boyd testified before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_the_Judiciary" title="United States House Committee on the Judiciary">United States House Committee on the Judiciary</a> about this legislation.</p>
<p>As the organization was making headway by gathering Congressional supporters in 2007 it was revealed that some USDA Farm Services Agency employees were engaged in activities aimed at blocking Congressional legislation that would aid the black farmers.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, then a U.S. Senator, lent his support to the black farmers' issues in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A bill cosponsored by Obama passed the Senate in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Senate and House versions of the black farmers bill, reopening black farmers discrimination cases, became law in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The new law could affect up to 74,000 black farmers according to some news reports.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 hundreds of black farmers, denied a chance to have their cases heard in the <i>Pigford</i> settlement, filed a new lawsuit against USDA.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the GAO issued a report criticizing the USDA's handling of discrimination complaints.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The GAO recommended an oversight review board to examine civil rights complaints.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>After numerous public rallies and an intensive NBFA member lobbying effort, Congress approved and Obama signed into law in December 2010 legislation that set aside $1.15 billion to resolve the outstanding black farmers cases.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> NBFA's John W. Boyd, Jr., attended the bill-signing ceremony at the White House.</p>
<p>As of 2013, 90,000 African-American, Hispanic, female and Native American farmers had filed claims, some fraudulent, or even transparently bogus. Lack of documentation is an issue complicated by the USDA practice of discarding denied applications after three years. In <a href="/wiki/Maple_Hill,_North_Carolina" title="Maple Hill, North Carolina">Maple Hill</a>, North Carolina, the number of successful claimants was four times the number of farms with 1 out of 9 African-Americans being paid.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT42512_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT42512-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The result of such longstanding discrimination is that black farmers have been forced out of business at a rate three times faster than white farmers. In 1920, 1 in 7 U.S. farmers was African-American, and now the number is 1 in 100. USDA spokesman Ed Loyd, when acknowledging that the USDA loan process was unfair to minority farmers, has claimed it is hard to determine the effect on such farmers.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
