			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Jevons paradox</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jevons_Paradox&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Jevons Paradox">Jevons Paradox</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>

Coal-burning factories in <a href="/wiki/Cottonopolis" title="Cottonopolis">19th-century Manchester</a>, England. Improved technology allowed coal to fuel the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, greatly increasing the consumption of coal.</div>
</div>

<p>In economics, the <b>Jevons paradox</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; sometimes the <b>Jevons effect</b>) occurs when <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological progress</a> increases the <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a> with which a <a href="/wiki/Resource_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resource (economics)">resource</a> is used (reducing the amount necessary for any one use), but the rate of <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of that resource rises because of increasing <a href="/wiki/Demand_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand (economics)">demand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Jevons paradox is perhaps the most widely known paradox in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-York_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-York-2">[2]</a></sup> However, governments and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a> generally assume that efficiency gains will lower <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a>, ignoring the possibility of the paradox arising.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1865, the English economist <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">William Stanley Jevons</a> observed that technological improvements that increased the efficiency of coal-use led to the increased consumption of coal in a wide range of industries. He argued that, contrary to common intuition, technological progress could not be relied upon to reduce fuel consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Coal_Question_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coal_Question-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alcott2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The issue has been re-examined by modern economists studying consumption <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effects</a> from improved <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>. In addition to reducing the amount needed for a given use, improved efficiency also lowers the relative cost of using a resource, which increases the quantity demanded. This counteracts (to some extent) the reduction in use from improved efficiency. Additionally, improved efficiency accelerates <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, further increasing the demand for resources. The Jevons paradox occurs when the effect from increased demand predominates, and better efficiency leads to more resources being used.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Considerable debate exists about the size of the rebound in energy efficiency and the relevance of the Jevons paradox to <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a>. Some dismiss the paradox, while others worry that it may be self-defeating to pursue <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> by increasing energy efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott1-3">[3]</a></sup> Environmental economists have proposed that efficiency gains be coupled with conservation policies that keep the cost of use the same (or higher) to avoid the Jevons paradox.<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel-6">[6]</a></sup> Conservation policies (such as <a href="/wiki/Cap_and_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Cap and trade">cap and trade</a>) do not display the paradox, and can be used to control the rebound effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Energy_Efficiency_Policies_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Efficiency_Policies-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jevons_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Engraving of William Stanley Jevons" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg/220px-PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg/330px-PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg/440px-PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1793" data-file-height="2397" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PSM_V11_D660_William_Stanley_Jevons.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">William Stanley Jevons</a>, after whom the paradox is named</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Jevons paradox was first described by the English economist <a href="/wiki/William_Stanley_Jevons" title="William Stanley Jevons">William Stanley Jevons</a> in his 1865 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Coal_Question" title="The Coal Question">The Coal Question</a></i>. Jevons observed that <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>'s consumption of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> soared after <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Watt_steam_engine" title="Watt steam engine">Watt steam engine</a>, which greatly improved the efficiency of the coal-fired <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newcomen" title="Thomas Newcomen">Thomas Newcomen</a>'s earlier design. Watt's innovations made coal a more cost-effective power source, leading to the increased use of the steam engine in a wide range of industries. This in turn increased total coal consumption, even as the amount of coal required for any particular application fell. Jevons argued that improvements in fuel efficiency tend to increase (rather than decrease) fuel use, writing: "It is a confusion of ideas to suppose that the economical use of fuel is equivalent to diminished consumption. The very contrary is the truth."<sup id="cite_ref-Coal_Question_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coal_Question-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At that time, many in Britain worried that coal reserves were rapidly dwindling, but some experts opined that improving technology would reduce coal consumption. Jevons argued that this view was incorrect, as further increases in efficiency would tend to increase the use of coal. Hence, improving technology would tend to increase the rate at which England's coal deposits were being depleted, and could not be relied upon to solve the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-Coal_Question_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coal_Question-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alcott2_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Jevons originally focused on the issue of coal, the concept has since been extended to the use of any resource, including, for example, <a href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">water usage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It is perhaps the most widely known paradox in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-York_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-York-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jevons_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">Rebound effect (conservation)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ElasticDemand.svg" class="image"><img alt="Diagram showing a shallow demand curve, where a drop in price from $100 to $80 causes quantity to increase from 10 to 14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/ElasticDemand.svg/320px-ElasticDemand.svg.png" width="320" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/ElasticDemand.svg/480px-ElasticDemand.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/ElasticDemand.svg/640px-ElasticDemand.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1052" data-file-height="744" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ElasticDemand.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Elastic Demand</i>: A 20% increase in efficiency causes a 40% increase in travel. Fuel consumption increases and the Jevons paradox occurs.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:InelasticDemand.svg" class="image"><img alt="Diagram showing a steep demand curve, where a drop in price from $100 to $80 causes quantity to increase from 10 to 11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/InelasticDemand.svg/320px-InelasticDemand.svg.png" width="320" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/InelasticDemand.svg/480px-InelasticDemand.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/InelasticDemand.svg/640px-InelasticDemand.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1052" data-file-height="744" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:InelasticDemand.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Inelastic Demand</i>: A 20% increase in efficiency causes a 10% increase in travel. The Jevons paradox does not occur.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Economists have observed that consumers tend to travel more when their cars are more fuel efficient, causing a 'rebound' in the <a href="/wiki/Demand_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand (economics)">demand</a> for fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-Small_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Small-9">[9]</a></sup> An increase in the efficiency with which a resource (e.g. fuel) is used, causes a decrease in the <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> of using that resource when measured in terms of what it can achieve (e.g. travel). Generally speaking, a decrease in the cost (or price) of a <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">good or service</a> will increase the quantity demanded (the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_demand" title="Law of demand">law of demand</a>). With a lower cost for travel, consumers will travel more, increasing the demand for fuel. This increase in demand is known as the <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effect</a>, and it may or may not be large enough to offset the original drop in demand from the increased efficiency. The Jevons paradox occurs when the rebound effect is greater than 100%, exceeding the original efficiency gains.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott2_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The size of the rebound effect is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Price_elasticity_of_demand" title="Price elasticity of demand">price elasticity of demand</a> for the good. In a perfectly competitive market where fuel is the sole input used, if the price of fuel remains constant but efficiency is doubled, the effective price of travel would be halved (twice as much travel can be purchased). If in response, the amount of travel purchased more than doubles (i.e. demand is <a href="/wiki/Price_elastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Price elastic">price elastic</a>), then fuel consumption would increase, and the Jevons paradox would occur. If demand is price inelastic, the amount of travel purchased would less than double, and fuel consumption would decrease. However, goods and services generally use more than one type of input (e.g. fuel, labour, machinery), and other factors besides input cost may also affect price. These factors tend to reduce the rebound effect, making the Jevons paradox less likely to occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott2_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Khazzoom.E2.80.93Brookes_postulate">Khazzoom–Brookes postulate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jevons_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Khazzoom–Brookes postulate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Khazzoom%E2%80%93Brookes_postulate" title="Khazzoom–Brookes postulate">Khazzoom–Brookes postulate</a></div>
<p>In the 1980s, economists Daniel Khazzoom and Leonard Brookes revisited the Jevons paradox for the case of society's <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">energy use</a>. Brookes, then chief economist at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Atomic_Energy_Authority" title="United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority">UK Atomic Energy Authority</a>, argued that attempts to reduce energy consumption by increasing <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> would simply raise demand for energy in the economy as a whole. Khazzoom focused on the narrower point that the potential for rebound was ignored in mandatory performance standards for domestic appliances being set by the <a href="/wiki/California_Energy_Commission" title="California Energy Commission">California Energy Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Saunders_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1992, the economist Harry Saunders dubbed the hypothesis that improvements in energy efficiency work to increase (rather than decrease) energy consumption the <i>Khazzoom–Brookes postulate</i>, and argued that the hypothesis is broadly supported by neoclassical <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">growth theory</a> (the mainstream economic theory of <a href="/wiki/Capital_accumulation" title="Capital accumulation">capital accumulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological progress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long-run" class="mw-redirect" title="Long-run">long-run</a> economic growth). Saunders showed that the Khazzoom–Brookes postulate occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_growth_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Neoclassical growth model">neoclassical growth model</a> under a wide range of assumptions.<sup id="cite_ref-Saunders_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sorrell_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorrell-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Saunders, increased energy efficiency tends to increase energy consumption by two means. First, increased energy efficiency makes the use of energy relatively cheaper, thus encouraging increased use (the direct rebound effect). Second, increased energy efficiency leads to increased economic growth, which pulls up energy use for the whole economy. At the <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomic</a> level (looking at an individual market), even with the rebound effect, improvements in energy efficiency usually result in reduced energy consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Greening_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greening-13">[13]</a></sup> That is, the rebound effect is usually less than 100%. However, at the <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomic</a> level, more efficient (and hence comparatively cheaper) energy leads to faster economic growth, which increases energy use throughout the economy. Saunders argued that, taking into account both microeconomic and macroeconomic effects, technological progress that improves energy efficiency will tend to increase overall energy use.<sup id="cite_ref-Saunders_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Jevons warned that fuel efficiency gains tend to increase fuel use. But this does not imply that improved fuel efficiency is worthless: higher fuel efficiency enables greater production and a higher material <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For example, a more efficient steam engine allowed the cheaper transport of goods and people that contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. However, if the Khazzoom–Brookes postulate is correct, increased fuel efficiency, by itself, will not reduce the rate of depletion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Saunders_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saunders-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is considerable debate about whether the Khazzoom-Brookes Postulate is correct, and of the relevance of the Jevons paradox to <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> policy. Most governments, environmentalists and NGOs pursue policies that improve efficiency, holding that it will lower resource consumption and reduce environmental problems. Others, including many <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economists" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental economists">environmental economists</a>, doubt this 'efficiency strategy' towards <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, and worry that efficiency gains may in fact lead to higher production and consumption. They hold that for resource use to fall, efficiency gains should be coupled with other policies that limit resource use.<sup id="cite_ref-Alcott1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alcott1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sorrell_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorrell-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Jevons paradox is sometimes used to argue that <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a> efforts are futile, for example, that more efficient use of oil will lead to increased demand, and will not slow the arrival or the effects of <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a>. This argument is usually presented as a reason not to enact environmental policies or pursue fuel efficiency (e.g. if cars are more efficient, it will simply lead to more driving).<sup id="cite_ref-Potter_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Potter-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Conservation_Wastes_Energy_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conservation_Wastes_Energy-17">[17]</a></sup> Several points have been raised against this argument. First, in the context of a mature market such as for oil in developed countries, the direct rebound effect is usually small, and so increased fuel efficiency usually reduces resource use, other conditions remaining constant.<sup id="cite_ref-Small_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Small-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Greening_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greening-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Second, even if increased efficiency does not reduce the total amount of fuel used, there remain other benefits associated with improved efficiency. For example, increased fuel efficiency may mitigate the price increases, shortages and disruptions in the global economy associated with peak oil.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Third, environmental economists have pointed out that fuel use will unambiguously decrease if increased efficiency is coupled with an intervention (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tax" title="Fuel tax">fuel tax</a>) that keeps the cost of fuel use the same or higher.<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Jevons paradox indicates that increased efficiency by itself may not reduce fuel use, and that <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a> policy must rely on other types of government interventions.<sup id="cite_ref-Energy_Efficiency_Policies_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Efficiency_Policies-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As the Jevons paradox applies only to technological improvements that increase fuel efficiency, the imposition of conservation standards or other government interventions that increase costs do not display the paradox and can be used to control the rebound effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Energy_Efficiency_Policies_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Efficiency_Policies-7">[7]</a></sup> To ensure that efficiency-enhancing technological improvements reduce fuel use, efficiency gains can be paired with government intervention that reduces demand (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ecotax" title="Ecotax">green taxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">cap and trade</a>, or higher <a href="/wiki/Emissions_standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Emissions standard">emissions standards</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economists</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathis_Wackernagel" title="Mathis Wackernagel">Mathis Wackernagel</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_E._Rees_(academic)" class="mw-redirect" title="William E. Rees (academic)">William Rees</a> have suggested that any cost savings from efficiency gains be "taxed away or otherwise removed from further economic circulation. Preferably they should be captured for reinvestment in natural capital rehabilitation."<sup id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel-6">[6]</a></sup> By mitigating the economic effects of government interventions designed to promote ecologically sustainable activities, efficiency-improving technological progress may make the imposition of these interventions more palatable, and more likely to be implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-Laitner_DeCanio_Peters_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laitner_DeCanio_Peters-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jevons.27s_Complementary_Corollary">Jevons's Complementary Corollary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jevons_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jevons's Complementary Corollary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Noted economist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Glaeser" title="Edward Glaeser">Edward Glaeser</a> has proposed what he calls "Jevons's  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Complementary Corollary", which</p>
<blockquote>
<p>predicts that improvements in information technology can lead to more demand for face-to-face contact, because face time complements time spent communicating electronically. All these electronic interactions [video conferencing, e-mail, texting, Facebook, etc.] are creating a more relationship-intensive world, just as improvements in steam engines led to a more coal-intensive economy; and those relationships need both e-mail and interpersonal contact. Better connections between people create far-ranging opportunities for trade and commerce.<sup id="cite_ref-triumph_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-triumph-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
