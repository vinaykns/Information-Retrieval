			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide removal</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Carbon dioxide removal</b> (CDR) methods refers to a number of technologies which reduce the levels of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">in the atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup> Among such technologies are <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">direct air capture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">ocean fertilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">enhanced weathering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup> CDR is a different approach than removing CO<sub>2</sub> from the stack emissions of large <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> point sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>. The latter reduces emission to the atmosphere but cannot reduce the amount of carbon dioxide already in the atmosphere. As CDR removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, it creates <a href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative emissions</a>, offsetting emissions from small and dispersed point sources such as domestic heating systems, airplanes and vehicle exhausts.<sup id="cite_ref-Vergragt_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vergragt-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Azar_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Azar-3">[3]</a></sup> It is regarded by some as a form of <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup> while other commentators describe it as a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> or extreme mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-GCCSI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GCCSI-4">[4]</a></sup> Whether CDR would satisfy common definitions of "climate engineering" or "geoengineering" usually depends upon the scale on which it would be undertaken.</p>
<p>The likely need for CDR has been publicly expressed by a range of individuals and organizations involved with climate change issues, including <a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> chief <a href="/wiki/Rajendra_Pachauri" class="mw-redirect" title="Rajendra Pachauri">Rajendra Pachauri</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/UNFCCC" class="mw-redirect" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> executive secretary <a href="/wiki/Christiana_Figueres" title="Christiana Figueres">Christiana Figueres</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Watch_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="World Watch Institute">World Watch Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Institutions with major programs focusing on CDR include the Lenfest Center for Sustainable Energy at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth Institute">Earth Institute, Columbia University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the Climate Decision Making Center,<sup id="cite_ref-CDMC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDMC-9">[9]</a></sup> an international collaboration operated out of Carnegie-Mellon University's Department of Engineering and Public Policy.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> effectiveness of air capture is limited by societal investment, land use, availability of geologic reservoirs, and leakage. The reservoirs are estimated to be sufficient to for storing at least 545 GtC.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Storing 771 GtC would cause an 186 ppm atmospheric reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-Lenton2009_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenton2009-11">[11]</a></sup> In order to return the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> content to 350 ppm we need atmospheric reduction of 50 ppm plus an additional 2 ppm per year of current emissions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_.26_storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture &amp; storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bio-energy with carbon capture &amp; storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage, or BECCS, uses biomass to extract carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, and carbon capture and storage technologies to concentrate and permanently store it in deep geological formations.</p>
<p>BECCS is currently (as of October 2012) the only CDR technology deployed at full industrial scale, with 550 000 tonnes CO<sub>2</sub>/year in total capacity operating, divided between three different facilities (as of January 2012).<sup id="cite_ref-Global_Status_of_BECCS_Projects_2010_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Status_of_BECCS_Projects_2010-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ECN_Global_Technology_Roadmap_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECN_Global_Technology_Roadmap-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>, the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tyndall_Centre_for_Climate_Change_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyndall Centre for Climate Change Research">Tyndall Centre for Climate Change Research</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.walker-institute.ac.uk/">Walker Institute for Climate System Research</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Grantham_Institute_for_Climate_Change" class="mw-redirect" title="Grantham Institute for Climate Change">Grantham Institute for Climate Change</a> issued a joint report on carbon dioxide removal technologies as part of the <i>AVOID: Avoiding dangerous climate change</i> research program, stating that "Overall, of the technologies studied in this report, <a href="/wiki/BECCS" class="mw-redirect" title="BECCS">BECCS</a> has the greatest maturity and there are no major practical barriers to its introduction into today’s energy system. The presence of a primary product will support early deployment."<sup id="cite_ref-AVOID_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AVOID-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/OECD" class="mw-redirect" title="OECD">OECD</a>, "Achieving lower concentration targets (450 ppm) depends significantly on the use of <a href="/wiki/BECCS" class="mw-redirect" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-OECD_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochar">Biochar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biochar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a> is created by the <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, and is under investigation as a method of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>. <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a> is a charcoal that is used for agricultural purposes which also aids in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>, the capture or hold of carbon. It is created using a process called pyrolysis, which is basically the act of high temperature heating biomass in an environment with low oxygen levels. What remains is a material known as char, similar to charcoal but is made through a sustainable process, thus the use of biomass.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Biomass is organic matter produced by living organisms or recently living organisms, most commonly plants or plant based material.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The offset of GHG emission, if biochar were to be implemented, would be a maximum of 12%. This equates to about 106 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents. On a medium conservative level, it would be 23% less than that, at 82 metric tons.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A study done by the UK Biochar Research Center has stated that, on a conservative level, biochar can store 1 gigaton of carbon per year. With greater effort in marketing and acceptance of biochar, the benefit would the storage of 5-9 gigatons per year of carbon in biochar soils.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_weathering">Enhanced weathering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enhanced weathering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">Enhanced weathering</a></div>
<p>Enhanced weathering refers to chemical approach to remove carbon dioxide involving land or ocean based techniques. Examples of land based enhanced weathering techniques are in-situ carbonation of silicates. <a href="/wiki/Ultramafic_rock" title="Ultramafic rock">Ultramafic rock</a>, for example, has the potential to store thousands of years worth of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions according to one estimate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ocean based techniques involve alkalinity enhancement, such as, grinding, dispersing and dissolving olivine, limestone, silicates, or calcium hydroxide to address ocean acidification and CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration. Enhanced weathering is considered as one of the least expensive of geoengineering options. One example of a research project on the feasibility of enhanced weathering is the <a href="/wiki/CarbFix" title="CarbFix">CarbFix</a> project in Iceland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_air_capture">Direct air capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Direct air capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide can be removed from ambient air through chemical processes, sequestered, and stored. One proposed method is by so-called <i>artificial trees</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This concept, proposed by climate scientist <a href="/wiki/Wallace_S._Broecker" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallace S. Broecker">Wallace S. Broecker</a> and science writer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kunzig" title="Robert Kunzig">Robert Kunzig</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> imagines huge numbers of artificial trees around the world to remove ambient CO<sub>2</sub>. The technology is now being pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Lackner" title="Klaus Lackner">Klaus Lackner</a>, a researcher at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth Institute">Earth Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> whose artificial tree technology can suck up to 1,000 times more CO<sub>2</sub> from the air than real trees can,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> at a rate of about one ton of carbon per day if the artificial tree is approximately the size of an actual tree.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> would be captured in a filter and then removed from the filter and stored.</p>
<p><br />
The chemistry used is a variant of that described <a href="#Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry">below</a>, as it is based on <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. However, in a more recent design proposed by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Lackner" title="Klaus Lackner">Klaus Lackner</a>, the process can be carried out at only 40&#160;°C by using a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange_resin" class="mw-redirect" title="Ion exchange resin">ion exchange resin</a>, which takes advantage of changes in <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> to prompt the release of captured CO<sub>2</sub>, instead of using a kiln. This reduces the energy required to operate the process.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another substance which can be used are <a href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">Metal-organic frameworks</a> (or MOF's).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A special MOF has been made specifically for locking CO<sub>2</sub> by Joeri Denayer.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a> covered<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the work of <a href="/wiki/David_Keith_(scientist)" title="David Keith (scientist)">David Keith</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Calgary" title="University of Calgary">University of Calgary</a>, who built a tower, 4 feet wide and 20 feet tall (1.2×6.1 meters), with a fan at the bottom that sucks air in, which comes out again at the top. In the process, about half the CO<sub>2</sub> is removed from the air.</p>
<p>This device uses the chemical process described in detail <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal#Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry" title="Carbon dioxide removal">below</a>. The system demonstrated on the Discovery Channel was a 1/90,000th scale test system of the capture section; the reagents are regenerated in a separate facility. The main costs of a full plant will be the cost to build it, and the energy input to regenerate the chemicals and produce a pure stream of CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<p>To put this into perspective, people in the U.S. emit about 20 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per person annually.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In other words, each person in the U.S. would require a tower like the one featured by the Discovery Channel to remove this amount of CO<sub>2</sub> from the air, requiring an annual 2 megawatt-hours of electricity to operate it. By comparison, a refrigerator consumes about 1.2 megawatt-hours annually (2001 figures).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> But, by combining many small systems such as this into one large system, the construction costs and energy use can be reduced.</p>
<p>It has been proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Solar_updraft_tower" title="Solar updraft tower">Solar updraft tower</a> to generate electricity from thermal air currents also be used at the same time for amine gravity scrubbing of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Some heat would be required to regenerate the amine.</p>
<p>A similar CO<sub>2</sub> scrubber has also been built by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Engineering" title="Carbon Engineering">Carbon Engineering</a>. Besides simply focusing on capturing the CO<sub>2</sub>, the company also puts emphasis on reuse of the CO<sub>2</sub>, for example in the production of fuels, which would thus be <a href="/wiki/Zero_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Zero emissions">carbon-neutral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Direct air capture has been proposed as a way of generating carbon-neutral organic chemicals, by harvesting the atmospheric compounds and then using them in the production and synthesis of polymers and fuels.</p>
<p>Finally the Swiss based Climeworks is currently building the first industrial scale direct air capturing plant in Hinwil, Switzerland. Starting in Mai 2017, the plant is going to scrub about 900 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> per year using heat from a local waste incineration plant. The CO<sub>2</sub> should be sold to a local vegetable growing company.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_fertilization">Ocean fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ocean fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">Ocean fertilization</a></div>
<p>Ocean fertilization or ocean nourishment is a type of climate engineering based on the purposeful introduction of nutrients to the upper ocean to increase marine food production and to remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. A number of techniques, including fertilization by iron, urea and phosphorus have been proposed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry">Example CO<sub>2</sub> scrubbing chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Example CO2 scrubbing chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">Carbon dioxide scrubber</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calcium_oxide">Calcium oxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Calcium oxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">Calcium oxide</a> (quicklime) will absorb CO<sub>2</sub> from <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric air</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> at 400&#160;°C (forming <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>) and release it at 1,000&#160;°C. This process, proposed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=A._Steinfeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A. Steinfeld (page does not exist)">A. Steinfeld</a>, can be performed using <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> from thermal <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newscientist1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist1-40">[40]</a></sup> Quicklime is made by heating limestone to release the CO<sub>2</sub> within it. Quicklime is mixed with sand for brick building as mortar, where it hardens by absorption of CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potassium_Hydroxide">Potassium Hydroxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Potassium Hydroxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zeman and Lackner outlined a specific method of air capture using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber#Potassium_hydroxide" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">sodium hydroxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Carbon Engineering, a Calgary, Alberta firm founded in 2009 and partially funded by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, is developing a process to capture carbon dioxide using a solution of potassium hydroxide mixed with some water at their <a href="/wiki/Pilot_plant" title="Pilot plant">pilot plant</a> . They hope to create and sell synthetic fuels at a cost of $100 a ton.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT01513_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT01513-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_issues">Economic issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economic issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A crucial issue for CDR methods is their cost, which differs substantially among the different technologies: some of these are not sufficiently developed to perform cost assessments. The <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> estimates the costs for direct air capture to be $600/tonne with optimistic assumptions.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The IEA Greenhouse Gas R&amp;D Programme and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecofys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ecofys (page does not exist)">Ecofys</a> provides an estimate that 3.5 billion tonnes could be removed annually from the atmosphere with <a href="/wiki/BECCS" class="mw-redirect" title="BECCS">BECCS</a> (Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage) at carbon prices as low as €50 per tonne,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> while a report from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Biorecro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Biorecro (page does not exist)">Biorecro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage_(timeline)#Global_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Institute" title="Carbon capture and storage (timeline)">Global Carbon Capture and Storage Institute</a> estimates costs "below €100" per tonne for large scale BECCS deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-GCCSI_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GCCSI-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks.2C_problems_and_criticisms">Risks, problems and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Risks, problems and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CDR is slow to act, and requires a long-term political and engineering program to effect.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> CDR is even slower to take effect on <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidified oceans</a>. In a <i>Business as usual</i> <a href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">concentration pathway</a>, the deep ocean will remain acidified for centuries, and as a consequence many marine species are in danger of extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
