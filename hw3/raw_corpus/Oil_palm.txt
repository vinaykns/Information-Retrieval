<p><i><b>Elaeis</b></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, meaning 'oil') is a genus of <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a> containing two species, called <b>oil palms</b>. They are used in commercial <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> in the production of <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a>. The African oil palm <i><a href="/wiki/Elaeis_guineensis" title="Elaeis guineensis">Elaeis guineensis</a></i> (the species name <i>guineensis</i> referring to its country of origin) is the principal source of palm oil, it is native to west and southwest <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, occurring between <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gambia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gambia">Gambia</a>. The American oil palm <i><a href="/wiki/Elaeis_oleifera" title="Elaeis oleifera">Elaeis oleifera</a></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">oleifer</span></i>, meaning 'oil-producing')<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is native to tropical <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and is used locally for oil production.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mature palms are single-stemmed, and can grow well over 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft) tall. The <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are <a href="/wiki/Pinnate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinnate">pinnate</a>, and reach between 3–5&#160;m (10–16&#160;ft) long. The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> are produced in dense clusters; each individual flower is small, with three sepals and three petals.</p>
<p>The palm fruit is reddish, about the size of a large plum, and grows in large bunches. Each fruit is made up of an oily, fleshy outer layer (the pericarp), with a single <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Palm_kernel" title="Palm kernel">palm kernel</a>), also rich in oil.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two species, <i>E. guineensis</i> and <i>E. oleifera</i> can produce fertile <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> of <i>E. guineensis</i> has been sequenced, which has important implications for breeding improved strains of the crop plants.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_palm_oil_cultivation">Commercial palm oil cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elaeis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial palm oil cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">Palm oil</a></div>
<p>Since palm oil contains more <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fats" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturated fats">saturated fats</a> than oils made from canola, corn, linseed, soybeans, safflower, and sunflowers, it can withstand extreme deep-frying heat and resists oxidation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It contains no <a href="/wiki/Trans_fat" title="Trans fat">trans fat</a>, and its use in food has increased as food-labelling laws have changed to specify trans fat content. Oil from <i>Elaeis guineensis</i> is also used as <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>.</p>
<p>Human use of oil palms may date back about 5,000 years in coastal west Africa. Palm oil was also discovered in the late 19th century by archaeologists in a tomb at <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">Abydos</a> dating back to 3000 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Kiple_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kiple-6">[6]</a></sup> It is thought that Arab traders brought the oil palm to Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-Obahiagbon_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obahiagbon-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Elaeis guineensis</i> is now extensively cultivated in tropical countries outside Africa, particularly <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> which together produce most of the world supply. Palm oil plantations are under increasing scrutiny for <a href="/wiki/Social_and_environmental_impact_of_palm_oil" title="Social and environmental impact of palm oil">social and environmental harm</a>, particularly because <a href="/wiki/Rainforests" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainforests">rainforests</a> with high <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> are destroyed, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> output is increased, and because people are displaced by unscrupulous palm-oil enterprises. Especially in Indonesia, there is also growing pressure for palm oil producers to prove that they are not harming rare animals in the cultivation process.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
