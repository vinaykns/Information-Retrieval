			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental politics</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">This article is about the discipline. For the journal, see <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Politics_(journal)" title="Environmental Politics (journal)">Environmental Politics (journal)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Environmental politics</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Academic_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic field">academic field</a> of study focused on three core components:<sup id="cite_ref-carter_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carter-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The study of <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">theories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">ideas</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>;</li>
<li>The examination of the environmental stances of both mainstream <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political parties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental social movements</a>; and</li>
<li>The analysis of <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policymaking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">implementation</a> affecting the environment, at multiple <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">geo-political</a> <a href="/wiki/Level_of_analysis" title="Level of analysis">levels</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Neil Carter, in his foundational text <i>Politics of the Environment</i> (2009), suggests that <i>environmental</i> politics is distinct in at least two ways: first, "it has a primary concern with the relationship between human society and the natural world" (p.&#160;3); and second, "unlike most other single issues, it comes replete with its own ideology and political movement" (p.&#160;5, drawing on Michael Jacobs, ed., <i>Greening the Millenium?</i>, 1997).<sup id="cite_ref-carter_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carter-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further, he distinguishes between modern and earlier forms of environmental politics, in particular <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservationism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilderness#Preservation" title="Wilderness">preservationism</a>. Contemporary environmental politics "was driven by the idea of a global ecological crisis that threatened the very existence of humanity." And "modern environmentalism was a political and activist mass movement which demanded a radical transformation in the values and structures of society."<sup id="cite_ref-carter_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carter-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Environmental concerns were rooted in the vast social changes that took place in the United States after World War II. Although environmentalism can be identified in earlier years, only after the war did it become widely shared social priority. This began with outdoor recreation in the 1950s, extended into the wider field of the protection of natural environments, and then became infused with attempts to cope with air and water pollution and still later with toxic chemical pollutants. After World War II, environmental politics became a major public concern.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The development of environmentalism in the United Kingdom emerged in this period following the great London smog of 1952 and the Torrey Canyon oil spill of 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is reflected by the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Green politics</a> in the Western world beginning in the 1970s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Democratic_challenges">Democratic challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Democratic challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate change is slow relative to political cycles of leadership in <a href="/wiki/Electoral_system" title="Electoral system">electoral democracies</a>, which impedes responses by politicians who are elected and re-elected on much shorter timescales.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, although "environmentalism" was once considered a White phenomenon, scholars have identified "pro-environment positions among Latino, African-American, and non-Hispanic white respondents," with growing environmental concern especially among Latinos.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Other scholars have similarly noted that Asian Americans are strongly pro-environmental, with some variation among ethnic subgroups.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Effectively responding to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> necessitates some form of international <a href="/wiki/Environmental_governance" title="Environmental governance">environmental governance</a> to achieve shared targets related to energy consumption and environmental usage.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Climate change complicates political ideology and practice, affecting conceptions of responsibility for future societies as well as economic systems.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Material inequality between nations make technological solutions insufficient for climate change mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Rather, political solutions can navigate the particularities of various facets of environmental crisis. Climate change mitigation strategies can be at odds with democratic priorities of prosperity, progress, and state sovereignty, and instead underscore a collective relationship with the environment.</p>
<p>The international political community is presently based on liberal principles that prioritize individual freedoms and capitalist systems that make quick and ambitious climate responses difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Interest_group_liberalism" title="Interest group liberalism">Interest-group liberalism</a> is guided by individual human priorities.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Groups unable to voice their self-interest, such as minorities without suffrage, or non-humans, are not included in the political compromise. Addressing environmental crises can be impeded when citizens of liberal democracies do not see environmental problems as impacting their lives, or when they lack the education to evaluate the importance of the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> The human benefits from environmental exploitation and protection compete.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> Considering the implications of ecological degradation for future human generations can give environmental concerns a basis in anthropocentric liberal democratic politics.</p>
<p>William Ophuls posits that liberal democracies are unfit to address environmental problems, and that the prioritization of these challenges would involve a transition to more authoritarian forms of government.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Others counter this by pointing to the past successes of environmental reform movements to improve water and air quality in liberal societies.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> In practice, environmentalism can improve democracy rather than necessitate its end, by expanding democratic participation and promoting political innovations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tensions between liberal democracy and environmental goals raise questions about the possible limitations of democracy (or at least democracy as we know it): in its responsiveness to subtle but large-scale problems, its ability to work from a holistic societal perspective, its aptness in coping with environmental crisis relative to other forms of government.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> Democracies do not have the provisions to make environmental reforms that are not mandated by voters, and many voters lack incentives or desire to demand policies that could compromise immediate prosperity. The question arises as to whether the foundation of politics is morality or practicality.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> A scheme that conceives of and values the environment beyond its human utility, an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">environmental ethics</a>, could be crucial for democracies to respond to climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_forms_of_democracy_for_environmental_policy">Alternative forms of democracy for environmental policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative forms of democracy for environmental policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In political theory, <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a> has been discussed as a political model more compatible with environmental goals. Deliberative democracy is a system in which informed political equals weigh values, information, and expertise, and debate priorities to make decisions, as opposed to a democracy based on interest aggregation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This definition of democracy emphasizes informed discussion among citizens in the decision making process, and encourages decisions to benefit the common good rather than individual interests.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Amy Gutmann and Dennis Thompson claimed that reason prevails over self-interest in deliberative democracy, making it a more just system.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The broad perspective that this discursive model encourages could lead to a stronger engagement with environmental concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In political theory, the lottery system is a democratic design that allows governments to address problems with future, rather than immediate, impacts. Deliberative bodies composed of randomly selected representatives can draft environmental policies that have short-term costs without considering the political consequences for re-election.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_materialism_and_environmental_justice">New materialism and environmental justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: New materialism and environmental justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New materialism is a strain of thought in philosophy and the social sciences that conceives of all material as having life or agency.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It criticizes frameworks of justice that center on human attributes like consciousness as insufficient for modern ethical problems that concern the natural environment. It is a post-humanist consideration of all matter that rejects arguments of utility that privilege humans. This politically relevant social theory combats inequality beyond the interpersonal plane.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">[15]</a></sup> People are ethically responsible for one another, and for the physical spaces they navigate, including animal and plant life, and the inanimate matter that sustains it, like soil. New materialism encourages political action according to this world vision, even if it is incompatible with economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jane_Bennett_(political_theorist)" title="Jane Bennett (political theorist)">Jane Bennett</a> uses the term "vital materialism" in her book <i>Vibrant Matter: A Political Ecology of Things</i>. She develops the concept of materialism with the aim of providing a stronger basis in political theory for environmental politics.</p>
<p>New materialists have invoked <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Derrida</a> and other historical thinkers to trace the emergence of their philosophy and to justify their environmental claims:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"No justice ... seems possible or thinkable without the principle of some responsibility, beyond all living present, within that which disjoins the living present, before the ghosts of those who are not yet born or who are already dead [...]. Without this non-contemporaneity with itself of the living present ... without this responsibility and this respect for justice concerning those who are not there, of those who are no longer or who are not yet present and living, what sense would there be to ask the question 'where?' 'where tomorrow?' 'whither?'"<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>All material, living and dead, is interrelated in "the mesh" as described by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Morton" title="Timothy Morton">Timothy Morton</a>. As all matter is interdependent, humans have obligations to all parts of the material world, including those that are unfamiliar.</p>
<p>New materialism is related to a shift from the view of the environment as a form of capital to a form of labor (see <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerging_nations">Emerging nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emerging nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brazil, Russia, India, and China (known as the "<a href="/wiki/BRICS" title="BRICS">BRIC</a>" nations) are rapidly industrializing, and are increasingly responsible for global carbon emissions and the associated climate change. Other forms of environmental degradation have also accompanied the economic growth in these nations.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">[19]</a></sup> Environmental degradation tends to motivate action more than the threat of global warming does, since air and water pollution cause immediate health problems, and because pollutants can damage natural resources, hampering economic potential.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:324px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Environmntal_Kuznets_Curve.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Environmntal_Kuznets_Curve.png/322px-Environmntal_Kuznets_Curve.png" width="322" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/Environmntal_Kuznets_Curve.png 1.5x" data-file-width="461" data-file-height="261" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Environmntal_Kuznets_Curve.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Kuznets curve is a hypothetical curve representing the trajectory of environmental degradation in developing nations as a function of per capita income.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With rising incomes, environmental degradation tends to decrease in industrializing nations, as depicted in the Environmental Kuznets Curve (described in a section of the <a href="/wiki/Kuznets_curve" title="Kuznets curve">Kuznets Curve</a> article). Citizens demand better air and water quality, and technology becomes more efficient and clean when incomes increase.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-19">[19]</a></sup> The level of income per capita needed to reverse the trend of environmental degradation in industrializing nations varies with the environmental impact indicator.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> More developed nations can facilitate eco-friendly transitions in emerging economies by investing in the development of clean technologies.</p>
<p>Laws implemented in response to environmental concerns vary by nation (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_laws_by_country" title="List of environmental laws by country">List of environmental laws by country</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China's environmental ills include acid rain, severe smog, and a reliance on coal-burning for energy.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup> China has instated environmental policies since the 1970s, and has one of the most extensive environmental conservation programs on paper.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, regulation and enforcement by the central government in Beijing are weak, so solutions are decentralized. Wealthier provinces are far more effective in their preservation and sustainable development efforts than poorer regions.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup> China therefore provides an example of the consequences of environmental damage falling disproportionately on the poor.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> NGOs, the media, and the international community have all contributed to China's response to environmental problems.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>For history, laws, and policies, see <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_in_China" title="Environmental policy in China">Environmental policy in China</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_politics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_India" title="Constitution of India">Constitution of India</a> was amended to reflect environmental priorities, motivated in part by the potential threat of natural resource depletion to economic growth:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The State shall endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wildlife." (Art. 48A)</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>"It shall be the duty of every citizen of India ... to protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers and wildlife, and to have compassion for living creatures." (Art. 51A)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, in India, as in China, the implementation of written environmental policies, laws, and amendments has proven challenging. Official legislation by the central government (see a partial list at <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_of_the_Government_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental policy of the Government of India">Environmental policy of the Government of India</a>) is often more symbolic than practical.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Environment_and_Forests_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Environment and Forests (India)">Ministry of Environment and Forests</a> was established in 1985, but corruption within bureaucratic agencies, namely the influence of wealthy industry leaders, limited any attempts at enforcement of the policies put in place.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
