			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Juncture</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">For the 2007 film, see <a href="/wiki/Juncture_(film)" title="Juncture (film)">Juncture (film)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Juncture</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, is the manner of moving (transition) or mode of relationship between two consecutive sounds. It is the relationship between two successive <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a> in speech.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENicolosiHarrymanKresheck2004166_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENicolosiHarrymanKresheck2004166-1">[1]</a></sup> A juncture is, formally, a <a href="/wiki/Segment_(linguistics)" title="Segment (linguistics)">suprasegmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemic</a> cue, a means by which a listener can distinguish between two otherwise identical sequences of sounds that have different meanings.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENicolosiHarrymanKresheck2004166_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENicolosiHarrymanKresheck2004166-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typology">Typology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juncture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Typology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several kinds of juncture, the most widely used typology of which is:</p>
<dl>
<dt>plus juncture</dt>
<dd>Also known as <b>open juncture</b>, this is subdivided into <b>internal open juncture</b> and <b>external open juncture</b>. It is the juncture that occurs at word boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup> In phonetic transcription open juncture is transcribed <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/+/</span>, hence the name <i>plus</i> juncture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>close juncture</dt>
<dd>Also known as a <b>normal transition</b>, this is a transition between segments (sounds) within a word.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>terminal juncture</dt>
<dd>Also known as <b>falling</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrystal2011238_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrystal2011238-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>clause terminal</b> or <b>terminal contour</b>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> this is the juncture at the end of a clause or utterance with falling pitch before a silence.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrystal2011238_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrystal2011238-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Other less common typologies exist,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup> such as the division (favoured by American <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">Structuralist linguists</a> in the middle twentieth century) into <b>plus</b>, <b>single bar</b>, <b>double bar</b>, and <b>double cross</b> junctures, denoted <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/+/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/|/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/||/</span>, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/#/</span> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERajimwale2006119.E2.80.93120_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERajimwale2006119.E2.80.93120-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-bar_juncture.22_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-bar_juncture.22-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-cross_juncture.22_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-cross_juncture.22-6">[6]</a></sup> These correspond to syllabification and differences in intonation, single bar being a level pitch before a break, double bar being an upturn in pitch and a break, and double cross being a downturn in pitch that usually comes at the end of an utterance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-bar_juncture.22_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-bar_juncture.22-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-cross_juncture.22_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22double-cross_juncture.22-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22single-bar_juncture.22_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrask1996.22single-bar_juncture.22-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalmkjaer2002409_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalmkjaer2002409-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_from_English">Examples from English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juncture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples from English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English, a syllable break at the plus juncture sometimes distinguishes otherwise homophonic phrases.</p>
<ul>
<li>"a name" <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span> and "an aim" <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENasr199745_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENasr199745-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>"that stuff" <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ð/ 'th' in 'thy'">ð</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span> and "that's tough" <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ð/ 'th' in 'thy'">ð</span><span title="/æ/ short 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERajimwale2006119_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERajimwale2006119-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A word boundary preceded or followed by a syllable break is called an <i>external open juncture</i>. If there is no break, so that words on either side of the juncture are run together, the boundary is called an <i>internal open juncture</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESkanderaBurleigh201162-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The distinction between open and close juncture is the difference between "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/night#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:night">night</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rate#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:rate">rate</a>", <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> with the open juncture between <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span></span>/</a></span></span>, and "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nitrate#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:nitrate">nitrate</a>", <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> with close juncture between <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oronyms">Oronyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juncture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oronyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Recreational_linguistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Recreational linguistics">recreational linguistics</a>, an <i>oronym</i> is a pair of phrases which are <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophonic</a>. When pronounced without a pause between words (internal open juncture), phrases which differ in meaning and spelling may share a similar pronunciation. Examples include "ice cream" <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aɪs+kriːm/</span> and "I scream" <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aɪ+skriːm/</span> (This is employed in the chant "I scream; you scream; we all scream for ice cream" that is familiar to many English-speaking children.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoldstein2010321_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldstein2010321-11">[11]</a></sup>) The label <i>oronym</i> was suggested by <a href="/wiki/Gyles_Brandreth" title="Gyles Brandreth">Gyles Brandreth</a> and first published in his book <i>The Joy of Lex</i> (1980).</p>
